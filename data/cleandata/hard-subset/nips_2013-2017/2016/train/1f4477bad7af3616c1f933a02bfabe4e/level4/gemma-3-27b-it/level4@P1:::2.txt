This paper studies a particular class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to a globally optimal solution with a linear convergence rate using a novel nonlinear spectral method. The method is applied to networks with one and two hidden layers, and its performance is evaluated on a series of real-world datasets. The key idea is to restrict the network weights to be nonnegative and to optimize a modified objective function involving the sum of the loss and the negative total sum of the outputs. While the authors acknowledge that these constraints are restrictive and somewhat counterintuitive, they argue that the resulting models are still sufficiently expressive. 

This work builds upon recent advances in globally optimal training of neural networks [8, 11], but distinguishes itself by presenting a practically feasible algorithm with guaranteed convergence. Previous approaches, such as those in [8] and [11], either suffer from computational challenges or rely on assumptions about the data-generating measure that are difficult to verify in practice. This paper, in contrast, requires only nonnegative data, a common characteristic of many real-world datasets (e.g., images, word frequencies), and a check on the spectral radius of a small matrix, which can be performed *before* training.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical novelty and the potential for developing truly globally optimal training algorithms for neural networks. The linear convergence rate is a significant advantage over stochastic gradient descent methods, which offer no such guarantee. The explicit conditions for global optimality, based on the spectral radius of a matrix, provide a clear criterion for assessing the suitability of a given network architecture. Furthermore, the authors begin to demonstrate the empirical viability of their approach on benchmark datasets.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The core assumption of nonnegative weights is a strong restriction that may limit the applicability of the method. While the authors claim sufficient expressive power, the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate this. The comparison with a *linear* SVM, while showing competitive performance, is somewhat questionable given that the proposed method is nonlinear and a nonlinear SVM (e.g., with a radial basis function kernel) would likely provide a more relevant benchmark. The UCI datasets used are relatively small, and it’s unclear how well the method would scale to higher-dimensional data, given the authors’ observation that the computational complexity grows with the number of hidden units. The current bounds on the matrix spectral radius appear conservative, and exploring tighter bounds could expand the range of applicable network architectures. Furthermore, the interplay between the modified objective function and the achieved optimality is not fully explored; investigating whether stochastic gradient descent might also converge to a global optimum under similar conditions would be valuable. Finally, while the authors propose a way to select valid candidate parameters, a discussion of methods for efficiently searching this space (given that it might still be large) would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical results demonstrating global optimality with a linear convergence rate.
*   Clear and explicit conditions for guaranteeing global optimality.
*   Initial empirical validation on benchmark datasets.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in deep learning – the lack of guarantees in training.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong assumption of nonnegative weights restricts applicability.
*   Experimental results are not fully convincing, especially the comparison to a linear SVM.
*   Limited scalability to high-dimensional data.
*   The objective function and its role in achieving optimality could be further investigated. 



**Overall:** This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization. While the practical limitations of the method and the somewhat unconvincing experimental results raise concerns, the novelty of the approach and the potential for future research warrant consideration. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly a more compelling experimental evaluation and a discussion of the method's scalability.
