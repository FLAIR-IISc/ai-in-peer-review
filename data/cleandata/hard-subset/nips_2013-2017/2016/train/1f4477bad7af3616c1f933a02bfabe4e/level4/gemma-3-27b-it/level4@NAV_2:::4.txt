The paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks, offering a linear convergence rate under relatively weak data assumptions. This is a noteworthy contribution, as achieving global optimality with guarantees in neural network training is a significant challenge. The approach’s reliance on computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix to verify the global optimality condition is a practical strength. 

The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the algorithm and associated theoretical results. It effectively positions the work within the broader context of neural network optimization, referencing relevant literature such as [8, 11] and acknowledging the limitations of prior approaches. The theoretical development, culminating in Theorem 1 and its extension to two hidden layers in Theorem 5, appears rigorous and well-motivated, building upon concepts from nonlinear eigenvalue problems and Perron-Frobenius theory [7, 14].

A key weakness lies in the restrictive requirement of non-negativity on network weights, and the corresponding use of generalized polynomials as activation functions. While the authors acknowledge this constraint, its potential impact on the expressiveness and performance of the network is substantial. The experimental results, while demonstrating competitive performance on some UCI datasets, do not definitively show superiority over standard methods like SVM, raising concerns about the practical benefits of the imposed constraints. The claim regarding a "typically few iterations" for convergence, based on the linear convergence rate, may be overstated; linear convergence does not automatically imply rapid convergence, and depends heavily on the magnitude of the spectral radius.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The work presents a novel approach to globally optimal training, a challenging problem in neural network research.
*   Theoretical Soundness: The method is supported by rigorous theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees.
*   Clear Presentation: The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its theoretical foundations.
*   Initial Empirical Validation: Experiments demonstrate feasibility and competitive performance on benchmark datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Strong Constraints: The non-negativity constraint on weights significantly limits the applicability and potential performance of the method.
*   Limited Experimental Superiority: The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate an advantage over existing methods.
*   Potential Overstatement: The claim about the speed of convergence based on the linear convergence rate needs further qualification.

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound and theoretically interesting contribution to the field of neural network optimization. However, the practical limitations imposed by the constraints and the lack of definitive empirical superiority raise concerns. A revision addressing the limitations of the method and providing more compelling experimental results would strengthen the paper considerably.
