This paper presents a compelling theory demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks has a global optimum that can be achieved at a linear convergence rate. The primary constraint is that all input data and weights of the linear layer must be positive, and the activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The proposed theory is first established for neural networks with a single hidden layer and then extended to networks with multiple hidden layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments show that the proposed algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable performance on some small real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

The strengths of this paper lie in its novelty and the simplicity of its preconditions. As claimed by the authors, this is the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network with optimal and convergence guarantees. Previous work with convergence guarantees often requires complicated methods to check preconditions, some of which are even impossible to verify in practice due to their dependence on the data-generating measure. In contrast, the theory proposed in this work has relatively simple preconditions that depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix consisting of the network's parameters. The authors also demonstrate the applicability of their theory to networks of arbitrary depth and show reasonable results on small real datasets.

However, the proposed network still has limited applications due to several constraints. The theory requires both input data and network parameters to be positive, activation functions to be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius (Theorem 1) to be less than 1. While the non-negative constraints on input data may not significantly limit potential usage, as most training data can be easily shifted to be all positive, the requirement that network parameters be positive might limit the expressiveness of the model. Furthermore, the fact that all activation functions across hidden layers must be different is unnatural and may also limit the model's applicability. Additionally, the authors note that to satisfy the spectral radius constraint, they need to increase p1 and p2 while decreasing pw and pu, effectively reducing the weights of linear units. If I understand correctly, this implies that as the dimension of the input and network parameters increases, the weights of linear units must be decreased to satisfy the spectral radius constraint. When linear weights are very small, activation functions will behave similarly to linear functions, and adding more layers will only add another linear function without increasing the network's expressiveness. This raises concerns about the ease of extending this method to deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, this paper presents an encouraging result as an initial attempt, and I believe it should be accepted. 

Minor comments: I am curious about how p1, p2, pw, pu, the dimension of the input, and the size of the network parameters affect the spectral radius rho(A). For example, does rho(A) increase linearly or exponentially with the dimension of the input, and how should pw and pu be adjusted to counteract this effect? While a formal proof may be challenging, it might be possible to empirically plot this relationship using synthetic data.
