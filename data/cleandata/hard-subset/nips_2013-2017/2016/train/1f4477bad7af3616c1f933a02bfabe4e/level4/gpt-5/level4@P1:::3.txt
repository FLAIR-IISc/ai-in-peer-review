This paper develops a rigorous theory showing that a certain class of neural networks admits a globally optimal solution that can be reached with a linear convergence rate via a nonlinear spectral method. The main constraints are that inputs and all layer weights are nonnegative, and the activations are generalized polynomials. The theory is first established for one hidden layer and then extended to multiple layers. The authors also propose an explicit optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods. Experiments demonstrate strong performance on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable results on several small real-world UCI datasets.

Strengths:
- As claimed, this appears to be the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network training method with both global optimality and linear convergence guarantees. Prior guarantees either rely on preconditions that are difficult or impossible to verify in practice (e.g., dependence on the data-generating measure) or are not practically applicable.
- The key condition for the guarantee reduces to checking the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix constructed from the network parameters. This is comparatively simple and can be verified before running the algorithm.
- The authors outline how the approach extends to deeper networks and provide a concrete two-hidden-layer result, together with experiments on real datasets.
- The optimization algorithm is clear and motivated by nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, and the convergence analysis is detailed and careful.

Weaknesses:
- The proposed architecture has limited applicability. The theory requires: (i) nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights, (ii) generalized polynomial activations, and (iii) a spectral radius condition (Theorem 1) strictly below 1. While nonnegativity of inputs can often be arranged by shifting data, the nonnegativity of weights may reduce model expressiveness.
- Requiring all hidden units to use different activation exponents is somewhat unnatural and may further limit practical use.
- The authors note that, to satisfy the spectral radius condition, they need to increase p1 and p2 and decrease pw and pu, which effectively reduces the magnitude of the linear weights. If I understand correctly, as dimensionality or network size grows, these constraints force smaller linear weights. When the linear weights are very small, the activations behave almost linearly, so adding more layers does not increase expressiveness; it is effectively composing near-linear functions. This raises concerns about scaling the method to deep architectures in practice.
- The experimental evaluation is limited to low-dimensional datasets. While this is understandable for a proof-of-concept, the constraints may become more restrictive as dimensionality and network width increase, potentially making it hard to achieve both global optimality and strong nonlinear expressiveness simultaneously.

Overall assessment:
- Quality: The theoretical development appears sound and thorough, with clear statements and proofs of the main results. The algorithmic mapping to fixed-point theory is well-motivated.
- Clarity: Generally well-written (though dense), with the algorithm and conditions stated explicitly. The reliance on spectral radius is clearly articulated.
- Originality: The nonlinear spectral approach for globally optimal training of this class of networks is novel, and the practical checkability of the condition is a notable advance relative to prior work.
- Significance: Despite restrictions, this is an important step toward practically verifiable global optimality guarantees for neural networks, and it opens a promising research direction.

Minor comments and suggestions:
- I am curious about how pw, pu, the input dimension, and network size affect the spectral radius ρ(A) in practice. For example, as input dimension increases, does ρ(A) grow linearly or exponentially? To counteract that, should pw and pu be adjusted linearly or exponentially? While a full proof may be difficult, empirical plots on synthetic data illustrating these relationships would be informative.
- It would be helpful to discuss more explicitly the trade-off between choosing large p’s (to satisfy ρ(A) < 1) and the resulting shrinkage of effective weights, and how that impacts expressiveness and depth.

Despite the concerns about expressiveness and scalability under the spectral radius constraints, this is an encouraging and rigorous first step. I recommend acceptance.
