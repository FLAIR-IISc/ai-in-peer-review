- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method achieving global optimality with linear convergence for certain feedforward networks (demonstrated on one- and two-hidden-layer models).
- Experiments performed on several real-world (UCI) datasets, but dataset choice is limited in size and feature dimensionality.
- Method is heavily constrained by non-negativity of weights and maximization of weight regularization, which may be impractical.
- Requires a generalized polynomial activation to satisfy optimality conditions, reducing applicability to common activations.
- Restrictive theoretical assumptions limit practical relevance; more application-oriented explanation and real-world cases are needed.
- Authors claim sufficient expressive power, but empirical results do not convincingly support that claim.
- Linear SVM baseline is used (and sometimes outperforms NLSM variants), which is surprising and makes comparisons unfair without nonlinear SVM baselines (e.g., RBF or polynomial kernels).
- Reported comparisons may be flawed: linear SVM appears suboptimally tuned while NLSM benefits from tuned network structure.
- Scalability concerns: spectral radius bounds grow with hidden units, casting doubt on applicability to high-dimensional or large-hidden-layer models.
- Ensuring the optimality condition without prior parameter knowledge or an enormous parameter search is problematic; a fast method to find valid parameter candidates is needed.
- Suggests investigating whether global optimality arises from the special objective and whether SGD can empirically reach global optima on the tested datasets.
