- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method for 1–2 hidden-layer feedforward nets with generalized polynomial activations, non-negative weights, and non-negative data.  
- Main theoretical contribution: provable linear convergence of an iterative algorithm to the unique global optimum of a modified objective under verifiable architectural conditions.  
- Practical advantage over prior global-optimality work: algorithm is feasible in practice and relies on weaker assumptions than methods like Haeffele & Vidal or Janzamin et al.  
- Strength: original and significant contribution to globally optimal training, with no tuning parameters like learning rates.  
- Strength: rigorous theory using Banach fixed-point theorem in the Thompson metric; convergence conditions are checkable via the spectral radius of a small matrix.  
- Strength: empirical proof-of-concept on UCI datasets showing competitiveness with SVMs and ReLU networks on some tasks.  
- Weakness: presentation clarity—Section 2 and the motivation for the non-standard objective Φ (including the sum of outputs and ε term) need clearer justification.  
- Weakness: Theorem 1 is dense and would benefit from a high-level, informal explanation of its conditions and implications.  
- Weakness: model expressiveness concerns—non-negative weight/data constraints are restrictive and require deeper discussion of representable functions and limitations vs. unconstrained models.  
- Weakness: experimental evaluation is limited—baselines lack more advanced optimization methods and there is no reporting of computational resources (training time, memory).  
- Weakness: the claim that convergence "typically in a few (less than 10) iterations" should be clarified as an empirical observation, not a direct theoretical consequence.  
- Minor issues: undefined notation (p_w, p_u in S_+; R_{++}), and a typo ("polyomial" → "polynomial").
