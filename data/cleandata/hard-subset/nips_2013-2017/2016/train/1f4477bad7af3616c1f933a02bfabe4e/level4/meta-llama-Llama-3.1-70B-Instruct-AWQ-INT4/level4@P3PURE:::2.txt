This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to achieve global optimality with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors apply this method to deep networks with one and two hidden layers and evaluate its performance on a series of real-world datasets. However, as noted by the authors, the class of feedforward neural networks studied is restrictive and counterintuitive, as it imposes non-negativity constraints on the network weights and maximizes the regularization of these weights. Furthermore, the optimality condition requires the use of the less popular generalized polynomial activation function. These assumptions are not entirely reasonable and could benefit from detailed explanations from the perspectives of applications or real-world datasets, rather than making more restrictive assumptions to achieve global optimality.

The authors claim that the targeted class of neural networks still possesses sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieves good performance. However, the empirical experimental results do not provide strong evidence to support this claim. In some cases, linear SVM appears to perform better than NLSM1 and NLSM2, even when the linear SVM does not seem to be properly tuned, while NLSM tunes its network structure. Moreover, it is surprising that a linear SVM is used as a baseline, rather than a nonlinear SVM, such as polynomial kernel or Gaussian kernel. As a result, the comparisons are not entirely fair, and the conclusions drawn from these comparable results are not convincing. In other words, these results do not demonstrate the sufficient expressive power of the studied class of neural networks.

The experiments conducted on datasets from the UCI repository are limited by their small sample size and feature dimensionality. According to the analysis, the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which may pose a problem for high-dimensional data. It would be beneficial to discuss potential solutions to this issue, as it may significantly limit the applicability of this method to a large proportion of applications. Additionally, it appears that the optimal condition is not guaranteed by any settings of parameters that could be used in the studied neural networks. Although the condition can be checked without running the neural networks, it remains a problem if no priors are available or the possible parameters are extremely large. It would be helpful to explore fast approaches to obtain a small set of valid candidate parameters.

It is also worth investigating whether the optimal property of the studied model stems from the special objective function used. Furthermore, it would be interesting to examine whether stochastic gradient descent can also converge to the global optimal empirically on some tested datasets.
