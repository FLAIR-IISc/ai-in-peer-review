This paper introduces a specific class of feedforward neural networks for which a unique global optimum is guaranteed to exist. The authors subsequently propose an algorithm that finds this global optimum and prove that it has a linear convergence rate.

The mathematical derivations are generally instructive; however, several points require clarification and raise concerns about the model's practical utility.

1.  The transition from Theorem 3 to Theorem 4 is unclear. Theorem 3 relies on first-order derivative conditions, whereas Theorem 4 introduces second-order derivative conditions. The motivation for this escalation in derivative order should be explained.

2.  The treatment of the parameter matrix `u`, which represents the connections to the hidden layer, is ambiguous. For instance, is the gradient with respect to `u` in G^Φ (Equation 3) computed with respect to the vectorized form of `u`? Similar ambiguity arises in other parts of the paper, such as the constraint that bounds `u` to a ball. Clarifying how operations are defined for this matrix parameter would improve readability.

3.  A significant concern is that the model's design appears to be primarily motivated by the needs of the theoretical proof rather than by practical modeling considerations. For example, the model maximizes the L1-norm of the connection parameters (Equation 2). This is contrary to standard regularization techniques like weight decay, which penalize large parameter norms. It appears this constraint, along with the small epsilon, exists solely to ensure the gradient is strictly positive (i.e., in V++), a condition required for the proof. The paper should provide a stronger justification for this unconventional choice from a machine learning perspective.

4.  The practicality of the proposed network design is questionable due to its restrictive constraints. The model requires all input data to be non-negative and all network weights to be strictly positive. This implies that the network can only perform additive aggregations of positive values. It is unclear how effective such a model can be on general-purpose tasks, limiting its applicability.

5.  The experimental evaluation is limited. While the comparison with SVMs is understandable, given that both have a unique global optimum, a more compelling comparison would be against standard, non-convex neural network architectures (e.g., those using ReLU activations). Such a comparison is crucial to demonstrate whether the guarantee of global optimality provides a tangible advantage that outweighs the potential trade-offs in model expressiveness and performance compared to more conventional deep learning models.
