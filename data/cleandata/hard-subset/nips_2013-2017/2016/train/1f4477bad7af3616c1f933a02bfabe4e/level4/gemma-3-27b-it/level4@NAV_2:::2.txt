This paper studies a particular class of feedforward neural networks provably trainable to a global optimum with a linear convergence rate using a novel nonlinear spectral method. The method is demonstrated on networks with one and two hidden layers, and evaluated on a series of real-world datasets. The core contribution lies in establishing conditions under which global optimality can be guaranteed, a significant challenge in neural network training. The approach, however, relies on restrictive assumptions, notably non-negativity constraints on network weights and the use of generalized polynomial activation functions. While the authors acknowledge these constraints, further justification regarding their impact on applicability and potential alternatives would be beneficial. More detailed explanations concerning the practical motivations for these choices, or analysis of how these restrictions affect performance in specific application scenarios, would strengthen the work.

The authors claim their restricted network class retains sufficient expressive power for complex decision boundaries. However, the empirical results do not strongly support this claim. While competitive on some datasets, performance is generally inferior to linear SVMs, and the comparison feels somewhat unbalanced given the nonlinear nature of the proposed method. The use of a linear SVM as a baseline, rather than a nonlinear counterpart (e.g., polynomial or Gaussian kernel SVM), raises concerns about fairness and potentially obscures the true advantages or disadvantages of the proposed method. This makes the conclusions regarding expressive power less convincing. Furthermore, the UCI datasets used are relatively small in both sample size and feature dimensionality, which limits the generalizability of the experimental findings. 

A key limitation appears to be the scaling of the spectral radius bound with the number of hidden units, posing a challenge for high-dimensional data. Addressing this issue – perhaps through alternative bounding techniques or network architectures – would significantly broaden the applicability of the method. While the authors mention the ability to check the optimality condition before training, the vast parameter space can make identifying valid configurations computationally expensive. Exploring strategies for efficiently narrowing down the search space for suitable parameter settings would be valuable. Finally, investigating whether stochastic gradient descent might also converge to a global optimum empirically on the tested datasets, even without the spectral method, would offer insightful comparisons and potentially illuminate the role of the objective function in achieving global optimality.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Novelty:** The paper presents a novel algorithm with provable guarantees of global optimality, which is a rare and valuable contribution in the field of neural network training.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis appears rigorous, and the proof strategy is well-defined.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the theoretical framework and algorithmic steps.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Restrictive Assumptions:** The strong assumptions (non-negativity, generalized polynomials) significantly limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are not compelling, and the baseline comparison is not ideal.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The scaling of the spectral radius bound with the number of hidden units raises concerns about the method's ability to handle high-dimensional data.
