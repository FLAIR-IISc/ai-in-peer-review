- Main contribution: a nonlinear spectral method that guarantees global optimality with linear convergence under weak data assumptions.
- Methodology: builds on nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones and Perron–Frobenius theory for nonnegative operators.
- Scope: applied to deep networks in theory but experiments limited to one- and two-hidden-layer architectures.
- Theoretical strength: rigorous mathematical framework with a detailed convergence proof to the global optimum.
- Algorithmic novelty: proposes a new training algorithm for neural networks based on the spectral approach.
- Empirical results: experiments on several UCI datasets show competitiveness but give mixed performance versus baselines.
- Restrictive model assumptions: requires non-negativity of weights and strong regularization, which is counterintuitive and limiting.
- Activation choice: relies on generalized polynomial activations that are uncommon and may not suit many applications.
- Comparison issues: unfair baseline choice (linear SVM) — nonlinear SVMs would be a more appropriate comparator.
- Dataset limitations: experiments use relatively small datasets in sample size and feature dimensionality, reducing real-world relevance.
- Scalability concern: bounds on the spectral radius matrix A grow with hidden units, problematic for high-dimensional networks.
- Practical gap: no guidance provided on how to choose method parameters in practice.
- Overall assessment: significant theoretical contribution but limited applicability and empirical support due to restrictive assumptions and experimental choices.
