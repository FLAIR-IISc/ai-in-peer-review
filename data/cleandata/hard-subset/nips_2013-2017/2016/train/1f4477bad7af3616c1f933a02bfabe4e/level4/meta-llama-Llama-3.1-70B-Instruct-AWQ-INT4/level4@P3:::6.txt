The paper proposes a method to train, under certain assumptions, a specific type of feed-forward neural networks. The authors prove optimal convergence and demonstrate that their method can be applied to a selection of "real-world" problems. The problem under investigation seems interesting, and any theoretical advance in this direction is appreciated.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The introduction provides a thorough background on the topic, and the authors do an excellent job of summarizing the main ideas and relating them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness and the authors' ability to provide a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper also demonstrates originality, as it presents a novel combination of familiar techniques and a unique approach to training neural networks.

However, the paper falls short in convincing the reader that the proposed class of neural networks is useful in practice. The authors raise the issue of the expressiveness of their restricted model but do not provide sufficient discussion on its limitations and potential applications. Concurrent approaches are not thoroughly discussed and contrasted, which would have strengthened the paper.

The experimental results are not comprehensive, and the authors only compare their approach to some SVM and ignore other methods, both other training methods for some neural networks and other algorithmic paradigms. Additionally, the authors do not provide information about the computational resources demanded by the three algorithms, which would have been useful for a more comprehensive evaluation.

Some specific remarks on the paper include the need for a clearer explanation of the function Φ and its motivation. The definition of the loss function is straightforward, but the role of the epsilon term is not well-explained in the paper. Theorem 1 is hard to parse, and an informal explanation before stating it would have been helpful. The statement immediately after Theorem 1 regarding the convergence of the nonlinear spectral method should be clarified to indicate that it stems from experimental observation.

There are also some minor errors and typos in the paper, such as "polyomial" on page 60, "one CAN model" on page 67, and the lack of introduction for p1 and p2 on page 67. Additionally, the notation R_{++} is not introduced, which may cause confusion for some readers.

In conclusion, while the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, it would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of the proposed method's limitations and potential applications, as well as a more thorough comparison with concurrent approaches.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

1. The paper proposes a novel method for training neural networks with optimal convergence guarantees.
2. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis and experimental results to support their claims.
3. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate the usefulness of the proposed class of neural networks in practice.
2. The experimental results are not comprehensive, and the authors do not provide a thorough comparison with concurrent approaches.
3. The paper lacks a clear explanation of the function Φ and its motivation, and some minor errors and typos are present.
