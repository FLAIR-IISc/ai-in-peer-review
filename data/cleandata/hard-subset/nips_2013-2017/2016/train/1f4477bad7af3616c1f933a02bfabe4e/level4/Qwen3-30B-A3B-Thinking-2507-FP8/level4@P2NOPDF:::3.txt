

This paper establishes a novel theoretical framework demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks achieves global optimality with linear convergence rates under precise conditions. The core constraints require all input data and linear layer weights to be non-negative, activation functions to be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius of a non-negative matrix (derived from network parameters) to be less than 1. The theory is first developed for single-hidden-layer networks and subsequently extended to multi-layer architectures. An optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods is proposed, with experiments showing effectiveness on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable performance on select UCI benchmark datasets.  

**Strengths:** This work represents the first practical neural network architecture with provable global optimality and convergence guarantees. Prior methods with convergence guarantees often rely on complex, impractical preconditions—some dependent on unverifiable data-generating measures. In contrast, this paper’s conditions are comparatively simple, requiring only the spectral radius of a parameter-derived matrix to be less than 1. The authors convincingly demonstrate applicability to arbitrarily deep networks and validate results on small-scale real-world data, addressing a significant gap in the literature.  

**Weaknesses:** The theoretical constraints substantially limit practical applicability. The requirement for non-negative weights and inputs, while potentially mitigated by data shifting, fundamentally restricts model expressiveness. More critically, the necessity for all hidden-layer activation functions to be distinct generalized polynomials is unnatural and likely impractical for real-world tasks. The spectral radius constraint (Theorem 1) further imposes severe limitations: to satisfy ρ(A) < 1, the authors must increase parameters *p₁*, *p₂* while decreasing *p_w*, *p_u*, effectively reducing linear layer weights. Crucially, as input dimension or network size grows, the required weight scaling (decreasing *p_w*, *p_u*) becomes increasingly aggressive. When weights become sufficiently small, activation functions approximate linear behavior, rendering deeper layers redundant (as stacking linear transformations does not enhance expressiveness). This raises serious concerns about scalability to deep architectures, as the method may fail to leverage depth for complex representations.  

**Recommendation:** Despite these limitations, the work is a valuable theoretical contribution that establishes a promising foundation for future research. The simplicity of the preconditions represents a meaningful advancement over prior guarantees, and the empirical results on small datasets validate the core idea. The constraints are acknowledged as restrictive, but the paper’s focus on *practical* guarantees justifies its significance. Given the novelty and potential impact, the paper merits acceptance.  

**Minor Comments:** The relationship between hyperparameters (*p₁*, *p₂*, *p_w*, *p_u*), input dimension, network size, and spectral radius *ρ(A)* warrants deeper exploration. For instance:  
- How does *ρ(A)* scale with input dimension (linearly/exponentially)?  
- To maintain *ρ(A) < 1* as dimension increases, should *p_w*, *p_u* decrease linearly or exponentially?  
While a full theoretical derivation may be challenging, an empirical analysis on synthetic data could provide valuable insights into this scaling behavior.  

**Summary:** This paper delivers a theoretically rigorous, first-of-its-kind approach to neural network optimization with verifiable guarantees. While the non-negativity constraints and activation function requirements limit immediate applicability, the simplicity of the conditions and scalability to depth (in principle) represent a significant step forward. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and provides a crucial benchmark for future research on practical convergence guarantees. The concerns raised are valid but do not undermine the paper’s core contribution. Acceptance is strongly recommended.
