Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a restricted class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with linear convergence. The networks use nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations, and they are trained by maximizing a modified objective: negative cross-entropy plus the sum of outputs, under p-norm constraints on weights. The key result shows that, under mild data assumptions (nonnegative inputs) and an explicit spectral radius condition on a small nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 for one hidden layer, K+2 for two), the fixed-point iteration GΦ converges linearly to the unique global optima. The condition ρ(A) < 1 is checkable a priori and scales with architectural and norm parameters. The method is extended from one to two hidden layers, and experiments on several small UCI datasets demonstrate feasibility.

This work connects nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and multi-homogeneous maps to neural network training, echoing recent efforts to obtain global guarantees for nonconvex models. Compared with tensor methods that require score functions and non-degeneracy conditions (Janzamin et al., 2015) and matrix-factorization views with challenging practical applicability (Haeffele & Vidal), this paper offers a practically implementable algorithm with explicit, testable conditions and linear convergence rates.

Strengths
- Novelty and theory: A clear, mathematically grounded method leveraging nonlinear spectral theory to obtain global optimality and linear convergence for a nontrivial neural architecture. The condition is explicit and verifiable offline via the spectral radius of a small matrix.
- Generality across depth: Although the main focus is one- and two-hidden-layer networks, the framework and proofs are stated in a way that suggests extensibility.
- Algorithmic simplicity: The fixed-point scheme is parameter-free during training (no learning rate), with normalization enforcing the constraints.
- Empirical feasibility: The method runs quickly and exhibits the predicted linear convergence in practice.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- The theoretical development appears careful and technically solid; the fixed-point analysis, Lipschitz characterization under the Thompson metric, and contraction via ρ(A) are elegant. However, several bounds are acknowledged to be conservative. The paper would benefit from reporting actual ρ(A) values encountered in experiments to support the claim that “effective” spectral radii are typically much smaller.
- The optimality guarantee hinges on strong modeling constraints: nonnegative inputs and weights, generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents, and a nonstandard objective that adds the sum of outputs and a small positive offset to enforce strict positivity of gradients. The practical consequences of these constraints deserve deeper discussion and empirical validation (e.g., calibration or margin interpretation of the added output-sum term, sensitivity to ε).
- Empirical scope is limited. The UCI datasets are small in sample size and dimension. The method’s known challenge with increasing hidden units (ρ(A) grows with architecture size) suggests that scaling to higher-dimensional settings will be nontrivial; the paper should more directly investigate and report when ρ(A) < 1 fails as model size increases.

Clarity
- The paper is dense and proof-oriented, which is appropriate given the contribution, but several notational passages are hard to parse on first read. It would help to include a compact “cheat sheet” table of symbols and a clearer narrative on how the architectural choices affect A and ρ(A).
- Minor issues: duplicated sentence in the introduction; inconsistent notation for norms and data norms (ρx vs ρ̃x); several references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted.

Originality
- The use of nonlinear spectral methods and multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory for global training guarantees is original and timely. The translation of these ideas into an implementable algorithm with checkable conditions is a strong contribution.

Significance
- If the method can be made to work for moderately sized problems while retaining guarantees, it could influence how we think about provable training of constrained neural models. At present, the restrictive assumptions and small-scale experiments limit immediate impact. More convincing evidence of practical relevance is needed.

Experimental evaluation
- Baselines: The paper compares against RBF-kernel SVM and ReLU networks trained with SGD. This is appropriate and stronger than a linear SVM baseline. That said, it would be helpful to include polynomial-kernel SVMs given the polynomial structure of the activations, and nonnegativity-constrained baselines (e.g., monotone networks, nonnegative matrix factorization-based classifiers) to better isolate the effect of model constraints.
- Fairness/scope: Report the hyperparameter search ranges succinctly (already provided), but also include training time comparisons on a per-iteration basis and total wall-clock time across methods.
- Ablations: Crucial ablations are missing. Please compare: (i) your architecture trained by SGD on the same modified objective (you partially do this with Batch-SGD, but a broader optimizer suite such as Adam would be informative), (ii) the effect of removing the output-sum term (no guarantee, but helps understand its modeling impact), and (iii) sensitivity to α choices and to nonnegativity preprocessing of features (e.g., shifting to make inputs nonnegative).

Questions for the authors
- Practical guidance: How should practitioners choose α, ρu, ρw, and p exponents so that ρ(A) < 1 while maintaining adequate expressiveness? Any heuristics beyond conservative theoretical bounds?
- Negative features: How to handle standard features with negative values? Does shifting or splitting into positive/negative parts preserve performance and interpretability?
- Objective design: Can you provide a statistical or margin-based interpretation of the added output-sum term? How sensitive are results to ε?
- Scaling: Do you have examples where ρ(A) ≥ 1 for larger models? Are there tighter, data-dependent bounds or adaptive reparameterizations that reduce ρ(A) without sacrificing capacity?

Reasons to accept
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution with clear algorithm and linear convergence to the global optimum under explicit, checkable conditions.
- Bridges nonlinear spectral theory and neural network training in a way that is implementable and demonstrated empirically.
- Extends to two layers and suggests a pathway to deeper architectures.

Reasons to reject
- Practical restrictions (nonnegativity, unusual objective, polynomial activations) limit applicability; empirical evidence on small datasets does not convincingly demonstrate competitive performance or expressive power relative to strong baselines.
- Limited guidance on choosing parameters to satisfy ρ(A) < 1 without severely constraining model capacity; unclear scalability to higher-dimensional problems.
- Clarity and completeness issues (typos, references, missing ablations) reduce accessibility and reproducibility.

Overall, this is an elegant and potentially impactful theoretical paper with a promising algorithmic instantiation. Strengthening the empirical section (larger datasets, richer baselines, and ablations), clarifying parameter selection and the practical implications of the constraints, and tightening exposition would substantially improve the work.
