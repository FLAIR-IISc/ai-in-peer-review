Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of a constrained class of feed-forward neural networks with one or two hidden layers. The networks use nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents per hidden unit. Training is recast as maximizing a modified objective: negative cross-entropy plus a sum of network outputs and small positive linear terms in the parameters. The key result shows that, under a checkable spectral-radius condition on a small nonnegative matrix determined by architecture and norms, a fixed-point iteration based on normalized gradients is a contraction in the Thompson metric and converges linearly to the unique global optimum. The method has no learning-rate parameter and the convergence rate is governed by the spectral radius.

This work connects Perron–Frobenius theory for nonlinear, multi-homogeneous maps with neural network training, and situates itself relative to prior results on the hardness of neural network training (e.g., Sima), and globally optimal but less practical approaches via tensor methods (Janzamin et al.) or structured matrix factorization (Haeffele & Vidal). The paper offers empirical demonstrations on several low-dimensional UCI datasets and a comparison to ReLU networks trained with SGD and RBF-SVMs.

Strengths
- Novelty: A practically implementable globally optimal training procedure for a nontrivial neural architecture, with a clear, verifiable spectral condition and a linear convergence guarantee.
- Theoretical depth: A careful fixed-point framework using Banach contraction in the Thompson metric; explicit construction of the Lipschitz bound via a nonnegative matrix; extension from one to two hidden layers.
- Clarity of the high-level goal and motivation in the introduction; ties to classical PF theory and recent global-optimality work.
- Practical aspects: No step-size tuning; condition can be checked pre-training by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix (size K+1 or K+2).

Weaknesses
- Practical scope and expressiveness: The model requires nonnegative inputs and weights, distinct polynomial exponents, and a modified objective that includes a sum over outputs. The paper raises expressiveness concerns but does not systematically analyze what functions are representable or where the approach will likely fail. Guidance on what is in/out of scope is limited.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are restricted to small UCI datasets. Baselines are narrow (RBF-SVM and ReLU with SGD). Missing are comparisons to other constrained/monotone models, NMF-like architectures, tensor-based global methods (even if approximate), and other strong classical baselines (e.g., logistic regression with calibrated features, tree ensembles).
- Practicality details: No wall-clock times, iteration counts, or per-iteration complexity to substantiate claims of “few iterations” and speed relative to SGD. The computational cost of forming gradients and normalizations vs. batch/mini-batch updates is not quantified.
- Presentation and readability: Section 2 is hard to follow. Theorem 1 is introduced without an informal explanation of the objects and condition; motivations for the modified objective and the epsilon term are not stated in the main text. Some notation (e.g., R_{++}, p1/p2) and typos hinder readability.
- Sensitivity and robustness: No ablation of the role of distinct α, the nonnegativity constraints, the added output-sum term, or the choice of p-norms and radius parameters. It is unclear how performance degrades when the spectral-radius condition approaches 1 or fails.

Quality
The theoretical contribution is technically solid and carefully grounded in nonlinear PF theory. Assumptions are clear, and the condition is checkable. However, the empirical validation is limited in scope and does not fully clarify practical benefits and limitations.

Clarity
The introduction is clear, but later sections are dense. The main theorem would benefit from an informal explanation of all quantities (e.g., why the A matrix has that structure, intuition for the spectral condition). The role of the epsilon term and the output-sum term should be motivated in the main text (not only the supplement/rebuttal). Several typos and missing definitions disrupt flow (see Specific comments).

Originality
The approach is original in blending multi-homogeneous fixed-point theory with neural network training to obtain global optimality and linear rates under pre-checkable conditions. It extends prior global-optimality results to a more implementable algorithm.

Significance
The work is significant theoretically. The practical impact remains uncertain due to modeling constraints and limited experiments. Strengthening empirical evidence and clarifying expressiveness could raise the significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an informal walkthrough before Theorem 1: what GΦ does, why normalization, intuition for A and the spectral-radius condition, and what drives linear convergence.
- Motivate the modified objective and epsilon term in the main text, explaining why strict positivity is needed for the contraction.
- Provide an expressiveness and limitations discussion: classes of decision boundaries realizable under nonnegativity and distinct exponents; when constraints likely harm performance; guidance on choosing α and norms.
- Expand empirical evaluation: include wall-clock times, iteration counts to tolerance, and per-iteration complexity; add baselines such as logistic regression, tree ensembles, monotone/constrained networks, and (where feasible) tensor-based methods; show higher-dimensional or sparse count data where nonnegativity is natural.
- Include ablations: effect of removing the output-sum term, relaxing nonnegativity (if possible), varying α and the norm parameters; behavior as ρ(A) nears 1.
- Improve readability: define all symbols on first use (R_{++}, p_w, p_u), ensure consistent notation, and move some technical details to the supplement.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, rigorous theoretical contribution with a clear, checkable global optimality condition and linear convergence.
- Novel and potentially influential connection between nonlinear spectral methods and NN training.
- Simple, parameter-free iteration with promising convergence behavior.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical relevance unclear due to restrictive modeling choices and limited empirical scope.
- Missing broader baselines, computational analysis, and ablations.
- Presentation issues in Section 2 and around Theorem 1; missing motivations/definitions.

Specific comments and typos
- Provide an informal explanation immediately before Theorem 1; clarify that the “few iterations” claim is empirical.
- Motivate the epsilon and output-sum terms in Φ in the main text.
- Define R_{++} on first use; p_w and p_u (not “p1 and p2”) should be introduced before appearing in constraints; ensure consistent notation.
- Typos: “polyomial” → “polynomial”; “one CAN model” → “one can model.”
- Improve flow in Section 2; consider a guiding paragraph and a figure or schematic of the fixed-point mapping.

Overall
A theoretically strong paper with an elegant global-optimality result for a constrained neural architecture. With a clearer presentation and stronger empirical evidence (including expressiveness analysis and computational metrics), this could be a compelling contribution. As is, I view it as borderline for acceptance, leaning positive on theory and negative on empirical/practical validation.
