

This paper examines a specific class of neural networks, demonstrating that it possesses a single stationary point. The authors introduce a rapid convergence method to the unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The work addresses an intriguing problem: showing that a relatively complex model, under carefully selected constraints, can exhibit only one stationary point. Beyond convexity or its generalizations (e.g., geodesic convexity), fixed-point theory is a standard approach for establishing stationary point uniqueness, which the paper employs. The authors construct a contractive mapping whose fixed-point coincides with the unique minimizer of the cost function. A limitation of the proposed method is its reliance on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix for the convergence proof; as noted by the authors, this constraint prevented exploration of networks with larger numbers of hidden units. Additionally, the method requires tuning numerous parameters, rendering the procedure unintuitive, and necessitates verification of the spectral condition for every parameter configuration. In experiments, the authors tested approximately 150,000 parameter combinations via cross-validation and selected the optimal set. However, their method outperformed linear-SVM on only two of seven datasets, casting doubt on the practical efficacy of the neural network architecture under investigation.
