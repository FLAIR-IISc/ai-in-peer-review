This paper investigates a specific class of neural networks and demonstrates the existence of a unique stationary point for its optimization problem. The authors introduce a novel method with fast convergence to this unique fixed-point, representing the global optimum of the cost function. The work addresses the important problem of achieving global optimality in neural network training through carefully chosen constraints, avoiding the need for convexity assumptions. The approach leverages fixed-point theory, constructing a contractive map whose fixed-point corresponds to the unique minimizer. A key limitation lies in the convergence proof’s dependence on bounding the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix, which currently restricts the application to networks with a limited number of hidden units, as acknowledged by the authors. Another challenge is the number of parameters requiring tuning, potentially making the method less intuitive and necessitating a verification step for the spectral condition for each parameter setting. The experimental evaluation involved exploring approximately 150,000 parameter combinations, selecting the best based on cross-validation accuracy. However, the method only consistently outperformed a linear SVM on two out of seven datasets, raising questions about the practical advantages of the investigated neural network model. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel algorithm with a theoretical guarantee of global optimality, a rare achievement in neural network training.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical framework, based on nonlinear spectral methods and fixed-point theory, appears sound and well-developed. The proofs are detailed, though some are relegated to supplementary material.
*   **Significance:** Establishing global optimality for even a restricted class of neural networks is a significant contribution, potentially informing the design of more robust and efficient training algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Scalability:** The method’s dependence on the spectral radius constraint limits its applicability to larger networks.
*   **Practicality:** The parameter tuning process and the need to verify the spectral condition appear cumbersome.
*   **Empirical Results:** The empirical performance is not consistently superior to standard methods like linear SVM, questioning the practical benefit.
*   **Originality:** While the spectral method itself is novel, the paper builds heavily on existing work in Perron-Frobenius theory and tensor decompositions (e.g., [8, 11]), and does not substantially improve upon the state of the art in practical performance.
