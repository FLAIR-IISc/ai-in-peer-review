- Paper proves a class of neural networks can reach global optimality with linear convergence under specific conditions.  
- Main constraints: all input data and linear layer weights must be positive, and activations are generalized polynomials.  
- Theory is developed for single-hidden-layer networks and extended to multiple hidden layers.  
- Authors propose an optimization algorithm based on the nonlinear spectral method.  
- Experiments show strong performance on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable results on small UCI datasets.  
- Strength: novelty—first potentially practical neural network with provable optimality and convergence guarantees.  
- Strength: preconditions are simple to check, depending only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative parameter matrix.  
- Applicability: theory extends to arbitrary depth and demonstrates empirical viability on small real datasets.  
- Limitation: positivity constraint on network parameters may reduce model expressiveness.  
- Limitation: requiring all hidden-layer activations to be different could limit practical applicability.  
- Limitation: spectral radius < 1 forces adjusting p1, p2, pw, pu (reducing linear weights), which may reduce expressiveness as network/input dimensionality grows.  
- Concern: as linear weights shrink, activations may behave nearly linearly and depth may not increase expressiveness.  
- Minor suggestion: empirically study how p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, and network size affect the spectral radius rho(A) (growth behavior and how to adjust pw, pu).
