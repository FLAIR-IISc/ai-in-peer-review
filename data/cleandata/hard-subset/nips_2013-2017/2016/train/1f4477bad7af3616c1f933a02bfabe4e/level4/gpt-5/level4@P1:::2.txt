Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a restricted class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with linear convergence. The networks use nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations, and the objective is modified by adding a sum over outputs (plus a small positive offset) to ensure strict positivity of gradients. The key theoretical result shows that, under mild data assumptions (nonnegative features) and architectural constraints, the fixed-point iteration induced by a multihomogeneous mapping converges linearly to a unique global maximizer whenever the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A (constructed from model parameters) is less than 1. The condition can be checked a priori. The method is extended from one to two hidden layers. Experiments on several UCI datasets compare the proposed method (NLSM1/2) against SVM and ReLU networks.

The work connects to recent efforts to give optimization guarantees for neural networks (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.). Compared to tensor-based or matrix-factorization approaches that are hard to use in practice, the authors aim for a practically implementable algorithm with a verifiable global optimality condition and linear convergence rate.

Strengths:
- Clear, rigorous fixed-point framework grounded in nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, with a concrete, checkable sufficient condition (ρ(A) < 1) for global convergence and rate.
- Extension to two hidden layers and a careful discussion of how the spectral radius scales with architectural parameters.
- Fast convergence in practice and no tunable learning-rate hyperparameters.
- Theoretical transparency: the role of nonnegativity, distinct activation exponents, and the added output-sum term are explicitly justified within the proof technique.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- The modeling assumptions are restrictive and somewhat counterintuitive: nonnegative weights throughout the network, generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents, and an objective augmented by the sum of outputs. The paper would benefit from deeper motivation for these choices from an application perspective. As written, these constraints seem driven primarily by the proof method rather than by modeling needs.
- Expressivity and empirical support: although the paper claims sufficient expressiveness to learn complex decision boundaries, the experimental evidence does not strongly support this. In several cases, SVM baselines appear to outperform NLSM1/2, and the tuning burden seems asymmetric (network structure tuned for NLSM, while the SVM baseline appears not to be equivalently tuned). Moreover, given that NLSM is nonlinear, it is unclear why a linear SVM would be used as the main baseline rather than a nonlinear SVM (e.g., polynomial or RBF). This makes the comparison potentially unfair and weakens the conclusions about expressivity.
- Dataset scale: the UCI datasets used are small in both sample size and dimensionality. Since the bounds on ρ(A) grow with the number of hidden units, and the required number of units typically grows with input dimension, the approach may struggle on higher-dimensional problems. The paper should address how to mitigate this (e.g., tighter bounds on A, architectural constraints, or alternative norms/metrics).
- Practical parameter selection: although ρ(A) can be checked without running the algorithm, it is not guaranteed that realistic parameter settings will satisfy ρ(A) < 1, especially when the search space is large and no priors are available. The paper would benefit from guidance or heuristics for efficiently narrowing down valid parameter candidates.
- Objective design: it is unclear to what extent the global optimality stems from the specific modified objective (cross-entropy minus outputs plus ε). An empirical ablation (with/without the output-sum and ε terms) and a discussion of how these terms affect classification performance would clarify this point.
- Convergence vs. SGD: since the paper emphasizes practical feasibility, it would be interesting to examine whether standard SGD can empirically reach the same optima on some datasets, even without guarantees, and to compare wall-clock time and final accuracy fairly.

Quality:
The theoretical analysis is careful and technically sound, and the connection to contraction mappings via the Thompson metric is well developed. However, the practical impact is limited by strong constraints and by conservative bounds that may become prohibitive as the network grows. The experiments do not yet convincingly demonstrate that the method is competitive when baselines are strong and fairly tuned.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear presentation of the main theorem, assumptions, and proof strategy. Some sections are dense (e.g., the definition and role of Ψ in the bounds), and more intuition would help readers understand the dependence of ρ(A) on model parameters. Explicit guidelines for selecting α, norms, and radii (ρu, ρw, etc.) would improve readability and reproducibility.

Originality:
Using nonlinear spectral methods and multihomogeneous maps to obtain global optimality with linear convergence for this network class is novel. The combination of nonnegativity, generalized polynomial activations, and a specially structured objective to enable a contraction mapping is an original perspective, albeit tailored to the proof technique.

Significance:
The theoretical guarantee is interesting, but the restrictive assumptions and limited empirical validation reduce immediate practical significance. Strengthening experiments (larger and higher-dimensional datasets, stronger baselines, ablations) and providing tighter, less conservative conditions could substantially increase impact.

Questions and suggestions:
- Provide stronger baselines and fair hyperparameter tuning (ideally including nonlinear SVMs and modern gradient-based training with comparable model selection).
- Offer practical heuristics to find valid parameter sets with ρ(A) < 1 without exhaustive search; e.g., constructive schedules for pw, pu vs. ρu, ρw, n1.
- Investigate tighter bounds for A (or data-dependent bounds) to reduce conservativeness and improve scalability with hidden units.
- Justify the modeling constraints with application-driven examples, or show that some can be relaxed (e.g., partial nonnegativity via reparameterization).
- Include ablations isolating the impact of the added output-sum and ε terms on accuracy and stability.
- Explore whether SGD empirically approaches the same solution on some tasks and compare compute requirements.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid and novel theoretical framework with linear convergence to a unique global optimum under a verifiable condition.
- Potentially useful for settings where nonnegativity and polynomial activations are natural (e.g., count data) and guarantees are valued.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Restrictive modeling assumptions with limited practical motivation.
- Empirical evidence on small datasets with potentially unfair or weak baselines; unclear competitiveness on realistic, high-dimensional tasks.
- Conservative conditions and open questions about parameter selection and scalability.
