This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights – to a globally optimal solution with a provable linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The method is demonstrated on networks with one and two hidden layers, and evaluated on several UCI datasets. This represents a significant contribution, as achieving global optimality in neural network training is generally difficult. The work builds upon recent advances in globally optimal training algorithms [8, 11], addressing some of their limitations, such as practical applicability and reliance on data distribution estimation. 

The core idea is compelling: by imposing non-negativity constraints on the weights and modifying the objective function, the authors leverage tools from nonlinear spectral theory to guarantee global convergence. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, culminating in Theorem 1 (and its extension to two hidden layers in Theorem 5), which provides conditions for global optimality based on the spectral radius of a matrix derived from the network architecture.  This is a valuable contribution to the field, offering a theoretical guarantee rarely seen in deep learning.  The inclusion of results for two hidden layers is a positive step towards broader applicability.

However, the strong assumptions required for the theoretical guarantees are a significant limitation. The non-negativity constraint on weights, while enabling the spectral method, is unconventional and potentially restrictive. Similarly, the use of generalized polynomial activation functions, instead of more common choices like ReLU, necessitates justification beyond merely satisfying the mathematical requirements of the method.  The authors acknowledge these restrictions, but further discussion regarding their impact on the practical applicability and expressiveness of the model would be beneficial. 

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating feasibility, does not strongly support the claim of competitive performance.  The comparison to a *linear* SVM is questionable, especially given the non-linear nature of the proposed method. A more appropriate comparison would involve non-linear kernel methods (e.g., polynomial or Gaussian kernel SVMs) to properly assess the expressiveness of the proposed approach. The datasets used are also relatively small and low-dimensional, which might not adequately showcase the method’s capabilities or highlight the scalability issues hinted at in the discussion of the spectral radius bounds.  The performance gains on some datasets (e.g., Cancer) are encouraging, but more extensive experimentation on larger, more complex datasets is needed. The authors also mention difficulty in choosing parameters and the possibility of small changes in parameters causing large variations in performance - this needs further investigation.

Several points warrant further clarification. The authors mention potential scalability issues related to the growth of the spectral radius with the number of hidden units. Addressing this limitation, or providing insights into how to mitigate it, is crucial for the broader adoption of the method.  The discussion of checking the convergence guarantee (spectral radius condition) is valuable, but the authors could elaborate on strategies for efficiently exploring the parameter space and identifying configurations that satisfy the condition. Finally, exploring the empirical performance of standard stochastic gradient descent on the same modified objective function could provide a baseline for assessing the benefits of the nonlinear spectral method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The method provides a globally optimal training algorithm with provable guarantees, a rare achievement in neural network optimization.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical analysis appears sound and the theorems are clearly stated.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-defined and the algorithm is clearly presented.
*   **Potential Impact:**  If the scalability issues can be addressed, this method could offer a valuable tool for training neural networks in specific applications where global optimality is critical.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Restrictive Assumptions:** The non-negativity constraint and the use of generalized polynomials limit the applicability of the method.
*   **Limited Empirical Evidence:** The experiments do not convincingly demonstrate competitive performance compared to standard methods.
*   **Questionable Baselines:** The comparison to a linear SVM is not appropriate.
*   **Scalability Concerns:**  The potential for the spectral radius to grow with network size raises concerns about scalability.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of neural network optimization. However, the restrictive assumptions, limited empirical evidence, and scalability concerns prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **weak accept** with the caveat that the authors address the concerns raised above, particularly regarding the empirical evaluation and scalability, in a revised version. A more compelling demonstration of practical performance and a discussion of strategies for mitigating the scalability issues are essential for this work to reach its full potential.
