This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks amenable to global optimization via a nonlinear spectral method, achieving a linear convergence rate. The method is applied to networks with one and two hidden layers and evaluated on several real-world datasets. While the theoretical result of globally optimal training is appealing, the practical implications are significantly limited by restrictive assumptions.

The core limitation lies in the necessary conditions for global optimality. The authors impose non-negativity constraints on network weights and maximize their regularization, conditions that are not intuitively justified and significantly constrain the model class. Furthermore, the requirement of using generalized polynomial activation functions—a relatively uncommon choice—further limits applicability. While the authors argue that this constrained class retains sufficient expressive power, the empirical evidence presented does not strongly support this claim. A more compelling justification, grounded in applications or the characteristics of real-world datasets, would strengthen the motivation for these restrictive assumptions.

The experimental comparison to linear SVMs is also problematic. Given that the proposed Nonlinear Spectral Method (NLSM) is a nonlinear model, a comparison to a nonlinear SVM (e.g., with polynomial or Gaussian kernels) would be more appropriate and fair. The presented results show linear SVMs performing favorably even with seemingly suboptimal tuning, while the NLSM models benefit from network structure optimization. This makes it difficult to attribute performance differences to the intrinsic qualities of the NLSM method and calls into question the claim of sufficient expressive power. The relatively small size of the datasets used from the UCI repository, both in terms of sample size and feature dimensionality, further exacerbates this issue.

A significant scalability concern arises from the analysis indicating that bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix *A* increase with the number of hidden units. This poses a potential obstacle to applying the method to high-dimensional data, restricting its applicability to a large proportion of practical problems. Addressing this scalability issue, perhaps through techniques for spectral radius control or approximation, would significantly broaden the potential impact of this work.

The paper also lacks discussion of the practical challenges in verifying the optimality condition. While the authors note that the condition can be checked without full network training, this verification becomes computationally intractable if the parameter space is large or lacks prior structure. Is there a fast, efficient method for identifying a small set of potentially valid parameter configurations? Furthermore, the origin of the global optimality—whether it stems from the specific objective function employed—deserves further investigation.

Finally, exploring the empirical behavior of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) on this model class would be valuable. It would be interesting to determine if SGD can also converge to a global optimum, even without the guarantees of the nonlinear spectral method, on the tested datasets. This could shed light on the practical advantages of the proposed approach compared to more standard training techniques.

In summary, while the theoretical contribution of demonstrating global optimality is noteworthy, the restrictive assumptions, questionable experimental comparisons, and scalability concerns significantly limit the practical value of this work. Substantial revisions addressing these points are necessary before this paper could be considered for acceptance.
