This paper develops a compelling theory showing that a certain class of neural networks admits a global optimum that can be reached at a linear convergence rate. The main constraints are that all input data and linear-layer weights are positive, and the activation functions are generalized polynomials. The theory is first established for a single-hidden-layer network and then extended to networks with multiple hidden layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments indicate that the algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable performance on small real-world datasets (UCI).

Pros: As the authors claim, this appears to be the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees. Prior work with convergence guarantees typically requires complicated checks of preconditions—sometimes impossible to verify in practice because they depend on the data-generating measure. By contrast, the preconditions here are comparatively simple: they depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix constructed from the network parameters. The authors also demonstrate that the theory applies to networks of arbitrary depth and show reasonable results on small real datasets.

Cons: The proposed network still has limited applicability. The theory requires positive input data and network parameters, generalized polynomial activations, and a spectral radius (Theorem 1) strictly less than 1. The nonnegativity constraint on inputs may not be a major obstacle, since most training data can be shifted to be positive. However, constraining network parameters to be positive could limit the model’s expressiveness. In addition, requiring all hidden layers to use different activation functions is somewhat unnatural and may further restrict applicability. Finally, the authors state that to satisfy the spectral-radius constraint they must increase p1 and p2 and decrease pw and pu, which effectively reduces the weights of the linear units. If I understand correctly, as the input dimension and the number of parameters grow, the spectral-radius constraint forces the linear weights to become smaller. When the linear weights are very small, the activations behave almost linearly; in that regime, adding more layers amounts to stacking linear functions, which does not increase expressiveness. Thus, I am a bit concerned that this approach may not extend easily to deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, this is an encouraging and important first step, and I believe the paper should be accepted.

Minor comments:
- I am curious how p1, p2, pw, pu, the input dimension, and the size of the network parameters affect the spectral radius ρ(A). For example, as the input dimension increases, does ρ(A) grow linearly or exponentially, and to counteract that, should pw and pu be decreased linearly or exponentially?
- While a proof may be difficult, it might be feasible to empirically plot this relationship on synthetic data.
