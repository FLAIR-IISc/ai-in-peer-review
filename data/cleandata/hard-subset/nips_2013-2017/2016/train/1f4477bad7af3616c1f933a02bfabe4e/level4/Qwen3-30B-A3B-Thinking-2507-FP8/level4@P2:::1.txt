

This paper presents a novel approach for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors introduce a nonlinear spectral method that achieves linear convergence to the global optimum under relatively weak assumptions on the data (nonnegativity), which is satisfied for common data types like images and word frequencies. The work builds upon recent advances in global optimization for neural networks [8, 11] but addresses their practical limitations by providing a method that requires no hyperparameter tuning (unlike SGD variants) and offers explicit convergence guarantees through spectral radius analysis.

The paper's key contribution is demonstrating that a generalized polynomial neural network (with real-valued activation exponents and nonnegative weights) can be trained globally optimally via a fixed-point iteration. The authors prove that under appropriate conditions (specifically, when the spectral radius of a derived matrix is less than 1), the optimization problem has a unique global maximum that can be reached via their nonlinear spectral method with linear convergence. This represents a significant theoretical advance over prior work [8, 11], which either lacked practical applicability or required estimating unknown data-generating measures.

The paper excels in technical quality, providing rigorous proofs that establish the convergence properties and global optimality guarantees. The authors carefully develop the necessary fixed-point theory framework, which is well-motivated by Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems. The clarity of presentation is generally good, with the main theoretical results well-organized and the proofs (though complex) logically structured. The supplementary material appropriately contains some intermediate results that would disrupt the main flow.

The work demonstrates strong originality by introducing a non-standard neural network architecture (with nonnegative weights and a modified objective function) that enables global optimality. This approach differs substantially from prior methods that either lack practical applicability [8] or require complex tensor computations [11]. The paper adequately references related work, including recent NIPS contributions on global optimization of neural networks.

Regarding significance, the method provides a theoretically grounded alternative to stochastic gradient descent for a specific class of networks, with the potential to eliminate the need for hyperparameter tuning in certain applications. The experiments confirm the approach's viability on several UCI datasets, achieving competitive performance compared to ReLU networks and SVMs. Notably, the authors demonstrate global optimality guarantees for all their experiments through spectral radius verification, which is a significant advantage over standard training methods.

However, the work has several limitations that warrant consideration. The nonnegativity constraint on weights is quite restrictive, potentially limiting the expressiveness of the model. While the authors acknowledge this in their discussion, they do not provide sufficient evidence that their constrained model can capture complex decision boundaries comparable to unconstrained networks. The experimental results show only modest performance improvements over baselines, with the proposed method outperforming linear SVMs on just two out of seven datasets. The parameter tuning process (involving over 100 random parameter combinations per dataset) also seems cumbersome for practical applications.

The spectral radius condition creates a practical barrier to scaling to deeper networks or larger hidden layers, as the authors explicitly note. While they justify their focus on 1-2 hidden layers, this limitation significantly restricts the method's applicability to the most challenging deep learning problems. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how the spectral radius condition translates to practical implementation constraints and whether these bounds can be tightened.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of global optimization in neural networks, but the practical impact is somewhat limited by the architectural constraints and experimental scope. The method represents a promising step toward theoretically grounded training procedures, but its real-world utility would be strengthened by demonstrating performance on more complex datasets or architectures.

In summary, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the problem of global optimization in neural networks with a novel fixed-point approach. The technical quality is excellent, and the method is clearly described. The main limitations are the restrictive weight constraints and limited experimental validation on more challenging datasets. The work is sufficiently original and significant for NIPS, though the practical applicability could be strengthened with further experimentation.

Pro: Novel theoretical approach to global optimization, rigorous proofs, clear presentation of fixed-point framework, linear convergence guarantees, practical implementation with spectral radius verification, competitive results on UCI datasets.

Con: Restrictive nonnegativity constraint on weights, limited experimental scope (low-dimensional datasets), parameter tuning complexity, spectral radius condition limits scalability, modest performance gains over baselines.
