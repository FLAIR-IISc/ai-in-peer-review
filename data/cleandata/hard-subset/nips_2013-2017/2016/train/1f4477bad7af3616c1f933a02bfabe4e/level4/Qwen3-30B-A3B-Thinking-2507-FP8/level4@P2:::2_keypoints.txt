- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that gives global optimality guarantees for a specific class of feedforward networks.  
- Theoretical framework leverages Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point analysis; key practical check is that a certain matrix spectral radius < 1.  
- Achieves linear convergence rates for one- and two-hidden-layer networks without careful hyperparameter tuning.  
- Rigorous, well-structured theoretical development with clear proofs.  
- Provides empirical validation on UCI datasets, showing competitive results in some cases.

- Assumptions (nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations) are highly restrictive and lack justification for real-world use.  
- Generalized polynomial activation is unusual and not well-motivated for common ML tasks.  
- Experimental comparisons are flawed: the method is compared to a linear SVM rather than appropriate nonlinear baselines (e.g., polynomial/RBF SVM).  
- Table 1 shows linear SVM outperforms NLSM on most datasets, calling the expressive power claim into question.  
- No explanation for dataset-specific performance differences (why NLSM wins on some datasets but not others).  
- Experiments use small, low-dimensional UCI datasets and do not demonstrate scalability to larger, complex problems.  
- Spectral-radius bounds grow with hidden units, suggesting potential impracticality for high-dimensional networks; no remedies or strategies given.  
- No guidance on how to efficiently find parameter settings that satisfy the global optimality condition.  
- Unclear whether the global optimality guarantees extend beyond the specific objective used to standard loss functions.  
- The claim of practical feasibility is not convincingly supported by the experimental evidence.
