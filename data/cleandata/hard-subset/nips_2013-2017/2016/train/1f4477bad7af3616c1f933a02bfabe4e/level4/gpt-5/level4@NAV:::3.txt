This paper develops a clear theoretical framework showing that a specific class of neural networks admits a global optimum that can be reached at a linear convergence rate. The key constraints are that all inputs and the linear-layer weights are positive, and the activation functions are generalized polynomials. The theory is first established for a single-hidden-layer network and then extended to networks with multiple hidden layers. The authors also introduce an optimization algorithm grounded in a nonlinear spectral method. Empirical results indicate strong performance on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable performance on several small real-world (UCI) datasets.

Pros: As the authors claim, this appears to be the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees. Prior results with convergence guarantees typically require intricate preconditions that are hard—or even impossible—to verify in practice because they depend on the data-generating measure. In contrast, the preconditions here are relatively simple, relying only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix constructed from the network parameters. The authors further show that the theory extends to networks of arbitrary depth and provide reasonable results on small real datasets.

Cons: The model’s applicability is still limited. The theory requires nonnegative inputs and parameters, generalized polynomial activations, and a spectral radius (Theorem 1) strictly less than 1. While the nonnegativity requirement on inputs may be manageable by shifting data, the constraint that all network parameters be positive could restrict expressiveness. Additionally, imposing distinct activation functions across all hidden layers feels unnatural and may further limit usability. Finally, to satisfy the spectral-radius constraint, the authors indicate increasing p1 and p2 while decreasing pw and pu, effectively shrinking the weights of linear units. If my understanding is correct, higher input dimensionality and larger parameter sizes would necessitate even smaller linear weights. When linear weights become very small, the activations behave nearly linearly; stacking more layers then amounts to composing near-linear functions, which does not enhance expressiveness. This raises concerns about extending the approach to deep architectures. Nevertheless, this is an encouraging first step, and I recommend acceptance.

Minor comments: I am curious about how p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimensionality, and parameter size influence the spectral radius ρ(A). For instance, as input dimension grows, does ρ(A) increase linearly or exponentially? Correspondingly, should pw and pu be reduced linearly or exponentially to compensate? While proving this may be challenging, it could be feasible to empirically chart these relationships on synthetic data.
