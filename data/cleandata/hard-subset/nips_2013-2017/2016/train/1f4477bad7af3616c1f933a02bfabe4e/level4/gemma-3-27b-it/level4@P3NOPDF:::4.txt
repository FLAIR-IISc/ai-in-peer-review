## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - Please Fill In]

This paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for optimally training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, guaranteeing a linear convergence rate. The core idea – leveraging spectral properties to achieve global optimality – is interesting and represents a potentially valuable contribution. The key condition for global optimality is elegantly expressed as the computation of the spectral radius of a small, nonnegative matrix, dependent on the network’s architecture. This provides a clear and theoretically grounded approach to training.

The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical result: a provably optimal training algorithm for the defined class of networks. This is a significant undertaking, and the authors should be commended for achieving a linear convergence guarantee. The connection between spectral analysis and neural network optimization is compelling and offers a different perspective compared to traditional gradient-based methods. 

However, I have several concerns regarding the practical applicability and generality of the proposed method. The most significant limitation is the requirement for nonnegative weights throughout the network. While the authors acknowledge this constraint, its impact appears substantial. Non-negativity is not a standard practice in neural network design, and imposing it could significantly restrict the representational capacity of the network and ultimately hinder performance on complex datasets. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of *why* non-negativity is crucial for the theoretical guarantees and a consideration of potential workarounds or modifications to relax this constraint.

Furthermore, the paper relies on non-standard activation functions. While the choice of activation function is a design decision, the very specific functions used here appear highly tailored to the spectral method and may not generalize well to other architectures or tasks. A stronger justification for these specific choices, alongside a comparison to more common activation functions (ReLU, sigmoid, tanh), would strengthen the paper. It’s unclear if the observed performance is a result of the optimal training procedure or a consequence of the specific network architecture and activation functions used.

The experimental results presented do not fully mitigate my concerns. The comparison to Support Vector Machines (SVMs) shows performance that is, at best, comparable. Given the restrictive constraints imposed by the method (non-negativity and specialized activation functions), outperforming a well-established algorithm like SVM on a standard benchmark seems a reasonable expectation. The authors should provide a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons against standard, similarly-sized feedforward neural networks trained with established methods (e.g., SGD with momentum, Adam). Additionally, exploring the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameters and network architectures would be beneficial.

Regarding a minor point, the claim in lines 90-91 – “Note that the nonlinear spectral method has a linear convergence rate and thus converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to…” – is potentially misleading. A linear convergence rate, while desirable, does *not* automatically guarantee fast convergence in terms of absolute iteration count. The rate constant plays a crucial role, and a small rate constant could still require a significant number of iterations to reach a desired level of accuracy. It would be more accurate to state that the method exhibits linear convergence, without implying a specific iteration count.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to training a constrained class of feedforward neural networks. The linear convergence guarantee is a noteworthy contribution. However, the significant limitations – the non-negativity constraint and the use of specialized activation functions – raise serious concerns about the practical applicability and generalizability of the method. The experimental results do not sufficiently demonstrate a clear advantage over existing techniques. 

**Recommendation:**

I currently lean towards **reject**, but would be open to reconsidering with a significantly revised version that addresses the concerns outlined above. Specifically, the authors should:

*   Provide a deeper discussion of the necessity of the non-negativity constraint and explore potential avenues for relaxation.
*   Justify the choice of non-standard activation functions and compare performance to more common alternatives.
*   Present a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons to standard neural network training methods and a sensitivity analysis.
*   Moderate the claim regarding the speed of convergence based on the linear convergence rate.
