- Novel approach: trains generalized polynomial feedforward networks with non-negative weights to guarantee global optimality.  
- Uses a nonlinear spectral method with linear convergence rate.  
- Addresses the fundamental challenge of non-convex optimization in deep learning.  
- Mathematically rigorous, building on nonlinear spectral theory and Perron-Frobenius.  
- Paper provides clear theorems, lemmas, and detailed proof strategy supporting convergence.  
- Includes a two-hidden-layer extension (Theorem 5), suggesting broader applicability.  
- Experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets but show feasibility and competitive performance vs SVM and ReLU networks.  
- Major limitation: strong requirement of non-negative data and weights restricts practical applicability.  
- Objective choice (maximizing sum of outputs / 1-norm) feels ad-hoc and motivated mainly by proof needs rather than intuition.  
- Relationship between spectral-radius conditions for global optimality and practical network design is insufficiently explained.  
- Chosen architecture appears driven by mathematical convenience, raising concerns about generalizability.  
- Transition from first-order to second-order derivatives (Theorems 3→4) is needed to prove Lipschitz continuity of GΦ for Banach fixed-point application.  
- Representation of 'u' as a matrix relates to vectorization in gradient calculations, which is implicit in the notation.  
- Maximization of the 1-norm follows from enforcing positivity and ties to Perron-Frobenius properties, contrasting with typical regularization.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty in achieving global optimality, strong theoretical rigor, and potential research impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: demonstrated feasibility through experiments despite limited scale.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive non-negativity assumptions and narrow architectural scope.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear practical benefits compared to more flexible methods like ReLU-based training.  
- Arguments against acceptance: ad-hoc components of the objective need clearer justification.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound and theoretically interesting; reviewer leans weak accept contingent on addressing practical relevance and objective motivation, and suggests future work to relax constraints and test larger architectures/datasets.
