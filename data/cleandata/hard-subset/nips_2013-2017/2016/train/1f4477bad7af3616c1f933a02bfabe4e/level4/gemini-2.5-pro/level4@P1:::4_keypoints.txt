- Introduces a "nonlinear spectral method" with a provable linear-rate convergence to the global optimum for a specific class of feedforward networks.  
- Method is derived from nonlinear eigenproblem/Perron-Frobenius theory on convex cones.  
- Requires non-negative weights and inputs, generalized polynomial activations, and optimizes a modified objective.  
- Global-optimality condition is verifiable a priori via the spectral radius of a small, explicit matrix.  
- Theoretical analysis covers networks with one and two hidden layers; experiments run on several UCI datasets.

Strengths:
- Novel connection between neural-network training and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory; significant theoretical departure from SGD.  
- Rigorous convergence proof using Banach fixed-point theorem in the Thompson metric; conditions are clearly stated and checkable.  
- Algorithm is implementable, distribution-free, and has no learning-rate hyperparameters.

Weaknesses:
- Strong constraints (non-negative weights) likely reduce expressive power compared to standard networks.  
- Uses non-standard generalized polynomial activations; common activations like ReLU are incompatible.  
- Optimizes a modified objective (negative loss plus sum of outputs); alignment with generalization/classification performance is unclear.  
- Empirically underperforms RBF-SVM and is only comparable to or slightly better than ReLU networks on some datasets, suggesting a trade-off between guarantees and practical performance.

Minor comments:
- Claim of "typically <10 iterations" is optimistic; convergence rate depends on spectral radius ρ(A), which may be close to 1.  
- ρ(A) grows with hidden units, making ρ(A)<1 harder for larger networks; theoretical bounds are conservative and tightening them would help scalability.
