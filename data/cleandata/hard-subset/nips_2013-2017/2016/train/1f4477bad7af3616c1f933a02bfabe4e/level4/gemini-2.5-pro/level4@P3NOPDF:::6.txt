This paper proposes a training method for a specific class of feed-forward neural networks and, under certain assumptions, provides a proof of its optimal convergence. The authors demonstrate the method's application on a selection of real-world problems. The problem under investigation is interesting, and theoretical advances in understanding the training dynamics of neural networks are highly valuable to the community. However, the paper falls short in demonstrating the practical utility and scope of the proposed network class, and the experimental evaluation could be significantly strengthened.

**Strengths**

*   The problem of developing training algorithms for neural networks with provable guarantees is both important and challenging. The paper makes a welcome theoretical contribution in this direction.
*   The introduction is well-written, clearly motivating the problem and outlining the paper's approach.
*   The core theoretical result, proving optimal convergence for a restricted network architecture, is a valuable step forward.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Discussion on Expressiveness and Practicality:** The primary concern is the limited discussion on the practical scope of the proposed restricted model. The authors correctly raise the issue of their model's expressiveness twice but do not provide a satisfactory analysis. To be convincing, a paper introducing a restricted model with theoretical guarantees must also characterize its capabilities. The paper would be much stronger with a thorough discussion of what function classes this model can and cannot represent. This would help readers understand the trade-off between theoretical guarantees and practical applicability.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are not sufficient to demonstrate the method's advantages or clarify its limitations.
    *   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison is restricted to Support Vector Machines. This is insufficient to position the work within the broader context of neural networks and machine learning. It is essential to compare against other relevant methods, including (a) standard gradient-based training (e.g., SGD, Adam) for similarly structured neural networks to highlight the benefits of the proposed trainer, and (b) other algorithmic paradigms that are competitive on the selected tasks.
    *   **Computational Resources:** The evaluation lacks any information about the computational cost of the proposed algorithm. To assess its practical viability, details on training time, memory requirements, or convergence in terms of wall-clock time are needed for all compared methods.
    *   **Exploring Limitations:** The experiments seem to be chosen to show where the method works, but they do not explore its boundaries. A more insightful evaluation would also help clarify the practical implications of the model's limited expressiveness.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** While the introduction is clear, the presentation quality declines in subsequent sections.
    *   Section 2, in particular, lacks fluency and is difficult to follow.
    *   The presentation of key theoretical concepts could be improved. For example, Theorem 1 is central to the paper but is very dense and hard to parse. Preceding the formal statement with an informal, high-level explanation of its meaning and implications would greatly improve its accessibility.

**Specific Questions and Suggestions for Clarification**

*   **Motivation for the Loss Function:** The definition of the loss function, specifically the function $\Phi$, lacks motivation. The rebuttal explained the role of the epsilon term, which is a crucial detail for understanding the formulation. This explanation should be incorporated into the main paper.
*   **Convergence Claim:** The paper states that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum." As clarified in the rebuttal, this is an empirical observation from the authors' experiments. The text should be revised to present it as an empirical finding rather than a general property, for example, by stating "in our experiments, we observed that the nonlinear spectral method..."
