- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method for training "Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks" with a provably convergent algorithm finding a unique global optimum at a linear rate.
- Positions the work among recent theoretical global-optimality results and demonstrates applicability on real datasets.
- Theoretical guarantees require non-negativity of data and weights and a checkable spectral-radius condition based on network architecture.
- Theory developed for one- and two-hidden-layer networks.
- Originality/significance: first practically feasible algorithm for a non-trivial architecture with a unique global optimum guarantee and linear convergence.
- Theoretical soundness: elegant framework using Banach fixed-point, Thompson metric, and nonlinear spectral methods with thorough derivations.
- Algorithmic simplicity: iterative mapping requires no learning-rate tuning, a practical advantage over gradient methods.
- Clarity issue: link between general framework and neural-network application needs clearer exposition, particularly how Lemma 4/Theorem 3 conditions yield Theorem 4 bounds.
- Notation ambiguity: gradients w.r.t. matrix parameters (e.g., ∇uΦ) and associated norm definitions are unclear and should be clarified.
- Objective design concerns: objective’s +sum(w) + sum(u) terms encourage large L1 norms counter to usual regularization, and the small ε term seems a technical proof device lacking ML motivation.
- Practicality limits: strict positivity constraint on data and weights restricts applicability (e.g., excludes inhibitory connections) to certain data types like counts.
- "No free parameters" claim is overstated: architectural choices (n1, α, ρw, ρu) act as hyperparameters tuned in experiments.
- Experimental comparison: experiments are a proof of concept but need stronger comparison/analysis versus standard ReLU networks and explanation of observed performance gaps.
