The paper introduces a specific type of feedforward neural network for which a unique global optimizer can be obtained, and subsequently defines an algorithm that achieves this global optimizer with a linear convergence rate. Although the mathematical derivation is instructive, several aspects require clarification. Firstly, the transition from first-order derivatives in Theorem 3 to second-order derivatives in Theorem 4 is not adequately explained. Secondly, the network design appears to treat the last layer separately from the hidden layers, which raises questions about the computation of the gradient with respect to the matrix u in equation (3). Specifically, is the gradient with respect to u simply the gradient of the vectorized u? Similar concerns arise in other parts of the paper, such as the bounding of u within a ball. Furthermore, the need to maximize the 1-norm of the connection parameters in equation (2) is not well-justified, as most neural network models employ weight decay to reduce the 1-norm. The authors' explanation, which relies on the introduction of an arbitrarily small epsilon to ensure a strictly positive gradient, seems to be a technical requirement for the proof rather than a practically motivated design choice. This raises concerns about the absence of free parameters in the model. Additionally, the requirement that all data be non-negative and the use of all positive weights may limit the practical applicability of this design. The experimental comparison with SVM is not entirely convincing, as SVM is not typically used for the tasks evaluated in the paper. A more relevant comparison would be with commonly used neural network architectures, such as those employing ReLU activations, to demonstrate the potential benefits of the proposed approach.
