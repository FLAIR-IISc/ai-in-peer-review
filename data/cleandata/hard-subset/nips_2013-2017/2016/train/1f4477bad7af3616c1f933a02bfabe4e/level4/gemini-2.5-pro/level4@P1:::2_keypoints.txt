- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method that provably converges linearly to a global optimum for a class of feedforward nets with generalized polynomial activations and non-negative weights.  
- Theoretical contribution: rigorous analysis using nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory yielding a spectral-radius condition guaranteeing global optimality.  
- Practical advantage: optimality condition can be checked before training and the method lacks optimization hyperparameters like learning rates.  
- Restrictive assumptions: requires non-negative weights/data, uncommon activation functions, and an objective modification that maximizes the sum of weights.  
- Weak motivation: the paper fails to justify why maximizing the sum of weights is a realistic or useful modeling choice.  
- Unconvincing empirical results: NLSM models are often outperformed by RBF-SVM and standard ReLU networks across UCI datasets.  
- Missing baseline experiment: authors should compare to SGD (or batch methods) optimizing the exact same objective to isolate algorithmic vs. objective-design benefits.  
- Scalability concerns: theoretical spectral-radius bounds worsen with more hidden units, casting doubt on applicability to larger, higher-dimensional problems.  
- Practicality issues: no principled or efficient method is provided to choose architectural/hyperparameter settings that ensure ρ(A) < 1, current approach relies on expensive random search.  
- Overall: novel and rigorous but limited practical impact without stronger application motivation, better empirical evidence, and strategies to address scalability and hyperparameter selection.
