This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks that are provably trainable to global optimality with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The method is applied to deep networks with one and two hidden layers, and evaluated on a series of real-world datasets. However, the assumptions required to guarantee these theoretical properties are restrictive and, in some respects, counterintuitive. The approach imposes non-negativity constraints on network weights and maximizes their regularization, while also requiring the use of the relatively uncommon generalized polynomial activation function to satisfy the optimality condition. While the authors claim the targeted class of networks retains sufficient expressive power for complex decision boundaries and achieves good performance, the empirical results presented do not strongly support this claim.

Specifically, the experimental comparisons appear unfavorable. Linear SVMs achieve superior performance to NLSM1 and NLSM2, even in cases where the linear SVM appears suboptimally tuned, while the NLSM models benefit from network structure optimization. Furthermore, given that NLSM is a nonlinear method, the use of a linear SVM as a primary baseline seems questionable; a comparison against nonlinear SVMs employing polynomial or Gaussian kernels would be more appropriate and provide a fairer assessment. Consequently, the conclusions drawn regarding the expressive power of the studied network class are not convincing. 

The datasets used, sourced from the UCI repository, are also limited in both sample size and feature dimensionality. The analysis reveals that bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A scale with the number of hidden units, which presents a significant challenge for high-dimensional data and potentially limits the applicability of the method to a large proportion of practical applications. 

Several further questions arise. Is the optimality condition guaranteed for any realistically chosen parameter settings within the studied networks? While the condition *can* be checked without training, its practicality is questionable without strong priors or when the parameter space is extremely large. Could a faster approach be developed to identify a manageable set of valid parameter candidates? Additionally, does the global optimality stem primarily from the specific objective function used? Finally, it would be interesting to investigate whether stochastic gradient descent can empirically converge to a global optimum on the tested datasets.
