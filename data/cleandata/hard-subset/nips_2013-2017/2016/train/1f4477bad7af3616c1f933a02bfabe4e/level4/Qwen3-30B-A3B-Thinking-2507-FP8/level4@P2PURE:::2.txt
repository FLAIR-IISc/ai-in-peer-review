

This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks that achieve global optimality with linear convergence rates via a nonlinear spectral method. The approach is applied to deep networks with one or two hidden layers, and experiments are conducted on multiple real-world datasets. As acknowledged by the authors, the studied network class imposes restrictive and counterintuitive constraints: non-negativity on weights and maximization of weight regularization. Additionally, the generalized polynomial activation function is required for the optimality condition. These assumptions lack strong justification. The authors should provide detailed, application-oriented explanations rather than further restricting the model to achieve global optimality. While the authors claim the targeted networks possess sufficient expressive power for complex decision boundaries and achieve strong performance, the empirical results do not robustly support this. Notably, linear SVM outperforms NLSM1 and NLSM2 in scenarios where linear SVM appears inadequately tuned (while NLSM tunes its network structure), and the use of linear SVM as a baseline for a nonlinear model (NLSM) is inappropriate; a nonlinear SVM (e.g., with polynomial or Gaussian kernels) would be a fairer comparison. Consequently, the experimental comparisons are unfair, and the conclusions regarding expressive power lack convincing evidence. The UCI repository datasets used are too small in both sample size and feature dimensionality. Given that the spectral radius bounds for matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, this poses a significant issue for high-dimensional data. The authors should address how this limitation can be mitigated, as it would severely restrict the method's applicability to most real-world problems. Furthermore, the optimal condition may not be guaranteed under any practical parameter settings. While the condition can be checked without training, its validity remains problematic without prior knowledge or if the parameter space is excessively large. The authors should discuss efficient methods to identify a small set of valid parameter candidates. Finally, it is crucial to determine whether the global optimality stems from the specific objective function. Empirical investigation of whether stochastic gradient descent converges to the global optimum on tested datasets would be valuable.
