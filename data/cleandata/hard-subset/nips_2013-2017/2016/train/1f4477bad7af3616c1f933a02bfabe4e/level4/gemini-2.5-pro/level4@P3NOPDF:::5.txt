This paper introduces a novel class of feedforward neural networks for which the existence of a unique global optimizer is proven. The authors propose a corresponding algorithm that is guaranteed to find this global optimum with a linear convergence rate. The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical contribution. Establishing the existence and uniqueness of a global optimum for a non-trivial neural network architecture is a significant and challenging problem, and the mathematical derivations provided are instructive.

Despite the interesting theoretical results, there are several points that require clarification and major concerns regarding the practical motivation and applicability of the proposed model.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Model Design and Motivation:** A significant concern is the motivation behind maximizing the L1-norm of the hidden layer weights (`u`) as formulated in equation (2). This is in stark contrast to conventional regularization techniques like weight decay, which aim to *minimize* weight norms to prevent overfitting and improve generalization. The paper seems to imply this maximization is necessary to ensure the gradient lies within the positive cone (V_++), which is a critical condition for the convergence proof. However, this appears to be a mathematical convenience for the theoretical analysis rather than a well-justified modeling choice for practical neural networks. This raises questions about whether the model is designed to solve a practical problem or tailored specifically to fit the requirements of the proof.

2.  **Practicality and Constraints:** The model's applicability appears severely limited by the constraint that all input data must be non-negative and, consequently, that all weights are positive. This design choice, where the network essentially keeps accumulating positive values, restricts its use to a narrow set of domains. For many real-world datasets, enforcing non-negativity would require data transformations that might discard important information (e.g., by taking the absolute value of features). The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of these constraints and the types of problems where such a model would be a natural fit.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental comparison with SVM, while logical from the perspective of comparing models with guaranteed global optima, may not be the most informative benchmark. The key question for practitioners is what advantages this specialized network offers over more standard, widely-used neural network architectures (e.g., networks with ReLU activations), even if those models lack the same theoretical guarantees. A comparison against such models, even on datasets that fit the non-negativity constraint, would be crucial to demonstrate the practical value and trade-offs of the proposed approach in terms of performance, generalization, and computational cost.

**Questions for Clarification:**

1.  In the theoretical development, could the authors elaborate on the transition from the first-order derivative conditions in the inequalities of Theorem 3 to the second-order derivative analysis in Theorem 4? The link between these two steps was not immediately apparent.

2.  The treatment of the weight matrix `u` could be clarified. For instance, in equation (3), is the gradient with respect to `u` computed with respect to its vectorized form? Similar ambiguity arises in other operations, such as bounding `u` within a ball.

3.  The model architecture appears to treat the parameters of the final layer separately from the hidden layer weights `u`. Could the authors provide a brief rationale for this design choice?

In summary, while the paper presents a theoretically elegant result by defining a class of NNs with a provably unique global optimum, its practical relevance is questionable due to restrictive constraints and unconventional modeling choices. The manuscript would be significantly strengthened by better justifying these choices from a practical standpoint and by providing a more comprehensive experimental comparison against standard neural network models.
