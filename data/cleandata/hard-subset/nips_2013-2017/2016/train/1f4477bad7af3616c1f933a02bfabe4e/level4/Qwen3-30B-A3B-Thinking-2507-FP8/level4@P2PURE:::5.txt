

The paper introduces a specialized class of feedforward neural networks that admit a unique global optimizer, along with an algorithm to compute this optimizer achieving linear convergence. The mathematical derivations are generally instructive, though some aspects require clarification:  
1. Theorem 3 computes first-order derivatives (as shown in the inequalities), while Theorem 4 proceeds to second-order derivatives. What motivates this shift from first-order to second-order analysis between these theorems?  
2. The network design treats the final layer distinctly from hidden layers. Since \( u \) is a matrix parameterizing connections to hidden layers, the gradient with respect to \( u \) in \( \mathcal{G}^\Phi \) (Equation 3) appears to be derived from the vectorized form of \( u \). Similar ambiguities arise elsewhere (e.g., bounding \( u \) within a ball).  

A key concern: Why does the model maximize the 1-norm of connection parameters in Equation (2)? In standard neural networks, weight decay typically minimizes 1-norms. Here, the arbitrarily small \( \epsilon \) in Equation (2) appears solely to ensure the gradient is strictly positive (i.e., in \( \mathcal{V}_{++} \)), which seems tailored for the proof rather than justifying practical neural network design. Is there a compelling reason this lacks free parameters? The requirement that all data be non-negative, coupled with the use of strictly positive weights (no zeros), implies the model perpetually accumulates contributions. How practical is this design constraint?  

The experiments compare the proposed network to SVM (presumably due to SVM's global optimizer property). However, does this network offer tangible advantages over commonly used architectures (e.g., ReLU-based networks) in practice? The comparison does not sufficiently address whether the theoretical properties translate to meaningful empirical gains.
