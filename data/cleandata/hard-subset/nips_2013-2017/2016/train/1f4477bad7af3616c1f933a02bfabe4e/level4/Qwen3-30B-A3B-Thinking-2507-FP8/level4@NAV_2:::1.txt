

This paper presents a novel approach for globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors demonstrate that under certain conditions (nonnegative weights and data, and specific activation functions), their method achieves global optimality with linear convergence rates, providing a theoretically grounded alternative to stochastic gradient descent. This work relates to recent NIPS contributions on global optimality in neural networks [8, 11], but distinguishes itself by offering a practically feasible algorithm where previous approaches either required impractical assumptions (e.g., knowledge of data-generating measures in [11]) or faced implementation challenges (as acknowledged in [8]). The key innovation lies in reducing the global optimality guarantee to checking the spectral radius of a small matrix, which can be verified without running the algorithm.

Strengths: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a globally optimal training method with linear convergence rates for a non-trivial neural network architecture. The proof framework using fixed-point theory and the Thompson metric is well-constructed and technically sound, with clear connections to Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis of the convergence conditions, including explicit bounds for the spectral radius. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on UCI datasets, and the method's lack of hyperparameters (compared to SGD) is a practical advantage. The paper is well-organized, with clear notation and logical progression from theoretical foundations to applications.

Weaknesses: The strong constraint of nonnegative weights significantly limits the applicability of the model, as the authors acknowledge, and the method's performance is only marginally better than standard baselines on most datasets (outperforming linear SVM on only 2 out of 7 datasets). The method's dependence on the spectral radius condition creates a practical barrier to scaling to larger networks, as the authors themselves note they restricted to low-dimensional datasets. The parameter tuning process is complex, requiring verification of the spectral condition for each parameter combination (as evidenced by the authors testing approximately 150,000 parameter combinations). The experimental section lacks thorough exploration of parameter sensitivity or ablation studies to understand why the method performs better on some datasets but not others.

Pro arguments for acceptance: 
- First practically feasible globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks with theoretical guarantees
- Linear convergence rate and no need for learning rate tuning
- Clear theoretical contribution with connections to established fixed-point theory
- Competitive performance on benchmark datasets despite constraints

Con arguments for acceptance:
- Strong nonnegativity constraints limit practical applicability
- Performance gains over baselines are marginal on most datasets
- Parameter tuning is complex and requires verification of spectral conditions
- Limited to low-dimensional datasets due to theoretical bounds on spectral radius

The paper is technically sound (Quality) but the constraints and limited empirical results question its significance (Significance) for broader machine learning practice. The clarity (Clarity) is high, with well-structured arguments, though the technical depth may limit accessibility. The originality (Originality) is strong in the theoretical approach, but the specific model constraints reduce its novelty as a general neural network framework. While the paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to the understanding of global optimization in neural networks, the practical limitations and modest empirical gains suggest it may not be sufficiently impactful for a top-tier conference like NIPS.
