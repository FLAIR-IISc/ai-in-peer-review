- Paper focuses on a specific feedforward NN class trained to global optimality via the nonlinear spectral method, applied to 1–2 hidden layers and real datasets.  
- The studied class is restrictive: non-negativity constraints, heavy weight regularization, and use of an uncommon generalized polynomial activation.  
- These restrictive assumptions are often unreasonable and lack justification for practical applications.  
- Authors claim sufficient expressive power, but empirical results do not convincingly support this claim.  
- Linear SVM sometimes outperforms NLSM methods, undermining the claimed advantages.  
- Baseline selection is weak/unfair: a nonlinear SVM (polynomial or Gaussian kernel) should be used instead of only linear SVM.  
- Experiments use small UCI datasets with limited sample size and dimensionality, limiting conclusions about broader applicability.  
- The spectral radius bound grows with hidden units, raising scalability and high-dimensional applicability concerns.  
- The optimality condition may not be satisfiable by practical parameter settings; checking is possible but finding valid candidates is impractical without prior knowledge.  
- Open questions: whether the global-optimal property stems from the special objective and whether SGD can empirically reach the global optimum on these datasets.
