

This paper introduces a specialized class of feedforward neural networks that admit a unique global optimizer, accompanied by a linearly convergent algorithm for its computation. The mathematical derivation is generally sound and offers instructive insights into the optimization landscape of this specific architecture. However, several critical points require clarification and justification to strengthen the contribution and assess its practical relevance.

The core theoretical claim—that a unique global optimizer exists and can be found efficiently—relies on the network's structural constraints. A key point of confusion arises in the transition from Theorem 3 (first-order conditions) to Theorem 4 (second-order analysis). The authors do not explicitly justify why the analysis escalates to second derivatives at this stage. Is this necessary for establishing uniqueness, or does it stem from a technical gap in the first-order approach? Clarifying this progression is essential for the proof’s coherence.

The architecture also treats the final layer distinctly from hidden layers, raising questions about parameterization. Specifically, in Equation (3), the gradient of \( G^\Phi \) with respect to \( \mathbf{u} \) (a matrix parameterizing hidden-to-output connections) appears to be defined via vectorization. However, the notation and bounding of \( \mathbf{u} \) within a ball (e.g., in Section 4.2) lack justification. If \( \mathbf{u} \) is a matrix, should the constraint apply to its Frobenius norm, or is the vectorization implicit? Similar ambiguities arise in other derivations, suggesting potential inconsistencies in how parameters are handled.

The most significant concern pertains to Equation (2), which maximizes the 1-norm of connection weights. This directly contradicts standard weight decay practices in neural networks, where 1-norm minimization (or regularization) is used to induce sparsity and prevent overfitting. The authors posit that the arbitrarily small \( \epsilon > 0 \) in Equation (2) is merely to ensure strictly positive gradients (i.e., membership in \( \mathcal{V}_{++} \)). While this may serve a technical purpose in the proof, it fails to justify the *practical necessity* of maximizing weight magnitudes. Why would a real-world model *want* to maximize the 1-norm of parameters? The paper does not address how this aligns with established optimization principles or empirical success in neural networks. This constraint appears to be a theoretical artifact rather than a well-motivated design choice.

Furthermore, the requirement for *all* input data to be non-negative and for *all* weights to be strictly positive (preventing cancellation) severely limits applicability. In practice, neural networks often rely on weight cancellation (e.g., via negative weights in ReLU networks) to model complex interactions. The proposed model’s inability to utilize negative weights or handle mixed-sign data renders it infeasible for most real-world tasks without significant modification. The authors provide no discussion of how this constraint might be relaxed or why it is unavoidable.

The experimental comparison to SVMs is problematic. SVMs are not neural networks and possess global optimizers due to their convex formulation, but this comparison does not meaningfully contextualize the proposed method. The paper does not compare against standard neural network baselines (e.g., ReLU-based networks, which are ubiquitous and often achieve strong results). Without such a comparison, it remains unclear whether this specialized architecture offers *any* advantage over existing, more flexible neural network approaches. The claim that "SVM also has a global optimizer" is irrelevant to the neural network context and distracts from the core question: *Why should practitioners adopt this specific NN variant?*

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to a narrow class of networks, but the practical value is questionable. The requirement to maximize weight 1-norm, enforce non-negative data, and avoid negative weights are strong constraints that lack justification beyond the proof’s technical needs. The comparison to SVMs is misleading, and the absence of comparisons to standard neural network architectures undermines the paper’s relevance. The mathematical exposition would benefit from greater clarity on the transition between Theorem 3 and Theorem 4, and the parameterization of \( \mathbf{u} \). While the linear convergence rate is a notable technical result, the practical utility of the model hinges on addressing these gaps. Without stronger motivation for the constraints or empirical evidence of superiority over conventional neural networks, the contribution risks being seen as a theoretical curiosity rather than a useful advance. The authors should either justify the constraints more thoroughly or explore how the model might be adapted to broader settings.
