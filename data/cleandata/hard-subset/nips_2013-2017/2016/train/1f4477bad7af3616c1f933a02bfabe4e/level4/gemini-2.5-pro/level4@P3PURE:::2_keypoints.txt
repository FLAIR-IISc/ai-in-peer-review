- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method for specific one- or two-hidden-layer networks with a provable global optimum and linear convergence.
- Theoretical contribution is interesting but undermined by major weaknesses in assumptions and experiments.
- Model class is highly restrictive: requires non-negative weights, maximization of a weight regularizer, and a nonstandard "generalized polynomial" activation.
- These restrictive assumptions are counterintuitive and not well-justified for practical applications.
- Experimental comparisons are unfair: NLSM models are only compared against a linear SVM rather than strong nonlinear baselines (e.g., polynomial or RBF SVMs).
- Empirical results are unconvincing: linear SVM sometimes outperforms NLSMs despite apparently poorer tuning of the SVM baseline.
- Conclusions do not convincingly demonstrate the expressive power of the proposed network class.
- Scalability concerns: experiments use small UCI datasets and theoretical bounds (spectral radius of A) grow with hidden units, suggesting poor scaling to high-dimensional data.
- Question whether there is any way to mitigate the scalability issue arising from the spectral radius bounds.
- Practicality concern: the optimality condition depends heavily on parameter choices—no efficient method provided to find valid candidate parameters.
- Suggestion/request: empirically compare standard optimizers (e.g., SGD) on the same objective to see if they also reach the global optimum in practice.
