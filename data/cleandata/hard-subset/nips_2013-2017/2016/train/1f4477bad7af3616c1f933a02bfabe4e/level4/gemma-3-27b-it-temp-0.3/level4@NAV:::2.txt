This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks demonstrably trainable to global optimality with a linear convergence rate via a nonlinear spectral method. The method’s applicability is demonstrated on deep networks featuring one and two hidden layers, assessed through experiments on several real-world datasets. However, the restrictive nature of this network class is a significant concern. The imposed non-negativity constraints on weights, coupled with the maximization of weight regularization and the requirement of the generalized polynomial activation function, appear counterintuitive and limit practical applicability. Greater emphasis on application-driven justifications or analysis of real-world data would be more valuable than further restricting assumptions to achieve global optimality.

While the authors assert sufficient expressive power within this constrained network class, the empirical results do not strongly support this claim. The observed performance suggests linear SVMs, even with potentially suboptimal tuning, often outperform the proposed NLSM1 and NLSM2 models. Furthermore, the choice of a linear SVM as a baseline, rather than a nonlinear counterpart like polynomial or Gaussian kernel SVMs, introduces a potential bias and weakens the comparative analysis. This makes the conclusions regarding the expressive power of the studied networks unconvincing. 

The datasets utilized from the UCI repository are limited in both sample size and feature dimensionality. The analysis reveals that the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A increase with the number of hidden units, posing a challenge for high-dimensional data. Addressing this scalability issue is crucial for broader applicability. 

Finally, while the optimality condition is theoretically verifiable, its practical application is questionable given the potential for an extremely large parameter space and lack of prior information. Efficient methods for identifying a valid, limited set of candidate parameters would be beneficial. It would also be insightful to investigate whether the observed optimality stems from the specific objective function employed and to explore the empirical convergence behavior of stochastic gradient descent on these datasets.
