The paper proposes a method for training a specific type of feed-forward neural network under certain assumptions. The authors prove optimal convergence and demonstrate the applicability of their method to a selection of real-world problems. The problem under investigation is indeed interesting, and any theoretical advance in this direction is appreciated. However, the paper falls short in convincingly demonstrating the practical usefulness of the proposed class of neural networks.

In particular, since the authors raise the issue of the expressiveness of their restricted model (which they do twice), I would recommend adding some discussion on the capabilities and limitations of their approach. Furthermore, a comparison with concurrent approaches would be beneficial. The experimental results do not provide clarity on the limitations of the model's applicability either. Upon reviewing the experimental results, I was surprised to find that the authors only compare their approach to SVMs and neglect other methods, including alternative training methods for neural networks and other algorithmic paradigms. Additionally, I would have expected information on the computational resources required by the three algorithms.

The introduction is well-written, but the subsequent presentation has room for improvement. Specifically, Section 2 lacks fluency. There are several typos, which I have listed below. Some specific remarks are in order. While the definition of the loss function is straightforward, the function Φ lacks motivation. The role of the epsilon term was explained in the rebuttal, and I believe this should be added to the paper as well. Theorem 1 would benefit from an informal explanation before its formal statement, as the statement itself is difficult to parse. The claim that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum," presented immediately after Theorem 1, was explained in the rebuttal as stemming from experimental observations. This clarification should be included in the paper.

Some minor corrections are also necessary: "polyomial" should be corrected to "polynomial" (line 60), "one CAN model" should be rephrased for clarity (line 67), the variables p1 and p2 should be introduced before their use (line 67), and the notation R_{++} should be defined, especially since it is referenced in the definitions of other variables. I appreciate the authors' clarification of this notation in the rebuttal, as I was unfamiliar with it prior to reading their response.
