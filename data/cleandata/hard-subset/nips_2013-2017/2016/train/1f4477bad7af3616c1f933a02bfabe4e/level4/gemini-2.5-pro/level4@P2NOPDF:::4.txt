This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for optimally training a specific class of feedforward neural networks. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves a linear convergence rate and provide a condition, based on the spectral radius of a small non-negative matrix, that guarantees global optimality.

The proposal of a nonlinear spectral method for training neural networks is novel and theoretically interesting. The ability to establish a computable condition for global optimality in a non-convex optimization setting is a significant contribution, even if it applies to a restricted model class.

However, the practical applicability of this approach appears to be severely limited by several strong assumptions, which raises major concerns.

1.  **Non-Negativity Constraint:** The method imposes a non-negativity constraint on all network weights. This is a significant departure from standard neural network design, where both positive and negative weights are crucial for learning complex data representations. This restriction may severely curtail the model's expressive power and its ability to fit complex functions.

2.  **Specific Activation Functions:** The choice of activation functions is non-standard and highly specific to the proposed framework. It is unclear how these functions compare to more conventional activations (e.g., ReLU, sigmoid, tanh) or whether the theoretical guarantees of the method can be extended to these more widely used functions. This specificity limits the generalizability and relevance of the work to the broader neural network community.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** The strong limitations mentioned above seem to negatively impact the model's performance in practice. The numerical experiments show that the proposed method is outperformed by a standard Support Vector Machine (SVM). While achieving a theoretically guaranteed optimum is valuable, its practical utility is questionable if the resulting "optimal" model underperforms well-established and simpler baselines on the selected tasks.

**Minor Comment:**

On lines 90-91, the claim that the method "converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations" because it has a linear convergence rate is an overstatement. A linear convergence rate does not necessarily imply convergence in a small, fixed number of iterations, as the convergence factor can be arbitrarily close to one. This statement should be rephrased more cautiously.
