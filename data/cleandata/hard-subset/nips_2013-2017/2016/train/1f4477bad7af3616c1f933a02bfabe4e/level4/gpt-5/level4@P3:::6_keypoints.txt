- Paper proposes a nonlinear spectral fixed-point method yielding global optimality for feedforward nets with generalized polynomial activations, nonnegative inputs/weights, and a modified objective.  
- Convergence (unique global optimum) holds under a verifiable spectral-radius condition of a small nonnegative matrix; convergence is linear.  
- Analysis uses nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, multi-homogeneous maps, the Thompson metric, and contraction mapping arguments.  
- Results presented for one hidden layer and extended to two; deeper networks sketched conceptually.  
- Compared favorably to prior global-training work by avoiding generative-density or tensor-decomposition assumptions, requiring only nonnegativity and a spectral check.  
- Strength: clear theoretical contribution of global optimality with explicit linear convergence rate for a nontrivial constrained class.  
- Strength: convergence conditions are explicit and computable via the spectral radius of a small matrix.  
- Strength: principled fixed-point formulation linking critical points and fixed points, grounded in spectral theory.  
- Strength: extension to two hidden layers broadens applicability beyond simplest architectures.  
- Strength: empirical results show feasibility on real datasets, filling a gap in prior theoretical work.  
- Weakness: strong modeling constraints limit practical applicability (nonnegative data/weights, distinct exponents, modified objective).  
- Weakness: limited discussion of model expressiveness and which decision boundaries/functions are representable.  
- Weakness: key theorems (e.g., Theorem 1) are hard to parse without an informal summary; epsilon and output-sum lack main-text motivation.  
- Weakness: narrow experiments (small UCI sets), missing constrained baselines (e.g., projected gradient), and no runtime/memory analysis.  
- Weakness: spectral-radius bounds appear conservative; paper does not quantify tightness or guidance when ρ(A) ≥ 1.  
- Technical soundness: proofs and fixed-point theory are appropriate and carefully adapted to two layers; assumptions are clearly stated.  
- Empirical quality: convergence is fast but analyses of statistical performance, sensitivity to parameters, and scalability are limited.  
- Clarity: introduction is clear but Section 2 is dense; the objective Φ and notation need earlier, clearer explanation.  
- Originality: novel use of nonlinear spectral methods and multi-homogeneous contraction for global training guarantees at this architectural complexity.  
- Significance: potentially impactful theoretical result, but real-world significance is limited by constraints and sparse empirical validation.  
- Key suggested improvements: add an informal prelude to main theorems, move ε/output-sum explanation to main text, expand expressiveness discussion, and add constrained baselines and runtime comparisons.  
- Practical suggestions: discuss preprocessing for nonnegative requirement, behavior when ρ(A) ≥ 1, and provide adaptive strategies to enforce ρ(A) < 1.  
- Presentation suggestions: report per-iteration complexity, compute-time comparisons to SGD, and improve Section 2 readability with pseudocode/diagrams.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong novel theory, checkable conditions, elegant spectral fixed-point approach, and first empirical demo of a globally optimal training method on real data.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited practical scope, narrow empirical evaluation, missing baselines and runtime analysis, and presentation issues.  
- Minor comments/typos: fix “polyomial”→“polynomial”, “one CAN model”→“one can model”, introduce R++, pw/pu and p′ when first used, and clarify notation consistency (p1/p2).  
- Overall: theoretically strong and original paper likely acceptable pending clearer presentation, broader empirical validation, and better articulation of modeling trade-offs.
