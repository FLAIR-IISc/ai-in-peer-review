This paper defines a specific class of feedforward neural networks for which a unique global optimum is guaranteed to exist. The authors also propose a corresponding algorithm to find this global optimum and prove that it exhibits a linear convergence rate.

The mathematical derivations are generally instructive, although I found a few points that could benefit from further clarification:

1.  Theorem 3 involves inequalities based on first-order derivatives. However, the analysis for Theorem 4 proceeds to the second-order derivative level. Could the authors clarify the reasoning behind this transition?

2.  The network design appears to treat the parameters of the final layer differently from those of the hidden layers. In G^Φ (3), the gradient is taken with respect to the matrix `u`. Is this gradient computed with respect to the vectorized form of `u`? This question also applies to other parts of the derivation, such as the constraint that bounds `u` within a ball.

Beyond these points, I have more significant concerns regarding the model's practical justification and experimental evaluation:

A major concern is the model's objective of maximizing the L1-norm of the connection parameters, as formulated in (2). This is contrary to standard practice in neural networks, where weight decay (i.e., L1 or L2 regularization) is used to *minimize* parameter norms to prevent overfitting. It seems this maximization, along with the arbitrarily small epsilon, is a theoretical construct required to ensure the gradient is strictly positive (i.e., in V_++), which is crucial for the proofs. However, the practical motivation for this choice is not well-justified from a machine learning perspective.

Furthermore, the model imposes several strong constraints: the input data must be non-negative, and all weights are constrained to be positive. This design implies that activations will be monotonically non-decreasing through the network. The practical implications and limitations of such a restrictive model are not sufficiently discussed. How applicable is this design to real-world datasets that do not naturally meet these criteria?

Finally, regarding the experiments, the proposed network is only compared to SVMs, presumably because SVMs also have a convex objective with a global optimum. However, a more compelling evaluation would involve comparisons against standard, widely-used neural network architectures (e.g., networks with ReLU activations). It remains unclear what practical advantages this model offers over conventional neural networks that, despite lacking guaranteed convergence to a global minimum, often perform very well in practice.
