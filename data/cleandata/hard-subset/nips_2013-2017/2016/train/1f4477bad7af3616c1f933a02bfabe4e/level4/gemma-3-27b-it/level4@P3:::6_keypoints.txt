- Proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial feedforward networks with non-negative weights.  
- Provides a theoretical guarantee of linear convergence under relatively weak data assumptions.  
- Well-motivated: addresses limits of SGD and NP-hardness of optimal training for simple networks; situates work within recent provable-training literature.  
- Main strength: a practical algorithm with a global optimality guarantee that distinguishes it from prior computationally limited or assumption-heavy work.  
- Positive extension: method is extended to networks with two hidden layers.  
- Includes experimental results on real-world datasets that demonstrate potential applicability.  
- Primary weakness: restrictive network architecture (non-negative weights and generalized polynomials) raises expressiveness concerns.  
- Authors acknowledge expressiveness limits but need a deeper discussion of trade-offs and mitigation strategies (e.g., different polynomial degrees or architectures).  
- Experimental evaluation is limited: comparisons focus on SVMs and ReLU networks and omit other relevant baselines and algorithmic paradigms.  
- Missing reporting of computational resources (training time, memory) for all compared methods.  
- Presentation clarity needs improvement: Section 2 is dense, equations lack intuitive explanation, and notation is overwhelming.  
- Theorem 1 is highly technical and should be preceded by an informal explanation of its implications.  
- The claim of fast convergence should be clearly labeled as based on experiments rather than a definitive theoretical conclusion.  
- Minor issues: typographical errors and unclear motivation for the epsilon term in the objective; these should be clarified and moved into the main text.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretically grounded method, practical algorithm with linear convergence, two-layer extension, real-data applicability, tackles a difficult optimization problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restricted architecture limits expressiveness, limited experimental scope and comparisons, readability issues, and highly technical theoretical presentation that may be inaccessible.  
- Overall assessment: valuable theoretical contribution with practical potential; recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to expand experiments and improve clarity.
