The paper proposes a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks with a nonlinear spectral method, ensuring global optimality under certain conditions. The authors provide a comprehensive framework for analyzing the optimization problem and establish a linear convergence rate for their algorithm.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a globally optimal training method for a specific class of feedforward neural networks. The authors achieve this by imposing nonnegativity constraints on the weights and using a modified objective function that includes a term to ensure the gradient is strictly positive. The proposed algorithm, GΦ, is shown to converge to the global optimum with a linear rate, making it an attractive alternative to traditional stochastic gradient descent methods.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach. The mathematical derivations are thorough, and the use of nonlinear spectral methods is innovative. However, some parts of the proof, such as the transition from first-order derivatives in Theorem 3 to second-order derivatives in Theorem 4, could be clarified further.

The design of the network architecture seems to treat the last layer separately from the hidden layers, which might raise concerns about the generality of the approach. Additionally, the use of a modified objective function with an arbitrarily small epsilon to ensure the gradient is strictly positive might not be well-justified in practical neural networks. The requirement for all data to be non-negative and the use of all positive weights might limit the practicality of this design.

The experimental results are promising, with the proposed nonlinear spectral method achieving competitive performance on several UCI datasets. However, the comparison with SVM might not be entirely fair, as SVM is not typically used for neural networks. A more relevant comparison would be with other neural network architectures, such as those using ReLU activation functions.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on originality, as the proposed approach is innovative and distinct from existing methods. The quality of the paper is also high, with thorough mathematical derivations and a clear explanation of the approach. However, the significance of the results could be improved by providing more context on the practical implications of the proposed method and its potential applications.

Overall, the paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks with a nonlinear spectral method, ensuring global optimality under certain conditions. While some aspects of the design and experimental results could be improved, the paper contributes to the advancement of neural network research and provides a promising direction for future work.

* The paper proposes a novel and innovative approach to training neural networks.
