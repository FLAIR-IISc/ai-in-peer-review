- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimizing a class of feedforward networks with a linear convergence rate tied to the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix.  
- Provides a novel alternative to SGD-based training by leveraging Perron–Frobenius theory.  
- Requires non-negative weights and generalized polynomial activations, which are nonstandard and may reduce expressiveness.  
- Experimental results show competitive performance on some datasets but do not consistently outperform SOTA methods like SVMs.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and detailed, with some proofs in the supplementary material.  
- Main strength: a provable global optimality guarantee with linear convergence.  
- Strength: introduces a novel nonlinear spectral approach to neural network optimization.  
- Strength: includes experimental validation demonstrating feasibility.  
- Major weakness: strong constraints (non-negativity and activation form) likely limit practical applicability and expressiveness.  
- Major weakness: empirical performance does not convincingly surpass established methods.  
- Major weakness: scalability to larger datasets and deeper networks is unclear and computational cost grows with hidden units.  
- Concern that claims of very fast convergence (“a few iterations”) may be overstated without specification of convergence constants.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically sound contribution that opens new research directions and provides global guarantees under constraints.  
- Against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, limited empirical advantage, and unresolved scalability issues.  
- Overall assessment: interesting theoretical contribution but practical limitations lead reviewer to lean toward a weak reject; recommends relaxing constraints or stronger empirical results.
