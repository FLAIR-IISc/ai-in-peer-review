

This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for globally optimal training of a specific class of neural networks using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors demonstrate that under relatively mild assumptions (nonnegative data and weights, generalized polynomial activations), feedforward networks with one or two hidden layers can be trained to global optimality with linear convergence rates. This represents a significant departure from typical stochastic gradient descent approaches that offer no such guarantees. The work builds on recent advances in tensor-based methods [11] and structured matrix factorization [8], but addresses their practical limitations by requiring only computable spectral radius conditions rather than data-generating measure assumptions.

The paper's main contribution is a theoretically sound algorithm with provable global optimality guarantees that is practically implementable. The key innovation lies in transforming the neural network training problem into a nonlinear eigenvalue problem on convex cones, leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices. The authors establish a convergence theorem (Theorem 1) showing that the global optimum can be reached in linear time when the spectral radius of a derived matrix A is less than 1. This condition can be checked without running the algorithm, making it far more practical than previous approaches [8, 11] that require data-generating measure knowledge or complex precondition checks.

Strengths:
1. Quality: The theoretical foundation is rigorous, with comprehensive proofs of global convergence and optimality. The authors provide detailed mathematical derivations and clearly establish the relationship between the spectral radius condition and convergence behavior. The experimental results on UCI datasets confirm the theoretical claims, showing competitive performance compared to standard methods like ReLU networks and SVMs.

2. Originality: This is the first work to demonstrate a practically feasible method for globally optimal neural network training with convergence guarantees. While previous work [8, 11] achieved global optimality, their methods were impractical due to data-dependent conditions. The nonlinear spectral approach requires only simple spectral radius checks, making it significantly more applicable.

3. Significance: The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of deep learning—lack of global optimality guarantees—by providing a theoretically sound alternative. The method's linear convergence rate and lack of hyperparameter tuning requirements (unlike SGD variants) represent substantial practical advantages.

4. Clarity: The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical results. The connection between neural networks and nonlinear spectral methods is well-explained, and the supplementary materials provide necessary technical details.

Weaknesses:
1. Quality: The practical applicability is limited by the nonnegativity constraints on both data and weights. While the authors note that data can be shifted to be nonnegative, the weight constraints significantly restrict model expressiveness. The requirement for distinct activation exponents across hidden units (αi ≠ αj) is unnatural and may hinder real-world application.

2. Originality: The restriction to generalized polynomial activations limits the scope of the approach compared to standard neural networks. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation but do not sufficiently explore how to overcome it.

3. Significance: The method's performance on UCI datasets is competitive but not superior to existing approaches. The theoretical advantages do not yet translate to clear empirical advantages, and the authors admit the model is "not as expressive as standard neural networks." The spectral radius constraint may become increasingly restrictive as network depth increases, potentially limiting scalability to more complex tasks.

4. Clarity: The paper would benefit from more discussion of the practical implications of the spectral radius condition. The authors mention that increasing p_w and p_u while decreasing ρ_w and ρ_u can satisfy the condition, but the exact relationship between these parameters and the spectral radius needs further elaboration.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- First practical method for globally optimal neural network training with convergence guarantees
- Theoretical foundation is rigorous and well-supported by proofs
- The spectral radius condition is simple to check and verify
- Demonstrates applicability to multi-layer networks and reasonable empirical results
- Provides valuable theoretical insights into the relationship between neural network training and nonlinear spectral methods

Con arguments for rejection:
- Significant constraints on data (nonnegative) and weights (nonnegative) limit practical applicability
- The requirement for distinct activation exponents across hidden units is unnatural
- Theoretical advantages do not yet translate to clear empirical advantages over standard methods
- Potential scalability issues with deeper architectures due to spectral radius constraints

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation in neural network training. While the practical constraints are notable, the paper successfully demonstrates a theoretically sound and practically implementable approach to global optimality that represents a significant step forward. The authors have provided the first method that is both theoretically guaranteed and practically feasible, which is a substantial contribution to the field.

The paper should be accepted. The theoretical contribution is substantial, the proofs are rigorous, and the experimental results confirm the theoretical claims. The constraints noted are reasonable limitations of the approach rather than fundamental flaws, and the authors acknowledge them appropriately. This work provides an important stepping stone toward theoretically sound neural network training methods that could inspire future research to relax the constraints while maintaining the guarantees.
