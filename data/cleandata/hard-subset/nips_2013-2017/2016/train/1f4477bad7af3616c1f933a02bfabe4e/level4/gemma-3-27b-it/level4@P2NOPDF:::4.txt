This paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with provable linear convergence. The core idea is appealing: the method guarantees global optimality under conditions related to the network’s architecture, specifically determined by calculating the spectral radius of a small, nonnegative matrix. This represents a potentially significant contribution, as it offers a theoretical framework for optimal training.

However, I have some concerns regarding the practical limitations of this approach. The method’s requirement that network weights be nonnegative is a substantial constraint, and likely restricts the representational capacity and therefore performance of the trained networks. Furthermore, the paper relies on highly specific and non-standard activation functions. These restrictions, taken together, appear quite strong and may significantly limit the applicability of the method to more complex or standard neural network architectures and datasets. 

The empirical evaluation provides some evidence supporting these concerns. While the method demonstrates a theoretical guarantee of convergence, the numerical experiments reveal performance that is not competitive with Support Vector Machines (SVMs) on the tested datasets. This raises questions about the practical effectiveness of the proposed method, despite its theoretical appeal.

Specifically, the claim in lines 90-91 – “Note that the nonlinear spectral method has a linear convergence rate and thus converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to…” – seems overly optimistic. Linear convergence, while desirable, does not automatically equate to rapid convergence in terms of iteration count. The convergence rate constant plays a crucial role, and a small constant could still lead to a significant number of iterations for achieving a desired level of accuracy. 

Overall, the paper presents an interesting theoretical result with a clear and well-defined convergence guarantee. However, the strong restrictions imposed on network architecture and activation functions, coupled with the underwhelming empirical performance compared to established methods, raise significant doubts about its broader impact and practical utility. Further research exploring the sensitivity of the method to these restrictions and its potential performance gains with relaxed constraints would be valuable.
