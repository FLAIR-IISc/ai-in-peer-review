This paper presents a novel method – a nonlinear spectral method – for globally optimally training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, namely generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights. The authors provide a theoretical guarantee of linear convergence under relatively weak assumptions on the data, a significant contribution given the generally non-convex nature of neural network optimization. The paper is well-motivated, acknowledging the challenges of training deep networks with stochastic gradient descent and the NP-hardness of optimal training for even single hidden layer networks. It correctly positions itself within the recent surge of research aiming for provable guarantees in neural network training, citing relevant work by Haeffele & Vidal (2016) and Janzamin et al. (2016).

The key strength of this work lies in its theoretical result: providing a practically feasible algorithm with a global optimality guarantee. This distinguishes it from prior work, which often suffers from computational limitations or strong assumptions about the data distribution. The extension to two hidden layer networks is also a positive step. The inclusion of experimental results, even if limited in scope, demonstrates the potential applicability of the method to real-world datasets.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The primary limitation is the restricted nature of the proposed neural network architecture. The requirement of non-negative weights and the use of generalized polynomials, while enabling the theoretical guarantees, raise questions about the expressiveness of the model. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., exploring different polynomial degrees or network architectures) would be beneficial. The experimental evaluation is also limited. The comparison is primarily to SVMs and ReLU networks, neglecting other relevant neural network training methods and algorithmic paradigms. Reporting computational resource requirements (training time, memory usage) for all methods would provide valuable context. 

The clarity of the presentation could be improved. Section 2, in particular, is dense and challenging to follow. Equations are presented without sufficient intuitive explanation, and the notation can be overwhelming. Theorem 1, while central to the paper, is stated in a highly technical manner that may be inaccessible to readers not intimately familiar with the field. An informal explanation of the theorem’s implications *before* the formal statement would greatly enhance understanding. The claim of fast convergence following Theorem 1 is based on experimental observation and should be clearly stated as such, rather than presented as a definitive conclusion. Several minor typos were noted (see original review), and addressing these would improve the polish of the manuscript. The motivation behind the epsilon term in the objective function is initially unclear and would benefit from inclusion in the main text.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a novel, theoretically grounded method for globally optimal neural network training.
*   Offers a practical algorithm with a linear convergence rate.
*   Extends the approach to networks with two hidden layers.
*   Demonstrates applicability on real-world datasets, even if limited.
*   Addresses a challenging problem in the field of neural network optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The restricted nature of the neural network architecture raises concerns about expressiveness.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks comprehensive comparisons.
*   The presentation is occasionally unclear and requires improvement in readability.
*   The theoretical results are highly technical and may be difficult for some readers to grasp.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization. While the practical limitations of the proposed method and the clarity of the presentation need to be addressed, the novelty and significance of the theoretical results warrant consideration. I believe the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to NIPS if the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by expanding the experimental evaluation and improving the clarity of the presentation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
