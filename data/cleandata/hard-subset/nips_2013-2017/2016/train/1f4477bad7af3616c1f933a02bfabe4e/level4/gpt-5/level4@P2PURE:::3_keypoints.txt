- Paper proves a class of neural networks has a global optimum reachable at a linear convergence rate.
- Theory requires all inputs and linear-layer weights to be positive and uses generalized polynomial activations.
- Results are developed for a single hidden layer and extended to arbitrary-depth networks.
- Authors propose a nonlinear spectral-method optimization algorithm.
- Experiments show strong performance on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable results on small UCI datasets.
- Novelty: appears to be the first practical NN with both optimality and convergence guarantees.
- Advantage: preconditions are simple and depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix from the network parameters, not the data distribution.
- Limitation: positivity constraints on parameters and polynomial activations may limit model expressiveness and applicability.
- Requirement that all hidden-layer activations be different is unnatural and may hinder practical adoption.
- The spectral-radius condition (ρ(A)<1) forces increasing some exponents and decreasing certain weights, effectively shrinking linear weights.
- Shrinking linear weights as network/input size grows may make layers nearly linear, limiting expressiveness and scalability to deep architectures.
- Minor suggestion: investigate empirically how p1,p2,pw,pu, input dimensionality, and parameter sizes affect ρ(A) (e.g., whether ρ(A) scales linearly or exponentially).
