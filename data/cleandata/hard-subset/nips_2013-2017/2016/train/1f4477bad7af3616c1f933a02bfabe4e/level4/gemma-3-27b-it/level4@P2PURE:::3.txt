This paper presents a compelling theoretical result demonstrating global optimality and linear convergence for a specific class of neural networks. The key constraints are positivity of both input data and linear layer weights, coupled with the use of generalized polynomial activation functions. The theory is initially proven for single-hidden-layer networks and then extended to those with multiple hidden layers. The authors also propose a novel optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments demonstrate effective performance on a small, synthetic 2D dataset and reasonable results on several small UCI datasets. 

Strengths: This work appears to be the first to propose a neural network architecture with both practical potential and provable optimality and convergence guarantees. Prior work offering convergence guarantees often involves complex precondition checks, some of which are intractable in practice due to their dependence on the data-generating measure. In contrast, the theory presented here relies on comparatively simple preconditions based solely on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix comprised of network parameters. Furthermore, the authors show the theory’s applicability to networks of arbitrary depth and validation on small, real-world datasets.

Weaknesses: The proposed network currently has limited applicability. The theory requires positive input data, generalized polynomial activations, and a spectral radius (as defined in Theorem 1) less than one. While the non-negativity constraint on input data is unlikely to be a significant limitation – as most data can be shifted to satisfy it – the requirement for positive network parameters may restrict model expressiveness.  The stipulation that each hidden layer employs a *different* activation function also seems unnatural and could further limit application. Finally, satisfying the spectral radius constraint necessitates adjustments to parameters p1, p2, pw, and pu, effectively reducing the weights of linear units. Consequently, and if I understand correctly, increasing input dimensionality or network parameter size requires a proportionally greater reduction in pw and pu.  When these linear weights become very small, the activation functions behave increasingly linearly, meaning adding layers provides minimal gain in representational power. This raises concerns about the scalability of this method to deeper architectures. 

Despite these concerns, this work represents an encouraging initial step, and I recommend acceptance.

Minor comments: I am interested in understanding the relationship between the parameters p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimensionality, network parameter size, and the spectral radius rho(A). Specifically, how does rho(A) scale with input dimension – linearly or exponentially? And to counteract this effect, should pw and pu be decreased linearly or exponentially? While a formal proof may be difficult, an empirical plot illustrating this relationship on synthetic data might be illuminating.
