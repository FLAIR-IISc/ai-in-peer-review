This paper explores a specific class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to achieve global optimality with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors apply this method to deep networks with one and two hidden layers and evaluate its performance on various real-world datasets.

The class of neural networks studied in this paper is restrictive, imposing non-negativity on the weights and maximizing the regularization of these weights. Additionally, the optimality condition requires the use of a generalized polynomial activation function, which is less common in practice. These assumptions may not be reasonable, and more detailed explanations from the perspectives of applications or real-world datasets would be helpful in justifying these constraints.

The authors claim that the targeted class of neural networks still possesses sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieves good performance. However, the empirical results do not provide strong evidence to support this claim. In fact, linear SVM often outperforms NLSM1 and NLSM2, even when the linear SVM appears not to be properly tuned, while NLSM tunes its network structure. It is surprising that a linear SVM is used as a baseline, rather than a nonlinear SVM such as a polynomial kernel or Gaussian kernel, which would provide a more fair comparison. As a result, the conclusions drawn from these comparisons are not convincing, and the results do not demonstrate the sufficient expressive power of the studied class of neural networks.

The experimental evaluation is also limited by the use of small datasets from the UCI repository, which have limited sample sizes and feature dimensionalities. Furthermore, the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which may be problematic for high-dimensional data. It is unclear whether there are any strategies to address this issue, which could significantly limit the applicability of this method.

Another concern is that the optimal condition may not be guaranteed for any settings of parameters that could be used in the studied neural networks. Although it is possible to check the condition without running the neural network, it remains a challenge if there are no priors or if the possible parameters are extremely large. It would be beneficial to have a fast approach to obtain a small set of valid candidates.

It is also worth noting that the optimal property of the studied model appears to be closely tied to the special objective function used. It would be interesting to investigate whether stochastic gradient descent can also converge to a global optimum empirically on some tested datasets.
