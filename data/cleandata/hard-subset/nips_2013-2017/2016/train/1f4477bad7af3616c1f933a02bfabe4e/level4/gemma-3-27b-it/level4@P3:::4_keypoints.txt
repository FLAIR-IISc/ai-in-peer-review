- Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights.  
- Leverages Perron-Frobenius theory by restricting to non-negative weights and a modified objective to obtain global optimality.  
- Provides a linear convergence guarantee and a practical spectral-radius-based check for global optimality.  
- Contextualizes contributions relative to recent global-convergence efforts, highlighting differences in practicality and assumptions.  
- Theoretical contribution is strong and significant for a constrained architecture, with an extension sketched for two hidden layers.  
- Major limitation: the non-negativity constraint on weights is a substantial restriction likely reducing expressiveness and practical utility.  
- Use of generalized polynomial activations is non-standard and may limit feature-learning compared to common activations like ReLU.  
- Experimental results are modest and do not clearly outperform established methods (often slightly worse than SVMs).  
- Claim that convergence occurs “in a few iterations” is overstated—linear convergence does not guarantee rapid convergence without the constant factor.  
- Dependence on ρx (max input norm) in bounds and implications of data scaling are not adequately discussed.  
- Paper lacks detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity and practical scaling of the theoretical bounds.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically interesting but practically constrained; reviewer rates as weak accept.  
- Recommendation: encourage authors to explore relaxing non-negativity, broaden empirical evaluation, and clarify convergence/scaling issues.
