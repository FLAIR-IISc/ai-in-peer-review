This paper introduces a specific type of feedforward neural network that allows for the existence of a unique global optimizer, along with an algorithm that can find this optimizer with a linear convergence rate. The mathematical derivation provided is instructive, although some parts require further clarification.

One aspect that is not entirely clear is the transition from first-order derivatives in Theorem 3 to second-order derivatives in Theorem 4. The reason behind this shift in derivative order is not explicitly stated. Additionally, the design of this network appears to treat the last layer separately from the hidden layers, which raises questions about the gradient computation with respect to the parameter matrix u in equation (3). Specifically, is the gradient with respect to u computed as a vectorization of u? Similar concerns arise in other parts of the paper, such as the bounding of u within a ball.

Another concern is the requirement to maximize the 1-norm of the connection parameters in equation (2). This is in contrast to most neural network models, which use weight decay to reduce the 1-norm. The introduction of an arbitrarily small epsilon in equation (2) seems to be solely for the purpose of making the gradient strictly positive, which is necessary for the proof. However, this does not provide a clear justification for this design choice in the context of practical neural networks. Furthermore, the absence of free parameters in this model is not explicitly explained.

The proposed neural network has several limitations that affect its practicality. For instance, it requires all data to be non-negative and uses only positive weights, which can lead to an accumulation of weights. This design choice may not be suitable for many real-world applications. The experimental comparison with Support Vector Machines (SVMs) is also not entirely convincing, as SVMs are not typically used for the same tasks as neural networks. A more relevant comparison would be with other neural network architectures, such as those using ReLU activation functions. It is unclear whether this specific type of neural network offers any advantages over more commonly used architectures.
