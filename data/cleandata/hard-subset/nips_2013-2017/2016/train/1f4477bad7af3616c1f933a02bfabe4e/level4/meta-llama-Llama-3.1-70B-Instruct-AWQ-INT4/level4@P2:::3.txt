This paper presents a novel theory that demonstrates the existence of a global optimum for a specific class of neural networks, which can be achieved at a linear convergence rate. The main constraint is that all input data and weights of the linear layer must be positive, and activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The proposed theory is first proven for neural networks with a single hidden layer and later extended to networks with multiple hidden layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a theory that provides a global optimum guarantee with a linear convergence rate, which is a significant improvement over existing methods that require careful parameter tuning and provide no guarantee of achieving the global optimum. The theory's preconditions are relatively simple, depending only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix consisting of the network's parameters. The authors demonstrate that the proposed theory can be applied to networks of arbitrary depth and show reasonable results on small real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

However, the proposed network has limited applications due to several constraints. The theory requires both input data and network parameters to be positive, activation functions to be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius (Theorem 1) to be smaller than 1. While the non-negative constraint on input data may not be a significant limitation, as most training data can be easily shifted to be all positive, the requirement that network parameters be positive might limit the expressiveness of the model. Additionally, the fact that all activation functions of all hidden layers must be different is unnatural and may also limit its application.

The authors' approach to satisfying the spectral radius constraint by increasing p1 and p2 and decreasing pw and pu, effectively decreasing the weights of linear units, raises concerns about the model's ability to generalize to deep architectures. As the dimension of the input and network parameters increases, the weights of linear units may become very small, causing the activation functions to act similarly to linear functions. Adding more layers in this case may not increase the expressiveness of the network.

Despite these concerns, this paper presents an encouraging result as an initial attempt. The authors' work provides a new perspective on the optimization problem behind neural networks and demonstrates the potential for global optimum guarantees with linear convergence rates. The paper's contributions and results make it a valuable addition to the field, and it should be accepted.

Minor comments:

* The relationship between p1, p2, pw, pu, the dimension of the input, and the size of the network parameters and the spectral radius rho(A) is not entirely clear. Empirical plots or further analysis could provide valuable insights into how these factors affect the spectral radius.
* The authors may want to consider discussing the potential implications of their theory on the design of neural networks, particularly in terms of the choice of activation functions and the structure of the network.
* The paper's experimental results are promising, but it would be beneficial to see more extensive evaluations on larger datasets to further demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
