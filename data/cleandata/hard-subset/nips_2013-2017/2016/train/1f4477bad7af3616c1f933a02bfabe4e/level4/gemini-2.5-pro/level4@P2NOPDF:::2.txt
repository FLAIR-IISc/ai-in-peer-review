This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, demonstrating that a global optimum can be achieved with a guaranteed linear convergence rate. The method is applied to networks with one and two hidden layers and evaluated on several real-world datasets.

While the theoretical contribution of finding a provably optimal training method for a class of neural networks is interesting, the paper's practical relevance and conclusions are undermined by several significant weaknesses.

**Major Weaknesses**

1.  **Highly Restrictive and Unintuitive Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantees hinge on a set of strong assumptions that are not well-justified from a practical standpoint.
    *   **Non-negative Weights:** This constraint severely limits the representational capacity of the network. Standard neural networks rely on both positive and negative weights to model complex functions.
    *   **Maximizing Weight Regularization:** The objective function seeks to maximize the L2 norm of the weights, which is counterintuitive to standard regularization practices (e.g., weight decay) that aim to *penalize* large weights to prevent overfitting and improve generalization.
    *   **Uncommon Activation Function:** The requirement of a "generalized polynomial" activation function further distances the proposed model from mainstream neural network architectures.

    The authors should provide a much stronger motivation for why this specific, constrained class of networks is useful for real-world applications, beyond the fact that it enables a theoretical guarantee of global optimality.

2.  **Unconvincing Experimental Evaluation and Unfair Baselines:** The authors claim that the targeted class of neural networks possesses sufficient expressive power for complex decision boundaries, but the empirical results fail to substantiate this.
    *   **Inappropriate Baseline:** The proposed models, NLSM1 and NLSM2, are inherently nonlinear. However, they are primarily compared against a linear SVM. This is not a fair comparison. A nonlinear model should be benchmarked against other strong nonlinear models, such as an SVM with a polynomial or RBF kernel, or a standard multi-layer perceptron trained with SGD.
    *   **Weak Empirical Performance:** The results show that a linear SVM often outperforms the proposed NLSM models. This is particularly concerning, as the linear SVM appears to be used with default settings, whereas the NLSM models have their network structures tuned. If a simple, potentially untuned linear model performs better, it strongly suggests that the proposed models lack the claimed expressive power or practical utility.
    *   **Small-Scale Datasets:** The experiments are conducted on UCI repository datasets that are small in both sample size and feature dimensionality. To validate claims about modeling complex decision boundaries, evaluations on more challenging and larger-scale datasets are necessary.

3.  **Scalability and Practicality Concerns:** The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the proposed method.
    *   The analysis indicates that the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units. This dependency suggests that the method may struggle with high-dimensional data or wider networks, which would severely limit its applicability to a large proportion of modern machine learning problems. The authors should comment on potential solutions or workarounds for this issue.
    *   The optimality condition must be checked for a given set of parameters. While this check can be performed without training the network, it is unclear how to efficiently find a valid set of parameters in the first place, especially if the valid parameter space is small or has a complex structure. This presents a significant practical hurdle.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Regarding the optimality condition: Is there a principled or efficient approach to identify a set of candidate parameters that are guaranteed (or likely) to satisfy the condition, especially when there is no prior knowledge about the problem domain?

2.  Could you elaborate on the source of the global optimality? Does it primarily stem from the unique structure of the objective function, or is it an inherent property of the spectral training algorithm itself? As a follow-up, it would be insightful to see if a standard method like stochastic gradient descent also empirically converges to the global optimum on the specific objective function you have proposed. This would help disentangle the contributions of the objective and the optimization algorithm.

3.  Given that the spectral radius bounds grow with the number of hidden units, how do you envision this method being applied to problems with high-dimensional inputs or architectures requiring a large number of hidden neurons?

**Conclusion**

In its current form, the paper presents a theoretical result for a niche class of neural networks, but its practical significance is highly questionable. The restrictive assumptions are not well-motivated, the experimental evaluation is unconvincing due to weak results and unfair comparisons, and critical scalability issues are left unaddressed. Consequently, the claim that these networks have "enough expressive power" is not supported. While the theoretical exercise is noted, the paper does not demonstrate a clear contribution to the practice of training neural networks.
