The authors propose a novel approach for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks under certain assumptions, and provide a proof of optimal convergence. They also demonstrate the applicability of their method to a selection of real-world problems. The problem being investigated is intriguing, and any theoretical advancements in this area are welcome. However, the paper could be strengthened by providing more convincing evidence of the practical utility of the proposed neural network class. In particular, the authors raise concerns about the expressiveness of their restricted model, but do not provide sufficient discussion on the limitations and potential workarounds. Furthermore, a comparison with concurrent approaches would be beneficial. The experimental results do not adequately clarify the limitations of the model's applicability, and the authors' decision to only compare their approach to SVM, while ignoring other neural network training methods and algorithmic paradigms, is surprising. Additionally, information on the computational resources required by the three algorithms would be a valuable inclusion. The introduction is well-written, but the presentation could be improved, particularly in Section 2, which lacks fluency. There are also several typos that need to be addressed. Specific suggestions include providing motivation for the function Φ, including an informal explanation of Theorem 1, and clarifying the statement following Theorem 1, which appears to be based on experimental observations. Minor corrections include "polyomial" (60), "one CAN model" (67), and introducing p1, p2, and R_{++} (67).
