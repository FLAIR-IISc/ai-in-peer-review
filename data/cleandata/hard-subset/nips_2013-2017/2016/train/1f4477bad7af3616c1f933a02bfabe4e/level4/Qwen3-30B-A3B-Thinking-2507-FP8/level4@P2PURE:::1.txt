

This paper investigates a special class of neural networks and demonstrates that it possesses only one stationary point. The authors introduce a fast method that converges to the unique fixed point, corresponding to the global minimum of the cost function. The work addresses an interesting problem: showing that a relatively complex model with carefully chosen constraints can possess only one stationary point. Beyond convexity (or its generalizations, such as geodesic convexity), fixed-point theory is a common approach for establishing stationary point uniqueness—a technique employed in this paper. The authors construct a contractive map whose fixed point corresponds to the unique minimizer of the cost function. A drawback of the proposed method is its dependence on the bounded spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix for the convergence proof, which—as the authors note—prevents them from testing networks with larger numbers of hidden units. Another issue is the large number of tunable parameters, rendering the procedure somewhat unintuitive. Moreover, for each parameter configuration, the spectral condition must be verified. In experiments, the authors evaluated approximately 150,000 parameter combinations and selected the best-performing one based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method outperformed linear SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets, casting doubt on the practical utility of the neural network model under investigation.
