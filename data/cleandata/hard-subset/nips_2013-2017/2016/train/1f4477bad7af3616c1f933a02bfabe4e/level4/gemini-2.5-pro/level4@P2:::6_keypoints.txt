- Paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method with provable linear-rate convergence to global optimum for a class of "generalized polynomial neural networks" with non-negative weights and inputs.
- Method reformulates training as finding a unique fixed point of a contraction on a convex cone using nonlinear Perron-Frobenius and Banach fixed-point theory (Thompson metric).
- Concrete algorithm provided and demonstrated on one- and two-hidden-layer networks with experiments on UCI datasets showing competitive performance.
- Strength: originality — provides a practical algorithm with provable global optimality, moving beyond mere existence proofs.
- Strength: technical rigor — solid use of nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory; conditions are verifiable by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix.
- Strength: clear motivation in introduction, contrasting approach with SGD and other global methods.
- Weakness: restrictive model assumptions (non-negative weights/data and non-standard objective) raise concerns about expressiveness and practical applicability.
- Weakness: presentation of main theoretical results is dense; Theorem 1 needs an informal explanation and intuition for matrix A and its spectral radius.
- Weakness: insufficient motivation for the non-standard objective Φ and its terms (sum of outputs and ε regularization) required for the theoretical machinery.
- Weakness: potential misinterpretation of convergence claims—empirical fast convergence (e.g., <10 iterations) should be clearly separated from theoretical guarantees.
- Weakness: experimental evaluation limited — narrow baselines and lack of comparisons with other optimization methods for the same architecture.
- Weakness: missing computational details (training time, memory) needed to assess practical feasibility relative to competitors.
- Specific issues: typos and notation problems (title misspelt "polyomial", grammar at line 67, norms pu/pw used before definition, R_{++} not defined).
- Overall: novel and technically strong contribution but needs clearer discussion of expressiveness, improved exposition of main results, and more comprehensive experiments to increase impact.
