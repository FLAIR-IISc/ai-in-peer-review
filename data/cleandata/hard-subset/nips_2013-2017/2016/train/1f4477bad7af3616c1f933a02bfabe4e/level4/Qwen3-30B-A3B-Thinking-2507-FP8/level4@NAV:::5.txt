

The paper introduces a specialized class of feedforward neural networks for which a unique global optimizer can be derived. It presents an algorithm to compute this global optimizer, demonstrating linear convergence. While the mathematical derivations are generally instructive, several points require clarification:  
1. Theorem 3 computes first-order derivatives (via the inequalities), yet Theorem 4 proceeds to second-order derivatives. What justifies this shift in derivative order?  
2. The network design treats the final layer distinctly from hidden layers. Given that $u$ is a matrix parameterizing hidden-layer connections, is the gradient $\nabla_u G^\Phi$ (Equation 3) computed via vectorization of $u$? Similar ambiguities arise in contexts like bounding $u$ within a ball.  
A key concern: Why does the model maximize the 1-norm of connection parameters in Equation (2)? Standard weight decay minimizes this norm, not maximizes it. The use of an arbitrarily small $\epsilon$ in Equation (2) appears solely to ensure strictly positive gradients (placing them in $V_{++}$), which seems proof-specific and lacks practical justification for neural networks. Why is this constraint necessary without free parameters? The requirement for non-negative data and exclusively positive weights (no zeros) implies unbounded accumulation—how practical is this design? Finally, the experimental comparison with SVM (presumably due to SVM's global optimizer) raises questions: Does this network offer tangible advantages over conventional architectures (e.g., ReLU-based networks) in real-world applications?
