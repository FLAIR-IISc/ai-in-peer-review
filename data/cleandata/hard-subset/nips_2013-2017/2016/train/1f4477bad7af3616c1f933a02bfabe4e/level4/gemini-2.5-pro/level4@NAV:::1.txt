This paper examines a specialized class of neural networks, for which the authors successfully demonstrate the existence of a single stationary point. An efficient algorithm is proposed that converges to this unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The work addresses the compelling theoretical problem of how a relatively complex model, under carefully designed constraints, can be guaranteed to have only one stationary point. Instead of relying on convexity, the paper employs a standard approach for proving uniqueness based on fixed-point theory. To this end, the authors construct a contractive map whose fixed-point is the unique minimizer of the cost function.

One significant limitation of the proposed method is that the convergence proof relies on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a complex matrix. As the authors themselves note, this dependency prevented them from experimenting with networks with a greater number of hidden units. Another challenge is the large number of hyperparameters that must be tuned, making the procedure unintuitive and difficult to apply in practice. Furthermore, the spectral condition must be verified for every new configuration of these parameters.

The experimental results underscore these practical difficulties. The authors tested roughly 150,000 different hyperparameter combinations, selecting the optimal configuration based on cross-validation accuracy. Nevertheless, their model managed to outperform a linear-SVM baseline on only 2 out of 7 datasets. This outcome casts doubt on the practical effectiveness of the investigated neural network architecture.
