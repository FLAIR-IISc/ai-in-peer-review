- Paper proves a class of neural networks has a global optimum reachable with linear convergence under certain conditions.
- Main constraints: all inputs and linear-layer weights must be positive, activations are generalized polynomials, and a spectral-radius condition (ρ(A) < 1) must hold.
- Theory developed for single-hidden-layer networks and extended to arbitrarily deep networks.
- Authors propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method.
- Experiments show strong performance on a 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable results on small UCI datasets.
- Major advantage: appears to be the first practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees.
- Preconditions are comparatively simple—depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix constructed from network parameters—unlike prior work requiring hard-to-verify assumptions.
- Applicability is limited by the positive-data/positive-weights requirement and the need for generalized polynomial activations.
- Requiring different activation functions for all hidden layers is unnatural and may restrict practicality.
- The spectral-radius constraint may force linear weights to shrink as dimension/parameters grow, making deep networks effectively linear and limiting expressiveness.
- Reviewer recommends acceptance despite concerns, viewing the work as an important first step.
- Minor questions/suggestions: clarify how p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, and network size affect ρ(A), and consider empirically plotting this relationship on synthetic data.
