

This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks that achieve globally optimal training with linear convergence via a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM). The approach is applied to deep networks with one- and two-hidden layers, and experiments are conducted on multiple real-world datasets. However, the studied class imposes restrictive and counterintuitive constraints: non-negativity on all weights and maximization of weight regularization, which lack clear justification. Additionally, the requirement for the less common generalized polynomial activation function further limits applicability. The authors claim this class retains sufficient expressive power for complex decision boundaries and achieves strong performance, but empirical results do not robustly support this. Specifically, linear SVM often outperforms NLSM1/NLSM2 even when SVM is suboptimally tuned (while NLSM tunes its network structures), and using linear SVM as a baseline for a nonlinear model (NLSM) is inappropriate; nonlinear SVMs (e.g., polynomial or Gaussian kernels) would provide a fairer comparison. Consequently, the conclusions about expressive power are unconvincing. Furthermore, the UCI datasets used are small in both sample size and feature dimensionality, which is problematic given that the spectral radius bounds of matrix *A* scale with hidden units—a critical issue for high-dimensional data. The paper does not address how to handle this scaling, severely limiting practical applicability. The optimal condition also lacks guaranteed parameter settings, and while it can be checked without training, the absence of prior knowledge or the vast parameter space makes validation impractical. The authors should clarify if a fast method exists to identify a small valid candidate set. Finally, it remains unclear whether the global optimality stems from the specific objective function, and whether stochastic gradient descent could empirically achieve global convergence on tested datasets.
