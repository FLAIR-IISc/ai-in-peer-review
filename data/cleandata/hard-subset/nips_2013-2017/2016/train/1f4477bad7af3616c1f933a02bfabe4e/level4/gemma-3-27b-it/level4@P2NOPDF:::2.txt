This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks demonstrably trainable to a globally optimal solution with a linear convergence rate, utilizing a nonlinear spectral method. The method’s application is demonstrated on deep networks with one and two hidden layers, evaluated across several real-world datasets. However, the restrictive nature of the network class considered raises concerns. The authors impose non-negativity constraints on network weights and maximize their regularization, assumptions that lack clear justification and appear counterintuitive. Furthermore, the requirement of a relatively uncommon activation function – the generalized polynomial – for achieving the optimality condition further limits the method’s practicality. More compelling justification, perhaps grounded in application-specific benefits or insights from real-world data, would be more valuable than pursuing global optimality through increasingly constrained assumptions.

While the authors assert that this constrained network class retains sufficient expressive power for modeling complex decision boundaries and achieves good performance, the empirical results provided do not strongly support this claim. The comparison to a linear Support Vector Machine (SVM) is problematic. The linear SVM appears undertuned in the reported experiments, while the nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) models benefit from network structure optimization. Given that NLSM is inherently a nonlinear model, a comparison against nonlinear SVMs (e.g., with polynomial or Gaussian kernels) would provide a more appropriate and fair benchmark. Consequently, the conclusions drawn from the current comparisons are not particularly convincing and fail to definitively demonstrate the expressive capacity of the proposed network class.

The datasets used in the experiments, sourced from the UCI repository, are relatively small in both sample size and feature dimensionality, potentially limiting the generalizability of the findings. The analysis reveals that the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A scale with the number of hidden units, suggesting a potential scalability issue when applied to high-dimensional data. Addressing this limitation – for example, through dimensionality reduction techniques or algorithmic modifications – would be crucial for broadening the method’s applicability, as it currently hinders its use in many practical scenarios. 

The paper's claim of global optimality also raises practical considerations. While the optimality condition can be verified without full network training, doing so becomes intractable when dealing with a large parameter space, lacking sufficient prior information or facing an extremely large set of possible parameters. Exploring efficient methods for identifying a smaller, valid candidate set would be a valuable addition. Furthermore, it’s unclear whether the observed optimality stems primarily from the specialized objective function employed. An intriguing direction for future work would be to investigate whether stochastic gradient descent, despite its typical convergence properties, can empirically converge to a global optimum on these tested datasets.
