This paper investigates a special class of neural networks and shows that these networks possess only one stationary point. The authors introduce a fast method that converges to this unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The paper addresses the interesting problem of how a relatively complex model, under well-chosen constraints, can be guaranteed to have only one stationary point. Beyond convexity (and its generalizations, such as geodesic convexity), a common technique for proving the uniqueness of a stationary point is to use fixed-point theory, which is the approach adopted in this paper. The authors construct a contractive map whose fixed-point is the unique minimizer of the cost function.

One drawback of the proposed method is that the convergence proof relies on the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix being bounded. As the authors note, this constraint is the reason they did not experiment with networks containing a larger number of hidden units. Another problem with the method is the large number of parameters that must be tuned, making the procedure unintuitive to apply in practice. Furthermore, the spectral radius condition must be verified for each new choice of parameters.

In their experiments, the authors tested approximately 150,000 different parameter combinations, selecting the best one based on cross-validation accuracy. Despite this extensive search, their method outperformed a linear SVM baseline on only 2 out of the 7 datasets tested. This result calls into question the practical utility of the proposed neural network architecture.
