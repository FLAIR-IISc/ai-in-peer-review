The paper presents a training method—under certain assumptions—for a particular class of feed-forward neural networks. The authors establish optimal convergence and show that their approach can be applied to a set of “real-world” problems. I find the problem compelling, and any theoretical progress in this direction is welcome. However, the paper is less persuasive regarding the practical utility of the proposed network class. In particular, since the authors themselves raise the question of the expressiveness of their restricted model (twice), I suggest they articulate what the approach can achieve and what lies beyond its scope. Moreover, competing approaches should be discussed and contrasted. The experiments also do not clarify the limits of applicability of their model. While reading the empirical section, I was surprised that the comparison is limited to some SVM baselines and omits both alternative training methods for neural networks and other algorithmic paradigms. I would also expect information about the computational resources required by the three algorithms. The introduction is well written; thereafter the presentation could be improved. Section 2, in particular, does not read smoothly. There are some typos; I list a few below. Some specific remarks: While the loss function is straightforward, the function $\Phi$ lacks motivation: the role of the epsilon term was explained in the rebuttal and should be added to the paper. Theorem 1 should be preceded by an informal explanation; the formal statement is difficult to parse. Below Thm 1: The authors explained in the rebuttal that the very general claim that “the nonlinear spectral method […] converges quickly, typically in a few (less than 10) iterations, to the global maximum,” given immediately after Theorem 1, is based on their experimental observation. This should be clarified. 60: polyomial 67: one CAN model. 67: p1 and p2 are not introduced. 67: R_{++} is not introduced, which is unfortunate since there are several other variables whose definition points to that one.
