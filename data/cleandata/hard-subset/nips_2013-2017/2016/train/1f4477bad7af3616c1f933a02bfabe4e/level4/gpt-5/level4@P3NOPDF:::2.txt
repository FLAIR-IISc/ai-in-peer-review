Summary
The paper studies a restricted class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to global optimality with linear convergence using a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM). The authors instantiate the approach for networks with one and two hidden layers (NLSM1 and NLSM2), provide associated optimality conditions that can be checked a priori, and evaluate on several real-world datasets.

Strengths
- The work targets a long-standing challenge—escaping bad local minima in neural network training—by proposing an algorithm with provable global optimality under explicit conditions and with linear convergence.
- The optimality conditions are checkable before training, which is practical in principle.
- Extending the method from one to two hidden layers is nontrivial and of theoretical interest.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The modeling assumptions appear highly restrictive and somewhat counterintuitive: nonnegativity constraints on weights, an apparent push toward maximizing weight regularization, and the requirement of generalized polynomial activations. The paper does not sufficiently motivate when these constraints are appropriate in practice or what classes of problems naturally satisfy them.
- The claim that the resulting models retain sufficient expressive power is not convincingly supported. On the reported datasets, linear SVM often performs better than NLSM1/NLSM2 despite limited evidence that the SVM was thoroughly tuned, whereas the NLSM architectures were tuned. More importantly, the baseline set is not well chosen for a nonlinear method: comparisons should include nonlinear SVMs (e.g., RBF or polynomial kernels) and standard MLPs trained with SGD/Adam under comparable hyperparameter search budgets.
- The empirical evaluation relies on small UCI datasets with limited sample sizes and feature dimensionalities. This setting does not stress-test expressivity, robustness, or scalability. As a result, the experimental evidence does not substantiate claims about modeling complex decision boundaries.
- The analysis suggests that the spectral radius bound controlling optimality becomes harder to satisfy as the number of hidden units grows. This raises concerns about scaling to high-dimensional data or wider networks. The paper does not provide guidance or mitigation strategies for this limitation.
- Although the optimality condition is checkable without running training, there is no practical method to identify architectures and regularization parameters likely to satisfy it. A naive search over architectures/hyperparameters becomes intractable as the space grows, undermining usability.
- It is unclear to what extent global optimality stems from the specific objective design versus the spectral method per se. The paper does not explore whether standard training (e.g., SGD) on the same architecture and objective might empirically converge to similar solutions, nor does it provide ablations on the activation function choice.

Requests for clarification
- Provide concrete justification for each key assumption:
  - When are nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations appropriate? Are there application domains (e.g., parts-based models, monotone or count-based features) where these constraints are natural and beneficial?
  - Why does the method appear to “maximize” regularization strength? Please clarify the role of the regularization term in ensuring the spectral conditions and how this impacts bias/variance and generalization.
- Elaborate on the expressive power: What function classes are representable under the proposed constraints? Can you provide formal approximation results or illustrative decision-boundary visualizations beyond toy cases?
- Detail the implications of the spectral radius bound:
  - How does it scale with network width, input dimension, and data normalization?
  - Are there design rules (e.g., normalization schemes, sparsity, constrained connectivity) that keep the bound manageable while preserving accuracy?
  - Can you propose a principled procedure to select architecture and regularization to satisfy the condition without exhaustive search?
- Experimental protocol:
  - Ensure fair and comprehensive baselines: include nonlinear SVMs, tuned MLPs trained with modern optimizers, and possibly tree ensembles. Match hyperparameter tuning budgets across methods.
  - Evaluate on larger and higher-dimensional datasets to probe scalability and expressivity. Report not only accuracy but also training time and the fraction of configurations satisfying the optimality condition.
  - Compare the proposed spectral method against SGD on the same objective and architecture to test whether global solutions are also reached empirically by standard training.
  - Provide sensitivity analyses for activation choices and the strength of the nonnegativity/regularization constraints.

Minor comments
- Clarify notation and the definition of the matrix whose spectral radius is constrained; connect its entries to architectural choices and data statistics for intuition.
- Specify dataset details (sizes, dimensionality), preprocessing, and the exact hyperparameter search spaces for all baselines to allow reproducibility and fair comparison.

Overall assessment
The paper presents an interesting and technically sound approach to achieving global optimality with linear convergence in a limited class of neural networks. However, the current empirical evidence and practical guidance are insufficient to support the central claims of expressivity and applicability. The method’s restrictive assumptions, the lack of strong and fair baselines, the reliance on small datasets, and unclear scalability substantially limit its impact. Strengthening the motivation for the constraints, broadening and equalizing the experimental comparisons, and providing concrete strategies to satisfy the optimality conditions at scale would significantly improve the paper. In its current form, I am not convinced of the practical relevance and would not recommend acceptance without substantial revisions.
