This paper presents a nonlinear spectral method for optimally training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, achieving a linear convergence rate. The condition for guaranteeing global optimality depends on the network's architectural parameters and reduces to computing the spectral radius of a small, non-negative matrix. The proposal of a nonlinear spectral method that can optimally train a class of neural networks is novel and interesting.

However, the practical applicability of this approach appears to be severely limited by several strong assumptions. The primary concern is the constraint that all network weights must be non-negative. Furthermore, the method is restricted to a set of non-standard and highly specific activation functions. These are strong limitations that likely hinder the expressive power and performance of the resulting networks. This concern is supported by the numerical experiments, which show that the proposed model underperforms a standard SVM.

Minor comment: The claim on lines 90-91 that the method "converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations" due to its linear convergence rate is an overstatement. A linear convergence rate does not necessarily imply convergence in a small number of iterations, as the constant factor can be arbitrarily close to one.
