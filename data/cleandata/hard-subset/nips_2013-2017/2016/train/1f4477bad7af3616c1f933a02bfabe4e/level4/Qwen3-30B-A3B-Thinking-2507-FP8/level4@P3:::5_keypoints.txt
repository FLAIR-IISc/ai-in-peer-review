- Presents a nonlinear spectral method that provably attains global optimality with linear convergence for a class of feedforward polynomial networks with nonnegative weights and power activations.
- Focuses on generalized polynomial neural networks under nonnegative data and specific parameter constraints, extending prior tensor and matrix-factorization global optimization work.
- Theoretical contributions are strong: rigorous proofs using fixed-point theory and Thompson metrics, with Theorem 1 and a two-hidden-layer extension (Theorem 5).
- Convergence conditions can be checked by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix, avoiding learning-rate tuning common in SGD.
- Claims practical feasibility relative to prior global methods and reports competitive performance on UCI benchmarks versus ReLU networks and SVMs.
- Main limitation: requirement of nonnegative data and weights is restrictive, limiting applicability to a narrow set of problems (e.g., counts, image/word frequencies).
- Modified objective (maximizing sum of outputs / 1-norm) appears primarily a technical device to ensure positive gradients, raising questions about practical relevance.
- The “no free parameters” claim is misleading: architectural hyperparameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) and spectral-radius verification are needed and were chosen by cross-validation in experiments.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets and lacks discussion of computational cost and scalability to high-dimensional or deeper networks.
- Spectral radius condition worsens with the number of hidden units, potentially making the method infeasible for larger or deeper architectures.
- Clarifications: first-order derivatives suffice for Theorem 3; second derivatives are bounded in Theorem 4 to establish Lipschitz contraction; last-layer treatment and ε>0 are technical artifacts for proof.
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, though some technical motivations (e.g., objective modification) could be better justified.
- Originality: novel practically feasible global-optimum training approach with distinct differences from Janzamin et al. and Haeffele & Vidal.
- Significance: strong theoretical impact for optimization in deep learning, but practical significance is constrained by restrictive assumptions and scalability concerns.
- Recommendation: accept for theoretical merit, but authors should address practical limitations, parameter selection guidance, and scalability in the final version.
