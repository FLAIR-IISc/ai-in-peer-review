- Presents a theoretical framework proving a class of neural networks admits a global optimum reachable at a linear convergence rate.
- Key constraints: all inputs and linear-layer weights must be positive, activations are generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius of a constructed nonnegative matrix must be < 1.
- Theory is developed for a single-hidden-layer network and extended to arbitrarily deep networks.
- Introduces an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method.
- Empirical results: strong performance on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable performance on several small UCI datasets.
- Novel contribution: likely first practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees, with simpler, verifiable preconditions compared to prior work.
- Applicability limitations: nonnegativity of inputs/parameters and distinct activations per hidden layer may restrict expressiveness and practicality.
- Practical concern: satisfying ρ(A)<1 by adjusting p1,p2,pw,pu may force very small linear weights, making deep compositions nearly linear and limiting representational power.
- Open question: unclear how the spectral radius ρ(A) scales with input dimensionality and parameter size, and whether pw and pu must decrease linearly or exponentially to compensate.
- Overall verdict: encouraging first step despite limitations; reviewer recommends acceptance.
