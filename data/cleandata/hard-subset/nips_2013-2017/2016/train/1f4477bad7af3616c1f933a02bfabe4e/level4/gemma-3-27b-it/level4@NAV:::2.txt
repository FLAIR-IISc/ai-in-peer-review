This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks provably trainable to global optimality with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The method’s application is demonstrated on deep networks with one and two hidden layers, evaluated across several real-world datasets. However, the restrictive nature of the network class – requiring non-negative weights and maximizing weight regularization – alongside the necessity of the generalized polynomial activation function, raises concerns regarding the practical applicability of these assumptions. While the authors assert sufficient expressive power within this constrained class, the empirical results presented do not strongly support this claim. 

The comparison to linear SVMs is particularly problematic. Linear SVMs appear to outperform the proposed NLSM methods (NLSM1 and NLSM2) even with potentially suboptimal tuning, while the NLSM networks undergo structure optimization. Given NLSM’s inherent nonlinearity, a comparison against nonlinear SVMs (e.g., using polynomial or Gaussian kernels) would offer a more equitable evaluation. Consequently, the conclusions regarding the expressive power of the studied networks are not entirely convincing. 

Furthermore, the UCI datasets used are relatively small in both sample size and feature dimensionality. The analysis indicates that the spectral radius bounds grow with the number of hidden units, which poses a scalability issue for high-dimensional data – a significant limitation for many real-world applications.  It remains unclear if this issue can be addressed.

The provable optimality relies on satisfying specific conditions, but checking these conditions can be computationally challenging when dealing with large or continuous parameter spaces.  Efficient methods for identifying valid parameter candidates would be beneficial. Finally, exploring whether the global optimality arises specifically from the chosen objective function, or if stochastic gradient descent could empirically converge to a global optimum on these datasets, represents a promising avenue for future research.
