The paper proposes a method to train – under certain assumptions – a specific type of feed-forward neural networks. The authors prove optimal convergence and demonstrate that their method can be applied to a selection of “real-world” problems. The problem under investigation is interesting, and any theoretical advance in this direction is appreciated. However, the paper falls a bit short in convincing the reader that the proposed class of neural networks is practically useful. In particular, the authors raise the issue of the expressiveness of their restricted model (and they do so twice); I recommend adding some discussion about what can be done and what is out of reach for their approach. Moreover, concurrent approaches should be discussed and contrasted. The experimental results do not clarify the limitations of applicability of their model. When reading the experimental results, I was surprised that the authors only compare their approach to SVMs and ignore other methods, both other training methods for neural networks and other algorithmic paradigms. Also, I would have expected some information about the computational resources demanded by the three algorithms. The introduction is well-written, but the subsequent presentation has room for improvement. In particular, Section 2 does not read fluently. There are some typos, a few of which are listed below. 

Specific remarks: While the definition of the loss function is straightforward, the function Φ lacks motivation; the role of the epsilon term, which the authors clarified in the rebuttal, should be added to the paper. Theorem 1 should be explained informally before being stated, as the statement itself is hard to parse. Immediately following Theorem 1, the authors state that “the nonlinear spectral method […] converges quickly, typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum.” This claim, stemming from experimental observation as explained in the rebuttal, should be clarified. 

Typographical errors: Line 60: “polyomial” should be “polynomial.” Line 67: “one CAN model” should be “one can model.” Line 67: p1 and p2 are not introduced. Line 67: R++ is not introduced, which is unfortunate given that several other variable definitions point to it. The authors clarified this in the rebuttal; I had not seen this notation before.

**Summary of strengths:** The paper addresses an important problem – the global optimization of neural network training. The authors provide a theoretical guarantee of global optimality under relatively weak assumptions on the data, which is a significant contribution. The method exhibits a linear convergence rate, suggesting efficient training. The experimental results, while limited in scope, demonstrate that the proposed models can achieve competitive performance on UCI datasets.

**Summary of weaknesses:** The restricted model class (generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights) may limit practical applicability. The paper does not fully address the expressiveness limitations of the proposed model. The experimental evaluation is limited, lacking comparison to a broader range of methods and lacking information on computational costs. The presentation, particularly Section 2, could be improved for clarity and readability. The motivation behind certain design choices (e.g., the epsilon term in the objective function) is not immediately clear.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides a theoretical guarantee of global optimality for a class of neural networks, a valuable contribution to the field.
*   Demonstrates a linear convergence rate, suggesting efficient training.
*   Achieves competitive performance on standard datasets, indicating practical potential.
*   Addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning – the non-convexity of the optimization landscape.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The restricted model class may limit practical applicability and widespread adoption.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited and does not provide a comprehensive comparison to existing methods.
*   The presentation could be improved for clarity and readability.
*   The lack of discussion regarding the computational cost of the method is a drawback.

**Overall Assessment:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to training neural networks with a guarantee of global optimality. However, the practical limitations of the proposed model and the limited scope of the experimental evaluation raise concerns. While the theoretical contribution is significant, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the model's limitations and a more comprehensive experimental validation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity of presentation and expanding the experimental evaluation.
