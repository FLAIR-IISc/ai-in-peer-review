Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies a restricted but nontrivial class of feedforward neural networks—generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative inputs and weights—and shows that, under explicit, checkable conditions, training converges linearly to a unique global optimum. The key idea is to cast training as a fixed-point problem on a positive cone and apply nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction mappings (Thompson metric) to a gradient-based update GΦ that normalizes the parameters to lie on Lp spheres. The authors provide a computable condition for global optimality: the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small nonnegative matrix (size K+1 for one hidden layer; K+2 for two hidden layers) must be less than 1. They also analyze how ρ(A) depends on model hyperparameters, show that choosing sufficiently large norms pw, pu can make ρ(A) < 1, and extend the result from one to two hidden layers. Experiments on small UCI datasets compare their nonlinear spectral methods (NLSM1/2) against RBF-SVM and ReLU networks trained with SGD.

The work relates to recent attempts to obtain global guarantees for neural networks via structured matrix/tensor factorizations (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.). Compared to tensor approaches that require access to score function tensors and nondegeneracy conditions, this method trades off generality for practicality by imposing nonnegativity and a specific architecture/activation class, yielding an algorithm with no learning-rate tuning and a linear convergence guarantee.

Strengths
- Novel and rigorous training guarantee: global optimality with linear convergence for a class of multilayer networks, under a condition that can be verified a priori by computing ρ(A).
- Clear mapping from an optimization problem to a nonlinear eigenproblem on a cone; the use of the Thompson metric and a tailored Lipschitz bound (via Lemma 4 and Theorem 3) is elegant.
- Extension to two hidden layers preserves the same proof strategy and yields analogous conditions.
- Useful discussion of parameter dependence and explicit (albeit conservative) sufficient bounds to ensure ρ(A) < 1.
- The algorithm is simple, requires no step-size tuning, and the convergence rate is explicit.

Weaknesses and questions
Clarity and notation
1) Theorem 3 vs. Theorem 4: Theorem 3 requires Φ ∈ C1(V) ∩ C2(B++), but Lemma 4 (the Lipschitz bound that drives Theorem 3) is phrased in terms of first-order quantities (bounds on directional derivatives scaled by the function values). In Theorem 4, the proof appears to rely on more detailed differential structure and introduces the Ψαp,q function; it reads as if second derivatives are used. Please clarify precisely where C2 is required, whether Theorem 4 actually uses second-order derivatives, and reconcile the role of C2 with the first-order inequalities in Lemma 4.

2) Treatment of u: u is a matrix in Rn1×d with nonnegative entries. In GΦ (Eq. 3), ψp′u and the Lpu norm appear to be applied to u as if it were a vector. Please state explicitly that the gradient ∇uΦ is taken entrywise, and that ψ and norms are applied to u after vectorization (equivalently, using the Lp norm over all entries, e.g., Frobenius Lp). Similarly, when you use inner products like ⟨|∇uF|, u⟩ and ball constraints ‖u‖pu ≤ ρu, please specify the exact matrix-to-vector identification and the induced norms.

Modeling choices and practicality
3) Modified objective and “positive bias” terms: The optimization maximizes
(1/n)∑i [−L(yi, f(xi)) + ∑r fr(xi)] + ∑r,l wr,l + ∑l,m ulm.
You note an “arbitrarily small ε” is added to make gradients strictly positive, but ε is not explicitly present in Eq. (2); as written, the linear terms in weights and the additional ∑r fr(xi) term are not scaled by ε. Please:
- Make ε explicit and explain whether both the parameter-sum term and the output-sum term are scaled by ε (or which one), and whether the optimization and guarantees hold in the limit ε→0+.
- Provide an ablation/sensitivity analysis to show the practical effect of the added terms on training and test performance. If these terms are purely technical, demonstrate that predictions and classification boundaries are stable as ε→0+.

4) Nonnegativity and expressiveness: Requiring x ≥ 0, w,u > 0, and distinct αl ≥ 1 makes the network monotone and removes inhibitory effects. While the toy example illustrates nontrivial decision boundaries, please discuss expressiveness more broadly (e.g., approximation guarantees for monotone functions, relation to monotone neural networks/polynomial networks/NMF-based classifiers). In practice, how should one preprocess general data (e.g., centering, signed features) to satisfy x ≥ 0 without losing discriminative signal? Are there extensions that allow signed weights (e.g., via input splitting x = x+ − x−) while retaining a contraction?

5) “No free parameter” claim: Although step-size tuning is not needed, the method has many hyperparameters (n1, n2, α, β, ρu, ρv, ρw, pu, pv, pw). The experiments choose them via random search and bounds derived from (4)/(9). Please soften the claim and discuss practical guidance for selecting these hyperparameters beyond ensuring ρ(A)<1. In particular, α strongly affects scaling when x ∈ [0,1]; your experiments note sensitivity—more systematic guidance would help.

Experiments
6) Scope and baselines: The datasets are small and low-dimensional. Given the method targets nonnegative data and monotone architectures, please consider domains where these constraints are natural (e.g., text frequency vectors, histogram/image features, count data) and include stronger baselines relevant to nonnegativity/monotonicity (nonnegative logistic regression, NMF+classifier, polynomial networks, monotone networks) in addition to SVM and ReLU.

7) Fairness and metrics: Since the objective differs from standard cross-entropy, report both training objective and standard classification metrics; include wall-clock time and iterations-to-ε for your method versus epochs and tuned learning rates for SGD to substantiate the efficiency claims.

8) Tightness of ρ(A) bounds: The paper argues the bounds are conservative. It would help to report actual spectral radii for selected models, the corresponding optimal γ (eigenvector of A^T) used in µ, and empirical contraction rates versus the theoretical ρ(A).

Quality
The theoretical development is interesting and appears sound under the stated assumptions, but several definitions and proofs would benefit from clearer, more precise notation (matrix norms, ε, differentiability assumptions). The experiments are a proof of concept rather than a thorough evaluation.

Clarity
Overall well-organized, but please:
- Define the cross-entropy explicitly (it is referenced but not written).
- Make ε explicit in the objective and proofs (Lemma 5).
- State clearly the norms on matrices and vectorization conventions.
- Justify the “distinct αl” requirement with a concise formal argument.

Originality
Using nonlinear Perron–Frobenius and contraction mapping to obtain global optimality with linear rates for a multilayer network under checkable spectral conditions is original and advances the line of work connecting deep learning to structured factorization/eigenproblems.

Significance
Under the modeling constraints (nonnegativity, generalized polynomial activations, modified objective), the contribution is significant: a practically checkable condition and a fast algorithm with guarantees. The current empirical scope is limited, but the approach may be valuable in domains with natural nonnegativity and monotonicity.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, novel theoretical contribution with practical verification (ρ(A) < 1) and linear convergence.
- Extends beyond single hidden layer.
- Bridges fixed-point/eigenvalue theory with neural network training.

Arguments against acceptance
- Restrictive modeling assumptions and modified objective; unclear practical impact beyond proof-of-concept.
- Clarity issues (ε, norms, derivative orders) and some overstatements (“no free parameter”).
- Limited experimental evaluation and baselines.

Requested clarifications
- Resolve Theorem 3/4 derivative requirements and make ε explicit.
- Clarify treatment of u (vectorization/norms) and inner products in Lemma 4 bounds.
- Provide ablations and guidance on α, ρ choices, and report empirical ρ(A) and contraction rates.
- Discuss expressiveness and data preprocessing for nonnegativity, and broaden empirical comparisons.
