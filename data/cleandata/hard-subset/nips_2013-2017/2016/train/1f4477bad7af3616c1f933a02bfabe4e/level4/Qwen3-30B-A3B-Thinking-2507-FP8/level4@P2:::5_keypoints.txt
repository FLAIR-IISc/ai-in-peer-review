- Presents a nonlinear spectral method for training certain feedforward nets with global optimality guarantees and linear convergence.
- Works under weak assumptions (nonnegative data/weights) and handles one- and two-hidden-layer networks with generalized polynomial activations.
- Establishes a rigorous connection between structured matrix factorization and neural network training via nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory.
- Theorem 1 gives checkable conditions for global optimality by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix.
- Proofs are well-structured, using fixed point theory and Banach fixed point theorem; supplementary material supplies additional detailed proofs.
- Algorithm is simple, practical to implement, and has no free parameters (unlike SGD).
- Experimental results on UCI datasets show competitive performance compared to ReLU networks and SVMs.
- Provides a significant theoretical advance by offering a practically feasible method with provable global optimality.
- Novel use of generalized polynomial activations with nonnegative weights and a nonlinear spectral approach distinguishes it from prior work.
- Nonnegativity requirement for data and weights (and no zero weights) substantially limits practical applicability.
- The 1-norm maximization objective (versus standard weight decay) is technically motivated but under-explained for practitioners.
- Lacks a thorough discussion of when this approach is preferable to standard neural-network training in practice.
- Comparison with SVM is interesting but insufficient; more direct comparisons with standard training methods (e.g., SGD) are needed.
- The transition from first-order to second-order analysis in the proofs is not clearly explained and may confuse readers.
- Overall technical quality is high: rigorous proofs and appropriate experiments, though limited to low-dimensional datasets.
- Clarity is generally good; some technical sections would benefit from additional explanation and accessibility improvements.
- Significance: important theoretical contribution that opens new directions, despite constraints on immediate practical adoption.
- Recommendation: accept—paper is valuable for its theoretical contribution, albeit with noted practical limitations due to nonnegativity constraints.
