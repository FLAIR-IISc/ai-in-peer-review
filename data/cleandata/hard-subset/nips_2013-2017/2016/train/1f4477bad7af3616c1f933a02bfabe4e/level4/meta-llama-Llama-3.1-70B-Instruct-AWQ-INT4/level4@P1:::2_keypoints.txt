- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method that achieves globally optimal training with linear convergence for a specific class of feedforward neural networks.  
- Method is applied to networks with one and two hidden layers and evaluated on several real-world datasets.  
- Main novelty: a globally optimal training algorithm using nonlinear spectral methods, enforcing nonnegativity on weights and adding a regularization term.  
- Strength: introduces a novel algorithm with clear exposition and empirical validation.  
- Weakness: restrictive and counterintuitive assumptions (nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations).  
- Experimental results show effectiveness but do not convincingly demonstrate sufficient expressive power for complex decision boundaries.  
- Baseline choice is questionable: comparison to linear SVM is unfair—should include nonlinear SVMs (polynomial/Gaussian kernels).  
- Scalability concern: spectral radius bounds grow with hidden units, which may hinder applicability to high-dimensional data.  
- Originality is high, but restrictive assumptions limit generality and impact.  
- Significance is moderate: meaningful for the targeted class but practical impact is reduced by assumptions and limited experiments.  
- Presentation is clear and well-written but needs stronger justification for assumptions and more appropriate baselines.  
- Acceptance pros: novel globally optimal algorithm, clear presentation, promising experiments; cons: restrictive assumptions, unfair baseline, scalability and limited empirical support.
