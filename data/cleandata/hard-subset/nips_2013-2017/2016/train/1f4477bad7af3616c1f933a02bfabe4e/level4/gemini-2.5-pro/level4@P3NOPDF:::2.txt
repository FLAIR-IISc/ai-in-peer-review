This paper presents a nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of one- and two-hidden-layer feedforward neural networks, proving that this method can find the global optimum with a linear convergence rate. While the theoretical contribution of guaranteeing global optimality is significant, the work is undermined by highly restrictive assumptions and an unconvincing empirical evaluation.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Restrictive and Poorly Motivated Assumptions:** The core theoretical guarantees of this paper rely on a set of assumptions that severely limit the practical applicability of the method. Specifically:
    *   **Non-negative Weights:** The requirement for all network weights to be non-negative is counterintuitive and deviates significantly from standard neural network design.
    *   **Maximization of Regularization:** The objective function maximizes the L2-norm of the weights, which is the opposite of conventional weight decay regularization aimed at preventing overfitting.
    *   **Generalized Polynomial Activation:** The method requires a "generalized polynomial" activation function, which is not commonly used in practice.

    While the authors make these assumptions to achieve their theoretical result, they do not provide sufficient motivation for why this specific class of networks is useful for real-world applications. Instead of tailoring the theory to fit restrictive conditions, it would be more impactful to justify why such a network architecture is desirable in the first place.

2.  **Unconvincing Empirical Evaluation and Unfair Comparisons:** The authors claim their proposed class of networks possesses sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieve good performance. However, the experimental results fail to substantiate this claim.
    *   **Weak Baseline:** The proposed nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) is a nonlinear model. Yet, it is primarily compared against a linear Support Vector Machine (SVM). This is not an appropriate or fair baseline. A much stronger comparison would involve nonlinear SVMs (e.g., with polynomial or RBF kernels), which are well-established for handling non-linearly separable data.
    *   **Questionable Results:** In many of the reported experiments, the linear SVM baseline outperforms the NLSM1 and NLSM2 models. This is particularly concerning given that the authors appear to have tuned the network structures for their method, while the linear SVM may not have been similarly optimized. The poor performance against even a linear model casts serious doubt on the expressive power and practical utility of the proposed networks.
    *   **Choice of Datasets:** The experiments are conducted on UCI datasets that are relatively small in terms of both sample size and feature dimensionality. To convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness, evaluation on larger and more complex benchmark datasets is necessary.

3.  **Scalability to High-Dimensional Data:** The paper's own analysis suggests that the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units. This implies that the method may face significant scalability challenges with high-dimensional data, which is common in many modern machine learning applications. This issue could severely restrict the method's adoption.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Practicality of Optimality Condition:** The optimality condition can be checked apriori, but it is unclear how to efficiently find parameter settings that satisfy it, especially without strong prior knowledge or when the parameter search space is immense. Is there a practical, efficient approach to identify a small set of valid candidate parameters?

2.  **Scalability:** Given the dependency of the spectral radius bound on the number of hidden units, how can this method be adapted to handle high-dimensional data effectively? Without a clear strategy, the method's applicability seems limited.

3.  **Source of Optimality:** Does the global optimality guarantee stem primarily from the unique structure of the objective function (i.e., maximizing the L2 norm), or is it more deeply tied to the network architecture and spectral method?

4.  **Comparison with Standard Optimization:** It would be highly informative to see how standard Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) performs on the exact same network architecture and objective function. Even without theoretical guarantees, does SGD empirically converge to the same global optimum on the tested datasets? This comparison would help contextualize the practical value of the proposed nonlinear spectral method.

In summary, the paper tackles the important problem of global optimization in neural networks and provides a novel theoretical result. However, the stringent and unusual assumptions, combined with a weak empirical evaluation that fails to demonstrate a clear advantage over even simple baselines, make the practical contribution of this work unclear. The claims of expressive power are not supported by the current experiments.
