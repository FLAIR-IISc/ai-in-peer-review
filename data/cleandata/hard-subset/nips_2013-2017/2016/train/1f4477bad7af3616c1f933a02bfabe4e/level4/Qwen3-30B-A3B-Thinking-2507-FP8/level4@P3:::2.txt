

This paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for training specific classes of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees and linear convergence rates. The authors focus on networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activation functions (where each hidden unit uses a distinct real power α ≥ 1), demonstrating that their approach can achieve global optimality for one- and two-hidden-layer networks under mild data assumptions (nonnegative features). This work builds upon recent advances in globally optimal neural network training [8, 11] but addresses practical limitations of previous methods by eliminating the need for data density estimation (as required in tensor-based approaches [11]) and providing a computationally checkable condition for global optimality (via spectral radius of a small matrix).

The paper's main strength lies in its technical contribution: establishing a rigorous global convergence guarantee for a practical algorithm that avoids the need for hyperparameter tuning (unlike SGD-based methods). The authors prove that for their specific network architecture, the optimization problem can be transformed into a fixed-point problem solvable via a contraction mapping, with convergence rate depending on the spectral radius of a matrix whose size depends only on the number of classes (K) and hidden layers. This represents a significant theoretical advance over prior work [8, 11] which either lacked practical applicability or required impractical assumptions.

The paper's clarity is generally good, with well-structured theoretical development. The key theorems are properly motivated and the proof strategy is logically presented. The supplementary material appropriately contains technical proofs, allowing the main paper to maintain readability. However, the paper could benefit from clearer exposition of the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly regarding how to select the architecture parameters (n₁, α, ρ_w, ρ_u) for real-world applications.

Regarding originality, the work makes a novel contribution by connecting neural network training to nonlinear eigenvalue problems on convex cones, extending Perron-Frobenius theory to multihomogeneous settings. While polynomial neural networks have been studied previously [15], this paper's global optimality guarantee for a practically implementable algorithm represents a substantial advance over prior work at NIPS (e.g., [8, 11]). The authors have appropriately referenced related work and clearly distinguish their contributions from previous approaches.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the restrictive assumptions required for global optimality: nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents, and nonnegative features. These constraints make the approach less directly applicable to standard deep learning problems (where ReLU activations with negative weights are ubiquitous). The authors acknowledge this limitation but could strengthen their argument by better addressing how these assumptions might be relaxed in future work.

The experimental evaluation has several weaknesses that undermine the paper's significance claims. First, the comparison to baselines is unbalanced: the authors use linear SVM as a baseline rather than nonlinear SVM (e.g., RBF kernel), which is a more appropriate comparison for a nonlinear model. Second, the UCI datasets used are small-scale (low-dimensional, few samples), making it difficult to assess performance on more challenging real-world problems. Third, the paper claims the model achieves "competitive performance," but Table 1 shows NLSM1/NLSM2 underperforms linear SVM on most datasets, with only minor improvements over ReLU networks. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of why their model performs well on specific datasets (e.g., Cancer, Haberman) while underperforming on others.

The paper's main technical limitation is the dependence of the convergence condition on the spectral radius ρ(A), which grows with the number of hidden units. The authors acknowledge this issue but don't provide practical solutions for high-dimensional data (where more hidden units would be needed). This is a significant barrier to real-world applications, as the paper states "the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units" without addressing how to overcome this for larger problems.

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong, but the practical limitations and unconvincing experimental results reduce its significance for the NIPS community. The authors should either:
1) Provide more compelling empirical evidence on larger datasets or
2) Discuss how to relax the restrictive assumptions to make the method more broadly applicable

The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution but falls short in demonstrating practical utility for real-world deep learning problems. While the global optimality guarantee is technically impressive, the restrictive assumptions and limited empirical validation prevent this work from making a strong case for inclusion in NIPS.

Strengths: Strong theoretical contribution with rigorous global convergence proof; clear connection to fixed-point theory; practical convergence check via spectral radius.

Weaknesses: Restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, specialized activation functions); unconvincing empirical comparison (linear SVM vs. nonlinear model); limited experimental validation on small, low-dimensional datasets; no discussion of scalability to high-dimensional problems.

Overall, this paper presents a theoretically interesting advance in neural network optimization, but the practical limitations and insufficient empirical validation make it a marginal contribution for NIPS. I recommend rejection.
