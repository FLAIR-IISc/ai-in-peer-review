

This paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for training feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees under specific conditions. The authors demonstrate that, with the constraints of nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activation functions (where each hidden unit uses a distinct real power α_i ≥ 1), their method achieves linear convergence to the global optimum. The key theoretical contribution is showing that the global optimality condition depends only on computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix, which can be checked before training begins.

The paper makes an important connection to previous work on neural network optimization. It acknowledges the limitations of stochastic gradient descent (which lacks global optimality guarantees) and builds upon recent advances like the tensor-based approach by Janzamin et al. (NIPS 2015) and the structured matrix factorization work by Haeffele and Vidal (NIPS 2015). However, unlike those approaches, the authors' method avoids requiring knowledge of the data-generating measure and provides a practically feasible algorithm with linear convergence. This represents a significant theoretical advance in understanding neural network optimization, addressing a key gap identified in the literature.

**Strengths:**
1. **Quality:** The theoretical contribution is sound and well-supported. The authors provide rigorous proofs of global optimality and linear convergence, establishing a clear connection between neural network training and nonlinear spectral methods. The mathematical framework is carefully developed with appropriate references to Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point theorems. The supplementary material provides additional proofs for intermediate results, enhancing the paper's technical credibility.

2. **Originality:** This work is genuinely novel in its application of nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training with global optimality guarantees. To the best of my knowledge, it's the first practical method that provides such guarantees for non-trivial neural network models. The specific constraints (nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations) are carefully chosen to enable the theoretical results.

3. **Significance:** The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding neural network optimization. While the practical significance is currently limited by the strong architectural constraints, the work provides important insights into the relationship between neural network structure, optimization, and global optimality. This could inform future research on more practical globally optimal training methods.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Practical Limitations:** The nonnegativity constraint on weights is quite restrictive, as neural networks typically benefit from both positive and negative weights. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide sufficient justification for why the constraint is necessary for their theoretical approach. While they demonstrate the method works on some UCI datasets, the performance is generally inferior to SVM and ReLU networks (Table 1), raising questions about the practical utility of the method.

2. **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, and the authors don't provide a thorough analysis of how to choose the method's parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) for optimal performance. The paper states that "up to our knowledge, we show for the first time a globally optimal algorithm for neural networks that leads to non-trivial classification results," but the results are not particularly impressive compared to standard methods.

3. **Convergence Rate Claim:** The draft review correctly notes that the claim "converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations" is exaggerated. While the method has a linear convergence rate, the actual number of iterations required depends on the spectral radius ρ(A), which increases with the number of hidden units. The paper doesn't provide concrete examples of convergence rates for specific datasets.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pros:**
- Provides the first practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees for neural networks
- The theoretical framework is elegant and well-developed, with rigorous proofs
- The condition for global optimality (computing spectral radius of a small matrix) is simple to verify
- The method requires no hyperparameter tuning (unlike SGD)

**Cons:**
- The nonnegativity constraint severely limits the model's expressive power
- Experimental results are not compelling compared to standard methods (SVM, ReLU)
- The approach is currently limited to shallow networks (one or two hidden layers)
- The activation functions are highly constrained and non-standard
- The practical significance is questionable given the performance trade-offs

**Recommendation:**
I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and novel, addressing an important gap in our understanding of neural network optimization. However, the practical limitations are substantial, and the experimental results don't demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and practical performance, as well as more detailed analysis of how to select parameters for optimal results. Future work should explore whether the nonnegativity constraint can be relaxed while maintaining global optimality guarantees. The current version is of interest to researchers focused on the theoretical foundations of neural network optimization, but its immediate practical impact appears limited.
