This paper investigates a special class of neural networks and proves that, under specific constraints, its cost function possesses only one stationary point. The authors propose a fast algorithm that converges to this unique point, which is guaranteed to be the global minimum. The theoretical approach is notable as it leverages fixed-point theory, rather than standard convexity arguments, to prove this uniqueness. Specifically, the authors construct a contractive map whose fixed point corresponds to the minimizer of the cost function.

Despite the interesting theoretical contribution, the proposed method has several practical drawbacks. First, the convergence proof depends on the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix being bounded. As the authors note, this condition is difficult to satisfy and is the primary reason they did not experiment with networks having a larger number of hidden units.

Second, the method involves a large number of hyperparameters that require tuning, making the procedure unintuitive. This issue is exacerbated by the need to verify the spectral radius condition for every combination of parameters. The scale of this challenge is evident in the experimental setup, where the authors report testing approximately 150,000 parameter combinations using cross-validation.

Finally, the empirical results are not compelling. After this extensive tuning, the model outperforms a linear SVM baseline on only two of seven datasets. This limited performance calls into question the practical utility and representative power of this class of neural networks.
