This paper investigates a special class of neural networks, where the authors succeed in showing that it has only one stationary point. The authors introduce a fast method converging to the unique fixed-point that is the global minimum of the cost function. The paper investigates an interesting problem of showing a relatively complex model with wisely chosen constraints can have only one stationary point. Apart from convexity (or its generalizations like geodesic convexity), the common practice for showing uniqueness of the stationary point is through using fixed-point theory; the approach that is used in this paper. The authors construct a contractive map, that its fixed-point is the unique minimizer of the cost function.

One drawback of the proposed method is its dependence on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix for the convergence proof. As the authors mention, this is the reason why they did not try networks with larger numbers of hidden units. Another problem with the method is the large number of parameters that need to be tuned, making the use of the procedure rather unintuitive. Furthermore, for each choice of the parameters, the spectral condition should be verified. In the experimental results, the authors tried about 150,000 different combinations of possible parameter values and chose the best of them based on cross-validation accuracy. Their method, however, could outperform linear-SVM for only 2 out of 7 datasets. This result questions the real power of the neural network they are investigating.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach. The use of fixed-point theory to show the uniqueness of the stationary point is a nice contribution. However, the dependence on the spectral radius condition and the large number of parameters to be tuned are significant limitations of the method. The experimental results are also somewhat disappointing, given the complexity of the model and the amount of tuning required.
