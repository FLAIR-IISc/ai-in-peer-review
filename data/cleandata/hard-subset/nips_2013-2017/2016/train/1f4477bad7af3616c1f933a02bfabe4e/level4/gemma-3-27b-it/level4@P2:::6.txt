This paper introduces a novel method for globally optimizing a specific class of feedforward neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods, guaranteeing linear convergence under certain assumptions. The problem addressed – achieving global optimality in neural network training – is an important and challenging one, and theoretical advances in this area are valuable. However, the paper doesn’t fully convince the reader of the practical utility of the proposed approach, primarily due to restrictions on the model class. While the authors acknowledge limitations in expressiveness, further discussion of what can and cannot be achieved with this method would be beneficial. 

The work builds on a recent line of research tackling neural network optimization, citing [2, 11, 8] as related work. However, a more comprehensive comparison with concurrent approaches is needed. The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating feasibility on UCI datasets, lacks sufficient context. Comparing only against SVMs feels limited; a broader evaluation including other neural network training techniques and algorithmic paradigms would strengthen the claims. Furthermore, reporting computational resource requirements (e.g., training time, memory usage) would provide valuable insights.

The introduction is well-written and clearly motivates the problem. However, the presentation becomes less fluent in Section 2, occasionally suffering from minor typos and notation issues (see specific remarks below). The introduction of the objective function $\Phi$ feels somewhat abrupt; a more intuitive explanation of the added $\epsilon$ term (currently only explained in the rebuttal) would enhance clarity. Theorem 1, while central to the paper, is stated in a complex manner and would benefit from an informal explanation preceding the formal statement. The claim of rapid convergence immediately following Theorem 1 relies on experimental observation and should be explicitly stated as such.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a theoretical guarantee of global optimality, which is a significant achievement in the field of neural network training.
*   **Novel Approach:** The use of nonlinear spectral methods for neural network optimization is a novel and potentially promising direction.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction effectively establishes the importance of the problem and positions the work within the existing literature.
*   **Demonstrated Feasibility:** The experimental results, although limited in scope, demonstrate that the method can be applied to real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Expressiveness:** The proposed method is restricted to a specific class of neural networks, and the authors acknowledge limitations in expressiveness. A more detailed discussion of these limitations and potential avenues for addressing them is needed.
*   **Insufficient Comparison:** The experimental evaluation lacks a comprehensive comparison with other relevant methods.
*   **Presentation Issues:** Section 2 and the statement of Theorem 1 could benefit from improved clarity and fluency.
*   **Missing Details:** The motivation for the $\epsilon$ term in the objective function is not clearly explained within the paper itself.
*   **Computational Cost:** No information is provided regarding the computational cost of the proposed method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in neural network training.
*   The guarantee of global optimality is a significant contribution.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the feasibility of the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limitations in expressiveness raise concerns about the practical utility of the method.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to fully assess the method's performance.
*   The presentation could be improved for clarity and fluency.



**Specific Remarks (typos/clarity):**

*   Page 60: “polyomial” should be “polynomial”.
*   Page 67: "one CAN model" should be "one can model".
*   Page 67: $p_1$ and $p_2$ are not introduced before being used.
*   Page 67: $R_{++}$ is not defined, making the subsequent references unclear.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution. However, the limitations in expressiveness and the scope of the experimental evaluation are concerning. While the global optimality guarantee is a significant achievement, the practical impact of this work remains unclear. I would lean towards **weak reject**, but would be open to reconsidering with a significantly expanded experimental evaluation and a more thorough discussion of the method’s limitations and potential extensions.
