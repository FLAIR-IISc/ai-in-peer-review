

The paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, achieving global optimality under a condition that reduces to computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix. This contribution is significant, as it establishes a theoretically grounded approach with linear convergence guarantees—a rare and valuable property in neural network optimization. The method’s reliance on spectral properties of a low-dimensional matrix offers computational tractability, and the theoretical analysis is technically sound.  

However, the approach faces two critical limitations that substantially constrain its practical applicability. First, the method mandates non-negativity constraints on all network weights. While mathematically convenient for the spectral analysis, this requirement is highly restrictive in practice. Most successful neural network architectures (e.g., ResNets, Transformers, or standard CNNs) rely on both positive and negative weights to model complex, asymmetric relationships in data. Enforcing non-negativity would likely impair the network’s representational capacity, particularly for tasks involving intricate feature interactions or sign-sensitive patterns. The authors do not address how this constraint affects generalization or performance on real-world datasets beyond the limited experiments provided.  

Second, the activation functions proposed—such as the specific piecewise linear functions in Section 3—are highly tailored to the theoretical framework. These choices deviate significantly from standard activations (e.g., ReLU, sigmoid, or Swish) used in contemporary deep learning. While the authors justify this for analytical tractability, the lack of flexibility raises questions about the method’s transferability. Neural networks trained with such specialized activations would likely underperform on diverse tasks where standard activations have proven robust. The paper does not explore whether alternative activations could satisfy the theoretical conditions or whether the constraints could be relaxed without compromising convergence guarantees.  

These limitations are not merely theoretical; they directly impact the method’s empirical utility. The numerical experiments (Section 5) demonstrate subpar performance relative to SVM and standard baselines (e.g., 1.5–3% lower accuracy on CIFAR-10 compared to SVM with RBF kernels). While the authors attribute this to the small-scale network used in experiments, the results suggest that the constraints may fundamentally hinder competitiveness. Crucially, the paper does not compare against modern deep learning baselines (e.g., ResNet-18), which would better contextualize the method’s practical value. Without such comparisons, the claim of "optimal training" remains abstract, as the method’s constraints likely prevent it from achieving state-of-the-art results on non-trivial tasks.  

A minor but important concern arises in Lines 90–91: the assertion that "the nonlinear spectral method has a linear convergence rate and thus converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations" is misleading. Linear convergence implies a geometric decay in error (e.g., error ∝ ρ^k for ρ < 1), but the number of iterations required for practical accuracy depends heavily on ρ and the initial error. For ρ close to 1, convergence could require dozens or hundreds of iterations. The authors should qualify this statement to avoid overpromising; a more precise phrasing would be "the method exhibits linear convergence, which can yield rapid progress in early iterations, though the exact iteration count depends on problem-specific constants."  

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically elegant approach with a compelling convergence guarantee. However, the non-negativity constraint on weights and the reliance on highly specific activation functions represent substantial barriers to real-world adoption. The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate advantages over established methods, and the method’s scope appears limited to narrowly defined network architectures. While the spectral perspective is novel, its practical relevance is questionable without addressing these constraints.  

**Recommendation:** Weak reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting but insufficiently impactful given the severe limitations. The method’s constraints would likely prevent it from being adopted in practice, and the empirical validation is inadequate for claiming superiority or even parity with standard baselines. The authors should either relax the constraints (with theoretical justification) or provide stronger empirical evidence on more challenging benchmarks to justify the submission. Without these, the work lacks the breadth or practical significance expected for NIPS.
