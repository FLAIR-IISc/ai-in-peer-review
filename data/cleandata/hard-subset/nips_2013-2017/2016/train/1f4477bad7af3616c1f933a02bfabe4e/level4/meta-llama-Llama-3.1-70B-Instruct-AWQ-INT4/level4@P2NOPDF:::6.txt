The paper proposes a method for training a specific type of feed-forward neural network under certain assumptions, providing a proof of optimal convergence and demonstrating its applicability to a selection of real-world problems. The problem under investigation is interesting, and any theoretical advancements in this direction are appreciated. However, the paper falls short in convincingly demonstrating the practical usefulness of the proposed class of neural networks.

In particular, the authors raise the issue of the expressiveness of their restricted model, but do not provide sufficient discussion on its limitations and potential workarounds. It would be beneficial to include thoughts on what can be achieved and what is beyond the reach of their approach. Furthermore, a comparison with concurrent approaches would strengthen the paper. The experimental results do not clarify the limitations of the model's applicability, and it is surprising that the authors only compare their approach to SVM, ignoring other training methods for neural networks and alternative algorithmic paradigms.

Additionally, information on the computational resources required by the three algorithms would be valuable. While the introduction is well-written, the presentation of the rest of the paper can be improved. Section 2, in particular, lacks fluency. There are several typos, including "polyomial" (line 60), "one CAN model" (line 67), and the undefined variables "p1" and "p2" (line 67). The notation "R_{++}" is also not introduced, which is unfortunate given its relation to other variables.

Specifically, the definition of the loss function is straightforward, but the function Φ lacks motivation. Although the role of the epsilon term was explained in the rebuttal, it would be beneficial to include this explanation in the paper. Theorem 1 would benefit from an informal explanation before its statement, as the statement itself is difficult to parse. The authors' claim that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum" should be clarified as stemming from experimental observations, as explained in the rebuttal.
