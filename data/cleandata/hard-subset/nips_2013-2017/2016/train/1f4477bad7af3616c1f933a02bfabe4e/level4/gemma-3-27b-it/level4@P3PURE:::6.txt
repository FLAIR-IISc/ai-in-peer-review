The paper proposes a method to train – under certain assumptions – a specific type of feed-forward neural network. The authors prove optimal convergence and demonstrate the applicability of their method to a selection of “real-world” problems. The problem under investigation is interesting, and any theoretical advance in this direction is appreciated. However, the paper falls somewhat short of convincingly demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed class of neural networks. In particular, the authors acknowledge the limited expressiveness of their restricted model (doing so twice in the paper); therefore, I recommend adding a discussion of potential workarounds and inherent limitations of their approach. Furthermore, concurrent work should be discussed and contrasted with the presented method.

The experimental results do not effectively clarify the limitations of the model's applicability. I was surprised to find that the authors only compare their approach to Support Vector Machines, omitting other relevant methods, including alternative training procedures for neural networks and different algorithmic paradigms altogether. I would also expect the inclusion of information regarding the computational resources required by each of the three algorithms. 

While the introduction is well-written, the presentation could be improved. Specifically, Section 2 does not read fluently. Several typos are present, a few of which are listed below. Regarding specific remarks: the definition of the loss function is straightforward, but the function $\Phi$ lacks clear motivation; the rationale behind the epsilon term, which was explained in the rebuttal, should be incorporated into the paper. Theorem 1 should be explained informally before its formal statement, as the statement itself is difficult to parse. Immediately following Theorem 1, the authors state that “the nonlinear spectral method […] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum.” This claim, as explained in the rebuttal, stems from experimental observation and should be explicitly stated as such.

* Line 67: p1 and p2 are not introduced before being used.
* Line 67: $R_{++}$ is not introduced, which is unfortunate given that several variable definitions refer to it. Thank you for clarifying this notation in the rebuttal; I had not encountered it previously.
