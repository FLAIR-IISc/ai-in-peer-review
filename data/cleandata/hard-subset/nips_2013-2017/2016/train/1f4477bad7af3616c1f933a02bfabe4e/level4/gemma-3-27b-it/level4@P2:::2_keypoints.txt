- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method that provably finds global optima for a class of feedforward networks with linear convergence under weak data assumptions, demonstrated for one- and two-hidden-layer nets on real datasets.  
- Main theoretical contribution: a practically feasible algorithm guaranteeing global optimality by imposing non-negativity and generalized polynomial activations and framing training as a Perron–Frobenius–inspired maximization.  
- Provides a clear, pre-verifiable condition that guarantees convergence to the global optimum.  
- The non-negativity and generalized polynomial activation constraints are restrictive, counterintuitive, and limit practical applicability; more justification and discussion of workarounds are needed.  
- Experimental results are competitive but do not convincingly demonstrate expressive power, partly due to inappropriate baseline choice (linear SVM) and lack of comparisons to nonlinear kernels.  
- Use of small UCI datasets (limited samples and dimensionality) restricts the generalizability of empirical findings.  
- A critical scalability issue: the spectral radius bound grows with the number of hidden units, suggesting difficulty scaling to high-dimensional networks.  
- The paper lacks an efficient procedure for finding feasible parameter settings satisfying the convergence condition and currently implies exhaustive search.  
- Recommends investigating whether stochastic gradient descent can empirically reach similar global optima to contextualize the proposed method’s practical advantages.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded algorithm; linear convergence rate; competitive empirical performance; pre-checkable optimality condition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, unconvincing empirical superiority, scalability concerns, and inappropriate baseline comparisons.  
- Overall assessment: interesting theoretical advance but limited practical impact; reviewer leans weak reject unless scalability, benchmarking, and assumption-justification issues are addressed.
