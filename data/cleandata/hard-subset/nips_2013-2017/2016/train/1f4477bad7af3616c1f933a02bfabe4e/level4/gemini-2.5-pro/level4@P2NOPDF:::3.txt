This paper presents a theoretical framework demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks possesses a global optimum that can be found with a linear rate of convergence. The primary constraints of this framework are that all input data and linear layer weights must be non-negative, and the activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The theory is first established for networks with a single hidden layer and subsequently extended to multi-layer architectures. The authors also propose a corresponding optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments on a small 2D synthetic dataset show that the algorithm performs well, and it achieves reasonable performance on several small-scale real-world UCI datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  To my knowledge, this is one of the first works to propose a potentially practical neural network architecture with theoretical guarantees on both global optimality and convergence. This is a significant contribution to a long-standing open problem in the field.

2.  The preconditions for the theoretical guarantees are a notable improvement over prior work. Many previous methods with convergence guarantees rely on complex conditions that are difficult or even impossible to verify in practice, as they often depend on the unknown data-generating distribution. In contrast, the conditions in this paper—primarily that the spectral radius of a specific non-negative matrix of parameters is less than one—are comparatively simple and directly checkable.

3.  The authors successfully extend their framework from single-layer to deep networks, demonstrating the generalizability of their theoretical approach. The empirical results, while limited to small-scale datasets, provide a valuable proof of concept for the proposed theory.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of this work lies in the strong constraints imposed by the theory, which may limit the model's practical applicability and expressive power.

1.  **Non-Negativity Constraints:** The requirement for non-negative inputs is a relatively minor issue, as many datasets can be shifted to satisfy this. However, the constraint that all network parameters must be non-negative is more restrictive. It prevents the model from learning inhibitory connections, which are fundamental to the expressiveness of standard neural networks.

2.  **Activation Function Constraint:** The requirement that each hidden layer must use a different activation function is somewhat unnatural from a practical standpoint and may complicate model design and hyperparameter tuning.

3.  **Scalability and the Spectral Radius Constraint:** My main concern relates to the implications of the spectral radius constraint, ρ(A) < 1 (Theorem 1). The authors note that satisfying this condition requires adjusting hyperparameters (p1, p2, pw, pu) to decrease the magnitude of the weights in the linear layers. It seems plausible that as the dimensionality of the input or the size of the hidden layers increases, the weights would need to be scaled down even further to maintain ρ(A) < 1. If the weights become exceedingly small, the generalized polynomial activations will behave approximately as linear functions. In such a regime, a deep network would essentially collapse into a series of near-linear transformations, negating the benefits of depth and severely limiting its capacity to model complex, non-linear functions. This raises questions about the scalability of the proposed method to the deeper and wider architectures prevalent in modern machine learning.

**Conclusion:**

Despite these concerns, this paper represents an important and encouraging initial step toward building neural networks with solid theoretical foundations. The work provides a novel perspective on a challenging problem, and the theoretical contributions are significant. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.

**Minor Comments and Questions:**

I am curious about the quantitative relationship between the spectral radius ρ(A) and the model's hyperparameters and dimensions. Specifically, how does ρ(A) scale with the input dimension, the number of hidden units, and the hyperparameters p1, p2, pw, and pu? For instance, as the input dimension increases, do the weights pw and pu need to be decreased linearly or exponentially to keep the spectral radius below one? While a formal theoretical analysis may be difficult, an empirical study on synthetic data plotting these relationships would be a valuable addition. This could provide practical guidance on hyperparameter tuning and offer better insights into the scalability of the proposed framework.
