This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a specific class of feedforward neural networks, guaranteeing convergence to the global optimum with a linear rate. The challenge of globally optimizing non-convex neural network loss functions is a central problem in the field, with most practical methods like SGD offering no such guarantees. This work relates to recent theoretical research that provides provable guarantees for training neural networks, such as the tensor methods in Janzamin et al. (2015) or the matrix factorization approaches in Haeffele and Vidal (2017). The main contribution of this paper is to present what appears to be the first practically feasible algorithm with such strong guarantees, where the condition for optimality can be verified a priori by computing the spectral radius of a small, well-defined matrix.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary strength is the novel application of nonlinear spectral methods, rooted in Perron-Frobenius theory, to neural network training. Providing a practical algorithm that finds a unique global optimum is a significant theoretical achievement. The convergence guarantee is not merely asymptotic; it is linear, and the condition for it can be checked before training begins.
*   **Parameter-Free Optimization:** Unlike SGD and its variants, the proposed iterative method does not require tuning of sensitive hyperparameters like learning rates, which is a major practical advantage.
*   **Proof of Concept:** The authors are the first among related works on global optimality to provide experimental results on real-world datasets, demonstrating that the proposed model can achieve non-trivial classification results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Highly Restrictive Model:** The theoretical guarantees come at a steep price. The method requires non-negative weights and input data, which is a major limitation. Standard practices like data standardization are not applicable, and the expressiveness of the network is severely constrained by preventing negative weights.
*   **Non-Standard Architecture and Objective:** The network relies on generalized polynomial activations, which are uncommon. More importantly, the objective function is modified to include the sum of all network outputs, which is non-standard and makes the learned model difficult to interpret in a conventional classification context.
*   **Underwhelming Empirical Performance:** While the experiments serve as an important proof of concept, the results show that the proposed NLSM models are generally not competitive with a standard RBF-kernel SVM and only sometimes outperform simple ReLU networks. This suggests that the architectural constraints imposed for the sake of the guarantee significantly hinder the model's practical performance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and elegant theoretical framework for a notoriously difficult problem.
*   It is the first work to translate theoretical guarantees of global optimality for neural networks into a practical algorithm tested on real data.
*   The approach opens a new potential research avenue for provable optimization in deep learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The constraints on the network (non-negative weights, specific activations, modified objective) are so severe that the practical significance of the method is questionable.
*   The empirical results, while a good first step, do not demonstrate a competitive advantage over existing, well-understood methods.

**Minor Comment:**
The claim that a linear convergence rate implies convergence "in a few (less than 10) iterations" is an overstatement. A linear rate of convergence can still be very slow if the contraction factor (the spectral radius `ρ(A)`) is close to 1. It would be more accurate to state that the linear rate guarantees steady progress toward the optimum, with the speed dependent on the magnitude of `ρ(A)`.
