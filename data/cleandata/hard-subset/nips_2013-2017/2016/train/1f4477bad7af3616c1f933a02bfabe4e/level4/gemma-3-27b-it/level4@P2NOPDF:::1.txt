This paper explores a specific class of neural networks and demonstrates a compelling theoretical result: the existence of a single stationary point. The authors present a rapid convergence method to this unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. Investigating the conditions under which complex models can possess a single stationary point is an interesting and valuable contribution. Beyond convexity and related concepts like geodesic convexity, fixed-point theory offers a common pathway to prove such uniqueness—the approach adopted successfully in this work. Specifically, the authors construct a contractive mapping whose fixed-point coincides with the unique minimizer of the objective function.

However, the proposed method has limitations. The convergence proof relies on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix. As acknowledged by the authors, this constraint currently restricts the applicability of the method to networks with a limited number of hidden units. A further challenge lies in the numerous parameters requiring careful tuning, making the procedure somewhat unintuitive to apply. Crucially, for each parameter configuration, verifying the necessary spectral condition adds significant complexity.

The experimental evaluation involved an extensive search over approximately 150,000 parameter combinations, with the best performing settings selected via cross-validation. Despite this effort, the method outperformed a linear SVM on only two out of seven datasets. This limited empirical improvement raises concerns about the practical advantages of the investigated neural network architecture. The significant computational cost of parameter tuning, coupled with modest performance gains, weakens the overall impact of the work. 

While the theoretical contribution regarding the uniqueness of the stationary point is noteworthy, the practical challenges associated with parameter selection and the limited empirical success warrant careful consideration. Further research exploring methods to relax the spectral radius constraint or automate parameter tuning would be beneficial.
