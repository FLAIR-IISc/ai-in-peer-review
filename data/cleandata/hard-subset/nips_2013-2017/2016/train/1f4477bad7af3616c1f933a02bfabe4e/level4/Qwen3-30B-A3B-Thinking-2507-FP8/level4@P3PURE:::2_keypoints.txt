- Claims a nonlinear spectral method yields global-optimal training with linear convergence for 1- and 2-hidden-layer networks, evaluated on real datasets.  
- Method enforces restrictive, counterintuitive constraints: non-negativity on all weights and strong/maximized regularization of those weights.  
- Requires the uncommon generalized polynomial activation function, which limits practicality and biological/plausibility justification.  
- These modeling assumptions lack justification from application or real-world dataset needs.  
- Authors claim retained expressive power, but empirical results do not convincingly support this.  
- Linear SVM sometimes outperforms NLSM variants, and comparisons are unfair because nonlinear baselines (e.g., polynomial/Gaussian-kernel SVM) were omitted.  
- NLSM tuning of network structure versus untuned linear SVM further biases comparisons.  
- Experiments use only small UCI datasets, limiting evidence for scalability and high-dimensional relevance.  
- Spectral radius bounds grow with number of hidden units, posing scaling problems for high-dimensional data; authors must address this spectral-scaling issue.  
- The optimality condition cannot be guaranteed for arbitrary parameters; although checkable, finding valid parameter regions is impractical without priors or efficient candidate-search methods.  
- Unclear whether global-optimal properties stem from the specific objective; recommend investigating whether SGD also attains global optima on the tested datasets.
