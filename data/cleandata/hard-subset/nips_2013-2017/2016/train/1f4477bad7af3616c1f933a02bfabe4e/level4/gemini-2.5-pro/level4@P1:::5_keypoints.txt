- Presents a practical nonlinear spectral method that provably converges linearly to a global optimum for a class of "generalized polynomial neural networks."
- Convergence conditions are checkable a priori and the method is directly applicable to data (unlike some prior theoretical work).
- Theoretical development is rigorous, leveraging nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory, the Thompson metric, and Banach's fixed-point theorem.
- Experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate practicality, addressing a gap in prior global-optimum training work.
- The link between the general fixed-point theory and the neural-network objective needs clearer exposition.
- Clarify how Lemma 4’s conditions on the first derivative of GΦ relate explicitly to second-order (Hessian) properties of Φ and derivation of matrix A bounds.
- Explicitly state conventions for applying vector norms and component-wise operations to weight matrices and gradients.
- The objective includes a term that maximizes L1 weight norm (opposite of usual regularization); its impact on generalization and interpretation is unclear.
- The claim of "no free parameter" is misleading—many hyperparameters (n1, α, pw, pu, ρw, ρu) must be tuned in practice.
- Restriction to non-negative inputs and non-negative weights is a strong limitation, yielding only monotone-increasing function classes.
- Practical advantages over standard heuristically-trained deep networks are not convincingly demonstrated; results are competitive but not consistently superior to ReLU networks or SVMs.
- The paper needs a deeper discussion of problem domains where the non-negativity inductive bias is natural and leads to clear benefits.
