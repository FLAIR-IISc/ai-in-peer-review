- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for a class of feedforward networks with provable global optimality and linear convergence rate.
- Main theoretical novelty: applies Perron-Frobenius–based nonlinear spectral theory to obtain a practically checkable optimality condition via the spectral radius of a small matrix.
- Algorithm is parameter-free—no learning-rate tuning required—unlike SGD and its variants.
- Authors provide the first empirical proof-of-concept showing the method can train on real datasets.
- Strong theoretical contribution: unique global optimum and linear convergence (rate depends on spectral radius).
- Major restriction: requires non-negative weights and non-negative inputs, preventing standard preprocessing and limiting expressiveness.
- Uses non-standard architecture and objective (generalized polynomial activations and sum-of-outputs loss), reducing interpretability and practical relevance.
- Empirical performance is underwhelming: generally inferior to RBF-SVM and only sometimes better than simple ReLU networks.
- Argument for acceptance: novel, elegant theoretical framework that translates provable global optimality into a practical algorithm tested on data.
- Argument against acceptance: severe modeling constraints and modified objective make practical significance questionable.
- Empirical results do not demonstrate a clear competitive advantage over well-established methods.
- Minor critique: claiming "few (<10) iterations" from linear convergence is misleading—convergence speed depends on the spectral radius and can be slow if it is close to 1.
