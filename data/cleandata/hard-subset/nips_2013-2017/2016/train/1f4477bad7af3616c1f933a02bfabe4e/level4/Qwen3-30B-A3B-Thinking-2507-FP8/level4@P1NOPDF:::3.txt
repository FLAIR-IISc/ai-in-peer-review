

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks achieves a global optimum under linear convergence rates. The core conditions require all input data and linear layer weights to be non-negative, activation functions to be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius of an associated nonnegative matrix to be less than one. The theory is first established for single-hidden-layer networks and later extended to multi-layer architectures. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods. Experiments show the algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable results on several small UCI benchmark datasets.  

**Strengths:** This work represents the first practical neural network framework with provable global optimality and convergence guarantees. Previous approaches with convergence guarantees often require complex, practically unverifiable preconditions (e.g., depending on unknown data-generating measures). In contrast, the proposed theory relies on simple preconditions involving only the spectral radius of a matrix derived from network parameters. The authors convincingly demonstrate applicability to arbitrary network depths and provide empirical validation on real-world datasets.  

**Weaknesses:** The theory’s practical scope remains limited due to restrictive assumptions. While shifting input data to non-negative values (e.g., via offset) is feasible, the requirement for strictly positive network weights significantly restricts model expressiveness. Furthermore, the necessity for *different* activation functions across all hidden layers is unnatural and likely limits applicability. Critically, to satisfy the spectral radius constraint (Theorem 1), the authors must increase parameters *p₁* and *p₂* while decreasing *p_w* and *p_u*, effectively reducing linear layer weights. As input dimensionality or network depth increases, this weight reduction becomes more pronounced. When linear weights approach zero, activation functions become effectively linear, rendering additional layers redundant (as stacking linear transformations yields a single linear map). This raises serious concerns about scalability to deep architectures, as the method may fail to leverage depth for enhanced representational capacity.  

**Conclusion:** Despite these limitations, this work constitutes a valuable initial step toward theoretically grounded deep learning. The theoretical insights and algorithmic proposal are promising, and the constraints, while restrictive, are clearly articulated. The paper should be accepted.  

**Minor Comments:**  
I would be interested in understanding how *p₁*, *p₂*, *p_w*, *p_u*, input dimension, and network size collectively influence the spectral radius *ρ(A)*. Specifically:  
- Does *ρ(A)* increase linearly or exponentially with input dimension?  
- To maintain *ρ(A) < 1* as dimensions grow, should *p_w* and *p_u* be decreased linearly or exponentially?  
While a formal proof may be challenging, empirical exploration of this relationship on synthetic data would strengthen the analysis.
