This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers, proving that a global optimum can be found with a linear convergence rate. The method is evaluated on several real-world datasets.

While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses regarding its assumptions and experimental evaluation.

**Restrictive and Poorly Justified Assumptions:**
The class of neural networks studied is highly restrictive. As the authors note, it requires non-negative weights, maximization of a weight regularization term, and the use of a less common "generalized polynomial" activation function to satisfy the optimality condition. These assumptions seem counterintuitive and are not well-justified from a practical standpoint. Instead of imposing these restrictions solely to achieve a theoretical guarantee of global optimality, the authors should provide a more detailed motivation for why this specific model class is relevant for real-world applications.

**Unconvincing Experimental Results and Unfair Comparisons:**
The authors claim that their proposed model has sufficient expressive power and achieves good performance. However, the empirical results do not provide strong support for this claim.
1.  The baseline comparisons are not fair. The proposed nonlinear models (NLSM1, NLSM2) are compared against a linear SVM. A more appropriate comparison would be against strong nonlinear baselines, such as an SVM with a polynomial or Gaussian kernel, which are also capable of learning complex decision boundaries.
2.  In several cases, the linear SVM appears to outperform the proposed models, even when the SVM baseline seems to be poorly tuned, while the NLSM models have their network structures tuned.
Consequently, the conclusions drawn from these comparative results are unconvincing and fail to demonstrate the claimed expressive power of the studied neural network class.

**Scalability and Practicality Concerns:**
1.  The experiments are conducted on UCI datasets that are small in terms of both sample size and feature dimensionality.
2.  The theoretical analysis indicates that the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units. This suggests the method may not scale well to high-dimensional data, which could significantly limit its applicability to many modern machine learning problems. Is there a way to mitigate this issue?

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  The optimality condition appears to depend heavily on the choice of parameters. While this condition can be checked without training the network, it remains a practical challenge if one has no prior knowledge or if the space of valid parameters is vast. Is there an efficient approach to identify a set of candidate parameters that satisfy the condition?
2.  Does the global optimality guarantee stem primarily from the specific objective function? It would be interesting to see an empirical comparison where a standard optimizer like stochastic gradient descent (SGD) is applied to the same objective function on the tested datasets to see if it also converges to the global optimum in practice.
