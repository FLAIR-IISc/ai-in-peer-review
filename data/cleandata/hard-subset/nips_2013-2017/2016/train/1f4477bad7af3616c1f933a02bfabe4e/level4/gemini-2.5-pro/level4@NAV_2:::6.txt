This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for training feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers. The proposed model uses generalized polynomial activation functions and is constrained to have non-negative weights and operate on non-negative data. The main contribution is a proof that, under verifiable conditions on the network architecture, the proposed iterative algorithm converges linearly to the unique global optimum of a modified objective function. This work builds upon a line of research aiming to provide global optimality guarantees for the highly non-convex problem of training neural networks. Unlike previous works such as Haeffele and Vidal [8] or Janzamin et al. [11], which are either difficult to apply in practice or rely on strong assumptions like knowing the data-generating distribution, this method presents a practically feasible algorithm with weaker requirements.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and practically feasible algorithm that guarantees global optimality for training a non-trivial class of neural networks. This is a significant theoretical contribution to a challenging and important problem in machine learning. The lack of tuning parameters like a learning rate is also a notable advantage over gradient-based methods.
*   **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical framework is rigorous, leveraging fixed-point theory (specifically, the Banach fixed-point theorem in a complete metric space endowed with the Thompson metric) to establish the existence, uniqueness, and convergence to the global optimum. The conditions for convergence are checkable a priori by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix, which is a strong and useful feature.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a proof-of-concept on several real-world UCI datasets. This is a crucial step forward, as prior work on globally optimal training methods has often lacked experiments on real data. The results, while not universally state-of-the-art, demonstrate that the model is expressive enough to be competitive with standard baselines like SVMs and ReLU networks on some tasks.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity of Presentation:** While the introduction is well-written, the technical presentation in Section 2 could be improved. The motivation behind the non-standard objective function $\Phi$ is not sufficiently explained. The paper would benefit from a clearer justification for the inclusion of the sum of all network outputs and the small $\epsilon$ term in the objective. Furthermore, Theorem 1 is dense and difficult to parse. Providing a high-level, informal explanation of the theorem's conditions and implications before the formal statement would greatly enhance readability.
*   **Expressiveness and Limitations:** The paper raises the important issue of the expressiveness of its constrained model but does not fully explore it. The constraints of non-negative weights and data are quite restrictive. A more in-depth discussion of the practical implications of these constraints would be valuable. For example, what types of functions or decision boundaries can this model represent well, and what are its fundamental limitations compared to unconstrained models like standard ReLU networks?
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental comparison, while a valuable first step, could be more comprehensive.
    *   The baselines are limited to SVM and SGD-trained ReLU networks. It would be insightful to contrast the proposed method with other advanced optimization techniques for neural networks.
    *   The paper would be strengthened by including information on the computational resources required (e.g., training time, memory usage) for the proposed method versus the baselines, as this is critical for assessing practical viability.
    *   The claim made after Theorem 1 that the method converges "typically in a few (less than 10) iterations" should be clarified to be an empirical observation rather than a direct consequence of the theoretical bounds, which can be conservative.

**Minor Points:**

*   The definition of the optimization set $S_+$ uses $p_w$ and $p_u$ without prior introduction.
*   The notation $R_{++}$ is used but not explicitly defined.
*   There is a typo in the introduction: "polyomial" should be "polynomial".
