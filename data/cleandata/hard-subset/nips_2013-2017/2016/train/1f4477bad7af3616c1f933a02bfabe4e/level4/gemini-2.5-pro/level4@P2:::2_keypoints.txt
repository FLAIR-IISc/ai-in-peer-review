- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method for a class of "Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks" with provable linear-rate convergence to the unique global optimum.
- Convergence guarantee depends on an a priori checkable spectral radius condition (ρ(A) < 1) of a small explicit matrix.
- Method developed for one- and two-hidden-layer networks under strong constraints: non-negative weights, non-negative inputs, generalized polynomial activations, and a modified objective.
- Theoretical contribution is original, linking neural network training to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory on convex cones.
- Rigorous technical development using fixed-point theory and the Thompson metric; the explicit spectral condition is elegant.
- Proposes a concrete, parameter-free iterative algorithm (GΦ) that is simple to implement and requires no learning rate tuning.
- Paper is well-written, clearly stating assumptions and proof strategy.
- Major limitation: highly restrictive model assumptions (non-negative weights/data and specific activations) limit applicability to standard neural networks.
- The modified objective (adds maximization of sum of outputs) is technically motivated but its impact on classification performance is not well-explored.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to small UCI datasets, raising scalability concerns as the spectral condition likely tightens with more hidden units.
- Baselines and comparisons are insufficient; NLSM is often outperformed by an RBF SVM and improvements over ReLU are marginal or inconsistent.
- Claim of modeling complex decision boundaries is supported mainly by a single 2D toy example, not by real-data evidence.
- Questions about tightening bounds on A to improve scalability and allow more hidden units remain unanswered.
- Need for systematic hyperparameter selection guidance, since ρ(A) < 1 depends on several hyperparameters found by random search.
- Suggests experiments applying standard optimizers (SGD/Adam) to the modified objective to disentangle the effects of objective vs. optimization method.
- Requests more details on baseline tuning (especially ReLU) to ensure fair comparisons.
- Conclusion: theoretically elegant and original but limited practical significance due to restrictive assumptions and unconvincing empirical results; needs better motivation and stronger empirical evidence.
