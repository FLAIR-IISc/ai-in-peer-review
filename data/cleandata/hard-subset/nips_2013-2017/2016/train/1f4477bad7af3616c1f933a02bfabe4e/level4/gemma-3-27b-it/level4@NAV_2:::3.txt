This paper presents a compelling theoretical framework demonstrating global optimality with a linear convergence rate for a specific class of feedforward neural networks. The key constraint is that data and network weights must be non-negative, and activation functions are generalized polynomials. The theory is initially proven for single-hidden-layer networks and then extended to networks with multiple layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments demonstrate promising results on synthetic and small real-world (UCI) datasets.

**Strengths:** As the authors claim, this work represents a significant step forward, being – to our knowledge – the first to propose a potentially practical neural network model with guaranteed global optimality and convergence. Prior work offering similar guarantees often involves preconditions that are complex to verify, or even intractable in practice, relying, for instance, on knowledge of the data-generating measure. In contrast, the presented theory hinges on relatively simple preconditions dependent on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix defined by network parameters. The authors also demonstrate the applicability of their approach to networks of arbitrary depth and showcase reasonable performance on small, real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:** The proposed network architecture currently faces limitations in broader applicability. The requirement for non-negative data and weights, alongside the use of generalized polynomial activation functions, and the constraint on the spectral radius (Theorem 1) restrict its use. While the non-negativity constraint on input data may not severely limit applicability – as data can often be shifted to be positive – the requirement for positive network weights could restrict model expressiveness. Furthermore, the necessity for distinct activation functions in all hidden units feels somewhat unnatural and potentially limits application. The authors mention adjustments to p1, p2, pw, and pu to satisfy the spectral radius constraint, potentially diminishing the weights of linear units.  This raises concerns about scaling to high-dimensional inputs and networks, where maintaining sufficient weight magnitude might become challenging. Specifically, if weights become very small, the activation functions may approximate linear behavior, reducing the benefit of adding more layers. This could hinder the method’s ability to scale to deep architectures.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The progression from the single-hidden-layer case to multiple layers is logical. The presented algorithm, while specialized, is clearly defined. However, the practical limitations related to the constraints on weights and activations should be discussed more thoroughly.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is standard and the explanations are relatively clear. However, certain sections, particularly those involving the spectral radius calculations, could benefit from further clarification to improve accessibility for a broader audience. Providing more intuitive explanations alongside the formal proofs would enhance understanding.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel approach to achieving global optimality in neural network training. The use of nonlinear spectral methods in this context is a significant contribution. The explicit connection to Perron-Frobenius theory provides a strong theoretical foundation. The work successfully differentiates itself from prior attempts by offering more practical preconditions for achieving global optimality.

**Significance:** The results are significant as they offer a potential pathway towards training neural networks with provable guarantees. Although the current constraints limit broad applicability, the theoretical insights gained could inform the development of more practical globally optimal training algorithms. The paper addresses a challenging problem and advances the state of the art, even if in a specialized setting. The unique theoretical approach and the accompanying convergence guarantee are valuable contributions to the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to globally optimal neural network training.
*   Provides a practical algorithm with a linear convergence guarantee.
*   Addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: the non-convexity of the optimization landscape.
*   Offers valuable insights into the relationship between network architecture and global optimality.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The restrictive constraints on data, weights, and activation functions limit the immediate applicability of the method.
*   Scaling to high-dimensional inputs and deep architectures remains an open question.
*   The theoretical bounds on the spectral radius may be conservative, requiring careful parameter tuning.

**Overall Assessment:** Despite the limitations, this is a valuable and encouraging contribution to the field. The theoretical results are strong, and the proposed method offers a promising avenue for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Minor comments:**

*   It would be beneficial to explore the relationship between the model parameters (p1, p2, pw, pu), input dimensionality, and the spectral radius ρ(A). Specifically, how does ρ(A) scale with increasing input dimension, and what adjustments to pw and pu are needed to maintain the convergence guarantee? While a rigorous proof may be challenging, empirical investigation through plotting this relationship on synthetic data could provide valuable insights.
*   A discussion of the potential impact of the non-negativity constraints on the expressiveness of the model would strengthen the paper.
*   Further investigation into the tightness of the bounds on pu and pw derived from the spectral radius condition would be useful.
