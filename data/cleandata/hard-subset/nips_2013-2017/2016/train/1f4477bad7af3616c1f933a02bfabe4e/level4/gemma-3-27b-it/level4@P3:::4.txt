This paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for globally optimally training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights – with a guaranteed linear convergence rate. The core idea is compelling: by restricting the problem to non-negative weights and a modified objective function, the authors leverage tools from Perron-Frobenius theory to achieve global optimality. This contrasts sharply with the typical challenges of non-convex optimization inherent in standard neural network training via stochastic gradient descent. The authors appropriately contextualize their work within the broader landscape of neural network optimization, citing recent efforts to provide global convergence guarantees [8, 11] and highlighting the limitations of those approaches (practicality [8] and reliance on data distribution estimation [11]). 

The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical contribution. Establishing a practical, globally optimal training algorithm, even for a constrained network architecture, is a significant achievement. The linear convergence guarantee is particularly noteworthy. The authors clearly articulate the conditions required for global optimality – the spectral radius of a relatively small matrix – which offers a computationally efficient check before training. The extension to two hidden layers, while briefly presented, demonstrates the potential for generalization.

However, several weaknesses raise concerns. The most significant limitation is the imposed non-negativity constraint on the network weights. While mathematically convenient for applying the spectral method, this restriction is substantial and likely impacts the expressiveness and performance of the network. The authors acknowledge this but do not fully explore the practical consequences. The choice of generalized polynomial activation functions, while allowing for non-negativity, is also non-standard and may limit the model’s ability to learn complex features compared to more commonly used activations like ReLU. The experimental results, while demonstrating competitive performance on some UCI datasets, do not unequivocally showcase an advantage over established methods like SVMs. In fact, performance is often slightly worse. The claim regarding the convergence rate being “typically in a few iterations” (around line 90-91) feels overstated, as linear convergence doesn't automatically translate to rapid convergence without knowing the constant factor.  The dependence on ρx (max norm of the input data) in the bounds and the practical implications of scaling the data should be discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical result: Globally optimal training with a linear convergence guarantee is a significant contribution.
*   Clear and well-defined conditions for optimality: The spectral radius check provides a practical way to ensure global convergence.
*   Potential for future generalization: The extension to two hidden layers suggests the possibility of scaling the method.
*   Addresses a fundamental challenge: Offers an alternative to the typical non-convex optimization challenges of neural network training.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong limitations: The non-negativity constraint and specialized activation functions significantly restrict the applicability and potential performance of the model.
*   Modest empirical results: The experiments do not demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   Overstated claims: The assertion about rapid convergence based on linear convergence is misleading.
*   Lack of detailed discussion on parameter sensitivity and scaling of the bounds.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting and novel approach to neural network training. However, the strong limitations of the method and the modest empirical results raise concerns about its practical impact. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the constrained nature of the solution hinders its broad applicability. I would rate this paper as **weak accept**.  The authors should be encouraged to address the limitations, explore methods to relax the non-negativity constraint, and provide a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods on a wider range of datasets. A more nuanced discussion of the convergence rate and the impact of data scaling is also recommended.
