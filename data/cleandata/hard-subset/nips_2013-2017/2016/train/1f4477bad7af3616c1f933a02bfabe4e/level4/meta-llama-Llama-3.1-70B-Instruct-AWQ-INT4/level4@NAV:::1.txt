This paper explores a specific class of neural networks, demonstrating that it possesses a single stationary point. The author presents an efficient method that converges to this unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The investigation focuses on an intriguing problem, where a complex model with carefully chosen constraints exhibits a single stationary point. Notably, this is achieved through fixed-point theory, a common approach for proving uniqueness of stationary points, aside from convexity and its generalizations. The author constructs a contractive map, whose fixed-point is the unique minimizer of the cost function. However, the proposed method has some limitations, including its reliance on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix for convergence proof. This constraint likely explains the authors' decision to avoid exploring networks with a larger number of hidden units. Furthermore, the method involves numerous parameters that require tuning, making the procedure somewhat counterintuitive. Additionally, the spectral condition must be verified for each parameter choice. In the experimental results, the authors evaluated approximately 150,000 different parameter combinations, selecting the best based on cross-validation accuracy. Nevertheless, their method outperformed linear SVM on only 2 out of 7 datasets, which raises questions about the true potential of the investigated neural network.
