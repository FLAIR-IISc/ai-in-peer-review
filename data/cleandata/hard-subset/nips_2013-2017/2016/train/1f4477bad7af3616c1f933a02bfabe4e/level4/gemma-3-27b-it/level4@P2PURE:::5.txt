The paper introduces a specific class of feedforward neural networks with a guaranteed global optimizer, and presents an algorithm to find this optimizer with a linear convergence rate. The mathematical derivations are generally insightful, though some steps lack clarity. Specifically, 1) Theorem 3 relies on first-order derivatives in its inequalities, while Theorem 4 progresses to second-order derivatives – the reason for this shift is unclear. 2) The network architecture appears to treat the final layer distinctly from the hidden layers. Given that *u* represents parameters connecting to the hidden layer, the gradient with respect to *u* in G<sup>Φ</sup> (3) seems to involve the gradient of the vectorization of *u*. (Similar questions arise when bounding *u* within a ball). A key concern is the motivation for maximizing the 1-norm of the connection parameters in (2), a strategy that contrasts with the weight decay typically employed in neural networks to *minimize* the 1-norm. This maximization appears to be solely to ensure a strictly positive gradient via the small epsilon in (2), enabling inclusion in V<sub>++</sub> – a condition seemingly driven by the proof rather than practical neural network considerations. It’s unclear whether this introduces any unconstrained free parameters. The requirement for non-negative data and the use of exclusively positive weights (preventing zero values) raise questions about the practical implications of this design and its potential for unbounded accumulation. The experimental comparison to SVM (which also possesses a global optimizer) is noted. However, the paper does not sufficiently address whether this specific network architecture offers advantages over more conventional neural networks (e.g., those utilizing ReLU) in practical applications.
