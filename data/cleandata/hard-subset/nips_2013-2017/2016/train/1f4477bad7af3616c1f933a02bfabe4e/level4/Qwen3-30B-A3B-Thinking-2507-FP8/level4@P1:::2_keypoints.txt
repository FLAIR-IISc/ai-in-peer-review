- Proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of certain feedforward networks with linear convergence guarantees.
- Technically strong: connects training to nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones and leverages Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems.
- Provides a detailed theoretical foundation and a proof (Theorem 1) that the method converges linearly under specific parameter choices.
- Restrictive assumptions: analysis limited to one- and two-hidden-layer networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations (not ReLU/sigmoid).
- Authors acknowledge but insufficiently justify or explore relaxation of these constraints for real-world applicability.
- Experimental baselines are weak/inappropriate: only compared to linear SVM despite NLSM being nonlinear (no nonlinear SVM or standard NN baselines).
- Empirical results (Table 1) show linear SVM outperforming NLSM, undermining the claim of competitive performance without reporting nonlinear baselines.
- Datasets used are small and low-dimensional, raising concerns about scalability given that spectral-radius bounds grow with hidden units.
- Practical feasibility of the global optimality condition is unclear: no efficient procedure for finding parameters that ensure ρ(A) < 1 and the condition appears sensitive.
- Comparison with SGD shows faster convergence in experiments, but lacks explanation for why linear convergence holds and whether it scales to larger datasets.
- Overall: important theoretical contribution but limited practical impact due to restrictive assumptions, weak experiments, and insufficient discussion of implementation and scalability.
