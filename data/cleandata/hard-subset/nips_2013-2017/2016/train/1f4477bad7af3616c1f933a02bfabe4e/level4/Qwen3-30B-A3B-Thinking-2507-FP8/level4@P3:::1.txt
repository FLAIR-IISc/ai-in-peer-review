

This paper addresses the challenging problem of achieving globally optimal training for neural networks, which is typically non-convex and difficult to solve. The authors present a novel approach for a specific class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and a modified objective function. They show that under certain conditions, their nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) achieves global optimality with linear convergence rates. This work is significant because it represents one of the first practically feasible methods for globally optimal neural network training, contrasting with previous approaches that either lack practical applicability [8] or require knowledge of the data-generating distribution [11].

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it establishes a theoretically grounded method for globally optimal training of neural networks with one or two hidden layers, which is a significant advancement over previous work that either provides only theoretical guarantees without practical algorithms [8, 11] or focuses on specific architectures without global optimality guarantees [15]. Second, the authors develop a rigorous mathematical framework using fixed-point theory and Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices to prove global convergence. Third, they provide empirical validation on several UCI datasets, showing that their method achieves competitive performance compared to standard approaches.

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:

Quality: The theoretical contribution is strong, with a well-structured proof that establishes global optimality under reasonable assumptions. The authors carefully address the conditions required for their method to work, particularly the spectral radius condition. The experimental section is generally sound, though the parameter search is extensive (150,000 combinations), which raises questions about practicality. The paper is technically complete, with proofs for intermediate results relegated to supplementary material as appropriate.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, though the notation can be dense at times. The main theorem (Theorem 1) is particularly complex, and the explanation of the nonlinear spectral method could benefit from a more intuitive presentation. The connection between the mathematical framework and practical implementation is somewhat obscured by the technical details. The authors could improve clarity by providing a more intuitive explanation of why the nonnegativity constraint and modified objective function are necessary.

Originality: The work is highly original in its approach to achieving global optimality for neural networks. While previous work has explored similar ideas [8, 11], this paper is the first to provide a practically feasible algorithm with provable global optimality guarantees. The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior art, noting that previous approaches either require knowledge of the data-generating distribution or are challenging to apply in practice. The specific use of nonnegative weights and the modified objective function represents a novel constraint that enables their theoretical results.

Significance: The significance of this work is somewhat limited by the restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions). While the authors demonstrate competitive performance on several datasets, the results are modest compared to standard deep learning methods. The paper shows that global optimality can be achieved for a restricted class of networks, but it's unclear how this translates to more general neural network architectures. The experimental results show NLSM outperforms SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets, which questions the practical significance of the theoretical contribution. The method's reliance on verifying spectral conditions for each model configuration also limits its practical utility.

Strengths:
- Theoretical contribution is strong with rigorous proof of global optimality
- First practically feasible method for globally optimal training of neural networks
- The paper is well-structured and technically sound
- The method has linear convergence rate, making it computationally efficient

Weaknesses:
- The nonnegativity constraint on weights is quite restrictive and limits the expressiveness of the model
- The need to verify spectral conditions for each model configuration adds significant practical burden
- The parameter search procedure (150,000 combinations) is excessive and not scalable
- Empirical results are modest, with only modest gains over standard methods
- The method's applicability is limited to low-dimensional datasets due to the growth of the spectral radius with hidden units

The authors make a compelling theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of neural network optimization. However, the practical limitations of their approach (nonnegative weights, need to verify spectral conditions, limited empirical performance) somewhat diminish the significance of the work for the broader machine learning community. The paper would be significantly strengthened by either demonstrating more substantial empirical gains, relaxing the nonnegativity constraint, or providing a more scalable method for verifying the spectral conditions.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution, first practically feasible method for globally optimal training, rigorous proof with clear conditions
Con: Restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights), modest empirical results, impractical parameter search procedure, limited scalability

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental question in neural network optimization with a novel theoretical approach. However, the practical limitations should be more thoroughly discussed in the revision to provide a balanced assessment of the method's value. The authors should clarify why their constrained model is still useful despite the restrictions, and whether their method could be extended to more general architectures. The experimental section could also better address the computational cost of verifying the spectral conditions for each model configuration.
