- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method with provable global convergence at a linear rate for a restricted class of feedforward networks.  
- Convergence guarantee depends on verifiable conditions (notably ρ(A) < 1) computed from a small matrix.  
- Frames training via Perron–Frobenius theory as finding a unique fixed point of a contraction mapping.  
- Extends theory from one to two hidden layers and demonstrates proof-of-concept experiments on UCI datasets.

Strengths:
- Novel theoretical connection between neural network training and nonlinear eigenproblems/fixed-point theory.  
- Rigorous, explicit convergence conditions and linear convergence rate that can be checked a priori.  
- Parameter-free iterative algorithm (no learning rates or usual hyperparameters).  
- Clear presentation and well-explained proof strategy.

Weaknesses:
- Strongly restrictive assumptions (non-negative weights and data) that limit applicability and expressive power.  
- Non-standard architecture choices (generalized polynomial activations with per-unit exponents and added sum-of-outputs term) deviate from common practice.  
- Empirical results are often outperformed by standard baselines (RBF SVM, ReLU networks), reducing practical impact.  
- Scalability concerns: ρ(A) tends to grow with hidden units, limiting network width and high-dimensional applicability.

Detailed comments:
- Claim that linear convergence implies convergence in “a few (<10) iterations” is misleading without reporting typical ρ(A) values.  
- Authors should clarify how the added sum-of-outputs term affects the learned decision boundary versus standard losses.  
- Sensitivity to the choice of per-unit exponents α_l is unexplored; current random-search selection may be suboptimal.

Summary arguments:
- For acceptance: novel, deep theoretical contribution; practical algorithm with provable global optimality; deterministic and hyperparameter-free; well-written.  
- For rejection: extremely restrictive assumptions, uncompetitive empirical performance, and clear scalability limitations.

Overall:
- Interesting and theoretically strong work, but significant gap between elegant theory and practical utility due to severe modeling and scalability restrictions.
