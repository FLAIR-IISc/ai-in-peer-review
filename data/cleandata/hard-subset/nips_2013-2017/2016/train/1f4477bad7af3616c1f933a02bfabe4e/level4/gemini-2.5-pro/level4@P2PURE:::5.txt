This paper introduces a specific class of feedforward neural networks for which a unique global optimum can be found. The authors propose an algorithm that finds this global optimizer and prove that it has a linear convergence rate.

The mathematical derivations are generally instructive; however, several parts would benefit from further clarification:

1.  Theorem 3 involves first-order derivative conditions, whereas Theorem 4 uses second-order derivatives. The motivation for this transition is not explained.

2.  The network design appears to treat the final layer's parameters differently from the hidden layer's. For the hidden layer weight matrix `u`, is the gradient in G^\Phi (3) calculated with respect to the vectorization of `u`? Similar questions about the handling of this matrix arise in other parts of the paper, such as when `u` is constrained to a ball.

I also have some concerns regarding the model's design and practical implications:

A primary concern is the maximization of the L1-norm of the connection parameters in equation (2). This is contrary to the common practice of using weight decay (e.g., L1 regularization) to *reduce* this norm and prevent overfitting. It appears this choice, along with the small epsilon, is a mathematical convenience needed to ensure the gradient is strictly positive (i.e., in V_++) for the proofs to hold. The paper would be stronger if it provided a clear practical or theoretical justification for this unconventional objective beyond its utility for the convergence proof.

Furthermore, the model's constraints seem highly restrictive. It requires non-negative input data and learns strictly positive weights, which means the output will increase monotonically with the inputs. This raises questions about the practical applicability of such a design.

Regarding the experiments, the proposed network is compared with SVMs, likely because SVMs also have a convex objective function with a global optimum. However, to demonstrate the practical value of this new architecture, it is crucial to compare it against more commonly used neural networks, such as those with ReLU activations. Such a comparison would help clarify what, if any, practical advantages the guarantee of a global optimum provides over these standard, empirically successful models.
