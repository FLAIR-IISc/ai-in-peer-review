- Novel approach: globally optimal training for generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights via a nonlinear spectral method.  
- Theoretical contribution: rigorous analysis with provable linear convergence (Theorem 1 and Theorem 5) based on a spectral radius condition.  
- Core idea: enforce non-negativity and modify the objective to leverage nonlinear spectral theory for guaranteed global convergence.  
- Demonstration: applied to one- and two-hidden-layer networks and evaluated on several UCI datasets showing feasibility.  
- Restrictive assumptions: non-negativity constraints and generalized polynomial activations limit applicability and model expressiveness.  
- Activation concern: generalized polynomial activations are unconventional and need justification beyond mathematical convenience.  
- Weak empirical evidence: experiments do not convincingly show competitive performance.  
- Questionable baselines: comparison to a linear SVM is inappropriate; non-linear kernel SVMs and stronger baselines are needed.  
- Dataset limitations: used small, low-dimensional datasets that do not demonstrate scalability or expressiveness.  
- Parameter sensitivity: parameter selection is difficult and small changes can cause large performance variations.  
- Scalability concerns: spectral radius may grow with number of hidden units, threatening scalability and practicality.  
- Need for clarification: authors should propose strategies to mitigate spectral-radius growth and efficiently explore parameter configurations that satisfy convergence conditions.  
- Additional experiments suggested: evaluate standard SGD on the modified objective and test on larger, more complex datasets.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, theoretical rigor, clear algorithm, and potential impact if scalability is addressed.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, limited empirical support, inappropriate baselines, and scalability issues.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept contingent on addressing empirical evaluation and scalability concerns.
