

This paper investigates a specialized class of neural networks for which the authors establish the existence of a single stationary point. They introduce a fast algorithm converging to the unique fixed point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The work addresses an interesting problem: demonstrating that a relatively complex model, with carefully chosen constraints, can possess only one stationary point. Beyond convexity or its generalizations (e.g., geodesic convexity), fixed-point theory is a standard approach for proving stationary point uniqueness, and the paper employs this method effectively. Specifically, the authors construct a contractive map whose fixed point coincides with the unique minimizer of the cost function.  

A significant limitation is the dependence of the convergence proof on the boundedness of the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix. As noted by the authors, this constraint prevents them from exploring networks with larger numbers of hidden units. Additionally, the method requires tuning numerous parameters, rendering the procedure non-intuitive. Crucially, for each parameter configuration, the spectral condition must be explicitly verified. The experimental section describes testing approximately 150,000 parameter combinations, selecting the optimal configuration via cross-validation accuracy. However, the proposed method outperforms linear SVMs on only 2 out of 7 datasets. This limited empirical success casts doubt on the practical utility of the investigated neural network architecture.
