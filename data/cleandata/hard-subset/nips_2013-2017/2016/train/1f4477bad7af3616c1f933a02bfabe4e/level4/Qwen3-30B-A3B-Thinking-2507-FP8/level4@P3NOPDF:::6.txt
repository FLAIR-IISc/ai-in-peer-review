

The paper introduces a theoretically grounded method for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks under certain assumptions, proving optimal convergence rates and demonstrating applicability on select "real-world" problems. The theoretical contribution is valuable, and the problem domain is indeed interesting. However, the paper falls short in establishing the *practical utility* of the proposed restricted model, which is a critical gap for a machine learning submission.  

The authors raise the expressiveness limitations of their model twice but provide no meaningful discussion of what tasks are feasible or infeasible under their constraints. Without addressing this, the practical relevance of the method remains unclear. For instance, how does the model compare to standard neural networks on tasks where expressiveness is critical? The authors should explicitly state the scope of applicability and limitations—e.g., "This approach is suitable for [X] but not for [Y] due to [Z]."  

More critically, the experimental evaluation is insufficient. The authors compare only to SVMs, ignoring both contemporary neural network training methods (e.g., SGD, Adam) and broader algorithmic paradigms (e.g., kernel methods, ensemble approaches). This omission obscures whether the proposed method offers a *competitive advantage* in practice. The reader cannot assess if the method is merely a theoretical curiosity or a practical alternative. Additionally, the computational resources (e.g., training time, memory) for all compared methods are unreported, making it impossible to evaluate efficiency trade-offs.  

The introduction is clear, but Section 2 requires substantial revision for fluency and precision. Key technical issues include:  
- **Undefined notation**: $R_{++}$ (used on p.67) is not introduced, creating confusion with other variables.  
- **Ambiguous definitions**: The role of $\epsilon$ in $\Phi$ lacks motivation; the rebuttal’s explanation (that $\epsilon$ controls approximation error) must be integrated into the main text.  
- **Theorem 1**: The formal statement is difficult to parse. An informal explanation of the intuition *before* the theorem is essential. The subsequent claim that "the nonlinear spectral method converges quickly (typically <10 iterations) to the global maximum" is presented as a general fact but stems solely from experimental observations—this must be clarified as empirical, not theoretical.  
- **Typos**: "polyomial" (p.60), "one CAN model" (p.67), and missing definitions for $p_1$, $p_2$ (p.67) undermine credibility.  

The paper’s core weakness lies in conflating theoretical novelty with practical significance. While the convergence proof is rigorous, the experimental section fails to answer the central question: *Why should practitioners adopt this method?* The limited comparisons and lack of computational context prevent a meaningful assessment. For example, if the method requires 10× more time than SGD to achieve similar accuracy, its utility is questionable. Conversely, if it scales better to large datasets, this should be highlighted.  

Minor writing improvements are also needed. Phrases like "the authors prove optimal convergence" should specify *convergence rates* (e.g., "proves $O(1/t)$ convergence"). The rebuttal clarified several points, but the paper itself must incorporate these to avoid reader confusion. The statement on p.67 about $p_1$ and $p_2$ should explicitly define these terms upon first use.  

In summary, the paper makes a valid theoretical contribution but does not convincingly demonstrate its practical value. To strengthen the work, the authors must:  
1. Explicitly address expressiveness limitations and their implications for real-world tasks.  
2. Expand experiments to include relevant baselines (e.g., standard NN optimizers, non-SVM methods) and report computational costs.  
3. Revise Section 2 for clarity, define all symbols, and contextualize Theorem 1’s intuition.  
4. Integrate rebuttal clarifications (e.g., $\epsilon$’s role, convergence claims) into the main text.  

The theoretical advance is appreciated, but without resolving these gaps, the paper’s impact is limited. As a reviewer, I recommend *major revision* to address these issues before acceptance.
