This paper introduces a method for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks under a defined set of assumptions, accompanied by a proof of optimal convergence and demonstration on several “real-world” problems. The investigated problem is interesting, and theoretical advances in this area are welcome. However, the paper does not fully establish the practical utility of the proposed network architecture. The authors acknowledge limitations in the expressiveness of their restricted model – a point raised on multiple occasions – and I recommend including a discussion of potential workarounds and inherent constraints of their approach. Furthermore, a more thorough comparison with concurrent work is needed, contrasting the proposed method with existing alternatives. 

The experimental results do not adequately address the limitations of the model’s applicability. I found the comparison limited, focusing primarily on SVMs while omitting other relevant methods, including alternative training techniques for neural networks and different algorithmic paradigms. Reporting computational resource requirements for each algorithm would also be valuable. While the introduction is well-written, the subsequent presentation could be improved; specifically, Section 2 lacks clarity and fluency. Several typos were identified (detailed below).

Regarding specific points: while the loss function is clearly defined, the function $\Phi$ is not sufficiently motivated; the explanation provided in the rebuttal regarding the role of the epsilon term should be incorporated into the paper. Theorem 1 would benefit from an informal explanation preceding its formal statement, as the current presentation is difficult to parse. The statement immediately following Theorem 1 – claiming that the nonlinear spectral method typically converges quickly to the global maximum in a few iterations – is based on experimental observation, as clarified in the rebuttal, and this should be explicitly stated.

Specific errors: Line 60: “polyomial” should be “polynomial”. Line 67: “one CAN model” should be “one can model”. Line 67: p1 and p2 are not previously defined. Line 67: R_{++} is not defined, which is problematic given its use in defining other variables; clarification, as provided in the rebuttal, would be helpful.
