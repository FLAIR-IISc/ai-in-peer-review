- Presents a nonlinear spectral method that yields globally optimal training for a class of feedforward networks with nonnegative inputs/weights and generalized polynomial activations.
- Shows global optimality reduces to checking the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix, a computationally feasible pre-check (must be < 1).
- Develops theory for single-hidden-layer networks and extends guarantees to two-hidden-layer architectures with linear convergence rates.
- Builds on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and provides technically sound proofs, leveraging recent multihomogeneous results.
- Practical advantage over prior global-optimality work: condition depends on architecture parameters only, not unknown data-generating measures.
- Experimental results on UCI datasets show competitive performance versus ReLU networks and SVMs while providing theoretical guarantees.
- Main strengths: first practically feasible global-optimality method, simple spectral check, multi-layer extension, competitive empirical performance, and linear convergence.
- Main weakness: nonnegativity constraints on inputs and weights limit applicability and require preprocessing for many datasets.
- Main weakness: requirement for distinct activation exponents across hidden units is unnatural and may restrict expressiveness.
- Main weakness: spectral radius condition forces trade-offs between depth/size and parameter scaling, potentially limiting scalability to deeper networks.
- Main weakness: paper lacks detailed analysis of how spectral radius depends on input dimension and network parameters.
- Main limitation: strong architectural constraints underpin theoretical guarantees; unclear how readily these can be relaxed for broader applications.
- Originality: novel bridging of nonlinear spectral methods and neural network training with global guarantees; polynomial activations used in prior work but global-optimality here is new.
- Significance: important theoretical advance toward globally optimal training, though practical impact may be limited by constraints.
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, meaningful theoretical contribution, practically verifiable conditions, and competitive experiments.
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions (nonnegativity, distinct exponents) and unclear scalability of the spectral radius condition to more complex/deeper models.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address scalability concerns and provide more discussion/evidence on spectral radius behavior and broader applicability.
