Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The models have nonnegative weights and use generalized polynomial activations; the training objective mixes the (negative) loss with a positivity-promoting term on the outputs and a small positive bias on weights for technical reasons. The main result shows that, under a condition expressed as the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix being less than one, the fixed-point iteration converges linearly to a unique global optimum. The condition can be checked a priori and depends on architecture and norm parameters. The framework extends from one to two hidden layers, with similar guarantees. The analysis leverages nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, multihomogeneous maps, and the Thompson metric. Experiments on several UCI datasets are presented as proof-of-concept.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to recent theoretical efforts on globally optimal training for neural networks. Compared with Haeffele & Vidal (structured factorization) and Janzamin et al. (tensor methods), the authors argue their approach is more practical: it avoids estimating score-function tensors, requires only nonnegativity of the data, and reduces the global optimality condition to a spectral radius check. The work also relates to results on polynomial networks and nonnegative matrix theory.

Strengths
- A clear, rigorous optimization framework that yields global optimality with linear convergence under explicit, checkable conditions.
- Elegant use of fixed-point theory, with an a priori computable contraction factor and no learning-rate tuning.
- Extension to two hidden layers and discussion of parameter dependencies (e.g., how the spectral radius grows with architectural size).
- The experiments, while modest, demonstrate that the method can train nontrivial classifiers and converge quickly in practice.

Weaknesses and questions
- Modeling constraints are strong: nonnegative inputs and weights, generalized polynomial activations, and a modified objective that adds the sum of outputs and a small positive bias. The paper would benefit from deeper discussion of practical scenarios where these assumptions are natural (e.g., counts, images with nonnegative preprocessing) and how much they limit applicability.
- Expressivity and empirical validation are not fully convincing. Datasets are small and low-dimensional; results are mixed against strong baselines. The method’s scalability is unclear, especially since the bounds imply the spectral radius (and thus contraction factor) worsens with more hidden units.
- Baselines: The paper compares with RBF-kernel SVM and ReLU networks trained by SGD with cross-validated hyperparameters. Additional baselines could strengthen the claims, such as polynomial-kernel SVM (given the polynomial structure), nonnegative/constrained NNs, or ablations of the modified objective and nonnegativity constraints.
- Practical parameter selection: Although the spectral condition can be checked without running the algorithm and large pw, pu can theoretically ensure contraction, the paper offers limited guidance on efficiently finding feasible, well-performing settings in larger models. The current reliance on random search with conservative bounds may be impractical as dimensionality grows.
- Sensitivity and ablations: The method relies on distinct α exponents to avoid triviality, and on the extra output-sum term and epsilon for strict positivity. The paper would be stronger with ablations quantifying sensitivity to α choices, the magnitude of the added terms, and the trade-off between contraction guarantees and predictive performance.
- Handling general data: For inputs with negative values, what preprocessing or reparameterization is recommended? Are sign-splitting or offsetting strategies viable without breaking the guarantees?
- Clarity and presentation: The theoretical development is thorough but dense. Some intuition (e.g., why the output-sum term ensures strict positivity of the gradient, how the multihomogeneous structure induces the contraction) and improved organization would help. Several references appear incomplete; please fix bibliographic details.

Quality
The mathematical development appears sound, with careful conditions and a complete fixed-point argument. The proofs rely on conservative bounds, which likely limit empirical scalability; discussing tighter bounds or empirical estimates of the effective Lipschitz constant would help. The experimental evidence supports feasibility and fast convergence but is insufficient to establish broad practical significance.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized but heavy on technicalities. Clearer explanation of the modeling choices (nonnegativity, generalized polynomials, modified objective) and their practical implications would improve accessibility. Ensure notation is consistent and references are complete.

Originality
The approach is original in combining nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory with neural network training to yield global optimality and linear convergence for a nontrivial architecture class. The explicit a priori condition and fixed-point iteration are novel contributions.

Significance
The theoretical contribution is interesting and potentially influential. However, the practical impact is uncertain due to the restrictive assumptions, conservative bounds, and limited empirical scope. Demonstrating effectiveness on larger, higher-dimensional, and naturally nonnegative datasets (e.g., text counts, histograms, nonnegative image features), along with tighter empirical bounds, would raise the significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, clean theory with global optimality and linear convergence.
- Simple, parameter-free iteration (no learning rate).
- Potentially broad implications for optimization under structural constraints.

Cons for acceptance
- Restrictive assumptions and modified objective limit applicability.
- Modest and low-dimensional experimental validation; mixed performance vs. strong baselines.
- Limited guidance on scalable parameter selection and feasibility checks.

Suggestions
- Provide practical recipes for choosing pw, pu, α, and architecture to satisfy ρ(A) < 1 while maintaining accuracy; include heuristics that narrow the search space.
- Add experiments on larger, nonnegative datasets and report empirical contraction factors and observed spectral radii.
- Include ablations isolating the effect of nonnegativity, the output-sum term, and α distinctness on both guarantees and performance.
- Consider additional baselines aligned with polynomial structure or nonnegative constraints, and discuss preprocessing for general (signed) inputs.

Overall evaluation
A thought-provoking theoretical contribution with promising guarantees but limited practical validation. Strengthening the empirical section and providing clearer guidance on modeling/practical choices would significantly improve the paper.
