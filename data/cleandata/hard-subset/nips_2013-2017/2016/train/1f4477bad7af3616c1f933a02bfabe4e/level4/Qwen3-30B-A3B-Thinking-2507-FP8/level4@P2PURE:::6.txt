

The paper proposes a method to train, under specific assumptions, a particular class of feedforward neural networks. The authors establish optimal convergence rates and demonstrate applicability to a selection of real-world problems. The problem investigated is interesting, and theoretical advances in this direction are welcome. However, the paper fails to convincingly establish the practical utility of the proposed neural network class. Specifically, since the authors raise the expressiveness limitations of their restricted model (mentioning this twice), I recommend adding discussion on achievable tasks and inherent limitations of their approach. Concurrent methods should also be compared and contrasted. The experimental results do not clarify the model's applicability boundaries: I was surprised to find the authors compare only against SVMs, ignoring both alternative neural network training methods and other algorithmic paradigms. Additionally, computational resource requirements for the three algorithms should be reported. The introduction is well written, but the presentation requires improvement. Section 2 lacks fluency. Typos and notation issues are present:  
- Line 60: "polyomial" → "polynomial"  
- Line 67: "one CAN model" (should be "one CAN model" but context unclear)  
- Line 67: Variables p₁ and p₂ are undefined  
- Line 67: R_{++} is used without definition (critical, as multiple variables reference it)  
While the loss function definition is straightforward, the motivation for function Φ is lacking; the role of ε (explained in the rebuttal) should be incorporated into the paper. Theorem 1 should be preceded by an informal explanation. The statement itself is difficult to parse. Below Theorem 1, the claim that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum" stems from experimental observation (per the rebuttal) and should be clarified as such.
