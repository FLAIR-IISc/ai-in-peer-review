This paper presents a compelling theoretical result demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks possesses a globally optimal solution achievable with a linear convergence rate. The key constraint is that input data and the weights of the linear layers must be positive, and activation functions are generalized polynomials. The theory is initially proven for single-hidden-layer networks and subsequently extended to networks with multiple hidden layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on the nonlinear spectral method. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed algorithm performs well on a small, synthetic 2D dataset and achieves reasonable performance on several small, real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

**Pros:** As the authors claim, this work is the first to propose a potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees. Prior work offering convergence guarantees often requires complex and impractical precondition checks, sometimes even dependent on the data-generating measure. In contrast, the theory presented here has comparatively simple preconditions, relying only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix comprised of the network’s parameters. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate the applicability of their theory to networks of arbitrary depth and provide promising results on small, real-world datasets.

**Cons:** The proposed network architecture currently has limited applicability. The theory requires positive input data and network parameters, generalized polynomial activation functions, and a spectral radius (as defined in Theorem 1) less than 1. While the non-negativity constraint on input data is unlikely to severely limit usage – most data can be shifted to satisfy this condition – the requirement for positive network parameters may restrict the model’s expressiveness. The stipulation that all activation functions in each hidden layer must be distinct is also somewhat unnatural and could potentially hinder application. Finally, the authors note that satisfying the spectral radius constraint necessitates increasing *p1* and *p2* while decreasing *pw* and *pu*, effectively reducing the weights of the linear units. If I understand correctly, this implies that as the input dimension and network parameter size increase, the linear weights must decrease. When these linear weights become very small, the activation functions approximate linear functions, and adding more layers simply adds additional linear transformations, diminishing the network’s expressive power. Therefore, I am somewhat concerned about the scalability of this method to deep architectures. 

Despite these concerns, this remains an encouraging initial attempt, and I believe the paper should be accepted.

**Minor Comments:** I am curious about the relationship between *p1*, *p2*, *pw*, *pu*, the input dimension, the size of the network parameters, and the spectral radius *ρ(A)*. For instance, how does *ρ(A)* scale with increasing input dimension – linearly or exponentially? And correspondingly, should *pw* and *pu* be decreased linearly or exponentially to counteract this effect? I understand a formal proof may be challenging, but it might be possible to empirically plot this relationship using synthetic data.
