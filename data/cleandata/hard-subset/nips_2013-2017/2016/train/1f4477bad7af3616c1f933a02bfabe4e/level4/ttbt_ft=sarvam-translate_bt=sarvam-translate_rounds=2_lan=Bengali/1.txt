This paper discusses a specific class of neural networks where the author successfully demonstrates that it has only one unique point. The author presents a rapid method that converges to a unique specific point, which is the global minimum of the cost function. This paper addresses an interesting problem of how to cleverly select constraints to show a relatively complex model converges to only one specific point. In addition to convexity (or its generalization, geodesic convexity), a common method for showing the uniqueness of a specific point is to use specific-point theory; this method is used in this paper. The author creates a compressive map, whose specific point is the unique minimizer of the cost function. A disadvantage of the proposed method is the dependence of the convergence proof on the spectral radius constraint of some non-trivial matrices. The authors note that this is why they did not attempt to use a network with a larger number of hidden units. Another problem with the method is the many parameters that need to be tuned, which makes the use of the method quite intuitive. Furthermore, spectral conditions should be verified for each choice of parameter. In the experimental results, the authors tried approximately 150,000 different combinations of possible parameter values and chose the best among them based on cross-validation accuracy. However, their method could only outperform linear-SVM for 2 out of 7 datasets. This result raises questions about the true capabilities of the neural network they are studying.
