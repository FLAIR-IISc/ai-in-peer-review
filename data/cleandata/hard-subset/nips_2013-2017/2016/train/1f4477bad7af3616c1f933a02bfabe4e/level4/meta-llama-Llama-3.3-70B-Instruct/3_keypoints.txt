- Introduces a theoretical framework showing a class of neural networks attains a global optimum with linear convergence when inputs, linear-layer weights, and generalized polynomial activations are all positive.  
- Theory developed first for single-hidden-layer networks and then generalized to arbitrary-depth networks.  
- Proposes an optimization algorithm based on the nonlinear spectral method.  
- Experiments: strong performance on a 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable results on several small UCI datasets.  
- Major advantage: first potentially practical neural network with both optimality and convergence guarantees.  
- Preconditions are simpler than prior work, depending only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix built from network parameters.  
- Demonstrates applicability to networks of arbitrary depth and shows promising small-scale empirical results.  
- Major limitation: requires nonnegative inputs and positive network parameters, which may restrict expressiveness.  
- Requires generalized polynomial activations and seemingly different activations across hidden layers, an unnatural constraint that may limit applicability.  
- To satisfy the spectral-radius <1 constraint authors reduce linear weights (increase p1,p2, decrease pw,pu), which can make activations nearly linear and limit depth expressiveness.  
- Raises empirical question of how p1,p2,pw,pu, input dimension, and network size affect the spectral radius (suggests plotting this relationship); overall contributions outweigh limitations and paper is worthy of acceptance.
