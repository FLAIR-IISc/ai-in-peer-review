The paper introduces a method that, under certain assumptions, trains a specific class of feed-forward neural networks to global optimality. The authors provide convergence guarantees and demonstrate applicability on several “real-world” tasks. The problem is compelling, and theoretical progress in this direction is valuable. However, the paper does not fully convince me that the proposed network class is practically useful. Since the authors themselves raise the question of expressiveness (twice), I recommend adding a clear discussion of what the model can and cannot capture. Competing approaches should also be discussed and contrasted. The empirical results likewise do not delineate the boundaries of applicability of the model.

In the experiments, I was surprised to see comparisons limited to a single SVM baseline, with no comparisons to other neural training schemes or alternative algorithmic paradigms. I also expected a report of computational resources for all three methods.

The introduction is well written, but later parts of the presentation could be improved. Section 2, in particular, does not read smoothly. There are also some typographical issues; I list a few below.

Specific remarks:
- While the loss is defined straightforwardly, the objective Φ lacks motivation: the role of the epsilon term was clarified in the rebuttal; this explanation should be included in the paper.
- Theorem 1 should be preceded by an informal explanation. The statement itself is difficult to parse.
- Immediately after Theorem 1: the broad claim that “the nonlinear spectral method … converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum” was, per the rebuttal, based on empirical observation; please make this explicit.

Typos/notation:
- Line 60: “polyomial”
- Line 67: “one CAN model.”
- Line 67: p1 and p2 are not introduced.
- Line 67: R_{++} is not introduced, which is problematic since several other definitions refer to it. Thanks for clarifying this in the rebuttal; I had not seen this notation before.
