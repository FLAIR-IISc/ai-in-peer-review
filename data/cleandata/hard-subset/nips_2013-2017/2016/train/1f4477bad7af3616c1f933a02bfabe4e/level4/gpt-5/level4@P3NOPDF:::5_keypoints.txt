- Summary: Paper defines a restricted feedforward NN class with a unique global optimizer and gives an algorithm with provable linear convergence, but modeling choices and experiments are unclear.  
- Strength: Clear goal of identifying a nontrivial NN class admitting a unique global optimum and efficient optimization.  
- Strength: Theoretical contribution of a constructive algorithm with linear convergence is valuable if assumptions hold.  
- Strength: Derivations reveal structure enabling global optimization and are instructive.  

- Weakness: Modeling constraints are highly restrictive (non-negative inputs, strictly positive weights, sometimes large L1 norms), producing monotone “only additive” networks that likely reduce expressivity.  
- Weakness: Objective includes maximizing L1 norm of connection parameters, which contradicts typical regularization and appears technically motivated rather than statistically.  
- Weakness: Practicality concerns: enforcing positivity and pushing parameters to large norms may harm generalization, sparsity, interpretability, and sensitivity to feature scaling.  
- Weakness: Empirical comparisons are limited (mostly SVM); missing baselines like standard ReLU MLPs and other monotone/nonnegative architectures.  
- Weakness: Some analysis steps jump derivative order without motivation and mix matrix vs vectorized treatments unclearly.

- Major question 1: Explain why Theorem 4 requires second-order analysis (e.g., to prove PL/curvature) and whether second-order quantities are uniformly bounded and which constants the rate depends on.  
- Major question 2: Clarify parameterization/gradients: is ∇u with respect to vec(u)? Define norms/inner products (Frobenius vs vector L2) and specify whether ball constraints use Frobenius or vector norms.  
- Major question 3: Justify maximizing L1 in (2); could positivity be enforced by lower bounds or reparameterization instead; report sensitivity/ablations for ε and whether ε affects the solution.  
- Major question 4: Address realism of non-negative inputs and strictly positive weights, possible preprocessing to preserve labels, expressivity lost (ability to represent subtractive interactions), and any scaling/identifiability ambiguities.  
- Major question 5: Provide explicit constants for the linear rate, state data-dependence, and compare per-iteration computational cost to standard backprop including any extra projections/normalizations.  
- Major question 6: Justify baseline choices, compare to ReLU MLPs and monotone models, report model sizes/hyperparameters/training time, and test robustness across datasets, noise, and feature scalings; quantify any stability or interpretability benefits.

- Suggestion: State all assumptions up front (data non-negativity, weight positivity, norm constraints, allowance of zeros) and discuss representable vs non-representable functions.  
- Suggestion: Make parameter-space geometry precise—consistently use Frobenius or vectorized norms across theorems and algorithms.  
- Suggestion: Replace or better-motivate L1 maximization; consider reparameterizing weights via exponentials with layer normalization to ensure positivity without unbounded norms.  
- Suggestion: Provide ablations on ε, strict vs non-strict positivity, and removal of L1-maximization to separate theory-necessary vs practice-necessary components.  
- Suggestion: Expand experiments to modern baselines, report statistical significance, and demonstrate that global optimality/linear convergence yields practical benefits (wall-clock, stability, predictive performance).

- Minor: Add intuition why second-order terms appear in Theorem 4 but not Theorem 3.  
- Minor: Include pseudocode with all projection/normalization steps and explicit per-layer constraints.  
- Minor: Fix notation around G^Φ and ensure symbol consistency across sections.

- Overall: Intriguing theoretical direction but needs stronger justification for modeling choices, clearer derivative-based exposition, and more compelling empirical evidence against relevant modern baselines.
