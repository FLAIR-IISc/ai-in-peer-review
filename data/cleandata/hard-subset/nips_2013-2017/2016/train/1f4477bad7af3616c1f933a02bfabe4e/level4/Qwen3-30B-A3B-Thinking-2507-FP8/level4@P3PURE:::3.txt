

This paper presents a novel theoretical contribution demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks achieves global optimality with linear convergence rates under certain constraints. The theory is first established for single-hidden-layer networks and subsequently extended to multi-hidden-layer architectures. The authors propose an optimization algorithm based on nonlinear spectral methods, with experiments showing effectiveness on a small 2D synthetic dataset and reasonable performance on select UCI benchmark datasets.  

Strengths: This work represents the first practical neural network framework with provable global optimality and convergence guarantees. Previous approaches with similar guarantees often require impractical precondition checks (e.g., depending on unverifiable data-generating measures), whereas the proposed theory relies on comparatively simple preconditions—specifically, the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix derived from network parameters being less than 1. The authors further demonstrate applicability to arbitrarily deep networks and provide empirical validation on small-scale real-world data.  

Limitations: The theory imposes restrictive conditions that limit practical applicability: (1) all input data and linear-layer weights must be nonnegative; (2) activation functions must be generalized polynomials; (3) the spectral radius (Theorem 1) must be strictly less than 1. While input data nonnegativity can likely be addressed via standard shifting, the requirement for nonnegative network parameters may constrain model expressiveness. Additionally, the necessity for *all* hidden-layer activation functions to differ across layers is unnatural and potentially restrictive. Crucially, to satisfy the spectral radius constraint, the authors must increase parameters $p_1$ and $p_2$ while decreasing $p_w$ and $p_u$ (effectively reducing linear-layer weights). As input dimension or network size grows, these weight reductions become more pronounced. When weights approach zero, activation functions approximate linearity, rendering additional layers redundant (as stacking linear functions yields only linear transformations). This raises significant concerns about the method’s scalability to deep architectures, as depth may not enhance expressiveness under the required weight constraints.  

Despite these limitations, this work constitutes a promising initial step toward theoretically grounded neural network optimization. The clear theoretical contribution and empirical validation warrant acceptance.  

Minor comment: It would be valuable to investigate how $p_1$, $p_2$, $p_w$, $p_u$, input dimension, and network size collectively influence the spectral radius $\rho(\mathbf{A})$. For instance, does $\rho(\mathbf{A})$ scale linearly or exponentially with input dimension, and what weight adjustment strategy (linear vs. exponential) would counteract this? While analytical proof may be challenging, empirical exploration on synthetic data could provide useful insights.
