- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method that provably finds the global optimum with linear convergence for certain one- and two-hidden-layer networks.  
- Theoretical guarantees depend on highly restrictive assumptions that limit practical relevance.  
- Assumes non-negative weights, which is nonstandard and counterintuitive for neural networks.  
- Objective maximizes L2-norm of weights (opposite of standard regularization), raising practicality concerns.  
- Requires a “generalized polynomial” activation that is uncommon in practice.  
- Authors do not justify why this restricted class of networks is useful for real-world applications.  
- Empirical evaluation is unconvincing and uses unfair/weak baselines (primarily compared to linear SVM).  
- NLSM often underperforms even the linear SVM baselines, despite tuning for the proposed method.  
- Experiments are limited to small UCI datasets and do not demonstrate performance on larger, complex benchmarks.  
- Analysis suggests spectral radius bounds grow with hidden units, indicating poor scalability to high-dimensional data.  
- Question: How can practitioners efficiently find parameter settings that satisfy the apriori optimality condition?  
- Question: How can the method be adapted to handle high-dimensional data given the spectral radius dependency?  
- Question: Is the global optimality due mainly to the unusual maximization objective or to the network/spectral structure?  
- Question: How does standard SGD perform on the same architecture and objective—does it reach the same global optimum empirically?  
- Overall: Novel theoretical contribution but stringent assumptions and weak experiments make the practical impact unclear.
