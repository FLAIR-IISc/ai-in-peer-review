

This paper presents a novel theoretical approach for achieving globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks under nonnegativity constraints. The authors introduce a nonlinear spectral method that guarantees linear convergence to the global optimum under mild conditions, marking the first practically feasible method with such guarantees. Unlike prior work (e.g., Janzamin et al., 2015; Haeffele & Vidal, 2015), which requires impractical checks of data-generating measures or suffers from implementation challenges, their method depends only on the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix derived from network architecture parameters. The approach applies to networks with one or two hidden layers (with extension to arbitrary depth implied), using generalized polynomial activations (real powers ≥1) and nonnegative weights/data. Experiments on UCI datasets demonstrate competitive performance versus ReLU networks and SVMs, with the method achieving global optimality guarantees throughout.

**Strengths**  
The paper’s core contribution is its practical feasibility: the convergence condition (ρ(A) < 1) is computationally tractable without data access, contrasting sharply with prior theoretically sound but impractical methods. The theoretical framework is rigorous, leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems (Gautier et al., 2016) and Banach fixed-point theorems. The authors effectively bridge abstract fixed-point theory with neural network optimization, providing clear conditions for global optimality. The empirical results are compelling as a proof of concept: NLSM1/2 achieve competitive accuracy on UCI datasets while guaranteeing global optimality—a significant advance over prior work that lacks real-data validation. The paper also thoroughly addresses related work, clarifying distinctions from tensor-based approaches (Janzamin et al., 2015) and structured matrix factorization (Haeffele & Vidal, 2015).

**Weaknesses**  
The constraints significantly limit applicability. The requirement for nonnegative inputs (which may be addressable via data preprocessing) and *nonnegative weights* is restrictive, as standard neural networks use sign-invariant activations (e.g., ReLU) and unconstrained weights. This constraint likely reduces expressiveness, as evidenced by the need for distinct activation exponents (α_i ≠ α_j) to avoid permutation invariance (see Section 2.1). While the authors note this is necessary for uniqueness, it introduces an unnatural architectural constraint. More critically, the spectral radius condition (ρ(A) < 1) necessitates adjusting parameters: increasing p_w or p_u (e.g., via Equation 4) reduces the magnitude of linear weights, potentially collapsing the network toward linear behavior. For high-dimensional data, this could severely limit expressiveness, as larger ρ_x (from higher input dimension) increases ρ(A), requiring further weight suppression. The authors acknowledge this trade-off but provide limited insight into its practical implications for deep architectures.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary**  
*Pros*: First practical globally optimal training method for non-trivial neural networks; theoretically rigorous with checkable conditions; competitive empirical results on small-scale datasets; clear comparison to prior work.  
*Cons*: Nonnegative weight constraint limits expressiveness; distinct activation exponents are unnatural; spectral radius condition may require excessively small weights for high-dimensional data, undermining expressiveness; limited to low-dimensional datasets due to conservative bounds on ρ(A).

**Recommendation**  
Despite the constraints, this is a significant theoretical contribution that opens a new direction for globally optimal neural network training. The paper addresses a critical gap in the literature—practical global optimality guarantees—and provides a framework that could inspire future work on relaxing constraints. While the current scope is limited, the results are promising for low-dimensional problems, and the method’s simplicity (no hyperparameter tuning beyond ρ(A) < 1) is a major advantage over SGD. The minor comments (e.g., exploring ρ(A)’s scaling with input dimension) are valuable for future work but do not undermine the core contribution.  

**Verdict**: **Accept**. This paper makes a strong, novel contribution to the theoretical foundations of neural network optimization. It advances the field by providing the first practically implementable globally optimal training method, with clear pathways for future work to address current limitations.
