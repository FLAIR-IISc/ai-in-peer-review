This paper analyzes a specific class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to a global optimum with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM). The approach is applied to deep networks with one and two hidden layers, and evaluated on several real-world datasets.

As the authors acknowledge, the class of networks studied is restrictive and somewhat counterintuitive: it imposes non-negativity constraints on the weights and maximizes the regularization of these weights, and it requires the use of a less common activation function (the generalized polynomial) to satisfy the optimality condition. These assumptions do not seem particularly reasonable. More detailed and careful justification from the standpoint of applications and real-world data would be helpful, rather than introducing increasingly restrictive assumptions solely to guarantee a global optimum.

Although the authors claim that the targeted class still has sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieves strong performance, the empirical results do not convincingly support this. In several cases, a linear SVM outperforms NLSM1 and NLSM2 even though the linear SVM does not appear to be properly tuned, whereas NLSM involves tuning of network structures. Moreover, since NLSM is a nonlinear model, it is puzzling that the main baseline is a linear SVM rather than a nonlinear SVM (e.g., with polynomial or Gaussian kernels). This makes the comparisons unfair and the resulting conclusions unconvincing. In other words, the reported results do not establish the claimed expressive power of the proposed network class.

The experimental datasets drawn from the UCI repository are also quite small, both in sample size and feature dimensionality. According to the analysis, the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A increase with the number of hidden units, which suggests difficulties for high-dimensional data. Is there any way to mitigate this issue? As it stands, this limitation would prevent the method from being applicable to a large fraction of practical problems.

It also appears that the optimality condition is not guaranteed to hold for any reasonable choice of the network’s parameters. Although this condition can be checked without training the network, it remains problematic if we lack informative priors and the parameter space is extremely large. Is there a fast method to identify a small set of candidate parameters that are likely to satisfy the condition?

Finally, does the optimality property stem from the particular objective function being optimized? It would be interesting to see whether stochastic gradient descent can also converge empirically to a global optimum on some of the tested datasets.
