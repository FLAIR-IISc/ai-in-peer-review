This work investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks for which a nonlinear spectral method can achieve a global optimum with a linear convergence rate. The authors apply this method to networks with one and two hidden layers and evaluate its performance on several real-world datasets.

As the authors note, the studied class of networks imposes significant and non-standard constraints, such as requiring non-negative weights while also maximizing the regularization of these weights. Moreover, the optimality guarantee is contingent on the use of a less common generalized polynomial activation function. These assumptions lack clear practical justification. The paper would be stronger if it provided careful explanations for these choices from the perspective of real-world applications, rather than framing them as necessary restrictions for achieving global optimality.

The authors claim that their targeted class of neural networks maintains sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieve good performance. However, the empirical evidence provided is not sufficiently compelling to support this claim. The results show that a linear SVM often performs much better than the proposed NLSM1 and NLSM2 models, even when the SVM does not appear to be properly tuned, whereas the NLSM models have their network structures tuned. Furthermore, since NLSM is a nonlinear model, comparing it to a linear SVM is an inappropriate baseline. A more equitable comparison would involve nonlinear SVMs, such as those with polynomial or Gaussian kernels. Therefore, the current comparisons are not fair, and the conclusions drawn from them about the model's expressive power are not convincing.

The experiments utilize datasets from the UCI repository that are of a limited scale in terms of both sample size and feature dimensionality. This is concerning, as the paper's analysis shows that the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which suggests the method may be problematic for high-dimensional data. Does a strategy exist to address this scalability issue? Without one, the method's applicability to a large proportion of real-world problems is greatly restricted.

It also appears that the optimality condition is not guaranteed by arbitrary parameter settings within the studied network class. While this condition can be checked before running the network, it remains a significant challenge when there is no prior knowledge of suitable parameters or when the search space is extremely large. Is there a fast approach to identify a small set of valid candidate parameters?

Finally, does the model's optimal property derive from its special objective function? It would be interesting to see an empirical study of whether a standard optimizer like stochastic gradient descent can also converge to the global optimum on the tested datasets for this specific model architecture.
