This paper investigates a special class of neural networks and demonstrates that under certain constraints, the network has only one stationary point. The authors introduce a fast iterative method designed to converge to this unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The paper addresses the interesting problem of how a relatively complex model can be constrained to have a unique solution. The theoretical approach is based on fixed-point theory—a common alternative to convexity for proving the uniqueness of stationary points—wherein the authors construct a contractive map whose fixed point is the unique minimizer of the cost function.

However, the proposed method has several significant drawbacks. A key limitation is that the convergence proof depends on the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix being bounded. As the authors acknowledge, this constraint is the reason they did not experiment with networks containing a larger number of hidden units. Another problem is the large number of hyperparameters that require tuning, which makes the procedure unintuitive. This difficulty is compounded by the fact that the spectral condition must be verified for each new choice of hyperparameters.

These limitations are reflected in the experimental results. The authors report testing approximately 150,000 different hyperparameter combinations and selecting the best one based on cross-validation. Despite this extensive search, their method outperformed a linear SVM baseline on only 2 out of 7 datasets. This result calls into question the practical utility and representative power of the class of neural networks investigated in this work.
