

This paper investigates a specific class of feedforward neural networks trainable to global optimality with linear convergence using a nonlinear spectral method, applied to one- and two-hidden-layer deep networks. While experiments on real-world datasets are presented, the core contributions face significant concerns. The authors' model imposes restrictive assumptions: non-negative weights and maximization of weight regularization, which are counterintuitive and lack justification for practical applications. Furthermore, the requirement of a less common activation function—generalized polynomial—is necessary for the optimality condition. These assumptions are not well-motivated, and the paper fails to provide detailed explanations linking them to real-world utility or dataset characteristics. Instead of justifying such restrictions, the authors should demonstrate how these conditions align with practical scenarios.

The claim that this network class possesses sufficient expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieve strong performance is not convincingly supported by the empirical results. Notably, linear SVMs outperform NLSM1 and NLSM2 in cases where linear SVMs appear suboptimally tuned, while NLSM variants adjust their network structures. This comparison is fundamentally flawed: using a linear SVM as a baseline for a nonlinear model (NLSM) is inappropriate; a nonlinear baseline like polynomial or Gaussian kernel SVM would be necessary for a fair evaluation. Consequently, the results do not substantiate the authors' claims about the model's expressive power.

The experimental datasets (from UCI) are too small in both sample size and feature dimensionality. Crucially, the analysis shows that bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which poses a major challenge for high-dimensional data. This scalability issue severely limits the method's applicability to most real-world problems. The paper does not address how to mitigate this, leaving the approach impractical for large-scale applications.

Additionally, the optimality condition appears highly sensitive: it may not be guaranteed under typical parameter settings, and verifying it without prior knowledge or extensive parameter searches is problematic. The authors do not propose a practical method to efficiently identify a small set of valid parameter candidates. Finally, it is unclear whether the model's global optimality stems from the specific objective function. It would be valuable to test if stochastic gradient descent (SGD) can empirically converge to the global optimum on the same datasets, as this would provide stronger evidence for the method's practical viability. Without addressing these critical gaps, the paper's contributions remain limited in scope and applicability.
