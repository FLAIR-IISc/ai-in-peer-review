This paper studies a restricted class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to a global optimum with linear convergence using a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM). The method is applied to networks with one and two hidden layers, and evaluated on several real-world datasets.

While the theoretical goal is appealing, the class of models considered appears highly restrictive and somewhat counterintuitive. The formulation enforces non-negativity on network weights, applies very strong regularization to those weights, and relies on a relatively uncommon generalized polynomial activation to satisfy optimality conditions. The paper would benefit from a clearer, application-driven justification for these constraints, including evidence that they are realistic or advantageous in practice, rather than being primarily introduced to guarantee global optimality.

The empirical results do not convincingly support the claim that the proposed class retains sufficient expressive power. In several cases, a linear SVM outperforms NLSM1 and NLSM2, despite the SVM appearing to receive limited tuning compared with the tuned NLSM architectures. More importantly, it is unclear why only a linear SVM baseline is used. Since NLSM is a nonlinear model, comparisons against stronger nonlinear baselines (e.g., SVMs with polynomial or RBF kernels) are necessary to assess expressive power fairly. As presented, the comparisons do not convincingly validate the performance claims.

The experimental setup also relies primarily on relatively small UCI datasets with limited sample sizes and low dimensionality, which makes it difficult to assess scalability and practical relevance. Based on the analysis, the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, suggesting potential difficulties for higher-dimensional data or larger models. Please discuss whether there are practical strategies to mitigate this (e.g., architectural constraints, normalization schemes, or data preprocessing), and clarify the implications for scaling to modern, higher-dimensional tasks.

It is also unclear how often the optimality conditions are satisfied under realistic hyperparameter ranges. Although these conditions can be checked a priori, the search space may be prohibitively large without informative priors. Providing concrete guidance, bounds, or heuristics to narrow the candidate parameter space would make the approach more practical.

Finally, it would be useful to clarify whether the observed global optimality is primarily a consequence of the specific objective and constraints. Do standard training methods (e.g., SGD) empirically converge to similar solutions under the same architectural and activation constraints on at least some datasets? Such experiments would help disentangle the contribution of the optimization method from that of the model class and objective.

In summary, the theoretical contribution is interesting, but the strong modeling assumptions, limited and potentially unfair empirical comparisons, and unclear scalability substantially weaken the paper’s impact. Stronger empirical validation, more competitive baselines, clearer justification of assumptions, and practical guidance on satisfying the optimality conditions are needed.
