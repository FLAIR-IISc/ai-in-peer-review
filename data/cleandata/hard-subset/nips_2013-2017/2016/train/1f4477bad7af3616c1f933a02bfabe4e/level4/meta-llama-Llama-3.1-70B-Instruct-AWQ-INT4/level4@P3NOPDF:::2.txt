This paper explores a specific class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained to achieve global optimality with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors apply this method to deep networks with one and two hidden layers and conduct experiments on a series of real-world datasets. 

However, the class of feedforward neural networks studied is restrictive and counterintuitive, as it imposes non-negativity constraints on the network weights and maximizes the regularization of these weights. Furthermore, the optimality condition requires the use of a less popular activation function called the generalized polynomial. These assumptions are not entirely reasonable and may not be applicable in many real-world scenarios. Instead of making more restrictive assumptions to achieve global optimality, the authors could provide more detailed and careful explanations from the perspectives of applications or real-world datasets.

The authors claim that the targeted class of neural networks still has enough expressive power to model complex decision boundaries and achieves good performance. However, the empirical experimental results do not provide strong support for this claim. In some cases, linear SVM performs significantly better than NLSM1 and NLSM2, even when the linear SVM does not seem to be properly tuned, while the NLSM methods tune their network structures. It is surprising that the authors use a linear SVM as a baseline, rather than a nonlinear SVM, such as a polynomial kernel or Gaussian kernel, given that NLSM is a nonlinear model. As a result, the comparisons are not entirely fair, and the conclusions based on these comparable results are not convincing.

The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the expressive power of the studied class of neural networks. Moreover, the datasets from the UCI repository used in the experiments are relatively small in terms of both sample size and feature dimensionality. According to the analysis, the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which could be problematic for high-dimensional data. It would be beneficial to discuss potential ways to address this issue, as it may limit the applicability of this method to a large proportion of applications.

Another concern is that the optimal condition is not guaranteed by any setting of parameters that could be used in the studied neural networks. Although the condition can be checked without running the neural networks, it is still problematic if there are no priors or the possible parameters are extremely large. It would be helpful to explore fast approaches to obtain a small set of valid candidates. Furthermore, it is interesting to investigate whether the optimal property of the studied model stems from the special objective function. Additionally, it would be valuable to examine whether stochastic gradient descent can also converge to the global optimal empirically on some tested datasets.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting approach to training neural networks, there are several concerns and limitations that need to be addressed. The authors could strengthen their claims by providing more detailed explanations, using more suitable baselines, and exploring ways to address the issues related to high-dimensional data and optimal conditions.
