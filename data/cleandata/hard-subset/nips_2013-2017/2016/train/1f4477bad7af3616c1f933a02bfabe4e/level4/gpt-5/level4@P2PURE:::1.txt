This paper studies a special class of neural networks and shows that the associated optimization problem has a single stationary point. The authors introduce a fast method that converges to the unique fixed point, which is the global minimum of the cost function. The paper tackles the interesting question of how a relatively complex model, under carefully chosen constraints, can be guaranteed to have only one stationary point. Beyond convexity (and generalizations such as geodesic convexity), a common route to uniqueness is fixed-point theory; this is the approach used here. The authors construct a contraction mapping whose fixed point is the unique minimizer of the cost.

A notable drawback is that the convergence proof depends on bounding the spectral radius of a nontrivial matrix. As the authors note, this is why they did not explore networks with larger numbers of hidden units. Another concern is the large number of parameters that must be tuned, which makes the procedure somewhat unintuitive; furthermore, for each parameter setting the spectral condition must be verified.

In the experiments, the authors evaluated approximately 150,000 combinations of parameter values and chose the best via cross-validation. However, their method outperformed a linear SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets. This outcome raises questions about the practical effectiveness of the neural network under study.
