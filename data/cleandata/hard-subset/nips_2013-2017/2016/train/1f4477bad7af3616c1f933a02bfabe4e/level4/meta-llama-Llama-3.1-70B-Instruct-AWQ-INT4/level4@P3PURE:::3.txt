This paper presents a novel theory demonstrating that a specific class of neural networks achieves global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The primary constraint is that all input data and weights of the linear layer must be positive, and activation functions must be generalized polynomials. The proposed theory is first established for neural networks with a single hidden layer and then extended to networks with multiple hidden layers. The authors also propose an optimization algorithm based on a nonlinear spectral method. Experiments show that the proposed algorithm performs well on a small 2D synthetic dataset and achieves reasonable performance on some small real-world datasets (UCI datasets).

The strengths of this paper lie in its contribution to the field. As claimed by the authors, this is the first work to propose a potentially practical neural network with both optimal and convergence guarantees. Unlike previous work with convergence guarantees, which often require complicated methods to check preconditions that may be impossible to verify in practice, the theory proposed here has relatively simple preconditions that depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix consisting of network parameters. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that the proposed theory can be applied to networks of arbitrary depth and show reasonable results on small real datasets.

However, the proposed network still has limited applications. The theory requires that both input data and network parameters be positive, activation functions be generalized polynomials, and the spectral radius (Theorem 1) be smaller than 1. While the non-negative constraint on input data may not significantly limit potential usage, as most training data can be easily shifted to be all positive, the requirement that network parameters be positive might limit the expressiveness of the model. Additionally, the constraint that all activation functions of all hidden layers be different may also limit its application. Moreover, to satisfy the spectral radius constraint, the authors need to increase p1 and p2 and decrease pw and pu, effectively decreasing the weights of linear units. As the dimension of the input and network parameters increases, the weights of linear units must be decreased to satisfy the spectral radius constraint. When linear weights are very small, activation functions will behave similarly to linear functions, and adding more layers will only add another linear function, which may not increase the expressiveness of the network. This raises concerns about the ease of extending this method to deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, this is still an encouraging result as an initial attempt, and I believe this paper should be accepted.

Minor comments:

I am curious about how p1, p2, pw, pu, the dimension of the input, and the size of the network parameters affect the spectral radius rho(A). For instance, does rho(A) increase linearly or exponentially with the dimension of the input, and how should pw and pu be adjusted to counteract this effect? Although a formal proof may be challenging, it may be possible to empirically plot this relationship using synthetic data.
