This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks. The primary contribution is a theoretical guarantee of convergence to a global optimum at a linear rate. This guarantee is contingent on a condition involving the spectral radius of a small, non-negative matrix, which is determined by the network's architectural parameters.

**Strengths**

The main strength of this work lies in its theoretical contribution. Proposing a training method for a neural network that guarantees global optimality is a notable achievement, as the optimization landscape for these models is typically non-convex and difficult to analyze. The connection established between the global optimality condition and the spectral radius of a matrix derived from the network architecture is elegant and novel.

**Weaknesses**

Despite its theoretical appeal, the practical applicability of the proposed method appears to be severely limited by its underlying assumptions. My main concerns are as follows:

1.  **Non-negativity Constraint on Weights:** The requirement that all network weights be non-negative is a very strong constraint. This significantly curtails the expressive power of the neural network, as it prevents the model from learning inhibitory relationships or negative correlations in the data. Most modern neural networks rely on both positive and negative weights to function effectively.

2.  **Specialized Activation Functions:** The method is developed for a specific and non-standard choice of activation functions. The broader applicability of the theoretical framework to more common activation functions (e.g., ReLU, sigmoid, tanh) is unclear. This specificity makes it difficult to assess the relevance of the proposed technique to the wider field of deep learning. The authors should discuss the possibility of extending their analysis to other, more standard activation functions.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** The aforementioned limitations appear to manifest in the experimental results. The paper shows that the proposed model does not outperform a standard SVM, which is a significant weakness. While the goal may be to provide a theoretical contribution, the lack of competitive empirical performance raises questions about the practical value of the guarantees provided, as they are tied to a heavily restricted model class.

**Minor Comment**

A minor point regarding the claims made on lines 90-91: The statement that the method "converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations" because it has a linear convergence rate could be misleading. A linear convergence rate does not, on its own, guarantee fast convergence in a small number of iterations; the convergence speed is highly dependent on the contraction constant. The authors should consider rephrasing this to be more precise or provide empirical evidence to substantiate this strong claim.
