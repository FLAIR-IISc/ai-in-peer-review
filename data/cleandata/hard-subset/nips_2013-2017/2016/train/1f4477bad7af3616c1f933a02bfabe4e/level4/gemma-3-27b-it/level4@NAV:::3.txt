This paper introduces a compelling theoretical framework demonstrating global optimality and linear convergence for a specific class of neural networks. The key requirement for this guarantee is positivity constraints on both input data and linear layer weights, coupled with the use of generalized polynomial activation functions. The theory is initially established for single-hidden-layer networks and then successfully extended to those with multiple hidden layers. Accompanying this theory, the authors present a novel optimization algorithm grounded in the nonlinear spectral method. Experimental results demonstrate effective performance on a small, synthetic 2D dataset and reasonable results on several small UCI datasets.

Strengths of this work include its novelty and potential practicality. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, this is the first instance of a neural network design with both optimality and convergence guarantees. Existing theoretical results with convergence guarantees often rely on preconditions that are either difficult or impossible to verify in practice, frequently depending on the underlying data distribution. In contrast, the presented theory depends on relatively simple preconditions – specifically, the spectral radius of a non-negative matrix derived from network parameters. The authors effectively demonstrate the theory’s applicability to networks of arbitrary depth and validate it with experiments on small real-world datasets.

However, the current work also has limitations. The proposed network’s applicability remains somewhat restricted by the need for positive input data, generalized polynomial activations, and a spectral radius (as defined in Theorem 1) less than one. While the positivity constraint on input data appears manageable through simple data shifting, the requirement for positive network parameters may limit the model’s expressive capacity. The stipulation that each hidden layer employs a *different* activation function feels somewhat artificial and potentially restricts practical application. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge that satisfying the spectral radius constraint necessitates adjustments to network parameters – increasing *p1* and *p2*, and decreasing *pw* and *pu* – effectively reducing the magnitude of linear unit weights. I am concerned that, as input and parameter dimensionality increase, maintaining a spectral radius below one will require a disproportionate reduction in linear unit weights, potentially causing the activation functions to behave nearly linearly, thus diminishing the benefits of deeper architectures. 

Despite these concerns, this work represents a promising initial step, and I believe the paper merits acceptance. 

Minor Comment: I am interested in understanding the relationship between the parameters *p1*, *p2*, *pw*, *pu*, input dimensionality, network size, and the spectral radius *ρ(A)*. Specifically, how does *ρ(A)* scale with input dimension – linearly, exponentially, or otherwise?  And, correspondingly, what is the required scaling of *pw* and *pu* to counteract this effect? While a formal proof may be challenging, an empirical investigation plotting this relationship on synthetic data could provide valuable insight.
