This paper introduces a specific class of feedforward neural networks for which the training objective admits a unique global optimum, and it presents an algorithm that provably finds this global optimum with linear convergence. The mathematical development is instructive, but several points remain unclear to me.

1) Relationship between Theorems 3 and 4. Theorem 3 appears to rely on first-order derivatives, as reflected in the stated inequalities, whereas Theorem 4 moves to second-order conditions. What necessitates the jump to second derivatives in Theorem 4? Is this due to a need for curvature (e.g., strong convexity or a uniform positive definiteness condition) that cannot be captured by first-order arguments alone? It would help to make explicit which assumption or step requires second-order analysis and why the first-order framework in Theorem 3 is insufficient there.

2) Treatment of the last layer and the parameterization by u. The architecture seems to treat the final layer differently from the hidden layers. Let u denote the matrix of parameters connected to the hidden layer. In G^Φ (3), is the gradient with respect to u taken with respect to the vectorization of u (i.e., the gradient in R^{dim(u)})? Similarly, when constraining u to lie within a ball, is this a Frobenius-norm ball interpreted after vectorizing u? Clarifying the notational convention and the exact norm used would make several steps easier to follow.

3) Objective in (2) and the 1-norm. The model maximizes the 1-norm of the connection parameters in (2), which contrasts with common practice in neural networks where L1 or L2 regularization penalizes large weights. My understanding is that the arbitrarily small ε in (2) is introduced to make gradients strictly positive so that parameters lie in V_++. If so, this appears to be primarily a technical device to facilitate the proofs rather than a modeling choice motivated by practice. Could you justify this choice empirically or theoretically from a learning perspective, and discuss the sensitivity to ε? If the maximization of the 1-norm is only to ensure strict positivity, is there a more practical alternative that preserves the proof while aligning better with standard regularization?

4) Positivity constraints and practicality. The framework seems to require nonnegative data and strictly positive weights (excluding even zero), so outputs can only accumulate positively. What guarantees that there are no “free parameters” here (i.e., no unconstrained degrees of freedom or hyperparameters that critically affect performance)? More importantly, how practical is this design for real-world data that are not naturally nonnegative or that benefit from sign flexibility? Do the authors recommend preprocessing (e.g., shifting or scaling) to satisfy nonnegativity, and does this restriction degrade representational capacity compared to standard architectures? Some empirical ablation (e.g., allowing zeros or relaxing strict positivity) would strengthen the case.

5) Experimental comparisons. The proposed network is compared to SVMs, presumably because both admit a global optimum. However, in practice, does this architecture provide advantages over commonly used neural networks (e.g., ReLU-based models) in terms of accuracy, robustness, sample efficiency, training stability, or interpretability? A comparison against standard neural baselines would clarify the practical value of the theoretical guarantees.

Overall, the theoretical contribution is interesting, and the algorithmic guarantee of linear convergence to a unique global optimum is appealing. Addressing the derivative-level gap between Theorems 3 and 4, clarifying the gradient and norm conventions for u, justifying the maximization of the 1-norm and the role of ε, and providing evidence for practicality under positivity constraints would significantly improve clarity and impact. Additional empirical comparisons with standard neural architectures would help position the proposed method in practice.
