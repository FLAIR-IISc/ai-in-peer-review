This paper studies a particular class of feedforward neural networks that can be trained globally optimal with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. This method is applied to deep networks with one- and two-hidden layers, and experiments are conducted on a series of real-world datasets. The authors' work is related to recent research that has tackled the optimization problem of neural networks, providing guarantees or convergence to the global optimum.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a nonlinear spectral method that achieves global optimality under weak assumptions on the data. The method is based on the theory of nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones and is inspired by the Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices. The authors show that their method has a linear convergence rate and can be applied to deep networks, although they restrict themselves to one- and two-hidden layer networks for simplicity.

The paper's strengths include its rigorous mathematical framework and the development of a novel algorithm for training neural networks. The authors provide a detailed proof of their main theorem, which guarantees the convergence of their method to the global optimum. The paper also includes experimental results on several UCI datasets, which demonstrate the competitiveness of the authors' method with other state-of-the-art algorithms.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The class of feedforward neural networks studied is restrictive and counterintuitive, imposing non-negativity on the weights of the network and maximizing the regularization of these weights. The use of a generalized polynomial activation function is also less popular and may not be suitable for all applications. The authors' claim that their method achieves good performance on real-world datasets is not strongly supported by their experimental results, which show that their method is outperformed by linear SVM on some datasets.

Furthermore, the authors' comparison with linear SVM is not entirely fair, as their method is a nonlinear model. A more appropriate comparison would be with nonlinear SVMs, such as polynomial kernel or Gaussian kernel. Additionally, the datasets used in the experiments are relatively small, both in terms of sample size and feature dimensionality, which may not be representative of many real-world applications.

The bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A, which is used to guarantee the convergence of the method, grow with the number of hidden units. This may be problematic for high-dimensional data, and it is unclear how to deal with this issue. The authors do not provide any guidance on how to choose the parameters of their method, which may be challenging in practice.

In conclusion, while the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network optimization, its restrictive assumptions and limited experimental results may limit its applicability to real-world problems. The authors should consider addressing these limitations in future work.
