

This paper presents a novel approach for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees and linear convergence rate. The authors introduce a "nonlinear spectral method" that requires nonnegative data and weights, and modifies the objective function by adding the sum of outputs to the loss. The work builds upon and extends previous results in global optimality for neural networks, particularly addressing limitations of Janzamin et al. [11] (which requires unknown data-generating measures) and Haeffele and Vidal [8] (which is challenging to apply in practice). The authors claim this is the first practically feasible method achieving global optimality guarantees for a non-trivial neural network model.

Strengths:
1. Quality: The theoretical contribution is strong, with rigorous proofs establishing global optimality and linear convergence. The condition for global optimality (ρ(A) < 1) can be checked without running the algorithm, and the method requires no hyperparameter tuning like learning rates. The proofs are well-structured, though some technical details (e.g., the transition from first to second derivatives between Theorems 3 and 4) could be clarified.

2. Originality: The paper makes a clear contribution by providing a theoretically sound method for global optimality in a specific neural network class, which differs significantly from previous work. The requirement of nonnegative weights and data is a distinctive constraint that enables the theoretical guarantees, and the authors properly acknowledge related work in NIPS proceedings.

3. Significance: While the method is limited to 1-2 hidden layers and nonnegative data, the results demonstrate competitive performance on UCI datasets compared to SVM and ReLU networks. The paper shows for the first time a globally optimal neural network algorithm that achieves non-trivial classification results, which is significant for the field.

4. Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with good mathematical notation. The presentation of the main theorem and proof strategy is logical, though the motivation for adding the sum of outputs to the objective function could be better explained. The experimental section is detailed and properly contextualized.

Weaknesses:
1. Quality: The nonnegativity constraint on both data and weights is quite restrictive and limits practical applicability. The modified objective function (adding sum of outputs) appears to be primarily for technical reasons to ensure strictly positive gradients rather than being well-justified for practical neural networks. The authors should clarify why this modification is necessary for practical implementation.

2. Significance: The comparison with SVM is questionable, as SVM is not a neural network model and has different optimization properties. The paper would benefit from comparing with other neural network approaches (e.g., standard SGD with ReLU networks) to better demonstrate practical value. The restriction to 1-2 hidden layers limits the significance for deep learning applications.

3. Clarity: The relationship between the gradient with respect to u (the weight matrix connecting to the hidden layer) and the vectorization of u is not sufficiently explained. The role of the small parameter ε in the objective function could be better motivated.

The paper makes an important theoretical contribution to the understanding of global optimality in neural network training, but the practical applicability is limited by the restrictive assumptions. The method is well-motivated theoretically and provides a compelling alternative to SGD for specific problem settings, though the authors should more clearly address the practical limitations and potential applications where this approach would be preferable to standard methods.
