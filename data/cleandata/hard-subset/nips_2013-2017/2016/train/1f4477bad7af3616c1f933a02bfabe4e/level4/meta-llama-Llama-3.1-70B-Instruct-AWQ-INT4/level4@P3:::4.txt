This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a particular class of feedforward neural networks, achieving global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The authors' contribution is novel and interesting, as it provides a practically feasible method for optimal training under weak assumptions on the data.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors do a good job of explaining the main ideas and relating them to previous work. The optimization problem behind neural networks is highly non-convex, and the authors' approach to addressing this issue is clear and well-motivated.

The strengths of the paper include its novel contribution to the field of neural networks, its clear and well-organized presentation, and its ability to achieve global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the optimization problem and the properties of the nonlinear spectral method.

However, there are some weaknesses to the paper. The main limitation of the approach is the imposition of non-negativity on the weights of the network, which may hinder the performance of the neural networks. Additionally, the choice of activation functions in the neural network is non-standard and very specific, which may limit the applicability of the method.

The numerical experiments provided in the paper show competitive performance with other methods, but not-so-good performance compared to SVM. This raises concerns about the effectiveness of the method in practice. Furthermore, the statement on page 90-91 that the nonlinear spectral method converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations seems exaggerated, as the linear convergence rate does not necessarily imply a few iterations.

In terms of the NIPS reviewing guidelines, the paper scores well on quality, clarity, and originality. However, its significance is somewhat limited by the strong assumptions imposed on the network architecture and the non-standard choice of activation functions.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting method for optimal training of neural networks.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the optimization problem and the properties of the nonlinear spectral method.
* The paper is well-organized and clearly written.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The method imposes strong assumptions on the network architecture, which may limit its applicability.
* The choice of activation functions is non-standard and very specific, which may hinder the performance of the neural networks.
* The numerical experiments show competitive but not state-of-the-art performance.

Overall, I would recommend acceptance of the paper, but with the caveat that the authors should be aware of the limitations of their approach and its potential impact on the performance of the neural networks.
