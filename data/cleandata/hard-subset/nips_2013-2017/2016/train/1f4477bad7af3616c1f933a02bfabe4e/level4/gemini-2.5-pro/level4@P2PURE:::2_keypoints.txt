- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for certain one- and two-hidden-layer networks with provable global optimum and linear convergence rate, evaluated on real datasets.  
- Practical relevance is limited by restrictive, counterintuitive assumptions: non-negative weights and maximization of the weight regularization term.  
- Theoretical guarantees depend on a non-standard "generalized polynomial" activation, raising questions about real-world applicability.  
- Authors need to justify why this specific model class and assumptions are relevant for practical problems.  
- Empirical results are unconvincing: linear SVM often outperforms NLSM1/NLSM2 despite questionable SVM tuning, undermining claimed expressive power.  
- Primary baseline choice (linear SVM) is inappropriate for a nonlinear model; comparisons should include strong nonlinear baselines (e.g., polynomial/Gaussian-kernel SVMs).  
- Experiments use small UCI datasets with limited sample size and feature dimensionality, limiting empirical validation.  
- Scalability concerns: bounds on the spectral radius grow with hidden-unit count, potentially preventing applicability to high-dimensional problems requiring more hidden units.  
- Practicality of the optimality condition is unclear—checking it a priori may be insufficient if the feasible parameter space is large and no priors guide search; need efficient ways to find feasible parameters.  
- Unclear whether global optimality stems from the specialized objective; authors should empirically compare to standard methods (e.g., SGD) to assess the spectral method’s true contribution.
