- Presents a novel nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that achieves global optimality with linear convergence for a class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and a modified objective.  
- Theoretical contribution is strong: rigorous proof of global optimality using fixed-point and Perron-Frobenius theory under a spectral radius condition.  
- First practically feasible algorithm claimed to provide provable global optimality for one- and two-hidden-layer networks, distinguishing it from prior theoretical-only work.  
- Empirical validation on UCI datasets shows competitive performance, but gains are modest and NLSM outperforms SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets.  
- Nonnegativity constraint on weights is a restrictive assumption that limits model expressiveness and generality.  
- Requirement to verify spectral conditions for each model configuration imposes a significant practical burden.  
- Parameter search was extensive (150,000 combinations), raising concerns about scalability and practicality.  
- Clarity is generally good but notation is dense and the main theorem and method would benefit from more intuitive explanation.  
- Connection between the mathematical framework and practical implementation is somewhat obscured by technical detail.  
- Originality is high: novel use of nonnegative weights and modified objective to enable provable global optimality.  
- Significance is limited by restrictive assumptions, modest empirical gains, and unclear extension to larger or more general architectures.  
- Method’s applicability is constrained to low-dimensional settings due to spectral radius growth with hidden units.  
- Strength: linear convergence rate makes the method computationally efficient in theory.  
- Strength: paper is well-structured and technically complete, with supplementary proofs provided.  
- Weakness: empirical results and practical utility need strengthening or clearer justification of constrained model usefulness.  
- Recommendation: suitable for NIPS but revision should better discuss practical limitations, clarify usefulness of constraints, and address scalability of spectral verification.
