

The paper presents a method for globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors establish convergence guarantees under mild assumptions on the data, demonstrating that their approach achieves linear convergence to the global optimum with a practically implementable algorithm. This work addresses an important gap in the literature, as previous methods for globally optimal training either lack practical applicability (e.g., Haeffele and Vidal, 2015) or require knowledge of the data-generating distribution (e.g., Janzamin et al., 2015).

The paper's main strengths include a theoretically rigorous approach with clear convergence guarantees, a novel connection between neural network training and nonlinear eigenvalue problems, and experimental results demonstrating competitive performance on standard benchmark datasets. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical foundation, including detailed proofs of convergence properties and conditions for global optimality.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that require attention. First, while the authors acknowledge limitations in their model's expressiveness (mentioned twice in the paper), they do not sufficiently discuss the practical implications of these constraints or provide guidance on how to address them. A more thorough analysis of which problem classes are tractable with this approach versus those requiring more flexible architectures would strengthen the paper.

Second, the experimental evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive. The authors compare their method only to SVMs and ReLU-based neural networks, ignoring other relevant approaches such as other global optimization methods for neural networks (e.g., the tensor-based method by Janzamin et al.) and alternative algorithmic paradigms. This limited comparison fails to establish the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.

Third, the paper lacks discussion of computational resources required by the proposed method compared to other approaches. Without this information, it is difficult to assess the practical viability of the method in real-world applications, especially given the claimed fast convergence.

The presentation could also be improved. The definition of the objective function Φ lacks clear motivation, particularly regarding the role of the ε term (which was clarified in the rebuttal but should be added to the main paper). The statement of Theorem 1 is dense and difficult to parse without an informal explanation first. The claim that "the nonlinear spectral method [...] converges quickly typically in a few (less than 10) iterations to the global maximum" (immediately following Theorem 1) should be explicitly tied to experimental observations rather than presented as a general claim.

Several specific issues require attention:
- "polyomial" should be corrected to "polynomial" (line 60)
- The terms p1 and p2 are not introduced (line 67)
- R_{++} is used without definition (line 67), which is problematic given the paper's reliance on positive orthant constraints

The introduction is well-written, but the rest of the paper would benefit from more careful editing for clarity. The technical contributions are significant, and the theoretical framework is sound, but the paper would be strengthened by addressing the above concerns.

In summary, while the paper makes an important theoretical contribution to the understanding of globally optimal training of neural networks, the practical implications of the approach require more thorough discussion. The experimental evaluation needs expansion to include more relevant baselines, and several presentation issues need to be addressed. The theoretical results are strong, but the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of the limitations and practical considerations of the proposed method.

I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that with significant revisions addressing the above concerns, this paper could be a strong candidate for NIPS. The authors should expand their discussion of model expressiveness limitations, provide a more comprehensive experimental comparison with relevant baselines, include computational resource analysis, and improve the clarity of the presentation.
