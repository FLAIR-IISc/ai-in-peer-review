This paper presents a compelling analysis of a specific neural network architecture, demonstrating the existence of a single stationary point. The authors develop an efficient algorithm that converges to this unique fixed-point, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost function. The work addresses the challenging problem of proving stationary point uniqueness in a non-convex model through the application of fixed-point theory – a common approach alongside convexity-based methods. Specifically, the authors skillfully construct a contractive mapping, ensuring the fixed-point coincides with the global minimizer. 

However, the convergence guarantee relies on a constraint regarding the spectral radius of a key matrix, limiting the scalability of the approach, as acknowledged by the authors, and hindering application to networks with larger hidden layers. Additionally, the method introduces a significant number of hyperparameters, making practical implementation and parameter tuning complex and less intuitive. Critically, verifying the spectral condition is required for each parameter configuration. The empirical evaluation involved an extensive search over 150,000 parameter combinations, selected based on cross-validation performance. Despite this effort, the proposed method only outperformed a linear SVM on two of seven datasets, raising concerns about the practical advantages of the investigated neural network architecture.
