The paper introduces and characterizes a specific class of feedforward neural networks for which the training problem admits a unique global optimizer. As a consequence, it presents an algorithm that finds this global optimum with a linear convergence rate. The mathematical development is informative, though several aspects are not entirely clear to me.

1. Theorem 3 relies on first-order derivatives as reflected in the stated inequalities, whereas Theorem 4 advances to second-order derivatives. What necessitates this move to second-order analysis in Theorem 4?

2. The architecture appears to handle the output layer differently from the hidden layers. Since u is a matrix representing the parameters connected to the hidden layer, is the gradient with respect to u in G^Φ (3) simply the gradient with respect to vec(u)? Similar questions arise elsewhere, e.g., when constraining u to lie within a ball.

A concern (am I missing something?) is why the model needs to maximize the 1-norm of the connection parameters in (2). In most NN formulations, weight decay penalizes (reduces) the 1-norm. It seems the arbitrarily small epsilon in (2) is introduced to make the gradient strictly positive so it lies in V_++, which reads as a proof device rather than a principle justified for practical NNs. What warrants that there is no free parameter here? The setup requires non-negative data and then enforces strictly positive weights (not even zero), so contributions just add up. How practical is this design?

In the experiments, the proposed NN is compared with SVM (presumably because SVM training also has a global optimum?). In practice, in what respects would this type of NN offer additional value over more commonly used NNs (e.g., with ReLU)?
