This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with non-negative weights – with provable guarantees of global optimality and linear convergence. The core contribution lies in formulating the training problem as a non-linear spectral problem, circumventing the typical non-convexity challenges associated with neural network optimization. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, culminating in theorems guaranteeing global convergence under certain conditions, and demonstrate the feasibility of their method on several UCI datasets.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the theoretical framework. The connection to the Perron-Frobenius theory and related work on non-negative matrices is well-motivated and provides valuable context. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing global optimization approaches for neural networks (e.g., [8, 11]) and position their work as a practical alternative. The experimental results, while limited in scope, serve as a proof-of-concept and demonstrate competitive performance.

One potential weakness lies in the strong assumptions required for global optimality, specifically the non-negativity constraints on both data and weights. While justified within the theoretical framework, the practical implications of this constraint – and its impact on expressiveness – warrant further investigation. The explanation for maximizing the sum of outputs in the objective function, related to ensuring a strictly positive gradient, could be more clearly articulated for readers less familiar with the underlying mathematical machinery. The discussion of the bounds on `pu` and `pw` could benefit from highlighting the conservativeness of the derived bounds and acknowledging potential for refinement. The link between the spectral radius and the practical convergence speed isn't fully explored; a more intuitive understanding of how the spectral radius dictates performance would be valuable.

Regarding specific questions: the difference in derivative order between Theorem 3 and 4 stems from the requirements for applying the Banach fixed-point theorem – ensuring sufficient smoothness and Lipschitz continuity of the mapping GΦ. The treatment of `u` as a matrix and the associated gradient calculations are standard for vectorized matrix operations, and the authors’ approach is mathematically sound. The maximization of the sum of connection parameters, while unconventional, is crucial for the theoretical guarantees, ensuring the gradient remains positive and enabling the application of the nonlinear spectral method; it’s a byproduct of leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory rather than a standard regularization technique. The claim of having “no free parameters” is accurate given the constraints, but the practical impact of those constraints on real-world data should be further explored.  The comparison to SVM is reasonable as another method with global optimization guarantees, but a more detailed discussion of the potential advantages of this specific network architecture over more commonly used architectures (e.g., those with ReLU activations) would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of utilizing nonlinear spectral methods to achieve globally optimal training is innovative.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid mathematical foundation and rigorous proofs for its claims.
*   **Practical Feasibility:** The authors demonstrate the practical applicability of their method on real-world datasets.
*   **Significant Contribution:** The work advances the understanding of neural network optimization and provides a potential path towards training networks with guaranteed performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Strong Assumptions:** The non-negativity constraints limit the applicability of the method to specific data types.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small datasets, and further validation on larger, more complex datasets is needed.
*   **Conservativeness of Bounds:** The derived bounds on parameters may be overly restrictive.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential. While the limitations regarding the assumptions and scope of experiments should be addressed, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and potential impact of the work justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity of the explanations and to acknowledge the limitations discussed above.
