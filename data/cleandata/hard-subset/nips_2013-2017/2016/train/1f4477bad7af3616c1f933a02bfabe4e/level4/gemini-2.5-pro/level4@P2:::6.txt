### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, which the authors term "generalized polynomial neural networks." The key contribution is a theoretical guarantee of convergence to the global optimum of the training objective at a linear rate. This guarantee holds under specific, verifiable conditions on the network architecture and data. The proposed model restricts the network weights and input data to be non-negative and utilizes generalized polynomial activation functions. The theoretical foundation of the method lies in recasting the non-convex optimization problem as a fixed-point problem for a contraction mapping on a convex cone, leveraging concepts from Perron-Frobenius theory and the Banach fixed-point theorem with respect to the Thompson metric. The authors demonstrate the method on networks with one and two hidden layers and provide experimental results on several UCI datasets, showing it is a practically feasible algorithm that achieves competitive performance.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the highly challenging and fundamental problem of non-convex optimization in neural network training. Providing a practical algorithm with a provable guarantee of global optimality is a significant theoretical contribution. This work moves beyond purely theoretical existence proofs by presenting a concrete algorithm and demonstrating its application on real-world data, which is a commendable step forward compared to related works that admit their methods are "challenging to apply in practice."

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical framework is rigorous and leverages sophisticated mathematical tools from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point theory on convex cones. The proof strategy, which connects the optimization problem to finding a unique fixed point of a contraction mapping, is elegant and appears technically sound. The conditions required for the guarantee are verifiable a priori by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix, which is a strong practical advantage.

3.  **Clarity of Motivation:** The introduction is well-written, clearly situating the work within the context of recent research on understanding and improving deep learning optimization. It effectively contrasts the proposed approach with standard stochastic gradient descent (no guarantees) and other recent globally optimal methods (e.g., tensor-based approaches that require knowledge of the data-generating distribution).

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Limited Discussion of Model Expressiveness and Trade-offs:** The primary weakness is the restrictive nature of the proposed model, which requires non-negative weights and data, as well as a non-standard objective function. The authors rightly raise the issue of their model's expressiveness but do not sufficiently explore it. A more thorough discussion of the trade-off between gaining a global optimality guarantee and sacrificing model flexibility is needed. What classes of functions can this network represent well, and what are its fundamental limitations compared to standard networks with ReLU or sigmoidal activations and unconstrained weights? This analysis is crucial for readers to understand the practical domain of applicability.

2.  **Clarity of Main Results:** While the introduction is clear, the presentation in Section 2, which contains the main theoretical results, is dense and difficult to follow.
    *   **Theorem 1:** The statement of Theorem 1 is hard to parse due to its technical density. The paper would be significantly improved by preceding the formal theorem with an informal, high-level explanation of its components and implications. For instance, an intuitive explanation of the matrix A and why its spectral radius governs the convergence would be invaluable.
    *   **Objective Function `Φ`:** The motivation for the non-standard objective function `Φ` is not sufficiently explained in the main text. The roles of the term summing all outputs and the small `epsilon` regularization term are critical to the theoretical machinery but are not intuitively obvious. The paper should explicitly state why these terms are necessary to satisfy the conditions of the fixed-point theorem (e.g., ensuring the gradient remains in the interior of the positive cone).
    *   **Convergence Rate Claim:** The statement following Theorem 1, "the nonlinear spectral method has a linear convergence rate and thus the globally optimal training of the network is very fast," could be misinterpreted. The subsequent claim, based on the rebuttal, that convergence is typically achieved in "less than 10 iterations" should be clearly attributed to empirical observations from the experiments, as the theorem itself only guarantees an upper bound on the linear rate via `ρ(A)`.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments serve as a proof of concept but are not comprehensive enough to fully assess the method's practical value and limitations.
    *   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison is primarily against an RBF-SVM and standard ReLU networks. The experimental section would be stronger if it included a wider range of baselines. For instance, comparing against other optimization methods on the same restricted network architecture would help isolate the benefits of the proposed spectral method.
    *   **Lack of Computational Details:** The paper does not provide information about the computational resources (e.g., training time, memory usage) required by the different methods. Such details are essential for evaluating the practical feasibility of the algorithm, especially in relation to its competitors.

**Specific Remarks and Typos:**

*   The term "generalized polyomial" appears in the title of the paper but is misspelled as "polyomial" on line 60.
*   Line 67: "one can model..." would be better grammar.
*   Line 67: The notation for the norms, `pu` and `pw`, is used before being formally introduced.
*   The notation `R_{++}` is used but not explicitly defined, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with it. It would be helpful to define it as `{t ∈ R | t > 0}` upon first use.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and theoretically impressive result on the global optimization of a specific class of neural networks. The strengths lie in its originality and technical depth. However, its weaknesses in terms of clarifying the model's practical trade-offs, the dense presentation of the main theorem, and the limited scope of the experimental evaluation currently detract from its impact. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper.
