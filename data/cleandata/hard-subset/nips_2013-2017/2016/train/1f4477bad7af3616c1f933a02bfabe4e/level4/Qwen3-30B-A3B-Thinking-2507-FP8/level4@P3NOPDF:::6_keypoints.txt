- The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by proving optimal convergence rates for a restricted class of feed-forward networks.  
- The practical utility of the restricted model is not established, leaving unclear whether the method is useful in real-world ML.  
- The authors note expressiveness limitations twice but fail to state which tasks are feasible or infeasible under their constraints.  
- The paper should explicitly state scope and limitations (e.g., "suitable for X but not for Y due to Z").  
- Experimental evaluation is insufficient: comparisons are limited to SVMs and omit standard neural optimizers (SGD, Adam), kernels, ensembles, and other relevant baselines.  
- Computational costs (training time, memory) for all methods are unreported, preventing assessment of efficiency trade-offs.  
- Section 2 needs substantial revision for fluency and precision; many technical points are unclear.  
- Undefined or ambiguous notation/definitions: R_{++} not introduced, role of ε in Φ not motivated, p1/p2 undefined.  
- Theorem 1 is hard to parse and requires an informal intuition before the formal statement; convergence claims presented as general facts are actually empirical.  
- Typos and wording errors (e.g., "polyomial", "one CAN model") undermine credibility and must be fixed.  
- Minor writing improvements needed: be specific about statements like "proves optimal convergence" by citing the convergence rate (e.g., O(1/t)).  
- Rebuttal clarifications (ε’s role, empirical nature of convergence claims) should be integrated into the main text rather than left in the rebuttal.  
- Core weakness: conflating theoretical novelty with practical significance; without broader experiments and computational context, the method may be a theoretical curiosity.  
- Required revisions: address expressiveness limits, expand experiments with relevant baselines and cost metrics, revise Section 2 for clarity, and incorporate rebuttal clarifications.  
- Recommendation: major revision before acceptance.
