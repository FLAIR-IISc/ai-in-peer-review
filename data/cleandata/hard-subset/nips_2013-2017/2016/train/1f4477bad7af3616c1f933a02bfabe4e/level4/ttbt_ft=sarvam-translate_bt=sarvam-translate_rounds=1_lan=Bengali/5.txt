This paper discusses and defines a specific type of feedforward neural network that can be used to find a unique global optimizer. As a product, it defines the algorithm that finds the global optimizer and has a linear convergence rate. The mathematical derivation is instructive, although some parts are not very clear to me. 1. Theorem 3 involves the calculation of first-order derivatives, as shown in those inequalities, but theorem 4 reaches the level of second-order derivatives. What is the reason for this? 2. The design of such a network seems to imply that the last layer is considered separately from any hidden layers. Since u is a matrix representing the parameters connecting to the hidden layer, is the gradient with respect to u in G^\Phi (3) a gradient in terms of the vectorization of u? (Similar questions arise in many other places, such as confining u to a ball). A concern (am I missing something?) is why the model needs to maximize the 1-norm of the connection parameters in (2). In most NN models, the 1-norm needs to be reduced to reduce weight. It seems that the reason for this is that intentionally small epsilon in (2) is only needed to make the gradient strictly positive, so it is in V_++. This seems necessary for their proof, but is not well supported in practical NNs. There are no free parameters here, what is the basis for saying this? All the data must be non-negative for this to be true, and then the model uses all the positive weights (not even zero), so it keeps adding. How realistic is this design? In the experiment, the proposed NN is compared to the SVM (because the SVM also has a global optimizer?). However, in reality, does this type of NN bring any additional value in any case compared to other NNs (e.g., the most commonly used NNs, such as ReLU)?
