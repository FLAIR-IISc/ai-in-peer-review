- Paper proves a class of neural networks (positive inputs/linear weights, generalized polynomial activations) has a global optimum reachable with linear convergence and extends from one hidden layer to deep networks.  
- Authors propose a nonlinear spectral-method optimization algorithm to find that global optimum.  
- Experiments on a 2D synthetic dataset and small UCI datasets show the algorithm performs well in those settings.  
- Main novelty: first potentially practical neural network with explicit optimality and convergence guarantees under simple, checkable preconditions.  
- Preconditions are comparatively simple: depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix formed from network parameters.  
- Major limitation: requires nonnegative inputs and nonnegative linear-layer weights, which may limit model expressiveness despite inputs being shiftable.  
- Another limitation: each hidden layer uses a different activation function, which is unnatural and may restrict applicability.  
- The spectral-radius < 1 constraint forces increasing some p-values and decreasing pw/pu, effectively shrinking linear weights and potentially collapsing deep networks to near-linear behavior.  
- Concern that as input/parameter dimensionality grows, satisfying the spectral-radius constraint may require reducing linear weights further, hindering scalability to deep architectures.  
- Reviewer suggests investigating/plotting empirically how p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, and network size affect the spectral radius rho(A).  
- Overall verdict: despite concerns, the reviewer finds the work encouraging and recommends acceptance.
