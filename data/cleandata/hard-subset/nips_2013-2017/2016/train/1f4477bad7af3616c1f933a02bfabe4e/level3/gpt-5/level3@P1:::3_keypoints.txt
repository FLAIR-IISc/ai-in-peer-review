Paper studies feedforward nets with nonnegative inputs/weights and unit-specific polynomial activations, using a nonlinear spectral training method.  
Proposes a fixed-point iteration based on multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory with a simple checkable condition: spectral radius ρ(A) < 1 ensures linear convergence.  
Theorem 1 gives an a priori, easily computed matrix A whose ρ(A)<1 guarantees convergence to the unique global optimum of a modified objective.  
Method extends nontrivially to two hidden layers (Theorem 5) with analogous guarantees.  
Experiments on a 2D toy problem and small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy with much faster and more stable optimization than SGD.  
Contrasts with prior tensor-method and convex-relaxation approaches; builds on polynomial networks and nonconvex landscape literature.  
Use of Thompson’s metric and multi-homogeneous PF theory is original and technically elegant.  
Theoretical development is solid: fixed-point map GΦ, contraction via weighted Thompson metric, and uniqueness of global maximizer are convincingly argued.  
Ensuring ∇Φ>0 via small positive linear terms and sum-of-outputs augmentation is justified but unusual.  
Bounds are conservative but acknowledged; Theorem 4 offers tighter constants than Theorem 1.  
Clarity is generally good though notation is heavy; introduction could emphasize key intuitions earlier and some references/formats need fixing.  
Originality is high — first practically implementable algorithm with explicit, checkable global optimality and linear convergence for a nontrivial NN class.  
Significance is moderate-to-high: promising direction for provable training, but practical impact limited by constraints and scaling concerns.  
Main weakness: modeling constraints — nonnegative inputs/weights and a modified objective (loss minus outputs + ε-regularization) may affect calibration and expressivity.  
Heterogeneous per-unit α parameters are unnatural and may restrict design space; symmetry-breaking alternatives should be discussed.  
Scalability concern: entries of A grow with hidden units and norm bounds, making ρ(A)<1 harder to satisfy for larger networks and possibly forcing near-linear behavior.  
Empirical scope is limited to small datasets and sometimes underperforms strong baselines (RBF-SVM, ReLU); the “no tuning” claim is softened by architecture CV.  
Conservativeness of theoretical bounds is not empirically quantified; gap between Theorem 1 and Theorem 4 needs demonstration.  
Suggested improvements: sensitivity plots of ρ(A) vs. hyperparameters and architecture, report actual ρ(A) and observed convergence rates per experiment.  
Suggested improvements: compare to nonnegative baselines trained with projected SGD (without sum-of-outputs) to isolate method benefits.  
Suggested improvements: ablate distinct-α requirement, try small perturbations or learnable α, and visualize decision boundaries beyond toy examples.  
Suggested improvements: test at least one medium-scale nonnegative dataset (e.g., MNIST, bag-of-words) and profile runtime vs. SGD; clarify preprocessing and release code.  
Suggested improvements: clean references and fix minor presentation/typographical issues.  
Arguments for acceptance: novel, rigorous, checkable global optimality with linear convergence; technically elegant and implementable; first empirical demonstration.  
Arguments against acceptance: strong modeling constraints, unclear scalability to larger/deeper models, and limited experimental validation.  
Overall recommendation: Accept — advances provable training for neural nets and will stimulate follow-up work despite limitations.
