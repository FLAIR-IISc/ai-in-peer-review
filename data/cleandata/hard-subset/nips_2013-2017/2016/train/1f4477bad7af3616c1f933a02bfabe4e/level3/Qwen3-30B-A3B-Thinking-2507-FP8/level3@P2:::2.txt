

# Review for NIPS 2016 Paper: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel approach to training feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers to global optimality using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors claim their method provides the first practically feasible algorithm that guarantees global convergence with linear convergence rate under certain conditions. This work builds on recent advances in globally optimal neural network training (Haeffele & Vidal, 2014; Janzamin et al., 2015), addressing their practical limitations by providing a method that requires only checking the spectral radius of a small matrix rather than computing complex tensor decompositions.

**Strengths:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing rigorous global optimality guarantees for a specific class of neural networks. The mathematical framework is well-constructed, with careful development of the fixed-point theory and Banach fixed-point theorem application. The authors provide comprehensive proofs for their main theorems, with intermediate results relegated to the supplementary material. The paper's organization is logical, with clear progression from problem statement to theoretical development to empirical validation. The experiments on UCI datasets demonstrate that the method achieves competitive results compared to standard approaches like ReLU networks and SVMs, which is valuable for a paper claiming practical feasibility.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of the paper is the highly restrictive assumptions required for the method to work. The requirement for non-negative weights and a generalized polynomial activation function (with specific constraints on the exponents) significantly limits the model's applicability to standard neural network architectures. The authors claim these constraints don't prevent the model from achieving complex decision boundaries, but this claim is not convincingly supported by the experimental results, which show only marginal improvements over standard methods.

The empirical evaluation suffers from several issues. The comparison with SVM baselines is unfair as it uses a linear SVM rather than the more appropriate nonlinear SVM with RBF or polynomial kernels. The authors' own experiments on the UCI datasets, while small-scale, do not demonstrate scalability to larger, more complex datasets or real-world applications. The paper's focus on small datasets (with low dimensionality) is problematic as the theoretical bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, suggesting limited scalability to high-dimensional data.

The method's practical applicability is further undermined by the lack of guidance on how to choose the model parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) to achieve good performance. The paper mentions that parameters are selected from 100 randomly generated combinations via cross-validation, but this represents a significant computational burden that would be prohibitive for real-world applications. The spectral radius condition ρ(A) < 1 must be checked for each model configuration, but the paper doesn't provide practical guidance on how to efficiently identify valid parameter combinations without extensive search.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper provides the first theoretically rigorous method for globally optimal training of neural networks with linear convergence rate.
- The mathematical development is sound and well-structured, with comprehensive proofs.
- The experiments demonstrate that the method achieves competitive performance on standard datasets, showing practical feasibility for small-scale problems.

*Con:*
- The restrictive assumptions (non-negative weights, specific activation functions) severely limit the method's applicability to standard neural network architectures.
- The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous (lack of nonlinear SVM baselines, limited dataset size).
- The method's practical feasibility is questionable due to the need for extensive parameter search to satisfy the spectral radius condition.
- The paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the global optimality provides meaningful improvements over standard training methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network training, but the restrictive assumptions and limited empirical validation significantly reduce its practical significance. While the global optimality guarantee is theoretically compelling, the paper fails to demonstrate that this guarantee translates to meaningful improvements in real-world applications. The method's practical limitations (non-negative weights, generalized polynomial activation, parameter search burden) make it difficult to envision widespread adoption in the deep learning community.

The paper's significance is limited by the lack of scalability to larger datasets and the questionable relevance of its assumptions to standard deep learning practice. The authors should consider whether their method could be extended to more standard neural network architectures or whether their approach has value for specific application domains where non-negative weights are meaningful (e.g., count data, image data with specific preprocessing).

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the restrictive assumptions and limited empirical validation prevent this paper from meeting NIPS's standards for significance and practical impact. The paper would benefit from stronger empirical evidence showing the method's advantages over standard training approaches, or a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential application domains where the method might be genuinely useful.
