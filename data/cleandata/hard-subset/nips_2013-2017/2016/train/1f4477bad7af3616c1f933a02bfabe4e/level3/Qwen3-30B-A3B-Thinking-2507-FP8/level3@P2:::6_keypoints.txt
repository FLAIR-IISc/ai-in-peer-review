- Proposes a theoretically grounded method (NLSM) for training a restricted class of feedforward networks with nonnegative weights and a modified objective, claiming global optimality and linear convergence under certain assumptions.

- Theoretical contribution is strong: rigorous proofs using nonlinear spectral methods and fixed-point theory, with well-motivated main theorems (Theorems 1 and 5).

- Novel use of nonlinear spectral methods inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory provides a fresh optimization perspective.

- Provides explicit, checkable convergence conditions (spectral radius criteria) guaranteeing global optimality.

- Practical significance is limited by strong constraints (nonnegative weights, specific activations) and unclear expressiveness compared to standard networks.

- Experiments are unconvincing: limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, show competitive but not superior performance, and do not demonstrate scenarios where the method is preferable.

- Baseline comparisons are inadequate, omitting other neural-network training methods and alternative paradigms for proper contextualization.

- Related work discussion is insufficient; claims of practical feasibility are not fully contrasted with Janzamin et al. (2016) and Haeffele & Vidal (2015).

- Presentation issues: unclear/missing motivation for the modified objective and ε term, fluency problems in sections (e.g., Section 2), typos, and notation inconsistencies.

- Experimental claims (e.g., "very fast convergence") lack explanation and fail to address scalability or computational resource requirements.

- Overall: a significant theoretical advance with solid foundations but limited practical impact without better discussion of expressiveness, comparisons, clarity, and more convincing experiments.

- Recommendation: Accept for its theoretical contribution, but require revisions addressing clarity, related-work comparisons, and expressiveness/experimental limitations.
