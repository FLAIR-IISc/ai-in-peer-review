- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method that trains restricted feedforward networks (1–2 hidden layers) to global optimality with linear convergence under nonnegativity and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Builds on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and Thompson-metric fixed-point contractions, giving a checkable sufficient condition via the spectral radius of a small (K+1)/(K+2)-dimensional matrix.  
- Positions work relative to tensor methods and structured factorization results, claiming greater practicality by avoiding density estimation and offering a simple convergence certificate.  
- Theoretical development and fixed-point/convergence analysis are rigorous and carefully stated.  
- Key assumptions are restrictive: nonnegative data and weights, heavy norm constraints, a modified objective that adds the sum of outputs, and generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents.  
- Not convincingly shown that these restrictions preserve sufficient expressive power for practical tasks.  
- Empirical comparisons are weak/unfair: linear SVM baseline appears untuned and nonlinear SVMs (RBF/polynomial) are missing.  
- Experiments use small, low-dimensional UCI datasets, leaving scalability and real-world impact unclear.  
- Bounds that define the matrix A grow with hidden-unit count, implying the spectral-radius condition likely fails as network width/dimensionality increase.  
- Although the optimality condition is checkable, finding parameter configurations that satisfy it seems to require extensive search; no fast screening or constructive parameter selection is provided.  
- Clarity is good overall, but intuition for the modified objective and practical meaning/choice of exponents needs strengthening.  
- The paper should provide guidance for choosing hyperparameters to meet ρ(A) < 1 without excessive regularization.  
- Originality: novel and interesting combination of nonlinear spectral methods with global training guarantees for restricted neural networks.  
- Significance: potentially impactful if the practicality gap (assumptions, scalability, empirical validation) can be narrowed.  
- Pros: clear novel fixed-point framework, extension to two hidden layers, transparent dependence of ρ(A) on architecture, and theory that could inspire further work.  
- Cons: strong/counterintuitive restrictions, unconvincing expressivity and limited experiments, scalability concerns, lack of efficient hyperparameter identification, and uncertainty whether SGD attains the same optima.  
- Suggested revisions: add tuned linear and nonlinear SVM baselines, evaluate larger/high-dimensional nonnegative-feature datasets, derive tighter/less conservative bounds on A, develop fast parameter screening methods, and test whether standard SGD reaches the same optima.
