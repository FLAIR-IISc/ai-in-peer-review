- Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks.  
- Defines a generalized polynomial neural network with nonnegative weights and data, proving a unique global maximizer under mild assumptions.  
- NLSM converges linearly to the global optimum using Perron–Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, with convergence verifiable via the spectral radius of a small matrix.  
- Main strength is the theoretical contribution: first practically feasible method with provable global optimality guarantees for neural networks.  
- Solid mathematical framework with careful development of fixed-point theory and Banach contraction arguments.  
- Experiments on UCI datasets show competitive performance against SVM and ReLU networks and faster convergence than SGD variants.  
- The move from first-order (Theorem 3) to second-order (Theorem 4) analysis is presented without clear justification.  
- Treatment of the last layer (u) is inconsistent, particularly in gradient computation.  
- Requiring strictly positive weights (no zeros) is an unnecessarily restrictive and impractical assumption.  
- Objective modification (maximizing 1-norm of parameters) is not well-justified and contrasts with typical weight-decay practices.  
- The small ε in Equation (2) appears to be a technical artifact to force strictly positive gradients rather than a meaningful modeling choice.  
- Assumption of nonnegative data limits applicability to domains like count data and reduces general practicality.  
- Practical limitations and unclear justification of key assumptions weaken the paper’s overall impact despite strong theory.  
- Comparison to SVM is misleading since SVMs are not neural networks, and the paper does not clearly outperform standard NN training methods.  
- Experiments are preliminary and fail to address parameter selection and applicability in real-world settings.  
- Overall: theoretically interesting and valuable progress toward global optimality in NN training, but limited practical relevance due to restrictive assumptions and insufficient justification of design choices.
