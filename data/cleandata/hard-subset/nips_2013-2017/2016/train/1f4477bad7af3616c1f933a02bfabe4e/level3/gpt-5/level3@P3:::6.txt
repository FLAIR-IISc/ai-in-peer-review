Summary
The paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method that provably trains a restricted class of feed-forward neural networks to global optimality with linear (geometric) convergence. The networks use nonnegative weights and operate on nonnegative inputs; hidden units apply generalized power activations with distinct exponents. The learning objective maximizes a modified criterion: negative cross-entropy plus a sum of network outputs and an arbitrarily small positive term, under norm constraints on weights. The main theorem establishes uniqueness of the global maximizer and a linear convergence rate when the spectral radius of a constructed (K+1)×(K+1) nonnegative matrix is below 1; the condition can be checked before training. The framework extends to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees. Experiments on small UCI datasets compare the method to RBF-SVMs and simple ReLU networks.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to Perron–Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous maps and fixed-point contractions in the Thompson metric. It contrasts with global optimality results via tensor methods (Janzamin et al.) and structured matrix factorization (Haeffele & Vidal), claiming greater practical applicability. It also relates to polynomial neural networks (Livni et al.). This is a novel synthesis of nonlinear spectral analysis with NN training.

Strengths
- Clear and interesting theoretical contribution: a checkable sufficient condition ensuring uniqueness and linear convergence to a global optimum for a nontrivial NN class.
- Solid proof strategy using contraction mappings and multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory; extension to two hidden layers is nontrivial.
- Practical algorithm with no learning-rate tuning; condition depends only on architecture and simple data norms.
- Initial empirical evidence that the restricted models can produce nontrivial decision boundaries and can be competitive on a few datasets.
- Well-situated with respect to two prominent global-optimality lines (tensor and factorization methods).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Expressiveness of the restricted class is insufficiently discussed. Nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights with generalized power activations imply a monotone, posynomial-like function class; the requirement of distinct exponents further narrows it. The paper should:
  - Formally characterize the hypothesis class (e.g., monotonicity, closure under positive scaling, inability to represent sign-sensitive interactions), and contrast it with ReLU networks and polynomial kernels.
  - Provide examples of functions that are representable and counterexamples that are not, and discuss whether input preprocessing (e.g., sign-splitting x → [x+, x−]) restores generality while preserving assumptions.
  - Clarify the impact of normalization constraints and the added output-sum term on decision boundaries.
- Related work is incomplete. Beyond [8, 11, 15], please discuss:
  - Connections to nonnegative/monotone networks and NMF-style models; polynomial kernel SVMs as a strong baseline for posynomial functions; spectral methods beyond tensors; and convex/monotone architectures.
  - Concurrent global/benign landscape analyses for shallow nets and alternative training paradigms (e.g., kernel approximations, random features).
- Experiments are too limited to establish practical value.
  - Add comparisons to: polynomial-kernel SVMs; logistic regression; stronger NN baselines (Adam/Adagrad, LBFGS, batch-normalized ReLUs), and random Fourier features.
  - Report computational resources: hardware, wall-clock time, iterations/epochs, and per-iteration cost for all methods (NLSM1/2, SGD, SVM). The “converges quickly” claim should be explicitly stated as an empirical observation; provide iteration counts vs. the spectral-radius bound.
  - Test larger and more diverse datasets (including higher-dimensional nonnegative data such as text TF-IDF or normalized images) and include failure cases that illuminate applicability limits (e.g., when ρ(A)≥1 or when inputs are not nonnegative).
  - Ablations: effect of the ε term, the output-sum term, distinct vs. repeated α, and sensitivity to pw, pu, ρw, ρu, and hidden width. Evaluate how ρ(A) correlates with actual convergence speed.
- Presentation can be improved. The introduction is strong, but Section 2 reads densely and abruptly.
  - Give an informal explanation of Theorem 1 before the formal statement: intuition about the map GΦ as a normalized positive, multi-homogeneous map that becomes a contraction in the Thompson metric under ρ(A)<1.
  - Motivate in the main text (not just rebuttal) why Φ includes the output-sum and ε terms: to ensure strict positivity of gradients and applicability of Perron–Frobenius/contraction arguments.
  - Reorganize to separate intuition, algorithm, and guarantees, pushing some technical bounds to the supplement; add pseudocode earlier and a figure illustrating the fixed-point iteration.

Quality
- Theory appears sound and carefully built; assumptions are clearly stated, though some bounds seem conservative (authors concede this). However, empirical validation is thin, and claims about practical usefulness are not convincingly supported by breadth of experiments or baselines.

Clarity
- Generally readable but technical sections lack fluency. Several notational issues (see below) hinder reproducibility. Please define all symbols at first use and maintain consistent notation.

Originality
- The combination of nonlinear spectral methods with NN training and a checkable global optimality condition is novel. The architectural restrictions are also distinctive.

Significance
- Potentially significant if the approach is shown useful beyond toy settings. As written, significance is limited by the narrow empirical scope and unclear expressiveness. Strengthening experiments and class characterization would substantially increase impact.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a precise characterization of the function class (e.g., monotonicity, universal approximation on R+ via sign-splitting) and formal limitations?
- How sensitive is convergence speed to the conservativeness of A? Any empirical proxy for “effective ρ(A)”?
- Does sign-splitting inputs violate any assumptions in Theorems 1/5? If not, can it expand applicability?
- What is the computational complexity per iteration and memory footprint vs. SGD/Adam?

Typos/notation
- “polyomial” (p60); “one CAN model” (p67).
- p1 and p2 are not introduced; likely pw and pu—please fix consistently.
- R_{++} is used before being defined; define R+ and R++ upfront.
- Use ε (not “> 0”) consistently and state its role and value.
- Ensure all α, β vector indices and sets J, Jc are defined on first use.

Pros
- Elegant, rigorous global optimality with a checkable condition.
- Practical, parameter-free iteration; extension to two hidden layers.
- Interesting link between spectral radius and architecture/data norms.

Cons
- Expressiveness and practical scope under strong nonnegativity and normalization constraints are not convincingly established.
- Limited experiments and missing strong baselines; no runtime/resource reporting.
- Related work and presentation need strengthening; typos/notation issues.

Overall: promising theoretical paper with an appealing algorithmic idea; currently below the bar due to limited empirical evidence and lack of clarity on expressiveness. I would lean toward a weak reject unless the authors can substantially expand the empirical section and add an explicit, nuanced discussion of the model class’s capabilities and limits.
