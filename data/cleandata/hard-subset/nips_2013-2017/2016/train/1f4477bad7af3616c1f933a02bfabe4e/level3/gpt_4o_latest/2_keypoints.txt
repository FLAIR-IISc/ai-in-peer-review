- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of certain feedforward networks (1–2 hidden layers, generalized polynomial activations, non-negative weights).
- Claims linear convergence rates and competitive performance versus standard models on small UCI datasets.
- Theoretical contribution: novel approach to global optimization in neural network training.
- Mathematical rigor: detailed proofs using fixed-point theory and spectral radius conditions with convergence guarantees.
- Efficiency: method avoids hyperparameter tuning (e.g., learning rates) and shows faster convergence than SGD in experiments.
- Practical implementation: authors implemented the method and ran experiments on real datasets.
- Restrictive assumptions: requires non-negative weights and polynomial activations, limiting expressivity and practical relevance.
- Lack of justification: paper does not sufficiently motivate or attempt to relax its strong assumptions.
- Dataset limitations: experiments use small, low-dimensional datasets that don’t demonstrate scalability or real-world effectiveness.
- Unfair baselines: comparing to linear SVMs is inadequate; nonlinear SVMs would be more appropriate baselines.
- Expressive power concerns: experimental results don’t strongly support claims of sufficient expressivity and underperform on tasks where ReLU nets excel.
- Scalability concerns: spectral radius growth with hidden units makes the method computationally challenging for high-dimensional models.
- Acceptance pros: rigorous novel theory, first practical implementation of a globally optimal NN training method, and faster convergence than SGD.
- Acceptance cons: impractical assumptions, insufficient experimental validation, and unresolved scalability issues.
- Recommendation: reject for NIPS; paper is better suited to a theoretical venue or as a foundation for future work addressing limitations.
