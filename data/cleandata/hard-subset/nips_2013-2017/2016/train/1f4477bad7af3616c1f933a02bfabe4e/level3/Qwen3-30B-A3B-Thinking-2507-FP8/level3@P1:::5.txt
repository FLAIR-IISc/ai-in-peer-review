

This paper presents a novel approach to globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors show that under weak assumptions (nonnegative data), a particular class of neural networks with generalized polynomial activations (real powers) can be trained to global optimality with linear convergence rate. This is the first practically feasible method providing such guarantees, contrasting with previous approaches like tensor methods [11] that require unknown data-generating measures or methods [8] that are "challenging to apply in practice."

The main contribution is a theoretical framework connecting neural network training to nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones, inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors develop an algorithm (Nonlinear Spectral Method) that converges linearly to the global optimum under mild conditions, which can be verified by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix. The method applies to one and two hidden layer networks, with theoretical extensions to deeper architectures.

Strengths include the clear theoretical foundation, the first practical globally optimal training method for neural networks, and the elegant connection to nonlinear spectral theory. The experiments on UCI datasets demonstrate competitive performance against SVM and ReLU networks, with NLSM2 outperforming others on several datasets (Cancer, Haberman, Pima). The linear convergence rate (Figure 2) is a significant advantage over SGD methods, which require careful learning rate tuning.

However, the work has several limitations. The nonnegativity constraints on data and weights are restrictive and may limit practical applicability, as real-world datasets often contain negative values. The requirement for strictly positive weights (no zeros) is problematic for real-world applications. The objective function includes an unusual term (maximizing the 1-norm of connection parameters) that contradicts standard weight decay practices in neural networks. The small epsilon in the objective appears to be only a proof artifact to ensure strictly positive gradients on the boundary, lacking practical justification.

The paper's mathematical development is insightful but contains unclear elements. The shift from first-order derivatives in Theorem 3 to second-order in Theorem 4 is unexplained, and the treatment of the last layer (gradient w.r.t. u) lacks consistency with standard neural network notation. Several constructions (bounding u in a ball) are not well-justified. The theoretical bounds on the spectral radius grow with hidden units, limiting applicability to low-dimensional datasets, though the authors note these bounds may be conservative.

The comparison to SVM is somewhat misleading, as SVMs are globally optimal by design, while the paper's main contribution is a globally optimal neural network approach. The comparison to ReLU networks is fair but overlooks the fact that ReLU networks with standard training (SGD) are the practical state of the art, while the proposed method requires a non-standard architecture.

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization, but the practical limitations (nonnegativity constraints, restricted dataset size, unusual objective function) significantly limit its applicability. The authors acknowledge these limitations but don't sufficiently address how they might be overcome in future work.

Pro: First practically feasible globally optimal training method for neural networks with linear convergence guarantees; elegant theoretical connection to nonlinear spectral theory; competitive experimental results on UCI datasets.

Con: Strong nonnegativity constraints on data and weights limit applicability; unusual objective function (maximizing 1-norm of parameters) contradicts standard practices; theoretical bounds grow with hidden units, limiting scalability; lack of comparison to standard neural network training approaches beyond ReLU.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization, but the practical limitations prevent it from being a significant advance for real-world applications. While the theoretical results are sound and the method is interesting, the constraints make it difficult to see how this approach could be broadly adopted. The work is clearly distinguishable from previous NIPS papers on neural network optimization (e.g., [8, 11]) by providing a practical algorithm with convergence guarantees, but the practical limitations are substantial. I recommend rejection due to the restrictive assumptions that limit the method's applicability to real-world problems, though the theoretical contribution is noteworthy.
