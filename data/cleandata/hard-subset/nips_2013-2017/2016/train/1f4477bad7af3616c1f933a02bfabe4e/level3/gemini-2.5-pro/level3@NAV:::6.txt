This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a restricted class of feed-forward neural networks with generalized polynomial activations and non-negative weights. The main contribution is a proof that, under certain checkable conditions, this method converges linearly to the unique global optimum. This work builds upon a line of research aiming to overcome the non-convexity of neural network training, seeking more practical alternatives to recent tensor-based methods [11] or theoretical results [8] that are difficult to apply.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its theoretical contribution. Tackling the non-convex optimization problem in neural networks with guarantees of global optimality is a significant and challenging direction. The proposed method, leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory, is novel and technically interesting. The ability to check the convergence condition a priori by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix is an elegant feature.

However, the paper has several major weaknesses that limit its impact. The most significant concern is the practical utility of the proposed network class. The authors impose strong constraints (non-negative data and weights, polynomial activations) but do not provide a clear discussion of the model's expressiveness. It is unclear what functions this network can approximate and, more importantly, what it cannot. Without this analysis, the practical significance of achieving a global optimum is diminished.

The experimental evaluation is also insufficient. Comparisons are limited to SVMs and simple ReLU networks, omitting other relevant neural network training methods and alternative algorithmic paradigms. The results do not clarify the model's limitations or its performance on more complex tasks. Furthermore, crucial information, such as the computational resources required by the different algorithms, is missing, making it difficult to assess the practical efficiency of the proposed method. The discussion of related work is also brief, failing to sufficiently contrast the proposed approach with other concurrent methods for provable network training.

Finally, the presentation needs significant polishing. While the introduction is well-written, later sections, particularly the dense theoretical exposition in Section 2, lack fluency and intuition. For example, an informal explanation should precede Theorem 1, and the motivation for the objective function Φ and the technical ε term should be better integrated. Numerous typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., “polyomial” in ref [1], undefined `p` in `ψp`) hinder readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and difficult problem of globally optimal neural network training.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound method with a proof of linear convergence.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Fails to convincingly demonstrate the practical utility or expressiveness of the restricted network class.
*   The experimental validation is too limited and lacks essential comparisons and details.
*   Insufficient discussion of related work.
*   Significant clarity and presentation issues in the technical sections.
