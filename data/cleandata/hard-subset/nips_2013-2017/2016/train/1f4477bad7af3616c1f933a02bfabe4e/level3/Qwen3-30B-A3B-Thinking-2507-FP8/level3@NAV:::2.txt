

This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees under specific assumptions. The authors claim to achieve linear convergence to the global optimum for 1- and 2-hidden-layer networks using a generalized polynomial activation function with non-negative weights and a modified objective function. The theoretical contribution is novel in providing convergence guarantees that are more practically feasible than previous tensor-based approaches [11, 8], with the key insight being that global optimality can be guaranteed by checking the spectral radius of a small matrix.

The main strengths include a technically sound theoretical framework with detailed proofs, clear presentation of the algorithm, and a demonstration of linear convergence in experiments. However, several significant weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution. The assumptions are highly restrictive: non-negative weights are counterintuitive for most applications, and the generalized polynomial activation function is not commonly used in practice. The experimental validation is insufficient—UCI datasets are too small to assess scalability, and the comparison with a linear SVM baseline is unfair (untuned) while omitting nonlinear SVM baselines (e.g., RBF kernel). The authors' claim that their model retains sufficient expressive power is not convincingly supported, as NLSM2 only slightly outperforms ReLU networks on some datasets, and the performance gap is not statistically significant.

The spectral radius bounds grow with hidden-unit count, raising serious scalability concerns for high-dimensional data, and the paper fails to demonstrate how to practically identify valid parameters without extensive search. The authors should test whether SGD can reach global optima on their datasets, as it's unclear whether global optimality stems from their special objective rather than the network architecture.

While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the practical limitations and weak experimental validation significantly reduce the paper's significance for the broader machine learning community. The work represents a niche theoretical advance rather than a practical improvement over existing methods.
