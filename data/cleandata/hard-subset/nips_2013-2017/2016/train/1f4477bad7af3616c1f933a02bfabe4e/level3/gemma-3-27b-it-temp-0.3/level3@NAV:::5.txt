## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a compelling approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – generalized polynomial networks with non-negative weights – with a provable guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence. This is a significant contribution, as achieving global optimality in neural network training is a notoriously difficult problem, typically tackled with heuristics like stochastic gradient descent. The authors build upon recent work attempting to provide global optimization guarantees (e.g., [8, 11]) and offer a method that, while restrictive in its assumptions, appears more practically feasible than prior approaches. 

The core strength of the paper lies in its rigorous mathematical development. The derivations leading to the main theorems are detailed and demonstrate a strong understanding of the underlying theory, drawing connections to Perron-Frobenius theory and nonlinear spectral methods. The authors clearly articulate the conditions required for global optimality and provide a method for verifying these conditions before training. The extension to two hidden layers, while brief, demonstrates the potential for generalization. The experimental results, though limited to UCI datasets, provide initial evidence that the proposed method can achieve competitive performance.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The assumptions of non-negative data and strictly positive weights are significant limitations. While justified as necessary for the theoretical guarantees, the potential for unbounded growth and impracticality in real-world applications are not fully addressed. The motivation for maximizing the 1-norm of connection parameters (equation 2) is unclear and feels counterintuitive given the common practice of weight decay. The small epsilon in (2) appears as a technical fix for gradient positivity rather than a principled component of the model. 

Several constructions, such as bounding *u* in a ball, lack sufficient justification. The shift in the theorems from using first-order derivative inequalities (Theorem 3) to second derivatives (Theorem 4) is not adequately explained. Furthermore, the treatment of the last layer’s gradient with respect to *u* seems simplified and requires clarification – is it simply the gradient of the vectorized *u*, and is this consistent throughout the paper? The experiments, while a good starting point, would benefit from a more thorough comparison to standard neural network architectures (e.g., ReLU networks) and a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach with provable global optimality guarantees.
*   Rigorous mathematical analysis and clear presentation of the theoretical framework.
*   Potential for advancing the understanding of neural network optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong and potentially limiting assumptions (non-negativity, strictly positive weights).
*   Lack of clear justification for certain design choices (1-norm maximization).
*   Limited experimental evaluation and comparison to standard methods.
*   Unclear explanations for certain mathematical transitions.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization. While the practical limitations are significant, the rigorous analysis and guarantee of global optimality are noteworthy. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity concerns and providing a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of the assumptions.
