- Proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method to train generalized polynomial neural networks with claimed global optimality under weak data assumptions and linear convergence rate.
- Method currently restricted to one- and two-hidden-layer networks, with suggested but unproven generalization to deeper networks.
- Main technical contribution: a fast algorithm that converges to the unique fixed point of a contractive map, which equals the global minimizer.
- Uses fixed-point theory to construct the contractive map and proves convergence by bounding the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix.
- Interesting theoretical insight: complex models with suitable constraints can admit unique stationary points.
- Convergence proof depends on a spectral-radius bound, which limits scalability to larger hidden layers.
- Requires many hyperparameters to be tuned, making the procedure unintuitive and cumbersome in practice.
- Spectral condition must be verified for every parameter choice, adding additional verification overhead.
- Experimental comparison to SGD and SVM on UCI datasets: only outperforms linear SVM on 2 of 7 datasets, raising questions about practical utility.
- Experiments involved about 150,000 parameter combinations via cross-validation, imposing significant computational burden.
- Paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of method and theory.
- Contribution is original: applying fixed-point/nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training is novel and significant.
- Significance is moderate: results are interesting but limited by scalability, computational cost, and restricted network class.
- Review scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 7/10.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel perspective, significant use of fixed-point theory, and potentially impactful results.
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability and computational burden, limited generalizability to other network types, and weak empirical advantages.
