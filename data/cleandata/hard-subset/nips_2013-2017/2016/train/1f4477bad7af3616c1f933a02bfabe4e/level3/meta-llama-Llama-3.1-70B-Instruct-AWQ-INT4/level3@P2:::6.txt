This paper proposes a method for training a restricted class of feed-forward neural networks, providing a theoretical guarantee of optimal convergence and demonstrating its applicability on some real-world problems. The authors tackle the highly non-convex optimization problem behind neural networks by introducing a nonlinear spectral method that achieves global optimality with a linear convergence rate.

The paper relates to previous work on neural networks and optimization, particularly in the context of deep learning. The authors acknowledge the current state of the art in machine learning and the limitations of stochastic gradient descent and its variants. They also discuss recent research that has directly addressed the optimization problem of neural networks, including the work of Haeffele and Vidal, and Janzamin et al.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a nonlinear spectral method that can train a particular class of feed-forward neural networks globally optimally. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the method, including the development of a key theorem that guarantees the convergence of the algorithm. They also discuss the application of the method to neural networks with one and two hidden layers.

The paper's strengths include its technical rigor and the novelty of the proposed method. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm and its properties, including its convergence rate and the conditions under which it achieves global optimality. The paper also includes experimental results that demonstrate the method's applicability to real-world problems.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. One major concern is that the paper does not convincingly show that the restricted network class is useful in practice. The authors do not provide an explicit discussion of the expressiveness of the proposed network class, including what is possible and what is out of reach. This limitation makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed method.

Another concern is that the paper does not sufficiently discuss or contrast concurrent or related approaches. The authors mention some previous work on neural networks and optimization, but they do not provide a comprehensive review of the relevant literature. This omission makes it difficult to evaluate the novelty and significance of the proposed method.

The paper's experimental results are also limited. The authors only compare their method to some SVMs and omit other neural-network training methods and alternative algorithmic paradigms. They also do not provide information on the computational resources required for the three algorithms, which makes it difficult to assess the practicality of the proposed method.

Finally, the paper's presentation needs polishing. The introduction is well-written, but later sections, especially Section 2, lack fluency. There are also several typos and notation issues that should be fixed.

In conclusion, while the paper makes a technical contribution to the field of neural networks and optimization, it has several limitations that need to be addressed. The authors should provide a more comprehensive discussion of the expressiveness of the proposed network class, contrast their method with other approaches, and provide more extensive experimental results.
