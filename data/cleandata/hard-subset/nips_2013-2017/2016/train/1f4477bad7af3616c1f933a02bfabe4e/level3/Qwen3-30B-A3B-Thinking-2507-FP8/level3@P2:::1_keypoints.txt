- Presents a theoretically grounded nonlinear spectral method for training feedforward nets with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations, yielding global optimality guarantees.
- Key insight: restricting to nonnegative weights and data transforms optimization into a nonlinear eigenvalue problem solvable by fixed-point iteration.
- Method builds on prior global-optimality work but requires only data nonnegativity rather than knowledge of the data-generating measure.
- Global optimality condition reduces to checking the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix, verifiable before running the algorithm.
- Uses Perron–Frobenius and nonlinear fixed-point theory to establish linear convergence and rigorous proofs.
- No hyperparameter tuning required for convergence rate, offering a practical advantage over SGD’s learning-rate sensitivity.
- Strong limitation: requirement of nonnegative weights and data restricts applicability to many real-world problems where negative weights are needed.
- Method demands careful selection of multiple hyperparameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu, etc.), and authors tested ~150,000 combinations, which is impractical.
- Spectral-condition checks must be repeated for each parameter choice, adding overhead and reducing scalability as hidden units increase.
- Empirical results are limited and show only marginal gains: outperforms linear SVM on 2/7 UCI sets and is comparable or slightly worse than ReLU networks and SVM overall.
- Paper inadequately addresses handling of datasets with negative-valued inputs after normalization; nonnegativity claim is too narrow.
- Suggested improvements: discuss nonnegativity limitations, explain dataset selection for applicability, compare more comprehensively with state-of-the-art for nonnegative data, and explore extensions to allow negative weights.
- Overall assessment: significant theoretical contribution but practical impact limited by strong constraints and cumbersome tuning.
- Final recommendation: reject, with acknowledgment that the theoretical insights are valuable and may inspire future work.
