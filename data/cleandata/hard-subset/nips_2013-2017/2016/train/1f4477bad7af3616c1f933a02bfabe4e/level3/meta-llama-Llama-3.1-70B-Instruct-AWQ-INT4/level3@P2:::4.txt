This paper proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method to train a particular class of feedforward neural networks, achieving global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The approach is new and interesting conceptually, as it tackles the non-convex optimization problem inherent in neural networks. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their method, including a proof of convergence and a characterization of the conditions under which global optimality is guaranteed.

The main idea behind the paper is to reformulate the optimization problem of neural networks as a nonlinear eigenproblem on a convex cone. This allows the authors to leverage existing results from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory to establish the convergence of their method. The global optimality condition is shown to depend on the network architecture parameters and can be verified by computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix.

The paper's strengths lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and the novelty of its approach. The authors provide a detailed proof of their main result, which is a significant contribution to the field of neural networks. The method's linear convergence rate is also a notable advantage, as it implies fast convergence to the global optimum.

However, there are several limitations to the paper. The most significant one is the requirement of non-negativity constraints on the network weights. This restriction may hinder the practical performance of the neural networks, as it limits their expressiveness. Additionally, the use of non-standard, very specific activation functions may not be suitable for all applications.

The empirical results presented in the paper are somewhat disappointing, as the method performs worse than SVM in the reported experiments. While the authors argue that their method is competitive with ReLU networks, the performance gap with SVM is significant. Furthermore, the claim that linear convergence "typically" implies convergence in fewer than 10 iterations is exaggerated, as the actual number of iterations required for convergence may be much higher.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough discussion of existing methods for training neural networks, including stochastic gradient descent and tensor-based approaches. However, they could have provided more context on the relevance of their method to other areas of machine learning, such as deep learning and optimization.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and interesting approach to training neural networks, but its practical applicability is limited by the strong restrictions imposed on the network architecture. While the theoretical analysis is rigorous and thorough, the empirical results are somewhat disappointing. Further research is needed to relax the constraints imposed by the method and to explore its potential applications in other areas of machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* Novel and interesting approach to training neural networks
* Rigorous theoretical analysis and proof of convergence
* Linear convergence rate is a significant advantage

Arguments against acceptance:

* Requirement of non-negativity constraints on network weights may hinder practical performance
* Use of non-standard, very specific activation functions may not be suitable for all applications
* Empirical results are somewhat disappointing, with performance worse than SVM in reported experiments
