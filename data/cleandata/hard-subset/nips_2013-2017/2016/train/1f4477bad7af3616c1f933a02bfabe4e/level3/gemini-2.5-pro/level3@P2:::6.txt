This paper proposes a novel "nonlinear spectral method" for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, which the authors term "generalized polynomial neural networks". The key contribution is a provable guarantee of convergence to the global optimum of a modified objective function, with a linear convergence rate. This guarantee holds under the condition that the spectral radius of a specific, easily computable matrix is less than one. This work connects the non-convex optimization problem of training neural networks to the theory of nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones, drawing inspiration from Perron-Frobenius theory. It positions itself relative to other recent work on globally optimal training (e.g., Janzamin et al., 2015; Haeffele & Vidal, 2015) by claiming to be the first "practically feasible" method with such guarantees. The authors provide a proof of concept on several real-world UCI datasets.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper's theoretical contribution is its main strength. The derivation of the algorithm and its convergence proof by mapping the optimization problem to a unique fixed-point problem in a complete metric space (using the Thompson metric) is elegant and appears technically sound. Providing a simple, checkable condition (ρ(A) < 1) for this guarantee is a significant result. However, the work is weakened by its experimental validation. While the authors are honest that this is a "proof of concept", the experiments are not sufficient to demonstrate that the proposed model is a useful tool in practice. The claims of practical feasibility are not fully supported by the results, where the proposed method is often competitive but does not consistently outperform well-established baselines like kernel SVMs.

**Clarity:** The introduction provides a good motivation and overview of the problem. However, the clarity deteriorates in the technical sections. Section 2, which presents the main result, is particularly dense.
*   The non-standard objective function Φ is introduced without sufficient motivation. The text states the extra terms are for technical reasons ("we need that the gradient ... is strictly positive"), but a more intuitive explanation for why this specific form is necessary and what its implications are for the learning problem would be invaluable for the reader. The role of the small ε term also needs to be clarified in the main text.
*   Theorem 1 is presented abruptly. An informal paragraph preceding the theorem that explains the core idea—that an iterative map GΦ is a contraction under certain conditions related to a matrix A, thus guaranteeing a unique fixed point which is the global optimum—would greatly improve readability.
*   The claim that the method "is very fast" (Section 2) appears to be an empirical observation from Figure 2. The theoretical convergence rate depends on ρ(A), which could be close to 1. This should be stated more carefully.
*   There are several typos and notational issues (e.g., "polyomial" in the function definition, "one can model" in Figure 1 caption, R++ is used but not defined).

**Originality:** The approach is highly original. Framing the neural network training problem in the context of nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory is a novel perspective that is fundamentally different from standard gradient-based optimization. This connection is a valuable contribution in itself. However, the discussion of related work is too brief. While Janzamin et al. and Haeffele & Vidal are cited, the paper would be stronger if it provided a more detailed comparison of the assumptions, guarantees, and practical limitations of its method versus these tensor-based and matrix factorization approaches.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is very high. A practical, globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks would be a major advance for the field. However, the current significance is limited by the strong constraints imposed by the method:
1.  **Restricted Model Class:** The method requires non-negative data and non-negative weights. While applicable to count data like images or word frequencies, this excludes many common data types.
2.  **Expressiveness:** The paper does not adequately discuss the expressive power of this network class. What kinds of functions can it represent well, and what is fundamentally out of its reach due to the non-negativity and polynomial activation constraints? This is a critical point for understanding the model's practical applicability.
3.  **Limited Empirical Performance:** The experimental results show the method is feasible but do not demonstrate a clear advantage over existing, well-understood methods. On several datasets, it underperforms standard baselines.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a highly novel and theoretically deep connection between neural network optimization and nonlinear spectral theory.
*   Provides a practical, parameter-free algorithm (no learning rate tuning) with a strong guarantee of global optimality.
*   The condition for guaranteeing convergence is simple to check a priori.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical utility of the restricted network class is not convincingly demonstrated. The expressiveness of the model is not discussed.
*   Experimental validation is limited to small datasets and does not show a compelling performance advantage over standard baselines.
*   The paper's clarity in the key technical sections needs significant improvement.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   Please provide a more detailed discussion on the expressive power of the generalized polynomial network with non-negative weights. A short theoretical analysis or even an empirical study on what decision boundaries are possible vs. impossible would greatly strengthen the paper.
*   The objective function Φ is central to the paper. Please add a more intuitive explanation in Section 2 for its structure, particularly the addition of the sum of the network outputs. What is the effect of maximizing this term alongside minimizing the loss?
*   The experimental section could be strengthened by:
    *   Including comparisons with other standard neural network optimizers like Adam or Adagrad on your specific objective function (Equation 2). This would isolate the contribution of your spectral method from the model class itself.
    *   Reporting computational resources (e.g., training time in seconds) for all methods. This is crucial for evaluating the practical speed of the proposed algorithm.
    *   Testing on slightly more challenging datasets to better understand the scaling and limitations of the method.
*   Please fix the typos and notational inconsistencies. For example: "polyomial" in Equation 1; R++ should be defined; the norms pw and pu are used in Section 2 before being defined in Theorem 1.

Overall, this is a very interesting paper with a strong, original theoretical idea. However, its current presentation and weak empirical validation limit its impact. If the authors can significantly improve the clarity and provide a more convincing case for the practical relevance and expressiveness of their model, it would be a much stronger contribution.
