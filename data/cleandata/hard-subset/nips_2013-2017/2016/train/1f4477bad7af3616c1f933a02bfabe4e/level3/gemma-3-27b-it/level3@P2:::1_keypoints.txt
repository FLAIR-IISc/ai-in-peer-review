- Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for training Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) with non-negative weights and a fixed-point iteration guaranteeing global convergence.  
- Proves linear (globally convergent) convergence under specific conditions, a rare theoretical guarantee in deep learning.  
- Leverages Perron-Frobenius theory and spectral radius calculations as the mathematical foundation for convergence.  
- Restricts focus to networks with one or two hidden layers, with authors suggesting potential generalization to deeper architectures.  
- Builds on prior global-convergence work but avoids estimating the data-generating measure and claims computational tractability under non-negativity.  
- Paper is well-organized with clear mathematical notation and logical presentation of technical details.  
- Provides proof-of-concept experiments on UCI datasets to demonstrate feasibility.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis offering global optimality and linear convergence.  
- Strength: novel connection between fixed-point theory, NLSM, and neural network training.  
- Strength: clear exposition and attempt at empirical validation.  
- Weakness: scalability concerns because the spectral-radius-bound matrix grows with hidden units, becoming a computational bottleneck.  
- Weakness: heavy hyperparameter sensitivity and costly tuning (≈150,000 combinations explored), requiring extensive cross-validation.  
- Weakness: non-negativity constraint on weights may limit model expressiveness despite generalized polynomials.  
- Weakness: limited empirical performance—outperforms linear SVM on only 2/7 datasets and is not clearly superior to standard techniques.  
- Weakness: spectral-radius bounds are conservative, potentially over-restricting the optimization space.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison to modern optimizers (e.g., Adam), making practical advantages unclear.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically sound, novel, linear convergence guarantee, and demonstrates feasibility.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scalability, onerous parameter tuning, mediocre empirical gains, and restrictive non-negativity assumption.  
- Overall: interesting and rigorous contribution with limited practical impact in current form.  
- Recommendation: weak reject; authors should address scalability, improve parameter selection efficiency, relax or justify non-negativity, and provide stronger empirical comparisons.
