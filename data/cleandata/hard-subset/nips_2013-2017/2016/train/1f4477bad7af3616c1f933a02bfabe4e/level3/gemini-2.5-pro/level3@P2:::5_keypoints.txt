- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (Perron-Frobenius / Thompson metric) that iteratively trains generalized polynomial neural nets to a unique global optimum with linear convergence under a checkable spectral condition.

- Technically rigorous and elegant: connects NN optimization to fixed-point theory on convex cones; main theorem gives an a priori spectral-radius (<1) condition and extends to two hidden layers.

- Objective function is non-standard: includes output-sum and a weight-maximizing (L1) term that contradicts typical regularization and appears engineered for the proof; practical implications are not discussed.

- Derivative mismatch concern: conditions on GΦ’s first derivatives implicitly involve Φ’s second derivatives (Hessian); the paper should clarify this and how matrix A is computed from those derivatives.

- Strict-positivity requirement: analysis works on S++ (strictly positive weights and non-negative data), disallowing zeros and potentially causing numerical instability or unbounded weight growth.

- Clarity is good overall; the proof-strategy section and main theorems are clear and well-organized.

- Notation issues: need explicitness on ∇uΦ (vectorized vs matrix gradient) and more intuitive justification for constraining weights on a sphere but analyzing a ball.

- Originality is high: novel use of nonlinear spectral methods and Thompson metric in machine learning, distinct from prior global-optimality works.

- Theoretical significance: provides a new tool showing guaranteed global optimality for a non-trivial class of networks in a practically-feasible algorithmic form.

- Practical significance limited: restrictive assumptions (non-negative data/weights, odd objective) reduce applicability; experiments are competitive but not superior to standard methods.

- Scalability concern: the requirement ρ(A) < 1 becomes harder to satisfy with larger networks, limiting applicability to small models.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel rigorous contribution, computationally feasible and parameter-free algorithm, first to show real-data experiments for globally optimal NN training.

- Arguments against acceptance: highly restrictive model assumptions, unjustified/engineered objective with weight-growth, and unclear practical advantage over flexible standard models.

- Overall recommendation: strong theoretical paper recommended for acceptance provided authors address and justify the unusual objective and practical implications.
