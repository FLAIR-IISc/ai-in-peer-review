Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) to train a class of feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers to global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The networks use generalized polynomial activations with nonnegative inputs and strictly nonnegative weights, p-norm normalization constraints on each layer, and a modified objective that equals negative cross-entropy plus the sum of network outputs and a small positivity term. The key algorithmic step is an iterative map GΦ built from normalized (componentwise) powers of gradients; its convergence is established by casting training as a multihomogeneous nonlinear eigenproblem on a convex cone and applying a Perron–Frobenius-type theory with the Thompson metric. The authors derive a sufficient condition for global optimality and linear convergence based on the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small nonnegative matrix A whose entries depend on model parameters and data norms; when ρ(A) < 1, there is a unique global maximizer in the strictly positive orthant and GΦ converges linearly to it. They extend the framework to two hidden layers. Experiments on small UCI datasets compare NLSM against ReLU networks trained by SGD and an RBF-kernel SVM.

This builds on recent work that studies global guarantees for neural nets via structured matrix/tensor factorization (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.), but those approaches are either challenging to apply or depend on modelled score functions and decomposition non-degeneracy. Here, the guarantee is driven by nonnegativity and multihomogeneity plus the new objective; the condition reduces to computing the spectral radius of a (K+1)×(K+1) or (K+2)×(K+2) matrix. The analysis draws on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction mappings in the Thompson metric.

Strengths
- Clear and rigorous theoretical development: identifies the fixed-point structure, proves uniqueness and linear convergence under explicit conditions, and carefully relates the Lipschitz constant to ρ(A).
- Novel application of multihomogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory to NN training; the construction of GΦ and the metric-based contraction argument are technically interesting.
- Guarantees are checkable a priori from model parameters and simple data norms, with explicit ways to pick pw, pu (and pv) to enforce ρ(A) < 1.
- Simple, parameter-free iteration (no learning rates), and the extension to two layers is nontrivial.
- Proof-of-concept experiments show the method yields nontrivial classifiers and converges rapidly on small datasets.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical restrictiveness: requires nonnegative inputs and weights, generalized polynomial activations with all hidden-layer exponents distinct and ≥ 1, and a modified objective that adds the total sum of outputs. This deviates substantially from widely used architectures and losses.
- Expressivity and realism: while the paper claims the class remains expressive, there is limited theoretical or empirical support (beyond a toy figure and small UCI tasks). The distinct-α requirement and nonnegativity constraints further limit modeling flexibility.
- Empirical scope: datasets are small and low-dimensional; no stress tests of scalability or higher-dimensional settings. The authors acknowledge that bounds tighten with more hidden units and that ρ(A) tends to grow with width.
- Guarantee–performance tension: although one can choose pw, pu large enough to force ρ(A) < 1, it is unclear how to choose these values and other hyperparameters to jointly meet the guarantee and achieve strong accuracy. The paper defaults to random search over models and conservative bounds, which may bias against performance.
- Baselines and fairness: baselines include ReLU (with large grids) and RBF SVM. However, the training objectives differ (standard ReLU versus the modified NLSM objective); more aligned baselines (e.g., SGD optimizing the same augmented objective with nonnegativity projection) would clarify whether global optimality is unique to the proposed method. Reporting wall-clock comparisons and ablations (effect of the added output-sum and the ε>0 term) would strengthen claims.
- Applicability: nonnegativity of inputs limits domains; preprocessing to enforce nonnegativity (e.g., shifting/scaling) may affect the ρx bound and performance. Guidance is needed.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the assumptions and steps (interior maximizer, fixed-point characterization, contraction in Thompson metric, spectral radius condition) are presented coherently. Proof details are split between main text and supplement; notation is heavy but consistent. The experimental section, while honest about limitations, is too narrow to validate practical impact or scalability.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with a clear statement of algorithms and theorems and helpful proof sketches. However, intuition for the added objective term and its impact on classification should be expanded, and a discussion of computational complexity per iteration would help. More guidance on choosing α, norms, and radius parameters for both guarantees and performance would improve clarity.

Originality
High on the theory/algorithmic side: the contraction-based, nonlinear spectral approach to achieving a unique global optimum for a nontrivial NN class seems new. The extension to multiple layers within this framework is also novel.

Significance
Moderate. The work advances our theoretical understanding of globally optimal training under structural constraints and may inspire further use of nonlinear spectral methods in ML. However, the strong modeling restrictions and modest empirical validation limit immediate practical significance.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the added “sum of outputs” term and the small ε? Can you show ablations removing or varying these terms?
- Can you provide theoretical or empirical evidence of expressivity (e.g., approximation guarantees) for the constrained class with distinct α and nonnegative weights?
- How do the guarantees and practical performance change under simple nonnegativity preprocessing (e.g., min–max scaling or offsetting features)? Any guidance on setting ρx-related bounds?
- What is the per-iteration complexity of GΦ versus a well-tuned SGD/Adam on the same objective, and how does wall-clock compare on larger data?
- Can you include a baseline that optimizes the same augmented objective with projected SGD (nonnegativity and norm constraints) to disentangle the effect of the objective from the spectral method?
- Are there tighter, data-dependent bounds for A (beyond worst-case Ψ) that mitigate the growth of ρ(A) with width?

Pros for acceptance
- Innovative theoretical contribution linking nonlinear PF theory to NN training.
- Clear conditions leading to a unique global optimum and linear convergence.
- First practically implementable algorithm with such guarantees (under stated constraints).

Cons for acceptance
- Strong restrictions (nonnegativity, generalized polynomial activations, modified loss) reduce applicability.
- Limited empirical validation on small datasets; unclear scalability and competitiveness.
- Limited guidance for selecting hyperparameters that satisfy ρ(A) < 1 while achieving good accuracy.
- Missing ablations and aligned baselines on the same objective.

Overall recommendation
Interesting and well-executed theory with promising ideas, but practical impact is currently limited by restrictive assumptions and sparse empirical validation. I lean toward rejection for NIPS in its current form, with enthusiasm to see a revised version addressing empirical scope, ablations, and guidance for practical use.
