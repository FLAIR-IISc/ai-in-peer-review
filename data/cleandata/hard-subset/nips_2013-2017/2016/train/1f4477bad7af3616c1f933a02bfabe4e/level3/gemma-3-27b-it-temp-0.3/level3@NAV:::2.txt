## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a novel approach to training feedforward neural networks, guaranteeing global optimality with linear convergence under specific conditions. The authors leverage a nonlinear spectral method applicable to 1- and 2-hidden-layer networks, relying on non-negative weights, heavy regularization, and a generalized polynomial activation function. While the theoretical results are intriguing, offering a rare guarantee in the notoriously non-convex landscape of neural network training, several concerns limit the practical impact and broader relevance of this work.

The core strength lies in the theoretical guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence, a significant contribution. The method’s reliance on checking the spectral radius of a matrix *before* training is also a positive aspect, offering a way to pre-qualify network architectures. However, the restrictive assumptions are a major weakness. The requirement for non-negative weights and the specific generalized polynomial activation function significantly limit the applicability of this method to real-world scenarios where these constraints are often undesirable or impractical. The claim that this restricted class retains sufficient expressive power is not convincingly demonstrated by the experiments.

The empirical evaluation suffers from several shortcomings. The comparison to a linear SVM baseline appears untuned, providing an unfair advantage to the proposed method. Critically, the absence of non-linear SVM baselines (e.g., RBF or polynomial kernels) prevents a proper assessment of the method’s competitive performance. Furthermore, the datasets used (UCI repository) are small in both sample size and dimensionality, hindering the evaluation of scalability and real-world applicability. The authors acknowledge the growth of the bounds on the spectral radius with the number of hidden units, raising scalability concerns for higher-dimensional data.

Finally, while the authors provide a method to check the optimality condition, it is computationally expensive and potentially infeasible for larger networks without prior knowledge or extensive parameter search. The lack of exploration of whether standard SGD can also reach global optima on these datasets further weakens the argument for the method’s necessity. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical guarantee of global optimality with linear convergence.
*   Potential for applications in specific domains where non-negativity constraints are natural.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Highly restrictive assumptions limiting practical applicability.
*   Unfair empirical comparisons and limited scalability evaluation.
*   Lack of demonstration that SGD cannot achieve similar results.
*   Scalability concerns due to spectral radius bounds.



**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting theoretical result, but its practical relevance is severely limited by the restrictive assumptions and inadequate empirical evaluation. While the theoretical contribution is noteworthy, the current form does not warrant acceptance to a competitive conference like NIPS. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to explore relaxing the constraints and conducting more comprehensive experiments on larger, more representative datasets.
