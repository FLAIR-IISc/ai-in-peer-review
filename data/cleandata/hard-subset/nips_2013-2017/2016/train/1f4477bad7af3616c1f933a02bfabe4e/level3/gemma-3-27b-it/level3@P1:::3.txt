This paper presents a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks, guaranteeing global optimality under relatively weak assumptions on the data and network architecture. The authors introduce a nonlinear spectral method that achieves linear convergence to the global optimum, a significant advance over stochastic gradient descent-based methods which lack such guarantees. While applicable in principle to deep networks, the work focuses on one and two hidden layer networks for simplicity. Experimental results on several real-world datasets demonstrate the method's effectiveness, albeit on relatively small, low-dimensional datasets.

The work builds upon recent advancements in understanding the optimization landscape of neural networks [5, 6, 9], motivated by the NP-hardness of training even single-hidden-layer networks [17]. It distinguishes itself from prior work offering global optimality guarantees [8, 11] by proposing a more practically feasible algorithm. Unlike Haeffele and Vidal [8], whose approach is described as challenging to apply, and Janzamin et al. [11], which relies on estimating the data-generating measure’s density, the proposed method only requires data non-negativity and a checkable spectral radius condition. This makes it a potentially valuable tool for applications where guarantees of optimality are critical.

The strengths of the paper are significant. The theoretical result – a globally optimal training algorithm with linear convergence – is compelling. The method's reliance on a readily checkable condition (spectral radius of a small matrix) is a major advantage. The clear presentation of the theoretical framework, although mathematically involved, is commendable.  The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their approach and suggest avenues for future research, demonstrating intellectual honesty. Finally, providing experimental validation, even on limited datasets, is a positive step towards demonstrating practical viability.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The requirement of non-negative weights and inputs is a substantial constraint that might limit the model's representational capacity. While the authors acknowledge this, the implications for real-world data, which is often not inherently non-negative, require further discussion. The enforced use of differing α values for each hidden unit, stemming from invariance properties, feels somewhat unnatural and could hinder the model’s adaptability. A further concern arises from the method's dependency on parameters *p<sub>w</sub>* and *p<sub>u</sub>*, and ρ<sub>w</sub> and ρ<sub>u</sub>, and the spectral radius constraint which essentially shrinks weights potentially collapsing network behavior.  

A deeper investigation into the relationship between network architecture (number of layers, hidden units), input dimensionality, and the spectral radius *ρ(A)* would be beneficial.  Specifically, a plot illustrating how *ρ(A)* changes with these parameters could provide valuable insights into the method's scalability and limitations. Understanding the conditions under which the spectral radius remains below 1 for realistically sized networks is crucial.  The discussion surrounding the bounds in Equation (9) suggests these may be conservative; exploring tighter bounds could expand the applicability of the method. The experimental evaluation, while a good starting point, would be strengthened by comparing the method to more state-of-the-art algorithms on larger, more complex datasets, even if this requires some relaxation of the non-negativity constraints.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:**  The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to neural network training with strong theoretical guarantees.
*   **Theoretical Significance:**  The result of achieving global optimality with linear convergence is a significant theoretical advance.
*   **Practical Potential:** The checkable precondition for global optimality offers a practical advantage over existing methods.
*   **Clear Presentation:** Despite the mathematical complexity, the paper is well-written and logically organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Restrictive Constraints:** The non-negativity constraint on inputs and weights could limit the method’s applicability.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The spectral radius constraint might hinder scalability to deeper and wider networks.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** Experiments are conducted on relatively small datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of neural network optimization. The theoretical results are strong, and the proposed method offers a promising avenue for developing more robust and reliable training algorithms. The constraints are significant, but the potential benefits of guaranteed global optimality justify further exploration. I believe the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses and recommend **acceptance** to NIPS 2016.
