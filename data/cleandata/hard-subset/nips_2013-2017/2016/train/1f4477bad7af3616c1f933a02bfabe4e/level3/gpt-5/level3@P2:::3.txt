Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies a restricted but nontrivial class of feedforward neural networks—generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights, normalized in ℓp, and with hidden-unit activations given by real powers. The authors propose a nonlinear spectral method, inspired by multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction mappings in the Thompson metric, to train these networks globally optimally. They construct an explicit update map GΦ based on normalized gradients and prove that, under a simple, checkable condition on the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 for one hidden layer, K+2 for two), the map is a contraction, implying existence and uniqueness of the global optimum and linear convergence from any strictly positive initialization. They further extend the theory to two hidden layers. Experiments on a toy 2D dataset and several small UCI datasets show competitive performance and very fast convergence compared to SGD.

This work connects to recent efforts on provable training of neural networks. Compared with tensor-based methods for shallow networks that require access to score functions/densities and nondegeneracy checks (e.g., Janzamin et al.), and with global optimality results framed via structured matrix factorization but acknowledged as hard to apply in practice (Haeffele and Vidal), the present paper offers a method that appears practically implementable with easily verifiable conditions. It also relates to polynomial networks (e.g., Livni et al.) and draws on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory (Lemmens & Nussbaum) and contraction mappings.

Quality
- The theoretical development is careful and technically solid. The main ingredients—positivity of gradients, identification of critical points with fixed points of GΦ, Lipschitz estimates via a weighted Thompson metric, and contraction via ρ(A) < 1—are clearly stated and assembled into a coherent proof of global optimality and linear convergence.
- The condition for contraction depends only on architecture and simple data bounds (via ρx), is computable before running the algorithm, and the iterative scheme has no step-size hyperparameters. The extension to two hidden layers is nontrivial and well argued.
- However, the modeling assumptions are strong: nonnegativity of inputs and weights, distinct hidden-unit exponents αi ≥ 1, and a modified training objective that adds the sum of outputs plus an ε-weight decay-like term to ensure strict positivity of gradients. The impact of this modified objective on the learned classifier vis-à-vis standard cross-entropy is only partially explored.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets; while it shows feasibility and speed, it is not sufficient to demonstrate robustness or scalability. The authors acknowledge conservatism in bounds for A but do not quantify how tight they are in practice.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized. The transition from the optimization problem to fixed-point theory is pedagogical; key lemmas and the role of the Thompson metric are explained with helpful intuition. The main theorems clearly state assumptions and conclusions.
- Some notation and definitions (e.g., the exact cross-entropy expression, the role and magnitude of ε, and the practical choice/normalization of α) could be clarified. A brief complexity discussion per iteration would help. Minor typos and incomplete references should be cleaned up.

Originality
- The combination of generalized polynomial networks with a nonlinear spectral method yielding global optimality and linear convergence appears new and interesting. The checkable spectral radius condition on a small matrix tied to architecture is particularly elegant. Extending the multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius framework to this neural setting is novel.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: provides perhaps the first practically implementable global optimality guarantee for a class of neural networks under simple conditions and with a fast algorithm.
- Practical significance is promising but currently limited by strong positivity constraints, the need for distinct exponents per hidden unit, and uncertain scalability: the spectral radius bound worsens with the number of hidden units, and satisfying ρ(A) < 1 may require large p-values, potentially shrinking effective capacity.
- Still, this is a valuable step toward principled, globally convergent training methods in neural networks and will likely stimulate follow-up work on relaxing constraints and tightening bounds.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, rigorous theory proving global optimality and linear convergence with a simple, parameter-free algorithm.
- Checkable precondition via the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix.
- Extension beyond one hidden layer.
- Experiments show feasibility and speed; first to demonstrate nontrivial classification with global optimality guarantees on standard datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong modeling restrictions (nonnegative inputs/weights; distinct α per unit; modified objective with added output sum).
- Limited empirical scope; no medium/high-dimensional tasks; performance lags SVM in several cases.
- Scalability concerns: ρ(A) increases with hidden units; bounds appear conservative; may force large p leading to reduced expressiveness.
- Limited ablation on how parameters (pw, pu, α, n1, data scaling) affect ρ(A), convergence rate, and accuracy.

Suggestions for improvement
- Empirically study ρ(A) as a function of n1, K, pw, pu, ρw, ρu, and input scaling; report observed contraction constants vs. theoretical bounds.
- Provide sensitivity analyses on α choices (spacing, magnitude) and discuss numerical stability when αi are close.
- Justify and analyze the effect of the added “sum of outputs” term on calibration and decision boundaries; compare to optimizing standard cross-entropy under the same positivity/normalization constraints.
- Discuss and experiment with preprocessing strategies to enforce nonnegativity (e.g., feature shifting) on common datasets, including images or bag-of-words.
- Include more details on computational cost per iteration and wall-clock comparisons to tuned SGD across datasets.
- Consider alternative monotone activations or reparameterizations (e.g., exponential weights) that maintain positivity but may improve expressiveness.
- Tighten the bounds for A if possible, or provide data-dependent estimates that better reflect observed convergence.

Overall recommendation
I find the theoretical contribution substantial and clean, with an algorithm that is simple and fast under verifiable conditions. Despite the modeling constraints and limited experiments, this paper meaningfully advances our understanding of globally optimal training for neural networks. I recommend acceptance.

Reviewer confidence: high on the theoretical assessment; moderate on empirical significance.
