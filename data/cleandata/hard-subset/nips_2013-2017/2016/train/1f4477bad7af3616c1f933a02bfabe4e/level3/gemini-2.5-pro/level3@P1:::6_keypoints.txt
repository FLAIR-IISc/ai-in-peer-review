- Proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that provably converges linearly to a unique global optimum of a modified objective for certain 1–2 hidden-layer networks.  
- Central theoretical contribution uses Perron–Frobenius and Banach fixed-point theory with a checkable spectral-radius condition (ρ(A) < 1).  
- Algorithm is parameter-free and practically executable, avoiding learning-rate tuning common in SGD.  
- Requires non-negative inputs and non-negative weights, and uses generalized polynomial activations—strong constraints on the model.  
- The objective is modified (sums of outputs plus small ε) for technical reasons, but the impact of this non-standard loss on real tasks is not explained.  
- Limited expressive power and applicability: non-negativity excludes many standard settings (standardized features, negative values).  
- Paper lacks a clear discussion of what functions this network class can approximate versus standard ReLU/sigmoid networks.  
- Experiments are only proof-of-concept on small UCI datasets and do not convincingly demonstrate practical significance.  
- Baselines are limited (RBF-SVM, basic ReLU); missing comparisons to modern optimizers like Adam/RMSProp and other competitive methods.  
- Results are mixed: NLSM is sometimes competitive but occasionally significantly worse (e.g., Iris, Banknote).  
- No analysis of failure modes, scaling with dimensionality/dataset size/hidden units, or robustness of the method.  
- Missing computational-cost reporting (wall-clock time, memory); per-epoch cost may be higher than SGD despite fast epoch-wise convergence.  
- Presentation issues: Theorem 1 is dense and lacks prior intuition or informal explanation.  
- Claim of "very fast convergence" needs caveats since linear rate ρ(A)^k can be slow if ρ(A) ≈ 1.  
- Several typos and undefined notation reduce readability (e.g., "polyomial", undefined R++, pw/pu norms).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a core ML problem, provides a rare global-optimality guarantee, and offers a parameter-free algorithm.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical relevance is questionable due to restrictive assumptions, experiments are insufficient, and clarity must be improved.  
- Recommendation: reject in current form but encourage revision—either strengthen empirical motivation/demonstration or reframe as a primarily theoretical contribution with clearer limitations and comparisons.
