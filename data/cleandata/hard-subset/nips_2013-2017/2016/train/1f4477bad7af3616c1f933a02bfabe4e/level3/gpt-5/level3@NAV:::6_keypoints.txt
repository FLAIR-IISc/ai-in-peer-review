- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train nonnegative generalized polynomial networks by maximizing a modified objective combining cross-entropy and a sum-of-outputs term with small positive offsets.  
- Core theoretical contribution: a fixed-point map on the positive orthant with provable global optimality and linear convergence when the spectral radius ρ(A) < 1.  
- Analysis leverages multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction in the Thompson metric; method extended to two hidden layers.  
- Experiments on several UCI datasets show competitive performance with simple ReLU baselines and slightly worse results than RBF SVMs.  
- Related to prior work on polynomial networks, tensor-based globally optimal training, and structured matrix factorization; claims improved practicality under nonnegativity assumptions.  
- Strength: solid, careful theory with a novel fixed-point construction and use of multi-homogeneous PF results in a neural-network context.  
- Strength: provides clear, a priori checkable conditions and an extension beyond one hidden layer.  
- Strength: tackles the important question of globally optimal training for nontrivial architectures.  
- Weakness: Practical utility of the restricted nonnegative polynomial network class is not convincingly established; expressiveness vs ReLU/MLPs unclear.  
- Weakness: Insufficient contrast with related/concurrent work (e.g., polynomial networks, nonnegative/monotone networks, NMF predictors, convex neural networks); needs clearer positioning relative to Janzamin et al. and Haeffele & Vidal.  
- Weakness: Limited experiments—missing important baselines (projected SGD/multiplicative updates for nonnegativity, kernel ridge/logistic regression, gradient boosting/trees, modern NN optimizers) and larger nonnegative datasets (e.g., MNIST, text BOW); lacks ablations on α and ρ(A) failure cases.  
- Weakness: Missing computational details—hardware, runtime, iteration counts, memory usage, per-epoch complexity; claim of “converges quickly” needs empirical substantiation.  
- Weakness: Technical clarifications needed—motivate objective Φ and ε before Theorem 1, add an informal explanation of Theorem 1, and discuss sensitivity to α and preprocessing for nonnegative inputs.  
- Weakness: Presentation and notation issues—Section 2 needs fluency fixes, typos ("polyomial", "one CAN model"), undefined symbols (p1/p2, R_{++}), inconsistent definitions of ρx.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution with checkable conditions and linear-rate guarantees; interesting conceptual bridge between spectral methods and NN training.  
- Cons for acceptance: expressiveness and practical impact not demonstrated; empirical evaluation and related-work coverage incomplete; presentation and notation problems.  
- Overall: Promising theory but requires stronger positioning, clarity, and broader experiments; reviewer leans toward rejection this round, encouraging revision.
