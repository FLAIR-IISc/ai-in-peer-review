- Novel method for globally optimal training of a class of feedforward neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods.
- Works under weak assumptions: nonnegative data and generalized polynomial activations (real powers).
- Theoretical framework links neural-network training to nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones, inspired by Perron–Frobenius theory.
- Presents the Nonlinear Spectral Method (NLSM) that provably converges linearly to the global optimum.
- Convergence conditions are verifiable by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix.
- Method applies to one- and two-hidden-layer networks, with theoretical extensions suggested for deeper architectures.
- Claimed novelty: first practically feasible algorithm providing global optimality guarantees, improving on prior tensor/measure-based methods.
- Experiments on UCI datasets show competitive performance; NLSM2 outperforms SVM and ReLU on several tasks and demonstrates empirical linear convergence.
- Major practical limitation: requirement of nonnegative data and weights restricts applicability to real-world datasets with negative values.
- Requirement for strictly positive (nonzero) weights is impractical and restrictive.
- Objective includes maximizing the 1-norm of parameters, which contradicts standard weight-decay regularization practices.
- Small epsilon term in the objective appears to be a proof artifact without clear practical justification.
- Mathematical exposition has unclear elements: unexplained shift from first- to second-order derivatives between Theorems 3 and 4.
- Notational/inconsistency issues in treatment of the last layer (gradient w.r.t. u) and other constructions (e.g., bounding u in a ball) lack justification.
- Scalability concern: theoretical spectral-radius bounds grow with number of hidden units, limiting applicability to low-dimensional problems.
- Comparisons: SVM comparison is misleading (SVMs are already globally optimal); ReLU comparison is fair but ignores practical dominance of SGD-trained ReLU networks.
- Overall verdict: important theoretical contribution but practical constraints and unclear/missing justifications lead reviewer to recommend rejection.
