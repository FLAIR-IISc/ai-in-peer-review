- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (non-negative weights/data, real-power activations).

- Formulates a modified optimization and derives a fixed-point iterative algorithm with a checkable spectral-radius convergence condition.

- Main theoretical result: linear convergence to a unique global minimum via a contractive map using the Thompson metric and Banach fixed-point theorem.

- Algorithm is self-contained and computationally feasible compared to prior tensor- or density-based global-optimality methods.

- Experimental evaluation on UCI datasets shows competitive but modest performance versus a simple ReLU network and limited wins over nonlinear SVM.

- The claim of being "parameter-free" is misleading: the model and convergence depend on many hyperparameters (n1, α, pw, pu, ρw, ρu).

- Authors performed extensive cross-validation (~150,000 parameter combinations), highlighting substantial tuning effort contrary to “parameter-free” implications.

- Paper is technically rigorous and well-written; the "Proof Strategy" section effectively clarifies the theoretical argument.

- The addition of the sum of network outputs in the objective lacks intuitive justification beyond ensuring a positive gradient.

- Approach is original: applies nonlinear spectral methods and fixed-point theory to neural network training and designs a special analyzable architecture.

- Significance is primarily theoretical—a proof of concept that constrained architectures can guarantee global optimality.

- Practical utility is limited by restrictive assumptions (non-negative weights/inputs, power activations, modified objective).

- Scalability is a major concern: the spectral-radius condition (ρ(A) < 1) becomes harder to satisfy as network size increases.

- Empirical results are unconvincing overall: only modest gains and limited dataset wins suggest low immediate practical impact.

- Conclusion: strong, original theoretical contribution likely to inspire further research, but currently of limited practical applicability.
