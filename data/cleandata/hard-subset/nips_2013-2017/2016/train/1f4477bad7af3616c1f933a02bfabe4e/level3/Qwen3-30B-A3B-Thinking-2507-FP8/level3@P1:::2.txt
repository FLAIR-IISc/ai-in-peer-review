

This paper presents a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers. The authors claim to provide the first practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees and linear convergence rates, requiring non-negative weights, generalized polynomial activations (with distinct exponents), and a modified objective function. The convergence guarantee depends on the spectral radius of a small matrix being less than 1, which can be checked without running the algorithm.

The work builds on recent advances in global optimization of neural networks (Haeffele & Vidal, 2015; Janzamin et al., 2015), but differs by providing a practical algorithm rather than just theoretical insights. Unlike Janzamin's tensor approach, their method avoids density estimation of the data-generating measure. The authors demonstrate their method on several UCI datasets, reporting competitive performance with ReLU networks and SVMs.

The paper's strengths include a technically sound theoretical framework with careful development of fixed point theory, elegant mathematical presentation, and clear demonstration of linear convergence rates. The theoretical contribution is significant as it provides a rare globally optimal training method for neural networks with practical convergence guarantees.

However, the paper has several substantial weaknesses. The restrictive assumptions (non-negative weights, generalized polynomial activations) severely limit applicability. The claim that "these models are rich enough to achieve good performance" is not convincingly supported - the results are marginal compared to standard methods, and the authors provide no evidence that the constraints don't prevent learning meaningful representations. The experimental comparisons are unfair, using a linear SVM rather than nonlinear SVM baselines (RBF or polynomial kernels), which are standard for these datasets. For example, the SVM column in Table 1 appears to be linear, inappropriate for datasets like Iris and Seeds where nonlinear boundaries exist.

The UCI datasets are too small in sample size and dimensionality to validate real-world applicability. The paper notes that the spectral radius grows with hidden unit count, raising serious scalability concerns for high-dimensional data, yet provides no solutions or empirical evidence addressing this limitation. The practical implementation requires checking the spectral radius condition before training, which the authors admit requires "100 randomly generated combinations" of parameters, making it impractical for real-world applications without guidance on parameter selection.

It's unclear whether the global optimality is due to the special objective function rather than the method itself. The authors should test whether standard SGD with appropriate tuning could reach the same global optima on their datasets. The paper's claim about "expressive enough to create complex decision boundaries" is not substantiated by the experimental results.

The quality of the theoretical work is strong, with careful development of the fixed point theory framework. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized presentation and consistent notation. The originality is substantial in providing a globally optimal training method with practical guarantees, though the restrictive assumptions limit the scope of the contribution. The significance is diminished by the practical limitations, as the method doesn't address the main challenges of deep learning (high-dimensional data, complex architectures).

Arguments for acceptance: Novel theoretical framework with global optimality guarantees, elegant mathematical development, linear convergence rate, and empirical validation on real datasets.

Arguments against acceptance: Restrictive assumptions severely limit practical relevance, experimental comparisons are unfair, small datasets not representative of real-world challenges, scalability concerns with hidden unit count, and unclear practical implementation guidance.

While the paper makes a genuine theoretical contribution to the field, its practical limitations and experimental shortcomings prevent it from being a strong candidate for NIPS. The authors should either demonstrate how to overcome their constraints or more clearly articulate the specific applications where these constraints are acceptable. Without addressing these limitations, the significance of the work is substantially reduced. The paper would be substantially stronger with nonlinear SVM baselines, larger datasets, and clearer guidance on parameter selection for practical implementation.
