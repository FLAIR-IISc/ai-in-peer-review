- Novel theoretical contribution: proposes a nonlinear spectral method yielding global optimality for polynomial neural nets with provable linear convergence.  
- Rigorous analysis: detailed mathematical derivation and formal convergence proof (Theorem 1) and extension to two hidden layers (Theorem 5).  
- Transformative idea: imposes non-negativity and objective modifications to convert a non-convex problem into one solvable by spectral methods.  
- Restrictive assumptions: non-negative weights and generalized polynomial activations deviate from standard practice and may limit applicability.  
- Questionable expressiveness claim: authors assert sufficient expressiveness but provide weak empirical support for this claim.  
- Scalability concerns: spectral radius bounds grow with hidden unit count, suggesting potential computational intractability for larger models.  
- Limited empirical validation: experiments confined to small, low-dimensional UCI datasets, insufficient to assess real-world performance.  
- Unfair/weak baselines: comparisons omit nonlinear SVMs (RBF/polynomial) and use an apparently undertuned linear SVM baseline.  
- Ambiguous empirical cause: unclear whether observed global optimality is due to the spectral method or trivial dataset properties that SGD could also solve.  
- Clarity and accessibility: generally well organized, but dense notation and relegation of detailed proofs to supplements may hinder broader readership.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel provable global optimizer, rigorous proofs, demonstration of extension beyond one hidden layer.  
- Rejection arguments: restrictive modeling assumptions, limited/biased experiments, scalability issues, and unclear practical relevance.  
- Overall recommendation: Reject; paper needs broader experiments, fair baselines, and discussion/solutions for scalability to improve impact.
