Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that trains a restricted class of feedforward neural networks (one or two hidden layers) to a unique global optimum with a linear convergence rate. The key ingredients are: (i) a generalized polynomial activation with unit-specific exponents αl ≥ 1; (ii) nonnegativity constraints on all weights and inputs; (iii) norm constraints and a modified objective that adds the sum of outputs and small positive linear terms to enforce positivity of gradients; and (iv) a contraction proof on the positive cone using multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and the Thompson metric. A computable condition for global optimality is given by ρ(A) < 1, where A is a (K+1)×(K+1) nonnegative matrix depending on architecture and hyperparameters; convergence is linear with rate ρ(A). The paper relates to guaranteed training via tensor methods (Janzamin et al.) and to global optimality for structured matrix factorization (Haeffele & Vidal), and positions itself as more practical by avoiding density estimation and by offering a simple iteration with no tunable learning rate. Experiments are shown on small UCI datasets.

Strengths
- Novelty: A fresh application of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory to NN training, with a clear fixed-point algorithm and a nonasymptotic linear convergence guarantee to the global optimum for 1–2 hidden layers.
- Theoretical care: The path from the constrained objective to a contraction mapping is well-structured, with explicit dependence of ρ(A) on architectural parameters and norms.
- Practical check: The global optimality condition reduces to computing the spectral radius of a small matrix, enabling pre-checks before training.
- Clarity of the core method: The mapping GΦ and its role are clearly specified; the need for distinct αl is explained via permutation invariance.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Restrictive modeling assumptions: Nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and generalized polynomial activations are counterintuitive for many applications. The resulting hypothesis class is monotone in inputs, which severely limits expressivity when features are signed or when negative interactions are essential. The paper would benefit from theoretical characterization of the approximation power under these constraints (e.g., which monotone functions on the positive orthant are representable, and how representation quality scales with n1, α).
- Objective mismatch: The modified objective (loss minus outputs plus small linear term) is central to the theory but deviates from standard cross-entropy training. It remains unclear whether the attractive convergence/global optimality is primarily a consequence of this special objective. An important control is to run SGD on the same modified objective and show whether it also consistently finds the global optimum with similar training values.
- Validity set identification: Although ρ(A) is easy to compute for a fixed architecture, the optimality condition may not hold for practically useful parameter ranges. The paper currently relies on conservative bounds and a search over (n1, α, ρw, ρu, …) to find feasible models. This can be expensive and unguided.
  • Suggest deriving tighter bounds for ρ(A) and giving actionable heuristics for setting pu, pw, ρu, ρw from data statistics (e.g., via upper bounds using Gershgorin discs or monotone rescaling to shrink ρx; continuation on norms starting from a feasible small-ρ regime; analytically bounding feasible n1 as a function of pu, pw, ρu, ρw, ρx).
  • Consider input preprocessing (splitting features into positive/negative channels or recentering) to improve both expressivity and feasibility of ρ(A) < 1.
- Scalability concerns: The entries of A and hence ρ(A) grow with the number of hidden units and with ρx, which typically increase with dimensionality. This threatens scalability to high-dimensional data. Empirically studying ρ(A) versus n1, d, and K, and the accuracy–ρ(A) trade-off on larger datasets would clarify practical limits.
- Experiments: The datasets are small (UCI) and low-dimensional; they do not probe scalability or modern workloads. The claim that the restricted class retains sufficient expressive power is not convincingly supported; the decision boundary toy and UCI results are insufficient. Stronger support should include:
  • Larger datasets (e.g., MNIST variants with positive inputs, tabular datasets with d≫100).
  • Nonlinear baselines: In the current comparisons, the SVM baseline appears under-specified and likely untuned; if linear, it is not competitive, and no polynomial/RBF SVM baselines are presented. Please include properly tuned nonlinear SVMs (RBF, polynomial) and modern shallow baselines (gradient-boosted trees, kNN).
  • Fair tuning: Ensure baselines (including linear SVM) are well-tuned with documented grids and validation procedures. Report training time and sample complexity.
  • Ablations: (i) SGD on the same modified objective; (ii) effect of removing the sum-of-outputs term; (iii) varying α, constraints, and norms; (iv) input sign-splitting to mitigate nonnegativity.
Clarity
- The paper is mathematically dense. Adding intuition for why the sum-of-outputs and positivity constraints yield a contraction would help. Provide practical guidance to choose pu, pw, ρu, ρw beyond conservative inequalities, and a flowchart for checking feasibility and selecting α.
- Some constants and sets (e.g., Ψαp,q) could be motivated with simple examples; consolidate definitions earlier and move longer derivative bounds to the appendix.
Originality
- The approach is original within the NN optimization literature, combining multi-homogeneous PF theory with a tailored NN architecture and objective to secure global optimality and linear convergence.
Significance
- Theoretical significance is solid; practical significance is currently limited by modeling constraints, feasibility issues for real-world scales, and modest empirical validation. With stronger experiments and guidance on feasibility, impact could increase as a template for globally optimal training under structural constraints.

Questions for the authors
- Can you formally characterize the function class’s expressive power under nonnegativity and generalized polynomial activations? Any universal approximation results on the positive orthant?
- How often does ρ(A) < 1 hold in practice as n1 grows? Can you provide empirical curves of ρ(A) vs. architecture and data scaling?
- Does SGD on the same modified objective reach the same optimum? How often is the global optimum also a local optimum for the standard cross-entropy objective?
- Can the nonnegativity constraint be emulated via reparameterization (e.g., squaring weights) without harming convergence?

Pros
- Clear, novel theory with computable sufficient condition and linear convergence.
- First practically implementable global-optimality method shown on real datasets.
- No learning-rate tuning; simple iteration.

Cons
- Restrictive assumptions (nonnegativity, special activation and objective) limit applicability.
- Scalability and feasibility concerns due to growth of ρ(A) with model size and data norms.
- Empirical validation is narrow; baselines appear unfair/incomplete; expressivity claims not convincingly supported.

Overall recommendation: Marginal reject (leaning reject). The theory is elegant and potentially influential, but the empirical support and practical guidance are currently insufficient for NeurIPS. With stronger, fair comparisons, larger-scale experiments, and clearer feasibility guidance, I would be inclined to support acceptance.
