

This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks with global optimality guarantees under specific constraints. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method for training feedforward networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activation functions (power activations with exponents ≥ 1), proving that their method achieves global convergence with linear rate under mild conditions. The key theoretical contribution is that the global optimality condition reduces to checking the spectral radius of a small (K+1)x(K+1) nonnegative matrix, where K is the number of classes. This is a significant departure from standard training approaches that rely on stochastic gradient descent with no optimality guarantees.

The paper builds on recent work by Haeffele and Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015) but addresses their practical limitations. Unlike Janzamin's tensor-based method, which requires knowledge of the data-generating distribution, the proposed method only requires nonnegativity of the data, which is satisfied by many real-world datasets (e.g., count data like images or word frequencies). The authors demonstrate that their approach can be extended to networks with two hidden layers, though they restrict the main presentation to one hidden layer for clarity.

The paper's strengths lie in its theoretical rigor and clear mathematical presentation. The proof strategy is well-structured, leveraging fixed-point theory and the Thompson metric on positive orthants to establish global convergence. The authors provide explicit conditions for convergence (Theorems 1 and 5) that are computationally checkable without running the algorithm. The method has no hyperparameters to tune (unlike SGD), which is a practical advantage.

However, the paper's major limitations are the strong constraints on the network architecture. The requirement of nonnegative weights and specific power activations (αl ≥ 1 with all αl distinct) significantly restricts the expressiveness of the model. This is a fundamental limitation that affects both theoretical and practical value. While the authors acknowledge these constraints, they don't adequately address whether these restrictions are necessary for their theoretical guarantees or whether they can be relaxed in practice.

The empirical evaluation is limited and somewhat underwhelming. On UCI datasets, the proposed method (NLSM1/NLSM2) achieves competitive but not superior performance compared to SVMs and ReLU networks. As noted in Table 1, NLSM2 outperforms other methods on only three datasets (Cancer, Haberman, Pima), while SVMs generally achieve the best results. The authors acknowledge that their method cannot match the performance of ReLU networks on Iris and Banknote datasets where ReLU networks can achieve perfect test accuracy due to the constraints they impose. The claim that "linear convergence typically implies convergence in fewer than 10 iterations" is unsubstantiated by the experiments, as Figure 2 shows that convergence takes more than 10 iterations on the tested datasets.

The paper's significance is limited by these practical constraints. While the theoretical contribution is valuable for understanding neural network optimization, the specific network class they consider is highly restricted and unlikely to be useful for most real-world applications where negative weights and ReLU activations are standard. The authors acknowledge this limitation, stating that "the resulting classifier is still expressive enough to create complex decision boundaries," but their experimental results don't strongly support this claim.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a genuinely novel approach to neural network training with global optimality guarantees, which is a significant theoretical contribution. However, the specific network class they consider is so constrained that the practical impact is questionable. The authors have made a clear effort to connect to relevant prior work, including the Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems (Gautier et al., 2016) and the tensor-based methods of Janzamin et al. (2015).

The paper is well-written with a clear structure, though the mathematical notation is dense. The supplementary material contains important proofs that should be included in the main paper for completeness.

In summary, this paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization. However, the strong architectural constraints significantly limit its practical applicability, and the empirical results don't demonstrate clear advantages over standard methods. The method is of interest primarily for theoretical understanding rather than as a practical alternative to SGD. The authors should acknowledge more explicitly that their method's practical utility is limited to specific scenarios where nonnegative weights are naturally present and the specific activation functions can be justified.

Overall, while the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical limitations and modest empirical results make the paper's significance somewhat constrained. The authors have made a valuable theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of neural network optimization, but the specific method they propose is unlikely to be widely adopted in practice due to its restrictive assumptions.
