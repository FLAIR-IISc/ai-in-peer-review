

# Review for NIPS 2016

This paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to training a specific class of neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors consider feedforward networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activation functions (real powers), and develop a nonlinear spectral method that converges linearly to the global optimum under mild assumptions. The key insight is that by restricting to nonnegative weights and data (common in applications like count data), the optimization problem can be transformed into a nonlinear eigenvalue problem solvable via fixed-point iteration.

The paper builds upon recent work on global optimality in neural networks, particularly the tensor-based approach of Janzamin et al. (2015) and the structured matrix factorization framework of Haeffele and Vidal (2015). Unlike these prior works, the proposed method requires only nonnegativity of the data (which is satisfied for many real-world applications including images and word frequencies) rather than knowledge of the data-generating measure. The global optimality condition depends solely on checking the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix, which can be verified without running the algorithm. This represents a significant practical advance over previous theoretical results.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by providing the first practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees for neural network training. The authors establish a rigorous connection between the optimization problem and fixed-point theory using Perron-Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous maps.

2. The method has a linear convergence rate and requires no hyperparameter tuning (unlike SGD, which requires careful learning rate selection). This eliminates a major practical barrier in neural network training.

3. The proof strategy is well-structured and leverages sophisticated mathematical tools from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory, demonstrating deep theoretical understanding.

4. The authors provide a clear explanation of the conditions under which global optimality holds and how to verify them, which is valuable for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The requirement for nonnegative weights and data represents a strong constraint that limits applicability to many real-world problems where negative weights are essential (e.g., for modeling complex decision boundaries with standard activation functions).

2. The method requires careful selection of multiple hyperparameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu, etc.), making it cumbersome to use. The authors note they tested ~150,000 parameter combinations in experiments, which is impractical for most practitioners.

3. The spectral condition must be verified for every parameter choice, adding significant overhead to the training process. The paper notes that "the magnitude of the entries of the matrix A grows with the number of hidden units," limiting scalability to larger networks.

4. The empirical results are limited in scope and show only marginal advantages over existing methods. The method outperforms linear SVM on only 2 of 7 UCI datasets, with overall performance comparable to or slightly worse than ReLU networks and SVM. This raises questions about the practical utility of the constrained model.

5. The paper doesn't adequately address how the authors would handle datasets where the data isn't nonnegative (e.g., most image data after normalization). The claim that "nonnegativity is true for all sorts of count data" is too narrow for the broad applicability claimed.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a theoretically significant contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization, providing the first practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees. However, the strong constraints (nonnegative weights and data) and the cumbersome hyperparameter tuning requirements significantly limit the practical applicability of the approach. While the theoretical contribution is substantial, the empirical results don't convincingly demonstrate superior performance over existing methods in real-world scenarios.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. More thorough discussion of the limitations of the nonnegativity constraint
2. A clearer explanation of how practitioners might identify suitable datasets where this approach would be applicable
3. More comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods that handle nonnegative data
4. A discussion of potential extensions to handle negative weights while maintaining global optimality guarantees

**Final Recommendation:**
The paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to the problem of global optimization in neural networks. While the theoretical framework is sound and the results are novel, the practical limitations significantly reduce the paper's impact. The method is limited to a very specific class of networks with strong constraints, and the empirical results don't demonstrate clear advantages over standard approaches. I recommend rejection, though the authors' theoretical insights are valuable and could inspire future work addressing the limitations of this approach.
