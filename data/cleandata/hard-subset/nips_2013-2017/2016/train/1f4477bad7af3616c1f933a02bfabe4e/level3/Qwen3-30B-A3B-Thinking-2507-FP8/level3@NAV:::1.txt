

This paper investigates a special class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations (real powers), proving global optimality for a specific training procedure. The authors introduce a nonlinear spectral method that converges linearly to the unique global minimum under certain conditions, using fixed-point theory via a contractive map construction. The key theoretical contribution lies in showing that carefully constrained complex models can admit unique stationary points, a significant departure from typical non-convex optimization challenges in neural networks.

The paper's main strengths include a technically sound theoretical foundation with a rigorous convergence proof relying on Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point analysis. The approach is novel in its application of nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training, distinct from previous work by Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015), which either lacked practical applicability or required data density assumptions. The theoretical framework is well-developed, with detailed proofs in supplementary materials.

However, several weaknesses significantly impact the paper's practical significance. The spectral radius condition must be verified for every parameter choice, creating a substantial verification overhead. The method requires extensive hyperparameter tuning (over 150,000 combinations in experiments), making it cumbersome despite the theoretical guarantees. Critically, the empirical results show only marginal improvements over linear SVM on 2 of 7 datasets, with NLSM2 outperforming other models on Cancer, Haberman, and Pima datasets but underperforming on others. The scalability limitation—where the spectral radius grows with hidden layers—severely restricts applicability to larger networks, contradicting the claim of "practically feasible" implementation.

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong but its practical utility is questionable given the experimental results and implementation complexity. While the mathematical framework represents a meaningful advance in understanding global optimality for constrained neural networks, the limited empirical validation and scalability concerns make the practical significance less compelling. The authors should address whether the theoretical guarantees translate to meaningful advantages in real-world applications beyond the limited datasets tested.

Pro: Novel theoretical approach to global optimality with rigorous convergence proof.  
Con: Limited empirical validation, scalability issues, excessive hyperparameter tuning requirements.
