This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a class of generalized polynomial neural networks to global optimality with linear convergence. The networks use nonnegative weights and nonnegative inputs and have activations of the form (u_l^T x)^{α_l} with α_l ≥ 1. The objective is a modified cross-entropy criterion: minimize cross-entropy while adding a positive sum over outputs and a small positive multiple of the sum of parameters; optimization is constrained to a product of p-norm spheres for the layers. The key technical contribution is to recast training as finding a fixed point of a multi-block, cone-preserving, multi-homogeneous map GΦ and to prove it is a contraction in the Thompson metric under an easily checkable condition: the spectral radius ρ(A) of an explicit nonnegative matrix (size K+1 for one hidden layer, K+2 for two) is < 1. The map then converges linearly to a unique fixed point, which is the unique global optimizer. The paper relates to prior lines on guaranteed training of shallow nets via tensors (Janzamin et al.) and global optimality via structured factorization regularizers (Haeffele & Vidal), and to Perron–Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous maps. The authors demonstrate proof-of-concept experiments on several UCI datasets and compare with RBF-SVM and ReLU networks trained by SGD.

Quality
- Theory: The fixed-point and contraction argument is clean and, to my knowledge, novel in the NN training context. The condition ρ(A) < 1 is explicit, checkable before training, and its dependence on architectural and norm parameters is characterized. The extension to two layers follows the same pattern. The linear convergence result and uniqueness of the maximizer are strong.
- Assumptions: The method requires nonnegative inputs and strictly positive weights, norm constraints on layers, distinct α_l, and the modified objective that adds the sum of outputs and a small positive sum of parameters. These are substantial modeling departures from standard practice, and their necessity versus convenience for the proof should be discussed more critically.
- Clarity and technical gaps:
  - Theorem 3 is framed via first-order inequalities (bounds on the Jacobian of GΦ in the Thompson metric), whereas Theorem 4 seems to rely on second-derivative information to obtain a tighter A. Please clarify where the Hessian enters, what smoothness assumptions are actually needed (C1 vs C2 in Theorem 3), and whether the improved A can be derived purely from first-order bounds leveraging multi-homogeneity.
  - The “last layer treated separately” uses u as a matrix block; it appears all gradients are computed w.r.t. vectorized u and then reshaped, and the Thompson metric weights γ allocate one block to u. Please make this explicit and check consistency across Lemmas 3–4 and Theorems 1, 4, 5.
  - The role of the ball B++ (≤ ρ) vs the sphere S++ (= ρ) alternates in the proofs and algorithm (GΦ maps B++ into itself but the optimizer lies on S++). A short lemma summarizing these inclusions and why contraction on B++ implies convergence to the maximizer on S++ would help.
  - The added terms in Φ: maximizing the 1-sum of connection parameters (even with fixed p-norms) and the sum over outputs are atypical. The text says they ensure strictly positive gradients on the cone, but this reads as a proof device. Please justify their practical effect (e.g., invariance of softmax cross-entropy to additive shifts vs the added sum of logits, bias toward dense positive weights) and report ablations: remove each term and show whether convergence or accuracy changes.
  - The ε > 0 seems introduced to force entrywise-positive gradients on the boundary. Is it needed numerically? What ε was used? What happens if ε = 0?
  - Nonnegativity and strict positivity: while many datasets are nonnegative (images, counts), strictly positive weights and the need for S++ could induce numerical fragility (no zeros). How do you handle zeros in x and weights in implementations? Is there a stable “flooring” scheme? Can data with signed features be accommodated by standard transforms (e.g., split x into positive/negative parts), and how does that affect guarantees?

Clarity
The presentation of the fixed-point framework and use of the Thompson metric is valuable, but some derivations are dense. Suggestions:
- Add a high-level derivation sketch of A for the one-layer case before introducing the refined Ψ function in Theorem 4.
- Unify notation for p, p′ across blocks; define all norms and conjugates in one place and stick to them.
- Provide a short algorithm box for both one- and two-layer cases with explicit vectorization conventions.

Originality
Using nonlinear Perron–Frobenius and multi-homogeneous contraction to obtain global optimality and linear convergence for NN training appears novel. Compared to tensor methods (which rely on access to score functions) and factorization regularization results, this is a different route with a practically checkable condition.

Significance
The theoretical contribution is interesting and may spur further work on cone-preserving neural architectures. Practically, the constraints (nonnegativity, fixed norms, distinct exponents, modified loss) and current empirical scope (small UCI datasets) limit immediate impact. It would help to:
- Evaluate on at least one higher-dimensional nonnegative dataset (e.g., MNIST) and report actual ρ(A) and observed convergence factors.
- Compare wall-clock time to reach a given training loss vs tuned SGD/Adam, and show sensitivity to architectural hyperparameters and α.
- Report ablations on α choices (including repeated α) and analyze the invariance argument leading to trivial solutions when α are equal.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, rigorous global optimality with linear convergence for a nontrivial NN class.
- Checkable precondition via a small spectral radius computation.
- Novel application of multi-homogeneous PF theory to NN training.
- Initial empirical results show the method converges fast and is competitive with ReLU on some datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong modeling constraints and modified objective that may be largely proof-driven.
- Limited empirical validation and mixed accuracy relative to SVM/ReLU.
- Some technical steps and notational choices are insufficiently clarified (order of derivatives, block treatment, B++ vs S++).
- Practical applicability to standard NN workloads remains unclear.

Questions to authors
- Can you formalize the role of second derivatives in Theorem 4 and reconcile with Theorem 3’s assumptions?
- Is the gradient with respect to u rigorously defined via vectorization throughout? Please specify in the main text.
- What ε values were used, and what changes when ε = 0?
- Can you report measured ρ(A), the chosen γ, and observed contraction rates across datasets?
- How do you preprocess signed data, and does the guarantee survive such transforms?

Recommendation
Leaning marginally positive for the theoretical contribution, contingent on clarifying the derivative order, the block-wise gradient treatment, and providing ablations on the added terms in the objective along with at least one more substantial empirical demonstration.
