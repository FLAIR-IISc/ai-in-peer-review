This paper presents a novel approach to training feedforward neural networks with one and two hidden layers, leveraging a nonlinear spectral method to achieve global optimality with linear convergence. The method imposes certain conditions on the problem, including non-negativity of the weights and a specific form of the activation function, which is a generalized polynomial. While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several real-world datasets, there are some concerns and limitations that need to be addressed.

Firstly, the assumptions underlying the method, such as non-negative weights and heavy regularization, may be restrictive and counterintuitive for many applications. The use of a generalized polynomial activation function, which is not commonly used in practice, also raises questions about the generality of the approach.

Secondly, the authors' claim that the targeted class of neural networks retains sufficient expressive power is not convincingly supported by the experiments. While the results on the UCI datasets are promising, they are not sufficient to demonstrate the scalability or real-world applicability of the approach.

Thirdly, the empirical comparisons presented in the paper are unfair, as the linear SVM baseline appears to be untuned, and no nonlinear SVM baselines (e.g., RBF or polynomial kernels) are used. This makes it difficult to assess the true performance of the proposed method.

Fourthly, the datasets used in the experiments are too small in sample size and dimensionality to validate the scalability or real-world applicability of the approach. Larger and more diverse datasets are needed to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.

Fifthly, the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which raises scalability issues for high-dimensional data. This could limit the applicability of the method to large-scale problems.

Lastly, it is unclear whether global optimality is due to the special objective function used in the paper. The authors recommend testing whether stochastic gradient descent (SGD) can also empirically reach global optima on the datasets.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting approach to training neural networks, it has several limitations and concerns that need to be addressed. Further research is needed to demonstrate the scalability, generality, and applicability of the method.
