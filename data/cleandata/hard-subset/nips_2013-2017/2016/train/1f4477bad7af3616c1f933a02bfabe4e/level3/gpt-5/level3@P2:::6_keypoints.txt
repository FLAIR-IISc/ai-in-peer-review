- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a restricted class of feedforward NNs (nonnegative inputs/weights, generalized polynomial activations) to global optimality with linear convergence.  
- Optimizes a modified objective Φ = −cross-entropy + output-sum + ε·sum(parameters); core algorithm is a fixed-point map GΦ using normalized gradients in ℓp spaces.  
- Convergence to the unique global maximizer is guaranteed under a checkable spectral-radius condition ρ(A) < 1 on a small nonnegative matrix A.  
- Framework uses multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, the Thompson metric, and Banach fixed point theorem; extension to two hidden layers provided.  
- Empirical comparisons on UCI datasets against RBF-SVM and ReLU networks trained with SGD; algorithm requires no learning-rate tuning and shows rapid convergence.  

Strengths:
- Provides a clear, substantive theoretical contribution with an easily checkable condition guaranteeing existence, uniqueness, and linear convergence.  
- Elegant, potentially broadly useful proof techniques using nonlinear Perron–Frobenius and contraction in the Thompson metric.  
- Shows extension beyond one hidden layer, indicating some generality of the approach.  
- Simple, implementable fixed-point mapping with no learning-rate tuning and fast empirical convergence.  
- First demonstration (to reviewer’s knowledge) of a globally optimal NN training method achieving nontrivial real-data classification.  

Weaknesses / Clarifications needed:
- Practical usefulness and expressiveness of the hypothesis class are not convincingly established given nonnegativity, non-integer/distinct exponents, and modified objective.  
- Paper lacks a clear discussion/characterization of the function class (representable decision boundaries, universality under constraints); needs more concrete examples beyond a toy 2D figure.  
- Motivation and informal derivation for Φ and the ε term are missing from the main text; need explanation of necessity and effect on learned classifiers plus sensitivity analyses.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited (small datasets) and missing higher-dimensional/image/text tasks where nonnegativity is natural.  
- Comparisons omit strong baselines tailored to the same hypothesis class and other global/provable methods; ablation studies are missing.  
- Missing computational resource reporting: per-iteration complexity, memory, wall-clock times, and hardware are not reported.  
- The ρ(A) < 1 condition appears conservative; paper should report distribution of ρ(A) across hyperparameters, rejection rates, and empirical contraction factors.  
- Presentation and notation need polishing; section 2 needs a high-level overview and several typos/notation issues must be fixed.  

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: Strong, careful theory with clear assumptions, but empirical section and some implementation details are lacking.  
- Clarity: Introduction clear, but main results need an informal roadmap and earlier motivation for Φ and ε; some notation introduced late/inconsistently.  
- Originality: High theoretical novelty—novel application of nonlinear spectral methods to NN training with verifiable global optimality.  
- Significance: Potentially significant if shown to scale and if hypothesis class expressiveness is demonstrated; currently limited by narrow empirical scope.  

Questions for the authors:
- Provide characterization/discussion of the hypothesis class induced by nonnegativity and generalized polynomial activations; are models universal for monotone functions on [0,1]^d?  
- How sensitive are results to ε and the output-sum term; can ε → 0 be used in practice without numerical issues?  
- What input preprocessing is recommended to satisfy nonnegativity (e.g., shifts), and how does it affect calibration/performance?  
- Report runtime/memory and hardware; include wall-clock comparisons to SGD and SVM.  
- How often does ρ(A) < 1 fail for moderate architectures, and can tighter bounds materially enlarge feasible architectures?  

Minor comments / typos:
- Fix typos: “polyomial” → “polynomial”; “one CAN model” → “one can model.”  
- Define R_{++} and introduce p_w, p_u (and p_v) when first used; standardize incomplete references and citation formatting.  
- Clarify that “converges quickly” is an empirical claim and provide runtimes; make figure/table captions more descriptive (dataset sizes, hyperparameter ranges).  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, novel theoretical framework with clean, checkable conditions and linear convergence guarantees.  
- Bridges Perron–Frobenius theory and NN training; proofs and methods could stimulate follow-up work.  
- First empirical demonstration of a globally optimal NN training algorithm, showing promise.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical value not convincingly established due to unclear expressiveness and applicability boundaries.  
- Experiments are limited and small-scale without strong/tailored baselines, ablations, or runtime reporting.  
- Presentation gaps (motivation of Φ/ε, informal intuition) and multiple typos/notation issues reduce clarity.  

Overall verdict:
- Theoretical advance is compelling, but the paper needs stronger empirical validation, expressiveness analysis, and presentation improvements to demonstrate practical relevance.
