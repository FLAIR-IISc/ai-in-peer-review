This paper proposes a novel approach to training neural networks globally optimally using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors focus on a specific class of feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers and nonnegative weights. They prove that under certain conditions, the optimization problem behind these networks has a unique global optimum that can be achieved using their proposed method.

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. The authors prove that a specific class of neural networks has a unique global optimum under certain conditions.
2. They propose a nonlinear spectral method that can achieve this global optimum with a linear convergence rate.
3. They provide a characterization of the Lipschitz constant of the mapping used in their method, which is crucial for proving convergence.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The authors provide a rigorous proof of the existence of a unique global optimum for a specific class of neural networks.
2. Their proposed method is novel and has a linear convergence rate, which is a significant improvement over traditional stochastic gradient descent methods.
3. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their method, including a characterization of the Lipschitz constant of the mapping used.

However, there are also some weaknesses and limitations to this paper:

1. The method requires careful tuning of several hyperparameters, which can be cumbersome and unintuitive.
2. The spectral condition required for convergence can be difficult to verify, especially for larger hidden layers.
3. The method is limited to neural networks with nonnegative weights, which may not be suitable for all applications.
4. The experiments are limited to low-dimensional datasets, and it is unclear how the method will perform on larger datasets.

In terms of originality, this paper proposes a novel approach to training neural networks globally optimally, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, the idea of using nonlinear spectral methods is not entirely new, and the authors build upon existing work in this area.

In terms of significance, this paper has the potential to impact the field of deep learning by providing a new approach to training neural networks. However, the limitations of the method, such as the requirement for nonnegative weights and the difficulty of verifying the spectral condition, may limit its practical applicability.
