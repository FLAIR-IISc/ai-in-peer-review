- Paper studies generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative inputs/weights, ℓp normalization, and power activations, and proposes a nonlinear spectral training method.
- Constructs a normalized-gradient fixed-point map GΦ and proves global optimality and linear convergence under a spectral-radius condition ρ(A) < 1.
- The contraction condition depends on a small nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 or K+2) and is computable before training.
- Theory uses multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and the Thompson metric to obtain Lipschitz estimates and contraction.
- Extension from one to two hidden layers is nontrivial and well argued.
- Theoretical development is careful, technically solid, and assembled into coherent proofs.
- The iterative algorithm is parameter-free (no step-size) and requires only strictly positive initialization.
- Modeling assumptions are strong: nonnegativity of inputs/weights, distinct hidden-unit exponents αi ≥ 1, and a modified objective adding sum-of-outputs + ε.
- Impact of the modified objective versus standard cross-entropy is only partially explored.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to toy 2D and small UCI datasets; shows fast convergence but limited evidence of robustness or scalability.
- Bounds for A appear conservative and the paper does not quantify their tightness in practice.
- Clarity is generally good; transition to fixed-point theory is pedagogical, but some notation, the role/magnitude of ε, and complexity per iteration need clarification.
- The combination of polynomial networks with a nonlinear spectral method and a checkable spectral-radius condition is novel and interesting.
- Conceptually significant as a practical global-optimality guarantee for a neural class; practical significance limited by positivity constraints and scalability issues.
- Scalability concern: ρ(A) worsens with the number of hidden units and may force large p-values, reducing expressiveness.
- Pros: rigorous global-optimality and linear convergence proofs, checkable precondition, extension beyond one layer, and feasible fast experiments.
- Cons: restrictive modeling assumptions, limited empirical scope, potential performance gap with SVMs, and conservative/tightness issues for bounds.
- Suggested improvements: empirically study ρ(A) vs architecture/hyperparameters and observed contraction, sensitivity analysis for α choices, and effect of the added output-sum term.
- Additional suggestions: discuss preprocessing for nonnegativity, report per-iteration complexity and wall-clock comparisons to SGD, explore alternative positivity-preserving parametrizations, and tighten or provide data-dependent bounds for A.
- Overall recommendation: accept — substantial, clean theoretical contribution with promising but currently limited practical impact.
- Reviewer confidence: high for theory, moderate for empirical significance.
