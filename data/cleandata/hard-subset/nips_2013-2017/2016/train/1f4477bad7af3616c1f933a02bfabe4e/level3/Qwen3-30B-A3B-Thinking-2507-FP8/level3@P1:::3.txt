

This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks with global optimality guarantees under specific constraints. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method for training generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative inputs, nonnegative weights, and generalized polynomial activation functions (x^α with α ≥ 1). The key contribution is establishing the first practically feasible method that achieves global optimality with linear convergence rates for neural networks, addressing a significant gap in the literature where most training methods (like SGD) provide no such guarantees.

The paper makes several important technical contributions. First, it identifies a specific class of neural networks for which global optimality can be achieved: those with nonnegative inputs, nonnegative weights, and activation functions that are generalized polynomials (not ReLU or standard activations). Second, the authors prove that under conditions that can be checked by computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix (which is straightforward to verify), the optimization problem has a unique global maximizer. Third, they demonstrate that their nonlinear spectral method converges linearly to this global optimum without requiring learning rate tuning. The main theorem (Theorem 1) provides precise conditions under which convergence is guaranteed, with the spectral radius condition being both checkable and practically verifiable.

The theoretical development is impressive. The authors leverage nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point analysis to establish their results, providing a rigorous foundation that extends previous work by Janzamin et al. (2015) and Haeffele and Vidal (2015). While Janzamin's tensor-based approach requires estimating the data-generating measure (which is infeasible for high-dimensional data), and Haeffele and Vidal's method is "challenging to apply in practice," this paper's approach requires only checking the spectral radius of a small matrix - a simple computational task. The authors also successfully extend their framework to two hidden layers (Theorem 5), demonstrating the approach's scalability.

The experimental results are promising as a proof-of-concept. On small UCI datasets (with dimensions up to 10), their method (NLSM1 for one hidden layer, NLSM2 for two) achieves competitive performance with ReLU networks and SVMs, particularly on Cancer, Haberman, and Pima datasets. The training curves (Figure 2) demonstrate the expected linear convergence rate, with NLSM1 converging significantly faster than SGD with various step sizes. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach by noting that they require nonnegative inputs (which can be achieved through simple shifting), nonnegative weights, and distinct activation functions for each hidden unit (to avoid permutation invariance issues).

The main limitations of the work are the restrictive assumptions. The requirement for nonnegative weights and inputs limits applicability to domains where these constraints are natural (like count data), but may not generalize to standard image or text data without preprocessing. The need for distinct activation functions per hidden unit (α_i ≠ α_j) is unnatural and may complicate implementation. Additionally, the spectral radius condition ρ(A) < 1 requires careful tuning of p_w, p_u, and potentially reduces the effective expressiveness of the network by shrinking weight magnitudes, potentially limiting scalability to deeper architectures as dimensionality increases.

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network training. It provides the first practical method with explicit global optimality guarantees, which is a major step toward addressing the non-convexity challenges in deep learning. The conditions for convergence are simple, checkable, and do not require any hyperparameter tuning. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and note that the spectral radius bounds are likely conservative, suggesting room for improvement in the theoretical analysis.

In terms of significance, this work advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded method for training neural networks with global optimality guarantees. While the restrictions on input and weight sign limit immediate applicability to standard deep learning tasks, the theoretical framework opens new avenues for research into globally optimal training methods. The experimental results demonstrate that the approach is viable for certain problems, and the method's computational efficiency (linear convergence without learning rate tuning) is highly valuable.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical detail for reproducibility. The theoretical development is rigorous, and the experimental section effectively demonstrates the practical viability of the method on small datasets. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature, acknowledging the limitations of previous approaches while clearly articulating their contributions.

Overall, this paper represents an important theoretical advance in neural network training with global optimality guarantees. While the restrictive assumptions limit immediate practical adoption for standard deep learning tasks, the framework provides a valuable foundation for future research. The clear conditions for convergence and linear convergence rate make this method particularly appealing for applications where theoretical guarantees are paramount. The work deserves acceptance as it makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network training, with the potential to inspire more practical methods in the future. The minor limitations noted are well-acknowledged by the authors and do not detract from the paper's substantial theoretical contribution.
