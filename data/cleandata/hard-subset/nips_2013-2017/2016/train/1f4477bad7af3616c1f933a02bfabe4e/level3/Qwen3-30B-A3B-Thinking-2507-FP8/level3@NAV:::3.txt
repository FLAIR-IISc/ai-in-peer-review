

This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors focus on a specific class of networks with nonnegative inputs, nonnegative linear weights, and generalized polynomial activations (real powers). They prove that under simple spectral radius conditions (ρ(A) < 1), their nonlinear spectral method achieves linear convergence to the global optimum for both single and two-hidden-layer networks. The key innovation is providing the first practically feasible algorithm with explicit convergence guarantees, contrasting with prior work that either lacked practical applicability (Haeffele & Vidal) or required unknown data distributions (Janzamin et al.).

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with careful proofs of global optimality and linear convergence, supported by well-structured mathematical arguments. The conditions for convergence are remarkably simple to check (just spectral radius of a small matrix), making the method theoretically appealing. The experiments on 2D synthetic data and small UCI datasets show competitive performance compared to ReLU networks and SVMs, with the NLSM1/2 methods achieving good results while guaranteeing global optimality.

However, the work has notable limitations. The nonnegativity constraints on inputs and weights significantly restrict model expressiveness, despite the authors' argument about input shiftable. The requirement for different activation functions per hidden layer is unnatural and may limit practical adoption. More critically, the spectral radius condition forces shrinking of linear weights (reducing pw/pu), potentially collapsing deep networks to near-linear behavior and raising scalability concerns as dimensionality increases. The authors acknowledge these limitations but could provide more empirical analysis of how parameters affect ρ(A) as suggested.

The work excels in originality, addressing a fundamental gap in neural network optimization theory with a practically implementable solution. While the constraints limit immediate applicability, the theoretical framework opens new avenues for understanding network optimization. The significance is substantial for the optimization community, though practitioners may require further work to relax the constraints.

Overall, this is a high-quality, technically sound contribution that advances the theoretical understanding of neural network training. Despite the limitations, the paper's novel approach and rigorous guarantees make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
