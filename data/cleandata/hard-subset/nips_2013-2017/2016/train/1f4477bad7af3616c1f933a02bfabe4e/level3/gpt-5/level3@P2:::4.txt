Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method that provides a globally optimal training algorithm (with linear convergence) for a restricted class of feedforward neural networks. The networks use nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and generalized polynomial activations with layer-wise ℓp norm constraints; the objective is a modified cross-entropy that adds the total sum of outputs and a small positivity term to ensure strictly positive gradients. The main theoretical contribution is a fixed-point iteration (defined via ψp maps) whose convergence is established using nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and the Banach fixed-point theorem with the Thompson metric. A key, easily checkable sufficient condition for uniqueness and linear convergence is that the spectral radius of a small, architecture-dependent nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 for one hidden layer, K+2 for two) is strictly less than 1. The method extends from one to two hidden layers.

This work connects to recent efforts on provable training of neural networks. Compared to tensor-based methods (e.g., Janzamin et al.) that require score-function tensors and nondegeneracy conditions, and to structured matrix factorization approaches (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal) that are insightful but hard to apply, the proposed method offers an arguably more operational test (ρ(A) < 1) and a direct algorithm. It also relates to optimization on cones and Perron–Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous maps.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper provides a clear fixed-point formulation, conditions under which the mapping is a contraction, and a linear convergence rate. The conditions are explicit and checkable before training. The extension to two hidden layers is nontrivial and carefully derived. Assumptions (nonnegative inputs/weights, αi ≥ 1 and pairwise distinct, additional output-sum term, norm constraints) are clearly stated.
- Empirics: Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive performance relative to small ReLU models, but typically below an RBF-SVM. The method converges in few iterations in the presented examples, and a comparison against batch-SGD on the modified objective is included. However, the evaluation is limited in scale and scope; there is no evidence on modern high-dimensional tasks or image/text datasets where the nonnegativity assumption could naturally hold after standard preprocessing.

Clarity:
The manuscript is generally well-organized, with the fixed-point framework leading to the main theorem, and detailed statements of the matrices and constants (including the Ψ function). Some parts are notation-heavy; a high-level “recipe” that walks the reader through (i) choosing α, p’s, ρ’s; (ii) constructing A; (iii) checking ρ(A) < 1; and (iv) implementing GΦ would improve readability. The motivation and implications of adding the sum of outputs term (and the small ε) deserve more explicit discussion, including how predictions are made at test time relative to the modified training objective.

Originality:
The use of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory to derive a globally convergent fixed-point training procedure for (restricted) neural architectures is novel and interesting. The reduction of global optimality to a spectral-radius condition of a small matrix tied to the architecture is elegant. While the modeling constraints are strong, the combination of these ideas for neural network training appears new and goes beyond prior practical guarantees.

Significance:
The theoretical contribution is significant: a practically checkable sufficient condition and a simple algorithm with linear convergence, extending beyond one hidden layer. However, the practical impact is currently limited by the modeling constraints (nonnegativity of inputs and weights, special activations, norm constraints, and modified objective), and by the modest empirical performance on small datasets compared to strong baselines like RBF-SVM. If broadened (e.g., to signed weights or more standard activations/objectives) or shown to work well on large-scale nonnegative domains (images, counts, bag-of-words), the impact could be higher.

Strengths:
- Clear, rigorous theoretical framework with an explicit, verifiable global optimality condition.
- Linear convergence with a contraction factor tied to ρ(A); no learning rate tuning.
- Extension to two hidden layers with detailed derivations.
- First real-data demonstrations (to my knowledge) of globally optimal NN training under explicit assumptions.

Weaknesses:
- Strong restrictions: nonnegative inputs and weights; generalized polynomial activations with pairwise distinct exponents; fixed ℓp norms; and a nonstandard training objective that includes the sum of outputs.
- Empirical performance typically below RBF-SVM; limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets; no large-scale studies.
- The sufficient condition becomes harder to satisfy as model size grows (ρ(A) increases with width); large p’s required by conservative bounds may be impractical.
- Limited guidance on selecting α and the ρ’s; sensitivity analyses are missing.
- The statement that linear convergence “typically” yields convergence in very few iterations is not broadly substantiated; please moderate this claim or provide more comprehensive evidence.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Provide practical guidance for choosing α (beyond “distinct”), p’s, and ρ’s. How sensitive is performance and the ρ(A) condition to these choices?
- Clarify how the added sum-of-outputs term affects calibration and test-time decision rules. Do you use argmax of fr(x), and does the extra term bias probabilities?
- Evaluate on domains where nonnegativity is natural (MNIST with [0,1] pixels, bag-of-words, counts) and with higher-dimensional inputs.
- Add ablations: remove the output-sum term; vary constraints; compare to the same architecture trained on the standard cross-entropy to show the price of global optimality.
- Report wall-clock times and iteration counts; include robustness across multiple random initializations.
- Consider releasing code.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and rigorous theory yielding a simple, checkable condition and a linear-convergent algorithm.
- Conceptual advance linking neural training to nonlinear spectral methods; extension to two hidden layers.
- First practical demonstrations (albeit small-scale) of globally optimal NN training.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Strong modeling and objective constraints limit practical applicability and expressiveness.
- Empirical performance below standard baselines; small-scale evaluation.
- The global optimality pertains to a modified objective under constraints, not to standard neural training.

Overall:
A thought-provoking and technically solid paper with limited current practical reach. I lean toward rejection in its present form due to empirical limitations and restrictive modeling assumptions, but I encourage the authors to strengthen the empirical section, clarify practical choices, and discuss pathways to relax assumptions. The theoretical ideas are valuable and may inspire follow-up work.
