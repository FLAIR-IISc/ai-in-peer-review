This paper proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method for globally optimally training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, demonstrating a linear convergence rate. The approach is conceptually interesting and represents a significant departure from typical stochastic gradient descent methods. The core idea – leveraging the Perron-Frobenius theory and formulating the training problem as a nonlinear spectral problem – is a strong point. The authors correctly position their work within the recent surge of research aiming for global optimality in neural network training, citing relevant papers like Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015). The theoretical result – guaranteeing global optimality under certain conditions – is impressive.

However, the practical applicability of the method is severely limited by several restrictions. The most significant drawback is the requirement for non-negative network weights. While the authors acknowledge this, its impact is substantial. Non-negativity constraints drastically reduce the expressiveness of the network and deviate significantly from the standard practice in deep learning, where both positive and negative weights are crucial. Furthermore, the method necessitates the use of specific, generalized polynomial activation functions, preventing the utilization of more common and empirically successful activation functions like ReLU. The authors attempt to justify the need for these functions, noting that ReLU is incompatible with the non-negativity constraint, but this doesn’t alleviate the issue of restricting the network architecture.

The empirical results further dampen enthusiasm. The method consistently performs worse than SVM in the reported experiments, despite the authors' claim of achieving competitive performance on some UCI datasets. While the inclusion of experimental results is positive (many theoretical papers lack them), the inferior performance relative to a well-established method like SVM raises concerns about the practical value of the proposed approach. The comparison to ReLU networks is also relevant, highlighting a disadvantage due to the imposed constraints.  The authors' observation that the performance is sensitive to the choice of α, particularly when data is in [0,1], suggests a fragility in the method.

Regarding the convergence claim, stating that linear convergence "typically" implies convergence in fewer than 10 iterations is an exaggeration. While theoretically true under ideal conditions, practical convergence depends heavily on the spectral radius and the initial point, and achieving convergence within 10 iterations is not guaranteed or typical.  

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a rigorous theoretical analysis. However, the restrictions imposed on the network architecture significantly limit its practical utility, casting doubt on the overall completeness of the work. The authors are honest about the limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections involving the theoretical details (e.g., Lemma 3, Lemma 4) require careful reading. The notation is consistently used, and the flow of the argument is logical. Providing the full derivation for the bounds in Theorem 5 and making the conditions for application more explicit would improve clarity.

**Originality:** The approach is undoubtedly original. Combining spectral methods with neural network training and leveraging the Perron-Frobenius theory is a novel contribution. The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous attempts to address global optimality.  The thorough referencing of related work is commendable.

**Significance:**  While theoretically significant, the practical significance is questionable. The strong restrictions on network weights and activation functions severely hinder the applicability of the method to real-world problems. It’s unclear whether the theoretical benefits outweigh the practical limitations. It is unlikely that practitioners would readily adopt this method given its constraints and inferior performance compared to existing techniques.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to global optimality.
*   Linear convergence guarantee.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Includes empirical results, albeit limited.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Severe restrictions on network architecture (non-negativity, specific activation functions).
*   Inferior empirical performance compared to SVM.
*   Exaggerated claim regarding linear convergence speed.
*   Limited practical applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting approach, the severe limitations regarding network architecture and the inferior empirical performance outweigh the benefits. The work feels more like a theoretical exploration than a practical contribution to the field of neural network training. Addressing the limitations (e.g., relaxing the non-negativity constraint) or demonstrating a significant advantage in a specific application domain would be necessary to warrant acceptance.
