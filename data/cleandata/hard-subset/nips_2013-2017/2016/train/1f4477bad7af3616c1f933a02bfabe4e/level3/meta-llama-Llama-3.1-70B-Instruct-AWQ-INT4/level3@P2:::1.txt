This paper proposes a novel method for training generalized polynomial neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors claim that their approach can achieve global optimality under certain weak assumptions on the data, with a linear convergence rate. The method is restricted to one and two hidden layer networks, but the authors suggest that it can be generalized to deeper networks.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a fast algorithm that converges to the unique fixed point of a contractive map, which equals the global minimizer of the cost function. The authors use fixed-point theory to construct this map and provide a convergence proof that depends on bounding the spectral radius of a non-trivial matrix. This result is interesting because it highlights the possibility that complex models with carefully chosen constraints can admit unique stationary points.

However, the method has several limitations. The convergence proof relies on bounding the spectral radius of a matrix, which limits the scalability of the approach to larger hidden layers. Additionally, the method requires many hyperparameters to be tuned, making the procedure unintuitive and cumbersome. The spectral condition must also be verified for every parameter choice, adding extra verification overhead.

The authors present experimental results on several UCI datasets, comparing their method to stochastic gradient descent and support vector machines. While the results are promising, the method only outperforms linear SVM on two out of seven datasets, calling the practical utility of the investigated network into question. Furthermore, the experiments involved approximately 150,000 parameter combinations selected by cross-validation, which is a significant computational burden.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its underlying theory. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the approach.

Regarding originality, the paper's contribution is significant, as it provides a new perspective on training neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors' use of fixed-point theory to construct a contractive map is a novel approach that has not been explored in previous work.

In terms of significance, the paper's results are interesting and potentially impactful. However, the limitations of the approach, including its scalability and computational burden, may limit its practical utility. Furthermore, the paper's focus on a specific class of neural networks may limit its generalizability to other types of networks.
