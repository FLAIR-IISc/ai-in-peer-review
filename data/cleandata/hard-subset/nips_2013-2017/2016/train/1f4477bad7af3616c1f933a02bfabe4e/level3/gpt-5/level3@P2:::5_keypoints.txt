- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a restricted class of feedforward nets to global optimality with linear convergence.  
- Models require nonnegative data, strictly positive weights, and "generalized polynomial" activations (exponents αl ≥ 1).  
- Uses a nonstandard objective: negative cross-entropy plus sum over outputs and ε times total-parameter L1 to ensure strictly positive gradients.  
- Parameters constrained to Lp spheres (equal-norm per output row and hidden layer); transitions between sphere and ball constraints need clearer justification.  
- Key technical idea: reformulate as a fixed-point problem on a cone and apply multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory with the Thompson metric.  
- Defines a normalized gradient map GΦ and proves contraction under a checkable spectral-radius condition ρ(A) < 1 for a small nonnegative matrix A.  
- Shows unique fixed point = global optimizer and provides linear convergence bounds; extends analysis to two hidden layers.  
- Empirical comparisons on small UCI sets show competitive accuracy and much faster convergence on the proposed objective versus batch-SGD and RBF-SVM.  
- Theoretical contribution is substantial and largely technically sound; Banach fixed-point and Thompson metric usage are coherent.  
- Some derivations and modeling choices are insufficiently justified, notably the jump from first- to second-order assumptions (Theorem 3 → Theorem 4).  
- Bounding the Jacobian of GΦ implicitly requires controlling second derivatives of Φ; this dependency should be made explicit.  
- Notation mixes vector and matrix gradients for u; must clarify ∇uΦ uses Frobenius inner product / vec(u) equivalence and standardize inner products/norms.  
- Transitions among S+, S++, B++ are terse; need explicit arguments why maxima lie in strictly positive S++ and why GΦ maps B++ into itself.  
- The added ∑r fr(xi) and ε∥·∥1 terms are nonstandard, break softmax shift-invariance, and require discussion or empirical ablation to justify or replace.  
- Data nonnegativity and strict positivity of weights are strong, potentially brittle assumptions; discuss preprocessing, robustness to zero inputs, and relaxing to cone-closure.  
- Maximizing an L1 parameter sum (even small ε) contradicts typical weight decay practices; provide rationale and sensitivity analysis.  
- Explain the role and necessity of ε concretely (proof device vs. practical modeling) and whether ε = 0 is feasible in practice.  
- Provide intuition for matrix A (what each block measures and where bounds are conservative) and report actual ρ(A) values in experiments.  
- Include an algorithm box for the two-layer case and discuss computational cost per iteration.  
- Empirical scope is limited and does not clearly outperform strong baselines; practical impact is currently theoretical/conceptual rather than applied.  
- Ask whether the method is useful empirically when ρ(A) ≥ 1 and how conservative the A-bounds are in practice.  
- Request larger/higher-dimensional experiments, runtime comparisons, and baselines like polynomial networks and NMF-style models given the nonnegative structure.  
- Pros: verifiable global optimality condition, linear convergence, novel application of multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory, extension to two layers, parameter-free iterations.  
- Cons: strong modeling restrictions, unclear derivations/notation, reliance on ε and added objective terms, limited empirical validation, unclear practical advantages.  
- Overall: technically interesting and novel but clarity, justification of modeling choices, and broader empirical validation need substantial improvement; reviewer leans toward rejection but encourages revision.
