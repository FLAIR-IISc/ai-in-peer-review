

# Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks with provable global optimality guarantees. The authors define a class of feedforward neural networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative data that can be globally optimized using a nonlinear spectral method inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory. The key contribution is a globally optimal training algorithm with linear convergence rate, which represents a significant theoretical advance over previous work on global optimization of neural networks (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal, 2015; Janzamin et al., 2015). While previous methods either lacked practical applicability or required strong assumptions about the data distribution, this approach requires only nonnegative data (common in count data) and provides a simple check for convergence via the spectral radius of a small matrix.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by establishing a class of neural networks with provable global optimality. The linear convergence rate is a compelling theoretical advantage over stochastic gradient descent, which lacks global optimality guarantees. The experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate that the method achieves competitive performance, which is significant as previous global optimization methods for neural networks have not included such results. The paper's approach to avoiding hyperparameter tuning (unlike SGD) is a practical advantage worth noting.

**Weaknesses:** Several concerns limit the paper's practical significance. The requirement for nonnegative data and strictly positive weights (no zeros) is a major restriction that may prevent application to most real-world problems. The objective function modification (maximizing the sum of outputs) is not well justified and contradicts standard practice of weight decay to control norm. The small ε in equation (2) appears to be a technical artifact to ensure strictly positive gradients (V_++), with no practical justification. The paper shifts from first-order derivative inequalities in Theorem 3 to second derivatives in Theorem 4 without explanation, creating confusion about the theoretical underpinnings. The bounding of weights in a ball and the treatment of u are not clearly justified, and the paper does not address whether the model can avoid unbounded growth under its constraints.

**Quality:** The theoretical development is technically sound with rigorous proofs for the main results. The paper clearly establishes the conditions for global optimality and linear convergence. However, the experimental section is limited to small UCI datasets, and the comparison to ReLU networks is somewhat superficial, as the authors do not address why their constrained model might be preferable to standard ReLU networks in practice. The paper acknowledges these limitations but does not sufficiently explore them.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, with clear exposition of the main results. The mathematical derivations are instructive, though some parts (like the shift from first to second derivatives) are unclear. The algorithm description is precise, but the justification for key design choices (nonnegativity, objective modification) could be strengthened.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by applying nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training with global optimality guarantees. It differs significantly from previous work by providing a practically feasible algorithm (unlike Haeffele & Vidal's method) and using a different theoretical approach than Janzamin et al.'s tensor method.

**Significance:** The theoretical contribution is significant for the field, as it provides a rare example of a globally optimal training method for neural networks with a linear convergence rate. However, the practical significance is limited by the restrictive assumptions, particularly the nonnegativity constraints. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate why these constraints are necessary or how the method could be extended to more standard neural network architectures.

**Pro for Acceptance:** The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization, providing the first practically feasible globally optimal training method for a non-trivial neural network class with linear convergence. The experimental results confirm the method's viability on real datasets.

**Con for Acceptance:** The restrictive assumptions (nonnegative data and weights) significantly limit the method's practical applicability. The theoretical advantages do not clearly translate to practical benefits over standard methods like SGD with ReLU networks, especially given the lack of justification for the objective function modification. The paper does not address how to handle the most common types of data (which are not nonnegative).

In summary, this paper makes an important theoretical contribution but has significant limitations in practical applicability. While the theoretical results are sound, the restrictive assumptions and lack of clear practical advantages over standard methods make the contribution somewhat niche. The authors should address the concerns about the objective function modification, the role of ε, and the justification for nonnegativity constraints to strengthen the paper's practical relevance.
