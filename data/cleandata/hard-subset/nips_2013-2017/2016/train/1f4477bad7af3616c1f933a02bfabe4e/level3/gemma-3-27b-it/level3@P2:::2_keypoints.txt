- Presents a novel nonlinear spectral method that guarantees global optimality with linear convergence for a class of generalized polynomial neural networks.  
- Core contribution is reformulating training to allow a globally optimal solution, addressing typical non-convex optimization issues.  
- Theoretical development (theorems/lemmas) is rigorous and well-presented, though dense and requiring careful reading.  
- Proof strategy overview is helpful, but the exposition needs more intuitive explanations alongside formal derivations.  
- Requires restrictive assumptions (non-negative weights and a modified objective adding sum of outputs) that deviate from standard neural network practice.  
- Justification and discussion of how these assumptions affect model expressivity are insufficient.  
- Generalized polynomial activations (used to satisfy non-negativity) are uncommon and may limit feature learning.  
- Empirical validation is weak: experiments use small, low-dimensional UCI datasets that do not demonstrate scalability or real-world applicability.  
- Baseline comparisons are unfair/incomplete (e.g., linear SVM lightly tuned; no non-linear SVM baselines such as RBF or polynomial kernels).  
- ReLU networks match or outperform the method in practice, calling into question the method’s practical benefit despite theoretical guarantees.  
- No experiment showing whether SGD can reach the same optima, leaving unclear if optimality stems from the spectral method or dataset/architecture choices.  
- Scalability concerns: verifying convergence (ρ(A) < 1) and finding parameter settings becomes harder as hidden units and dimensionality increase.  
- Overall: interesting and novel theoretical contribution but limited practical relevance due to restrictive assumptions, weak empirical evidence, and scalability issues.  
- Recommendation: reject, with encouragement to expand experiments, compare against stronger baselines, justify assumptions, and analyze scalability.
