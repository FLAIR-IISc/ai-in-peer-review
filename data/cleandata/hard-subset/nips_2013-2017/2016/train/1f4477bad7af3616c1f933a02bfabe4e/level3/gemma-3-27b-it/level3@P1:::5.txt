This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights – with a guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence. This is a significant contribution, as training neural networks is typically hampered by non-convex optimization landscapes and the lack of guarantees regarding the solution’s quality. The authors leverage nonlinear spectral methods and build upon recent work in structured matrix factorization and tensor decomposition to achieve this result, addressing a crucial challenge in deep learning. The paper builds on prior work such as [11, 8] which also attempt to find globally optimal training algorithms, but argues their approaches are less practical, citing computational complexity or reliance on estimating the data generating measure.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The theoretical results are compelling, demonstrating a practically feasible algorithm for global optimization. The authors provide clear convergence guarantees under relatively weak assumptions (non-negativity of data), which is a strong advantage. The linear convergence rate is particularly noteworthy. The inclusion of experimental results, albeit on relatively small UCI datasets, is a positive step towards demonstrating the practical viability of the proposed method, and the comparison to SVM and ReLU networks provides some context. The authors also acknowledge the limitations and potential issues with the approach.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the mathematical derivations are detailed, they are also dense and occasionally unclear. The shift from using first-order derivative inequalities in Theorem 3 to second derivatives in Theorem 4 requires further explanation; the motivation for this change isn’t immediately obvious. The treatment of the input layer weights ‘u’ is also somewhat opaque; it's unclear how the gradient is defined for this vectorized quantity and whether this is consistently applied throughout the paper. Several constructions, such as bounding 'u' within a ball, lack sufficient justification, potentially hindering reproducibility.  

The addition of the small epsilon term in the objective function (2), while mathematically convenient for ensuring strictly positive gradients, appears to be a purely technical artifact with no clear practical motivation. The constraint of requiring non-negative data and strictly positive weights is a significant limitation. While this simplifies the optimization problem, it may render the model unsuitable for many real-world applications and could potentially lead to instability or unbounded growth in the network’s outputs. Furthermore, the choice to maximize the 1-norm of connection parameters is unusual given the more common practice of using weight decay (L2 regularization) to penalize large weights – the rationale for this specific choice needs further elaboration.  

The experiments, while present, are limited in scope. The comparison to SVM is relevant given its ability to find a global optimum, however, it remains unclear whether this proposed network architecture offers a compelling practical advantage over more established neural network architectures (e.g., those employing ReLU activations and standard stochastic gradient descent).  The performance on the UCI datasets is competitive but not demonstrably superior to existing methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The paper tackles a fundamental problem in deep learning - global optimization - and proposes a unique solution.
*   Theoretical Rigor: The theoretical results are mathematically sound and provide strong guarantees.
*   Potential Impact:  If the limitations can be addressed, the proposed method could offer a significant advantage in specific applications.
*   Clear Presentation of Mathematical Framework: The paper provides a detailed development of the necessary mathematical tools.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited Practicality: The restrictive assumptions (non-negativity of weights and data) may limit the applicability of the method.
*   Lack of Thorough Experimental Validation: The experiments are limited in scope and don’t conclusively demonstrate superior performance compared to standard neural networks.
*   Clarity Issues: Some mathematical derivations and justifications are unclear or incomplete.
*   Unusual Objective Function Component: The maximization of the 1-norm of connection parameters requires further explanation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound contribution to the field of neural network optimization. However, the practical limitations and some clarity issues raise concerns. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the weaknesses raised in the review, particularly regarding the practicality of the non-negativity constraints, the justification for the unusual objective function component, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation. Addressing these points could significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
