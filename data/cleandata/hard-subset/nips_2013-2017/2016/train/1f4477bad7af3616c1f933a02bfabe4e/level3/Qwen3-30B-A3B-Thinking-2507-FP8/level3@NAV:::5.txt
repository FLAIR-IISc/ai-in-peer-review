

This paper introduces a novel approach to achieving globally optimal training for a specific class of feedforward neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors define a generalized polynomial neural network architecture with nonnegative weights and data, and prove that under mild assumptions, the optimization problem admits a unique global maximizer. Their algorithm, NLSM, converges linearly to this global optimum by leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, with convergence guarantees verifiable through spectral radius computation of a small matrix.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical contribution: it provides the first practically feasible method with provable global optimality guarantees for neural networks, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The mathematical framework is well-constructed, with careful development of fixed point theory and Banach contraction arguments. The experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate competitive performance compared to SVM and ReLU-based networks, with the algorithm converging significantly faster than SGD variants.

However, several concerns require clarification. First, the shift from first-order (Theorem 3) to second-order (Theorem 4) analysis appears arbitrary without justification. The treatment of the last layer (u) as a separate entity lacks consistency in gradient computation, and the requirement for strictly positive weights (no zeros) seems unnecessarily restrictive for practical applications. The objective function's modification (maximizing 1-norm of parameters instead of minimizing) is not well-justified, especially since typical NNs use weight decay to reduce norms. The small ε in Equation (2) appears to be a mere technical artifact to ensure strictly positive gradients rather than a meaningful modeling choice. The assumption of nonnegative data limits applicability to specific domains like count data, potentially making the model impractical for general use.

While the theoretical contribution is significant, the practical limitations and unclear justifications of key assumptions weaken the paper's overall impact. The comparison to SVM is misleading since SVMs aren't neural networks, and the paper fails to demonstrate clear advantages over standard neural network training approaches. The experiments provide only preliminary evidence of the method's viability without addressing how to select parameters for real-world applications.

Overall, this is a theoretically interesting contribution that makes a valuable step toward globally optimal neural network training, but its practical relevance is limited by restrictive assumptions and insufficient justification of key design choices.
