This paper studies a restricted but nontrivial class of feedforward neural networks—generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative parameters—and proves the training objective has a single stationary point. The authors construct a nonlinear spectral fixed-point map on the positive cone (via Perron–Frobenius/Thompson-metric tools) and show it is a contraction under a verifiable spectral-radius condition, implying linear-time convergence to the unique fixed point, which equals the global minimum. The framework extends to two hidden layers. Relative to prior work on globally optimal training (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.), this approach avoids tensor score-function estimation and provides a practically checkable condition, albeit at the cost of strong architectural constraints and a modified objective.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound; the fixed-point construction and contraction analysis are well motivated. However, the convergence guarantee hinges on bounding the spectral radius of a nontrivial matrix whose entries grow with width. The resulting bounds appear conservative, limiting scalability to larger hidden layers. Practically, the method requires choosing many hyperparameters (norm exponents, radii, layer widths, exponents α) and verifying the spectral condition for each choice, creating significant overhead. Empirically, despite exploring ~150,000 configurations via cross-validation, the method outperforms a linear SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets, calling practical utility into question.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized with a clear proof roadmap; still, notation is heavy. Suggestions: provide an intuition-first overview of the map GΦ, isolate the role of each hyperparameter, and summarize the spectral condition with concrete, tighter bounds. An ablation relating hidden-layer size to ρ(A) and runtime would help.

Originality: Using nonlinear spectral methods to obtain uniqueness and linear convergence for a neural-network objective under constraints is novel and distinct from tensor and matrix-factorization guarantees.

Significance: The theoretical contribution is interesting and could influence work on globally convergent training under structural constraints. Yet current empirical impact is limited by scalability, restrictive nonnegativity/activation choices, and cumbersome tuning.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, rigorous fixed-point framework with global optimality and linear convergence
- Conceptual insight that constrained architectures can admit unique stationary points
- Extension beyond one hidden layer

Cons against acceptance:
- Conservative spectral bounds and poor scalability with width
- Heavy hyperparameter burden and per-configuration verification
- Limited empirical gains; underperforms a simple linear SVM on most datasets
- Narrow applicability due to data/weight nonnegativity and modified objective

Constructive suggestions: tighten spectral bounds; develop parameter-selection heuristics that obviate per-instance checks; reduce hyperparameter count via normalization schemes; evaluate on higher-dimensional datasets with runtime analyses; compare to stronger baselines and include ablations on constraint necessity.
