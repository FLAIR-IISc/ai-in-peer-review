- Proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method to train generalized polynomial neural networks with claimed global optimality and linear convergence under certain conditions.  
- Training is reformulated with non-negativity constraints and a modified objective, enabling a spectral-radius-based global optimality check.  
- Theoretical results are provided for one- and two-hidden-layer networks with rigorous derivations and links to Perron–Frobenius theory.  
- Provides a practical pre-check: global optimality conditions can be verified by computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix.  
- Linear convergence guarantee is a strong theoretical result suggesting fast asymptotic training when applicable.  
- Non-negativity constraint on weights is a major limitation, restricting the class of representable functions and diverging from standard practice.  
- Use of generalized polynomial activations (αi ≥ 1) excludes common activations like ReLU and further limits expressiveness and applicability.  
- Empirical results on UCI datasets do not show clear advantages; reported accuracies are often lower than SVM and standard methods.  
- Experiments are limited to low-dimensional datasets, leaving scalability to high-dimensional or large-scale settings unclear.  
- Computational cost may grow prohibitively with data dimensionality due to the matrix operations involved.  
- The claim that training typically converges in fewer than 10 iterations is overstated and not convincingly supported by experiments.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, rare global optimality guarantee, and useful pre-check for feasibility.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive architectural constraints, unconvincing empirical performance, and unresolved scalability concerns.  
- Overall recommendation: reject; authors should relax constraints, demonstrate stronger empirical gains on harder datasets, and address scalability.
