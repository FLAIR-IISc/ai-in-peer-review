This paper proposes a novel method for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors demonstrate, under relatively weak assumptions, that their approach achieves linear convergence to a global optimum, a significant theoretical advance in the field of neural network optimization. While the work focuses on one and two hidden layer networks for simplicity, the authors argue the method is generalizable. Experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate the feasibility and competitive performance of the approach.

The problem addressed is highly relevant. The non-convexity of neural network optimization remains a central challenge, and guaranteed global optimality is a highly desirable property. This paper makes a significant contribution by providing such a guarantee for a specific class of networks. The introduction effectively motivates the work and positions it within the broader research landscape, referencing key papers in the field (e.g., [13, 16, 8, 11]). 

However, a major concern lies in the practical utility of the proposed method. The restriction to non-negative weights and the specific polynomial activation functions significantly limit the expressiveness of the networks. While the authors attempt to demonstrate expressiveness with Figure 1, a more thorough discussion of what kinds of functions *can* and *cannot* be effectively represented by these networks is crucial. The paper lacks a clear articulation of the trade-offs between global optimality and representational power.

Furthermore, the paper does not adequately contrast its approach with related work. While [8, 11] are mentioned, a more comprehensive discussion of concurrent or recent advances in globally optimal training methods, or alternative algorithmic paradigms that attempt to mitigate the challenges of non-convex optimization (e.g., different initialization strategies, landscape analysis) is needed. The authors claim their approach is "practically feasible" but do not thoroughly justify this claim relative to other methods.

The experimental evaluation is also limited. Comparisons are primarily against SVMs and ReLU networks, but lack comparisons against more sophisticated neural network training techniques or other optimization methods tailored for neural networks. The paper also lacks details regarding the computational resources required for each algorithm (training time, memory usage). This information is essential for assessing the practical viability of the proposed method, especially considering the theoretical guarantees come at the cost of potentially increased computational complexity. The observed competitive performance on UCI datasets is encouraging, but further evaluation on more challenging and higher-dimensional datasets is necessary.

The presentation could benefit from polishing. While the introduction is well-written, later sections, particularly Section 2, feel less fluent and are sometimes difficult to follow. Several minor typos and notation inconsistencies were observed (e.g., “polyomial” on p60, “one CAN model” on p67, undefined variables p1 and p2, R_{++}). These should be addressed.

Specifically, the motivation behind the objective function Φ, including the added ε term, is not sufficiently explained. A more intuitive explanation before stating Theorem 1 would improve readability. Similarly, the claim that the method “converges quickly” relies on empirical observation and should be substantiated with more detailed analysis or empirical evidence.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantee:** The paper provides a strong theoretical guarantee of global optimality with a linear convergence rate, which is a significant contribution.
*   **Novel Approach:** The use of nonlinear spectral methods for neural network training is a novel and potentially fruitful direction.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction effectively motivates the work and highlights the challenges in neural network optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Expressiveness:** The restriction to non-negative weights and polynomial activations limits the expressiveness of the networks, and the paper does not adequately address this concern.
*   **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion of related work and fails to adequately contrast its approach with alternative methods.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks sufficient details regarding computational resources.
*   **Presentation Issues:** The presentation could benefit from polishing, and several typos and notation inconsistencies need to be addressed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution is significant and addresses a fundamental challenge in neural network optimization.
*   The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited expressiveness of the networks raises concerns about the practical utility of the method.
*   The lack of a thorough comparison to related work weakens the claim of novelty.
*   The limited experimental evaluation makes it difficult to assess the scalability and performance of the approach on more challenging datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical relevance is questionable. The limitations in expressiveness and the lack of a comprehensive comparison to related work are significant concerns.  I recommend **rejection** with the encouragement to address the concerns outlined above, particularly by providing a more thorough analysis of the expressiveness of the proposed network class and conducting a more extensive experimental evaluation. A clearer justification for the chosen objective function and a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical results would also be beneficial.
