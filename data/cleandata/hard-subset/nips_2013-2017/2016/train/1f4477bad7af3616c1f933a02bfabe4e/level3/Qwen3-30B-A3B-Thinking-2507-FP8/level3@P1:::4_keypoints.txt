- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method that gives global optimality and linear convergence for feedforward nets with nonnegative weights and power activations (α ≥ 1), reducible to checking the spectral radius of a small (K+1)x(K+1) matrix.  
- Major theoretical advance away from SGD: provides provable global convergence under mild conditions.  
- Does not require knowledge of the data-generating distribution (unlike tensor methods); only needs nonnegative input data.  
- Method can be extended to two hidden layers, though the paper focuses on one for clarity.  
- Strong theoretical rigor: uses fixed-point theory and the Thompson metric, with explicit, computationally checkable convergence conditions.  
- Practical advantage: no hyperparameters to tune compared to SGD.  
- Major limitation: restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, distinct power activations αl ≥ 1) severely limit model expressiveness.  
- Authors do not adequately justify whether the restrictive assumptions are necessary or can be relaxed.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited and unimpressive: competitive but generally outperformed by SVMs and ReLU nets, with NLSM2 winning only on three datasets.  
- Claim of “convergence in fewer than 10 iterations” is not supported by experiments (Figure 2 shows >10 iterations).  
- Practical significance is limited: unlikely to be broadly useful where negative weights and ReLU activations are standard.  
- Originality is strong theoretically, with good connections to prior work (Perron–Frobenius, tensor methods), but practical impact is questionable.  
- Presentation is clear but notation is dense; key proofs currently in supplementary material should be included in the main paper.  
- Overall: valuable theoretical contribution for understanding optimization, but restricted applicability and modest empirical gains limit its practical relevance.
