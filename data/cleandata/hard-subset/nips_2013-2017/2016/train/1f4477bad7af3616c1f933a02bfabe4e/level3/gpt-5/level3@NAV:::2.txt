This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method that trains a restricted class of feedforward networks (one or two hidden layers) to global optimality with linear convergence, under nonnegativity constraints and a generalized polynomial activation. The approach builds on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and fixed-point contractions in the Thompson metric, providing a checkable sufficient condition via the spectral radius of a small (K+1)- or (K+2)-dimensional matrix. The manuscript situates the work relative to tensor methods (Janzamin et al.) and structured factorization/global optimality results (Haeffele & Vidal), and claims improved practicality by avoiding density estimation and by providing a simple convergence certificate.

Quality: The theoretical development is rigorous, and the fixed-point analysis and convergence guarantees are carefully stated. However, the assumptions underpinning the guarantees are restrictive: nonnegative data and weights, heavy norm constraints, a modified objective that adds the sum of outputs, and the use of generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents. It is not convincingly demonstrated that these constraints preserve sufficient expressive power for practical tasks. Empirically, the comparisons appear unfair: the linear SVM baseline seems untuned, and there are no nonlinear SVM baselines (e.g., RBF or polynomial kernels). The UCI datasets used are small and low-dimensional, so the method’s scalability and real-world impact remain unclear. Moreover, the bounds that define matrix A grow with hidden-unit count, suggesting the spectral-radius condition will be violated as dimensionality/width increase. Finally, while the optimality condition can be checked, identifying configurations that satisfy it appears to require a large search; the paper lacks fast screening or constructive parameter selection.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized, though intuition for the modified objective and the practical meaning of the exponents could be strengthened. Guidance for choosing hyperparameters to satisfy ρ(A) < 1 without excessive regularization would help.

Originality: The combination of nonlinear spectral methods with global training guarantees for (restricted) neural networks is novel and interesting.

Significance: Potentially impactful if the practicality gap can be narrowed, but current empirical support is limited.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, novel fixed-point framework with linear convergence and global optimality under checkable conditions.
- Extension to two hidden layers and transparent dependence of ρ(A) on architecture.
- Theoretical contribution may inspire further work on monotone/homogeneous networks.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong, counterintuitive restrictions (nonnegativity, generalized polynomial activations, modified objective).
- Unconvincing expressivity and limited, small-scale experiments; unfair baselines (no nonlinear SVMs).
- Scalability concerns: ρ(A) grows with width; feasibility of meeting the condition at scale is unclear.
- No efficient procedures to identify valid hyperparameter regions; optimality may not hold in practical regimes.
- Unclear whether global optima are special to the objective; recommend testing if SGD also attains the same optima on these datasets.

Suggestions: Add nonlinear SVM baselines and tuned linear SVM; evaluate on larger, higher-dimensional datasets with nonnegative features; provide tighter, less conservative bounds on A; develop fast parameter screening; and empirically test whether standard SGD can reach the same optima for the proposed objective.
