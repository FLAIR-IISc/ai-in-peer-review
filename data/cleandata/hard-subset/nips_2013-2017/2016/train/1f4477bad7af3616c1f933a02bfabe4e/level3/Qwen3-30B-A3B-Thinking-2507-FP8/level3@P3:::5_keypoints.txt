- Main contribution: Presents a globally optimal training algorithm for a class of feedforward neural nets with nonnegative data/weights using nonlinear spectral methods, with linear convergence guarantees.
- Theoretical novelty: Applies Perron–Frobenius–inspired nonlinear spectral methods to obtain provable global optimality and linear convergence, advancing prior global-opt work.
- Convergence check: Provides a simple practical convergence check via the spectral radius of a small matrix.
- Avoids hyperparameter tuning: Claims practical advantage over SGD by not requiring hyperparameter tuning.
- Empirical results: Demonstrates competitive performance on small UCI datasets, showing practical viability for the constrained model.
- Major restriction: Requires nonnegative data and strictly positive weights (no zeros), severely limiting applicability to many real-world problems.
- Objective modification concern: Maximizing the sum of outputs (modified objective) is not well motivated and conflicts with standard regularization practices like weight decay.
- Epsilon artifact: The small ε in Eq.(2) appears to be a technical device to ensure positive gradients without practical justification.
- Theoretical clarity issue: The shift from first-order derivative inequalities (Theorem 3) to second-derivative arguments (Theorem 4) is unexplained and confusing.
- Boundedness concerns: The bounding of weights in a ball and treatment of parameter u lack clear justification; potential for unbounded growth is not addressed.
- Limited experiments: Experimental evaluation is restricted to small datasets and comparisons to ReLU are superficial, lacking discussion of practical trade-offs.
- Quality of proofs: The theoretical development is rigorous and proofs support the main claims under stated assumptions.
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-written and algorithm description is precise, but key design choices (nonnegativity, objective change, ε) need stronger justification.
- Originality: Novel and distinct from prior methods (Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.), offering a practically feasible globally optimal method for a nontrivial network class.
- Significance trade-off: Theoretical significance is high, but practical significance is limited by restrictive assumptions and unclear extensions to standard architectures.
- Recommendation pros: Valuable theoretical advance with feasible algorithm and supporting experiments — argues for acceptance.
- Recommendation cons: Restrictive assumptions and unjustified design choices reduce practical impact, arguing against acceptance until concerns are addressed.
