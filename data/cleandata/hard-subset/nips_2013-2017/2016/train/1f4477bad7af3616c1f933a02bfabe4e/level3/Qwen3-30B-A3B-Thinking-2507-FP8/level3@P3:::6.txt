

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel approach to training a restricted class of feed-forward neural networks with provable global optimality guarantees. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method that, under specific conditions (nonnegative data and weights, modified objective function), converges linearly to the global optimum. The work builds on recent advances in structured matrix factorization and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory, extending the theoretical framework of Haeffele and Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015) to provide a practically feasible algorithm for global optimization. While previous works have established theoretical guarantees for neural network training, they often lack practical applicability, which this paper claims to address.

**Strengths:** The theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed. The paper provides rigorous proofs for convergence guarantees under weak assumptions, which is a notable advancement over previous work. The method's linear convergence rate and lack of hyperparameter tuning (unlike SGD) are practical advantages. The paper correctly acknowledges the limitations of prior approaches that require knowledge of the data-generating distribution or are computationally infeasible.

**Weaknesses:** The major concern is the lack of convincing evidence that the restricted network class (requiring nonnegative weights) is practically useful. The paper does not adequately address the expressiveness of the model—what problems it can solve versus those it cannot. The experiments are limited to UCI datasets with no comparison against other neural network training methods (e.g., standard SGD with careful hyperparameter tuning), and the paper omits discussion of computational resources required for the three algorithms compared. The claim that "these models are rich enough to achieve good performance" is not substantiated with a clear discussion of the model's limitations.

The related work section is insufficiently developed. The paper should explicitly contrast its approach with concurrent methods like those of Haeffele and Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015), particularly regarding the practical feasibility of implementation. The paper mentions that "up to our knowledge" this is the first practically feasible method, but this claim requires stronger justification through comparative analysis.

The presentation requires polishing. Section 2 lacks fluency, and there are several typos and notation issues (e.g., "polyomial" on p.60, "one CAN model" on p.67, undefined R_{++}). The motivation for the function Φ and the ε term in the objective is not adequately explained beyond the mathematical derivation. The paper claims "converges quickly" based on empirical observation but does not provide a theoretical basis for this statement.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development, though the practical relevance is questionable. The experiments are limited and fail to demonstrate the method's applicability limits or how it compares to standard neural network training approaches.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured overall, but the presentation of technical details in Section 2 is confusing. The motivation for key choices (like the modified objective) should be more clearly explained before stating theorems. Several technical terms (R_{++}, ρ(A) dependence on parameters) require better exposition.

**Originality:** The work represents a meaningful advance in the theoretical understanding of neural network training. The use of nonlinear spectral methods to achieve global optimality is novel, though the restricted class of networks is a significant limitation.

**Significance:** The theoretical contribution is important for the field, but the practical significance is questionable due to the restrictive assumptions. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the method is useful for real-world problems beyond the limited UCI datasets shown. The expressiveness of the model needs explicit discussion—what problems are within reach versus out of reach for this approach.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Strong theoretical contribution, rigorous proofs, linear convergence rate, no hyperparameter tuning required, addresses a fundamental limitation in neural network training.

*Con:* Restrictive network class (nonnegative weights), limited experimental validation, insufficient comparison with alternative approaches, unclear practical utility, lack of discussion on model expressiveness and limitations.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution that advances the understanding of neural network training. However, the practical relevance is not convincingly demonstrated, and the experiments are insufficient to support the claim that the method is useful in practice. The authors should significantly expand the discussion on the expressiveness of their model, provide more comprehensive experimental comparisons (including standard neural network training methods), and clarify the practical applicability limits of their approach. Without these improvements, the paper's significance for the NIPS community is limited. I recommend rejection due to the lack of convincing evidence for practical utility, though the theoretical contribution is sound.
