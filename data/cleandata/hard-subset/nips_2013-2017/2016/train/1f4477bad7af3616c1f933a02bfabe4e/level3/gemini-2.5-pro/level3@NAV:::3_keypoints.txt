- Proposes a nonlinear spectral-method for feedforward nets with non-negative inputs/weights and generalized polynomial activations, proving convergence to a unique global optimum.  
- Main theoretical result: linear convergence to the global optimum under a clear precondition based on the spectral radius of a small matrix (rho(A) < 1).  
- Builds on prior guaranteed-training work (e.g., tensor methods) but offers simpler, a-priori checkable conditions and potentially more practical applicability.  
- Theoretical framework is rigorous, leveraging nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and the Banach fixed-point theorem, and extends from single-layer to deeper networks.  
- Empirical validation on synthetic and UCI datasets provides a proof-of-concept grounding the theory in practice.  
- Major limitation: the non-negativity requirement for inputs and weights severely restricts model expressiveness and real-world applicability.  
- Scalability concern: satisfying rho(A) < 1 for larger networks may force parameters (pw, pu) to grow and linear weights to shrink, risking degeneration to near-linear behavior.  
- Unconventional constraint that each hidden unit uses a distinct activation (different alpha_i) is impractical and may hinder adoption.  
- Suggestion: perform empirical studies showing how rho(A) changes with network size, input dimension, and hyperparameters to assess practical boundaries.  
- Overall judgment: despite limitations, the theoretical novelty and provable guarantees are compelling; reviewer recommends acceptance.
