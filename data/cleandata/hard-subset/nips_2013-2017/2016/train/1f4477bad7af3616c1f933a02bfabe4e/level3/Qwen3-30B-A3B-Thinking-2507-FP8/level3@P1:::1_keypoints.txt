- Proposes a globally optimal training method for neural nets with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method that guarantees global convergence to the unique optimum under certain conditions.  
- Offers a practically feasible algorithm with linear convergence rate, improving over prior purely theoretical results.  
- Key innovation: reformulates the nonconvex problem as a fixed-point problem via nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory.  
- The global optimum corresponds to a unique fixed point of a contractive mapping.  
- Strong theoretical novelty and rigorous mathematical foundation with linear convergence under weak (nonnegative data) assumptions.  
- Elegant proof technique using fixed-point theory on the positive orthant and explicit spectral-radius bounds.  
- Insight that nonnegativity constraints plus a tailored regularizer enable global optimality guarantees.  
- Builds on Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, giving solid theoretical grounding.  
- Major scalability issue: convergence depends on the spectral radius of a (K+1)×(K+1) matrix that grows with hidden units.  
- Method effectively limited to shallow networks and low-dimensional datasets due to spectral-radius growth.  
- Requires extensive hyperparameter tuning (n1, α, pw, pu, ρw, ρu) and checking ρ(A)<1 for each configuration (~150k combos tested).  
- Practical implementation burden and verification overhead make the approach infeasible for most real-world applications.  
- Empirical results are weak: outperforms linear SVM on only 2/7 UCI datasets and is comparable or worse than ReLU networks.  
- Fails to match unconstrained ReLU performance on simple datasets (Iris, Banknote), questioning practical utility.  
- Clarity issues: insufficient motivation for the regularizer and weak connection between theory and experimental choices.  
- Overall significance is theoretical but limited in practice; the claim of being "practically feasible" is questionable.  
- Recommendation: reject due to limited practical impact, scalability constraints, and excessive implementation complexity; suggests stronger empirical evidence or more generalizable methods.
