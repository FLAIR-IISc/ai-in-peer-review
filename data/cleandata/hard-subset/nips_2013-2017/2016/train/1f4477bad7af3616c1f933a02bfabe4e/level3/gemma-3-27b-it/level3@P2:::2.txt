This paper presents a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks, guaranteeing global optimality with linear convergence under certain conditions using a nonlinear spectral method. The core contribution lies in formulating the training problem in a way that allows for a globally optimal solution, a significant departure from the typical non-convex optimization challenges in deep learning. While the theoretical results are interesting and potentially impactful, several concerns regarding the practicality, scalability, and empirical validation of the method limit its overall contribution.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear motivation and introduction to the challenges of training neural networks. The authors correctly identify the NP-hardness of training even single-hidden-layer networks and position their work within the recent research aiming for global optimality guarantees (e.g., [8, 11]). The mathematical development, particularly the theorems and lemmas concerning the convergence analysis, is rigorous and well-presented, although dense and requiring careful attention. The inclusion of a proof strategy overview is helpful. The exposition could benefit from more intuitive explanations alongside the formal derivations.

A significant weakness lies in the restrictive assumptions required for global optimality. The imposition of non-negative weights and a modified objective function (adding the sum of outputs) deviate substantially from standard neural network practices. While the authors acknowledge the non-standard nature of their model, the justification for these choices and a thorough discussion of their potential impact on expressivity are lacking. The claim that the resulting model remains expressive is supported by a single figure showing a toy dataset, which is insufficient to convince the reader. The generalized polynomial activation function, necessitated by the non-negativity constraint, is also less common and may limit the model's ability to learn complex features.

The experimental evaluation further exacerbates these concerns. The UCI datasets used are small and relatively low-dimensional. This limits the ability to assess the scalability and real-world applicability of the proposed method. While the authors correctly point out the lack of similar empirical results in related work, the chosen baseline comparisons are somewhat unfair. The linear SVM baseline appears to be minimally tuned, and crucially, there are no comparisons against non-linear SVMs (e.g., using RBF or polynomial kernels), which would provide a more relevant benchmark. The fact that the ReLU networks perform comparably or better, despite not having global optimality guarantees, raises questions about the practical benefits of the proposed method. The authors speculate about the impact of α being large, but more systematic analysis is needed. A crucial experiment would be to attempt to train the same datasets with SGD and see if it can reach the same global optima, potentially indicating that the optimality is not solely attributable to the spectral method but rather to the specific dataset and architecture.

The scalability of the method is also a concern. The authors acknowledge that the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the number of hidden units. This suggests that the computational cost of verifying the convergence guarantee (checking ρ(A) < 1) could become prohibitive for larger networks and high-dimensional datasets. Furthermore, the process of identifying parameter settings that satisfy the convergence condition may require a computationally expensive parameter search.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical guarantee of global optimality with linear convergence for a specific class of neural networks.
*   Rigorous mathematical analysis and clear presentation of the convergence proof.
*   First empirical demonstration of a globally optimal algorithm for neural networks applied to real-world data (albeit limited).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Restrictive assumptions (non-negative weights, modified objective) limit the practical applicability.
*   Insufficient empirical evidence to support the claim of retained expressivity and competitive performance.
*   Unfair baseline comparisons and limited scalability testing.
*   Scalability concerns due to the growing complexity of verifying the convergence condition with increasing network size.
*   Unclear if global optimality is a consequence of the spectral method or the specific dataset/architecture.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical relevance is questionable. The restrictive assumptions, limited experimental validation, and scalability concerns outweigh the novelty of the global optimality guarantee. While the authors have demonstrated a proof of concept, significant further work is needed to address the limitations and demonstrate the potential of this approach for real-world applications. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the concerns raised in the review and expand the experimental evaluation. A more thorough comparison against standard neural network architectures and a detailed analysis of the scalability limitations are crucial for future work.
