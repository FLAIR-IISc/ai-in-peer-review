This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that trains a restricted but nontrivial class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The models considered have nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations with unit-specific exponents αl ≥ 1, norm constraints on each layer, and a modified objective equal to negative cross-entropy plus a sum of model outputs (and a small ε term for technical reasons). The central tool is a multihomogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius framework: training is cast as a fixed-point iteration GΦ on the positive orthant, and under a model-dependent spectral condition ρ(A) < 1 for a small (K+1)-by-(K+1) nonnegative matrix, GΦ is a contraction in the Thompson metric, yielding uniqueness of the global maximizer and linear convergence. The paper presents the one-hidden-layer case in detail and sketches the two-hidden-layer extension, with proofs partly deferred to the supplement. Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy with ReLU networks and RBF-SVM on some tasks and much faster training than SGD for the proposed objective.

Relation to prior work: The approach differs from tensor-based global training for one-hidden-layer networks with squared loss [11] (which relies on score function tensors and nondegeneracy), and from matrix-factorization-based global optimality results [8] (which the authors and prior work note are hard to deploy). It also connects to polynomial networks [15] and leverages nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous maps [7,14,18]. The authors’ claim of being the first “practically feasible” globally optimal algorithm for a nontrivial NN class seems plausible relative to [8,11], though it would benefit from a more systematic contrast with related constrained/monotone networks and nonnegative matrix/tensor factorization methods.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically solid. The fixed-point characterization, the contraction analysis under the Thompson metric, and the spectral-radius condition are clearly stated; the extension to two layers is nontrivial. The linear convergence guarantee and uniqueness are strong. However, the paper’s practical relevance hinges on the expressiveness of the constrained architecture and on how conservative the ρ(A) < 1 condition is in realistic settings. The experiments are limited in scope and do not fully support claims about practical utility or scalability. Important clarifications are needed about the modified objective: how the added output-sum and ε terms affect the classifier and calibration, and whether the same decision boundaries would arise if those terms were removed (besides enabling the proof technique).

Clarity: The introduction is clear, but later sections become heavy quickly. Section 2 could benefit from an informal roadmap before Theorem 1, with intuition about why the fixed-point map is well behaved and how the A matrix captures Lipschitz constants. A brief, example-driven explanation of Φ and the role of ε should appear where Φ is first introduced, not only in the discussion. Several typos/notation issues impede readability (examples below).

Originality: The use of nonlinear spectral methods to derive a globally convergent training algorithm for these generalized polynomial networks is novel and interesting. The explicit, check-before-run condition via ρ(A), and the extension beyond one layer, are noteworthy contributions.

Significance: The theoretical contribution is significant for our understanding of global optimization in neural training under structural constraints. The empirical evidence is suggestive but too narrow to establish practical significance. The constrained architecture (nonnegativity, fixed norms, distinct αl, output-sum augmentation) restricts applicability; the paper does not yet delineate what function classes are efficiently representable nor when the method is expected to work well.

Strengths
- Rigorous global optimality and linear convergence guarantees under a verifiable spectral condition; unique minimizer in S++.
- Elegant connection to multihomogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction mappings.
- Extension to two hidden layers with an explicit A matrix and condition ρ(A) < 1.
- Simple, parameter-free iteration once the architecture is fixed; no step-size tuning.
- Clear articulation of how ρ(A) depends on architectural parameters and norms.

Weaknesses
- Restricted model class; practical expressiveness is not characterized. Nonnegativity, generalized polynomial activations, and the added output-sum term may limit utility.
- Experimental evaluation is too small-scale and omits critical baselines (e.g., projected/penalized nonnegativity-constrained training via SGD or mirror descent; polynomial-kernel SVMs; tensor methods on synthetic data; monotone networks).
- No runtime/compute comparison (wall-clock, iterations to tolerance) across methods; the “converges quickly” claim is mostly empirical and not tied to measured ρ(A).
- Incomplete discussion of related/concurrent approaches and when each set of assumptions is preferable.
- Presentation issues and typos/notation problems.

Requested clarifications and suggestions
- Motivate Φ: Explain, with a small example, why adding the sum of outputs and ε yields strictly positive gradients and why this choice is the minimal/least distorting modification. Discuss the effect on decision boundaries and calibration; is there an equivalent formulation as a constrained problem with KKT conditions leading to the same fixed point?
- Provide an informal overview before Theorem 1: what GΦ does, why it is order-preserving/subhomogeneous, and how A encodes sensitivities. Clarify that “fast convergence” refers to linear rate with factor ρ(A) and that observed speedups are empirical.
- Expressiveness: Characterize the hypothesis class induced by nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations. Are the class-conditional scores fr monotone in inputs? What functions can/can’t be approximated (e.g., need for subtraction/cancellation across classes)? Give examples where the model fails/succeeds, and justify the necessity of distinct αl.
- Practical guidance: Discuss data preprocessing to ensure nonnegativity and its effect on ρ(A). Provide sensitivity analyses for α, ρw, ρu, pw, pu; report the computed ρ(A) and the observed contraction factor. Show ablations removing the output-sum term and varying ε.
- Experiments: 
  - Add polynomial-kernel SVM baselines (to match the activation class).
  - Compare to standard NNs trained with nonnegativity constraints (projection or reparameterization) on the same objective, to isolate algorithmic vs architectural effects.
  - Include runtime and iteration counts; plot objective gap versus iterations against the predicted linear rate using measured ρ(A).
  - Test on at least one moderate-scale nonnegative dataset (e.g., MNIST with [0,1] inputs; bag-of-words text) to probe scalability.
  - If [11] code or a proxy is unavailable, include synthetic setups satisfying their assumptions to contrast accuracy and sample complexity.
- Fix typos/notation: “polyomial” → “polynomial”; “one CAN model” → “one can model”; define R++ and S++ at first use; ensure p, q, pw, pu are introduced consistently; check p1, p2 occurrences; unify ρx notation (ρ̃x vs ρx); standardize vector/matrix norms and ψp notation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution with a practical algorithm and verifiable convergence condition.
- Bridges spectral fixed-point methods and neural network training; potential to inspire further globally optimal training under structure.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation; unclear practical impact and expressiveness of the constrained architecture.
- Incomplete positioning relative to related methods and missing key baselines and runtime analysis.
- Presentation needs polishing and additional intuition for accessibility.

Overall, this is a compelling theoretical paper with promising ideas; strengthening the empirical section, clarifying the objective modification, and articulating the model’s expressiveness and limits would substantially improve its impact.
