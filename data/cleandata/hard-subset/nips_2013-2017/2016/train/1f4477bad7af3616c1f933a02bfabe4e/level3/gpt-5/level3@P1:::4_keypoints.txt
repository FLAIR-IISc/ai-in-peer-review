- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) with global optimality guarantees for one- and two-hidden-layer feedforward networks with nonnegative inputs/weights and unit-specific polynomial activations.  
- Training objective is a modified cross-entropy (negative loss plus sum of outputs and small ε) to ensure strictly positive gradients.  
- Main theoretical result: if the spectral radius ρ(A) of a constructed nonnegative matrix A (<1) then the fixed-point map is a contraction in the Thompson metric, yielding a unique global optimum and linear convergence with rate ρ(A).  
- The spectral-radius condition depends only on architecture and normalization parameters and can be checked a priori.  
- Uses nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and multihomogeneous fixed-point contraction arguments; the fixed-point operator is explicit and parameter-free at run time.  
- Extends the framework from one hidden layer to two with a slightly larger matrix A, showing the approach is not limited to very shallow networks.  
- Contrasts with prior global-optimality approaches (Haeffele & Vidal, tensor methods) by offering a practically implementable, verifiable condition for a specific NN family.  
- Strength: clear, rigorous, and checkable spectral-radius condition implying uniqueness and linear convergence.  
- Strength: elegant theoretical synthesis and explicit contraction rate ρ(A).  
- Strength: first practically executable globally optimal training procedure for a nontrivial NN class (per reviewer).  
- Major limitation: strong modeling constraints (nonnegative inputs/weights, specific polynomial activations, normalization, modified loss) that may reduce expressivity and depart from standard classifiers.  
- Additional limitation: requirement that all αl be distinct restricts design choices and complicates model selection.  
- Scalability concern: conservative bounds for A grow with hidden units, likely limiting applicability to larger networks.  
- Empirically underperforms an RBF SVM on UCI tasks and is only sometimes competitive with ReLU baselines; experiments are small-scale and proof-of-concept.  
- Claim that linear convergence implies very few iterations is overstated without reported ρ(A) values and sensitivity analysis.  
- Quality: technical development is sound, assumptions explicit, and proofs carefully structured; bounds acknowledged as conservative.  
- Clarity suggestions: add intuition on the output-sum term and ε, move an algorithm box earlier, include complexity analysis, and give guidance for choosing α, ρu, ρw, pu, pw.  
- Originality: high — novel synthesis of multihomogeneous Perron–Frobenius contraction with NN training and a checkable spectral criterion.  
- Significance: conceptually important for provable NN training, but practical impact limited by constraints and conservative conditions; relaxing constraints or sharpening bounds would increase impact.  
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity to α and distinctness requirement, calibration/Bayes-consistency with added term, handling negative inputs/weights (e.g., sign-splitting), empirical reporting of ρ(A) and observed contraction, comparisons to related baselines, and evaluation on naturally nonnegative modalities.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel, rigorous global-optimality result with practical checkability and extension beyond one hidden layer.  
- Cons for acceptance: strong model/loss constraints, conservative bounds limiting scalability, limited empirical validation, and optimistic speed claims without quantitative backing.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution with clear novelty but constrained practical scope and modest empirical evidence; reviewer leans borderline accept pending additional experiments, ρ(A) reporting, and discussion on relaxing constraints.
