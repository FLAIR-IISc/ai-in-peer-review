## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a novel method for globally optimizing the training of feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers. The authors leverage a nonlinear spectral method, imposing constraints like non-negative weights and employing generalized polynomial activations, to achieve linear convergence to a global optimum under specific data assumptions. This work builds on recent efforts to provide global optimization guarantees for neural networks [8, 11], aiming for a more practical approach than prior tensor-based methods [11] or those requiring restrictive structured factorization [8]. 

The core strength of this paper lies in its theoretical contribution: establishing conditions for global optimality and linear convergence.  The explicit check for these conditions, based on the spectral radius of a small matrix, is a valuable insight and contrasts favorably with methods requiring estimation of the data generating measure. The extension to two hidden layers, while briefly described, demonstrates the potential for generalization.

However, the practical relevance of the method is questionable. The imposed constraints—non-negative weights and the specific generalized polynomial activation function—are highly restrictive and deviate significantly from standard neural network practices. While the authors attempt to argue for sufficient expressive power, this is not convincingly demonstrated.  The experimental validation on small UCI datasets is insufficient to support claims of scalability or real-world applicability.  Moreover, the empirical comparison is unfair; the linear SVM baseline appears untuned, and crucial nonlinear SVM baselines (RBF, polynomial kernels) are absent. The authors acknowledge the conservative bounds on the spectral radius, which suggest scalability issues with increasing hidden unit count. The authors also rightfully point out the need for faster ways to identify valid parameters in order to fulfill the optimality condition, as checking it is infeasible for most practical parameter settings.  Finally, it remains unclear whether the observed global optimality is a result of the modified objective function or simply achievable through standard methods like SGD. Testing SGD’s ability to reach global optima on the same datasets would be a crucial control experiment.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical novelty in providing global optimality guarantees with linear convergence.
*   Practical check for convergence conditions based on spectral radius.
*   Extension to two hidden layers, hinting at potential for broader applicability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Highly restrictive assumptions (non-negative weights, specialized activation).
*   Insufficient experimental validation; small datasets and unfair baselines.
*   Scalability concerns due to spectral radius bounds.
*   Unclear if global optimality is specific to the modified objective function.



**Overall:** While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the practical limitations and shortcomings in the experimental evaluation raise serious concerns. I recommend **rejection** at this time. Significant revisions addressing the experimental setup, demonstrating scalability, and exploring the relationship with standard optimization methods (like SGD) would be necessary for reconsideration.
