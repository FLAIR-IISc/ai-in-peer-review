## Review of “Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods”

This paper presents a fascinating approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks—Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs)—with a guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence. The authors achieve this by leveraging nonlinear spectral methods, restricting themselves to non-negative data and weights, and modifying the objective function. This work is highly relevant to the ongoing research into understanding and improving the optimization landscape of neural networks, building upon prior efforts like Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015) which also aim for global optimality, but with practical limitations. 

The core strength of the paper lies in its theoretical rigor. The mathematical derivations, while dense, are generally instructive, and the proof strategy of leveraging fixed-point theory is well-articulated. Achieving a provable guarantee of global optimality with linear convergence is a significant contribution, especially given the non-convexity inherent in neural network training. The experimental validation, while limited to UCI datasets, provides initial evidence that these constrained GPNNs can achieve competitive performance.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The justification for several constructions, such as bounding the hidden layer activations 'u' in a ball, is not entirely clear. The shift from first-order derivative inequalities in Theorem 3 to second derivatives in Theorem 4 is unexplained and feels somewhat arbitrary. Furthermore, the treatment of the last layer's gradient, referencing a vectorized 'u', requires more explicit clarification to ensure consistency throughout the paper. A core concern revolves around the imposed constraints: the requirement for non-negative data and weights, and the maximization of the 1-norm in the objective function (2). The latter is particularly puzzling, given the typical use of weight decay (L2 regularization) to *reduce* norms in standard neural networks. The small epsilon in (2) feels like a purely technical artifact to enforce strictly positive gradients, lacking intuitive justification. The potential for unbounded growth with strictly positive weights and the practical limitations of non-negative data are also valid concerns. Finally, the comparison to SVM feels somewhat limited; a more comprehensive evaluation against common neural network architectures (e.g., those employing ReLU) would better establish the practical advantages (if any) of this approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical result: provably globally optimal training with linear convergence.
*   Clear and detailed mathematical derivations.
*   Initial empirical validation demonstrating feasibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Unclear justification for several constructions and design choices.
*   Practical limitations of constraints (non-negativity, 1-norm maximization).
*   Limited experimental comparison to standard neural network architectures.
*   Questions regarding the shift in derivative usage between theorems.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable, though niche, theoretical contribution. While the constraints and practical considerations raise concerns, the guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence is a significant achievement. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarification requests and a more thorough discussion of the practical implications and limitations of the proposed approach. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation would also strengthen the paper.
