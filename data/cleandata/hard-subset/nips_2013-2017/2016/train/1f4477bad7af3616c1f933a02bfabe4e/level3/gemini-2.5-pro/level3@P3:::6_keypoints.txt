- Proposes a novel method using nonlinear spectral theory to train certain feed-forward networks with non-negative data/weights and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Theorem 1: if the spectral radius ρ(A) < 1 for an explicitly defined matrix A, a simple iterative algorithm converges linearly to a unique global maximizer.  
- The ρ(A) < 1 condition is checkable a priori from architecture and data.  
- Demonstrated for one- and two-hidden-layer networks with proof-of-concept experiments on UCI datasets.  
- Major theoretical strength: a provable global-optimum guarantee and linear convergence rate for a non-convex training problem.  
- Method is novel: applies nonlinear Perron–Frobenius and fixed-point theory to neural network training.  
- Practical advantage: convergence condition can be verified before training, unlike methods needing unknown data properties.  
- Main practical concern: restrictive assumptions (non-negative data/weights, modified objective) may severely limit real-world utility.  
- Unclear expressive power: paper lacks thorough analysis of what functions/decision boundaries this network class can or cannot represent.  
- Insufficient comparison to related global-optimal training work and to analyses of neural network loss landscapes.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak: limited baselines (only RBF-SVM and one SGD ReLU), omits common optimizers like Adam/RMSProp.  
- Experiments are small-scale (low-dimensional UCI datasets); scalability and dependence of ρ(A) on hidden units/data norms are unexplored.  
- Missing empirical resource metrics: no wall-clock time or memory usage reported to substantiate practical efficiency claims.  
- Presentation issues: technical exposition abrupt, pre-Theorem informal proof sketch recommended, and motivation for objective modifications (sum of outputs, ε) needs clearer intuition.  
- Typos and notation problems (e.g., “polyomial”, R_{++} used before definition, inconsistent norms p1/p2 vs pw/pu) should be fixed.  
- Technical clarifications requested: report typical ρ(A) values from experiments, clarify “converges quickly” vs empirical evidence, and link choice of α to avoiding degenerate solutions and expressiveness.
