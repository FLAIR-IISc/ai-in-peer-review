- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method that yields a globally optimal training algorithm with linear convergence for a restricted class of feedforward networks.  
- Networks require nonnegative inputs/weights, generalized polynomial activations, layer-wise ℓp norm constraints, and a modified cross-entropy adding output sum plus a small positivity term.  
- Main theory: a fixed-point iteration (ψp maps) with convergence proved via nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and Banach fixed-point theorem using the Thompson metric.  
- Provides an easily checkable sufficient condition: spectral radius ρ(A) < 1 of a small architecture-dependent nonnegative matrix (size K+1 for one hidden layer, K+2 for two).  
- Extends the method nontrivially from one to two hidden layers with detailed derivations.  
- Situates contribution relative to prior provable-training work (tensor methods, structured matrix factorization), offering a more operational spectral test and direct algorithm.  
- Theoretical quality: clear fixed-point formulation, explicit checkable contraction conditions, and stated linear convergence rates.  
- Empirics: competitive with small ReLU models on small UCI datasets but typically worse than RBF-SVM; converges in few iterations in presented cases.  
- Empirical limitations: small-scale evaluation only, no evidence on high-dimensional image/text tasks where nonnegativity is natural.  
- Manuscript clarity: generally well-organized but notation-heavy; suggests a high-level recipe for parameter selection and clearer motivation for the output-sum and ε terms.  
- Originality: novel use of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory for globally convergent fixed-point training and reduction to a spectral-radius condition.  
- Significance: theoretically significant and practically checkable, but practical impact limited by strong modeling constraints and modest empirical results.  
- Strength: rigorous, verifiable global optimality condition and linear convergence without learning-rate tuning.  
- Strength: first practical demonstrations (to reviewer’s knowledge) of globally optimal NN training under explicit assumptions, with extension to two hidden layers.  
- Weakness: strong restrictions—nonnegative inputs/weights, special polynomial activations with distinct exponents, fixed ℓp norms, and nonstandard objective.  
- Weakness: empirical performance below strong baselines and scale; sufficient condition harder to satisfy as model size grows; conservative bounds may force impractical large p’s.  
- Weakness: limited guidance and sensitivity analysis for choosing α, p’s, and ρ’s; claims about very fast convergence are insufficiently substantiated.  
- Requested clarifications/experiments: guidance for choosing α/p/ρ, effect of output-sum term on calibration and test-time rules, evaluations on naturally nonnegative domains (MNIST, bag-of-words), ablations removing the output-sum term, comparisons to standard cross-entropy training, wall-clock and robustness metrics, and code release.  
- Acceptance pro: novel, rigorous, and checkable theory with linear-convergent algorithm and extension to two layers.  
- Acceptance contra: restrictive modeling and objective constraints, small-scale/weaker empirical performance, and global optimality applies to a modified constrained objective rather than standard training.  
- Overall: technically solid and thought-provoking but currently limited practical reach; reviewer leans toward rejection pending stronger empirical validation and relaxation/clarification of assumptions.
