

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees and linear convergence rate. The authors consider networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activation functions (real powers α_i ≥ 1), showing that under mild data assumptions (nonnegative features), the optimization problem can be solved globally optimally using a nonlinear spectral method inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory. The key insight is that the global optimality condition depends only on computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix, which can be checked without running the algorithm. This contrasts with previous work on globally optimal training (e.g., Janzamin et al. [11], Haeffele and Vidal [8]), which either requires unknown data distributions or is challenging to apply in practice.

**Quality:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis. The main theorem (Theorem 1) provides clear conditions for global optimality and linear convergence, with a proof strategy that carefully builds on fixed-point theory and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors demonstrate the approach for one and two hidden layer networks, extending to deeper architectures in principle. However, the paper has significant limitations: the requirement for nonnegative weights and specialized activation functions severely restricts practical applicability. The empirical results (Table 1) show that the method performs worse than SVM on most UCI datasets, and the claimed "linear convergence" is exaggerated since the convergence rate depends on the spectral radius ρ(A) which can be close to 1 in practice. The authors acknowledge these limitations but don't sufficiently address how to overcome them.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from theoretical development to empirical validation. The main theorem and proof strategy are well-explained, and the supplementary material provides necessary technical details. The experimental section is clear, though it would benefit from more detailed analysis of why the method underperforms compared to SVM and ReLU networks. The claim that linear convergence "typically" implies convergence in fewer than 10 iterations (Section 5) is unsubstantiated and should be removed.

**Originality:** The approach is conceptually novel, as it applies nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training with global optimality guarantees. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems (citing [7]) is insightful and represents a new perspective on neural network optimization. This distinguishes the work from previous approaches that rely on tensor decompositions [11] or structured matrix factorizations [8]. The paper adequately references related work at NIPS and beyond.

**Significance:** While the theoretical contribution is interesting and addresses an important problem (global optimization of neural networks), the practical significance is limited by the strong constraints (nonnegative weights, specialized activations) that make the method impractical for most real-world applications. The empirical results showing worse performance than SVM undermine the practical relevance of the method. The paper does provide the first globally optimal algorithm that achieves competitive performance on real datasets, but this is a modest advance given the practical limitations.

**Strengths:**
- First practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees for neural networks
- Linear convergence rate with no hyperparameter tuning required
- Clear theoretical analysis with well-defined global optimality conditions
- Demonstrates the approach on real datasets (unlike previous global optimization work)
- Conceptually interesting connection to Perron-Frobenius theory

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires strong non-negativity constraints on weights (hinders practical use)
- Uses non-standard activation functions (real powers α_i ≥ 1)
- Empirical results show worse performance than SVM on UCI datasets
- Exaggerated claim about convergence rate ("typically fewer than 10 iterations")
- Limited to low-dimensional datasets due to conservative bounds on spectral radius
- No clear guidance on how to select parameters for real-world applications

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution but has significant practical limitations that undermine its significance for the NIPS community. The strong constraints on weights and activation functions make the method impractical for most applications, and the empirical results fail to demonstrate clear advantages over standard methods. While the theoretical approach is novel and well-executed, the paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential paths to relax the constraints.

The paper should be rejected due to the limitations on practical applicability and the lack of compelling empirical results demonstrating advantages over standard methods. The theoretical contribution is interesting but insufficient to justify acceptance given the severe restrictions on the model class and the poor empirical performance. The authors would need to either relax the constraints significantly or demonstrate clear practical advantages to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
