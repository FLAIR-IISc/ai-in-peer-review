

This paper proposes a globally optimal training method for a restricted class of feedforward neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods, with convergence guarantees under weak assumptions. The authors prove that for generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights and specific activation functions (real powers), their method achieves global optimality with linear convergence rates. This work builds on recent advances in globally optimal training (Janzamin et al., 2016; Haeffele & Vidal, 2015), but distinguishes itself by being practically feasible where previous approaches required estimating unknown data-generating measures or imposed impractical computational requirements. The key innovation is a modified objective function Φ that enables the application of Perron-Frobenius theory to neural network training.

The paper's strengths lie in its theoretically sound approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning. The authors provide rigorous proofs for global optimality and convergence rates for one and two hidden layer networks, which is a significant theoretical advance. The method's practical feasibility is demonstrated through experiments on UCI datasets, showing competitive performance compared to SVMs and ReLU networks. The authors' claim that this is "the first practically feasible method which achieves such a guarantee" is well-supported by their theoretical analysis and empirical results.

However, several concerns undermine the paper's impact. The most significant issue is the limited practical relevance of the restricted network class. The authors impose nonnegative weights and use generalized polynomial activations (real powers ≥1), which substantially limits expressiveness compared to standard neural networks. The paper lacks a clear discussion of what decision boundaries are possible versus impossible with this restricted class. For instance, it's unclear how the model handles XOR-like problems or complex patterns that standard ReLU networks easily capture. The authors claim the model "is rich enough to achieve good performance" but provide no theoretical justification for this claim or analysis of the expressiveness limitations.

The related work discussion is insufficiently comprehensive. The authors cite Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2016) but fail to adequately contrast their approach with these concurrent works, particularly regarding practical feasibility. The paper also omits comparison with other recent methods for globally optimal training, such as those using sum-of-squares programming (Parrilo, 2000) or convex relaxation techniques.

The experimental section is notably limited. The authors only compare against SVMs and ReLU networks (with no mention of other standard optimization methods like Adam or Adagrad), omitting crucial baselines that would demonstrate the method's practical value. The comparison against SGD is superficial, with no discussion of computational resources required (e.g., wall-clock time, memory usage) for the spectral method versus SGD. The paper also lacks analysis of the model's applicability limits—how does performance degrade with increasing dimensionality, and how do the required parameters (ρ(A) < 1) scale with dataset size?

The presentation requires significant polishing. Section 2 contains technical density without sufficient intuitive explanation, making it difficult to follow the theoretical development. The motivation for the modified objective function Φ and the ε term is not clearly explained beyond "technical reasons." The authors should provide an informal explanation before stating Theorem 1 to help readers grasp the key ideas.

Several typos and notation inconsistencies need correction (e.g., "polyomial" on p.60, undefined R_{++}, inconsistent use of p1/p2). The claim that the method "converges quickly" is based on empirical observations without theoretical justification, which should be clarified.

In terms of the four NIPS criteria:
- Quality: High technical quality with rigorous proofs, but the restricted model limits applicability.
- Clarity: Poor in later sections; requires significant revision for readability.
- Originality: Strong theoretical contribution, but the restricted model reduces practical novelty.
- Significance: Limited by the restricted model class; the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the method solves a problem of practical importance.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding globally optimal training of neural networks, but its practical relevance is questionable due to the restrictive assumptions. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1) Provide a thorough analysis of the expressiveness limitations of their model class
2) Compare against more relevant baselines (including other optimization methods)
3) Discuss computational costs and scalability
4) Clarify the motivation for Φ and the ε term
5) Address the limitations of their approach more transparently

While the theoretical advance is noteworthy, the paper falls short of demonstrating that the proposed method solves a meaningful practical problem. The current version would benefit from substantial revision before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. The method's practical utility remains unproven, and the paper would be significantly stronger if it demonstrated how to overcome the expressiveness limitations while maintaining the convergence guarantees.
