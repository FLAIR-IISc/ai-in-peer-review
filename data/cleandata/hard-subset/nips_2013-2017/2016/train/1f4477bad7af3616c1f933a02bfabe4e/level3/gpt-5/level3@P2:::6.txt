Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a restricted class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The networks use nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and generalized polynomial activations with unit-specific exponents αl ≥ 1 (and pairwise distinct). Training optimizes a modified objective Φ that equals (minus) the cross-entropy loss plus two additional terms: a sum over outputs and a small ε-weighted sum of all parameters. The core algorithm is a fixed-point iteration GΦ built from normalized gradients in appropriate ℓp spaces. Under a checkable condition ρ(A) < 1 on the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A determined by the architecture and norms, the method converges linearly to the unique global maximizer. The framework is developed rigorously using multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, the Thompson metric, and the Banach fixed point theorem. An extension to two hidden layers is provided. Experiments on several UCI datasets compare against RBF-SVM and ReLU networks trained with SGD.

Relative to prior work, this paper aims to be a practically implementable alternative to (i) tensor-based global methods that require access to the score function of the data distribution (e.g., Janzamin et al., 2015), and (ii) matrix/tensor factorization formulations guaranteeing global optima but with limited practical recipes (Haeffele & Vidal). The paper’s guarantees rest on nonnegativity and a modified objective; the global optimality condition is verifiable from architecture hyperparameters without running the algorithm.

Strengths
- Clear and substantive theoretical contribution: an easily checkable condition (spectral radius of a (K+1)×(K+1) matrix) guarantees existence, uniqueness, and linear convergence to the global optimum.
- Elegant use of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction arguments in the Thompson metric; the proof techniques may be of independent interest.
- Extension beyond one hidden layer (two layers) suggests generality of the approach.
- Algorithm has no learning-rate tuning and converges rapidly in practice on the reported tasks; the fixed-point mapping is simple to implement.
- First demonstration (to my knowledge) of a globally optimal NN training method yielding nontrivial classification results on real datasets.

Weaknesses and clarifications needed
- Practical usefulness and expressiveness are not convincingly established. The model class imposes nonnegativity on data and weights, requires non-integer polynomial activations with distinct exponents, and optimizes a nonstandard objective (adding the sum of outputs and an ε-term). A careful discussion is needed of the function class induced by these constraints: which decision boundaries are representable (e.g., monotone separations), which are out of reach, and whether universal approximation holds under nonnegativity/normalization. Concrete examples beyond the toy 2D figure would help.
- Motivation for Φ and the ε term should be presented in the main text before Theorem 1, with an informal derivation explaining why the output-sum and ε-regularization are necessary (e.g., to ensure strict positivity of the gradient on the cone and to avoid degenerate boundary solutions), how their magnitudes affect the learned classifier, and empirical sensitivity analyses.
- Empirical evaluation is limited. The datasets are small; no report on higher-dimensional or image/text tasks where nonnegativity holds naturally (e.g., count data, TF–IDF, pixel intensities). Comparisons omit:
  - Strong baselines tailored to the same hypothesis class: e.g., polynomial feature expansions with logistic regression; nonnegative networks trained by projected/positivity-constrained SGD; monotone models or NMF-based classifiers.
  - Other global or provable methods (even if impractical) to help position the contribution empirically.
  - Ablations isolating the impact of the output-sum and ε terms, and of the nonnegativity constraints.
- Missing computational resource reporting. Please provide per-iteration complexity, memory cost, wall-clock times, and hardware for NLSM1/NLSM2 versus SGD and SVM. The “converges quickly” claim should be clearly labeled as empirical and supported with runtime tables in addition to iteration plots.
- The ρ(A) < 1 condition is conservative by the authors’ admission. It would help to report (a) the distribution of computed ρ(A) across the random hyperparameter samples, (b) how often candidate architectures are rejected, and (c) empirical contraction factors versus the bound.
- Presentation and notation need polishing. Section 2 would benefit from a high-level overview of the fixed-point map and intuition for why multi-homogeneity yields contraction. Several typos/notation issues (see below).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Strong and careful theory; assumptions are clearly stated. However, the empirical section is too limited to substantiate practical impact, and several implementation details (stability for large α, normalization, stopping criteria) are missing.
- Clarity: The introduction is clear, but the main results would benefit from an informal roadmap and motivation for Φ and ε before the formal statements. Some notation is introduced late or inconsistently.
- Originality: High on the theoretical side—novel application of nonlinear spectral methods to NN training with explicit global optimality and linear convergence under a verifiable condition. The architectural/objective constraints are unusual.
- Significance: Potentially significant if the approach can be shown to work on larger problems and if the hypothesis class is demonstrated to be sufficiently expressive for practical tasks. As written, significance is limited by the narrow empirical scope and unclear applicability boundaries.

Questions for the authors
- Provide a characterization (or at least discussion) of the hypothesis class induced by nonnegativity and generalized polynomial activations. Are these models universal for monotone functions on [0,1]^d? What tasks are inherently unsuitable?
- How sensitive are results to ε and to the magnitude of the output-sum term? Can ε be sent to zero in practice without numerical issues?
- What preprocessing is recommended to satisfy input nonnegativity (e.g., shifts), and how does it affect calibration/performance?
- Report runtime/memory and hardware; include wall-clock comparisons to SGD and SVM.
- How often does ρ(A) < 1 fail for moderately sized architectures? Can tighter bounds (Theorem 4) materially enlarge the feasible region?

Minor comments and typos
- “polyomial” → “polynomial”; “one CAN model” → “one can model.”
- R_{++} should be defined explicitly when first used; likewise introduce p_w, p_u (and p_v) properly.
- Several references are incomplete; please standardize citation formatting.
- Clarify in text that “converges quickly” is an empirical observation; provide corresponding runtimes.
- Figure and table captions should be more descriptive (dataset sizes, hyperparameter ranges).

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, novel theoretical framework with clean, checkable conditions and linear convergence.
- Bridges Perron–Frobenius theory and NN training; methods and proofs could stimulate follow-up work.
- First empirical demonstration (albeit small-scale) of a globally optimal NN training algorithm.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical value not convincingly established; unclear expressiveness and applicability boundaries.
- Limited and small-scale experiments without strong or targeted baselines, ablations, or runtime reporting.
- Presentation gaps (motivation of Φ/ε, informal intuition) and several typos/notation issues.

Overall, I find the theoretical advance compelling, but the paper in its current form does not yet demonstrate sufficient practical relevance or clarity. Strengthening the empirical section, adding expressiveness analysis, and improving presentation would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
