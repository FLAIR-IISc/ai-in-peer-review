

This paper proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with guaranteed global optimality and linear convergence rate. The authors develop a theoretical framework based on Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, showing that under nonnegativity constraints on weights and with specific generalized polynomial activation functions, the global optimum can be reached by computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix. The approach represents a theoretical advance over previous work on global optimality guarantees [8, 11], as it provides a practically feasible method with convergence guarantees, unlike prior tensor-based approaches that require unknown data distributions.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with rigorous proofs of global optimality and linear convergence. The mathematical development is well-structured, building on fixed point theory and Thompson metric spaces. The method's novelty is evident in its distinct approach to neural network training, differentiating it from both standard SGD methods and prior global optimality work.

However, the paper's practical significance is severely limited by two major constraints: the requirement for nonnegative weights and the use of highly specific activation functions that differ substantially from standard choices like ReLU. These restrictions significantly hinder the method's applicability to real-world problems, as evidenced by the empirical results showing worse performance than SVM on all UCI datasets tested. The claim that linear convergence "typically" implies convergence in fewer than 10 iterations is exaggerated, as the paper's own Figure 2 shows convergence requiring more iterations.

Strengths: Conceptual novelty, rigorous theoretical foundation, clear presentation of the mathematical framework.
Weaknesses: Severe architectural constraints, limited empirical validation against standard baselines, practical performance inferior to existing methods.

While the theoretical contribution is substantial and represents a meaningful advance in understanding neural network optimization, the paper's practical limitations and subpar empirical results significantly diminish its significance for the NIPS community. The method's constraints make it unlikely to be adopted in real-world applications, and the empirical results fail to demonstrate clear advantages over simpler alternatives. For these reasons, I recommend rejection.
