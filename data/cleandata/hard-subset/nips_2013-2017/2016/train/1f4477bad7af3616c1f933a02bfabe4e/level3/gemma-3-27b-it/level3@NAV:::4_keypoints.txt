- Presents a novel nonlinear spectral method for training generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights, reducing global optimality to computing the spectral radius of a small non-negative matrix.  
- Provides a rigorous theoretical framework and a theorem guaranteeing linear convergence to a global optimum under specific conditions.  
- Extends the analysis to networks with two hidden layers and supplies a clear algorithm and proof strategy.  
- Proofs leverage fixed-point theory and the Perron–Frobenius theorem, and are well-motivated and apparently sound.  
- Requires non-negative weights, which is a strong and limiting constraint on model applicability.  
- Uses specific power-based activation functions instead of common activations like ReLU, further narrowing practical relevance.  
- Experimental results demonstrate the method works but generally underperform compared to SVM on tested UCI datasets.  
- Claim that linear convergence implies convergence in fewer than 10 iterations appears exaggerated and is not clearly supported by experiments.  
- Novelty is a strong point: conceptually new perspective on neural network optimization.  
- Theoretical soundness and mathematical completeness are strengths supporting acceptance.  
- Major weaknesses: restrictive assumptions (non-negativity and activation choice) limit real-world use.  
- Empirical performance shortcomings and overstated convergence claims argue against acceptance.  
- Overall recommendation: not suitable for NIPS in current form; better fit for a theory-focused workshop unless assumptions are relaxed and empirical validation improved.  
- Suggested revisions: loosen restrictive assumptions and investigate sensitivity to architecture and data characteristics.
