- Presents a novel training method for networks with nonnegative inputs/weights and generalized polynomial activations that provides explicit global optimality guarantees.  
- Reformulates the training problem as a nonlinear eigenvalue problem and solves it via a nonlinear spectral method.  
- Proves linear convergence to the unique global optimum under a spectral-radius condition on a derived nonnegative matrix.  
- The spectral-radius precondition is simple and checkable without running the algorithm.  
- Extends prior theoretical work (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.) by giving a practically applicable method with explicit guarantees.  
- Demonstrates applicability to one- and two-hidden-layer networks and claims extendability to deeper architectures.  
- Experiments on a 2D synthetic dataset and small UCI datasets show competitive performance and much faster convergence than SGD.  
- Strong theoretical rigor, clear presentation, and a well-motivated connection to Perron–Frobenius and nonlinear eigenvalue theory.  
- Major limitation: requirement of nonnegative inputs and weights significantly restricts model expressiveness.  
- Unnatural constraint that each hidden layer must use different activation parameters (α_l ≠ α_m), limiting applicability.  
- Spectral-radius < 1 constraint forces weight reduction (increasing p-values/reducing p_w/p_u), shrinking model capacity and risking near-linear behavior.  
- Scalability concern: as input dimensionality grows, satisfying the spectral constraint may require further weight shrinking, hindering large-scale use.  
- Experiments are limited to small datasets and shallow architectures; more empirical study on how parameters (p1, p2, p_w, p_u, dimension, size) affect ρ(A) is needed.  
- Overall: significant theoretical contribution that opens a promising direction despite practical constraints; reviewer recommends acceptance.
