This paper presents a fascinating and ambitious effort to develop a globally optimal training algorithm for a specific class of feedforward neural networks. The core contribution – a nonlinear spectral method guaranteeing global optimality with linear convergence – is a significant departure from the typical stochastic gradient descent-based approaches that dominate deep learning. The theoretical results are compelling, and the experimental validation, while limited, provides initial evidence of the method's feasibility. However, several aspects of the work require further clarification and justification.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear motivation and relating the work to existing challenges in neural network optimization (e.g., NP-hardness of single-layer training, limitations of SGD). The authors correctly identify recent attempts to address global optimality [8, 11] and position their work as offering a more practical solution, avoiding the difficulties of tensor computations or data-generating measure estimation. The mathematical development, while dense, attempts to provide a rigorous foundation for the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The guarantee of global optimality with linear convergence is a strong and novel contribution, distinguishing this work from the vast majority of neural network training algorithms.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a significant amount of theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1 and subsequent refinements. The use of Perron-Frobenius theory and related concepts is interesting and provides a solid mathematical framework.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors clearly define the class of networks they are considering and the specific conditions under which global optimality is achieved.
* **Initial Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while limited to UCI datasets, demonstrate that the proposed method can achieve competitive performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Mathematical Derivations:** While generally rigorous, some parts of the mathematical development are unclear and could benefit from more detailed explanations. Specifically, the jump between Theorem 3 (first-order derivatives) and Theorem 4 (second derivatives) isn’t adequately explained. Why is a shift in the derivative order necessary, and what implications does it have?
* **Treatment of the Last Layer:** The handling of the output layer gradient (w.r.t. *u*) as simply the gradient of the vectorized *u* is not fully justified. Is this consistent across all derivations, and could it be clarified? It feels like a simplification that might be hiding some complexities.
* **Unjustified Constructions:** Several constructions, such as bounding *u* in a ball, feel ad-hoc and lack sufficient justification. The motivation behind these choices is not always clear.
* **1-Norm Maximization:** The objective function maximizes the 1-norm of connection parameters (Eq. 2). This is unusual, as weight decay (L2 regularization) is far more common in neural network training. The rationale for this choice, and its potential impact on generalization, needs further explanation.  It seems the epsilon term is solely to guarantee strictly positive gradients, acting as a proof artifact rather than having a practical justification.
* **Non-Negativity Constraints:** The requirement of non-negative data and strictly positive weights is a major limitation. This severely restricts the applicability of the method and raises concerns about unbounded growth or instability.  While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth discussion of the practical implications and potential mitigations would be valuable.
* **Practical Advantages:** The comparison to SVM is sensible given the latter's global optimizer, but the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that the proposed NN offers *practical* advantages over commonly used NNs (e.g., with ReLU). The experimental results are not strong enough to outweigh the significant constraints imposed by the method.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind the shift from first-order to second-order derivatives between Theorem 3 and Theorem 4?
* Please clarify the treatment of the gradient with respect to *u* in the final layer.
* What is the motivation for maximizing the 1-norm of the weights, and how does this choice affect the properties of the learned models?
* How sensitive is the method to the choice of epsilon in Equation (2)?
* Could you discuss potential strategies for mitigating the limitations imposed by the non-negativity constraints?

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound contribution to the field of neural network optimization. The guarantee of global optimality is a significant achievement. However, the practical limitations imposed by the non-negativity constraints and the lack of a compelling demonstration of practical advantages raise concerns.  The clarity of some mathematical derivations could also be improved.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novelty and theoretical significance.
* Rigorous mathematical foundation.
* Initial evidence of feasibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Practical limitations of non-negativity constraints.
* Lack of strong experimental results demonstrating practical advantages.
* Clarity issues in certain mathematical derivations.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a more compelling justification for the 1-norm maximization, elaborating on the implications of the non-negativity constraints, and presenting more convincing experimental results. A clearer explanation of the mathematical derivations would also be beneficial. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the practical limitations currently outweigh the advantages.
