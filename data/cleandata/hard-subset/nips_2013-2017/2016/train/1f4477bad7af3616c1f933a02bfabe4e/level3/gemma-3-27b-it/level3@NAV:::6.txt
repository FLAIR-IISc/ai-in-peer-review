## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel method for training a restricted class of feedforward neural networks – generalized polynomial networks with non-negative weights – and provides a theoretical guarantee of global convergence with a linear rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The problem addressed is interesting and tackling the non-convexity of neural network training is a significant challenge. The theoretical contribution, establishing global optimality, is appreciated and represents a worthwhile advance.

However, the practical relevance of this work is not convincingly demonstrated. While the authors claim good performance on UCI datasets, the restricted network class raises concerns about expressiveness. A more thorough discussion of what types of functions *can* and *cannot* be effectively modeled by this approach is crucial.  The paper lacks a clear articulation of the trade-offs imposed by the non-negativity constraint and the generalized polynomial activation function. 

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is limited. Comparisons are made to SVMs and ReLU networks, but crucial omissions include other globally optimal or efficiently trainable neural network methods (beyond the cited works) and alternative optimization paradigms. The paper doesn’t detail the computational resources required for the proposed method and the benchmarks, making a fair comparison difficult. The experimental results do not effectively delineate the applicability limits of the model; it is unclear under what conditions this method outperforms others or what types of datasets it struggles with.

The presentation requires polishing. While the introduction is well-written, subsequent sections, particularly Section 2, lack fluency and could benefit from clearer explanations. Numerous typos and notation issues (e.g., “polyomial” on p60, “one CAN model” on p67, undefined p1 and p2, R_{++}) detract from the readability. Technical clarifications are also needed: the motivation for the function Φ and the ε term should be better explained, and the claim of "quick convergence" needs to be explicitly linked to empirical observations rather than being presented as a direct consequence of the theory. An informal explanation preceding Theorem 1 would also enhance understanding. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical result: establishes global optimality for a specific network class.
*   Interesting problem: Addresses a fundamental challenge in neural network training.
*   Initial empirical validation: Demonstrates applicability on some real-world datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited expressiveness: Lack of clear discussion regarding the model's limitations.
*   Insufficient experimental evaluation: Limited comparisons and missing resource information.
*   Presentation issues: Typos, notation errors, and clarity problems.
*   Lack of context: Insufficient discussion of related and concurrent work.



**Overall:** While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the lack of a compelling demonstration of practical utility and the issues with presentation and experimental rigor currently outweigh the strengths. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses, particularly focusing on a more detailed discussion of the model's expressiveness, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, and careful proofreading and clarification of the presentation.
