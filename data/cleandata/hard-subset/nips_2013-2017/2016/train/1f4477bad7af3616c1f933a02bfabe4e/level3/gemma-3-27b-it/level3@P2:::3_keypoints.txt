- Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method guaranteeing global optimality and linear convergence for feedforward networks with nonnegative inputs/weights and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Provides a practical global-optimum check via the spectral radius of a relatively small matrix.  
- Builds on recent globally optimal training work but addresses prior practical limitations.  
- Extends the approach to two hidden layers, showing potential for scaling.  
- Experimental results on UCI datasets are limited but show competitive performance.  
- Strong theoretical contribution: rigorous proofs of global optimality and linear convergence under reasonable assumptions.  
- Novel use of Perron–Frobenius theory applied to neural network optimization.  
- Simplicity of the spectral-radius check lowers the barrier to use.  
- Major limitation: requires nonnegative inputs and weights, significantly constraining applicability and expressiveness.  
- Restriction to generalized polynomial activations further limits practical applicability.  
- Requirement for distinct α values per hidden unit appears artificial and may be a design artifact.  
- Scalability concerns: satisfying the spectral-radius constraint may become harder with higher input/parameter dimensionality, potentially forcing aggressive weight shrinkage.  
- Theoretical bounds (Sections 3–4) are abstract and lack intuitive explanation.  
- Suggested future work: empirically study how model parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu, pw, pu) affect the spectral radius and scalability.  
- Suggested future work: explore other activation functions that might retain global-optimality guarantees.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, practical algorithm, novelty, clear presentation, and initial real-data results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: strong assumptions, scalability concerns, limited expressiveness, and the possibly artificial distinct-α constraint.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — significant and encouraging contribution, but future work should address scalability and expressiveness limitations.
