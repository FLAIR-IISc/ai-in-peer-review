**Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"**

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for training generalized polynomial neural networks (GPNs) with a nonlinear spectral method, which guarantees global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The authors restrict themselves to one and two hidden layer networks and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several UCI datasets. The method is based on a specific objective function that includes a modified loss term and a regularization term, which allows for the application of a nonlinear spectral method.

**Strengths**

1. **Global optimality guarantee**: The paper provides a rigorous proof of global optimality for the proposed method, which is a significant contribution to the field of neural network training.
2. **Linear convergence rate**: The method converges linearly to the global optimum, which is a desirable property in optimization algorithms.
3. **Competitive performance**: The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive performance with other state-of-the-art methods on several UCI datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Restrictive assumptions**: The method imposes restrictive assumptions on the data and model parameters, such as non-negative weights and a specific form of the activation function.
2. **Uncommon activation function**: The use of a generalized polynomial activation function may not be widely applicable in practice.
3. **Scalability issues**: The bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units, which may lead to scalability issues for high-dimensional data.
4. **Optimality condition**: The optimality condition may not be satisfied for practical parameter settings, which requires a large parameter search to identify valid candidates.
5. **Lack of comparison with nonlinear SVM**: The paper does not provide a comparison with nonlinear SVM baselines, which would be a useful addition to the experimental evaluation.

**Originality**

The paper proposes a novel method for training GPNs with a nonlinear spectral method, which is a new contribution to the field of neural network training. The use of a generalized polynomial activation function and the specific objective function are also original aspects of the paper.

**Significance**

The paper provides a significant contribution to the field of neural network training by proposing a method that guarantees global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the method on several UCI datasets, which suggests that the method may be useful in practice.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and the theoretical results, which makes the paper accessible to a wide range of readers.

**Recommendations**

1. **Provide more experimental evaluations**: The paper would benefit from more experimental evaluations on larger datasets and with different architectures.
2. **Compare with nonlinear SVM**: The paper should include a comparison with nonlinear SVM baselines to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the method.
3. **Discuss the scalability issues**: The paper should discuss the scalability issues associated with the method and propose potential solutions to address these issues.
4. **Provide more insights into the optimality condition**: The paper should provide more insights into the optimality condition and discuss how to identify valid candidates in practice.

**Acceptance**

Based on the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, I recommend accepting the paper for publication in NIPS. The paper provides a significant contribution to the field of neural network training, and the experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the method on several UCI datasets. However, the paper could benefit from additional experimental evaluations and a more comprehensive discussion of the scalability issues and optimality condition.
