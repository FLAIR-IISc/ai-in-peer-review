## Review of “Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods”

This paper proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method for globally optimally training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with a linear convergence rate. The core idea is to restrict the network to utilize non-negative weights and a modified objective function, allowing for a globally optimal solution to be found by computing the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix. This is a conceptually interesting approach, building on the Perron-Frobenius theory and offering a deterministic alternative to stochastic gradient descent. The work clearly positions itself within the growing body of research aiming for provable guarantees in neural network training, referencing relevant works like Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2016).

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical novelty and the clear presentation of the method. The authors provide a detailed analysis and proof of convergence, extending the framework to two hidden layers. The claim of linear convergence is particularly appealing, and the authors correctly highlight the lack of practical methods guaranteeing global optimality with such a rate. The inclusion of experimental results, even if limited, demonstrates an attempt to validate the theoretical findings.

However, the practical limitations of the approach are significant. The requirement for non-negative weights is a strong constraint that may severely limit the expressiveness of the networks. Similarly, the use of generalized polynomial activation functions, while mathematically convenient for the analysis, deviates from standard practice and may hinder performance. The empirical results, showing worse performance than SVM on the tested datasets, further reinforce these concerns. While the authors acknowledge these limitations, their impact on the broader applicability of the method is substantial. The claim that linear convergence “typically” implies convergence in fewer than 10 iterations feels exaggerated and lacks strong justification. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical framework for globally optimal training.
*   Clear and detailed mathematical analysis.
*   Addresses a fundamental challenge in neural network optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong restrictions on network architecture (non-negativity, specific activations).
*   Empirical performance inferior to standard methods (SVM).
*   Limited scope and potential for practical application.
*   Overstated claim regarding linear convergence speed.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution, its practical limitations and underwhelming empirical results raise concerns. The strong constraints imposed on the network architecture significantly restrict its applicability. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should consider relaxing the constraints or demonstrating a clear advantage in specific application domains where these constraints are not detrimental. Further investigation into the tightness of the theoretical bounds and more extensive empirical evaluation are also needed.
