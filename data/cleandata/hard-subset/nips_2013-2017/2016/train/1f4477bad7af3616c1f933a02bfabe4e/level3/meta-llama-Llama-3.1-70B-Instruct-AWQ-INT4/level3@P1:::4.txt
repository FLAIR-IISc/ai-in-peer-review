This paper proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, achieving global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The authors demonstrate that, under certain weak assumptions on the data, their method can be applied to train neural networks with one or two hidden layers, achieving competitive performance on several real-world datasets.

The main idea behind the paper is to reformulate the neural network training problem as a nonlinear eigenproblem, which can be solved using a spectral method. The authors show that their method can achieve global optimality, unlike traditional stochastic gradient descent methods, which provide no guarantee of convergence to the global optimum.

The paper makes several contributions to the field of neural networks. Firstly, it proposes a new approach to training neural networks, which is based on nonlinear spectral methods. This approach is conceptually interesting and provides a new perspective on the neural network training problem. Secondly, the paper demonstrates that the proposed method can achieve global optimality, which is a desirable property in many machine learning applications.

However, the paper also has several limitations. One major limitation is that the method requires non-negativity constraints on the network weights, which may hinder the practical performance of the neural networks. Additionally, the method uses non-standard, very specific activation functions, which may not be widely applicable. These strong restrictions may limit the usefulness of the method in practice.

The empirical results presented in the paper show that the method performs worse than SVM in the reported experiments. While the authors claim that their method achieves competitive performance, the results suggest that the method may not be as effective as other state-of-the-art methods.

A minor comment is that the claim that linear convergence "typically" implies convergence in fewer than 10 iterations is exaggerated. While the method may converge quickly in some cases, the number of iterations required for convergence can vary widely depending on the specific problem and the choice of hyperparameters.

Overall, the paper proposes an interesting new approach to training neural networks, but its practical usefulness is limited by the strong restrictions on the network weights and activation functions.
