- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method that provably converges to the global optimum at a linear rate for a specific class of neural networks.  
- Applicable to one- or two-hidden-layer networks with generalized polynomial activations (real powers α ≥ 1) and non-negative weights/data.  
- Convergence condition is checkable a priori via the spectral radius of a small non-negative matrix (requirement ρ(A) < 1).  
- Proof technique reduces optimization to finding a unique fixed point of a contraction mapping in the Thompson metric, connecting to Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous maps.  
- Method is parameter-free (no learning rate) and presented as a practical alternative to SGD and prior global-optimum approaches.  
- Theoretical derivation is rigorous and original, but relies on strong practical constraints (non-negativity and specific activations) that limit expressiveness.  
- The objective is modified with an added term summing network outputs for the proof; the impact of this modification on classifier quality is not fully explored.  
- Experiments on UCI datasets demonstrate feasibility but often underperform standard SVM and sometimes ReLU networks.  
- Scalability is a concern: ρ(A) increases with hidden units n1, limiting applicability to small/wide networks in experiments.  
- The requirement that activation exponents α_l be distinct and chosen (via random search) is restrictive and lacks intuition about sensitivity.  
- The claim of "very fast convergence in few iterations" is unclear given convergence plots spanning many epochs; epoch definition needs clarification.  
- Raises the open question whether the global optimum of this constrained model yields better classifiers than local optima of less constrained models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: highly original, theoretically sound, checkable convergence condition, parameter-free, and one of the first practical demonstrations of global-opt training.  
- Arguments against acceptance: severe model restrictions, unimpressive empirical performance, and uncertain practical significance of the constrained global optimum.
