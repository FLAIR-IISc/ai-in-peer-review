- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method that provably trains a restricted class of feed-forward NNs to global optimality with linear convergence under a checkable spectral-radius condition.  
- Networks require nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights with generalized power activations and distinct exponents; objective is negative cross-entropy plus output-sum and a small positive term under norm constraints.  
- Main theorem: unique global maximizer and geometric convergence when the spectral radius of a constructed (K+1)×(K+1) nonnegative matrix ρ(A)<1; condition verifiable before training; extends to two hidden layers.  
- Connects novelly to Perron–Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous maps and contraction in the Thompson metric, differing from tensor and factorization global-optimality work.  
- Strength: clear theoretical contribution giving a checkable sufficient condition for uniqueness and linear convergence for a nontrivial NN class.  
- Strength: solid proof strategy using contraction mappings and multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius; nontrivial extension to two hidden layers.  
- Strength: practical algorithm requires no learning-rate tuning and condition depends only on architecture and simple data norms.  
- Strength: initial empirical results show the restricted models can learn nontrivial decision boundaries and be competitive on some small datasets.  
- Weakness: expressiveness of the restricted class (nonnegativity, monotonicity, posynomial-like form, distinct exponents) is insufficiently characterized.  
- Suggestion: formally characterize hypothesis class (monotonicity, scaling properties, missing sign interactions) and contrast with ReLU and polynomial kernels.  
- Suggestion: provide representable and non-representable function examples and discuss input preprocessing like sign-splitting to restore generality.  
- Suggestion: clarify how normalization constraints and the output-sum/ε terms affect decision boundaries and gradients.  
- Weakness: related work section is incomplete; expand connections to nonnegative/monotone networks, NMF, polynomial-kernel SVMs, other spectral methods, and benign-landscape analyses.  
- Weakness: experiments are too limited to establish practical value and miss strong baselines (polynomial SVMs, logistic regression, advanced NN optimizers, random features).  
- Experimental suggestions: report hardware, wall-clock time, iterations/epochs, per-iteration cost, iteration counts vs. spectral-radius bound, and test larger/diverse datasets including failure cases (ρ(A)≥1, negative inputs).  
- Ablation suggestions: evaluate effects of ε, output-sum term, distinct vs repeated α, sensitivity to pw/pu/ρw/ρu/hidden width, and correlation between ρ(A) and convergence speed.  
- Presentation issues: Section 2 is dense; recommend an informal explanation of Theorem 1, motivate Φ’s output-sum and ε terms in main text, reorganize intuition/algorithm/guarantees, add early pseudocode and a figure.  
- Quality: theory appears sound and assumptions clear though some bounds may be conservative; empirical validation is thin and practical usefulness not convincingly demonstrated.  
- Clarity: generally readable but technical parts lack fluency and have notational inconsistencies; define symbols at first use and fix typos.  
- Originality: novel synthesis of nonlinear spectral methods with NN training and a checkable global optimality condition; distinctive architectural restrictions.  
- Significance: potentially significant if applicability beyond toy settings is shown; currently limited by empirical scope and unclear expressiveness.  
- Key questions for authors: precise characterization of the function class, sensitivity of convergence to conservativeness of A, effect of sign-splitting on assumptions, and per-iteration complexity/memory vs SGD/Adam.  
- Noted typos/notation issues: misspellings (“polyomial”), unclear p1/p2 vs pw/pu, R_{++} used before definition, inconsistent ε usage, undefined α/β indices and sets J, Jc.  
- Pros: elegant rigorous global optimality with a checkable condition, parameter-free iteration, extension to two layers, and linking spectral radius to architecture/data norms.  
- Cons: limited expressiveness under strict nonnegativity/normalization, insufficient experiments and baselines, missing runtime reporting, and presentation/notation weaknesses.  
- Overall recommendation: promising theory and algorithmic idea but currently below acceptance threshold without substantially expanded experiments and explicit discussion of model capabilities and limits.
