## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) – with guaranteed global optimality. The authors leverage fixed-point theory, constructing a contractive map whose fixed point corresponds to the unique minimizer of the cost function. This is a significant contribution, as achieving global optimality in neural network training is notoriously difficult. The work builds upon recent advances in globally optimal training (e.g., [8, 11]) but distinguishes itself by offering a practically feasible method under relatively weak assumptions (non-negativity of data and weights). 

The core strength of the paper lies in its theoretical guarantees. Proving the existence and uniqueness of a stationary point, and then demonstrating linear convergence to this global minimum, is a substantial achievement. The authors clearly articulate the conditions under which these guarantees hold, specifically relating to the spectral radius of a matrix derived from the network architecture. The extension to two hidden layers, while brief, demonstrates the potential for generalization.

However, the practical applicability of the method is questionable. The convergence proof hinges on bounding the spectral radius, which limits scalability to larger hidden layers. More critically, the method requires extensive hyperparameter tuning, and the spectral condition must be verified for *every* parameter choice, creating a significant overhead. The experiments, while demonstrating the method's functionality, are not particularly compelling. The authors performed a large hyperparameter search (~150,000 combinations) but only outperformed a linear SVM on 2 out of 7 datasets. This raises concerns about the practical utility of GPNNs trained with this method. Furthermore, the reliance on non-negativity constraints may limit the expressiveness of the network.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and novel approach to global optimization.
*   Clear and rigorous mathematical analysis.
*   Demonstrates a unique result: complex models *can* admit unique stationary points under carefully chosen constraints.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scalability due to spectral radius constraint.
*   Cumbersome hyperparameter tuning and verification process.
*   Empirically weak performance compared to standard methods.
*   Strong assumptions (non-negativity) may restrict applicability.



**Overall:** While the theoretical contributions are noteworthy, the practical limitations and underwhelming empirical results make this paper a borderline case. The authors have identified an interesting theoretical property, but significant work is needed to address the scalability and performance issues before this method can be considered a viable alternative to existing training techniques. I would lean towards **weak reject** and encourage the authors to focus on addressing the scalability and empirical performance concerns in future work.
