This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method that trains a class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with linear convergence, under checkable conditions. The models use nonnegative weights and inputs, normalized layers, and generalized polynomial activations with exponents αl ≥ 1 (distinct across hidden units). The training objective is nonstandard: a maximization of “negative loss + sum of outputs” plus a small positive linear term on the parameters to ensure strict positivity of the gradient on the cone. The core result shows that, when the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix is < 1, an iterative map GΦ is a contraction in the Thompson metric on a product cone, implying a unique fixed point equal to the global maximizer and linear convergence. The approach extends from one to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees. Empirically, the paper presents proof-of-concept experiments on small UCI datasets, reporting competitive performance to some baselines and fast convergence compared to SGD variants.

Relation to prior work: Building on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and multi-homogeneous maps, the paper aligns with a line of work that seeks global guarantees for neural network training (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.). Compared to tensor-based approaches that require access to high-order score functions/densities, this method trades that requirement for positivity constraints and a modified objective, arguing better practical feasibility. It also connects to results on NP-hardness of general training even for a single hidden layer, motivating the need for structural constraints.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically solid. The proof strategy—from showing the optimizer lies in the positive interior, to characterizing critical points as fixed points of GΦ, and finally establishing contraction using Lipschitz bounds tied to A—appears sound. The conditions are transparent: ρ(A) < 1 is checkable a priori; the dependence of ρ(A) on architecture and norms is clearly discussed; and the linear rate follows from Banach’s theorem. However:
- The bounds used to construct A are conservative; achieving ρ(A) < 1 often requires large pw, pu (potentially hurting optimization geometry and expressiveness). The dependence of A on the number of hidden units suggests scalability issues as depth/width grow.
- The method’s practical relevance hinges on restrictive modeling choices (nonnegative inputs and weights, generalized polynomial activations, and a modified objective). While the paper motivates these choices for theory, it remains unclear how often they yield competitive models in practice.
- Experimental validation is limited. The datasets are small and low-dimensional; there is no stress test of scalability, nor ablations disentangling the effect of the added output-sum term from the spectral method itself.
- The optimality condition ρ(A) < 1 is easy to check but hard to satisfy without a broad hyperparameter search. The paper would benefit from heuristics that produce valid models without extensive random search.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and well organized, given the technical nature. The flow from the optimization problem to fixed-point theory to application is clear. Notation is heavy but consistent. Practical guidance is thin: readers would benefit from a concise “how to set α, norms, and ρ’s” section; guidance on handling nonnegative inputs in practice (e.g., shifts, scaling); and tighter, interpretable bounds for A. The rationale for the extra objective terms could be emphasized further, with empirical ablations.

Originality: The combination of multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory, the Thompson metric, and a fixed-point formulation to obtain globally optimal training with linear convergence for nontrivial neural architectures is novel and interesting. It advances the theory of globally optimal training in a direction distinct from tensor or matrix factorization approaches and appears to be the first practically implementable method with such guarantees for these architectures.

Significance: The work is theoretically significant; it offers a new pathway to provably optimal training. The practical significance is currently limited by the modeling constraints and the small-scale experiments. Demonstrating competitive performance and reasonable model selection on larger, higher-dimensional datasets (images as nonnegative pixels, bag-of-words text) would strengthen the case. Further, guidance to systematically obtain ρ(A) < 1 without overly conservative norms would increase impact.

Pros:
- Clear, rigorous theory with a checkable global optimality condition and linear convergence.
- Extends to two hidden layers; analysis tools are potentially generalizable.
- Potentially impactful direction connecting nonlinear spectral theory to network training.

Cons:
- Strong modeling constraints (nonnegativity, generalized polynomial activations, modified objective) limit applicability.
- Conservative bounds and increasing ρ(A) with width raise scalability concerns.
- Experiments are small-scale; baselines and tuning do not convincingly establish practical competitiveness.
- Limited practical guidance to pick hyperparameters satisfying ρ(A) < 1 without heavy search.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide tighter, data-dependent bounds for A (e.g., using Theorem 4’s refined Ψ terms) and develop heuristics to set pw, pu that reliably yield ρ(A) < 1.
- Expand experiments: larger datasets; image/text tasks with naturally nonnegative features; stronger baselines (well-tuned nonlinear SVMs such as RBF and polynomial, boosted trees, kernel logistic regression); and training-time/runtime comparisons.
- Ablate the added output-sum/epsilon terms to quantify their effect on optimization/performance; compare against training the same architecture with standard losses using SGD/Adam from many restarts to probe whether global optimality is due to the special objective.
- Study expressivity: characterize approximation properties within the nonnegative cone; analyze the necessity of distinct α’s; provide intuition on selecting α for a given task.
- Discuss preprocessing for data with negative features and the effect on guarantees.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The theoretical contribution is strong and original, but the current experimental validation and practical guidance are insufficient to support significance at NIPS. Strengthening the empirical section and providing tighter, more usable conditions would move this to a clear accept.
