## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a compelling approach to training a specific class of neural networks – those with non-negative inputs, non-negative linear weights, and generalized polynomial activations – with provable global optimality and linear convergence. The authors propose a novel nonlinear spectral method to achieve this, extending previous work on globally optimal training to deeper networks (though demonstrated here up to two hidden layers). This work addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: the non-convexity of the optimization landscape and the lack of guarantees for reaching a global optimum with standard methods like SGD. 

The core novelty lies in providing the *first* potentially practical neural network model with explicit optimality guarantees under readily checkable preconditions – specifically, a constraint on the spectral radius of a matrix derived from network parameters. This is a significant contribution, as prior work often relied on complex tensor decompositions or assumptions about the data generating measure. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of their approach, namely the non-negativity constraints and the use of different activation functions per layer, which could limit expressiveness. 

The experimental validation on 2D synthetic data and small UCI datasets demonstrates the algorithm's effectiveness in those settings. However, the experiments are limited in scope, and the performance gains over standard methods like SVM and ReLU networks are modest. A key concern, as highlighted by the authors themselves, is the potential for the spectral radius constraint to force the network towards near-linear behavior as dimensionality increases, hindering scalability. The authors acknowledge this and suggest further investigation, which is a crucial direction for future work. 

Specifically, I strongly agree with the reviewer’s suggestion to empirically investigate the relationship between p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, network size, and the spectral radius ρ(A). Plotting this relationship would provide valuable insight into the practical limitations of the method and guide the development of strategies to mitigate the scaling issue. Furthermore, exploring the impact of the non-negativity constraints on the model’s expressiveness with more complex datasets would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: Provides a practical approach to globally optimal training with guarantees.
*   Theoretical Soundness: Rigorous mathematical analysis supports the claims.
*   Significance: Addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited Scalability: The spectral radius constraint may hinder performance in high-dimensional settings.
*   Restricted Expressiveness: Non-negativity constraints and per-layer activation functions may limit model capacity.
*   Limited Experimental Scope: Experiments are conducted on small datasets.



**Overall:** Despite the limitations, I find this work encouraging and recommend acceptance. The authors have made a significant contribution by providing a theoretically grounded and practically implementable algorithm for training neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The identified limitations are clearly stated and provide a clear roadmap for future research.
