This paper proposes a novel approach to training neural networks by leveraging nonlinear spectral methods to achieve global optimality. The authors focus on a specific class of feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers, where the activation functions are real powers of the form of a generalized polynomial. They introduce a modified objective function that includes a sum of loss and the negative total sum of the outputs, which allows them to apply nonlinear spectral methods to find the global optimum.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a fast algorithm that converges to the unique fixed point of the modified objective function, which corresponds to the global minimum of the cost. The authors employ fixed-point theory by constructing a contractive map whose fixed point equals the unique minimizer. This approach is novel and interesting, as it highlights the possibility of achieving unique stationary points in complex models with carefully chosen constraints.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method, including a proof of convergence and a characterization of the Lipschitz constant of the mapping. The authors also discuss the application of their framework to neural networks with two hidden layers and provide experimental results on several low-dimensional UCI datasets.

The strengths of this paper include:

* The introduction of a novel approach to training neural networks that achieves global optimality.
* The development of a fast algorithm that converges to the unique fixed point of the modified objective function.
* A thorough analysis of the proposed method, including a proof of convergence and a characterization of the Lipschitz constant of the mapping.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

* The method requires many hyperparameters to be tuned, making the procedure unintuitive and cumbersome.
* The spectral-condition must be verified for every parameter choice, adding extra verification overhead.
* The experiments involved ~150,000 parameter combinations selected by cross-validation, which is a significant computational burden.
* The empirical results show that the method outperformed linear SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets, calling the practical utility of the investigated network into question.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks that achieves global optimality. The use of nonlinear spectral methods and the construction of a contractive map are new and interesting contributions to the field.

The significance of the paper is moderate, as it provides a new perspective on training neural networks and achieves global optimality in a specific class of models. However, the practical utility of the method is still uncertain, and further research is needed to fully explore its potential.

Overall, I would rate this paper as follows:

* Quality: 7/10
* Clarity: 8/10
* Originality: 8/10
* Significance: 6/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above, including the need for hyperparameter tuning and the verification of the spectral-condition. Additionally, they should provide more insights into the practical utility of the method and its potential applications.
