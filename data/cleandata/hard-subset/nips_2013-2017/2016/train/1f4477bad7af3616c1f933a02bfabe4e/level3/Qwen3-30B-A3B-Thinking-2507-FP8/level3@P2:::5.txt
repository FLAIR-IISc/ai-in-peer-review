

# Review for NIPS 2016

This paper introduces a novel approach for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors consider networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative data, using generalized polynomial activation functions (real powers α ≥ 1) and propose a nonlinear spectral method that converges linearly to the global optimum under certain conditions. The work builds on recent efforts by Janzamin et al. (2015) and Haeffele & Vidal (2015) on global optimization of neural networks, but differs in providing a practically feasible method with a convergence rate guarantee.

**Quality:** The paper provides a theoretically sound contribution with a rigorous proof of global optimality and linear convergence. The authors successfully establish the connection between neural network training and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory, which is well-motivated. However, the proof contains some gaps that need clarification. For instance, Theorem 3 uses first-order derivative inequalities while Theorem 4 involves second derivatives without clear justification for the shift in analytical approach. Additionally, the treatment of the gradient with respect to the weight matrix u (as a vectorized quantity in some places and as a matrix in others) is inconsistent and not clearly justified. The nonnegativity constraints on both weights and data are quite restrictive, potentially limiting practical applicability. The requirement to maximize the 1-norm of connection parameters in the objective function (instead of using standard weight decay) is not well-motivated, and the small ε term appears to be primarily a proof artifact without clear practical justification.

**Clarity:** The mathematical development is generally well-structured, with clear definitions and a logical progression of ideas. The introduction of the Thompson metric and its role in the proof is particularly insightful. However, some technical details are unclear, particularly regarding the construction of the matrix A in Theorem 1 and the treatment of the gradient with respect to u. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of why certain constraints are necessary for the theoretical guarantees.

**Originality:** The work is original in providing a practically feasible method for global optimization of neural networks, which is a significant contribution to the field. While it builds on recent work by Janzamin et al. (2015) and Haeffele & Vidal (2015), the authors make a clear distinction by providing a method that is both theoretically grounded and practically implementable. However, the restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights and data) limit the scope of the contribution compared to more general neural network architectures.

**Significance:** The theoretical contribution is significant for understanding the global optimization landscape of neural networks. The experiments on UCI datasets show competitive performance compared to SVM and ReLU networks, though the comparison is somewhat limited as the authors compare to SVM (which has a global optimizer) rather than standard neural network training methods. The practical significance is constrained by the nonnegativity constraints, which make the model less applicable to standard neural network settings. The authors acknowledge this limitation, noting that their experiments are "a proof of concept" and that they have not yet developed a good understanding of how to select model parameters for optimal performance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- First practically feasible method for global optimization of neural networks with convergence guarantees
- Theoretical contribution bridges neural network training and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory
- Linear convergence rate is a significant advantage over stochastic gradient descent
- Experimental results demonstrate non-trivial classification performance on standard datasets

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Nonnegativity constraints on weights and data are highly restrictive, limiting practical applicability
- Theoretical conditions for convergence (ρ(A) < 1) are conservative and may not be easily satisfied for larger networks
- The approach does not generalize to standard neural network architectures with ReLU activations
- The comparison to standard neural networks is limited (only to ReLU networks, not to standard training procedures)
- The small ε term and the 1-norm maximization in the objective lack clear practical justification

**Overall:** This paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to the global optimization of neural networks, providing a method with provable global optimality guarantees. While the restrictive assumptions limit the practical significance, the work is valuable for advancing theoretical understanding of neural network training. The paper would benefit from addressing the technical gaps in the proofs, providing more intuition for the theoretical choices, and comparing to standard neural network training procedures beyond ReLU networks. The experimental results are promising as a proof of concept but do not yet demonstrate clear practical advantages over standard methods.

I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the work represents an important theoretical step, though the practical impact may be limited by the restrictive assumptions. The authors should address the technical gaps and provide more context for the theoretical choices in the final version.
