This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a restricted class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The networks use nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents, and optimize a modified objective (loss plus a positive “sum-of-outputs” term). The main theorem gives a simple, architecture-dependent sufficient condition for global optimality and linear convergence: the spectral radius of a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix (size K+1 or K+2) must be < 1. The method is derived via multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and a Banach contraction argument in the Thompson metric. The authors relate their work to guaranteed training via tensors (Janzamin et al.) and structured factorizations (Haeffele & Vidal), claiming improved practical applicability; they also connect to polynomial networks (Livni et al.). Empirically, on several small UCI datasets, performance is generally below RBF-SVM and comparable to or slightly above simple ReLU baselines; convergence is fast.

Quality: Theoretical development is careful and technically solid, with a clear fixed-point construction and verifiable sufficient conditions. The spectral-radius condition is computable and the extension to two layers is nontrivial. However, guarantees hinge on strong modeling changes (nonnegativity constraints, specialized activations, and objective modification), and the empirical section is limited in scope and does not demonstrate state-of-the-art accuracy. The claim that linear convergence “typically” means <10 iterations is over-stated without systematic evidence.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized, though notation is heavy. The role and necessity of the added output-sum and ε-terms could be explained more intuitively. Practical guidance for choosing α, norms, and radii is limited.

Originality: Conceptually novel and interesting: using nonlinear Perron–Frobenius and multi-homogeneous eigenmaps to obtain global optima for NN training with an easily checked condition is new to NIPS.

Significance: Theoretical significance is high, but practical impact is constrained by nonnegativity of data and weights, non-standard activations, and underwhelming accuracy vs. SVM. The approach may inspire follow-up work on monotone or sign-split architectures.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, rigorous theory with a simple verifiable condition.
- New conceptual bridge between spectral methods and NN training.
- Extension to two layers and linear-rate convergence proof.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong modeling restrictions (nonnegativity; specialized activations; modified objective) limit applicability and expressivity.
- Empirical performance lags SVM and is confined to small datasets.
- Practical guidance and ablations are limited; some claims (iteration counts) are overstated.

Suggestions: Broaden experiments (e.g., MNIST), compare to monotone/nonnegative NNs and polynomial networks, provide ablations on constraints and α choices, calibrate iteration-count claims, and discuss pathways to relaxing nonnegativity (e.g., sign-splitting).
