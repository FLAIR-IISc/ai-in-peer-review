- Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method that guarantees global optimality and linear convergence for generalized polynomial neural networks.
- Achieves stronger theoretical guarantees than SGD-based methods, with a checkable spectral radius precondition for global optimality.
- Method is demonstrated mainly on one- and two-hidden-layer networks for simplicity, though applicable in principle to deeper nets.
- Builds on recent optimization-landscape work and claims greater practical feasibility than prior global-optimality approaches (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.).
- Requires non-negative inputs and weights, a substantial constraint that may limit representational capacity and real-world applicability.
- Enforced differing α values per hidden unit (due to invariance) feels unnatural and may reduce adaptability.
- Dependence on parameters p_w, p_u and ρ_w, ρ_u, plus the spectral-radius constraint, can effectively shrink weights and risk collapsing network behavior.
- Scalability concerns: unclear how spectral radius ρ(A) behaves as network depth, width, and input dimensionality grow; may hinder use in larger architectures.
- Theoretical presentation is clear and mathematically rigorous, and authors acknowledge limitations and future work directions.
- Proposed spectral-radius condition is easily checkable (small matrix) — a practical advantage over methods requiring density estimation.
- Bounds (e.g., Eq. 9) may be conservative; tightening them could broaden applicability.
- Experiments validate the method on several real-world datasets but are limited to small, low-dimensional settings.
- Empirical evaluation would be stronger with comparisons to state-of-the-art methods on larger, more complex datasets and/or relaxed non-negativity constraints.
- Overall assessment: valuable theoretical contribution with promising practical potential despite restrictive assumptions; reviewer recommends acceptance.
