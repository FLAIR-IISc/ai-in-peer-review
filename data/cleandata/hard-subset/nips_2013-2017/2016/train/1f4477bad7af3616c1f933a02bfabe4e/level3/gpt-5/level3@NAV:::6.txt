Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method that trains a restricted class of feed-forward neural networks—generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative data and nonnegative weights—by maximizing a modified objective that combines cross-entropy with a sum-of-outputs term and small positive offsets. The core contribution is a fixed-point map on the positive orthant with provable global optimality and linear convergence when the spectral radius of a small, architecture-dependent nonnegative matrix A is below 1. The analysis leverages multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction in the Thompson metric; an extension to two hidden layers is provided. Experiments on several UCI datasets suggest competitive performance with simple ReLU baselines and slightly worse than RBF SVMs. The work is related to polynomial networks (e.g., Livni et al.), globally optimal tensor-based training (Janzamin et al.), and structured matrix factorization/convex formulations (Haeffele & Vidal), but claims improved practicality under nonnegativity assumptions.

Strengths (Quality/Originality): 
- Solid and careful theory with a clear convergence rate tied to ρ(A); the fixed-point construction and use of multi-homogeneous PF results are novel in this NN context.
- Clear conditions that can be checked a priori, and an extension beyond one hidden layer.
- Addresses a central question of globally optimal training for nontrivial architectures.

Weaknesses (Significance/Clarity/Empirics):
- Practical utility of the restricted class is not convincingly established. The expressiveness of nonnegative generalized polynomial networks with distinct α should be explicitly characterized (what functions are realizable; what is impossible compared to ReLU/SGD-trained MLPs; any universal approximation under nonnegativity and normalization?).
- Related/concurrent work is insufficiently contrasted (e.g., polynomial networks; nonnegative/monotone networks; NMF-based predictors; convex neural networks). Please position your guarantees vis-à-vis Janzamin et al. and Haeffele & Vidal beyond brief remarks.
- Experiments are limited: only RBF SVM and ReLU baselines. Missing comparisons to NN training under nonnegativity (projected SGD, multiplicative updates), kernel ridge/logistic regression, gradient boosting/trees, and modern NN optimizers. Datasets are small; include larger nonnegative domains (MNIST, text BOW) and ablations on α choices and ρ(A) feasibility/failure cases.
- Report missing computational details: hardware, runtime, iteration counts, memory, per-epoch complexity for NLSM1/2 and SGDs. Clarify that “converges quickly” is empirical.
- Technical clarifications: motivate the objective Φ (and the ε term) in the paper with intuition before Theorem 1; add an informal explanation of Theorem 1; discuss sensitivity to α and preprocessing for nonnegative inputs.
- Presentation/typos: Section 2 lacks fluency; fix “polyomial” (p60), “one CAN model” (p67), undefined p1/p2 and R_{++}, and inconsistent definitions of ρx.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical contribution with checkable conditions and linear-rate guarantees.
- Conceptually interesting bridge between spectral methods and NN training.

Cons for acceptance:
- Expressiveness and practical impact not established.
- Limited and incomplete empirical evaluation and related-work coverage.
- Presentation and notation issues.

Overall: Promising theory; needs stronger positioning, clarity, and broader experiments to demonstrate practical significance. I lean toward rejection this round, with encouragement to revise.
