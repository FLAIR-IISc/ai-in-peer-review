This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – those with nonnegative inputs and weights, and generalized polynomial activations – with a guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors address a critical challenge in deep learning: the non-convexity of the optimization landscape, and, remarkably, claim to provide a *practical* algorithm achieving global optimality, a feat rarely demonstrated in the literature. This work builds upon recent advances in globally optimal neural network training [8, 11], but circumvents the practical limitations of those methods by proposing a condition – checking the spectral radius of a relatively small matrix – that determines global optimality and convergence. The extension to two hidden layers is also presented. The experimental results on UCI datasets, while limited in scope, demonstrate competitive performance.

The core strength of this work lies in its theoretical guarantees. The authors convincingly demonstrate, under reasonable assumptions, that their nonlinear spectral method provably converges to a global optimum with a linear rate. This is a significant contribution, as it offers a rare instance of a practically feasible algorithm with such strong theoretical foundations in the notoriously complex field of neural network training. The simplicity of the check for global optimality – merely computing a spectral radius – is also noteworthy and dramatically lowers the barrier to utilizing this method. The novelty of the approach, combining Perron-Frobenius theory with neural network optimization, is compelling. The inclusion of a two-layer extension, even briefly, shows the potential for scaling this work.  The authors also correctly position their work within the broader context of non-convex optimization and the challenges of training deep networks.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The requirement of nonnegative inputs and weights is a significant constraint. While the authors acknowledge this, its impact on the expressiveness of the network is a key concern. The restriction to generalized polynomial activations, while mathematically convenient, also limits the applicability of the method. The paper’s requirement for distinct α values for each hidden unit feels somewhat artificial, stemming from invariance properties, and could be explored further to understand if it’s a fundamental limitation or a design artifact.  A crucial limitation is the scaling of the algorithm; the authors themselves acknowledge this, noting that satisfying the spectral radius constraint may become increasingly difficult as the input and parameter dimensionality increases, potentially requiring aggressive shrinkage of weights and hindering the ability to train deep networks. The discussion of theoretical bounds in section 3 and 4 seems quite abstract, and could benefit from more intuition.

A key area for future investigation, and one the authors hint at, is a deeper understanding of the relationship between the model parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu, pw, pu) and the spectral radius ρ(A). An empirical investigation, perhaps through plotting the spectral radius as a function of these parameters, would be valuable.  It would allow researchers to better understand the trade-offs involved in choosing these parameters and to assess the scalability of the method to higher-dimensional datasets and deeper architectures.  Exploring the impact of different activation functions beyond generalized polynomials while maintaining the guarantee of global optimality would be another important step.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The proof of global optimality and linear convergence is rigorous and a significant contribution.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed nonlinear spectral method offers a potentially feasible path to training networks with provable guarantees.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of spectral methods and neural network optimization is innovative.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and presents a complex topic in a relatively accessible manner.
*   **First Results on Real Datasets:** Demonstrates the method is not merely theoretical.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Assumptions:** The nonnegativity constraints significantly limit the applicability of the method.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The spectral radius constraint may become restrictive as dimensionality increases.
*   **Limited Expressiveness:** The choice of generalized polynomial activations may hinder performance on complex datasets.
*   **Artificial Constraint:** The requirement for distinct α values feels somewhat contrived.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations outlined above, I believe this paper represents a significant and encouraging contribution to the field. The theoretical guarantees, coupled with the potentially practical algorithm, make this work stand out. The authors have addressed a challenging problem and offered a novel solution with clear strengths. While the scalability concerns and the restrictive assumptions warrant further investigation, the paper's potential impact justifies acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016, with the caveat that future work should focus on addressing the limitations related to scalability and expressiveness.
