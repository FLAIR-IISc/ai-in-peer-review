This paper proposes a novel method for globally optimizing a restricted class of feed-forward neural networks using a nonlinear spectral method, and provides a convergence guarantee. The problem addressed – achieving global optimality in neural network training – is undoubtedly interesting and a significant challenge. The theoretical contribution, a provably convergent algorithm, is commendable and a welcome addition to the field. However, despite this strong theoretical foundation, the practical implications and overall impact of the work remain unclear.

The core limitation lies in the restrictive nature of the network class considered. While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough discussion of the expressiveness limits is crucial. What kinds of functions *can* these networks represent, and, more importantly, what functions are *beyond* their reach? Providing concrete examples or a theoretical characterization of their representational capacity would significantly strengthen the paper. The assertion that these models are "rich enough" for good performance on real-world datasets lacks compelling justification without a detailed comparison to more standard neural network architectures.

Furthermore, the paper insufficiently addresses related work. While acknowledging some contributions on global optimization in neural networks (e.g., [8, 11]), it fails to adequately contrast the proposed method with other relevant approaches, including more recent advances in non-convex optimization and alternative algorithmic paradigms like those based on semidefinite programming or alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM). A comprehensive discussion of these alternatives, highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method, is essential.

The experimental evaluation is limited. Comparisons are made only against SVMs, omitting any benchmark against other neural network training methods (e.g., standard gradient descent with various optimizers) and alternative optimization algorithms. The limited scope of the datasets – primarily low-dimensional UCI datasets – further restricts the generalizability of the findings. Crucially, the paper lacks information on the computational resources (e.g., runtime, memory usage) required for each algorithm, making it difficult to assess the practical viability of the proposed method. How does the computational cost of this globally optimal method scale with network size and dataset dimensionality?

The presentation also requires improvement. While the introduction is well-written, subsequent sections, particularly Section 2, suffer from a lack of fluency. Several typos and notation inconsistencies detract from the overall clarity – examples include "polyomial" (p60), “one CAN model” (p67), and the undefined variables p1 and p2 and R_{++}. Technical clarifications are also necessary. The motivation for the function Φ and the ε term needs to be more clearly articulated, extending beyond the rebuttal. The claim of "quick convergence" appears to be based solely on empirical observation and should be stated as such. An informal explanation preceding Theorem 1 would also improve accessibility.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides a provably convergent algorithm for training a specific class of neural networks, which is a significant achievement.
*   **Interesting problem:** Addressing global optimality in neural network training is a crucial and challenging problem.
*   **Novelty:** The approach using nonlinear spectral methods appears novel within the context of neural network optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited expressiveness:** The restricted network class raises concerns about practical applicability.
*   **Insufficient comparison to related work:** The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion of alternative approaches.
*   **Limited experimental evaluation:** The experiments are too narrow in scope and lack crucial details.
*   **Presentation issues:** Typos, notation inconsistencies, and clarity issues detract from the overall quality of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically sound contribution, but its practical significance is questionable due to the restrictive nature of the model and the limited experimental evaluation. While the theoretical guarantee of global optimality is valuable, the lack of a compelling argument for the utility of this restricted network class weakens the paper considerably. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should address the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the expressiveness of their model, the comparison to related work, and the scope of the experimental evaluation, before resubmission. A more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and scaling behavior would also be highly beneficial.
