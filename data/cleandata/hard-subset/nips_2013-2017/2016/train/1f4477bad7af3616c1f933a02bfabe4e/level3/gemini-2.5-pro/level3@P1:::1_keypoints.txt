- Proposes a novel method for training Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with a global convergence guarantee via a "nonlinear spectral method."
- Reformulates the problem by restricting to non-negative data/weights, using real-valued power activations, and optimizing a modified objective.
- Shows the iterative algorithm is a contraction under the condition ρ(A) < 1, invoking nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and Banach fixed-point to guarantee a unique global optimum and linear convergence.
- Theoretical development is rigorous, mapping critical points to fixed points in B++ with the Thompson metric.
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a demonstrated extension to two hidden layers.
- Requires strong constraints (non-negative inputs/weights and specific activations), limiting expressiveness and applicability.
- Convergence condition ρ(A) < 1 becomes harder to satisfy as hidden units, weight norms, or data scale increase, hurting scalability.
- Claim of "no free parameter" is misleading because many hyperparameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu, pw, pu) must be chosen and verified.
- Hyperparameter search used (100 random combos + 5-fold CV) is more cumbersome and less intuitive than tuning SGD learning rates.
- Empirical results are unimpressive: competitive with simple ReLU nets but outperformed by RBF SVM on 5/7 UCI datasets.
- The modified objective adds sums of outputs/weights to ensure positive gradients, but its impact on learned models is not well explored.
- Derived bounds for matrix A are conservative; tighter bounds could substantially improve practical applicability.
- Distinct αi per hidden unit is necessary to avoid trivial rank-one solutions, highlighting subtle model design requirements.
- Overall: a strong theoretical contribution with limited practical impact due to restrictive assumptions, a stringent convergence condition, and weak empirical performance.
