- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for feedforward nets with global optimality guarantees under specific assumptions.  
- Claims linear convergence to global optimum for 1- and 2-hidden-layer networks using generalized polynomial activations, non-negative weights, and a modified objective.  
- Novel theoretical contribution: convergence guarantees more practically feasible than prior tensor-based approaches, with checkable global optimality via the spectral radius of a small matrix.  
- Strength: technically sound theoretical framework with detailed proofs.  
- Strength: clear algorithm description and experimental demonstration of linear convergence.  
- Major weakness: assumptions are highly restrictive—non-negative weights are counterintuitive for most applications.  
- Major weakness: generalized polynomial activation is uncommon in practice, limiting applicability.  
- Experimental validation is insufficient—UCI datasets are too small to assess scalability.  
- Baseline comparisons are weak/unfair (untuned linear SVM) and omit important baselines (e.g., nonlinear/RBF SVM).  
- Expressive power claim is unconvincing—NLSM2 only slightly outperforms ReLU on some datasets with no statistically significant gap.  
- Spectral radius bounds grow with hidden-unit count, creating serious scalability concerns for high-dimensional models.  
- Paper does not show how to practically find valid parameters without extensive search.  
- Missing experiment: need to test whether SGD can reach global optima on the same datasets to isolate benefits of objective vs. architecture.  
- Overall assessment: interesting theoretical niche advance but limited practical significance for the broader ML community.
