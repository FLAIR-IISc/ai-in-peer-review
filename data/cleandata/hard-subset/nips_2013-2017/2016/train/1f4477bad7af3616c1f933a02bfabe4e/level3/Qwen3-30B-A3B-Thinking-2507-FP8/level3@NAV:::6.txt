

This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a restricted class of feed-forward neural networks with one or two hidden layers, achieving global optimality with linear convergence rates under certain conditions. The authors prove that their method, which requires nonnegative weights and a modified objective function, guarantees global convergence by verifying the spectral radius of a small matrix. This contrasts with prior work by Janzamin et al. [11] (which requires difficult tensor computations) and Haeffele and Vidal [8] (which is "challenging to apply in practice").

The theoretical contribution is strong: the paper provides the first practically feasible globally optimal training method for neural networks with convergence guarantees. The conditions for global optimality are easy to verify, and the algorithm eliminates the need for learning rate tuning. The introduction and motivation are well-written, and the technical content is rigorous.

However, several significant weaknesses undermine the paper's impact. The most critical issue is the lack of convincing evidence that the restricted network class is practically useful. The paper fails to explicitly discuss the expressiveness limits of this class (what problems are out of reach) and provides no clear comparison with standard neural network architectures. The experiments are severely limited: comparisons are only against some SVMs, omitting standard neural network training methods like SGD with ReLU networks. The experimental section lacks computational resource information for all algorithms, making it impossible to assess efficiency. The presentation also needs polishing - Section 2 lacks fluency, and there are multiple typos (e.g., "polyomial" on p60) and undefined notation (e.g., R_{++}).

The paper would benefit from clarifying why the modified objective (with Φ and ε) is necessary, providing an informal explanation before Theorem 1, and explicitly stating that "converges quickly" is based on empirical observation. The related work section is insufficiently contrasted with concurrent approaches.

While the theoretical advance is valuable, the limited experimental validation and lack of practical relevance significantly weaken the paper's contribution. The authors should address the expressiveness question, expand the experimental comparison to standard neural network methods, and provide computational resource details before the paper can be recommended for acceptance.
