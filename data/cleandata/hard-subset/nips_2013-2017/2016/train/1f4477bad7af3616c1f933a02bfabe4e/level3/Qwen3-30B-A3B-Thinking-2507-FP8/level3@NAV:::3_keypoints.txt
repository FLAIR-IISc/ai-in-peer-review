- Proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for training certain neural nets with provable global optimality and linear convergence.  
- Applies to networks with nonnegative inputs, nonnegative linear weights, and generalized polynomial (real-power) activations.  
- Proves linear convergence to the global optimum for one- and two-hidden-layer networks under the spectral radius condition ρ(A) < 1.  
- Provides the first practically feasible algorithm with explicit convergence guarantees, contrasting prior impractical or distribution-dependent work.  
- The convergence condition is simple to check (spectral radius of a small matrix), making the theory practically appealing.  
- Offers careful, well-structured proofs supporting global optimality and linear convergence.  
- Empirical results on 2D synthetic data and small UCI datasets show competitive performance vs ReLU networks and SVMs while maintaining global guarantees.  
- Major limitation: nonnegativity constraints on inputs and weights significantly restrict model expressiveness despite input-shifting arguments.  
- Unnatural requirement for different activation functions per hidden layer may hinder practical adoption.  
- The spectral radius constraint forces shrinking of linear weights (reduces pw/pu), potentially collapsing deep nets toward near-linear behavior and raising scalability concerns with higher dimensionality.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations but should provide more empirical analysis of how parameters affect ρ(A).  
- Overall original and significant for optimization theory; high-quality, technically sound contribution recommended for acceptance.
