- Summary: Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that finds global optima with linear convergence for 1–2 hidden-layer NNs under nonnegativity, p-norm constraints, and a modified loss, using a Perron–Frobenius-style fixed-point map GΦ.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on global-guarantee neural-net literature but uses nonnegativity and multihomogeneity plus a new objective to obtain checkable spectral-radius guarantees.

- Key theoretical result: If the spectral radius ρ(A)<1 for a small parameter-dependent matrix A, there is a unique positive global maximizer and GΦ converges linearly to it.

- Algorithmic novelty: Constructs GΦ from normalized componentwise gradient powers and applies Thompson-metric contraction arguments via nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory.

- Extension: Framework and guarantees are extended nontrivially to networks with two hidden layers.

- Strength — rigor: Clear, rigorous theory identifying fixed-point structure, uniqueness, and linear convergence with explicit Lipschitz/spectral relations.

- Strength — novelty: Novel application of multihomogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory to neural-network training and an interesting metric-based contraction proof.

- Strength — checkable guarantees: Conditions are verifiable a priori from model parameters and simple data norms, with explicit ways to choose pw, pu, pv to enforce ρ(A)<1.

- Strength — simplicity: Parameter-free iterative method (no learning rates) with rapid convergence demonstrated in proof-of-concept experiments.

- Weakness — restrictive assumptions: Requires nonnegative inputs/weights, generalized polynomial activations with distinct ≥1 exponents, and a modified loss including the sum of outputs.

- Weakness — expressivity concerns: Limited theoretical/empirical evidence that the constrained class is sufficiently expressive; distinct-α and nonnegativity limits flexibility.

- Weakness — limited experiments: Evaluations are on small, low-dimensional UCI datasets with no scalability or high-dimensional stress tests; ρ(A) worsens with width.

- Weakness — guarantee vs performance: Unclear how to choose pw, pu to simultaneously satisfy ρ(A)<1 and achieve high accuracy; paper relies on random search and conservative bounds.

- Weakness — baselines fairness: Baselines optimize different objectives (e.g., standard ReLU loss); need aligned baselines (projected SGD on the augmented objective), wall-clock times, and ablations (effect of added output-sum and ε).

- Weakness — preprocessing impact: Nonnegativity preprocessing (shifting/scaling) may affect ρx and performance; paper lacks guidance.

- Quality: Technically sound with coherent proofs, but heavy notation and split proofs; experimental section too narrow to assess practical impact.

- Clarity: Well written with clear theorems and algorithms, but needs more intuition for the added objective, computational-complexity per iteration, and guidance on hyperparameter selection.

- Originality: High — contraction-based nonlinear spectral approach and multilayer extension appear novel.

- Significance: Moderate — advances theory and may inspire further work, but strong modeling restrictions and limited empirical validation limit immediate practical relevance.

- Questions for authors: Sensitivity to output-sum and ε; evidence of expressivity for constrained class; effects of nonnegativity preprocessing and guidance on ρx; per-iteration complexity and wall-clock comparisons; baseline with projected SGD on same objective; tighter data-dependent bounds for A.

- Pros for acceptance: Innovative theoretical contribution, clear conditions for unique global optimum and linear convergence, and a first implementable algorithm with such guarantees under the stated constraints.

- Cons for acceptance: Strong modeling restrictions, limited/small-scale empirical validation, insufficient hyperparameter-selection guidance to meet ρ(A)<1 while preserving accuracy, and missing ablations/aligned baselines.

- Overall recommendation: Interesting and rigorous theory but practical impact limited by restrictive assumptions and sparse experiments; reviewer leans toward rejection while encouraging a revised submission addressing empirical scope and ablations.
