Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method that trains a specific class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with a provable linear convergence rate. The networks use generalized polynomial activations: each hidden unit computes a positive linear combination of nonnegative inputs, raised to a unit-specific real power αl ≥ 1, and outputs are nonnegative linear combinations of these hidden activations. Weights are constrained to be nonnegative and norm-normalized. The training objective is a modified cross-entropy: negative loss plus a sum of outputs, with a small positive bias term to ensure strict positivity of gradients on the positive orthant.

The algorithm is a fixed-point iteration GΦ built from normalized positive gradients (via ψp′) on the cone, with the analysis grounded in nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory on cones, the Thompson metric, and the Banach fixed-point theorem. A sufficient condition for global optimality and linear convergence is that the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small, data-independent nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 for one hidden layer; K+2 for two hidden layers) is less than 1. This condition depends on network architecture (numbers of units, α, norm exponents) and input scale, and can be checked before training. The method extends to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees.

The work connects to (i) globally optimal training via tensor methods (e.g., Janzamin et al.) and structured matrix factorization/positively homogeneous models (Haeffele & Vidal), which have strong assumptions or are challenging to use in practice; (ii) polynomial networks (Livni et al.); and (iii) nonlinear PF theory for multi-homogeneous maps. Conceptually, it introduces a novel way to certify and realize global optima for a nontrivial NN class via contraction mappings.

Quality
- Theory: Technically solid and carefully developed. The fixed-point framework, Lipschitz analysis under the Thompson metric, and the spectral-radius condition are well justified. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement but the main line is complete.
- Correctness and scope: The global optimality proof requires nonnegativity of data and weights, distinct α per hidden unit, norm constraints, and a modified objective with an output-sum term. The authors are clear about these assumptions and explain the need for positivity to invoke cone-based PF theory.
- Experiments: Proof-of-concept on small UCI datasets. The proposed method (NLSM1/2) is generally worse than RBF-SVM and roughly competitive with simple ReLU baselines. Convergence speed is demonstrated, but the empirical section is limited in scale and breadth. No ablations to isolate the impact of nonnegativity, the output-sum term, or the α choices.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written given the technical content. The motivation for the unusual architectural and objective choices is explained. That said, usability would improve with:
- More intuition up front for the added “sum of outputs” term and its effect on calibration and decision boundaries.
- Concrete guidance for selecting α, pw, pu (and pv for two layers) beyond conservative bounds, and discussion of trade-offs between expressivity and ensuring ρ(A) < 1.
- A brief complexity analysis per iteration and wall-clock comparisons versus SGD/SVM.
- Clarification on biases (or adding a constant input feature) under nonnegativity constraints.

Originality
High on the conceptual side: using nonlinear spectral methods to get a checkable precondition for global optimization of a neural network class is novel relative to prior global training results (tensor decomposition, convex surrogates, or homogeneous factorization frameworks). The synthesis of PF theory for multi-homogeneous maps with NN training is original.

Significance
Mixed. The theoretical contribution is elegant and likely to inspire follow-up work on optimization on cones and globally optimal training under structural constraints. However, the practical impact is limited by:
- Nonnegativity constraints on weights (and data), rendering the networks monotone in their inputs.
- Nonstandard, restricted activation family (distinct real powers ≥1).
- Modified objective function that deviates from standard cross-entropy.
Combined, these conditions curtail applicability and, in the reported experiments, the method underperforms SVMs. As a result, the near-term practical significance is modest, though the conceptual advance is noteworthy.

Strengths
- Novel, rigorous framework yielding global optimality with a linear convergence rate.
- Pre-checkable condition via the spectral radius of a small matrix; no tuning of learning rates.
- Extension to two hidden layers with explicit rates.
- Clear connection to nonlinear PF theory and contraction mappings.

Weaknesses
- Strong modeling restrictions: nonnegative weights and inputs; distinct α ≥ 1; norm constraints; modified objective.
- Empirical performance generally worse than RBF-SVM; limited scale and datasets.
- Sparse guidance on choosing α and norm exponents to balance ρ(A) < 1 and model capacity.
- The added output-sum term may degrade calibration and comparability to standard classifiers.

Minor comments and suggestions
- The statement that linear convergence “typically” yields convergence in fewer than 10 iterations is overstated; the rate depends on ρ(A) and R, which can vary substantially with architecture and scaling.
- Provide ablations: (i) remove the output-sum term; (ii) compare to the same architecture trained with projected/penalized SGD under nonnegativity; (iii) test with and without distinct α’s to empirically validate the necessity argument.
- Include logistic regression and monotone NN baselines, or nonnegative matrix factorization-based classifiers.
- Discuss adding a constant input to emulate biases under nonnegativity.
- Offer practical recipes for selecting α and p exponents and for normalizing inputs to keep ρ(A) comfortably below 1 without overly shrinking capacity.
- Explore larger, inherently nonnegative datasets (e.g., MNIST, bag-of-words) to better showcase scalability.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Conceptually innovative approach with clean, verifiable optimality conditions.
- Solid theoretical development; bridges an interesting mathematical toolbox with NN training.

Cons:
- Heavy restrictions on architecture and objective hurt practicality and accuracy.
- Empirical results do not match strong baselines; limited experimental scope.
- Usability guidance is minimal; claims about iteration counts are optimistic.

Recommendation
Lean reject. This is a compelling theoretical contribution with novel ideas, but the current practical limitations and weaker empirical performance make it a better fit for a theory-focused venue or a future submission with stronger experimental validation and relaxed modeling constraints.

Questions for the authors
- Can biases be incorporated (e.g., via constant inputs) without breaking the theory?
- How sensitive is performance to the added output-sum term? Any calibration effects?
- Are there relaxations of nonnegativity (e.g., sign-symmetric cones or offset decompositions) that preserve a contraction?
- Can tighter bounds on A (beyond the conservative ones) materially enlarge the feasible set with ρ(A) < 1?
