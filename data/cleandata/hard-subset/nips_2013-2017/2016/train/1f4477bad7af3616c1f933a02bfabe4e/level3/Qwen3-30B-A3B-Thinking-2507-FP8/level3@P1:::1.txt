

This paper proposes a globally optimal training method for a specific class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method that guarantees global convergence to the unique optimum under certain conditions, which is a significant theoretical contribution compared to standard SGD-based approaches. The work builds on recent advances in globally optimal neural network training (e.g., Janzamin et al. [11], Haeffele and Vidal [8]), but distinguishes itself by providing a practically feasible algorithm with linear convergence rate rather than just theoretical guarantees. The key innovation lies in transforming the non-convex optimization problem into a fixed-point problem using nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory, where the global optimum corresponds to a unique fixed point of a contractive mapping.

The paper's main strengths are its theoretical novelty and rigorous mathematical foundation. The authors successfully prove that their method achieves global optimality with linear convergence under weak assumptions (nonnegative data), which is the first practically feasible globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks. The proof technique using fixed-point theory on the positive orthant is elegant and well-executed, with careful attention to the technical details of the convergence conditions. The authors also provide a thorough exploration of the conditions under which convergence is guaranteed, including explicit bounds for the spectral radius of a matrix that determines the convergence rate.

The method's theoretical contributions are compelling, particularly the insight that imposing nonnegativity constraints on weights (while adding a carefully designed regularization term) allows for global optimality guarantees. This is an interesting departure from standard neural network training approaches, demonstrating how constrained architectures can admit unique stationary points. The authors effectively connect their work to Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems [7], which provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that impact its practical significance and clarity. The most critical limitation is the scalability issue: the convergence guarantee depends on the spectral radius of a (K+1)×(K+1) matrix, which increases with the number of hidden units. The authors acknowledge this, noting that their bounds are conservative and that the spectral radius grows with network size, limiting the method to shallow networks (one or two hidden layers) on low-dimensional datasets. The paper's experimental section confirms this limitation, as the authors restrict their experiments to low-dimensional UCI datasets with small networks.

Another major weakness is the practical implementation burden. The method requires careful tuning of numerous hyperparameters (n1, α, pw, pu, ρw, ρu) to satisfy the spectral radius condition ρ(A) < 1, which must be verified for each parameter combination. The paper states that they tested "approximately 150,000 parameter combinations" selected by cross-validation, which suggests the method is not practically feasible for most real-world applications. The requirement to check the spectral radius condition for every model configuration adds significant verification overhead that would be prohibitive in most practical settings.

The empirical results are underwhelming for a paper claiming to provide a globally optimal training method. The authors report that their method (NLSM1 and NLSM2) outperformed linear SVM on only 2 of 7 UCI datasets, with overall performance comparable to or slightly worse than ReLU-based networks. While the authors acknowledge these results are "proof of concept," the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that the theoretical advantages translate to practical benefits. The method's inability to match the performance of unconstrained ReLU networks (which can achieve perfect accuracy on Iris and Banknote) while imposing strict constraints on the architecture raises questions about its practical utility.

The paper also suffers from some clarity issues. The motivation for the specific form of the regularization term (sum of all outputs) is not sufficiently explained, and the connection between the theoretical guarantees and practical implementation is not well bridged. The experimental setup is somewhat ad-hoc, with the authors selecting parameters from a large space without clear justification for the chosen ranges.

In terms of significance, while the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical impact is limited by the scalability issues and implementation burden. The method is theoretically interesting but appears to be a specialized solution for a narrow class of problems rather than a broadly applicable technique. The authors' claim that this is "the first practically feasible method which achieves such a guarantee" is questionable given the extensive parameter tuning required and the limited scope of the experiments.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of global optimality in neural network training, but its practical significance is limited by scalability constraints and implementation complexity. The method is elegant from a theoretical perspective but would require substantial improvements to be considered a practical alternative to standard SGD-based training. The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate advantages over existing methods, particularly given the large number of hyperparameters that must be tuned to satisfy the convergence conditions.

Strengths: Theoretical novelty, rigorous proof, elegant fixed-point approach, first practically feasible globally optimal training method.

Weaknesses: Limited scalability, excessive hyperparameter tuning, weak empirical results, unclear practical utility, implementation complexity.

I recommend rejection due to the limited practical significance and the fact that the method's requirements make it impractical for most real-world applications, despite its theoretical merits. The paper would benefit from either stronger empirical evidence of practical advantages or a more generalizable approach that does not require such extensive parameter tuning.
