## Review of “Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods”

This paper presents a compelling approach to achieving global optimality in training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – those with non-negative inputs, non-negative linear weights, and generalized polynomial activations. The authors demonstrate that, under these constraints, a global optimum can be reached with linear convergence using their proposed nonlinear spectral method. This is a significant contribution, as it directly addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: the non-convexity of the optimization landscape. Building on recent work attempting global optimization (e.g., [8, 11]), this paper offers a potentially *practical* method, a crucial distinction highlighted by the authors and a key strength of the work. 

The core novelty lies in the combination of a restricted network architecture with a spectral method, yielding explicit optimality guarantees under checkable preconditions – namely, the spectral radius of a small, easily computed matrix. This contrasts favorably with existing methods requiring complex tensor decompositions [11] or being difficult to apply in practice [8]. The extension from one to two hidden layers is also a positive step, though limited by the aforementioned architectural constraints. The experiments, while performed on relatively small datasets, do demonstrate competitive performance and validate the approach.

However, the imposed constraints are significant limitations. The requirement of non-negative inputs and weights restricts model expressiveness, and the use of different activation functions per layer feels unnatural and potentially hinders broader applicability. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge, and the reviewer shares the concern, that satisfying the spectral radius constraint might necessitate shrinking linear weights as dimensionality grows, potentially leading to near-linear behavior and hindering scalability. The empirical investigation suggested by the reviewer (plotting ρ(A) as a function of p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, and network size) is crucial to understanding this scalability issue and should be a priority for future work. The choice of activation function might also require some tuning and the analysis of the impact on the convergence rate could be included. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and potentially practical approach to globally optimal neural network training.
*   Clear theoretical guarantees and checkable preconditions.
*   Addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning.
*   Provides a starting point for exploring provably optimal neural network architectures.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Significant limitations due to architectural constraints (non-negativity, distinct activations).
*   Potential scalability issues as dimensionality grows.
*   Experiments limited to small datasets.



**Overall:** Despite the limitations, the work represents a valuable and encouraging step towards understanding and achieving global optimality in neural network training. The presented method, while constrained, offers a unique and theoretically sound approach. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the scalability concerns and elaborating on the impact of the architectural constraints in the camera-ready version.
