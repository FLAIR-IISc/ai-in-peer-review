This paper presents a novel method for the globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors demonstrate, under relatively weak assumptions, that their approach achieves linear convergence to a global optimum, a significant theoretical advance in a field often plagued by non-convex optimization challenges. While the work focuses on one and two hidden layer networks for simplicity, the potential for extending it to deeper architectures is noted. The paper is well-motivated, acknowledging the difficulties in training deep networks with standard stochastic gradient descent methods and situating their contribution within the existing literature on provably optimal neural network training.

The strength of this work lies primarily in its theoretical contribution. Achieving global optimality with a provable convergence rate is a substantial result, particularly given the empirical nature of much of deep learning research. The authors carefully develop the necessary mathematical framework and provide a clear (though dense) presentation of their method, culminating in Theorem 1, which establishes the convergence guarantee.  The extension to two hidden layers (Theorem 5) further demonstrates the generality of their approach. The inclusion of experimental results, although limited, is a positive step toward validating the theoretical findings.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall impact of the paper. The most significant concern is the limited discussion of the expressiveness of the constrained network class. While the authors acknowledge the non-standard nature of the model, they do not thoroughly explore the trade-offs between global optimality and representational power. What kinds of functions *can* and *cannot* be effectively approximated with these networks?  A more detailed discussion of this point would significantly strengthen the paper.  Furthermore, the related work section could be more comprehensive.  While key papers like [8, 11] are cited, the authors should more explicitly contrast their approach with other recent work on provably optimal training, such as those based on tensor methods or structured matrix factorization.

The experimental evaluation is also limited. The comparisons are primarily against SVMs and ReLU networks, neglecting other established neural network training techniques and alternative algorithmic paradigms.  The results, while encouraging, do not provide a strong justification for adopting this method over more standard approaches. Crucially, the paper lacks information regarding computational resources – training time, memory usage – for each algorithm, making it difficult to assess the practical viability of the proposed method. The observation that the algorithm “converges quickly” appears to be based primarily on anecdotal evidence and requires more rigorous substantiation.

Clarity is another area for improvement. While the introduction is well-written, subsequent sections, particularly Section 2, suffer from a lack of fluency and can be difficult to follow. The authors employ a dense mathematical notation, which, while appropriate for a theoretical paper, could benefit from more intuitive explanations and intermediate steps. Several typos and inconsistencies are present (e.g., “polyomial” on page 60, “one CAN model” on page 67, undefined notations such as p1 and p2, and R_{++}).  The motivation behind the specific form of the objective function Φ, including the small ε term, requires clearer explanation, and should not be relegated to a rebuttal.  The informal explanation preceding Theorem 1 would aid understanding.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant theoretical contribution: provably optimal training with linear convergence.
*   Novel approach: introduces a new method for neural network optimization.
*   Potential for future research: extension to deeper networks is promising.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of expressiveness: unclear if the constrained network class is practically useful.
*   Weak experimental validation: limited comparisons and lack of resource information.
*   Clarity and presentation issues: dense notation and occasional typos.
*   Insufficiently thorough related work discussion.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical advance in the field of neural network optimization. However, the limited experimental validation and lack of detailed discussion regarding the practical implications of the proposed method raise concerns. While the theoretical results are valuable, the paper does not yet convincingly demonstrate that this approach offers a significant advantage over existing techniques in real-world applications. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and address the concerns outlined above, particularly focusing on a more thorough experimental evaluation and a more detailed analysis of the model’s expressiveness.
