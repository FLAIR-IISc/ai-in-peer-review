This paper introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for training feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers, guaranteeing convergence to the global optimum at a linear rate. The approach is inspired by the Perron-Frobenius theory for non-negative matrices. This work relates to a line of research aiming to provide theoretical guarantees for the highly non-convex optimization problem of training neural networks, differing from prior works like those using tensor methods by aiming for a more practically feasible algorithm.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work is its novel theoretical contribution. Tackling the non-convex nature of neural network training is a significant challenge, and the authors provide a rare and valuable guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence for a specific class of networks. The proposed algorithm is also parameter-free (no learning rate tuning), which is a clear practical advantage over SGD-based methods. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate the method’s applicability with experiments on real-world datasets, which is a step beyond purely theoretical work in this area.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the theoretical elegance, the practical significance of this result is undermined by severe limitations that impact the work's quality and significance.
1.  **Restrictive Assumptions:** The method relies on non-negative weights, a non-standard generalized polynomial activation, and a heavily modified objective function. These constraints are counterintuitive and distance the model from architectures commonly used in practice, raising questions about its general applicability.
2.  **Unconvincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted on small-scale UCI datasets that are insufficient to validate scalability. The claim of retaining sufficient expressive power is not convincingly supported. While the authors compare against an RBF SVM and ReLU networks, the performance is inconsistent, and the baseline tuning is not described in enough detail to ensure a fair comparison.
3.  **Scalability Concerns:** Scalability is a major concern. Theoretically, the bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the number of hidden units. The paper does not offer an efficient way to find model parameters that satisfy the crucial optimality condition (ρ(A) < 1) without a potentially infeasible parameter search.

**Constructive Feedback:**
To strengthen the claims, the authors should test whether standard SGD can also empirically reach the global optimum on their proposed constrained objective function; this would help isolate whether the guarantee is due to a fundamentally simplified optimization landscape. A more thorough experimental comparison with rigorously tuned baselines on more challenging, higher-dimensional datasets is necessary to substantiate claims of competitiveness and scalability.
