- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method to globally optimize generalized polynomial feedforward networks with non-negative weights.  
- Proves linear convergence to the unique global minimum under specific conditions using a contractive fixed-point construction.  
- Leverages Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point analysis to ground the method theoretically.  
- Provides a clear theoretical framework with detailed lemmas and theorems.  
- Supplies a concrete, implementable algorithm based on the theory.  
- Experimental validation on UCI datasets shows feasibility but limited empirical gains.  
- Builds on prior theoretical work (Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.) while avoiding reliance on hard-to-estimate data statistics.  
- Requires non-negativity of weights, a strong constraint that may reduce model expressiveness.  
- Convergence conditions depend on bounding the spectral radius of a matrix that grows with hidden units, limiting scalability.  
- Method is highly hyperparameter-sensitive and unintuitive, requiring expensive cross-validation over ~150,000 combinations.  
- Empirical performance is weak: outperforms a linear SVM on only 2 of 7 datasets and struggles with zero-error ReLU scenarios.  
- Theoretical bounds are likely conservative, so practical limitations might be overstated but remain unaddressed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, mathematically rigorous approach that advances theoretical guarantees for neural network optimization.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, limited empirical performance, and restrictive non-negativity make practical impact questionable.  
- Overall recommendation: interesting theoretical contribution but practical concerns lead the reviewer to lean toward rejection pending improvements on scalability and parameter tuning.
