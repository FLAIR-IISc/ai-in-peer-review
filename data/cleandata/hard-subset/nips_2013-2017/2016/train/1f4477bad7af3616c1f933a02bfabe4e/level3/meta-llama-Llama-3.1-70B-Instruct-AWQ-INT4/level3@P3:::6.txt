This paper proposes a method to train a restricted class of feed-forward neural networks, proves optimal convergence, and demonstrates applicability on some real-world problems. The authors present a nonlinear spectral method that achieves global optimality for a non-trivial neural network model. The method requires nonnegativity on the weights of the network and minimizes a modified objective function that includes the sum of loss and the negative total sum of the outputs.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the optimization problem. The main theorem, which guarantees the convergence to the global optimum, is well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors also provide experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on several UCI datasets.

However, there are some concerns regarding the practicality of the method. The authors do not convincingly show that the restricted network class is useful in practice, and the expressiveness of the model needs explicit discussion. The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other neural-network training methods and alternative algorithmic paradigms. The experimental results do not clarify the model's applicability limits, and there is missing information on computational resources for the three algorithms.

The presentation of the paper needs polishing, particularly in Section 2, which lacks fluency. There are several typos and notation issues that should be fixed. Additionally, the authors should provide technical clarifications, such as motivating the function Φ and the ε term in the paper, and giving an informal explanation before stating Theorem 1.

The paper's originality lies in its novel combination of familiar techniques, and it is clear how this work differs from previous contributions. However, the authors should discuss concurrent or related approaches more thoroughly. The significance of the paper is evident, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a unique theoretical or pragmatic approach.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written, but there are some areas that need improvement. The paper is not entirely clear, and some sections could be better organized. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results, but some sections could be more detailed.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neural networks, but it needs some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel method for training neural networks that achieves global optimality.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the optimization problem and prove optimal convergence.
* The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the method on several UCI datasets.
* The work addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper does not convincingly show that the restricted network class is useful in practice.
* The experimental results do not clarify the model's applicability limits.
* The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other neural-network training methods and alternative algorithmic paradigms.
* There are several typos and notation issues that should be fixed.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Provide a more thorough comparison with other neural-network training methods and alternative algorithmic paradigms.
* Clarify the model's applicability limits and provide more information on computational resources.
* Address the concerns regarding the practicality of the method and provide more discussion on the expressiveness of the model.
* Fix the typos and notation issues.
* Improve the presentation of the paper, particularly in Section 2.
