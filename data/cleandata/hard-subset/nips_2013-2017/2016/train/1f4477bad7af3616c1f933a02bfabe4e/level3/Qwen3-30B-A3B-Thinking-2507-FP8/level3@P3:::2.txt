

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
This paper proposes a "nonlinear spectral method" for training feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers that achieves global optimality with linear convergence rate under specific assumptions. The authors claim this is the first practically feasible method providing such guarantees. The approach builds on Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems and requires nonnegative weights, a modified objective function (adding the negative total sum of outputs), and a generalized polynomial activation function with real powers α_i ≥ 1. The paper extends previous work on tensor-based approaches [11] and structured matrix factorization [8] by providing a more practical method for global optimization, though it imposes significant constraints.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by establishing conditions for global optimality with linear convergence for a specific class of neural networks. The proof techniques using fixed-point theory and Thompson metrics are well-developed and technically sound. The authors provide a detailed theoretical framework that could be valuable for understanding the optimization landscape of neural networks. The method is indeed parameter-free (no learning rate tuning needed), which is a practical advantage over SGD.

## Weaknesses
The paper's main weakness lies in the restrictive assumptions that significantly limit its practical applicability. The requirement for nonnegative weights contradicts standard practice in neural network training where weights can take both positive and negative values. The generalized polynomial activation function (with real powers) is highly non-standard and not applicable to most real-world problems where ReLU, tanh, or sigmoid are used. The heavy regularization (through the modified objective) further restricts the model's expressiveness.

The experimental validation is insufficient for several reasons. First, the UCI datasets used are small and low-dimensional (e.g., Iris has only 4 features and 150 samples), making it impossible to assess scalability or real-world applicability. Second, the empirical comparisons are unfair: the authors compare against a linear SVM (not a nonlinear SVM with RBF or polynomial kernels) and do not tune the SVM parameters properly. Third, the claim that the model's expressive power is sufficient is not convincingly supported—the authors acknowledge that without constraints, ReLU networks can achieve zero test error on Iris, while their constrained model cannot.

The theoretical analysis contains important scalability concerns. The bounds on the spectral radius ρ(A) grow with the number of hidden units (as shown in Equation 4 and 9), which means the method may become infeasible for larger networks or higher-dimensional data. The authors acknowledge this but provide no practical solution. Additionally, the condition ρ(A) < 1 may not be satisfied for practical parameter settings, and the paper does not provide a practical way to identify valid parameter ranges without extensive search.

The paper also fails to address whether global optimality is truly due to the special objective or if SGD could reach similar solutions with proper tuning. The authors' claim that the targeted class retains sufficient expressive power is not substantiated by the experiments—NLSM1 and NLSM2 achieve slightly worse performance than kernel SVMs on most UCI datasets.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance
**For:** Theoretical contribution to global optimization of neural networks is novel and technically sound. The method has linear convergence and no hyperparameters to tune. The proof framework is well-developed.

**Against:** The restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activation) make the method impractical for most real-world applications. The experiments are insufficient to validate the claims. The scalability concerns are not adequately addressed. The empirical comparisons are unfair and do not properly evaluate the method against relevant baselines.

## Assessment Against Criteria
**Quality:** The theoretical work is technically sound and well-motivated, but the experimental validation is insufficient. The authors do not adequately address the practical limitations of their assumptions. The paper is complete as a theoretical contribution but falls short as a practical machine learning method.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with detailed proofs in the supplementary material. The main theorem is clearly stated, though the practical implications are not sufficiently discussed.

**Originality:** The approach is novel in the context of globally optimal neural network training, though it builds on recent work in tensor methods [11] and fixed-point theory. The specific combination of Perron-Frobenius theory with neural networks is original.

**Significance:** The significance is limited by the restrictive assumptions. The method is unlikely to be adopted in practice due to the nonnegative weight constraint and specialized activation function. The UCI dataset results do not demonstrate clear advantages over standard methods. The paper does not provide a compelling case for why practitioners should adopt this method over existing approaches.

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization, but its practical significance is limited by restrictive assumptions that make it unsuitable for most real-world applications. The experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims of practical relevance. While the theoretical framework is valuable for researchers studying optimization guarantees, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence that the method is useful for practitioners. I recommend rejection, as the limitations outweigh the theoretical contribution for a NIPS venue.
