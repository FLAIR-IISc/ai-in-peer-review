This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks with one or two hidden layers. The core idea is to impose constraints (non-negative weights, heavy regularization) and utilize a specific polynomial activation function to transform the non-convex optimization problem into a globally solvable one, guaranteeing linear convergence. While the theoretical results are compelling – a practically feasible globally optimal algorithm – the work suffers from several limitations concerning its practical applicability and the convincingness of its empirical validation.

The paper builds upon recent work aiming to provide global optimality guarantees for neural network training, referencing Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015). However, it distinguishes itself by avoiding the challenges of tensor decomposition (Janzamin et al.) and relying on a simpler condition based on the spectral radius of a small matrix. This is a valuable contribution, as previous approaches were deemed “challenging to apply in practice.” The theoretical framework, detailed in sections 2 and 3, feels solid and rigorously presented, leveraging tools from nonlinear spectral theory and the Perron-Frobenius theorem. The extension to two hidden layers (section 4.1) is a welcome addition, demonstrating the method’s potential scalability (though limited, as discussed below).

However, the strengths are overshadowed by critical weaknesses. The restrictive assumptions are a major drawback. Requiring non-negative weights and a generalized polynomial activation severely limits the method’s applicability to real-world problems where negative weights are often crucial for representational power. While the authors acknowledge this, their claim that the targeted class retains "sufficient expressive power" isn't convincingly supported by experiments. The toy example in Figure 1 is insufficient to demonstrate complex decision boundary learning, and the UCI datasets used are too small to adequately test this claim. 

The experimental evaluation is particularly concerning. The linear SVM baseline appears untuned, and the complete absence of *nonlinear* SVM baselines (e.g., RBF or polynomial kernels) is a significant omission. This makes it difficult to assess the true competitive advantage of the proposed method. The datasets themselves (UCI repository) are too small in both sample size and dimensionality to convincingly validate scalability or real-world relevance. The authors even acknowledge that the matrix A’s spectral radius grows with the hidden unit count, raising scalability concerns for larger datasets. This dependence limits the method’s practicality.

Furthermore, while the authors provide a theorem outlining conditions for global optimality, verifying those conditions in practice can be challenging. Checking the spectral radius is computationally feasible, but the bounds derived may be conservative. A large parameter search, or more sophisticated prior knowledge, might be necessary to find valid configurations, which contradicts the appeal of a method supposedly requiring no careful tuning. The authors correctly point out the need for faster ways to identify valid candidates.

A crucial point, raised in the paper itself, is the question of whether the global optimality is an artifact of the modified objective function (including the regularization term summing outputs). It would be extremely valuable to test whether standard SGD can *also* empirically reach global optima on the same datasets. This would help determine if the global optimality is inherent to the problem or a consequence of the specific optimization approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical guarantee of global optimality with linear convergence.
*   Rigorously developed mathematical framework.
*   Extension to two hidden layers.
*   First demonstration of a practically feasible globally optimal algorithm for neural networks (albeit with limitations).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Highly restrictive assumptions limiting practical applicability.
*   Unconvincing empirical validation due to small datasets and unfair baseline comparisons.
*   Scalability concerns related to spectral radius growth.
*   Questionable relevance of the global optimality guarantee to standard neural network training.
*   The modified objective could be the key to the result, not the algorithm.



**Overall:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution with a limited practical impact. The novelty of achieving global optimality is commendable, but the restrictive assumptions and weak empirical validation significantly diminish its value. While the work is technically sound and well-written, it falls short of convincingly demonstrating its relevance to real-world machine learning problems. I would recommend **rejection** with encouragement to explore less restrictive assumptions and conduct more comprehensive empirical evaluations, including comparisons to stronger baselines and larger, more representative datasets. Addressing the question of whether standard SGD can achieve similar results would be particularly beneficial.
