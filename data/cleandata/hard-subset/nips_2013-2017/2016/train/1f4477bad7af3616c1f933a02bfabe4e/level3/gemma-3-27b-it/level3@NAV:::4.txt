## Review of “Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods”

This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – generalized polynomial networks with non-negative weights – with a guaranteed linear convergence rate to a global optimum. The core idea leverages a nonlinear spectral method, reducing the global optimality condition to computing the spectral radius of a small, non-negative matrix. This is a conceptually interesting contribution, particularly given the challenges of non-convex optimization in deep learning. The work builds upon recent efforts to provide global optimality guarantees for neural network training (e.g., [11, 8]), but attempts to overcome their practical limitations by focusing on a more readily applicable method.

The strength of the paper lies in its theoretical development. The authors meticulously lay out the mathematical framework, extending it to networks with two hidden layers. They provide a clear algorithm and a theorem guaranteeing convergence under specific conditions. The proof strategy, connecting the optimization problem to fixed-point theory and leveraging the Perron-Frobenius theorem, is well-motivated and appears sound.

However, the practical applicability of the proposed method is severely limited by several key restrictions. The requirement for non-negative weights is a significant constraint, and the use of specific power-based activation functions, rather than common choices like ReLU, further narrows the scope. The experimental results, while demonstrating a functioning method, do not show competitive performance compared to SVM on the tested UCI datasets. In fact, the performance is generally worse. This raises concerns about the practical utility of the method, despite the theoretical guarantees. The claim regarding linear convergence typically implying convergence in fewer than 10 iterations also appears exaggerated; the provided experimental results don’t clearly demonstrate such rapid convergence.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The approach is conceptually new and offers a unique perspective on neural network optimization.
*   Theoretical soundness: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis and a convergence guarantee.
*   Completeness: The work appears comprehensive in its mathematical development.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong restrictions: Non-negativity constraints and specific activation functions significantly limit applicability.
*   Empirical performance: The method underperforms compared to standard techniques like SVM.
*   Overstated claims: The linear convergence claim feels overblown.



**Overall:** I believe this paper, in its current form, is not suitable for acceptance to NIPS. While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the severe practical limitations and underwhelming empirical results outweigh the benefits. The work could be considered for a workshop focused on theoretical foundations of deep learning or optimization, but requires substantial revision and potentially a loosening of the restrictive assumptions to be competitive for a main NIPS conference. Further investigation into the sensitivity of the method’s performance to variations in architecture and data characteristics is crucial.
