- Proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of a specific class of feedforward neural networks with a linear convergence rate.
- Core idea leverages Perron-Frobenius theory by formulating training as a nonlinear spectral problem.
- Positions work well within recent literature on global optimality (cites Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.).
- Theoretical guarantee of global optimality under certain conditions is impressive and technically sound.
- Method requires non-negative network weights, which severely limits expressiveness and departs from standard practice.
- Requires specific generalized polynomial activation functions, excluding common activations like ReLU and limiting architecture choices.
- Empirical results are weak: method consistently underperforms SVM on reported experiments despite claims of competitiveness on some UCI datasets.
- Comparison to ReLU networks highlights practical disadvantages due to imposed constraints.
- Performance is sensitive to the choice of α and to data scaling (e.g., data in [0,1]), indicating fragility.
- Claim that linear convergence "typically" implies convergence in fewer than 10 iterations is an overstatement dependent on spectral radius and initialization.
- Paper is technically rigorous and honest about limitations, but practical utility is questionable.
- Writing and organization are generally clear, though some theoretical sections (Lemma 3, Lemma 4, Theorem 5 bounds) need clearer derivations and explicit conditions.
- Approach is original and distinct from prior global optimality attempts, with thorough related-work referencing.
- Significance is mainly theoretical; strong restrictions on weights and activations undermine real-world applicability.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretically sound approach, linear convergence guarantee, clear presentation, and inclusion of empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: severe architectural restrictions, inferior empirical performance to SVM, exaggerated convergence claims, and limited practical applicability.
- Overall recommendation: reject—novel and interesting theory but too limited practically; suggest relaxing non-negativity or demonstrating clear practical advantage.
