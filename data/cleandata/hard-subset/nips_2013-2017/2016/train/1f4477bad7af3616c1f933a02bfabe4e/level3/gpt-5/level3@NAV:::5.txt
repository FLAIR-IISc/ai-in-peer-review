Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies a class of feedforward networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations and recasts training as a nonlinear Perron–Frobenius fixed-point problem. Under a spectral radius condition that depends only on architecture/hyperparameters, it proves existence and uniqueness of a global optimizer and presents an iterative map with linear convergence to that optimizer. The framework extends to two hidden layers. This connects recent globally optimal NN training via tensors/structured factorization (Janzamin et al.; Haeffele & Vidal) with nonlinear spectral methods and cone contractions, and is practically checkable (ρ(A) < 1) before training.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical target: unique global optimum with a concrete, computable condition and linear convergence.
- Elegant use of multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and Thompson metric; constructive algorithm without learning-rate tuning.
- Extension to two-layer networks and proof strategy that may generalize further.
- Initial experiments show nontrivial classifiers and competitive accuracy on several UCI datasets; fair baseline includes SVM and ReLU.

Weaknesses and questions (Quality/Clarity):
- Derivation gaps: Theorem 3 is framed via first-order contraction bounds (Lemma 4), yet Theorem 4 assumes C2 and appears to use second-order information to control GΦ; please clarify precisely where second derivatives enter and why first-order inequalities are insufficient.
- Notation/consistency: The last layer is treated separately; is ∇uΦ the gradient with respect to the vectorized u with ψ applied elementwise? Please state this explicitly and ensure consistency across (3), Lemma 4, and Theorem 4.
- Objective design: In (2) you maximize the 1-norm of all connection parameters (scaled by ε>0). This seems opposite to standard weight decay and encourages large weights. What is the statistical motivation? Can you bound/generalize without this term? An ablation showing performance and convergence with ε=0 (or using a negative weight penalty) would help.
- ε appears to serve only to force strict positivity of gradients (V++). Is this a proof artifact? Can you achieve the same via interior initialization or barrier terms without biasing the solution?
- Assumptions/practicality: Requiring nonnegative data and strictly positive, nonzero weights is restrictive; data often has signed features, and positivity plus a term that rewards large outputs risks unbounded growth (tempered only by norm equalities). Please justify bounding u and w on Lp-spheres (vs. standard regularization), and discuss preprocessing for signed data, sparsity (zeros disallowed), and stability.
- Experiments are small-scale and sometimes below SVM. Beyond “global optimum,” what practical advantage is offered over ReLU (accuracy/runtime on larger tasks)? Include parameter-matched comparisons, runtime to a target accuracy, sensitivity to α, p, and ρ(A), and ablations of the sum-of-outputs term.

Originality and significance: Conceptually novel synthesis of nonlinear spectral methods with NN training and a checkable global optimality condition. However, significance is limited by restrictive modeling assumptions and modest empirical gains.

Pros for acceptance:
- Theoretically clean global optimality with linear convergence.
- Checkable condition and extension to two layers.
- Bridges PF theory and NN optimization.

Cons for acceptance:
- Restrictive assumptions (nonnegativity, strictly positive weights, ε term).
- Objective design conflicts with standard regularization.
- Some derivations/notations are unclear.
- Limited experimental scope and practical advantage not yet demonstrated.

Overall: Promising theory; I encourage clarifications and stronger empirical validation to support practical relevance.
