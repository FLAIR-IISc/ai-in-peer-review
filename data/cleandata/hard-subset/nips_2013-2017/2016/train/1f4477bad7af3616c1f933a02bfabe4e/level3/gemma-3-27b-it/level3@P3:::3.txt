This paper presents a compelling approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks with provable global optimality and linear convergence guarantees. The authors tackle a core challenge in deep learning – the non-convexity of the optimization landscape – by imposing constraints that allow for a tractable, globally optimal solution. This is a significant contribution, as it represents one of the first practically feasible methods with such guarantees. The work builds upon recent advances in neural network optimization [8, 11] but avoids the limitations of those approaches, specifically the difficulty of estimating data-generating measures [11] or the limited practical applicability [8]. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important problem.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical results appear rigorous. The authors provide a clear roadmap to the proof in Section 3, and the extension to two hidden layers in Section 4.1 is logical. The experimental results, while limited to small datasets, serve as a useful proof of concept. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, notably the non-negativity constraint and the potential for collapsing network behavior. The inclusion of the algorithm as an appendix is a nice touch for reproducibility. The bounds derived for guaranteeing convergence (Equations 4 and 9) are conservative, a point the authors also acknowledge. This honesty regarding the tightness of the bounds strengthens the overall quality.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally very clear, with a logical flow. The notation is well-defined, and the key concepts are explained thoroughly. The organization is strong, allowing readers to easily follow the development of the theory and the application to neural networks. The inclusion of Lemma 1-4 and Theorem 2-3 provides important context and helps guide the reader through the complex theoretical derivations. A minor improvement would be to provide more intuition *before* presenting the equations in Section 4, explaining the motivation behind the introduction of Ψαp,q.

**Originality:** The originality lies in the combination of several factors: the specific class of neural networks (positive inputs, linear weights, generalized polynomial activations), the nonlinear spectral method, and the resulting guarantees of global optimality and linear convergence. While polynomial neural networks have been studied before [15], the authors' approach provides a unique optimization perspective. The constraint satisfaction check based on the spectral radius of a small matrix is a novel and potentially powerful technique.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant. A globally optimal training algorithm, even for a restricted class of networks, is a major step forward. While the non-negativity constraint limits the expressiveness, the framework opens avenues for exploring similar guarantees under broader conditions. The practical implications of guaranteed convergence are substantial, potentially reducing the need for extensive hyperparameter tuning and improving the reliability of trained models. Further investigation into relaxing the non-negativity constraint or finding alternative conditions for global optimality would be highly valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Global Optimality Guarantee:** The primary strength is the provable global optimality and linear convergence rate.
*   **Practicality:** Compared to prior work [11, 8], the proposed method appears more feasible for application.
*   **Clear Theoretical Framework:** The paper provides a well-defined theoretical foundation for the algorithm.
*   **Honest Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Non-Negativity Constraint:** The requirement of non-negative inputs and weights restricts model expressiveness.
*   **Unnatural Activation Functions:** The use of different activation functions in each layer is unusual and may limit applicability.
*   **Potential for Collapsing Networks:** The spectral radius constraint could lead to reduced linear weights and a near-linear behavior.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The constraint may become increasingly difficult to satisfy as input/parameter dimensionality grows.
*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments are confined to small datasets.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning – optimization.
*   The theoretical results are compelling and the method is provably optimal under certain conditions.
*   The work represents a significant step toward understanding and controlling the training process of neural networks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The non-negativity constraint severely limits the applicability of the method.
*   The scalability concerns need to be addressed.



**Specific Suggestions:**

*   **Empirical Investigation of Spectral Radius:** As the reviewer suggested, the authors should investigate and plot empirically how p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, and network size affect the spectral radius ρ(A). This could provide valuable insights into the limitations and scalability of the approach. Specifically, plots showing ρ(A) as a function of these parameters would be highly informative.
*   **Discussion of Relaxation Strategies:** Explore potential strategies for relaxing the non-negativity constraint while still retaining some form of optimality guarantee.
*   **Larger Datasets:** Evaluate the performance of the algorithm on larger, more realistic datasets.



**Overall:** Despite the limitations, I find this work highly encouraging and recommend acceptance. The novelty and potential significance of the results outweigh the drawbacks. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and opens up new avenues for research in neural network optimization.
