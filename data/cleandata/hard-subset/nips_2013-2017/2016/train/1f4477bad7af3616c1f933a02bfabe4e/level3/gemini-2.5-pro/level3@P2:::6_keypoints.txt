- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train "generalized polynomial neural networks" with provable global convergence to a modified objective and linear rate under ρ(A) < 1.  
- Connects neural network training to nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones, inspired by Perron–Frobenius theory.  
- Authors claim practical feasibility and provide proof-of-concept experiments on several UCI datasets.  

- Strength: strong, elegant theoretical contribution mapping the problem to a unique fixed-point contraction (Thompson metric) with a simple, checkable condition ρ(A) < 1.  
- Weakness: experimental validation is weak—method is often competitive but does not consistently beat baselines like kernel SVMs.  

- Clarity: introduction is good but technical sections (especially Section 2) are dense and hard to follow.  
- Need clearer motivation and intuition for the non-standard objective Φ and the small ε term.  
- Theorem 1 is presented abruptly and would benefit from an informal lead-in explaining GΦ as a contraction related to matrix A.  
- The claim that the method "is very fast" should acknowledge dependence on ρ(A), which may be near 1 and slow convergence in practice.  
- Several typos and notation issues (e.g., "polyomial", undefined R++, norms used before definition).  

- Originality: highly original framing; novel and fundamentally different from gradient-based approaches.  
- Related-work discussion is too brief; needs a detailed comparison with Janzamin et al. and Haeffele & Vidal on assumptions and guarantees.  

- Significance: potentially high if practical, but currently limited by strong constraints of the method.  
- Constraint: requires non-negative data and non-negative weights, restricting applicability.  
- Constraint: unclear expressive power of the network class under non-negativity and polynomial activations; not analyzed.  
- Constraint: limited empirical performance on small datasets, no clear advantage over standard methods.  

- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical connection, parameter-free algorithm with global optimality guarantee, simple a priori convergence check.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical utility of restricted model class not demonstrated, limited experiments, and poor clarity in key technical parts.  

- Suggestions: provide theoretical or empirical analysis of expressive power of the network class.  
- Suggestions: add intuitive explanation for Φ (and the summed outputs term) and clarify role of ε.  
- Suggestions: compare the spectral method to standard optimizers (Adam/Adagrad) on the same objective, report runtime/resource usage, and test on larger/more challenging datasets.  
- Suggestion: fix typos and notation inconsistencies and define all symbols before use.
