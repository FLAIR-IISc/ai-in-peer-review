- Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method for training a class of feedforward networks with global optimality and linear convergence guarantees.  
- Leverages the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix (dependent on architecture) as the convergence criterion.  
- Conceptually innovative way to address non-convexity in neural network training.  
- Requires restrictive assumptions: non-negativity of weights and non-standard activation functions.  
- These constraints limit generalizability and make the method incompatible with common architectures (e.g., ReLU).  
- Theoretical strengths: rigorous proofs of convergence, uniqueness of global optimum, and no need for hyperparameter tuning like learning rates.  
- Linear convergence rate is a clear theoretical advantage.  
- Experiments demonstrate feasibility on real datasets but are limited in scope.  
- Empirical performance is worse than SVMs and standard SGD-trained networks.  
- The claim of convergence in "less than 10 iterations" appears exaggerated and weakly supported.  
- Scalability is unclear: spectral radius grows with hidden units, potentially limiting applicability to high-dimensional and deeper networks.  
- Recommendation: valuable theoretical contribution but better suited for a specialized audience; accept only if the conference prioritizes theory over practical impact.  
- Suggestion: paper would benefit from further refinement and stronger empirical validation for broader relevance.
