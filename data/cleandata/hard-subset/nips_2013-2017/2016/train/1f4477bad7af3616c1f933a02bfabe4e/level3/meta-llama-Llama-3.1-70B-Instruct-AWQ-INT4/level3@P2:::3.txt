The paper "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods" presents a novel approach to training neural networks with a guarantee of global optimality. The authors propose a nonlinear spectral method that can be applied to a specific class of feedforward neural networks, which they call generalized polynomial neural networks. The main contribution of the paper is the development of an optimization algorithm that can find the global optimum of the network's parameters with linear convergence.

The authors' approach is based on the idea of using a nonlinear spectral method to optimize the network's parameters. They show that under certain conditions, the optimization problem can be solved globally optimally with a linear convergence rate. The conditions are relatively simple and depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix formed from the network's parameters.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. They also provide experimental results on a 2D synthetic dataset and several small UCI datasets, which demonstrate the effectiveness of their algorithm.

The main novelty of the paper is the development of a potentially practical neural network with explicit optimality and convergence guarantees under simple, checkable preconditions. The preconditions are indeed simple and depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix formed from the network's parameters.

However, there are some limitations to the approach. One major limitation is that the method requires nonnegative inputs and nonnegative linear-layer weights, which may limit the model's expressiveness. Additionally, each hidden layer uses a different activation function, which is unnatural and may restrict the applicability of the approach.

Another concern is that the spectral-radius < 1 constraint forces increasing some p-values and decreasing pw/pu, effectively shrinking linear weights and potentially collapsing deep networks to near-linear behavior. This raises concerns about the scalability of the approach to deep architectures.

Despite these concerns, the paper presents an encouraging result, and the reviewer recommends acceptance. However, the reviewer suggests that the authors investigate and plot empirically how p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, and network size affect the spectral radius rho(A). This would provide a better understanding of the limitations and potential of the approach.

The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.
