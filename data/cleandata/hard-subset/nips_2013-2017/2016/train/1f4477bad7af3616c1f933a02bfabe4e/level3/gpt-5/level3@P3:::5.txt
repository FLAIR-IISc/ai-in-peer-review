Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a class of feedforward neural networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations for which training has a unique global optimizer. The core idea is to recast training as finding a fixed point of a multi-homogeneous, order-preserving map GΦ constructed from the gradient of the objective, and to prove it is a contraction in the Thompson metric when the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A is < 1. Under this condition, the fixed-point iteration converges linearly to the unique global optimum. The authors give results for one and two hidden layers and demonstrate proof-of-concept experiments on UCI datasets.

The work builds on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous maps, contraction mappings in cones, and relates to recent global optimality results for neural networks via tensor methods (Janzamin et al.) and structured matrix factorization (Haeffele & Vidal). Compared to these, the proposed method is practically implementable, needs only nonnegativity of data/weights and a checkable spectral radius condition, and yields linear convergence under mild architectural constraints.

Quality
- The theoretical development is careful and technically strong. The proof strategy—interior attainment, fixed-point characterization, contraction in Thompson metric, and linear convergence—is coherent and well-motivated.
- The bounds used to construct A are conservative; the authors acknowledge this. Assumptions (nonnegative data, strictly positive weights, distinct α’s, fixed ℓp norms) are strong and limit direct applicability.
- The role of several modeling choices (sum of outputs term, ε-weight sum, norm constraints) is only partially justified; more intuition would help.
- Experiments are small-scale and show competitiveness but not superiority to strong baselines (RBF-SVM, ReLU nets).

Clarity
- The high-level derivations are instructive, but some steps lack transparency:
  - The need for ε and the positivity of the gradient at the boundary (Lemma 5) should be illustrated with simple examples.
  - The necessity of fixed ℓp norms and their relation to scale invariance of multi-homogeneous networks should be explained more prominently.
  - The switch from first- to second-order information in establishing bounds (see detailed question below) is not explicitly highlighted.
- Notational choices for gradients with respect to matrix parameters (u) would benefit from an explicit statement that vectorization is assumed when applying ψp and norms.

Originality
- Combining multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory with NN training to obtain a checkable global optimality condition and a linearly convergent algorithm is novel and interesting.
- The extension to two hidden layers is a meaningful step, though the conditions become more restrictive.

Significance
- The work advances the theory of globally optimal NN training with a practically implementable algorithm for a restricted yet nontrivial class.
- Practical impact is limited by data and weight nonnegativity, strictly positive weights, the modified objective, and norm constraints. Demonstrated accuracy is competitive but not state of the art.

Specific technical questions and concerns
- Why Theorem 3 uses first-order inequalities while Theorem 4 involves second derivatives?
  - Theorem 3 is an abstract fixed-point result: it assumes first-order Lipschitz-type bounds on F = GΦ to derive contraction via Lemma 4. To verify those bounds for this particular GΦ, one must control how GΦ changes with (w,u). Since GΦ is defined in terms of ∇Φ, controlling ∇GΦ requires bounding derivatives of ∇Φ, i.e., the Hessian of Φ. Hence, Theorem 4 leverages second derivatives to instantiate the first-order bounds required by Theorem 3.

- Treatment of the last layer and the gradient with respect to u
  - The paper treats the K output weight vectors wi as K separate blocks and the hidden-layer weight matrix u as a single block. The gradient ∇uΦ has the same shape as u; when norms and ψp are applied, the matrix is implicitly vectorized (entrywise operations). This convention appears consistent throughout.

- Bounding u in a ball and overall treatment of u
  - The ℓp-sphere constraints ‖wi‖pw = ρw and ‖u‖pu = ρu serve multiple purposes: (i) remove scale indeterminacies due to positive homogeneity (otherwise the objective with a “sum of outputs” term can be driven upward by rescaling layers); (ii) yield a compact feasible set and well-posed fixed-point normalization; and (iii) keep the contraction constants finite. This is mathematically necessary for uniqueness and convergence but should be made more explicit; without these constraints the modified objective can be unbounded.

- Why maximize the 1-norm of connection parameters in (2) when typical NNs penalize weight norms?
  - The additive linear term ε(∑ wr,l + ∑ ulm) is not intended as a capacity control but as a technical device to ensure strict positivity of gradients on S+ (needed for interior attainment and the Perron–Frobenius machinery). Since parameters also lie on fixed-norm spheres, this term does not allow unbounded growth; with ε chosen arbitrarily small, its effect on the optimizer is intended to be negligible. That said, it does bias solutions slightly; alternative positivity enforcements (e.g., barrier −δ∑log w − δ∑log u or reparameterization via exponentials) could be explored and discussed.

- Role of the small ε in (2)
  - As above, it appears introduced solely to force ∇Φ ∈ V++ (Lemma 5), i.e., a proof artifact. The paper should clarify the exact coefficient and empirically show insensitivity to ε over several orders of magnitude, or replace it with a cleaner positivity mechanism.

- Nonnegative data and strictly positive, nonzero weights
  - Nonnegative inputs are common (images after scaling, counts), but strictly positive weights (no zeros) and monotone activations impose strong modeling constraints (no inhibitory effects, no sign changes). Risk of “unbounded growth” is mitigated by fixed-norm constraints, but expressivity is reduced relative to standard NNs; the paper partially addresses this by using distinct exponents α. Please discuss representational limitations, potential re-centering of inputs to use signed features, or extensions to mixed-sign cones.

- Experiments and practical advantage over standard NNs
  - The method’s key practical advantage is the existence of a global optimum with linear convergence and no learning-rate tuning; however, accuracy is generally slightly worse than RBF-SVM and comparable to small ReLU nets on low-dimensional data. To argue practical benefit, consider:
    - Larger-scale datasets and higher-dimensional inputs, with analysis of spectral-radius feasibility.
    - Ablations on α, number of units, and the gap between theoretical and empirical contraction rates.
    - Comparisons to monotone networks and polynomial kernel methods, not only ReLU.
    - Sensitivity to ε and the “sum of outputs” term.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, original theory yielding a unique global optimizer and a linearly convergent algorithm.
- Clear connection to nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory; checkable condition via a small matrix.
- First practical demonstration (to my knowledge) of globally optimal NN training with nontrivial accuracy.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong modeling restrictions (nonnegative data/weights, strictly positive parameters, fixed-norm spheres).
- Modified objective (sum of outputs and ε-weight sum) lacks compelling practical justification.
- Some derivations and modeling choices are under-explained; reliance on supplementary material.
- Limited experiments; no clear advantage over strong baselines like RBF-SVM.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Add an intuitive subsection explaining each modification (sum of outputs, ε term, norm constraints), with simple 1D examples showing why they are needed.
- Make explicit the need for second derivatives in Theorem 4 and provide a short derivation sketch linking Hessian bounds to Lemma 4’s first-order inequalities.
- Clarify the gradient conventions for matrix parameters and the role of vectorization.
- Report sensitivity to ε and explore alternative positivity mechanisms.
- Expand experiments, include monotone/polynomial baselines, and provide an empirical study of spectral-radius margins versus convergence speed and accuracy.

Overall recommendation
- Borderline. The theoretical contribution is strong and novel; however, practical relevance is constrained by modeling assumptions and limited empirical validation. Strengthening clarity and empirical evidence would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
