### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel approach for training a specific class of feed-forward neural networks, guaranteeing convergence to the global optimum of the training objective. The proposed method, based on nonlinear spectral theory, applies to networks with non-negative weights and data, using generalized polynomial activation functions. The core theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes that under a checkable condition—namely, that the spectral radius of a small, explicitly defined matrix `A` is less than one—a simple iterative algorithm converges linearly to a unique global maximizer. The authors frame this work as a practically feasible alternative to other recent approaches for globally optimal neural network training [8, 11] that are either challenging to apply or rely on estimating unknown data densities. The method is demonstrated for networks with one and two hidden layers, and experimental results on several UCI datasets are presented as a proof of concept.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The problem of non-convex optimization in neural network training is a central challenge in deep learning. This paper provides a rare positive result: a provable guarantee of finding the global optimum for a non-trivial class of networks. The linear convergence rate is also a very strong theoretical guarantee.

2.  **Novel Approach:** The application of nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point methods on convex cones to neural network training is highly original. It offers a fresh and mathematically elegant perspective on a problem dominated by gradient-based heuristics.

3.  **A Priori Verifiable Guarantee:** A major strength of the proposed framework is that the condition for guaranteed convergence, `ρ(A) < 1`, can be checked *before* training begins, based on the network architecture and data properties. This is a significant practical advantage over methods where convergence guarantees are either asymptotic or depend on unknown properties of the optimization landscape.

**Weaknesses and Major Concerns**

1.  **Limited Practical Utility and Unclear Expressiveness:** The primary concern is that the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the restricted class of networks is useful in practice. The constraints—non-negative data, non-negative weights, and a modified objective function—are severe. The paper lacks a dedicated discussion on the expressive power of this network class. What types of decision boundaries or functions can it model effectively, and what common machine learning tasks are fundamentally beyond its reach due to these constraints? While Figure 1 shows a complex boundary is possible, this single example is insufficient to build confidence.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper cites key works on globally optimal training [8, 11], the comparison is brief. A more in-depth discussion contrasting the underlying assumptions, theoretical machinery, and practical trade-offs of these different tensor- and factorization-based methods would be valuable. Furthermore, the paper omits discussion of other relevant lines of research, such as analyses of the neural network loss landscape that suggest local minima are not a major practical impediment for certain architectures.

3.  **Weak and Incomplete Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are presented as a "proof of concept" but are too limited to substantiate the method's value.
    *   **Baselines:** The comparison is only against RBF-SVMs and a single implementation of SGD for ReLU networks. Crucially, state-of-the-art neural network optimizers like Adam or RMSProp are omitted, as are other algorithmic paradigms beyond SVMs. This makes it difficult to assess how the proposed method (NLSM) compares to what a practitioner would typically use.
    *   **Scope:** The experiments are confined to small, low-dimensional UCI datasets. This avoids addressing the key question of how the method scales. The bounds on `ρ(A)` appear to depend on the number of hidden units and data norm, but the practical implications for higher-dimensional problems are not explored.
    *   **Missing Information:** The paper does not report essential information on computational resources. What is the wall-clock time and memory usage of NLSM compared to the baselines? Without this, it is impossible to evaluate the practical efficiency promised by the linear convergence rate.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The introduction is clear and motivating, but the technical exposition in Section 2 is abrupt.
    *   Before formally stating Theorem 1, it would be extremely helpful to provide a paragraph with a high-level, informal explanation of the proof strategy: framing the optimization as a fixed-point problem for the operator `GΦ` on a complete metric space, and then using the Banach fixed-point theorem.
    *   The motivation for the specific form of the objective function `Φ` (adding the sum of outputs) and the inclusion of the small `ε` term should be explained more intuitively in the main text. The current explanation ("technical reasons," "gradient is strictly positive") is correct but not very illuminating for the reader.

*   **Typos and Notational Issues:** The manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading. For instance: "polyomial" appears in the text; `R_{++}` is used before it is defined; the norms `pw` and `pu` are used in the algorithm box as `p1` and `p2` without being introduced as such.

*   **Technical Clarifications:**
    *   The claim that the method "converges quickly" (Section 2) and is "much faster than SGDs" (Section 5) is based on empirical results in Figure 2. This should be made explicit. The theoretical convergence rate is `ρ(A)^k`, but the paper gives no indication of the typical values of `ρ(A)` achieved in the experiments. How close to 1 is it? This is critical for understanding the practical speed of convergence.
    *   The uniqueness of the global optimum is a powerful result. The discussion on why different `α` values are needed to avoid degenerate (rank-one) solutions is interesting and should be connected more strongly to the broader question of the model's expressiveness.

In conclusion, this paper presents a theoretically impressive and original result on a fundamental problem in machine learning. However, its potential for practical impact is significantly undermined by the restrictive nature of the model class and the limited experimental validation. The paper would be much stronger if it included a thorough discussion of the model's expressiveness and a more comprehensive and rigorous experimental comparison against modern, standard baselines.
