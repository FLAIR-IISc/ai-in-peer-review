### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel approach for training a specific class of neural networks, termed Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks, to a global optimum with a guaranteed linear rate of convergence. The proposed model is a feedforward network with non-negative weights and inputs, using generalized polynomial activation functions (i.e., real-valued powers). The core of the contribution is a "nonlinear spectral method," an iterative algorithm derived from the theory of nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones, which is shown to converge to the unique global maximizer of a modified objective function.

The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) establishes that this convergence is guaranteed under a simple, a priori checkable condition: the spectral radius of a small, well-defined non-negative matrix A, whose entries depend on the network's hyperparameters, must be less than one (ρ(A) < 1). This work positions itself as a practically feasible alternative to recent theoretical results on globally optimal training (e.g., Janzamin et al., 2015; Haeffele and Vidal, 2015), which often rely on strong assumptions or computationally challenging procedures like estimating data-generating density functions. The authors demonstrate the method for one and two hidden layers and provide proof-of-concept experiments on synthetic and several UCI datasets, showing competitive performance against baseline ReLU networks and SVMs.

**Strengths and Weaknesses (Qualitative Evaluation)**

**Originality & Significance:**
The primary strength of this paper is its high degree of originality and potential theoretical significance. Providing a practical algorithm for globally optimal neural network training, even for a restricted class of models, is a significant step forward in a field dominated by local optimization methods. The connection drawn between neural network optimization and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory is novel and elegant. The condition for guaranteed convergence—checking if ρ(A) < 1—is remarkably simple and depends only on the network architecture and hyperparameters, not the training data itself. This makes the guarantee far more accessible than those in prior works. If this theoretical framework can be extended to more general network architectures, it could have a substantial impact on our understanding of neural network optimization landscapes.

**Quality:**
The theoretical development is rigorous and appears technically sound. The paper builds its argument carefully, starting from the properties of the objective function, connecting critical points to fixed points of the proposed mapping GΦ, and finally using the Banach fixed-point theorem on a complete metric space (endowed with the Thompson metric) to prove the existence, uniqueness, and convergence to the global optimum. The extension to a two-hidden-layer network demonstrates the generality of the framework.

However, the quality of the contribution is tempered by the restrictive assumptions required for the theory to hold, which raises significant questions about the practical utility and expressiveness of the resulting models.

1.  **Non-negativity Constraint:** The requirement of non-negative inputs and weights is a major limitation. While the authors correctly note that count-based data (images, word frequencies) are non-negative, and other data can be shifted, this fundamentally restricts the model's expressive power. It prevents the learning of inhibitory connections, which are crucial components of biological and artificial neural networks. This constraint may limit the complexity of the functions the network can represent.

2.  **Unnatural Architecture:** The requirement that each hidden unit `l` must have a distinct activation power `α_l` to ensure a unique optimum is an artifact of the proof technique rather than a principled architectural choice. This feels unnatural compared to standard architectures where units in a layer share the same activation function.

3.  **Impact of the ρ(A) < 1 Constraint:** This is a more subtle but critical concern. The paper shows that ρ(A) increases with the weight norms (ρw, ρu) and the number of hidden units (n1), and decreases as the norm exponents (pw, pu) increase. To satisfy ρ(A) < 1 for wider or deeper networks, one might be forced to choose very small weight norms and/or very large values for pw and pu. This could have the effect of strongly regularizing the weights towards a uniform distribution and shrinking their magnitude, potentially collapsing the network into a near-linear model and diminishing the benefit of its nonlinear structure. The work would be much stronger if it addressed whether the conditions for optimality inherently restrict the model class to be less powerful.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and organized. The authors clearly state their main result upfront and provide a "Proof Strategy" section that outlines the theoretical roadmap, which is very helpful for the reader. The mathematical derivations are dense but follow a logical progression. The algorithm is presented clearly.

**Experiments:**
The experiments serve as a useful proof of concept, demonstrating that the algorithm works and can achieve reasonable performance on small-scale, real-world problems. The comparison against SGD on the same objective function (Figure 2) effectively showcases the superior and stable convergence of the proposed nonlinear spectral method. However, the experiments are limited to low-dimensional datasets and do not address the scalability concerns raised above. A crucial missing piece is an empirical investigation into the trade-offs imposed by the ρ(A) < 1 constraint.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
1.  **Novelty:** Presents a new, theoretically-grounded algorithm for globally optimal NN training.
2.  **Significance:** Addresses a fundamental open problem in deep learning. The connection to nonlinear spectral methods is an original and valuable contribution.
3.  **Strong Guarantees:** Provides a simple, a priori checkable condition for linear convergence to the unique global optimum, a significant improvement over prior work.
4.  **Practical Feasibility:** Unlike some contemporary theoretical work, the proposed algorithm is implemented and tested on real datasets.

**Arguments for Rejection (Cons):**
1.  **Restrictive Assumptions:** The non-negativity constraint on weights and data severely limits the model's applicability and expressive power.
2.  **Potential for Trivial Solutions:** The ρ(A) < 1 condition may force hyperparameters that result in near-linear or otherwise non-expressive models, especially as network size increases. The practical significance of finding a global optimum for such a constrained model class is unclear.
3.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments are on small datasets and do not investigate the scalability of the method or the practical impact of the constraints on model capacity.

**Recommendation:**
Despite the significant limitations, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the theoretical result are compelling. This paper introduces a new set of tools and a new perspective on the notoriously difficult problem of neural network optimization. While the practical applicability of the current model is questionable, the work is an encouraging and important theoretical contribution. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors explicitly discuss the limitations regarding model expressiveness in the final version. A valuable addition would be an empirical study or plot showing how ρ(A) behaves as a function of key parameters (n1, d, ρw, pw) to provide insight into the scalability of the guarantee.
