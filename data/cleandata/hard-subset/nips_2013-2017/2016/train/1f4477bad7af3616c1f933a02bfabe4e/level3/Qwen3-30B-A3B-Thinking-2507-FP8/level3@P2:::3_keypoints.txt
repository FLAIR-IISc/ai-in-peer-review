- Introduces a nonlinear spectral method that achieves global optimality for a class of neural nets with nonnegative inputs/weights and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Provides rigorous proofs of global optimality for one- and two-hidden-layer networks with linear convergence rate.  
- Main theorem gives explicit, checkable convergence condition (spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix < 1).  
- Theoretical analysis is thorough and technically sound, with supplementary material detailing proofs.  
- Experiments on UCI datasets show competitive performance versus ReLU networks and SVMs without hyperparameter tuning.  
- Relies on nonnegativity of inputs and weights, which significantly limits applicability to many real-world problems.  
- Requires different activation functions per hidden unit, an unnatural architectural constraint.  
- Spectral radius condition may become more restrictive as network size/dimensionality increases, raising scaling concerns.  
- Empirical validation is limited to small, low-dimensional datasets; larger-scale performance is untested.  
- Potential risk of network collapse due to constraints on weight magnitudes.  
- Original and significant theoretical advance: first practically feasible method with explicit, checkable global-optimality guarantees for a non-trivial architecture.  
- Offers practical benefits over SGD (linear convergence, no hyperparameter tuning) in applicable settings.  
- Opens a promising research direction to generalize global-optimality methods to more standard architectures.  
- Recommendation: accept — substantial theoretical contribution despite practical constraints and limited experimental scale.
