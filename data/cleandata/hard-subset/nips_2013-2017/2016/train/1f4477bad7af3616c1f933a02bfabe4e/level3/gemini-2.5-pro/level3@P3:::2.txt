### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel nonlinear spectral method for training feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers. The central contribution is a proof that, under a specific set of conditions, this method converges linearly to the unique global optimum of the training objective. This work positions itself as a practically feasible alternative to other theoretical approaches for globally optimal training, such as the tensor methods of Janzamin et al. (2015), which require knowledge of the data-generating distribution. The proposed method is derived from the Perron-Frobenius theory for nonlinear eigenproblems, which necessitates several key modifications to the standard neural network framework: (1) all network weights and input data must be non-negative; (2) the network uses a generalized polynomial activation function where each unit has a distinct power; and (3) the objective function is modified to include the negative sum of all network outputs, which acts as a strong regularizer. The authors provide a theorem establishing that if the spectral radius of a specific, easily computable matrix is less than one, the algorithm is guaranteed to converge to the global optimum. The paper includes experiments on several UCI datasets to demonstrate the method's performance.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**

The theoretical development is the paper's main strength. The connection between nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and neural network optimization is elegant, and the proofs for the existence, uniqueness, and linear convergence to the global optimum are rigorous. This represents a complete and technically sound piece of theoretical work.

However, the empirical validation is weak and does not convincingly support the authors' claims about the model's practical utility.
*   **Restrictive Assumptions:** The method's requirements—strictly non-negative weights, a highly specific generalized polynomial activation, and a modified objective function—are severe departures from standard practice. The non-negativity constraint, in particular, fundamentally limits the functions the network can represent. While the authors argue this is necessary for the theoretical guarantees, it makes the model less applicable to general machine learning problems.
*   **Unconvincing Expressive Power:** The claim that the targeted class of networks "is still expressive enough to create complex decision boundaries" is not well-supported. On several datasets (e.g., Iris, Banknote, Seeds), the proposed method underperforms a standard RBF SVM, suggesting that the constraints significantly harm its representational capacity.
*   **Weak Experimental Setup:** The experiments are conducted on small UCI datasets with low dimensionality and sample sizes. This setting is insufficient to validate the scalability of the method or its applicability to the kind of complex, high-dimensional problems where neural networks typically excel. The comparisons to ReLU networks are interesting, but the proposed method's inferior performance on several tasks undermines the argument for its practical adoption.

**Originality:**

The paper is highly original. Applying techniques from the theory of nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones to train neural networks is a novel and creative approach. It provides a fresh theoretical perspective on the challenging non-convex optimization landscape of neural networks and clearly distinguishes itself from prior work on global optimality guarantees, which have relied on different mathematical machinery like tensor decompositions or specific convex relaxations.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-structured, presenting the main algorithm and theorem upfront before delving into the technical proofs. However, the mathematical exposition is dense and assumes significant familiarity with fixed-point theory and the Thompson metric. While appropriate for a theoretical paper, more intuition behind the key steps—particularly the formulation of the objective function and the construction of the matrix A—would have made the work more accessible to a broader deep learning audience.

**Significance:**

The paper addresses a problem of fundamental importance: the non-convex nature of neural network training. Establishing a framework where global optimality is guaranteed is a significant theoretical goal.

The practical significance, however, is questionable.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The authors correctly note that the entries of matrix A, and thus its spectral radius, grow with the number of hidden units. This poses a serious scalability challenge. For complex problems requiring many hidden units, satisfying the condition ρ(A) < 1 may become infeasible or require such heavy regularization (via large `pw`, `pu`) that the resulting model is trivial.
*   **Feasibility of the Optimality Condition:** The core guarantee hinges on ρ(A) < 1. While the authors show it is always possible to find parameters `pw`, `pu` to satisfy this, it is unclear if these settings correspond to useful models. A more practical analysis is needed to understand the trade-off between the convergence guarantee and model performance. Checking the condition requires a parameter search, and faster methods for identifying valid and effective model configurations are necessary.
*   **Isolating the Contribution:** A key unanswered question is whether the global optimality is primarily a feature of the novel spectral algorithm or a byproduct of the highly constrained and regularized objective function. The objective may create a much simpler optimization landscape than standard loss functions. To clarify this, it would be valuable to see experiments attempting to optimize the *same objective function* (Eq. 2) with a standard method like SGD or Adam. If SGD also consistently finds the global optimum on these datasets, it would suggest the problem formulation, not the algorithm, is the primary reason for success.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and original theoretical framework for guaranteeing global optimality in neural network training.
2.  The mathematical contribution, connecting Perron-Frobenius theory to network optimization, is elegant and sound.
3.  Addresses a long-standing and fundamentally important problem in the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The method relies on highly restrictive and impractical assumptions (non-negative weights, special activations) that limit its applicability.
2.  The experimental validation is weak, conducted only on small-scale datasets, and fails to demonstrate competitive performance or scalability.
3.  The practical significance is unclear, as the conditions required for the optimality guarantee may lead to poor-performing models.
4.  The empirical analysis lacks a crucial baseline: testing whether standard optimizers could also solve the proposed objective function.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a theoretically impressive and original contribution. However, the gap between the theory and practical application is substantial. The restrictive assumptions and weak empirical results make it difficult to see this method being adopted by practitioners or serving as a foundation for future practical algorithms. While the theoretical novelty is high, the lack of demonstrated utility or convincing empirical support is a major drawback.
