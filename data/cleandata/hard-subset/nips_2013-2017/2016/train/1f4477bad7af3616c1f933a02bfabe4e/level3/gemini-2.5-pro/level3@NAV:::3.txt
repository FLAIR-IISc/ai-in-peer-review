This paper addresses the highly non-convex nature of neural network optimization by identifying a specific class of networks for which a global optimum is provably attainable. The authors focus on feedforward networks with non-negative inputs and weights, and generalized polynomial activation functions. They propose a novel nonlinear spectral-method algorithm and prove that it converges linearly to the unique global optimum. This contribution builds upon a line of research seeking guarantees for neural network training, such as the tensor-based methods of Janzamin et al. (2015), but distinguishes itself by offering a potentially more practical approach with simpler, a-priori checkable preconditions for convergence.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The main contribution is a significant theoretical advancement. Providing a practical algorithm with explicit global optimality and linear convergence guarantees for a non-trivial neural network class is a novel and important result. The precondition, which depends only on the spectral radius of a small, well-defined matrix, is a clear improvement in usability over prior works that rely on unknown properties of the data distribution or complex tensor decompositions.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical framework, derived from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and the Banach fixed-point theorem, is rigorous and well-developed. The authors clearly lay out the proof strategy and extend their results from single-layer to deeper networks.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of experiments on synthetic and UCI datasets, while limited, serves as a valuable proof of concept. This is a commendable step that grounds the theoretical claims in practice and is an advantage over some purely theoretical predecessors in this area.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Model Expressiveness:** The primary weakness lies in the restrictive model assumptions. The requirement of non-negative inputs and weights severely limits the class of functions the network can represent, potentially hindering its performance on many real-world tasks.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The core `rho(A) < 1` constraint may pose a scalability challenge. To satisfy this condition for larger networks, the parameters (`pw`, `pu`) must increase, effectively shrinking the linear weights. There is a concern that for deep or wide networks, this could cause the model to degenerate towards near-linear behavior. The authors acknowledge this, but its practical impact could be more deeply investigated. I would strongly suggest an empirical study plotting how `rho(A)` is affected by network size, input dimension, and other hyperparameters to better understand the practical boundaries of this approach.
*   **Unconventional Architecture:** The requirement that each hidden unit use a different activation function (i.e., distinct `alpha_i`) is an unnatural constraint that may limit practical adoption.

Despite these significant limitations, the novelty of the theoretical guarantees is compelling. This work opens an encouraging new avenue for provably optimal deep learning. I recommend acceptance.
