## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a compelling approach to training a specific class of neural networks – generalized polynomial networks with non-negative weights – to global optimality. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, under certain conditions, these networks can be trained with a linear convergence rate using a novel “nonlinear spectral method.” This method leverages fixed-point theory by constructing a contractive map whose fixed point corresponds to the unique minimizer of the cost function. The authors highlight a significant result: carefully constrained complex models can indeed admit unique stationary points, a departure from the typical non-convex optimization landscape of neural networks.

The work builds upon recent efforts in globally optimizing neural network training (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.), aiming to overcome the practical limitations of prior approaches.  Unlike tensor-based methods requiring estimation of the data generating measure (Janzamin et al.), the proposed method relies on a spectral radius calculation, theoretically simplifying the process.  The paper is well-structured, clearly laying out the theoretical foundation, algorithm, and experimental setup. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory provides a solid mathematical basis. 

However, the practicality of the method is questionable. A significant weakness lies in the scalability of the convergence proof, which relies on bounding the spectral radius of a matrix whose size grows with the number of hidden units. This limits the applicability to larger hidden layers.  Furthermore, the method introduces a considerable burden on the user. A large number of hyperparameters need to be tuned, making the procedure unintuitive and cumbersome.  Critically, the spectral condition *must* be verified for every parameter choice, adding significant overhead. The experimental results, while showcasing a proof-of-concept, are not particularly convincing.  Despite extensive hyperparameter tuning (approximately 150,000 parameter combinations), the method outperforms a linear SVM on only 2 out of 7 datasets. This casts doubt on the practical utility of the investigated network architecture, even with guaranteed global optimality.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical result demonstrating global optimality for a specific network class.
*   Development of a new training algorithm with provable linear convergence.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the theoretical framework.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scalability due to spectral radius calculation.
*   Significant hyperparameter tuning required.
*   Weak empirical performance compared to simpler methods.
*   Practical relevance is questionable given the experimental results.



**Overall:** While the theoretical contribution is interesting and the mathematics sound, the practical limitations and underwhelming empirical results make this paper a marginal submission. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should address the scalability issues and demonstrate stronger empirical performance before resubmission. Perhaps exploring techniques to relax the constraints or approximate the spectral radius calculation could improve scalability.
