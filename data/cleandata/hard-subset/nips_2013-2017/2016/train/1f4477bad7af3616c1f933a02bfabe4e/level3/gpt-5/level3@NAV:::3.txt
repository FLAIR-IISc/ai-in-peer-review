This paper studies a restricted yet meaningful class of neural networks—nonnegative inputs and linear-layer weights, with generalized polynomial activations—and proves the existence and uniqueness of a global optimum reachable by a nonlinear spectral method with linear convergence. The core technique draws on multihomogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and fixed-point contractions in the Thompson metric; the optimality condition reduces to checking that the spectral radius of a small, nonnegative (K+1)-by-(K+1) matrix A is < 1. The method is extended from one hidden layer to deeper networks (two layers in detail), and experiments on a 2D synthetic dataset and several small UCI datasets demonstrate competitive performance and very fast convergence. Relative to prior work (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.), this is the first approach that appears practically feasible while providing explicit optimality and convergence guarantees under simple, checkable preconditions.

Quality: The theory is careful and technically sound, with clear conditions under which GΦ is a contraction and global optimality follows. The A-matrix construction and bounds are conservative, as admitted by the authors. Empirically, the scope is modest but consistent with the theory: low-dimensional settings and small networks where ρ(A) < 1 is attainable.

Clarity: The manuscript is dense but generally well-organized; key assumptions and mappings are explicit. Some proofs are deferred; adding more intuition for the added terms in the objective and the necessity of distinct per-unit activations would improve readability.

Originality: High. The nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal NN training with linear-rate convergence under simple spectral conditions is novel and advances a growing literature on provable neural optimization.

Significance: Potentially impactful as a rare practical algorithm with global guarantees. However, applicability is curtailed by constraints: nonnegative inputs/weights, distinct activations per hidden unit, and a spectral-radius condition that tightens with depth/width.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The nonnegativity requirement may limit expressiveness despite shiftability of inputs; discuss alternatives (e.g., signed features via two-channel encoding) and empirical impact.
- Requiring different activations per hidden unit is unnatural; quantify the accuracy cost vs. standard tied activations.
- The ρ(A) < 1 constraint effectively pushes larger p-values and smaller pw/pu, shrinking linear weights and risking near-linear behavior, especially as dimensionality/network size grow. Please empirically study and plot how ρ(A) varies with p1, p2, pw, pu, input dimension, n1/n2, and K; report feasible regimes.
- Explore tighter bounds for A to improve scalability to deeper/wider networks.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, novel theory with linear-rate global convergence.
- Simple, verifiable preconditions depending only on ρ(A).
- First practical global-optimal NN training with nontrivial performance.

Cons:
- Expressiveness constraints (nonnegativity, heterogeneous activations).
- Conservative bounds limit scalability; small-scale experiments only.

Verdict: Encouraging and technically solid; recommend acceptance.
