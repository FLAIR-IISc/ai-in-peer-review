

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a theoretically grounded method for training a restricted class of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees and linear convergence rates. The authors focus on networks with nonnegative weights and a modified objective function that includes a negative sum of outputs. Under certain assumptions (nonnegative data, specific parameter choices), they prove that their nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) can find the global optimum. The work relates to recent NIPS papers on global optimality in neural networks, particularly Janzamin et al. (2016) and Haeffele & Vidal (2015). Unlike Janzamin et al., who require knowledge of the data-generating distribution to compute a score function tensor, and unlike Haeffele & Vidal, whose approach is "challenging to apply in practice," the authors claim their method is both theoretically sound and practically feasible.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Quality**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing rigorous proofs of global optimality and linear convergence for a specific class of neural networks. The proof strategy is well-structured, leveraging nonlinear spectral methods and fixed-point theory. The main theorems (Theorems 1 and 5) are well-motivated and technically sound.

2. **Novel Approach**: The use of nonlinear spectral methods inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices is innovative and provides a fresh perspective on neural network training optimization.

3. **Clear Convergence Guarantees**: The paper provides explicit conditions (checkable via spectral radius calculation) under which global optimality is guaranteed, which is a significant theoretical advance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Practical Significance**: The most serious weakness is the lack of convincing demonstration that the restricted network class (with nonnegative weights and specific activation functions) is useful in practice. The paper does not adequately address what expressiveness is achievable with this constraint compared to standard networks. The experiments on UCI datasets show competitive but not superior performance to SVMs and ReLU networks, and the paper fails to demonstrate where this method would be preferable.

2. **Inadequate Comparison**: The experimental comparisons are limited to SVMs and ReLU networks (which use different architectures with negative weights), omitting other neural network training methods and alternative algorithmic paradigms that could better contextualize the contribution.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Related Work**: The paper does not sufficiently contrast its approach with concurrent work on global optimality (particularly the work by Janzamin et al. and Haeffele & Vidal). The claim of being "the first practically feasible method" is not fully supported by a comprehensive comparison.

4. **Clarity and Presentation Issues**: Several sections (particularly Section 2) lack fluency, and there are multiple typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., "polyomial" on page 60, undefined R_{++}). The motivation for the modified objective function Φ and the ε term is not sufficiently explained. The paper would benefit from an informal explanation before stating Theorem 1.

5. **Experimental Limitations**: The experiments are too limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, without addressing computational resources or scalability. The paper claims "very fast convergence" but this is based on empirical observation without clear explanation of why this occurs.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network training by providing the first practically feasible method with global optimality guarantees for a non-trivial neural network class. The theoretical advances are significant and well-supported.

However, the practical significance is limited by the strong constraints on the network architecture (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions), and the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that this restricted class is useful for real-world problems. The experimental results do not clarify the model's applicability limits or show where this method would be preferable to standard approaches.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of expressiveness limitations, a more comprehensive comparison with related work, and improved clarity in the presentation of the method and its motivation.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a theoretically interesting advance with solid mathematical foundations, but its practical significance is limited by the restrictive network class and insufficient demonstration of real-world utility. I would recommend acceptance with the understanding that the paper's contribution is primarily theoretical rather than practical. However, the authors should address the clarity issues, provide a more thorough comparison with related work, and better clarify the expressiveness limitations of their approach in the final version.
