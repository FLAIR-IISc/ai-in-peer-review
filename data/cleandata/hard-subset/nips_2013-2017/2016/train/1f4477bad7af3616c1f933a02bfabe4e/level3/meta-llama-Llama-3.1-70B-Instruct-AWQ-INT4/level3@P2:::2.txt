This paper presents a novel approach to training feedforward neural networks using a nonlinear spectral method, which guarantees global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The authors restrict their attention to one and two hidden layer networks, and their method requires non-negative weights and a generalized polynomial activation function. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed method, including a characterization of the Lipschitz constant of the mapping and a proof of the uniqueness of the fixed point.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a practically feasible method for globally optimal training of neural networks. The authors demonstrate that their method can achieve competitive performance on several UCI datasets, and they provide a detailed analysis of the dependence of the spectral radius of matrix A on the model parameters.

However, there are several limitations and concerns with the proposed method. Firstly, the restrictive assumptions of non-negative weights and heavy regularization may not be realistic for many applications. Additionally, the use of a generalized polynomial activation function may not be suitable for all problems. The authors' claim that the targeted class retains sufficient expressive power is not convincingly supported by the experiments.

Furthermore, the empirical comparisons in the paper are unfair. The linear SVM baseline appears to be untuned, and no nonlinear SVM baselines (e.g., RBF or polynomial kernels) are used. The datasets used in the experiments are also too small in sample size and dimensionality to validate scalability or real-world applicability.

Another concern is that the bounds on the spectral radius of matrix A grow with the hidden-unit count, which raises scalability issues for high-dimensional data. Additionally, the optimality condition may not be satisfied for practical parameter settings, and checking is possible but infeasible without priors or a large parameter search. It is unclear if global optimality is due to the special objective, and it is recommended to test whether SGD can also empirically reach global optima on the datasets.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their method. However, the claims made in the paper are not entirely supported by the experiments, and the limitations of the method are not fully addressed.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its theoretical analysis. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential applications of the method.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their method. However, the use of nonlinear spectral methods is not entirely new, and the authors could have provided more context and comparison with existing methods.

In terms of significance, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of neural networks, and the authors' method has the potential to be applied to a wide range of problems. However, the limitations and concerns mentioned above need to be addressed before the method can be widely adopted.
