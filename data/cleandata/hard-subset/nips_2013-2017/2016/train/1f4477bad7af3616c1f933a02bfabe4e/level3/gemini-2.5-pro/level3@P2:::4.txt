### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel "nonlinear spectral method" for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, guaranteeing convergence to the global optimum at a linear rate. The method is applicable to networks with one or two hidden layers that use generalized polynomial activation functions (i.e., real powers α ≥ 1) and have non-negative weights. The theoretical guarantee hinges on a condition that can be checked a priori by computing the spectral radius of a small, well-defined non-negative matrix. This work positions itself as a practically feasible alternative to standard non-convex training methods like SGD, and as an improvement over recent works on globally optimal training [8, 11] which are either difficult to apply in practice or rely on estimating the unknown data-generating distribution. The authors demonstrate their approach on several UCI datasets, showing it is a viable proof of concept.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical contribution. The derivation of the algorithm and its convergence proof, based on reducing the optimization problem to finding a unique fixed point of a contraction mapping in a complete metric space (endowed with the Thompson metric), is rigorous and elegant. This is a complete and technically sound piece of theoretical work. However, the theoretical guarantees come at the cost of significant practical limitations. The constraints on the model—non-negative weights and data, and very specific polynomial activation functions—are severe and likely limit the expressive power of the network. The objective function is also non-standard, modified with an additional term summing the network outputs, which is required for the proof but whose impact on the resulting classifier is not fully explored.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The authors do a good job of outlining their proof strategy in Section 2 before delving into the technical details in Section 3. The main theorem is stated clearly, and the conditions for global optimality are explicit. The extension to two hidden layers is also presented straightforwardly. One minor point of confusion is the claim that the linear convergence rate implies "very fast convergence in only a few iterations," which seems somewhat at odds with the convergence plots in Figure 2, which span many "epochs." Clarifying what constitutes an epoch for the proposed method would be helpful.

**Originality:** The approach is highly original. Connecting the non-convex problem of training neural networks to the Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous maps is a novel and insightful contribution. This provides a fresh perspective on a fundamental problem in machine learning. While other works have sought global optimality guarantees, the specific spectral method and its theoretical underpinnings are new and distinct from prior tensor-based or matrix factorization approaches.

**Significance:** The paper is significant as it presents one of the first *practically demonstrated* algorithms for globally optimal neural network training. Moving from pure theory to implementation on real-world datasets is a crucial step for this research direction. The fact that the algorithm is parameter-free (no learning rate to tune) is also a valuable feature. However, the practical significance is currently hampered by the strong restrictions on the network architecture and data. The experimental results, which show performance that is competitive with but often worse than a standard SVM baseline, raise the question of whether the global optimum of this constrained model is a sufficiently good solution for general classification tasks. The ideas presented are thought-provoking, but it remains unclear if they can be extended to more general and powerful network architectures.

### Detailed Comments

1.  The primary contribution is the novel theoretical framework that guarantees global optimality. This is a beautiful result and a significant conceptual advance. The authors should be commended for bridging ideas from nonlinear eigenvalue problems and neural network optimization.

2.  The main concern is the practicality of the model. The non-negativity constraint on both weights and input features is very restrictive. While applicable to count-based data like word frequencies or image pixel intensities, it excludes a vast range of problems where features are centered or have negative values. It would be helpful if the authors could discuss the potential for relaxing this assumption in future work.

3.  The use of generalized polynomial activations with the constraint that each exponent `α_l` must be unique is another strong limitation. How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of these exponents? The paper mentions they are chosen from a random search, but provides little intuition on what makes a set of exponents "good."

4.  The experiments serve as a valuable proof of concept. However, the performance is not state-of-the-art. On several datasets (Iris, Banknote, Seeds), the method is clearly outperformed by SVM and sometimes by standard ReLU networks. This suggests that the constraints imposed to achieve optimality may be too limiting for the network to learn the target function effectively. Is it possible that the globally optimal solution for this constrained network is simply a worse classifier than a locally optimal solution for a less constrained network (like a ReLU network)? A discussion on this trade-off would strengthen the paper.

5.  The scalability of the approach is an important consideration. The condition for convergence, ρ(A) < 1, depends on the number of hidden units `n1`, among other parameters. The paper notes that ρ(A) increases with `n1`, limiting the experiments to low-dimensional datasets. While the authors suggest their bounds may not be tight, this appears to be a key hurdle for applying the method to larger, more complex problems that typically require wider layers.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly original and theoretically sound method for globally optimal training of a class of neural networks.
*   The connection to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory is a novel and significant conceptual contribution.
*   Provides a parameter-free algorithm with a checkable condition for convergence.
*   It is one of the first papers to demonstrate a globally optimal training algorithm on real-world datasets, advancing the field beyond pure theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The model is subject to very strong constraints (non-negative weights/data, specific non-standard activations) that severely limit its practical applicability and expressive power.
*   The empirical results are not compelling, with performance generally lagging behind a standard SVM baseline.
*   The significance of finding a global optimum for such a restricted model is not fully established, as this optimum may not correspond to a high-quality solution for the underlying learning problem.
