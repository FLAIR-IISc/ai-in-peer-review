This paper proposes a novel nonlinear spectral method for training generalized polynomial neural networks with one or two hidden layers. The authors claim that their method can achieve global optimality with a linear convergence rate under certain assumptions on the data and the network architecture. The main contribution of this paper is the development of a practically feasible algorithm that can guarantee global optimality for a non-trivial neural network model.

The authors' approach is based on a specific class of feedforward neural networks with non-negative weights and a generalized polynomial activation function. They introduce a modified objective function that includes a sum of loss and the negative total sum of the outputs, which allows them to apply nonlinear spectral methods to the optimization problem. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of their method, including a proof of convergence to the global optimum under certain conditions.

The paper also presents experimental results on several UCI datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors compare their approach with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and other baseline methods, showing that their method can achieve competitive performance and faster convergence.

However, there are several limitations and concerns with the proposed method. Firstly, the assumptions on the data and the network architecture are quite restrictive, requiring non-negative weights and a specific type of activation function. These assumptions may not be realistic for many practical applications. Secondly, the authors' claim that the targeted class retains sufficient expressive power is not convincingly supported by the experiments. The experimental results are limited to small-scale datasets, and it is unclear whether the method can be scaled up to larger datasets.

Furthermore, the empirical comparisons with other methods are not entirely fair. The linear SVM baseline appears to be untuned, and no nonlinear SVM baselines are used for comparison. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how to choose the parameters of their model, which may affect the performance of the method.

The paper also raises several questions about the optimality condition and the scalability of the method. The bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A grow with the hidden-unit count, which may lead to scalability issues for high-dimensional data. Moreover, the optimality condition may not be satisfied for practical parameter settings, and checking this condition is possible but infeasible without priors or a large parameter search.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting approach to training neural networks, it has several limitations and concerns that need to be addressed. The authors should provide more convincing evidence of the expressive power of their method, as well as more comprehensive experimental results on larger datasets. Additionally, they should clarify the choice of parameters and provide more insights into the optimality condition and scalability of the method.
