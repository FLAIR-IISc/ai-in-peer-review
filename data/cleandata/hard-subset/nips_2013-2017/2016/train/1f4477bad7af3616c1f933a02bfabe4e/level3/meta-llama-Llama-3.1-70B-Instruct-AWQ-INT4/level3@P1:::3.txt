This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks with a global optimality guarantee. The authors propose a class of neural networks with positive inputs, linear weights, and generalized polynomial activations, and show that these networks can be trained globally optimal with a linear convergence rate using a nonlinear spectral method. The method is based on the theory of (sub)-homogeneous nonlinear eigenproblems on convex cones, and is inspired by the Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a practically feasible algorithm for training neural networks with a global optimality guarantee. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on a 2D synthetic dataset and small UCI datasets. The results show that the algorithm performs well in these settings, and the authors argue that their method is the first potentially practical neural network with explicit optimality and convergence guarantees under simple, checkable preconditions.

The preconditions for the global optimality guarantee are relatively simple and depend only on the spectral radius of a nonnegative matrix formed from network parameters. The authors also extend their approach to deep networks, and provide a characterization of the Lipschitz constant of the mapping GΦ, which is used to derive conditions under which GΦ is a contraction.

However, there are some limitations to the approach. The requirement of nonnegative inputs and nonnegative linear-layer weights may limit the expressiveness of the model, despite the fact that inputs can be shifted. Additionally, each hidden layer uses a different activation function, which is unnatural and may restrict the applicability of the approach.

The spectral-radius < 1 constraint forces increasing some p-values and decreasing pw/pu, effectively shrinking linear weights and potentially collapsing deep networks to near-linear behavior. This raises concerns about the scalability of the approach to deep architectures, as satisfying the spectral-radius constraint may require reducing linear weights further as input/parameter dimensionality grows.

In conclusion, despite some concerns, the reviewer finds the work encouraging and recommends acceptance. The paper presents a novel and practically feasible approach to training neural networks with a global optimality guarantee, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the theoretical foundations of their method.
