This paper proposes a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks (GPNs) with a provable global optimum. The authors present a nonlinear spectral method that achieves linear convergence to the global optimum under certain conditions. The main contributions of the paper are the definition of a class of feedforward neural networks with a unique global optimizer and an algorithm that finds this global optimizer with linear convergence.

The paper is well-written, and the mathematical derivations are instructive. However, some parts of the derivations are unclear, and the reader may need to spend some time understanding the proofs. The authors provide a clear explanation of the main theorem and its implications, making it easier for non-experts to understand the significance of the results.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a provable global optimum for a class of neural networks. This is a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, where the optimization problem is often non-convex, and there is no guarantee of convergence to the global optimum. The authors' approach provides a new perspective on the optimization problem and offers a potential solution to the problem of convergence to the global optimum.

However, there are some concerns and questions that need to be addressed. Firstly, the shift from first-order derivative inequalities in Theorem 3 to second derivatives in Theorem 4 is not clearly justified. The authors should provide a clear explanation for this shift and its implications for the results.

Secondly, the treatment of the last layer is not consistent across the paper. The gradient with respect to u is not clearly defined, and it is not clear whether it is the gradient of vectorized u. The authors should clarify this point and ensure that the treatment of u is consistent throughout the paper.

Thirdly, some constructions, such as bounding u in a ball, are not clearly justified. The authors should provide a clear explanation for these constructions and their implications for the results.

Fourthly, the model maximizes the 1-norm of connection parameters in (2), which is not typical in neural networks. Typically, neural networks use weight decay to reduce norms. The authors should provide a clear explanation for this choice and its implications for the results.

Fifthly, the small epsilon in (2) appears to be only to force strictly positive gradients (V_++), which seems like a proof artifact lacking practical justification. The authors should provide a clear explanation for this choice and its implications for the results.

Finally, the requirement of non-negative data and strictly positive weights (no zeros) may make the model impractical and prone to unbounded growth. The authors should discuss the implications of these requirements and potential solutions to mitigate these issues.

In terms of experiments, the comparison to SVM is not entirely fair, as SVM has a global optimizer. The authors should compare their proposed neural network to common neural networks (e.g., with ReLU) to demonstrate its practical advantages.

However, there are some concerns and questions that need to be addressed to make the paper more complete and convincing.
