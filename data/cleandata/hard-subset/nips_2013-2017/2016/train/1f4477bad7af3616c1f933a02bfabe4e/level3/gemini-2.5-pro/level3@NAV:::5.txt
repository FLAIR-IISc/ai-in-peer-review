This paper addresses the challenging non-convex optimization problem in neural network training. The authors define a specific class of feedforward networks, termed "generalized polynomial neural networks," and propose a nonlinear spectral method for training. The main contribution is a theoretical proof that, under certain conditions, their algorithm converges linearly to a unique global optimizer. This work builds upon a line of research aiming for provably optimal training, such as the tensor-based methods of Janzamin et al., but distinguishes itself by presenting a practically feasible algorithm.

The primary strength of this paper is its significant theoretical contribution. Establishing a framework that guarantees a unique global optimum and linear convergence for a non-trivial neural network architecture is a novel and important result. The mathematical development, leveraging nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and the Banach fixed-point theorem, is elegant and instructive. The presented method is also parameter-free, removing the need for learning rate tuning.

However, the paper's practical significance is limited by several key weaknesses and unclear justifications. The model's core assumptions—requiring non-negative data and strictly positive weights—are highly restrictive and limit its applicability. Furthermore, the objective function in (2) is counter-intuitive. It maximizes a term related to the 1-norm of the weights, contradicting the standard practice of weight decay. The inclusion of a small `epsilon` appears to be a proof artifact to enforce strictly positive gradients, lacking a clear practical motivation. Several other constructions, such as bounding `u` in a ball, also lack sufficient justification.

For clarity, the authors should address a few points. Why does the analysis in Theorem 3 require the objective function to be twice differentiable (C2), suggesting reliance on second-order derivatives, when the core conditions in Lemma 4 are expressed as first-order inequalities? Could you also clarify if the gradient with respect to `u` is treated consistently as the gradient of its vectorized form?

Finally, while the comparison to SVM is logical, the experimental results do not demonstrate a clear practical advantage over standard ReLU networks. While theoretically elegant, the restrictive assumptions and unconventional objective design currently position this work more as a specialized theoretical result than a broadly applicable method. Addressing the aforementioned concerns would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.
