## Review of “Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods”

This paper presents a novel method for training a restricted class of feedforward neural networks – Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) – with provable global convergence guarantees using a nonlinear spectral method. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, under certain conditions (specifically non-negativity of weights), the optimization problem becomes tractable and can be solved efficiently. The authors provide a theoretical analysis establishing a linear convergence rate and validate their approach on several UCI datasets. 

The problem addressed is interesting and the theoretical advance of achieving global optimality is significant, particularly given the challenges of non-convex optimization in deep learning. The paper builds upon recent work attempting to provide guarantees for neural network training (e.g., [8, 11]) and offers a more practical approach than some existing methods, such as the tensor-based approach in [11] which relies on estimating the data generating measure. The introduction effectively contextualizes the work within the broader landscape of neural network optimization.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The most critical is the lack of a compelling argument for the practical utility of the restricted network class. While the authors claim the models are “rich enough,” a more thorough discussion of their expressiveness – what they *can* and *cannot* represent – is crucial.  The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate why these limitations are acceptable or under what circumstances GPNNs might be preferred over more flexible architectures. 

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is limited. Comparisons are primarily against SVMs and lack a broader comparison with other neural network training methods (e.g., Adam, SGD with momentum) and alternative algorithmic paradigms.  The results don’t clearly delineate the applicability limits of the model. Crucially, the paper lacks information regarding the computational resources (e.g., runtime, memory usage) required for each algorithm, hindering a fair comparison.

The presentation needs polishing. While the introduction is well-written, Section 2 and subsequent sections lack fluency and clarity. Numerous typos and notation issues (e.g., “polyomial” p60, “one CAN model” p67, undefined p1 and p2, R_{++}) detract from the readability. Technical clarifications are needed regarding the motivation for the function Φ and the ε term, and the claim of “quick convergence” should be explicitly qualified as an empirical observation. An informal explanation preceding Theorem 1 would also improve understanding.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel theoretical result guaranteeing global optimality; potential for applications where constraints are beneficial; first demonstration of a practical algorithm with such guarantees.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited practical demonstration of utility; insufficient comparison to existing methods; unclear expressiveness of the restricted network class; presentation issues and technical gaps.



**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the paper needs significant work to demonstrate its practical relevance and improve its clarity and rigor. Addressing the concerns regarding expressiveness, experimental evaluation, and presentation is essential for the paper to be considered a significant contribution to the field.
