- Presents a novel algorithm to train generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights to global optimality using nonlinear spectral theory and Perron-Frobenius tools.  
- Introduces a modified objective (with a summation term and regularization) and demonstrates competitive performance on several UCI datasets.  
- Builds on prior global-optimality work [8,11] but claims greater practicality by avoiding measure estimation and complex tensor conditions.  
- Key theoretical advantage: global optimality depends only on the spectral radius of a small matrix that can be checked a priori.  
- Main strength: provable global optimality for a non-trivial neural network model.  
- Practicality: algorithm appears computationally feasible and the spectral-radius condition is simple to verify.  
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous, detailed proofs based on nonlinear spectral theory.  
- Novel objective function design ensures gradient positivity and supports theoretical guarantees.  
- Initial empirical results on UCI datasets show competitive performance and faster convergence than SGD.  
- Provides a thorough analysis of algorithm dependence on model parameters and discusses limitations of the bounds.  
- Major weakness: strict non-negativity constraint on weights limits expressiveness and practical applicability.  
- Unclear benefit of generalized polynomial activations over standard activations; lacks convincing empirical comparisons.  
- Scalability concerns: experiments limited to low-dimensional datasets and no detailed complexity/memory analysis.  
- Parameter sensitivity: relies on careful tuning of several parameters, with only a random search strategy reported.  
- Limited experimental scope and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., wider ReLU or SVM benchmarks).  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically strong, practical-feeling algorithm with promising initial results and clear writing.  
- Against acceptance: practical applicability limited by non-negativity, unresolved scalability, and limited empirical validation.  
- Recommended revisions: expand discussion on non-negativity impact, analyze computational complexity/scalability, run more diverse experiments with systematic parameter search, and compare to standard-activation networks.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant theoretical contribution; recommended for acceptance if weaknesses are addressed (score 8/10).
