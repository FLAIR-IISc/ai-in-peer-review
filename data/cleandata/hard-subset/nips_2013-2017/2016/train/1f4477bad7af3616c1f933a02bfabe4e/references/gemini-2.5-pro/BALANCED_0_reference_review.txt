### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper introduces a novel "nonlinear spectral method" for training a specific class of neural networks, which the authors term Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks. The main contribution is a provable guarantee of convergence to the unique global optimum of the training objective. This is achieved by reformulating the optimization problem as finding the unique fixed point of a carefully constructed mapping on the cone of non-negative weights. The convergence guarantee holds under a checkable condition: the spectral radius of a small, explicitly defined matrix must be less than one. The theoretical foundation of the work is rooted in nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and the Banach fixed-point theorem applied in a space equipped with the Thompson metric.

The work is well-positioned within the existing literature on globally optimal neural network training. It explicitly contrasts its approach with prior tensor-based methods (Janzamin et al., 2015) and matrix factorization approaches (Haeffele and Vidal, 2017). Unlike the former, this method does not require knowledge or estimation of the data-generating density. Unlike the latter, it provides a practical iterative algorithm. The authors claim this is the first practically feasible algorithm for globally optimal training of a non-trivial neural network, a claim that is largely substantiated by the inclusion of experiments on real-world datasets, which were absent in the cited prior work.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical result. Providing a simple, a priori checkable condition (`ρ(A) < 1`) that guarantees convergence to the unique global optimum is a significant achievement in the non-convex landscape of neural network optimization. The linear convergence rate is also a strong theoretical property.

2.  **Novel Technical Approach:** The application of nonlinear spectral methods, Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous maps, and fixed-point theory with the Thompson metric to neural network training is highly original. The theoretical development in Section 3, which connects the optimization problem to a contraction mapping, is elegant and technically deep.

3.  **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed method translates directly into a concrete iterative algorithm (`(w_k+1, u_k+1) = G_Φ(w_k, u_k)`). A major advantage is that this algorithm is free of tuning parameters like a learning rate, which is a common challenge for gradient-based methods.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The "Proof Strategy" section provides an excellent high-level overview of the technical machinery, making the dense theoretical sections more accessible. The logic flows clearly from the general fixed-point theory (Section 3) to its specific application to the neural network model (Section 4).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Highly Restrictive Model and Data Assumptions:** The applicability of the method is severely limited by a confluence of strong constraints:
    *   **Non-negativity:** Both the input data and the network weights are required to be non-negative. This is a major limitation, as many datasets contain meaningful negative values or are standardized to have zero mean. While applicable to count-based data (e.g., images, word counts), it excludes a vast range of other problems.
    *   **Activation Function:** The use of "generalized polynomial" activations (`x^α` with `α_i ≥ 1` and all `α_i` distinct) is non-standard and excludes widely-used activations like ReLU, tanh, or sigmoid.
    *   **Modified Objective Function:** The objective function is not the standard loss function. It is a sum of the negative loss, the total sum of the network's outputs, and a small regularization term. The motivation for adding the sum of outputs is purely technical (to ensure a strictly positive gradient). The practical implications of optimizing this surrogate objective for the original classification task are not well explored, making it difficult to understand what problem is truly being solved.

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The condition for guaranteed convergence, `ρ(A) < 1`, depends directly on the number of hidden units (`n1`), the norm of the data (`ρ_x`), and other architectural parameters. As the authors note, `ρ(A)` is increasing with these parameters. The provided bounds to satisfy the condition (e.g., Equation 4) require the norm exponents `p_w, p_u` to grow rapidly with the network size (`K`, `n1`). This suggests the method may only be applicable to relatively small networks and low-dimensional data, which is corroborated by the choice of small UCI datasets for experiments.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** While it is commendable that the authors included experiments, the results are presented as a "proof of concept" and are not consistently state-of-the-art. The proposed NLSM models are competitive but are often outperformed by a standard SVM with an RBF kernel or even a simple ReLU network. This raises a critical question: is the guarantee of global optimality worth the trade-off in model flexibility and empirical performance? For problems where standard models perform very well (e.g., Iris, Banknote), the constrained NLSM struggles.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Justification of the Objective Function:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the modified objective function. Can the authors provide more intuition beyond the technical necessity? For example, does adding the sum of outputs (`Σ f_r(x)`) have a regularizing effect? Does it relate to a known probabilistic model or learning principle? This would help bridge the gap between the elegant theory and the practical machine learning task.

2.  **Addressing the Non-negativity Constraint:** The authors should discuss the non-negativity constraint in more detail. Is there a way to adapt the method for general real-valued data, for instance by decomposing features into positive and negative parts (`x = x_+ - x_-`) and doubling the input dimension? This would dramatically increase the method's potential impact.

3.  **Investigating the Tightness of the Bounds:** The authors correctly state that their bounds for the matrix `A` (and thus for `ρ(A)`) are likely conservative. It would be valuable to empirically investigate this. For instance, does the algorithm still converge in practice for architectures where the theoretical condition `ρ(A) < 1` is not met? If so, it would suggest the practical applicability of the method is wider than the current theory supports, which would be an important finding.

4.  **Guidance on Hyperparameter Selection:** The hyperparameters (`n1`, `α`, `ρ_w`, `ρ_u`, etc.) are chosen via random search. While this is a standard procedure, some guidance on how these parameters influence model behavior would be helpful. For example, how does the choice of exponents `α` affect the shape of the decision boundary?

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel, technically deep, and theoretically significant result: a practical algorithm for training a class of neural networks with a guarantee of convergence to the global optimum. The originality of the approach and the strength of the theoretical guarantees are high. However, the price for these guarantees is a set of very restrictive assumptions on the data, model architecture, and objective function, which limits its current practical applicability and leads to mixed empirical results.

The work is a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network optimization. It opens up a new and interesting direction of research based on nonlinear spectral methods. While its immediate practical impact may be limited, its theoretical novelty and rigor make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Highly original theoretical contribution connecting optimization to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory.
*   Provides a rare and valuable guarantee of global optimality for a non-trivial neural network.
*   The proposed algorithm is practical and free of learning rates.
*   The paper is clearly written and technically sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The model and data assumptions (non-negativity, specific activations) are very restrictive.
*   The non-standard objective function is not well-motivated from a machine learning perspective.
*   Scalability to larger networks and datasets is a major concern.
*   Empirical performance is not consistently superior to standard baselines.

**Decision:** I lean towards acceptance. The theoretical novelty and significance of providing a tractable algorithm for globally optimal training outweigh the current practical limitations. This is a foundational paper that could inspire future work on relaxing these strong assumptions.
