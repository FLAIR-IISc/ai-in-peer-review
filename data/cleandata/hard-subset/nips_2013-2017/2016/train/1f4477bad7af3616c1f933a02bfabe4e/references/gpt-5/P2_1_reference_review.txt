Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) to train a constrained class of feedforward neural networks globally optimally. The networks use nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and generalized polynomial activations with exponents αl ≥ 1. The training objective maximizes (i) minus cross-entropy plus (ii) the sum of class outputs over data, plus small positive linear terms in parameters (for strict positivity). The method constructs a multi-block nonlinear map GΦ from gradients, normalizes with p-norm constraints, and shows that under a computable contraction condition (spectral radius ρ(A) < 1 of a small nonnegative matrix depending on model hyperparameters), iterates converge linearly to a unique fixed point, which is the unique global maximizer of the constrained problem. The theory builds on Perron–Frobenius and its nonlinear/multihomogeneous extensions, the Thompson metric, and the Banach fixed point theorem. The framework is given for one hidden layer and extended to two. Empirically, on small UCI datasets, NLSM is competitive with ReLU nets trained by SGD and slightly worse than RBF SVMs, with fast convergence.

This fits into a line of work aiming at global training guarantees for neural models, e.g., tensor methods (Janzamin et al.) and convex relaxations/structured factorizations (Haeffele & Vidal). The key novelty is a practically checkable contraction condition and an implementable fixed-point iteration with linear rate, albeit for a restricted, monotone, nonnegative network class and a modified loss.

Quality:
– Theory: The statements are mathematically careful. The path from constrained optimization to fixed-point iteration is clear: (1) show interior maximizer in S++ using strict positivity of gradients (enforced by the added terms), (2) identify critical points with fixed points of GΦ, (3) show GΦ is a contraction on a product space under a weighted Thompson metric if ρ(A) < 1, yielding uniqueness and linear convergence. The dependence of ρ(A) on architecture and norms is explicit and conservative bounds are acknowledged. Extension to two layers mirrors the one-layer case.

– Assumptions/limitations: The guarantees hinge on strong restrictions: nonnegative data and weights; polynomial activations with distinct exponents; tight norm constraints; and a modified objective that adds the sum of logits. The added sum-of-outputs term is unusual for classification. Because softmax cross-entropy is shift-invariant in the logits, this term may be neutral in unconstrained settings, but here it plays a critical technical role (strict positivity and contraction), and it can bias the solution under the imposed constraints. The expressiveness is limited to monotone models; the necessity of distinct αl to avoid permutation-induced degeneracies is a nonstandard design burden. The ρ(A) < 1 condition becomes harder to satisfy as the number of units grows, potentially limiting capacity.

– Experiments: The empirical evaluation is modest (small, low-dimensional UCI datasets, random search over 100 configurations). Results are mixed: close to ReLU, generally shy of SVM. The main empirical asset is convergence speed. There is no ablation to quantify the effect of the extra output-sum term or of the positivity constraints. No larger or higher-dimensional datasets (e.g., MNIST, where inputs are nonnegative) are shown. No comparison to related nonnegative/monotone models (e.g., NMF-based classifiers) is provided.

Clarity:
The paper is generally structured and the main ideas are traceable. However, there are several presentation issues: the definition of the loss is incomplete in the current draft; some equations/figures appear truncated; references are poorly formatted and in places garbled; there are copy-edit artifacts (“th Conference…”). More intuition on why the added terms are needed and how they affect the classifier would help. It would also help to report typical values of ρ(A) encountered and demonstrate the tightness of bounds.

Originality:
The use of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and multihomogeneous contractions to obtain a globally convergent training algorithm for a (restricted) neural network class is novel and interesting. The explicit, computable contraction condition and linear convergence are attractive. The architectural and objective constraints, while severe, are different from prior globally optimal approaches and appear to make the method more implementable.

Significance:
The theoretical contribution is solid and could stimulate further work on globally convergent training under structural constraints. Practical significance is limited by the restrictive setting: monotone, nonnegative networks; modified objective; and contraction conditions that may preclude larger models. The current experiments do not yet demonstrate advantages in accuracy or scalability over strong baselines.

Pros:
- Clear, rigorous fixed-point framework with provable global optimality and linear rate.
- Checkable condition (ρ(A) < 1) that depends only on architecture/hyperparameters.
- Extension to two hidden layers in the same framework.
- Fast convergence empirically.

Cons:
- Strong constraints: nonnegative inputs/weights, monotone polynomial activations, added output-sum term.
- Potentially limited expressiveness; requirement of distinct α per unit.
- Contraction condition becomes harder with more units; conservative bounds.
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale; lacks ablations and comparisons to related constrained models.
- Clarity and presentation issues (missing definitions, reference formatting).

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Provide a complete definition of the loss and clarify how adding the sum of outputs affects the decision function under softmax. Include an ablation without this term.
- Report actual computed ρ(A) values and observed convergence factors; discuss the gap between conservative bounds and practice.
- Explore larger nonnegative datasets (e.g., MNIST) and higher-dimensional settings; analyze how often the contraction condition holds as model size grows.
- Compare to monotone/nonnegative baselines (e.g., NMF classifiers, lattice/monotone nets) and to polynomial networks without the extra term.
- Give guidance for choosing α, pw, pu, and radii beyond conservative formulas; analyze sensitivity.
- Improve references and fix typos/formatting.

Overall assessment:
A theoretically interesting and original contribution with rigorous guarantees for a constrained network class, but with significant practical restrictions and a limited empirical demonstration. As it stands, it meets the quality/originality bar on theory, is middling on clarity due to presentation issues, and is weak-to-moderate on significance. I would lean toward rejection unless the empirical and clarity issues can be substantially strengthened, but I view the theoretical ideas as valuable.
