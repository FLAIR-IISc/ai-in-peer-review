- Summary: Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) using Perron-Frobenius theory to globally optimize generalized polynomial neural nets with non-negative weights.  
- Strength: Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for global optimality under clear conditions (non-negativity and spectral radius constraint).  
- Strength: Novel application of nonlinear spectral/Perron-Frobenius methods to neural network optimization.  
- Strength: Gives a practical spectral-radius check to verify when global-optimality guarantees apply.  
- Strength: Shows linear convergence rate, improving over typical iterative gradient methods.  
- Strength: Experimental results on UCI datasets are competitive with SVM and ReLU networks.  
- Strength: Paper is well-written and logically organized.  
- Weakness: Non-negative weight constraint is a strong limitation that reduces expressiveness and may hurt performance.  
- Weakness: Scalability concerns: computing the spectral radius of A may be expensive for large networks.  
- Weakness: Derived bounds on p_u and p_w appear conservative, limiting applicability.  
- Weakness: Experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets and comparisons to ReLU are somewhat unbalanced.  
- Weakness: Practical impact of the added summation term in the objective is not thoroughly evaluated.  
- Weakness: Insufficient discussion on how to choose distinct α values in practice.  
- Quality/Clarity/Originality/Significance: Scores 7/10, 8/10, 8/10, 6/10 respectively.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound algorithm with clear convergence conditions and competitive empirical performance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Applicability limited by non-negativity, potential scalability issues, conservative parameter bounds, and limited experiments.  
- Overall assessment: Borderline accept — valuable theoretical contribution but practical applicability concerns remain.  
- Requested revisions: Discuss strategies to mitigate non-negativity constraint, analyze spectral-radius computational complexity/optimizations, study the objective modification's impact, and run experiments on larger/higher-dimensional datasets.
