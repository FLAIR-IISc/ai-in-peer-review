- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative inputs and weights.  
- Network uses hidden units computing powered linear responses with distinct exponents α_l ≥ 1 to avoid degeneracy.  
- Training objective is nonstandard: maximize average negative cross-entropy plus the sum of class outputs, with a small positive linear parameter term to ensure strict positivity of gradients.  
- Core algorithm is a fixed-point iteration GΦ based on normalized ψ_{p′} of gradients under L_p weight constraints.  
- Global existence/uniqueness and linear convergence follow from multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction in the Thompson metric.  
- The contraction/checkable condition reduces to computing the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small nonnegative matrix A determined by architecture and norm parameters.  
- Results are given for one hidden layer (A is K+1 dimensional) and extended to two hidden layers (A is K+2 dimensional).  
- Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy versus ReLU trained by SGD, somewhat worse than RBF-SVM, and much faster convergence on the modified objective.  
- Positioned against prior global-optimality theory (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.); differs by avoiding score functions/density estimation and providing a practical fixed-point method with a checkable contraction.  
- Conceptual ties to classical Perron–Frobenius theory, multiplicative NMF updates, and multi-homogeneous maps are noted.  
- Strength: novel application of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory to neural-network training with an explicit fixed-point map and linear-rate convergence under a spectral condition.  
- Strength: practical contraction check requiring no iterative tuning like learning rates.  
- Strength: framework extends beyond a single hidden layer with explicit constants and conditions.  
- Strength: empirical proof-of-concept demonstrates nontrivial decision boundaries and rapid convergence.  
- Weakness: “global optimality” applies to a heavily modified objective (sum-of-outputs + small linear term), not standard cross-entropy, with unclear statistical implications.  
- Weakness: strong modeling constraints (nonnegative data/weights, powered activations with distinct exponents, norm normalizations) may limit expressivity versus standard architectures.  
- Weakness: contraction bounds yielding ρ(A)<1 are conservative; no empirical study of how often ρ(A)<1 holds for useful architectures.  
- Weakness: limited experimental evaluation—small, low-dimensional datasets, no ablations isolating the sum-of-outputs term, no comparisons to related nonnegative/tensor/NMF models, and limited sensitivity analysis for α, p norms, and radius.  
- Clarity issues: loss L referenced but not explicitly written; ε term missing in displayed objective; notation overload (α used inconsistently); typos and missing references; some claims need fuller justification.  
- Originality: combining multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius fixed-point theory with NN training and explicit contraction analysis is original but needs better contextualization with monotone/positive networks and NMF literature.  
- Significance: theoretical contribution could stimulate further work in monotone/positive networks, but applicability is constrained and empirical evidence is too limited for strong practical impact.  
- Key requests for authors: clarify and display the precise loss (including ε), analyze how ∑_r f_r(x) interacts with softmax, formalize the distinct-α necessity, report empirical ρ(A) values and eigenvectors, and provide tighter/adaptive Lipschitz bounds.  
- Additional requests: include synthetic expressivity comparisons (ReLU/NMF), sensitivity analysis over α and norms, per-iteration complexity and memory vs SGD, discuss handling signed inputs and relaxing nonnegativity, and release code/hyperparameters for reproducibility.  
- Pros for acceptance: elegant rigorous theory, clear/checkable global optimality condition tied to architecture/norms, and first practical demonstration of globally convergent training in this constrained setting.  
- Cons for acceptance: differing objective with unclear statistical effects, restrictive constraints and conservative conditions limiting practicality, and limited small-scale experiments with insufficient ablations.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject — interesting theoretical contribution but needs stronger empirical validation and clearer treatment of the modified objective and practicality.
