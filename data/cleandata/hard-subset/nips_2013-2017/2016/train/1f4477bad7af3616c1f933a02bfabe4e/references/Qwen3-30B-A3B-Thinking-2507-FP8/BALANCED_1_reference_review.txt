

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel approach for globally optimal training of a specific class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, proving that their algorithm converges to the global optimum with linear convergence rate under conditions that can be verified by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix. The work builds on recent advances in global optimization of neural networks (e.g., Janzamin et al., 2015; Haeffele & Vidal, 2015) but claims to provide a more practically feasible algorithm than previous approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a rigorous convergence proof for a globally optimal training algorithm for a specific neural network class. The connection to nonlinear eigenvalue problems and Perron-Frobenius theory is well-motivated and novel for neural network optimization.

2. **Computational Feasibility**: The key insight that the global optimality condition can be checked without running the algorithm (via spectral radius calculation) is valuable and represents a practical advantage over prior work.

3. **Linear Convergence Rate**: The linear convergence rate demonstrated in Theorem 1 is a strong theoretical advantage over standard SGD, which typically exhibits sublinear convergence rates.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to proof strategy to experimental validation. The mathematical notation is clear and consistent.

## Weaknesses

1. **Strong Constraint on Weights**: The requirement for nonnegative weights significantly limits the model's expressiveness. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not sufficiently address how this constraint impacts the model's ability to learn complex patterns in real-world applications. This constraint makes the method inapplicable to standard neural networks used in practice.

2. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The experimental results are restricted to small UCI datasets, which do not demonstrate the method's utility on more challenging problems. The performance is competitive but not superior to standard methods (SVM, ReLU networks), and the authors don't convincingly demonstrate why global optimality translates to practical advantages.

3. **Lack of Comparison to Relevant Work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently compare its approach to recent work on global optimization of neural networks. For instance, it doesn't address how its method differs from the tensor-based approach of Janzamin et al. (2015) beyond the technical details of the algorithm.

4. **Parameter Selection Guidance**: The authors acknowledge they don't yet have a good understanding of how to choose parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) for good performance. This is a significant limitation for practical adoption, and the paper doesn't provide any principled guidance for parameter selection.

5. **Limited Scope of Application**: The method is restricted to generalized polynomial activations with real powers, which are not commonly used in practice. The paper doesn't clearly explain how this model class relates to standard neural network architectures.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization. The proof of global convergence with linear rate is rigorous and well-executed, and the connection to nonlinear spectral methods is novel. However, the practical significance is limited by the strong constraint on nonnegative weights, which significantly restricts the model's applicability to real-world problems.

The paper would be stronger if it:
1. Provided more convincing evidence of practical utility beyond small UCI datasets
2. Addressed the relationship between the nonnegative weights constraint and the model's expressiveness more thoroughly
3. Offered concrete guidance on parameter selection for real-world applications
4. More explicitly compared the method to existing approaches for global optimization of neural networks

While the theoretical results are solid, the paper falls short in demonstrating how this approach advances the state of the art in a way that addresses practical challenges in neural network training. The requirement for nonnegative weights makes it difficult to see how this method could be applied to standard neural network architectures used in practice.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution, but the strong constraint on weights and limited experimental validation prevent it from making a significant contribution to the field as it stands. The theoretical results are valuable for the optimization community, but the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate their relevance to neural network training as practiced in the field. With more thorough experimental validation on larger datasets, clearer discussion of the practical limitations, and better comparison to existing work on global optimization, this paper could be strengthened for consideration.
