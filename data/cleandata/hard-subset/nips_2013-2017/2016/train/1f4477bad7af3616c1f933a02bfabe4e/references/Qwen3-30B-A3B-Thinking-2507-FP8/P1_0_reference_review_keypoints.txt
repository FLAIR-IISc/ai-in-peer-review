- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of neural networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Guarantees convergence to the global optimum under conditions involving the spectral radius of a small matrix.  
- Provides rigorous proofs and linear convergence rates based on nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory.  
- Requires nonnegative weights and a modified objective, differing substantially from SGD.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance on several low-dimensional UCI datasets as a proof of concept.  
- Novel application of nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training, distinct from prior work.  
- Claims practical feasibility as the first algorithm achieving global optimality for neural networks (as asserted).  
- No hyperparameters to tune, offering a practical advantage over SGD.  
- Nonnegative weights constraint is highly restrictive and limits real-world applicability.  
- Experimental validation is limited to low-dimensional datasets and does not demonstrate scalability to high-dimensional tasks.  
- Theoretical bounds (e.g., ξ1, ξ2) are conservative, making the spectral radius condition difficult to satisfy in practice.  
- Key theory-to-practice connections are unclear and lack empirical substantiation (e.g., effective spectral radius claims).  
- The influence of activation exponents α on performance is not thoroughly explored or guided.  
- Paper does not convincingly show advantages over existing or more recent global optimization methods.  
- Reviewer recommends weak acceptance: strong theoretical contribution but limited practical significance without further experiments and comparisons.  
- Suggested improvements: broader experiments, comparison to recent methods, clearer trade-off discussion, and concrete guidance on choosing α and parameters.
