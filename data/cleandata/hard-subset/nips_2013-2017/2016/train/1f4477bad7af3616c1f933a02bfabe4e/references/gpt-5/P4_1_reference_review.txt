Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a globally convergent training algorithm for a restricted class of neural networks: generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative inputs, trained on a modified objective (negative cross-entropy plus the sum of outputs and an arbitrarily small positive linear term in the weights). The core idea is to recast training as finding the unique fixed point of a nonlinear, multi-homogeneous map GΦ derived from a normalized gradient update, and to prove contraction under the Thompson metric by bounding the Lipschitz constant through a small nonnegative matrix A. If the spectral radius ρ(A) < 1, the method yields a unique global maximizer in the positive orthant and a linear convergence rate. The approach extends to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees.

The work connects to and aims to address limitations of: (i) globally optimal methods relying on tensor decompositions (Janzamin et al.), which require score functions and nondegeneracy conditions often impractical in high dimensions; and (ii) matrix-factorization views with global optimality under specific regularization (Haeffele & Vidal), acknowledged as challenging to apply broadly. It also relates to polynomial networks (Livni et al.) and leverages nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous maps. The contribution is to provide a practically checkable global optimality condition (ρ(A) < 1) and a fast fixed-point algorithm without learning-rate tuning.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Theory: The fixed-point construction GΦ, the use of the Thompson metric, and the Banach fixed-point theorem are standard and correctly combined. The condition ρ(A) < 1 is explicit and checkable; bounds via Collatz–Wielandt and the construction of A are carefully laid out. The extension to two hidden layers is systematic.
- Assumptions: The guarantees hinge on nonnegativity of inputs and weights, distinct activation exponents αl ≥ 1, norm constraints on parameters, and the modified objective that includes a sum of outputs and an ε > 0 linear term to ensure strictly positive gradients on the cone. The need for distinct α to avoid degeneracy under uniqueness is a useful and clearly argued observation.
- Practicality: The paper asserts “no free parameter” in the algorithm (no step-size), yet many architectural and norm parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu, and their two-layer analogs) must be chosen, and pw, pu must be set large enough to satisfy ρ(A) < 1 (with conservative bounds). Thus, global convergence is parameter-dependent; in practice the condition may favor small networks due to growth of ρ(A) with width.
- Empirics: Proof-of-concept experiments on small UCI datasets show the method is competitive with ReLU and slightly below RBF-SVM on average; a figure demonstrates linear convergence and faster optimization than SGD on the modified objective. The empirical section is limited in scale and does not include ablations (e.g., effect of the added output-sum term or of nonnegativity constraints).

Clarity
- The high-level story, assumptions, and main theorems are stated clearly. The motivation for the additional terms in the objective (strict positivity of gradients) is given.
- Some notation is heavy and formatting is occasionally broken (e.g., the explicit form of the cross-entropy is omitted; some reference entries are incomplete). The function Ψ and the sets J, Jc are defined but could use more intuition. A concise worked example of computing A and checking ρ(A) < 1 for a small architecture would improve readability.

Originality
- Using nonlinear spectral methods and multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory to obtain global optimality and linear convergence for constrained neural networks is novel. The explicit, small-matrix spectral-radius condition is a distinctive and potentially impactful contribution relative to prior global methods requiring intractable quantities (e.g., score tensors).

Significance
- The theoretical contribution is significant within the niche of provable neural training under structural constraints, offering a practically checkable condition and a fast algorithm.
- The scope is restricted: nonnegative data and weights, polynomial activations with fixed, distinct exponents, norm constraints, and a modified training objective. Empirical evidence is limited to low-dimensional datasets, and scaling to larger architectures may be hampered by the ρ(A) condition. The method advances understanding of when global training is tractable, but its immediate applicability to mainstream deep learning remains limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, rigorous development of a globally convergent algorithm with a verifiable condition and linear rate.
- Novel application of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory to neural training, including a multi-layer extension.
- Practical diagnostic (ρ(A)) computable without running the algorithm.
- Proof-of-concept experiments and a speed/comparison plot illustrating convergence behavior.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong modeling restrictions (nonnegativity; fixed, distinct exponents; normalization constraints) and a modified objective that departs from standard training.
- The necessity of adding the sum-of-outputs term and ε > 0 linear term to ensure positivity changes the optimization target; no ablation shows its empirical impact.
- Guarantee depends on conservative bounds; ρ(A) grows with network size, potentially limiting scalability; no experiments on moderate/high-dimensional datasets.
- Clarity issues: missing explicit loss expression, some formatting and reference problems; limited guidance on choosing α and norm parameters beyond random search.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the added output-sum term and to the ε > 0 linear term? Does performance degrade as ε → 0? Provide ablations.
- Can α be learned or adapted (e.g., via alternating optimization) while preserving convergence guarantees?
- How does the method behave after monotone feature transforms to enforce nonnegativity (e.g., shifting/scaling)? Any guidance for data that are not naturally nonnegative?
- Provide empirical study of ρ(A) vs. network width/depth on real datasets and its tightness relative to observed convergence.
- Are biases allowed (e.g., via constant features)? Clarify expressivity implications under nonnegativity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a small worked example demonstrating the computation of A and checking ρ(A) < 1.
- Add ablations isolating the effect of each modeling constraint and the added objective terms.
- Improve clarity on the cross-entropy definition and complete/standardize references.
- Discuss computational complexity per iteration and wall-clock comparisons on larger datasets.

Overall, the paper offers a theoretically solid and original contribution with a practical, checkable global optimality condition, but with significant modeling restrictions and limited empirical validation.
