This paper presents a globally optimal training algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The core idea is to reformulate the training problem as a maximization problem on a convex cone, leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory to guarantee convergence to a global optimum under certain conditions. The authors demonstrate theoretical results for one and two hidden layer networks and provide preliminary experimental results on UCI datasets.

While the pursuit of globally optimal training for neural networks is a highly valuable direction, and the mathematical framework developed here is undeniably sophisticated, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to potentially overstated claims and a lack of thorough experimental validation. My assessment leans towards rejection due to the cumulative effect of these flaws.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of using nonlinear spectral methods and Perron-Frobenius theory to achieve global optimality is a genuinely novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper includes a significant amount of mathematical development, aiming to provide a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm.
*   **Guaranteed Optimality:** Under the stated conditions, the algorithm *guarantees* global optimality, a rare and desirable property.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Clumsiness:** The paper is replete with inconsistent notation. For example, the definition of *S+* and *S++* uses inequalities including equality, but the notation doesn't consistently adhere to this convention. The use of multiple definitions for the same concept (e.g., different definitions of ξ1, ξ2) creates confusion. The repeated use of "resp." is overly frequent and disrupts readability.
*   **Overly Conservative Bounds:** The authors acknowledge that their bounds on the spectral radius (ρ(A)) are likely conservative. However, they don’t adequately address the practical implications of this conservatism. Highly restrictive conditions for ensuring convergence (e.g. requiring the spectral radius to be less than 1) render the algorithm less applicable in practice. Equations (4) and (9), providing lower bounds on *p<sub>w</sub>*, *p<sub>u</sub>*, etc., appear incredibly stringent and unrealistic for many practical scenarios.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are meager. Testing on only a handful of low-dimensional UCI datasets provides insufficient evidence of the algorithm’s scalability or performance on more complex problems. The comparison to ReLU networks feels somewhat unfair, as the ReLU networks are allowed to leverage non-negativity (which is a constraint imposed by the authors' method). The claim of “competitive performance” is weakly supported. Figure 2 lacks proper axis labels and a clear explanation of the data presented. The discussion of the parameter tuning process is superficial.
*   **Unclear Justification for Objective Function Modification:** The rationale behind adding the sum of outputs to the objective function (Φ) is not entirely convincing. While explained, the justification feels ad-hoc and lacks deeper motivation.
*   **Proof Strategy Details are Deferred:** Several key proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, hindering a comprehensive assessment of the theoretical results. While moving details to supplementary material is acceptable, critical aspects of the proof (e.g., the Lipschitz constant derivation) should be presented within the main body.
*   **Overuse of Technical Jargon:** The paper frequently employs highly technical language without sufficient explanation or context, making it inaccessible to a broader audience.
*   **Choice of α:** While the discussion around the choice of α is insightful, it feels rather tacked on towards the end of Section 2 and doesn’t have adequate integration within the broader narrative of the paper.
* **Minor errors**: Equation (6) uses Ψαp,q and then mentions Ψαp′,q, seemingly a typo.
* **Formatting issues**: The figure with two plots isn't properly labeled and has an odd layout. The formatting of matrix A in Theorem 1 is difficult to read.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical novelty of the approach.
*   The guarantee of global optimality (under the stated conditions).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The numerous notational and presentational flaws.
*   The highly conservative convergence conditions.
*   The insufficient experimental validation.
*   The lack of a compelling demonstration of practical applicability.
*   The overall lack of clarity and accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the theoretical framework presented in this paper is intriguing, the cumulative impact of the identified flaws is substantial. The paper is marred by notational inconsistencies, overly conservative conditions, and a lack of compelling experimental evidence. The pursuit of global optimality is commendable, but the practical limitations of this approach, as currently presented, are significant. Therefore, I recommend **rejection** of this paper. A major revision addressing the concerns outlined above, with a significantly expanded experimental evaluation, would be necessary for reconsideration.
