

# Peer Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses the challenging problem of achieving global optimality in neural network training. The authors build upon recent work by Haeffele and Vidal [8] and Janzamin et al. [11] who provided theoretically grounded approaches to global optimality, but note that these methods require impractical tensor computations or unverifiable conditions. The authors propose a novel "nonlinear spectral method" that achieves global optimality under the restrictive but practical assumption of nonnegative weights and nonnegative data (common in count data like images and word frequencies). The approach leverages nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and transforms the optimization problem into a fixed point problem solvable via Banach fixed point theorem. This represents a significant theoretical contribution that makes global optimality practically achievable for a specific class of networks.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor**: The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by connecting neural network training to nonlinear spectral methods. The proof strategy using Banach fixed point theorem is elegant and well-executed, with clear exposition of the technical machinery needed (Thompson metric, Collatz-Wielandt formula, etc.). The authors successfully establish linear convergence guarantees to the global optimum.

2. **Practical Feasibility**: Unlike previous global optimization methods, this approach requires no hyperparameter tuning (no learning rate) and has a simple condition (spectral radius < 1) that can be checked before training begins. The paper demonstrates rapid convergence in experiments (see Figure 2), with convergence typically occurring in 10-20 iterations.

3. **Experimental Validation**: This is the first paper to demonstrate global optimality for a non-trivial neural network on real datasets (UCI). The results show competitive performance, particularly on Cancer, Haberman, and Pima datasets where the two-hidden-layer variant outperforms all baselines.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-structured, with clear theorems, proofs, and a helpful algorithm description. The connection between the optimization problem and fixed point theory is particularly well-explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Restrictive Assumptions**: The requirement for nonnegative weights and data severely limits applicability. While the authors cite count data as a motivating example, most real-world datasets (including standard benchmarks like MNIST and CIFAR) contain negative features. The paper fails to address how to handle such cases, making the method seem impractical for most real applications.

2. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The experiments are restricted to low-dimensional UCI datasets. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but the paper doesn't discuss how the method would scale to high-dimensional data (where neural networks are most commonly applied). The spectral radius bound (Equation 4) grows with dimension, suggesting potential scalability issues.

3. **Parameter Selection Strategy**: The authors admit they lack a principled approach for selecting key parameters (α, n1, etc.). Their method of randomly sampling from a limited parameter space (2-20 hidden units, α ∈ [1,4]) is not theoretically justified and would be impractical for large-scale applications.

4. **Comparative Analysis**: The comparison with ReLU networks is limited because ReLU networks allow negative weights while the authors' method does not. This fundamental difference in model capacity isn't adequately discussed, making the performance comparison less informative.

5. **Theoretical Bounds**: The bounds on the spectral radius (Equations 4 and 9) are quite conservative. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't provide empirical evidence that the actual spectral radius is significantly smaller in practice, which would strengthen the practical relevance.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the understanding of global optimization in neural networks. The mathematical framework is sound, the proofs are rigorous, and the experimental results demonstrate the method's viability for a specific class of problems. However, the restrictive assumptions and limited experimental scope significantly reduce the paper's impact and applicability to real-world problems.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) Discussing extensions to handle negative data (e.g., via data transformation or alternative formulations)
2) Providing more principled guidance for parameter selection
3) Demonstrating performance on higher-dimensional datasets
4) Better explaining the trade-offs between the nonnegative constraint and model expressiveness

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to global optimality that is practically feasible for a specific class of networks. While the restrictive assumptions limit the method's applicability, the theoretical contribution is significant and the experimental validation on UCI datasets is valuable as the first such demonstration. The paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the scope of applicability.

This work advances the state of the art in understanding neural network optimization by providing a practically implementable algorithm for global optimality under specific conditions. For the NIPS community, it offers a new theoretical framework that connects neural network training to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory, with potential for future extensions to more general settings. The paper meets NIPS's standards for technical quality, novelty, and significance within its specific domain.
