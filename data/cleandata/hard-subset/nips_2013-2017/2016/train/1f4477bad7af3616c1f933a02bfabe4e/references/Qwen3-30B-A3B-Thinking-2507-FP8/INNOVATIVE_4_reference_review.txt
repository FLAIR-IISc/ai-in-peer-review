

**Review**

This paper introduces a novel approach to globally optimal training of a specific class of neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors propose a globally optimal algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights, which requires checking the spectral radius of a small matrix to guarantee convergence. This represents a significant theoretical advance in understanding neural network training, as it bridges the gap between theoretical global optimality guarantees and practical algorithms—addressing the "challenging to apply in practice" limitation noted by Haeffele and Vidal [8].

The core novelty lies in connecting neural network training to nonlinear spectral problems on convex cones. The authors demonstrate that under their nonnegativity constraints and modified objective function (adding the sum of outputs), their method achieves global optimality with linear convergence rate. This is a major theoretical contribution compared to previous work on global optimality [8, 11], which either lacked practical applicability or required impractical assumptions (e.g., knowledge of data-generating measures).

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated and theoretically rigorous. The proof strategy—using Banach fixed point theorem on Thompson metric spaces—provides clear insights into why their approach works. The authors carefully address the necessity of their constraints (e.g., distinct activation powers α to avoid trivial classifiers) and provide explicit conditions for global optimality (ρ(A) < 1), which can be checked without running the algorithm. The linear convergence rate and absence of hyperparameters (unlike SGD) are particularly compelling practical advantages.

The experimental results serve as a valuable proof of concept, showing competitive performance on UCI datasets compared to ReLU networks and SVMs. While the nonnegativity constraint is restrictive, the authors demonstrate that their model can still create complex decision boundaries (Figure 1) and achieve good results on several datasets (Table 1). The comparison with SGD (Figure 2) vividly illustrates the linear convergence advantage of their method.

The main limitations are the nonnegativity constraint on weights (which limits applicability to standard neural network settings) and the restriction to low-dimensional datasets due to conservative theoretical bounds. However, these limitations are acknowledged by the authors, and the paper explicitly positions itself as a first step toward practical global optimality. The authors note that their bounds for the spectral radius are "very conservative," suggesting room for improvement in future work.

What makes this paper stand out is its bold theoretical direction rather than incremental improvements. It successfully moves beyond the "global optimality" literature that has been largely theoretical [8, 11] to provide a practically feasible algorithm with clear conditions for global optimality. This represents a significant conceptual leap that could inspire future work on optimizing neural networks.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and thoroughly developed. The theoretical contribution is substantial, and the experimental validation, while limited to low-dimensional datasets, is sufficient for a proof of concept. The authors have carefully considered both strengths and limitations, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical contributions, I find this paper exceptionally strong. It introduces a fundamentally new approach to neural network optimization with rigorous theoretical guarantees and practical feasibility—a rare combination in the field. The constraints they impose are necessary for their theoretical framework, and they demonstrate that their model remains expressive enough for meaningful classification tasks.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a significant conceptual advance that opens a new research direction in neural network optimization, and its theoretical contributions outweigh the limitations of the current experimental scope. This work has the potential to influence future research on understanding and optimizing neural networks.
