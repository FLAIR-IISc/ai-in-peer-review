- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks under specific constraints (nonnegative weights, modified objective).  
- Leverages Perron-Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous problems to derive conditions ensuring linear convergence of a fixed-point iteration.  
- Main theorem (Theorem 1) gives concrete, checkable conditions for global convergence via the spectral radius of a small matrix.  
- Proof strategy combines fixed-point theory, Banach fixed point theorem, and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and is technically sound.  
- Experimental results on UCI datasets show competitive performance compared to SVM and ReLU networks while providing global optimality guarantees.  
- Restrictive nonnegativity assumption on weights significantly limits applicability to standard architectures and may reduce representational capacity.  
- Paper does not sufficiently explain the motivation for adding the "sum of outputs" term to the objective.  
- Lacks guidance on choosing practical hyperparameters (α, ρw, ρu) and how they affect performance.  
- Experimental section fails to analyze how the spectral radius condition (ρ(A) < 1) impacts training speed and practical performance.  
- Originality: novel connection between neural network training and nonlinear spectral methods; spectral-radius-based global convergence is a fresh contribution.  
- Claim of being the "first practically feasible" global-optimality algorithm is insufficiently justified relative to related work (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.).  
- Practical significance is limited by nonnegativity constraint and unclear scalability to larger datasets or deeper networks.  
- Strengths: guaranteed global convergence, checkable conditions, careful theoretical development, and competitive small-scale experiments.  
- Weaknesses: restrictive assumptions, insufficient practical guidance and comparisons, limited experimental advantage, and no scalability analysis.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject — strong theoretical contribution but limited practical impact and insufficient empirical justification for claims of practical feasibility.
