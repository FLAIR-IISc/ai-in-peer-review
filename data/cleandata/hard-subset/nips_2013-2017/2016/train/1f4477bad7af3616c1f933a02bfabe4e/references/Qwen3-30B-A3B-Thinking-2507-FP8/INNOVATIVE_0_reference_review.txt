

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a novel approach to achieving global optimality in neural network training through nonlinear spectral methods. The authors introduce a globally optimal training algorithm for a specific class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and a modified objective function. Their key insight is to leverage Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems to establish a globally convergent method with linear convergence rates.

The paper's main contribution is a theoretically grounded framework that guarantees global optimality under specific conditions. The authors prove that for their constrained neural network model (with nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations with distinct powers, and a modified objective function), the global optimum can be achieved by checking the spectral radius of a small matrix. This is a significant departure from previous work on global optimality (e.g., Janzamin et al. [11], Haeffele and Vidal [8]), which either required impractical assumptions or lacked concrete implementation.

The paper is highly original in its approach. The use of nonlinear spectral methods to solve the neural network training problem represents a bold new direction in the field. While prior work has explored global optimality for neural networks, this is the first method that provides a practically feasible algorithm with theoretical guarantees that can be verified without running the algorithm. The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous literature, noting that their approach is "challenging to apply in practice" for prior methods.

The theoretical contributions are well-developed, with rigorous proofs of convergence rates and uniqueness of the global optimum. The authors provide a complete proof strategy based on fixed point theory and the Banach fixed point theorem, which is elegantly applied to their specific problem. The paper's main theorem (Theorem 1) is particularly strong, showing that the global optimum can be found through a simple iterative process with linear convergence.

The experimental results, while limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, are significant because they represent the first globally optimal neural network training method to achieve competitive results on real data. The authors demonstrate that their method (NLSM1 and NLSM2) is competitive with ReLU networks and SVMs, with notable performance on several datasets. The comparison with SGD shows that their method converges much faster, with a clear linear convergence rate, which aligns with their theoretical guarantees.

I find the paper's limitations to be well-acknowledged by the authors, including the nonnegative weights constraint and the need for distinct activation powers. The paper explicitly states that the nonnegative weights requirement is "a strong constraint," but argues it is necessary for their theoretical approach. The authors also note that their method is currently limited to low-dimensional datasets due to the conservative bounds on the spectral radius.

The paper's main weakness is the restrictive nonnegative weights constraint, which limits its applicability to real-world problems where negative weights are common. However, this limitation is a necessary trade-off for their theoretical guarantees, and the authors are transparent about it. The paper also doesn't fully address how to choose the optimal parameters (n1, α, etc.) for new applications, but this is a reasonable limitation for a proof-of-concept paper.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper highly compelling. It introduces a genuinely novel direction in neural network training with strong theoretical guarantees, and demonstrates that global optimality can be achieved in practice for a non-trivial neural network model. This represents a significant step forward from prior work on global optimality, which lacked practical implementation or real dataset validation.

The paper's significance lies in opening up a new research direction for globally optimal neural network training. While the constraints are restrictive, the framework provides a foundation for future work that could potentially relax these constraints. The authors have made a bold claim with a theoretically sound approach that is well-motivated and rigorously proven.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of neural network training. The theoretical framework is elegant, the proofs are rigorous, and the experimental results demonstrate that the approach is practically viable. While the nonnegative weights constraint limits immediate applicability, this is a necessary consequence of the authors' theoretical approach, and the paper clearly acknowledges this limitation.

I believe this paper should be accepted as it makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a novel approach to global optimization in neural networks with verifiable theoretical guarantees. The authors have demonstrated that global optimality is achievable in practice for a specific class of networks, which is a major advancement beyond previous theoretical work.

**Strengths:**
- Highly novel approach to global neural network training
- Strong theoretical guarantees with verifiable conditions
- First globally optimal method achieving competitive results on real datasets
- Linear convergence rate with practical implementation
- Clear differentiation from prior work on global optimality

**Weaknesses:**
- Nonnegative weights constraint limits applicability
- Limited experimental validation to low-dimensional datasets
- Modified objective function lacks strong motivation
- Parameter selection for new applications not fully addressed

This paper represents a bold step forward in understanding and solving the global optimization problem in neural networks, and I believe it would be a valuable contribution to NIPS.
