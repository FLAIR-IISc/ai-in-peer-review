- Presents a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights, offering linear convergence and competitive UCI results.  
- Positions work relative to Haeffele & Vidal (2016) and Janzamin et al. (2016), arguing it avoids practical/data-dependent drawbacks of prior methods.  
- Leverages Perron-Frobenius theory and nonlinear spectral methods as a solid theoretical foundation.  
- Avoids hyperparameter tuning (e.g., learning rates) common in SGD.  
- Provides a provable global optimality guarantee and a linear convergence rate.  
- Demonstrates empirical competitiveness on several UCI datasets.  
- Clearly explains non-negativity constraint and need for distinct α values per hidden unit.  
- Major limitation: requires non-negative weights and excludes ReLU, reducing model expressivity and generality.  
- Theoretical conditions (spectral radius bounds) appear conservative, potentially excluding many practical configurations.  
- Scalability concerns: computing the matrix A and its spectral radius may be costly as hidden units scale; analysis is insufficient.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited to small UCI datasets; lacks experiments on larger/complex tasks and broader baselines.  
- The added summation term in the objective (to ensure gradient positivity) needs clearer, more intuitive motivation.  
- Key proofs are relegated to supplementary material, making it hard to fully assess correctness and rigor.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically sound method addressing a challenging problem with provable guarantees and feasible empirical results.  
- Against acceptance: strong model constraints, conservative bounds, scalability doubts, limited empirical validation, and heavy reliance on supplementary proofs.  
- Overall assessment: interesting and technically sound advance with significant theoretical contributions but practical and evaluation limitations.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Revision request 1: provide a detailed computational complexity and scalability analysis as hidden units increase.  
- Revision request 2: investigate and provide tighter spectral-radius bounds to broaden applicability.  
- Revision request 3: expand experiments to larger, more challenging datasets and compare to stronger baselines.  
- Revision request 4: offer a clearer, intuitive explanation for the added objective term (sum of outputs).  
- Revision request 5: include key lemmas or proof sketches in the main text to aid verification.
