

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a "nonlinear spectral method" for training neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors claim to provide the first practically feasible algorithm for achieving global optimality in neural network training, with linear convergence rate, no hyperparameter tuning, and competitive performance on UCI datasets. The approach requires nonnegative weights and nonnegative data, and imposes a modified objective function.

The paper builds on recent work on global optimality in neural networks [8, 11], but claims their method is more practical by requiring only nonnegativity (which they claim holds for count data like images and word frequencies). The theoretical contribution involves proving that a nonlinear spectral mapping converges linearly to the global optimum under certain conditions on the spectral radius of a matrix derived from the network architecture.

**Strengths:**
The paper is mathematically rigorous with well-structured proofs, and the theoretical framework is carefully developed. The authors clearly identify the conditions for global optimality and provide convergence guarantees. The approach is novel in its application of nonlinear spectral methods to neural networks, and the linear convergence rate is theoretically interesting.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims to be "the first practically feasible algorithm" for global optimality, but fails to adequately compare with prior work [8, 11] that also claims global optimality guarantees. The authors acknowledge that Haeffele & Vidal's approach is "challenging to apply in practice," but don't convincingly demonstrate that their method is more practical. The paper doesn't provide evidence that their approach is actually more feasible than existing methods in real-world applications.

2. **Unrealistic assumptions**: The requirement for nonnegative weights and nonnegative data severely limits applicability. Most real-world datasets (including standard image datasets like CIFAR-10 or MNIST) don't satisfy these constraints. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't convincingly argue why negative weights would be suboptimal in general. The claim that "nonnegativity of the weights is a strong constraint, but one can model quite complex decision boundaries" is not sufficiently supported by evidence.

3. **Limited experimental validation**: The experiments are restricted to low-dimensional UCI datasets, which are not representative of modern deep learning challenges. The comparison to standard methods is minimal - the authors compare to ReLU networks and SVMs but don't provide a thorough comparison to standard SGD with careful hyperparameter tuning (which is the standard practice in the field). The paper states their method "achieves competitive performance" but doesn't demonstrate clear advantages over standard methods.

4. **Questionable practicality**: The claim that "there is no free parameter in the algorithm" is misleading. The authors must carefully choose parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) and compute bounds to ensure ρ(A) < 1. This requires significant effort to set up, contrary to their claim. The paper states they "choose the parameters of our model out of 100 randomly generated combinations" based on cross-validation, which is essentially hyperparameter tuning - contradicting their claim of no hyperparameters.

5. **Lack of theoretical justification for the modified objective**: The paper adds a term that sums all outputs to ensure the gradient is strictly positive. This is a non-standard modification to the objective function, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient theoretical justification for why this modification is necessary or why it doesn't adversely affect performance.

**Critical assessment:**
The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but the claims of practical feasibility are unsupported by evidence. The assumptions required (nonnegative weights and data) are so restrictive that the method would not be applicable to most real-world neural network tasks. The experimental validation is insufficient to demonstrate practical utility, and the comparison to standard methods is inadequate.

The authors cite [8, 11] as prior work on global optimality, but fail to address the core issue: both these papers also claim global optimality, and the authors don't convincingly argue why their approach is more practical than these existing methods. The paper's contribution appears to be a more constrained version of existing approaches, not a significant improvement.

**Recommendation:**
The paper has theoretical interest but lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of practical feasibility and competitive performance. The experimental validation is limited, the assumptions are unrealistically restrictive, and the claims of "no hyperparameters" are misleading. I cannot recommend acceptance at NIPS as the evidence provided does not adequately support the authors' claims about practical applicability.

**Pro-con acceptance summary:**
Pro: Mathematically rigorous, novel theoretical approach, clear convergence guarantees.
Con: Unrealistic assumptions (nonnegative weights/data), limited experimental validation (only UCI datasets), overstated claims about practicality, misleading claims about no hyperparameters.

The paper would benefit from more extensive experimental validation on larger, more challenging datasets, a thorough comparison to existing global optimality methods (including [8, 11]), and a clearer justification for the restrictive assumptions. Without these improvements, the paper does not demonstrate sufficient practical relevance to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
