

# Peer Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel method for achieving globally optimal training of neural networks under specific constraints. The authors introduce a "nonlinear spectral method" inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices, which guarantees global convergence to the optimum under conditions that can be verified before training (via the spectral radius of a small matrix). The key innovation is that the method requires nonnegative weights and a modified objective function (adding a term that sums all outputs), and uses generalized polynomial activation functions with exponents α_i ≥ 1. The authors prove that under certain conditions, their algorithm converges linearly to the global optimum, and demonstrate competitive performance on UCI datasets.

This work relates to previous efforts in globally optimal neural network training (e.g., [8, 11]), but addresses their limitations: Haeffele and Vidal's method [8] is "challenging to apply in practice," while Janzamin et al.'s tensor approach [11] requires knowledge of the data-generating distribution. The authors claim their method is the first "practically feasible algorithm" for achieving global optimality in non-trivial neural network models.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with a complete proof strategy. The main theorem (Theorem 1) provides clear conditions for global optimality and linear convergence, with a well-structured proof that builds on nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach and provide detailed bounds for the spectral radius condition.

However, the practical applicability of the method is limited by the strong constraints:
1. Nonnegative weights constraint (which significantly restricts the expressiveness of the network)
2. Requirement that α_i ≠ α_j for all i ≠ j (to avoid trivial classifiers)
3. The conservative theoretical bounds that may require very specific parameter choices

The experimental section shows competitive results on UCI datasets, which is a strength given that previous globally optimal methods [8, 11] didn't include real dataset results. However, the experiments are limited to low-dimensional datasets, and the authors don't provide a clear path for scaling to high-dimensional problems like image classification.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The proof strategy is well-explained, and the main theorem is stated precisely with all necessary conditions. The notation is consistent throughout the paper.

Some aspects could be clarified:
- The motivation for the nonnegative weights constraint could be better explained. Why is this constraint necessary for the spectral method, and why is it acceptable for practical applications?
- The choice of α (the exponents in the activation functions) is somewhat arbitrary in the experiments. The paper doesn't provide a principled way to choose α values for different datasets.
- The connection between the theoretical bounds and practical implementation could be strengthened. For example, the paper states that "the bounds for the matrix A are very conservative" but doesn't provide guidance on how to choose parameters to achieve better bounds.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network optimization. The application of nonlinear spectral methods from Perron-Frobenius theory to neural network training is novel and represents a fresh perspective on the optimization problem.

The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous global optimization approaches [8, 11], noting that their method is the first that is both theoretically grounded and practically feasible. However, the constraint of nonnegative weights is not entirely novel (nonnegative matrix factorization is a well-studied area), and the generalized polynomial activation functions are a slight extension of existing work [15].

## Significance

The paper's significance lies in providing a theoretically grounded globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks, which addresses a long-standing gap in the theoretical understanding of neural network training. This is a significant contribution to the field, particularly since previous globally optimal methods were either impractical or required knowledge of the data-generating distribution.

The experimental results showing competitive performance on UCI datasets are valuable as they demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical contribution. However, the limitation to low-dimensional datasets and the strong constraints on the network architecture (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions) limit the broader impact of the work.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Provides the first theoretically grounded globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks that is practically feasible
- Proves linear convergence rate under verifiable conditions
- Demonstrates competitive performance on real datasets (a key strength missing in previous globally optimal approaches)
- Well-structured theoretical development with clear proof strategy

**Weaknesses:**
- Strong constraints (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions) limit practical applicability
- Theoretical bounds are conservative, potentially limiting the range of problems the method can solve
- Experiments limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets without discussion of scalability
- No principled method for choosing key parameters (α, pw, pu) for different datasets
- The requirement that α_i ≠ α_j for all i ≠ j is arbitrary and may not generalize well

## Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper provides a theoretically sound proof of global optimality under specific conditions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
- The method has a linear convergence rate and the conditions for global optimality can be verified before training.
- The experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate practical relevance, which is rare in globally optimal neural network training papers.
- The paper is well-written and clearly structured.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The strong constraints (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions) significantly limit the practical applicability of the method.
- The theoretical bounds are too conservative for practical use, requiring very specific parameter choices.
- The experiments are limited to low-dimensional datasets, making it unclear how the method would scale to more challenging problems.
- The paper doesn't provide a clear path for extending the method to more standard neural network architectures.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization, providing the first practically feasible globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks. While the constraints (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions) limit the practical applicability, the authors clearly acknowledge these limitations and the experimental results demonstrate that the method can still achieve competitive performance on real datasets.

The main weakness is that the theoretical bounds are too conservative, which may limit the range of problems the method can solve. I recommend the authors add a discussion of how to choose parameters to achieve tighter bounds, or provide more guidance on practical implementation. The paper would also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the method and potential paths for future work to address these limitations.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of neural network optimization, and the experimental results demonstrate its practical relevance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of neural network theory and should be accepted for publication at NIPS.
