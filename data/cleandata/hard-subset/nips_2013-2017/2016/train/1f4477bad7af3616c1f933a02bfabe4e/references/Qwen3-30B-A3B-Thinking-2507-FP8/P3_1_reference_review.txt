

# Peer Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes a globally optimal training algorithm for a specific class of neural networks: generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative weights and real-valued exponents (α ≥ 1). The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices, which guarantees global convergence to the optimal solution under certain conditions. The key innovation is the use of a modified objective function (adding the negative sum of outputs) and constraints on weights to enable the application of nonlinear spectral methods. The paper proves linear convergence with a condition that can be verified by checking the spectral radius of a small matrix.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a genuine theoretical contribution by establishing global optimality guarantees for a non-trivial neural network model. The proof strategy, leveraging fixed-point theory on the positive orthant, is elegant and well-executed. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems (citing [7]) is insightful.

2. **Clear Convergence Guarantee**: The authors provide a concrete condition (ρ(A) < 1) that can be checked before training, which is a significant improvement over previous approaches [8, 11] that were "challenging to apply in practice." The linear convergence rate is also a valuable theoretical contribution.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The paper is the first to demonstrate globally optimal training on real datasets (UCI), which is a notable contribution to the field. The comparison with SGD and ReLU networks provides useful context.

4. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear separation of theoretical development and experimental results. The supplementary material for additional proofs is a good practice.

## Weaknesses

1. **Highly Restrictive Assumptions**: The requirement for nonnegative weights is a major limitation. While the paper acknowledges this, it doesn't convincingly justify why this constraint is meaningful for real-world problems. Standard neural networks (with ReLU activations, for example) can't be represented under these constraints, making the method applicable to a very narrow class of problems.

2. **Lack of Comparative Empirical Analysis**: The experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, which are too simple to demonstrate the method's advantages. The paper doesn't compare with other globally optimal approaches [8, 11] beyond stating they're impractical. The comparison with ReLU networks is unfair because ReLU networks can have negative weights, while the proposed method cannot.

3. **Practical Implementation Challenges**: The condition ρ(A) < 1 requires careful tuning of multiple parameters (pw, pu, ρw, ρu), and the authors acknowledge their bounds are conservative. The requirement to check this condition before training is a significant barrier to practical adoption. The paper doesn't provide guidance on how to choose these parameters for new problems.

4. **Questionable Motivation for Modified Objective**: The paper adds the negative sum of outputs to the objective function to ensure the gradient is strictly positive, but it doesn't explain why this modification doesn't substantially alter the learning objective or how it affects generalization. The authors acknowledge the model is "non-standard" but don't address this adequately.

5. **Limited Scope of Results**: While the paper claims to be "the first practically feasible algorithm to achieve global optimality for a non-trivial neural network model," the "non-trivial" networks they consider are severely restricted by the nonnegativity constraint and specific activation functions. The extension to two hidden layers (Section 4.1) feels like a minor technical extension rather than a meaningful advancement.

## Critical Analysis

The paper's main contribution is theoretical, but its practical relevance is questionable due to the restrictive assumptions. The nonnegativity constraint is particularly problematic: it eliminates the expressive power of standard neural networks that rely on negative weights to create complex decision boundaries. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address why it's justified for real-world applications.

The experimental results are underwhelming: the method achieves competitive but not superior performance compared to SVM and ReLU networks. The paper doesn't demonstrate cases where the global optimality guarantee leads to significantly better results, which is the main motivation for the approach. For instance, in the Cancer dataset (Table 1), NLSM1 and NLSM2 achieve 96.4% accuracy, while SVM achieves 95.7% - a negligible difference.

The theoretical contribution is strong within its constrained framework, but the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why this framework is meaningful for practical machine learning. The connection to "count data" (images, word frequencies) is tenuous, as these typically don't require nonnegative weights to be effective.

The condition ρ(A) < 1 is theoretically interesting, but the conservative bounds (Equation 4) suggest that in practice, the spectral radius is likely to be close to 1, resulting in slow convergence. The paper's claim of "very fast convergence in only a few iterations" seems optimistic given the conservative bounds.

## Recommendations

1. **Clarify Practical Relevance**: The authors should better justify why nonnegative weights are meaningful for real-world problems. Do they have specific applications in mind where nonnegativity is a natural constraint?

2. **Expand Experimental Analysis**: Include more challenging datasets (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR-10) to demonstrate the method's scalability and performance limitations. Compare directly with other globally optimal approaches [8, 11].

3. **Address Parameter Selection**: Provide more guidance on selecting parameters (pw, pu, ρw, ρu) to satisfy ρ(A) < 1. The current approach of randomly sampling from a grid is not a practical solution.

4. **Explain the Objective Modification**: Clarify how the negative sum of outputs affects the learning objective and whether it's necessary for the theoretical guarantees.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a technically sound theoretical contribution to the problem of globally optimal training for a very specific class of neural networks. The proof is elegant and the convergence guarantee is meaningful within the constrained framework. However, the practical significance is limited due to the restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights) that make the method inapplicable to standard neural network architectures. The empirical results are promising but not compelling enough to justify the method's broader adoption.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject**

While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the practical limitations and lack of compelling empirical evidence for real-world utility make this paper unsuitable for NeurIPS. The authors should either significantly broaden the scope of their method (e.g., relax the nonnegativity constraint) or provide stronger empirical evidence demonstrating the advantages of global optimality in practical applications. As it stands, the paper contributes to a narrow theoretical niche rather than advancing the field toward more practical globally optimal neural network training.
