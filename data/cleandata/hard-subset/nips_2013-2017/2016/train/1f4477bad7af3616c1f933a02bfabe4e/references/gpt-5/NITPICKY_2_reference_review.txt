Summary
This paper proposes a globally convergent training algorithm for a restricted class of neural networks: one- and two-hidden-layer feedforward models with nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and generalized polynomial activations with exponents αi ≥ 1 (and, crucially, distinct across hidden units). The authors recast training as a fixed-point problem on the positive orthant, define an iteration GΦ based on normalized gradients (using ψp and p-norm constraints), and use nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory with the Thompson metric to prove that, if the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 or K+2) is < 1, then GΦ is a contraction with a unique fixed point that is the global maximizer of a modified objective. They provide conservative, checkable conditions ensuring ρ(A) < 1, extend the framework to two hidden layers, and show small-scale UCI experiments that are competitive with ReLU networks and slightly worse than RBF SVM.

Relation to prior work: This connects to Haeffele & Vidal (structured matrix factorization) and Janzamin et al. (tensor methods), aiming for practical global optimality under strong structural constraints. The use of multi-homogeneous nonlinear PF theory and Thompson metric is interesting and, to my knowledge, relatively novel in the NN training literature.

Strengths
- Novel perspective: a clean fixed-point algorithm with a spectral-radius condition (easily checkable) that guarantees uniqueness and linear convergence.
- Clear high-level proof strategy leveraging contraction mappings in ordered cones; extension to two layers is nontrivial.
- Some practical discussion of how ρ(A) depends on model parameters and a path to ensuring ρ(A) < 1.
- Empirical sanity checks against simple baselines; demonstrates feasibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and clarity
- Loss definition/sign errors and missing equations. The “negative cross-entropy loss” is announced but not defined; subsequently the objective maximizes −L + Σr fr(xi). If L is “negative cross-entropy,” then −L is positive CE, which is strange; if L is standard CE, then the description is inconsistent. The role of softmax or normalization of f is never specified. Without a precise L, the theoretical assumptions and experiments are ambiguous.
- The objective adds Σr fr(xi) and an ε-weight sum term to force positive gradients. The symbol ε is missing in text (rendered as a blank “ ”), and its choice is never specified beyond “arbitrarily small,” which matters numerically and for reproducibility.
- Inconsistent norms/parameters: Theorem 1 defines ρx = maxi ||xi||1, while Theorem 4 defines ρx = maxi ||xi||p′u. The text elsewhere uses both ρ̃x with l1. These inconsistencies directly affect ξ1, ξ2 and the bounds in A.
- α indexing and notation: α ∈ Rn1 with “αi ≥ 1, i ∈ [K]” (likely [n1]); later α is both a vector and a scalar “α = minl∈J αl” in Ψαp,q. Several indices (i, l, m, r) are overloaded, making it easy to misread equations.
- Symmetry/uniqueness argument for “Choice of α”: The claim that using identical α leads (by permutation invariance and uniqueness) to rank-one A and B is hand-wavy and likely incorrect as stated. The set of matrices commuting with all permutations is constrained (aI + b11^T), but the implications for the NN weights under the given constraints are not rigorously derived here. If global optimum uniqueness fails under symmetry, the fix should be explained more carefully; moreover, the experiments do not state that distinct α were enforced.
- Assumptions for AT to have a positive eigenvector are left as an assumption in Theorem 3; a brief justification for the specific A constructed in Theorems 1/4/5 would help.
- Growth of ρ(A) with width renders the guarantees increasingly conservative as n1, n2 grow; this is acknowledged but limits practicality.

Algorithmic and experimental issues
- Practicality: The framework demands nonnegative inputs and weights and modifies the objective by adding Σr fr(x) and linear weight sums. The paper argues necessity for positivity of ∇Φ, but the impact on classification calibration and decision boundaries is not analyzed. The choice of ε matters and is not reported.
- Preprocessing: Several UCI datasets contain negative features. The authors mention data in [0,1] in passing but don’t specify preprocessing per dataset (shifts, scaling), which is critical given the nonnegativity assumption.
- Hyperparameters: The claim “no free parameter” contrasts with heavy hyperparameter search over (n1, α, ρw, ρu) (and (n2, β, ρv) for two layers), plus pw, pu chosen via conservative bounds. The effective search space is large; distinctness of α is not enforced in the description.
- Fairness of baselines: SGD baselines use fixed step-sizes from a coarse grid and batch size 5%. No Adam/Adagrad or tuned schedules; ReLU uses logistic loss+L2 but no careful capacity control matching the constrained model. “Faster” comparisons are based on iterations/epochs rather than wall-clock or equal gradient calls; per-iteration complexity of GΦ (full gradients) vs stochastic updates is not normalized.
- Figures and tables: Figure 2 axes/units are unclear (p* − f notation unexplained); Figure 1 referred to but not shown; some plots look like placeholders (numbers at top of Section 2). Table 1 lacks standard deviations and data split protocol. No code/seed for reproducibility.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous typos and grammatical issues: “polyomial,” “componentwise,” “radius basis function,” “preparation,” etc.
- References are often incomplete, garbled, or missing years/titles/authors (e.g., [6], [8], [11]); several author names are corrupted; page numbers and venues missing; inconsistent styles.
- Several equations are missing or malformed; symbols drop out (ε), and some indices are wrong ([K] used where [n1] is intended).

Questions for the authors
- Precisely define the loss L used in theory and experiments. Is there a softmax? If not, what is the exact cross-entropy variant?
- How is ε chosen in practice? How sensitive are results to ε? Is it needed if all inputs/weights are strictly positive already?
- Did you enforce αi ≠ αj in experiments? If yes, how? If not, do you observe degeneracy?
- What preprocessing guarantees xi ≥ 0 across all UCI datasets? Please specify per dataset.
- Provide wall-clock comparisons and normalize for number of processed examples (or gradient evaluations).
- Can you tighten the bounds leading to A to mitigate the width dependence and enable higher-dimensional datasets?

Pros
- Interesting and rigorous use of nonlinear PF theory and Thompson metric to obtain global optimality and linear convergence.
- Checkable condition via small matrix spectral radius.
- Extension to two hidden layers.

Cons
- Many clarity issues, missing definitions, and formatting/reference problems that impede verification.
- Strong modeling constraints (nonnegativity, modified objective, distinct α) with unclear practical relevance.
- Experimental evaluation is small-scale, under-specified, and not convincingly fair; reproducibility is low.
- Several notational inconsistencies and potentially incorrect symmetry argument.

Recommendation
Reject. The theoretical angle is promising, but the paper, as written, has substantial clarity/rigor issues (missing loss definition and ε, inconsistent norms/indices), numerous presentation errors, and limited, under-specified experiments. With significant rewriting, clearer problem/formulation, stronger empirical evaluation, and corrected references, this could be a valuable contribution. Confidence: high.
