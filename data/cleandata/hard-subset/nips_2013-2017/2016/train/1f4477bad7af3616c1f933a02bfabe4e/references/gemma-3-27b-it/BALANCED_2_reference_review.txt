This paper proposes a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks, aiming for global optimality using a nonlinear spectral method. The core idea is compelling: by imposing non-negativity constraints on weights and modifying the objective function, the authors derive a globally convergent algorithm, unlike standard gradient-based methods which often get stuck in local optima. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory and the efficient check for global optimality via spectral radius calculation are significant strengths.

The paper is well-structured. It begins with a clear motivation, outlining the challenges of training deep neural networks and the limitations of existing methods. The mathematical formulation is relatively concise, and the core theorem (Theorem 1) is stated explicitly. The extension to two hidden layers (Theorem 5) demonstrates a potential for broader applicability. The experimental results, while limited, provide a proof-of-concept and show competitive performance on UCI datasets.

However, several weaknesses hinder the paper's overall impact.

**Technical Soundness & Completeness:** While the authors claim global optimality, the constraints imposed (non-negativity of weights) are quite strong. The paper doesn’t fully address the potential loss of expressivity due to these constraints. The choice of α (generalized polynomial exponents) being distinct is also a crucial requirement, and the explanation, while present, feels slightly hand-wavy. The bounds derived for ensuring convergence (based on the spectral radius) appear conservative, and the authors acknowledge this. Further investigation into tighter bounds could significantly improve the practical applicability of the method. The leap from the theoretical analysis to the experimental setup isn't fully clear. The objective function modification (adding the sum of outputs) feels somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation.  The connection between the spectral radius calculation and actual training speed isn't fully explored.  The proofs, while sketched, rely heavily on supplementary material, making it difficult for the reader to fully verify the technical details without substantial effort.

**Clarity:**  The notation is dense and could be improved. While the authors attempt to define each symbol, the sheer number of variables and indices makes it challenging to follow the derivations. The link between the theoretical framework and the experimental settings isn't perfectly transparent.  For example, it's not immediately clear how the parameters were selected based on the spectral radius condition.  The explanation of why the additional term is added to the objective function could be clarified – the motivation linked to strict positivity of gradients isn’t fully convincing without more detailed explanation. The section explaining the choice of α and its impact on invariance is a bit convoluted and could benefit from simpler language.

**Originality:**  The idea of leveraging spectral methods for neural network training is relatively novel. However, the connection to Perron-Frobenius theory has been explored in related works (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal, 2016, mentioned in the paper). The paper needs to more clearly delineate its contributions compared to existing work in this area. While the authors acknowledge related work, a more in-depth discussion would be beneficial.  For instance, discussing the limitations of the Haeffele & Vidal approach and *how* the presented method overcomes those limitations would strengthen the originality argument.

**Significance:** The promise of a globally optimal training algorithm is significant. However, the strong constraints and the limited scope of the experiments raise questions about the real-world applicability. The current experiments are on low-dimensional datasets, which don't fully demonstrate the scalability of the method. While competitive with other methods on these datasets, the improvements are not substantial enough to justify the additional constraints.  The authors do mention that the bounds are likely conservative, suggesting potential for improved scalability, but this is not demonstrated empirically.  The method’s dependence on appropriate parameter selection (α, pw, pu, ρw, ρu) adds complexity and might negate some of the benefits of avoiding gradient-based optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to globally optimal training of neural networks.
*   Strong theoretical foundations based on Perron-Frobenius theory.
*   Efficient check for global optimality using spectral radius calculation.
*   Proof-of-concept experimental results demonstrating competitiveness.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong constraints (non-negativity of weights) might limit expressivity.
*   Limited scope of experiments (low-dimensional datasets).
*   Conservative bounds and potential scalability issues.
*   Dense notation and lack of clarity in certain explanations.
*   The advantage over standard methods is not substantial enough to outweigh the limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement for revisions. The paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, but the current version suffers from significant limitations in clarity, scalability, and practical applicability. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above—specifically tightening the bounds, demonstrating scalability on higher-dimensional datasets, and providing a more detailed comparison with related work—would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact. A more comprehensive discussion of the limitations of the non-negativity constraint and potential workarounds would also be beneficial. If these issues are addressed, I would be happy to reconsider the paper.
