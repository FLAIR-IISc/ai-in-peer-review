Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of a class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations. The networks operate on nonnegative inputs; activations are monomials with real exponents αl ≥ 1 (and required to be pairwise distinct). The training objective is a modified version of standard empirical risk: the average negative cross-entropy plus a sum over outputs, plus an arbitrarily small positive regularization on the sum of weights, under Lp-norm equality constraints on all layers. The key algorithm is an iteration that normalizes the positive part of the gradient (via ψp′) to the prescribed norms. Using nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory on cones and contraction arguments in the Thompson metric, the authors derive sufficient conditions under which this iteration is a contraction with Lipschitz constant bounded by the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix A. If ρ(A) < 1, the method converges linearly to a unique fixed point, which they prove is the unique global maximizer in the interior of the feasible set. The condition depends only on model parameters and can be checked before training. The framework is presented for one hidden layer, and extended to two hidden layers.

This work relates to: guarantees for neural network training via structured matrix factorization (Haeffele and Vidal) and tensor methods (Janzamin et al.), which provide global optimality under different assumptions (e.g., score function tensors, nondegeneracy). It also connects to polynomial neural networks (Livni et al.) and to nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory. Compared to prior works, the contribution is a practically implementable fixed-point scheme with an a priori checkable sufficient condition and a linear convergence rate, at the cost of modeling constraints (nonnegativity, generalized polynomial activations) and a modified loss.

Quality
- The theoretical development is careful and uses established tools: multihomogeneous maps, Thompson metric, Banach fixed-point theorem, and Collatz–Wielandt bounds. The mapping-to-fixed-point and the contraction proof strategy are sound under the stated assumptions.
- The main assumptions are transparent: nonnegativity of data and weights; αl ≥ 1 and pairwise distinct; strict positivity of gradients secured by adding a small positive term; Lp-norm equality constraints; and the spectral radius condition ρ(A) < 1.
- The sufficiency condition is conservative; the authors acknowledge looseness and provide both tighter and looser versions (Theorems 4 and 1) and simple sufficient lower bounds on pw, pu that ensure ρ(A) < 1.
- Experimental validation is limited but consistent with claims: fast linear convergence for the proposed objective, and competitive accuracy on small UCI datasets, generally below RBF-SVM.

Points needing clarification or potential issues:
- The loss L is referenced as negative cross-entropy but not fully specified; an explicit formula would aid reproducibility and clarity regarding the interaction with the added sum of outputs.
- The added “sum of outputs” term affects the objective in a nonstandard way; while needed for positivity of gradients, a deeper discussion of its effect on decision boundaries and calibration would be useful.
- The argument requiring distinct αl to avoid triviality is sketched via permutation invariance; a formal statement with conditions under which non-distinct α leads to rank-one solutions would strengthen the case.
- The nonnegativity of inputs is assumed; guidance on preprocessing for general datasets (e.g., images with mean subtraction) would help practitioners.
- The spectral radius condition appears to worsen with number of hidden units; empirical evidence of how often realistic architectures satisfy ρ(A) < 1 would be valuable.

Clarity
- The paper is mathematically dense but mostly well organized. However, several notational and formatting issues impede clarity: missing explicit definition of L, occurrences of “> 0” likely meant as ε, and some typos in references. The derivations around A and constants ξ1, ξ2, Cw, Cu would benefit from a consolidated summary.
- The connection between Theorems 3–4 and Theorem 1 is explained but could be streamlined, highlighting clearly when tighter versus looser bounds apply.
- Some references are incomplete or malformed; these should be fixed.

Originality
- The combination of nonlinear spectral methods on cones with a tailored network class and a checkable spectral-radius condition for global optimality is novel.
- Compared to prior global optimality results, the method does not require knowledge of data-generating densities or tensor score functions, and provides a simple iterative scheme with linear rate under explicit conditions.

Significance
- The theoretical significance is solid: a constructive, linearly convergent method with a priori verifiable global optimality conditions for a nontrivial network class.
- Practical significance is currently limited by modeling constraints (nonnegativity, generalized polynomial activations, modified objective), conservative conditions that may preclude larger architectures, and relatively modest empirical performance versus strong baselines. Nonetheless, the framework may inspire further work on globally convergent training for constrained architectures and deepen links between spectral theory and deep learning optimization.

Pros
- Clear theoretical contribution with global optimality and linear convergence under explicit, checkable conditions.
- Elegant use of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and Thompson metric; fixed-point interpretation is insightful.
- Extension to two hidden layers; discussion of parameter effects on ρ(A).
- Some empirical evidence of practicality and fast convergence on small datasets.

Cons
- Nonstandard objective (sum of outputs, ε term) and constraints; unclear impact on generalization and calibration.
- Assumes nonnegative inputs and weights; applicability to common pipelines not addressed.
- The spectral radius condition may be restrictive for larger models; bounds are conservative.
- Limited experiments; results generally below RBF-SVM; no medium/large-scale tasks.
- Clarity issues: missing explicit loss definition, notational glitches, incomplete references.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide the explicit form of the cross-entropy loss used and clarify how the added sum-of-outputs term interacts with it.
- How sensitive are results to the choice of ε, ρw, ρu, and pw, pu? Any heuristics for setting these beyond conservative bounds?
- Can the method accommodate standardized inputs (with negatives) via affine shifts without breaking guarantees?
- What is the per-iteration complexity and wall-clock comparison to SGD on your objective?
- Empirically, how often does ρ(A) < 1 hold as n1, K, and d increase? Any tighter, data-dependent bounds?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper offers a technically interesting and novel global optimization framework with rigorous guarantees for a constrained network class. However, the modeling restrictions, nonstandard objective, conservative conditions, limited and small-scale experiments, and clarity issues reduce the overall impact for NeurIPS at this stage. Strengthening empirical validation, clarifying the loss and the role of the added terms, tightening bounds, and improving presentation would substantially increase the paper’s value.
