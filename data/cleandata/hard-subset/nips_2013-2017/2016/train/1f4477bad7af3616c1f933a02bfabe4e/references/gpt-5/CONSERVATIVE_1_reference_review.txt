Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a class of generalized polynomial neural networks (one or two hidden layers) to global optimality under strong structural constraints. Inputs and weights are constrained to be nonnegative; hidden units apply power activations with unit-specific exponents αl ≥ 1; and the optimization is over fixed ℓp-norm spheres. Crucially, the objective is modified: the negative cross-entropy is augmented by the sum of network outputs and small positive linear terms to ensure strict positivity of gradients. The core technical result shows that a fixed-point map GΦ built from normalized gradients is a contraction in the Thompson metric when the spectral radius ρ(A) of an explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix (depending on architecture and norms) is < 1. Under this condition, the method converges linearly to a unique global maximizer of the modified objective. The authors extend the construction to two hidden layers and provide preliminary experiments on small UCI datasets, showing competitive accuracy relative to simple baselines and faster convergence versus SGD on the modified objective.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against recent guarantees for special neural architectures (e.g., tensor methods of Janzamin et al. and structured matrix factorization results by Haeffele & Vidal). It also leverages nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and multi-homogeneous maps. Polynomial networks and nonnegative factorization literature are relevant. The paper claims to be the first “practically feasible” globally optimal algorithm for a nontrivial NN model.

Strengths
- Clear, nonasymptotic convergence guarantee to a unique global optimum under an explicit spectral radius condition; linear rate with a computable constant.
- Elegant use of contraction mappings in the Thompson metric and multi-homogeneous variants of Perron–Frobenius theory; the analysis is technically interesting.
- The condition ρ(A) < 1 is checkable a priori and links architectural choices to convergence behavior.
- Extension to two hidden layers follows a similar template.
- Algorithm is hyperparameter-light at optimization time (no step sizes); proof-of-concept experiments show the method can produce nontrivial decision boundaries.

Weaknesses / Concerns
- Practical relevance: The model materially departs from standard neural networks. It enforces nonnegative data and weights, power activations with per-unit exponents (αl all distinct), fixed ℓp-norm constraints on all layers, and a modified objective that adds the sum of outputs and a small linear term. The guarantees therefore do not apply to the standard cross-entropy training used in practice. This raises questions about the usefulness of the “global optimality” claim for typical applications.
- Necessity and impact of the modified objective: The sum-of-outputs term is introduced to ensure strictly positive gradients. This biases the classifier toward larger logits and changes the optimization landscape. There is no analysis of calibration, margin properties, or how this modification affects generalization. An ablation is missing.
- Strong and somewhat opaque hyperparameterization: The spectral condition often requires large pw, pu (and pv), pushing norms toward ∞-like behavior. The choice of α is constrained (distinct per unit) and selected by random search. There is little guidance or theory on how these choices affect approximation ability, stability, or generalization.
- Scaling and tightness of conditions: The matrix A grows with hidden units, making ρ(A) increase; authors acknowledge the bounds are conservative and experiments are restricted to low-dimensional, small models. It remains unclear if the method scales to realistic sizes or whether ρ(A) < 1 can be maintained for useful capacities.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: Only small UCI datasets; SVM often wins; ReLU baselines are weakly tuned (fixed-step SGD, no modern training tricks). No comparisons on larger or higher-dimensional problems, no runtime/memory analysis beyond a small plot, and no sensitivity studies for α, p, ρ parameters. No ablation of the nonnegativity constraint or the output-sum term.
- Clarity and presentation: Several notational and formatting issues (e.g., missing or malformed equations, ambiguous use of “> 0” for ε, S+ defined with equality norms while B++ with ≤, mixed use of ρx norms) hinder readability and reproducibility. Some key proofs are deferred; the tightness of A is largely qualitative.
- Missing components: No bias terms or discussion of how to handle signed data beyond a brief claim that many modalities are nonnegative; many real pipelines zero-center inputs. It is unclear how to adapt the method to such settings without violating assumptions.

Quality
The theoretical core appears sound within stated assumptions, but relies on strong and sometimes nonstandard conditions. The empirical evidence is too limited to support claims of practical feasibility.

Clarity
Technically dense but generally understandable; however, the manuscript suffers from formatting/notation problems and lacks sufficient implementation details (preprocessing to enforce nonnegativity, normalization, stopping criteria, computational complexity per iteration).

Originality
Combining nonlinear Perron–Frobenius contraction arguments with a tailored NN class and a checkable spectral condition is novel. The requirement of distinct power activations per unit is unusual.

Significance
As a theoretical contribution, this provides an interesting angle on globally optimal training under structure. Practically, impact is likely modest due to restrictive assumptions and the modified loss. The current experiments do not demonstrate benefits over established methods.

Questions for the authors
- Can you quantify the effect of the sum-of-outputs term on classification risk? Is there a way to ensure strict positivity without altering the objective (e.g., smoothing, barrier functions) and does the theory still go through?
- How sensitive is performance to α, p, and the norm radii? Any heuristic guidance beyond random search?
- What happens empirically when ρ(A) ≥ 1? Does the method still converge in practice?
- Can bias terms be included without breaking the theory?
- How do you handle inputs with negative values (e.g., standardized pixels, word embeddings)?

Minor comments
- Please fix missing/malformed equations and define all quantities at first use (cross-entropy L, ε, p′, norms).
- Clarify S+ (equality constraints) vs. B++ (inequalities) and the “interior” argument.
- Provide code or detailed implementation notes for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical framework is elegant, but the gap to practical neural network training is large, and the empirical validation is limited and not competitive with strong baselines. Strengthening the empirical section (larger datasets, stronger baselines, ablations), clarifying the impact of the modified objective, and tightening/illustrating the spectral condition in realistic regimes would significantly improve the paper. Confidence: high.
