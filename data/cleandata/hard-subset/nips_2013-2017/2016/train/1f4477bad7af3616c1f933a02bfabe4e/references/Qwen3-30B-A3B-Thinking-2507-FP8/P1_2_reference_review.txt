

# Peer Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach for globally optimal training of neural networks under specific constraints. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method that guarantees global convergence to the optimum for generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights and a modified objective function. The key insight is leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous problems to establish conditions (based on the spectral radius of a small matrix) under which a fixed-point iteration converges linearly to the global optimum. The approach is theoretically grounded, with proofs showing global optimality under certain conditions, and the authors demonstrate competitive performance on UCI datasets. The work relates to recent efforts on global optimality for neural networks (Haeffele & Vidal [8], Janzamin et al. [11]), but claims to overcome their practical limitations.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with a clear proof structure. The main theorem (Theorem 1) provides concrete conditions for global convergence that are checkable without running the algorithm (via the spectral radius of a small matrix). The proof strategy effectively combines fixed point theory, Banach fixed point theorem, and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory. The experimental results on UCI datasets are well-organized and demonstrate competitive performance compared to standard methods (SVM, ReLU networks), with the added benefit of guaranteed global optimality.

However, the restrictive nonnegativity assumption on weights significantly limits the applicability of the approach to standard neural network architectures, which typically use ReLU or other activations that allow negative values. The paper does not sufficiently address how this constraint affects the representational capacity of the network or provide guidance on how to choose the parameters (α, ρw, ρu, etc.) for practical applications. The experimental section also lacks analysis of how the spectral radius condition (ρ(A) < 1) impacts practical performance.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized with clear mathematical notation. The main theorem and proof strategy are logically presented, and the experimental setup is adequately described. However, some key points could be clarified:

1. The motivation for adding the "sum of outputs" term to the objective is not sufficiently explained.
2. The paper states that "nonnegativity of the weights is a strong constraint" but doesn't clearly articulate how this constraint affects the network's ability to model complex functions.
3. The experimental section would benefit from more detailed analysis of how the spectral radius condition impacts training speed and performance.

## Originality
The paper makes a novel contribution by connecting neural network training to nonlinear spectral methods via Perron-Frobenius theory. The approach of using matrix spectral radius conditions to guarantee global convergence is original and well-motivated. However, the paper could better distinguish itself from related work on global optimality (Haeffele & Vidal [8], Janzamin et al. [11]) by more clearly articulating why their method is "practically feasible" where others are not. The claim that their method is the "first practically feasible algorithm" for global optimality requires stronger justification, as the experimental results don't demonstrate significant advantages over standard methods.

## Significance
The significance is somewhat limited by the restrictive weight nonnegativity assumption, which makes the method less applicable to real-world neural network architectures. While the theoretical guarantee of global optimality is valuable, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this advantage translates to practical benefits over standard SGD methods. The experimental results show competitive but not superior performance compared to other methods, and the paper doesn't address scalability to larger datasets or deeper networks.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network training, but the practical significance is constrained by the nonnegativity assumption. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't sufficiently address how to overcome it in future work.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Provides a theoretically sound method with guaranteed global convergence
- Clear conditions (checkable via spectral radius) for global optimality
- Demonstrates competitive performance on UCI datasets with guaranteed optimality
- Well-structured theoretical development with careful proofs

**Weaknesses:**
- Restrictive nonnegativity constraint on weights limits applicability
- Insufficient guidance for choosing model parameters in practice
- Limited comparison with related work on global optimality
- Experimental results don't demonstrate significant advantages over standard methods
- Doesn't address scalability to larger datasets or deeper architectures

## Recommendation
This is a technically strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of neural network training. However, the restrictive assumptions and limited practical significance make it less compelling for NIPS. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparison with related work on global optimality, clearer articulation of the practical advantages (or limitations) of the nonnegativity constraint, and more detailed experimental analysis.

**Decision: Weak Reject**

While the theoretical results are sound and interesting, the practical limitations of the approach (nonnegative weights) and the lack of clear advantages over standard methods for real-world applications make this paper less suitable for NIPS. The authors should consider addressing these limitations in future work before resubmission.
