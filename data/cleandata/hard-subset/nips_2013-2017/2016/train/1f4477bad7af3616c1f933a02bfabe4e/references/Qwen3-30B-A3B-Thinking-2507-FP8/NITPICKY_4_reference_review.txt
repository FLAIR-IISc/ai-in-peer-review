

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a globally optimal training algorithm for a specialized class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative inputs. The authors claim to provide the first practically feasible globally optimal algorithm for neural networks, with linear convergence rate. While the problem is interesting and the theoretical framework is novel, the paper contains numerous technical and presentation issues that undermine its scientific contribution.

**Major Issues:**

1. **Technical Inconsistencies and Unjustified Claims:** The paper claims to be the "first practically feasible algorithm" for global optimality but fails to properly compare with Haeffele and Vidal [8] and Janzamin et al. [11], which are explicitly cited as the previous works on global optimality. The paper dismisses [8] as "challenging to apply in practice" without providing any quantitative evidence or benchmarks, which is a significant oversight.

2. **Overly Conservative Theoretical Bounds:** The paper's main theorem (Theorem 1) requires ρ(A) < 1, with the matrix A defined in terms of conservative bounds ξ1 and ξ2. The authors admit these bounds are "very conservative" and "sub-optimal" but don't provide better bounds or evidence that these conditions are satisfied in practice beyond the "small" datasets they test. The paper claims "the effective spectral radius is typically much smaller" but provides no empirical evidence for this.

3. **Unjustified Constraints:** The paper requires nonnegative weights and nonnegative inputs, which is a severe limitation for real-world applications. The authors don't adequately address how to handle datasets with negative values, which is common in practice. The paper states "the model is non-standard" but doesn't explain why this constraint is acceptable or how it relates to standard neural network theory.

4. **Experimental Shortcomings:** The experimental section is inadequate for a NIPS submission. The paper compares to SVM and ReLU networks but fails to compare with previous globally optimal methods [8, 11] on real datasets, which is a major omission. The results on UCI datasets show only marginal improvements (Table 1) and the paper admits "the resulting classifier is still expressive enough" without providing evidence of sufficient expressivity. The paper claims "we show for the first time a globally optimal algorithm... that leads to non-trivial classification results" but provides no compelling evidence of this.

5. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper contains numerous formatting errors that make it difficult to follow, including:
   - Inconsistent mathematical notation (e.g., "R+ = {x ∈ R |x ≥ 0}" vs "R++ = {t ∈ R | t > 0}")
   - A misplaced sequence of numbers "0.01 0.02 0.03..." that appears to be a misplaced figure label
   - Inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions
   - Incomplete references (e.g., "Deep Learning in Neural Networks: An Overview, J. Schmidhuber, Neural Networks, 61:85–117, ,")

6. **Algorithmic Implementation:** The paper claims "no free parameter" but fails to explain how to choose the parameters (n1, α, pw, pu, ρw, ρu) for a given dataset. The paper states they "choose the parameters out of 100 randomly generated combinations" but doesn't explain why this is a principled approach rather than a heuristic search.

**Minor Issues:**

- The paper states "the nonlinear spectral method has a linear convergence rate" but doesn't provide experimental evidence of this in the main text (it's mentioned in the abstract but not shown in the results).
- The paper claims "the global optimum is unique" but doesn't provide a proof beyond the conditions in Theorem 1.
- The paper requires "nonnegative weights" but doesn't address what happens if the gradient pushes weights to be negative during optimization.
- The paper mentions "we have to minimize a modified objective function" but doesn't explain why this modification is necessary for global optimality.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework for a specialized class of neural networks, the numerous technical inconsistencies, overly conservative theoretical bounds, experimental shortcomings, and formatting issues significantly undermine its contribution. The paper fails to adequately compare with previous work on global optimality, doesn't address the severe constraints of the approach, and provides insufficient experimental evidence to support its claims. The paper's theoretical claims are not practically verifiable for realistic network sizes due to the conservative bounds, and the experimental results are not compelling enough to justify the paper's claims of being the "first practically feasible algorithm."

The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical guarantees, more comprehensive experimental comparisons with previous globally optimal methods, and a clearer explanation of how to choose parameters for real-world applications. Until these issues are addressed, the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for quality and significance. I recommend rejection.
