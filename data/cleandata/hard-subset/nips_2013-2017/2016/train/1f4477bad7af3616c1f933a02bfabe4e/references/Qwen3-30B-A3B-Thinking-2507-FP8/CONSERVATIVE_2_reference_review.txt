

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a "nonlinear spectral method" for training neural networks with global optimality guarantees under restrictive conditions. The authors claim to provide the first practically feasible algorithm for achieving global optimality in neural network training, requiring only nonnegative weights and a modified objective function. The approach leverages Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices and claims linear convergence rates with a simple implementation.

The paper builds on recent work on global optimality in neural networks (e.g., Janzamin et al. [11], Haeffele and Vidal [8]), but introduces significant constraints: nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activation functions with α ≥ 1, and a modified objective function that adds the sum of all outputs. The authors claim their method is "practically feasible" while previous approaches [8, 11] were "challenging to apply in practice," but the paper fails to demonstrate this in meaningful comparison.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical development is mathematically rigorous, with careful proofs of convergence under specific conditions.
- The paper clearly states the conditions required for global optimality (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions, etc.).
- The linear convergence rate claim is theoretically sound under the specified conditions.
- The paper includes some experimental results on UCI datasets, which is rare for papers claiming global optimality.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Unrealistic constraints:** The requirement for nonnegative weights is extremely restrictive and eliminates the expressive power of standard neural networks. As noted in the paper itself, ReLU activations "make no sense" in their setting, which severely limits applicability to most real-world problems. The paper fails to address how to handle negative weights in practical applications.

2. **Lack of practical evidence:** The experimental results (Table 1) show only competitive performance, not superior results compared to standard methods. The authors claim "competitive performance," but the results are actually worse than SVM on most datasets and slightly worse than ReLU networks on most. The paper doesn't demonstrate how their method would outperform standard approaches in real-world applications.

3. **Parameter selection problem:** The paper acknowledges they must "choose the parameters of our model out of 100 randomly generated combinations" and select the best one via cross-validation. This undermines the claim of "practical feasibility" since it requires extensive hyperparameter tuning, which the authors claim is unnecessary for their method.

4. **Theoretical claims overstate practical value:** While the paper proves global optimality under specific conditions, the conditions (ρ(A) < 1) are so restrictive that they require careful parameter selection that is not practical. The paper admits their bounds are "sub-optimal" and "quite conservative," suggesting that in practice, the method would require even more restrictive parameter choices than the theory suggests.

5. **Lack of comparison with standard approaches:** The paper doesn't compare against standard optimization methods like Adam or SGD with appropriate hyperparameter tuning. The comparison with "SGD" (Figure 2) uses fixed step sizes from a very limited range (10^-2 to 10^2), which is not how SGD is typically applied. The paper doesn't show how their method compares to standard practice.

6. **Misleading claims about novelty:** The paper claims to be "the first practically feasible algorithm to achieve global optimality for a non-trivial neural network model," but this is questionable. Previous work [11] already provided a globally optimal algorithm, though it required tensor decomposition. The authors don't demonstrate why their method is "practically feasible" while previous approaches weren't.

**Significance concerns:** The paper claims to address "the problem of global optimality in neural network training," but the solution requires such restrictive assumptions that it's unlikely to be useful for most applications. The experimental results don't demonstrate any significant advantage over standard approaches, and the theoretical conditions are too restrictive for practical use. The paper doesn't advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way for real-world applications.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and mathematically precise, but the claims about practical utility are not supported by the evidence provided. The experimental section is too brief to assess the practical value of the method.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the theoretical development is sound, the practical value of the method is severely limited by the restrictive assumptions. The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach but fails to demonstrate its practical utility in a way that would justify its publication at NIPS. The claims about "practical feasibility" and "global optimality" are misleading given the severe constraints required. The experimental results show only competitive performance at best, with no clear advantage over standard methods. For a skeptical reviewer who prefers established methods, this paper's unproven approach with limited practical value doesn't meet NIPS's standards for a significant contribution.
