Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a class of “generalized polynomial” neural networks with nonnegative weights and inputs. The network uses power activations (x -> (u^T x)^α) with α ≥ 1, normalized Lp constraints on weights, and optimizes a modified objective: negative cross-entropy plus a sum of network outputs, plus a small positive multiple of the sum of weights to ensure positivity of gradients. The central contribution is a fixed-point map GΦ derived from multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and a contraction argument in the Thompson metric. Under a verifiable condition—spectral radius ρ(A) < 1 of a (K+1)-by-(K+1) nonnegative matrix built from conservative bounds on architecture and data norms—the method provably converges linearly to the unique global optimum. The framework extends to two hidden layers with a slightly larger matrix A. Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy with ReLU networks and slightly worse than RBF SVMs, with fast convergence.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory (Lemmens & Nussbaum), structured matrix/tensor factorization perspectives on NN training (Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.), and prior analyses of polynomial networks. Unlike tensor methods that require score function tensors or strong distributional assumptions, this approach requires nonnegativity and modified loss/constraints to enable global optimality guarantees.

Strengths
- Clear high-level theoretical story linking multi-homogeneous maps, Thompson metric contraction, and fixed-point iteration.
- Verifiable precondition (ρ(A) < 1) that depends only on model/data bounds; no learning rate tuning.
- Extension to two hidden layers and a linear convergence rate bound.
- Empirical demonstration that the method converges rapidly and produces nontrivial classifiers.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Quality/technical correctness
- Missing and ambiguous definitions:
  - The negative cross-entropy loss is announced but never defined; the equation is blank.
  - The objective (2) includes additive regularization terms “(∑ wr,l + ∑ u_lm)” but the small positive coefficient ε is referenced in prose and omitted in equations; this makes Lemma 5 (strictly positive gradients) ill-posed.
  - α ∈ R^{n1} but “αi ≥ 1, i ∈ [K]” conflicts with dimensions; should be i ∈ [n1].
- The necessity and sufficiency of the added “sum of outputs” term for strict positivity of gradients is asserted but not convincingly justified. For cross-entropy with softmax-like outputs, gradient signs are typically data- and label-dependent. Please provide a precise, complete proof (with explicit ε) that all partial derivatives are strictly positive on S+.
- The uniqueness-of-α argument (to avoid permutation invariances leading to degenerate rank-one solutions) is informal and potentially flawed: uniqueness of a global maximizer is typically only up to inherent symmetries. If distinct α break symmetry, please formalize this as a proposition with assumptions; otherwise the restriction may be unnecessarily strong or not sufficient.
- Practical scope is narrow: requires x ≥ 0 and nonnegative weights, no biases, power activations with α ≥ 1, and a modified objective that rewards larger outputs. There is no discussion of calibration or decision boundary implications of this modified loss versus standard cross-entropy.
- The spectral radius bounds are acknowledged to be conservative; however, there is no quantitative empirical analysis of ρ(A) for the chosen models, nor ablations showing how ρ(A) correlates with observed convergence rates.
- Experiments: limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets; ReLU baselines trained with basic SGD without advanced optimizers or tuning beyond step sizes; no wall-clock/time-to-accuracy comparison; no evaluation on images or text where nonnegativity holds naturally; no robustness analysis to scaling of inputs or choice of α.

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous typographical, notation, and formatting issues hinder readability:
  - “polyomial” (typo), “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems…” inserted mid-paragraph, stray decimals in Section 2, inconsistent use of indices and sets, missing definition of R+, R++ after first occurrence, “wrt” instead of “w.r.t.”
  - Figures referenced (Figure 1) are not shown; Figure 2 axes and legends are poorly formatted, “p*” is undefined, and “score” is unclear.
  - Several equations use undefined symbols (ε not shown in (2); ψp applied to matrices/vectors without clarifying conventions).
- The transition from Theorem 3/4 to Theorem 1 is terse; the core bounding steps to construct A (esp. Ψ terms) should include more intuition and at least one worked example with numeric constants to orient readers.

Originality
- Combining multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius contraction with a specific nonnegative, polynomial NN and a modified objective to obtain global optimality appears novel. However, the method’s constraints and the loss modification are substantial departures from standard practice, limiting broader impact.

Significance
- The theoretical guarantee is elegant but currently applies to a restricted model class and a nonstandard objective. Without stronger empirical evidence or extensions (e.g., to include biases, standard cross-entropy without added output sum, or relaxation of nonnegativity), practical significance is limited.

References
- The bibliography is in poor shape: missing years, incorrect author names (“Hena”, “Rene Vidal” formatting, “and Y, A Daniely, R. Frostigy, Singer”), “preparation” entries, incomplete conference details, inconsistent styles. This must be fixed.

Questions for authors
1. Explicitly write the loss, the full objective with ε, and the softmax/logit mapping (if any). Are outputs fr used directly in cross-entropy or via softmax?
2. Do you include bias terms? If not, can the method be extended to include them while preserving the theory?
3. How are UCI datasets transformed to satisfy x ≥ 0? What is the effect of re-scaling on performance and the constants in A?
4. Provide typical values for ρ(A) and the contraction rate observed; compare to actual linear rate constants.
5. Can the “sum of outputs” term be replaced by a weaker term or adapted per-class without distorting the learning objective?

Pros
- Clear, verifiable sufficient condition (ρ(A) < 1).
- Linear convergence with no learning rate tuning.
- Extension to two hidden layers.

Cons
- Many presentation/notation/reference errors; missing definitions.
- Strong modeling restrictions (nonnegativity, unique α, no biases) and nonstandard objective.
- Limited and weak empirical evaluation; lack of ablations/time comparisons.
- Key technical claims (strict positivity; permutation/α argument) under-explained.

Recommendation: Reject (confidence: high). While the theoretical direction is interesting, the paper in its current form has too many clarity and correctness issues, a narrow and nonstandard model, and insufficient empirical validation. I encourage the authors to (i) fix the many typos and missing definitions, (ii) formalize and prove the key positivity/invariance claims with correct ε usage, (iii) provide deeper empirical analysis of ρ(A) and convergence, include biases, and test on naturally nonnegative data (images, counts), and (iv) substantially improve the references.
