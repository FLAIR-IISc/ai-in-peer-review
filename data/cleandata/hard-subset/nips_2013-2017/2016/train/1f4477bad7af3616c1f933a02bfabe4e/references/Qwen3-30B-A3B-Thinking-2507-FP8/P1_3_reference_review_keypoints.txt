Summary: Proposes a nonlinear spectral-method algorithm that guarantees global optimality for certain neural networks by enforcing nonnegative weights and a modified objective, with convergence characterized by the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix.

Strength — Theoretical contribution: First practically feasible globally optimal training algorithm for certain neural nets, leveraging nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory.

Strength — Clarity of theory: Well-structured definitions, theorems, and proofs using Thompson metric and Banach fixed-point theorem.

Strength — Practical advantages: Linear convergence, no hyperparameter tuning, and a checkable convergence condition (ρ(A) < 1).

Strength — Experimental validation: Competitive results on UCI datasets, giving practical evidence absent from prior global-optimality work.

Weakness — Nonnegative weight constraint: Requiring nonnegative weights severely limits representational capacity and practical applicability.

Weakness — Limited experiments: Validation is confined to low-dimensional UCI datasets and does not demonstrate scalability to high-dimensional tasks like vision.

Weakness — Lack of comparisons: No empirical or complexity comparison with other global-optimality methods (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.).

Weakness — Conservative bounds: Spectral-radius bounds (ξ1, ξ2) appear loose, potentially making the convergence condition too restrictive for useful architectures.

Quality: Strong theoretical rigor but practical limitations weaken overall contribution.

Clarity: Exceptionally well-written with clear math and logical flow; experimental analysis could be deeper.

Originality: Novel application of nonlinear spectral methods to neural-net global optimality, though practical novelty limited by weight constraint.

Significance: Theoretical significance is high; practical impact is limited without addressing nonnegative-weight drawback and scalability.

Recommendation: Weak accept pending revisions to address the nonnegative weight limitation and expand experimental and theoretical analyses.

Required revision 1: Discuss practical implications and acceptable use-cases for nonnegative weights (e.g., count-data scenarios).

Required revision 2: Expand experiments to include parameter-sensitivity analysis and comparisons with other global optimization methods.

Required revision 3: Investigate or tighten theoretical bounds to make the spectral-radius condition more practically applicable.
