Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a class of feedforward neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The networks use nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and generalized polynomial activations of the form (u·x)αl with αl ≥ 1. The training objective is a modified version of cross-entropy: maximize the average negative cross-entropy plus the sum of outputs over classes, with an additional small positive term on the weights to ensure strict positivity of the gradient. Parameters are constrained to lie on product spheres in ℓpw/ℓpu norms. The key algorithm maps parameters to normalized positive powers of the gradient (a Perron–Frobenius-inspired, multi-homogeneous fixed-point map). Under a computable contraction condition—spectral radius ρ(A) < 1 of a small nonnegative matrix A depending on architecture hyperparameters and data norm bounds—the map is a contraction in the Thompson metric, yielding uniqueness of a fixed point, linear convergence, and hence a unique global maximizer within the positive orthant. The method extends to two hidden layers with an analogous A of size (K+2)×(K+2). Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive performance with small ReLU networks and RBF-SVM, with faster, linear convergence than batch SGD on the modified objective.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and fixed-point methods on cones (Lemmens & Nussbaum), and connects to recent global optimality results for neural networks via structured factorizations (Haeffele & Vidal) and tensor methods (Janzamin et al.). The approach is reminiscent of NMF-style multiplicative updates and geometric programming for posynomials; these connections could be elaborated. Compared to [8,11], the present guarantees do not require score function estimation or identifiability of tensor decompositions, but instead impose positivity, norm constraints, and a modified objective.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean, self-contained fixed-point formulation that turns nonconvex training into a contraction mapping problem in a weighted Thompson metric, with a simple spectral-radius check for global convergence.
- Theoretical completeness: Clear pathway from optimization to fixed-point theory (existence of interior maximizer, critical point = fixed point, contraction via matrix A, linear rate). Extension to two layers is nontrivial and appreciated.
- Algorithmic simplicity: Parameter-free iteration (once the architecture and norms are chosen), with explicit rates and stopping criterion based on ρ(A).
- Practical checkability: The “global optimality regime” is characterized by a small matrix dependent only on model hyperparameters and a norm bound of inputs.
- Empirical proof-of-concept: Demonstrates that the constrained model can learn nontrivial decision boundaries and provides first real-data experiments for such global methods.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Nonstandard model and limited expressivity: The network has no biases, weights and inputs must be nonnegative, activations are monotone powers, and outputs are further encouraged to be large via a sum-of-outputs term in the objective. This restricts the hypothesis class to monotone functions in each input coordinate (given nonnegative weights and no biases). Many practical classification problems require non-monotone decision boundaries. The constraints may significantly limit applicability versus modern networks.
- Modified objective: The added sum over outputs (with coefficient 1, not ε) is not a benign perturbation; it substantially alters the cross-entropy objective, encouraging uniformly larger logits. Although norms are fixed, this term affects the learned classifier. An ablation (with/without the output-sum term) and discussion of its effect on decision boundaries and calibration are needed. The text mentions an ε term on weights, but ε is missing in Eq. (2); please clarify coefficients.
- Distinct αl requirement: To avoid symmetry-induced non-uniqueness, the paper requires αl to be all different, which is unusual and potentially undesirable. The provided permutation invariance argument is not entirely convincing: uniqueness of the fixed point is a property of the dynamics under ρ(A)<1, but the function class can still admit symmetries; forcing distinct α’s trades away symmetry rather than addressing identifiability. Empirical guidance on selecting α’s and the impact on performance is missing.
- Guarantee regime and practicality: Equation (4) shows that for any architecture one can pick pw, pu large enough to force ρ(A)<1. But making these exponents large effectively pushes the constraints toward ℓ∞–type spheres and may degrade optimization geometry and expressivity. The paper does not quantify how conservative the bounds are in practice or how the choice of p affects accuracy; reporting measured ρ(A), the observed Lipschitz constant, and sensitivity to p would strengthen the case.
- Clarity and missing details: The loss L(y,f) (softmax cross-entropy?) is not explicitly defined; the small ε term’s placement and magnitude are unclear in Eq. (2); there is no discussion of bias terms (can positive biases be included while preserving the analysis?); the data preprocessing step to ensure nonnegativity is not specified. Some constants (e.g., Ψ) are intricate; additional intuition and bounds would help.
- Experiments are limited: Only small UCI datasets are considered, with SVM typically stronger. ReLU baselines could benefit from better tuning and regularization strategies; reporting variance over multiple runs and proper cross-validation splits is important. No runtime comparisons (wall-clock) are provided; only iterations/epochs are shown. No ablations (effect of α distribution, p values, with/without output-sum term, with/without distinct α’s). The claim of “first practically feasible globally optimal training” is strong given the small scale and modified objective.

Questions for the authors
- Can you incorporate positive bias terms in each layer and retain the analysis (e.g., by augmenting inputs with a constant 1 feature)? If not, why?
- Please clarify the exact loss and the coefficients of the additional terms in Eq. (2). Is the sum-of-outputs term scaled? How sensitive are results to this scaling?
- How do you choose αl in practice? What happens if some αl coincide? Can you present ablations over α choices?
- Report measured ρ(A) for the selected models and compare to the theoretical upper bounds to justify the “conservative” claim.
- Can the nonnegativity assumption be relaxed by a simple transformation (e.g., splitting features into positive/negative parts)? Does the theory carry over?

Minor comments
- Define L(y,f) precisely. Clarify the role and magnitude of ε in the third term.
- Explain data preprocessing for nonnegativity. Discuss normalization of inputs and its effect on ρx.
- Clean up typos and missing references (some citations appear incomplete).

Evaluation
- Quality: Solid theoretical development; experimental support is limited and the modeling choices substantially depart from standard neural classifiers.
- Clarity: Generally readable, but some definitions and motivations (loss, ε, α choice, biases) need clarification.
- Originality: The contraction-based global optimality for these networks via nonlinear spectral methods is novel and interesting.
- Significance: Conceptually interesting for theory of nonconvex learning on cones; practical impact appears limited due to model constraints and altered objective.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The theoretical contribution is elegant, but the modeling restrictions and limited empirical validation impede significance for NeurIPS. Strengthening experiments, clarifying the objective, and addressing expressivity (e.g., biases) would greatly improve the paper.
