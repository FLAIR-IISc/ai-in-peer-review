This paper introduces a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks, aiming for global optimality using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors address a significant challenge in deep learning – the difficulty of achieving global optima with standard gradient-based methods – and build upon recent work attempting to provide global convergence guarantees. The core idea of imposing non-negativity constraints on weights and modifying the objective function is interesting, and the connection to Perron-Frobenius theory provides a solid theoretical foundation. The claim of practical feasibility, coupled with experimental results on UCI datasets, sets this work apart from prior theoretical contributions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1 and its extension in Theorem 5 for two hidden layers. The convergence guarantee under specific conditions is a substantial contribution.
*   **Global Optimality Focus:**  Addressing the global optimality problem is crucial, and this work offers a concrete algorithm with a guarantee, unlike many heuristic training methods.
*   **Novel Approach:** The use of nonlinear spectral methods and the modified objective function represent a unique approach to neural network training.
*   **Clear Presentation of Theory:** The mathematical derivations and explanations are generally clear and well-structured, making the theoretical aspects accessible. The proof strategy is well-outlined.
*   **Experimental Validation:**  The inclusion of experiments, even on relatively small datasets, demonstrates the potential of the method and provides a starting point for further investigation. The comparison to SGD and SVM provides useful context.
*   **Detailed Analysis of Conditions:** The careful analysis of conditions for convergence, including the dependence on model parameters (p, ρ, α) and the derivation of bounds, is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Non-Negativity Constraint:** The requirement of non-negative weights is a significant limitation. While justified by the theoretical framework, it restricts the expressiveness of the network and potentially limits its applicability to real-world datasets where negative weights are often beneficial.  The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough discussion of the impact on representational capacity would be helpful.
*   **Modified Objective Function:** The addition of the sum of outputs to the loss function is non-standard and its impact on performance beyond enabling the theoretical guarantees isn’t fully explored. Further justification or analysis of this term is needed.
*   **Scalability Concerns:**  The computational complexity of the method, particularly the computation of the spectral radius of the matrix *A*, is not fully discussed. The authors mention that the bounds are likely not tight, but a more detailed analysis of the computational cost with respect to network size (number of hidden units) is crucial. The focus on low-dimensional datasets in the experiments hints at potential scalability issues.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The algorithm's performance seems sensitive to the choice of parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu, p, >0).  The paper briefly mentions selecting parameters via cross-validation but doesn’t provide a detailed explanation of how to do so effectively or the computational burden involved.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons to SVM and ReLU networks are included, a more thorough comparison to recent advances in neural network training techniques (e.g., normalization methods, advanced optimizers) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to relatively small UCI datasets. Demonstrating the method’s effectiveness on larger, more complex datasets (e.g., image classification) would significantly enhance the impact of the work.
*   **Clarity in Notation:** While generally clear, some notation, especially in the proofs, could be more consistently defined and explained.

**Originality:**

The combination of nonlinear spectral methods, non-negativity constraints, and a modified objective function to achieve global optimality in neural network training is a novel contribution. While building on existing work in tensor decomposition and Perron-Frobenius theory, the application to this specific problem and the resulting algorithm are original. The extension to two hidden layers is also a worthwhile addition.

**Significance:**

The potential to achieve global optimality in neural network training is highly significant. If the method can be scaled to larger networks and datasets, it could overcome limitations of current training techniques and lead to more robust and reliable models. However, the practical impact depends heavily on addressing the scalability and expressiveness concerns.  The work opens up new avenues for research in non-convex optimization and neural network theory.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and convergence guarantees.
*   Novel and potentially impactful approach to global optimization.
*   Clear presentation of the theoretical results.
*   Initial experimental validation demonstrating feasibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Restrictive non-negativity constraint on weights.
*   Potential scalability issues.
*   Limited scope of experimental validation.
*   Sensitivity to parameter choices.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a compelling and theoretically sound approach to achieving global optimality in neural network training. While the non-negativity constraint and potential scalability issues are significant concerns, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the theoretical guarantees warrant serious consideration.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit.  

**Recommendations for Revision:**

*   **Address the non-negativity constraint:** Explore techniques to mitigate the impact of this constraint on representational capacity or investigate alternative formulations that allow for negative weights.
*   **Analyze Scalability:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm and discuss potential strategies for scaling it to larger networks and datasets.
*   **Expand Experimental Evaluation:**  Conduct experiments on larger, more complex datasets (e.g., image classification) to demonstrate the method’s effectiveness in realistic scenarios.
*   **Parameter Selection Guidance:** Provide more detailed guidance on how to choose appropriate parameters for the algorithm and assess its sensitivity to parameter variations.
*   **Comparison to SOTA:** Compare the method to more recent state-of-the-art training techniques.
*   **Clarify Notation:** Review the paper for consistency and clarity in notation, particularly in the proofs.
*   **Further Justification of Objective:** Provide additional rationale for the modified objective function and analyze its impact on the learned models.
