

# Peer Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel algorithm for training neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors propose a "nonlinear spectral method" that requires nonnegative weights and a modified objective function (sum of loss plus negative total sum of outputs). The key contribution is a globally optimal training algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks, with a convergence proof showing linear convergence to the global optimum under conditions that can be checked by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix. The approach differs from prior work (e.g., Janzamin et al. 2015, Haeffele & Vidal 2015) by avoiding the need for tensor decomposition or unknown data-generating measures, and by providing a practically implementable algorithm with no hyperparameters to tune.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides the first theoretically grounded globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks that is practically implementable. The proof strategy using fixed point theory and the Banach fixed point theorem is elegant and well-executed.

2. **Clear Convergence Guarantees**: The authors establish precise conditions for global optimality (ρ(A) < 1) that can be checked before training begins, without requiring the algorithm to run first. This is a significant practical advantage over previous approaches.

3. **Linear Convergence Rate**: The paper demonstrates linear convergence (‖(w^k,u^k)-(w*,u*)‖_∞ ≤ Rρ(A)^k), which is much faster than typical SGD convergence rates.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The authors provide empirical evidence of their method's performance on UCI datasets, showing competitive results compared to ReLU networks and SVMs, and demonstrating the linear convergence rate in practice.

5. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with a clear progression from problem statement to theoretical development to experimental validation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Restrictive Assumptions**: The requirement for nonnegative weights is a major limitation. Most practical neural networks use both positive and negative weights, and this constraint significantly limits the expressiveness of the model. The paper doesn't adequately address how this limitation affects the model's ability to represent complex functions.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for the Modified Objective**: The authors add the negative total sum of outputs to the objective function to ensure strict positivity of the gradient. While this is technically necessary for the proof, the paper doesn't provide sufficient intuition or justification for why this modification doesn't harm performance.

3. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The experiments are restricted to low-dimensional UCI datasets. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't explore how the method scales to high-dimensional problems, where neural networks typically excel. The authors note that the spectral radius grows with the number of hidden units, which would limit the method's applicability to larger networks.

4. **Comparison with Relevant Work**: While the paper mentions Janzamin et al. and Haeffele & Vidal, it doesn't thoroughly compare with more recent work on global optimization of neural networks, such as the work by Zhang et al. (2016) on convex relaxation approaches.

5. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail on how to choose the hyperparameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) in practice. The authors mention they select these through random search and cross-validation, but this is a significant limitation for a method claiming to be "practically feasible" without hyperparameter tuning.

## Quality Assessment
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical proofs and clear convergence guarantees. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, though the scope is limited. The authors are careful to note the limitations of their approach (nonnegative weights, restricted to low-dimensional datasets), but could have been more explicit about the implications of these limitations for real-world applications.

## Clarity Assessment
The paper is well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation. However, the dense mathematical content may be challenging for readers without a strong background in nonlinear spectral theory. The supplementary material is appropriately used for some proofs, which is standard practice for theoretical papers. The experimental section is clear and well-presented.

## Originality Assessment
The paper makes a novel contribution by bridging nonlinear spectral theory with neural network training. The specific combination of nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activation functions, and the modified objective function is original. The work builds on but significantly extends prior research on global optimization of neural networks.

## Significance Assessment
The significance of the work is tempered by the restrictive nonnegative weight assumption. While the theoretical guarantee of global optimality is valuable, the practical impact is limited by the constraint that makes the method unsuitable for most real-world neural network applications. The paper provides a theoretical stepping stone but doesn't yet demonstrate a practical alternative to SGD for most applications.

## Recommendation

**Accept with reservations**

This is a technically strong paper with an important theoretical contribution to the understanding of global optimization in neural networks. However, the restrictive nonnegative weight assumption significantly limits the practical applicability of the method. The authors should address the following concerns before publication:

1. Provide a more thorough discussion of the implications of the nonnegative weight constraint for the expressiveness of the model.
2. Include a more detailed comparison with recent work on global optimization of neural networks.
3. Discuss how the method might be extended to handle negative weights (even if only as future work).
4. Provide more guidance on practical hyperparameter selection beyond random search and cross-validation.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field, but the practical limitations should be more clearly acknowledged. The authors have shown that global optimality is achievable for a specific class of neural networks, but the method's applicability to real-world problems remains limited.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Summary

**Pro:**
- First practically implementable globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks
- Rigorous theoretical guarantees with clear conditions for convergence
- Linear convergence rate (advantage over typical SGD)
- Competitive results on small UCI datasets
- Clear, well-structured presentation of theoretical development

**Con:**
- Nonnegative weight constraint significantly limits practical applicability
- Experimental results limited to low-dimensional datasets
- Lack of justification for the modified objective function
- Limited guidance on hyperparameter selection
- Method doesn't address the most common neural network architectures

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that should be of interest to the NIPS community, but the practical limitations should be more thoroughly addressed to justify its significance for the broader machine learning community.
