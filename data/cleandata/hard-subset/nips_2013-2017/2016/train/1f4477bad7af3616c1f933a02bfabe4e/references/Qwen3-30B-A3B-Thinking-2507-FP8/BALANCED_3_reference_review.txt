

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper introduces a novel "nonlinear spectral method" for training generalized polynomial neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors build upon recent work by Haeffele and Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015) on global optimality in neural networks, but make a significant contribution by connecting the problem to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous problems. The key innovation is a globally optimal algorithm that requires only nonnegative weights and data, with convergence guarantees that can be checked via spectral radius of a small matrix.

**Strengths:**
The paper's main theoretical contribution is strong and novel. The connection to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory provides a fresh perspective on neural network optimization, and the authors successfully develop the necessary mathematical framework to establish global optimality guarantees. The convergence rate is linear, which is a significant advantage over stochastic gradient methods that require careful hyperparameter tuning. The paper also provides the first experimental results for a globally optimal neural network training method on real datasets, which is a notable contribution given previous global optimality works (like Janzamin et al., 2015) lacked empirical validation.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary limitation is the restrictive assumption of nonnegative weights and data, which severely limits the method's applicability. The paper states nonnegativity is "true for all sorts of count data such as images, word frequencies," but this is misleading—most image data (after preprocessing) has zero or negative values, and word frequencies are nonnegative but typically processed with log transformations or other operations that introduce negative values. The authors don't sufficiently justify why this constraint is acceptable or how it affects the model's representational capacity.

The scope is also limited to 1-2 hidden layers, which is far from the deep networks used in practice. While the authors mention generalization to deeper networks, the theoretical results are only provided for 2 layers, and the experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets. The experimental comparison with ReLU networks is unfair since ReLU networks allow negative weights, a fundamental difference in the model class. The paper doesn't discuss how this difference affects the performance comparison.

The modified objective function (adding the sum of all outputs) is introduced without sufficient theoretical justification. The authors state it's needed to ensure the gradient is strictly positive, but don't explore how this modification affects the optimization landscape or generalization properties. The bounds in Theorem 1 are acknowledged to be conservative, which weakens the practical claim of "practical feasibility."

**Originality and Significance:**
The connection to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory is original and well-motivated. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the restrictive assumptions. While the method provides a theoretically sound approach to global optimality, the practical relevance is questionable given the nonnegativity constraints. The paper would be stronger if it more thoroughly addressed how the method compares to state-of-the-art methods in terms of actual performance on relevant benchmarks.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written with clear mathematical notation and logical flow. The proofs are structured well, and the connection to previous work is appropriately contextualized. The supplementary material provides additional technical details that support the main claims.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to global optimality in neural network training, but the restrictive assumptions and limited scope significantly reduce its practical significance. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical applicability is questionable due to the nonnegative weight constraint.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. More thoroughly justify the nonnegative weight constraint and discuss how it affects representational capacity. Consider experiments with nonnegative data (e.g., word counts) to better demonstrate practical relevance.
2. Provide a more balanced comparison with standard methods, acknowledging the difference in model class (nonnegative vs. standard weights).
3. Explore whether the nonnegativity constraint can be relaxed or approximated in practice.
4. Extend the experimental results to higher-dimensional datasets and deeper architectures to better demonstrate practical utility.
5. Discuss the trade-offs between the global optimality guarantee and the restrictive assumptions in more depth.

While the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution, the practical limitations prevent it from being a strong candidate for NIPS. With more thorough exploration of the practical implications and limitations, this work could become significantly more impactful. As it stands, the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and practical applicability.
