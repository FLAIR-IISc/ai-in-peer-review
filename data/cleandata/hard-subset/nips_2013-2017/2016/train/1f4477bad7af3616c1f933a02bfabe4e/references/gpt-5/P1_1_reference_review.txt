Summary
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method that provably converges linearly to a unique global optimum for training a class of nonnegative feedforward neural networks with generalized polynomial activations. The model assumes nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights, normalized in p-norm, and uses a modified objective: negative cross-entropy plus the sum of the network outputs, along with a small positive bias to ensure strict positivity of gradients. The training algorithm is a fixed-point iteration GΦ based on normalized positive gradients (via ψp′) whose contraction is established in the Thompson metric on a product of positive cones. A checkable sufficient condition for global optimality reduces to requiring the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small nonnegative matrix (size K+1 for one hidden layer, K+2 for two) to be below 1; this depends on architecture and norm parameters, not on labels. The paper develops the contraction analysis via multi-homogeneous Perron-Frobenius theory, gives explicit bounds on Lipschitz constants, and extends the theory to two hidden layers. Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy vs. small ReLU networks and RBF-SVM, and a clear linear convergence advantage over SGD when optimizing the same modified objective.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against two strands: (i) global optimality via structured factorization (Haeffele & Vidal), which the authors note is difficult to apply, and (ii) tensor methods needing score-function estimation (Janzamin et al.). Here, global convergence is obtained through nonlinear spectral (Perron-Frobenius) methods on cones and contraction in the Thompson metric, which is a distinct and interesting approach. The paper also connects to multi-homogeneous PF theory and classical nonnegative matrix theory for bounding spectral radii.

Strengths
- Novelty: A genuinely different path to global training guarantees using nonlinear spectral theory, with a clear and elegant fixed-point iteration and a linear convergence rate.
- Checkable condition: ρ(A) < 1 can be computed prior to training from architecture/data norms; no step-size tuning is needed.
- Clarity of theory: The high-level proof strategy (interior maximizer, fixed-point characterization, contraction in Thompson metric, Banach fixed point) is well structured. The extension to two hidden layers is nontrivial and worked out.
- Practicality within scope: For nonnegative count data (images with nonnegative pixels, word counts), the assumptions are plausible; the algorithm is simple to implement.
- Empirical evidence: Proof-of-concept results demonstrate fast convergence and competitive accuracy on several small datasets, plus a direct comparison to SGD on the same objective.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- The guarantees hinge on several strong modeling choices: nonnegative inputs and weights; generalized polynomial activations with exponents αl ≥ 1 and moreover all distinct; p-norm constraints with fixed radii ρu, ρw; and a modified objective that adds the sum of outputs plus a small positive bias. While the paper motivates these as needed for strict positivity and contraction, the statistical implications are under-discussed. In particular, adding ∑r fr(x) to the loss could bias predictions; it would help to bound or characterize the effect (e.g., show that for ε→0 the solution remains close to the cross-entropy optimum within the constrained class).
- The spectral-radius condition can be conservative and appears to scale poorly with the number of hidden units (as acknowledged). In practice the method may preclude larger networks unless p’s are chosen large, which changes the feasible set and may harm expressivity. More empirical characterization of when ρ(A) < 1 holds in realistic settings would strengthen the paper.
- The distinct-α requirement to avoid permutation-induced degeneracy is unusual and may be fragile in practice; a clearer argument and ablations would help (e.g., show failure modes when α’s are equal, and how much distinctness is needed).
- Some proof details are deferred to the supplement; while acceptable, key derivations (e.g., the bounds defining A and Ψ) could use more intuition in the main text.

Clarity
- The exposition is mathematically dense but mostly coherent. Some notation is heavy (e.g., ψp, index conventions), and a few definitions (the loss L) are easy to miss in the flow; tightening notation and giving a small worked example of GΦ updates would help reproducibility.
- The motivation for the sum-of-outputs term and the small ε is critical and should be explained more intuitively, with a discussion of their empirical impact.

Originality
- High. Applying multi-homogeneous Perron-Frobenius and Thompson-metric contractions to globally train nonnegative polynomial NNs appears novel, distinct from both convex factorization and tensor-score approaches.

Significance
- Mixed. The theoretical contribution is significant for the theory community: a globally convergent, linearly convergent algorithm with a simple checkable condition. However, the practical scope is limited by strong constraints (nonnegativity, fixed α’s, norm normalizations, modified objective). Empirical results are small-scale and not state-of-the-art; still, they effectively demonstrate feasibility and speed.

Questions for the authors
- Can you quantify the effect of adding ∑r fr(x) on classification calibration or margins? Is there a bound relating the modified objective optimum to pure cross-entropy under your constraints?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of α and to ensuring αi ≠ αj? Could α be learned (e.g., jointly with weights) while maintaining contraction?
- Can the nonnegativity requirement be relaxed by reparameterizations (e.g., splitting features/weights into positive/negative parts) while preserving the theory?
- What is the per-iteration complexity vs. SGD in practice, and how does it scale with n, d, K, n1?
- How often does ρ(A) < 1 fail as networks scale, and are there tighter bounds that reduce the conservatism?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical ablations on the sum-of-outputs term and ε; show accuracy vs. ε.
- Include datasets with negative features after a standard nonnegativity transform and discuss the impact.
- Add runtime plots and per-iteration cost analysis.
- Offer guidance for choosing α, pw, pu, ρw, ρu beyond random search; perhaps a heuristic informed by your bounds.
- Temper claims about practicality and clarify the regimes where the method is useful.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and rigorous global convergence framework using nonlinear spectral methods.
- Simple, parameter-free iteration with linear rate and checkable preconditions.
- Extension to two hidden layers and generalizable proof machinery.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong modeling constraints and nonstandard objective limit applicability and make comparisons to standard neural training less compelling.
- Conservative conditions that may preclude larger models; limited empirical validation.
- Open questions about statistical properties and hyperparameter selection (α, norms).

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theory is elegant and original, but practical impact is currently limited by restrictive assumptions and a modified objective with unclear statistical consequences. With stronger empirical validation and more guidance on model selection and the effect of the added terms, this could be a solid accept in a future iteration.

Confidence
High in assessment of theory and limitations; medium in empirical conclusions due to small-scale experiments.
