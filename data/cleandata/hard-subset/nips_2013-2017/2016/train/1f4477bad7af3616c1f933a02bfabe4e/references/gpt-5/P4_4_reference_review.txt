Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a constrained class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The networks use nonnegative weights and nonnegative inputs, a one- or two-hidden-layer architecture, and generalized polynomial activations with (possibly non-integer) exponents α ≥ 1, distinct per hidden unit. The objective is a modified cross-entropy: maximize negative cross-entropy plus a sum of outputs and small linear terms in the parameters; parameters are constrained to lie on Lp-norm spheres. The algorithm is a fixed-point iteration defined by normalizing componentwise powers of the gradient (a Perron–Frobenius inspired update). The key guarantee: if the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 for one hidden layer; K+2 for two) is below 1, then the map is a contraction in the Thompson metric, the unique fixed point is in the positive orthant interior, and it is the unique global maximizer with linear convergence rate O(ρ(A)^k). The spectral radius condition depends on architecture hyperparameters, Lp exponents, norm radii, and a bound on the input norms. The paper provides conservative sufficient bounds that ensure ρ(A) < 1, and an extension to two hidden layers. Experiments on several small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy with ReLU baselines and slightly worse than RBF-SVM, and demonstrate fast, linear convergence of the proposed method on its objective.

The work connects to: globally optimal training under structural/regularity assumptions via matrix/tensor factorization [8, 11], Perron–Frobenius and nonlinear eigenproblems on cones [7, 14, 18], polynomial networks [15], and recent analyses of neural loss surfaces [5] and optimization/generalization of SGD [9]. In contrast to [11], it avoids score function estimation; compared to [8], it yields an implementable procedure with a simple a priori check (ρ(A) < 1).

Strengths:
- Clear, checkable global optimality condition: compute the spectral radius of a small matrix determined by architecture and input norms. No learning-rate tuning is needed.
- Solid fixed-point framework: establishes existence, uniqueness, and linear convergence via contraction in the Thompson metric; the theory is internally consistent and leverages multi-homogeneous PF theory.
- Extension beyond one hidden layer with analogous guarantees.
- First empirical proof-of-concept, to my knowledge, of a practically implemented globally convergent training algorithm for a nontrivial (albeit constrained) neural model.
- Reproducible algorithmic recipe, including conservative parameter choices ensuring ρ(A) < 1.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment:

Quality (technical soundness):
- The main proof strategy (interior solution via strict positivity of gradients; equivalence between critical points and fixed points; contraction via Lipschitz bounds encoded by A; Banach fixed point theorem) is coherent. However, many technical details are deferred; some key bounds (Theorems 1/4/5) rely on conservative estimates. The paper acknowledges suboptimal constants; practical tightness remains unclear.
- The requirement of strictly positive gradients is enforced by adding small linear terms and the sum of outputs to the objective. While epsilon is stated to be arbitrarily small, the effect of the sum-of-outputs term on the solution (bias toward larger activations) is not theoretically characterized.
- The necessity of distinct α per hidden unit to avoid permutation symmetry and “trivial” solutions is plausible; the rank-one argument is sketched but would benefit from a more rigorous treatment.
- The guarantee depends on input nonnegativity, weight nonnegativity, and equality norm constraints. These are strong modeling constraints; their impact on expressivity and calibration of cross-entropy is only briefly discussed.

Clarity:
- Overall organization is reasonable, but there are several presentation issues: the cross-entropy definition is missing in the typeset excerpt; some equations are truncated; notation is dense; references have missing years and incomplete citations. More intuition around the role of the added output-sum term and the normalization constraints would help.
- Computational complexity per iteration and memory footprint are not discussed; practical runtime scaling with n, d, n1, K should be reported.
- The function Ψ and sets J, Jc are introduced with minimal intuition; a brief explanation of how these terms arise in the derivative bounds would improve readability.

Originality:
- Applying nonlinear Perron–Frobenius and Thompson-metric contraction to globally train generalized polynomial neural networks under nonnegativity is, to my knowledge, novel within the neural networks literature. The a priori spectral-radius check is a neat contribution.
- Related threads (tensor methods [11], structured matrix factorization [8], polynomial nets [15]) are properly acknowledged, and the differences are clear.

Significance:
- The theoretical contribution is interesting and could stimulate further research on globally convergent training under structural constraints. However, practical impact is limited by strong assumptions: nonnegative inputs and weights, nonstandard objective, distinct α per unit, and a spectral-radius condition that likely gets harder to satisfy as model size grows (ρ(A) increases with hidden units, input/weight radii).
- Experiments are small-scale and show competitive but not SOTA performance; no medium/large-scale benchmarks are presented. The applicability to typical datasets with signed features is not discussed (e.g., preprocessing strategies).

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Provide a clear statement and discussion of the cross-entropy form used, the positivity of logits, and how the added sum-of-outputs term affects classification calibration.
- Analyze representational capacity of the proposed class under nonnegativity and distinct α, and the effect of the added linear term as ε → 0.
- Report computational cost per iteration and end-to-end training time vs. SGD for comparable model sizes; include scaling experiments with n1, d, K.
- Discuss preprocessing to handle datasets with negative features; quantify sensitivity to rescaling (ρx) and how it affects ρ(A).
- Empirically explore how often practical architectures satisfy ρ(A) < 1 and how tight the sufficient bounds are.
- Clarify and formalize the argument about distinct α avoiding permutation degeneracy.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled global optimality guarantee with a practical, checkable condition.
- Solid fixed-point analysis with linear convergence; extension to two layers.
- First proof-of-concept empirical results for a globally optimal NN training method.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong modeling constraints and a modified objective limit practical relevance.
- Conservative bounds; unclear scalability as model size grows.
- Presentation issues and missing details; limited empirical evaluation and analysis of computational cost.

Overall recommendation: Borderline. The theoretical contribution is interesting and original, but the practical scope is currently limited and the exposition needs polishing. A stronger empirical section and clearer discussion of assumptions’ implications would strengthen the case.
