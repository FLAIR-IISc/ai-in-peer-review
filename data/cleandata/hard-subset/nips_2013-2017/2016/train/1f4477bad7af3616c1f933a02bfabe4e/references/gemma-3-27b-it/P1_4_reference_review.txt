## Peer Review for "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a fascinating and ambitious approach to training neural networks, offering a globally optimal algorithm for a specific class of networks – generalized polynomial networks with non-negative weights. While the theoretical results are strong and the claimed linear convergence is compelling, the experimental validation is limited. The paper is well-written and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature, but a more thorough exploration of the practical limitations and a more extensive experimental evaluation are needed. I lean towards a **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns outlined below.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas:**

The authors address the challenging problem of training neural networks globally optimally. They move beyond stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and tensor methods, proposing a "nonlinear spectral method" based on Perron-Frobenius theory. The key contribution is an algorithm that *guarantees* global optimality for a single-hidden-layer feedforward network with generalized polynomial activation functions and non-negative weights. This guarantee hinges on checking the spectral radius of a small matrix derived from the network’s architecture and parameters, a computationally inexpensive operation.  The authors provide a detailed theoretical framework for the algorithm and demonstrate its applicability to networks with up to two hidden layers. The method requires modifying the objective function to include a regularization term promoting non-zero outputs.

**2. Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The core strength of this paper lies in its rigorous theoretical foundation. The authors provide a clear convergence proof and a practical check (spectral radius calculation) to ensure global optimality.  This is a significant contribution, as it offers a departure from the typical challenges of non-convex optimization in deep learning.
*   **Novel Approach:** The application of nonlinear spectral methods and Perron-Frobenius theory to neural network training is a novel and insightful approach. It opens up new avenues for exploring alternative optimization strategies.
*   **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression of ideas. The authors explain the mathematical concepts in a reasonably accessible manner.
*   **Linear Convergence:** The claim of linear convergence is particularly attractive, suggesting efficient training compared to iterative methods like SGD.
*   **Addresses a Key Problem:** The pursuit of global optimality in neural network training is a central challenge in the field, making this work highly relevant.

**3. Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Assumptions:** The requirement of non-negative weights is a significant limitation. While justified by the theoretical framework, this constraint significantly restricts the expressiveness of the network and may limit its applicability to real-world problems. ReLU activation functions are therefore not usable.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is relatively weak. The results on UCI datasets are competitive but not demonstrably superior to existing methods. The comparison with SGD is limited, and the authors acknowledge the need for better parameter tuning. More extensive experiments are needed, ideally on more complex datasets and tasks.
*   **Conservatism of Bounds:** The authors admit that the bounds derived for ensuring ρ(A) < 1 might be conservative. This suggests that the method could potentially be applicable to a wider range of network architectures and parameters than currently indicated. Investigating and tightening these bounds would be beneficial.
*   **Impact of Modified Objective:** The addition of the sum of outputs term in the objective function feels somewhat ad-hoc. The authors provide justification, but a more detailed analysis of its impact on the learned representations and the resulting classifier's performance would be valuable.
*   **Scalability Concerns:**  While the spectral radius check is efficient, the scalability of the method to very large networks (many hidden units, deep architectures) is not fully addressed. The memory requirements associated with storing and manipulating the matrices involved could become a bottleneck.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

*   **Non-negativity Constraint:** Could the authors discuss potential strategies for mitigating the limitations imposed by the non-negativity constraint? Could techniques like shifted activations or different parameterizations be explored to expand the expressiveness of the network while retaining the theoretical guarantees?
*   **Experiment Design:** The choice of datasets seems limited to low-dimensional problems. It would be valuable to test the method on higher-dimensional datasets, even if it requires some modifications to handle the increased computational complexity.
*   **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods (e.g., more sophisticated optimizers, regularization techniques) is needed to demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions a process of selecting the best parameters. How sensitive are the results to the choices of n1, α, ρw, and ρu?  A more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity would be useful.
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the algorithm, especially for larger networks. How does the cost scale with the number of hidden units and the dimensionality of the input data?
*   **Two-Layer Extension:**  The extension to two hidden layers is presented briefly. A more detailed discussion of the challenges and limitations associated with deeper architectures would be helpful.



**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   Provides a strong theoretical guarantee of global optimality, a significant achievement in the field.
*   Presents a novel and insightful approach to neural network training.
*   The paper is well-written and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions (non-negative weights) limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited and does not fully demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.
*   Scalability concerns remain unaddressed.

**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend a **weak accept**. The theoretical contributions of this paper are significant, and the proposed approach has the potential to be a valuable addition to the toolkit for training neural networks. However, the authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the limited experimental evaluation and the scalability concerns, to strengthen the impact of their work.  A more comprehensive empirical study and a more thorough discussion of the practical limitations are essential for convincing the community of the broader relevance of this research.
