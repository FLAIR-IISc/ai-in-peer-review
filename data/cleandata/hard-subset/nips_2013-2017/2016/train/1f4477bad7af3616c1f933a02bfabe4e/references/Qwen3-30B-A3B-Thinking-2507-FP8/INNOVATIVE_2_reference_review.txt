

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper introduces a novel approach to globally optimal training of neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods, a significant departure from standard stochastic optimization techniques. The authors propose a globally optimal algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative data, which they show can be efficiently implemented with linear convergence rate. The key insight is leveraging nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory to establish global optimality guarantees through spectral radius conditions, avoiding the computational challenges of previous approaches.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by connecting neural network training to nonlinear eigenvalue problems on convex cones. Unlike prior work by Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015), which require difficult-to-estimate score function tensors or have challenging practical implementation, this method only requires checking the spectral radius of a small matrix - a computationally feasible condition. The authors provide a detailed proof of global optimality with linear convergence rate, establishing a significant theoretical advance in the field.

The most compelling aspect of this work is its bold theoretical approach. While previous global optimization methods for neural networks (like the tensor approach) were either computationally intractable or required strong assumptions about data distributions, this method requires only nonnegativity of data - a condition satisfied by many real-world applications (images, word frequencies, etc.). The authors demonstrate that their constraint doesn't prevent expressive power, showing competitive performance on UCI datasets despite the nonnegativity constraint on weights.

The paper's main limitation is the strong nonnegativity constraint on both data and weights. This restricts applicability to problems where features naturally take nonnegative values (like count data), which is a significant limitation for broader machine learning applications. The experimental section is appropriately modest, acknowledging this as "proof of concept" and noting that they're limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets due to the conservative spectral radius bounds. While the results are competitive with SVMs and ReLU networks on these datasets, the paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why this method would be preferred over standard approaches in practice.

The technical quality is strong, with careful theoretical development and well-structured proofs. The connection to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory is elegantly developed, and the authors demonstrate the method's practical feasibility with their linear convergence rate. The experimental validation, while limited, is sufficient for a proof-of-concept paper.

The paper's significance lies in its novel theoretical approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning. While the nonnegativity constraint limits immediate applicability, the method opens a new direction for globally optimal neural network training. The authors have successfully established a theoretically grounded, practically implementable algorithm for a non-trivial neural network model - something that has been elusive in the field.

This paper represents a significant step forward in understanding global optimization of neural networks. The authors have made a bold theoretical contribution that, while currently limited by the nonnegativity constraint, opens up an entirely new approach to the problem. The method is theoretically sound, has a clear computational advantage (no learning rate tuning), and provides a practical path to global optimality.

I find the novelty and theoretical contribution to be substantial. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous global optimization approaches by providing a method that is both theoretically grounded and practically feasible. The reviewers' emphasis on valuing novel ideas over methodological perfection is well-served by this paper.

**Strengths**:
- First practically feasible globally optimal algorithm for non-trivial neural networks
- Clear theoretical connection to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory
- Linear convergence rate with no hyperparameter tuning
- Global optimality condition is easily checkable (spectral radius of small matrix)
- Competitive results on standard UCI datasets

**Weaknesses**:
- Strong nonnegativity constraint limits applicability
- Experiments limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets
- No clear guidance for parameter selection in practice
- Theoretical bounds appear conservative, limiting scalability

**Recommendation**: Accept

This paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a novel approach to globally optimal neural network training that is both theoretically sound and practically implementable. While the nonnegativity constraint is a limitation, the theoretical framework and proof of concept are strong enough to merit acceptance at NeurIPS. The paper successfully opens a new direction for research in global optimization of neural networks, which is a fundamental problem in the field. The authors have provided a clear theoretical foundation and initial experimental validation that demonstrates the method's viability, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
