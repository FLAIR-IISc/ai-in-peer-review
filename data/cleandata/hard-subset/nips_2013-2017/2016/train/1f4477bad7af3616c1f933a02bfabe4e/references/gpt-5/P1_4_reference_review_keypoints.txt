- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train feedforward nets with generalized polynomial activations, nonnegative inputs/weights, Lp-norm constraints, and a modified objective including output-sum and small linear weight term.  
- Algorithm: block-wise normalized gradient map GΦ inspired by multi-homogeneous Perron-Frobenius theory and contractions in the Thompson metric.  
- Main theorem: if the spectral radius ρ(A)<1 for a small nonnegative matrix A, GΦ is a contraction with a unique positive fixed point and iterates converge linearly to the global maximizer.  
- The ρ(A)<1 condition is checkable a priori from architecture and norm parameters but is conservative.  
- Framework extends to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees.  
- Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive performance with small ReLU nets, worse than RBF-SVM, and empirical linear convergence consistent with theory.  
- Novelty: first to combine multi-homogeneous PF theory, Thompson metric contractions, and a normalized gradient fixed-point map for explicit, checkable global training guarantees.  
- Theory quality: mapping, metric choice, contraction analysis, and role of output-sum/epsilon are well developed; linear rates and proof strategy are clear.  
- Key limitations: requires nonnegative data and weights, distinct per-unit activation exponents, and a modified objective that changes the hypothesis class.  
- Scalability concern: sufficient bounds to enforce ρ(A)<1 become very conservative as hidden units increase.  
- Experimental limitations: small datasets, limited baselines, no thorough sensitivity analysis (α, p, ρ, epsilon), and limited scaling tests.  
- Clarity issues: dense notation, missing equations/references, typos, and insufficient intuition/ablation for the modified objective and epsilon choice.  
- Practical guidance lacking on preprocessing to ensure nonnegative inputs and on choosing activation exponents α.  
- Suggested improvements: ablate the output-sum term, show preprocessing strategies, analyze sensitivity to α/ρ, report wall-clock times and iteration counts, test higher-dimensional data, tighten bounds for A, and improve presentation/reproducibility.  
- Pros: novel rigorous global convergence to a unique optimum with linear rates, simple parameter-free iterative algorithm, extension to two layers, and theory-matching empirical convergence.  
- Cons: strong modeling constraints reduce practical relevance, conservative conditions may not scale, limited experiments, and presentation/practical guidance gaps.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — valuable theoretical contribution warranting discussion despite practical and experimental shortcomings.
