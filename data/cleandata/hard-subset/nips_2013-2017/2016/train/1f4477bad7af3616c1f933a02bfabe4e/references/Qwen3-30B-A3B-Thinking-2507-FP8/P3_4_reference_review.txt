

# Peer Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary and Context

This paper proposes a novel approach to achieving global optimality in neural network training by imposing nonnegativity constraints on weights and using a generalized polynomial activation function. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method that converges linearly to the global optimum under conditions that can be verified by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix. This addresses a key limitation in prior work [8, 11] which provided global optimality guarantees but were "challenging to apply in practice."

## Quality

The paper makes a technically sound theoretical contribution. The main theorem (Theorem 1) provides rigorous conditions for global optimality with linear convergence rate, and the proof is carefully constructed using nonlinear spectral theory and Banach fixed-point theorem. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems [7] is well-motivated.

However, the paper has significant limitations:
1. The nonnegativity constraint on weights is extremely restrictive for practical applications (e.g., the method cannot match ReLU networks' performance on Iris dataset where ReLU achieves perfect accuracy).
2. The requirement for distinct α values for each hidden unit (to avoid trivial solutions) is not well-justified beyond the permutation argument.
3. The theoretical bounds for the spectral radius (Theorem 1) are highly conservative, likely making the method more restrictive in practice than the theory suggests.
4. The experimental validation is limited to small UCI datasets without scalability analysis.

The authors acknowledge these limitations but don't provide sufficient justification for why the constraints are acceptable for real-world applications. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how to relax these constraints while maintaining global optimality guarantees.

## Clarity

The paper is well-structured and clearly written for a technical audience. The mathematical notation is precise, and the connection to nonlinear spectral theory is well-explained. However, some aspects could be clarified:

- The motivation for adding the term ∑_r f_r(w,u)(x_i) to the objective function is not sufficiently explained.
- The parameter selection process (choosing n1, α, ρ_w, ρ_u) is described as "randomly generated combinations" without principled guidance.
- The experimental results could be presented more clearly with statistical significance analysis.

The paper's organization is logical, moving from theoretical foundations to specific applications, but the experimental section could better highlight the trade-offs between global optimality and model expressiveness.

## Originality

The connection between neural network training and nonlinear spectral methods is novel and represents a valuable theoretical contribution. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of prior global optimality approaches [8, 11] and demonstrates a method that is theoretically sound and practically verifiable.

However, the approach is limited by its specific constraints (nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations), which significantly restrict its applicability. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate from previous work on global optimization of neural networks beyond claiming "practical feasibility." The authors should more thoroughly address why their specific constraints are worth the trade-offs compared to other methods.

## Significance

The theoretical insights are valuable for understanding the relationship between structured matrix factorization and neural network training. The paper demonstrates for the first time a globally optimal training method that achieves non-trivial classification results on real datasets.

However, the practical significance is limited by the strong constraints:
- The method is outperformed by SVMs on most UCI datasets (Table 1).
- The nonnegativity constraint makes it unsuitable for many real-world applications where negative weights are essential.
- The experiments are confined to small-scale datasets without scalability analysis.

The paper makes an important theoretical contribution but doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the global optimality is worth the severe restrictions imposed on the model architecture.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following required revisions:

1. **Address the nonnegativity constraint limitations**: Provide a more thorough discussion of why this constraint is acceptable for real-world applications, and explore potential ways to relax it while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

2. **Improve experimental validation**: Include scalability analysis on larger datasets and better comparison with prior global optimality methods [8, 11]. The paper should clarify whether the observed performance gaps are due to the constraints or the algorithm itself.

3. **Refine the theoretical bounds**: Provide tighter bounds for the spectral radius or discuss how to improve the conservative estimates in Theorem 1.

4. **Clarify parameter selection**: Offer more principled guidance for choosing the architecture parameters (n1, α, ρ_w, ρ_u) rather than relying on random searches.

## Summary of Key Points

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical connection to nonlinear spectral methods
- Rigorous proof of global optimality with linear convergence rate
- Practical verification of the convergence conditions (spectral radius check)
- Demonstrates global optimality can yield competitive results on real datasets

**Weaknesses:**
- Severe nonnegativity constraint on weights (limits expressiveness)
- Conservative theoretical bounds (likely makes method more restrictive than necessary)
- Limited experimental validation to small UCI datasets
- No principled guidance for parameter selection

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network training. With the suggested revisions addressing the limitations of the nonnegativity constraint and improving the experimental validation, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the practical limitations significantly reduce the paper's significance despite its theoretical soundness.
