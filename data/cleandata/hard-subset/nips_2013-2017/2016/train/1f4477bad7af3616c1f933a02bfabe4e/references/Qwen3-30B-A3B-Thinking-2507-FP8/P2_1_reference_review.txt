

# Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel method for achieving globally optimal training of a specific class of neural networks, called "Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks" (GPNs), using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors introduce a modified optimization problem with nonnegative weights and data constraints, and prove that their algorithm converges to the global optimum under certain conditions on the spectral radius of a small matrix. The method is claimed to be the first "practically feasible" globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks, with linear convergence rate and no hyperparameters to tune.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution. The main theorem (Theorem 1) is rigorously proven by connecting the optimization problem to fixed-point theory and leveraging nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory. The proof strategy is well-structured, building on established mathematical concepts (Thompson metric, Banach fixed point theorem) and carefully addressing the necessary conditions for convergence.

The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach: the requirement for nonnegative data and weights, the need for α_i ≠ α_j, and the conservative bounds on the spectral radius. The experiments on UCI datasets provide reasonable evidence that the method can achieve competitive performance, though the authors appropriately note these are "proof of concept" results.

However, the theoretical contribution's practical significance is limited by the strong constraints imposed on the network architecture (nonnegative weights, nonnegative data, specific activation function form). The paper does not convincingly demonstrate why these constraints are necessary or how they might be relaxed for broader applicability. The experimental evaluation is also limited to small, low-dimensional datasets, which doesn't adequately test the method's potential for real-world applications.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The main contributions are presented logically, with a good balance between theoretical development and practical implementation. The notation is consistent throughout, and the algorithm is clearly described in pseudocode.

The motivation for the modified objective function (with the added term summing over all outputs) could be clearer. While the authors explain that this ensures the gradient is strictly positive, a more intuitive explanation would strengthen the paper. Similarly, the requirement for α_i ≠ α_j (to avoid trivial classifiers due to permutation invariance) is stated but not thoroughly justified in terms of practical implementation.

The connection to prior work (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.) is well-explained, and the paper clearly positions itself as addressing the "challenging to apply in practice" limitation of previous work.

## Originality

The paper makes a novel contribution by applying nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training, which is distinct from previous approaches that relied on tensor decomposition or structured matrix factorization. The authors' key insight is using the Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems to develop a practical globally optimal algorithm.

This work is indeed original in its approach to global optimality for neural networks, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by emphasizing practicality. The authors' claim that this is the first "practically feasible" globally optimal algorithm for neural networks is supported by the literature review, though the strong constraints on the problem make this claim debatable.

## Significance

The significance of this work lies in providing a theoretically grounded, globally optimal training method with linear convergence and no hyperparameters to tune. For the specific class of networks they consider, this represents a meaningful advancement over previous methods that either lack global optimality guarantees or are impractical to implement.

However, the significance is substantially limited by the strong constraints on the network architecture and data. Most real-world applications of neural networks do not involve nonnegative data or weights, making the method's applicability to practical problems questionable. The experimental results on UCI datasets show competitive performance but not significant advantages over standard methods, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why these constraints are acceptable for real-world applications.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper with a strong theoretical contribution, but its practical significance is limited by the restrictive assumptions required for global optimality. The method is elegant within its constrained framework, but the authors overstate its practical feasibility by not adequately addressing how these constraints limit real-world applicability.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of global optimality in neural networks, but its practical impact is questionable without evidence that the constraints can be relaxed or that the method offers advantages in more complex, high-dimensional settings.

## Recommendation

**Accept** (with minor revisions)

The paper makes a genuine theoretical contribution to the problem of global optimality in neural network training, which is a significant challenge in the field. While the constraints limit the practical applicability, the work provides important theoretical insights that could inform future research on globally optimal training methods. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and clearly positions itself relative to previous work.

The main weaknesses are the limited experimental evaluation and the lack of convincing argument for why the constraints are acceptable in real-world applications. I recommend that the authors:

1. Expand the experimental section to include more diverse datasets and potentially discuss how the method might be extended to handle nonnegative data constraints in more typical applications (e.g., via data transformation).

2. Provide a more thorough discussion of how the constraints (nonnegative weights, α_i ≠ α_j) affect practical implementation and how these might be addressed in future work.

3. Clarify the motivation for the modified objective function with a more intuitive explanation.

With these revisions, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field. As it stands, it is a valuable theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of global optimality in neural networks, even if its immediate practical impact is limited.
