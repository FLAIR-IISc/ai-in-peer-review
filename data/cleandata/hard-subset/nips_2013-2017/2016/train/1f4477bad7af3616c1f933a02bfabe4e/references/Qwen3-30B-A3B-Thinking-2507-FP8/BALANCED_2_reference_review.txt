

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel approach for globally optimal training of neural networks under specific constraints. The authors introduce a nonlinear spectral method that guarantees global convergence for generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative data, which they claim is the first practically feasible globally optimal algorithm for neural networks. The core contribution is a theoretical framework based on Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, with a convergence guarantee that depends on checking the spectral radius of a small matrix. The method is demonstrated on UCI datasets, showing competitive performance compared to ReLU networks and SVMs.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical novelty:** The paper provides a fresh perspective by connecting neural network training to nonlinear spectral theory, which is distinct from previous work on global optimality (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal [8], Janzamin et al. [11]). The use of the Thompson metric and Banach fixed point theorem is well-motivated and appropriately applied.

2. **Clear theoretical contribution:** The main theorem (Theorem 1) provides a concrete condition (ρ(A) < 1) for global optimality that can be checked without running the algorithm. The linear convergence rate is a theoretically appealing property not commonly found in global optimization approaches for neural networks.

3. **Well-structured proof:** The paper carefully builds the necessary mathematical machinery, with a logical progression from the optimization problem to fixed point theory. The proof strategy is sound and the key lemmas are well-motivated.

4. **Promising experimental results:** The method achieves competitive performance on UCI datasets, which is notable since other globally optimal neural network approaches (e.g., [11]) have not included real dataset results. The linear convergence rate demonstrated in Figure 2 is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Strong constraints limit applicability:** The requirement for nonnegative weights and nonnegative data (only applicable to count data like images or word frequencies) significantly restricts the practical utility of the method. The paper doesn't adequately address how this limitation affects the method's applicability to standard deep learning tasks with real-valued data.

2. **Experimental limitations:** The experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, which don't reflect the scale and complexity of typical deep learning applications. The paper doesn't compare with other globally optimal methods (since they haven't included real dataset results), making it difficult to assess the method's relative advantages.

3. **Parameter selection challenges:** The method requires careful selection of parameters (α, ρw, ρu, etc.) to satisfy the spectral radius condition, with the authors noting they need to test 100 random combinations. The paper lacks guidance on how to choose these parameters for new tasks.

4. **Theoretical bounds appear conservative:** The conditions for global optimality (Equations 4 and 9) require large values for pw, pv, pu, suggesting the theoretical bounds may be overly conservative. The paper doesn't discuss how to improve these bounds to make the method more practical.

5. **Limited discussion of practical challenges:** The paper doesn't address how the method would scale to larger networks or datasets, despite noting that "the magnitude of the entries of the matrix A... grows with the number of hidden units."

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of global optimization in neural networks. However, the strong constraints (nonnegative weights and data) significantly limit its practical relevance for the broader deep learning community. The experimental results are promising for the constrained setting but insufficient to demonstrate the method's value for real-world applications. The theoretical bounds also appear overly conservative, which would limit practical utility.

**Suggestions for improvement:**
1. Extend the experiments to more challenging datasets, including higher-dimensional data where nonnegativity constraints would be more restrictive (e.g., natural images with standard preprocessing).

2. Investigate whether the nonnegativity constraint can be relaxed through appropriate transformations or approximations, potentially broadening the method's applicability.

3. Provide a more thorough discussion of the relationship between the theoretical bounds and practical implementation, including empirical evidence of how conservative the bounds are in practice.

4. Compare with other globally optimal methods (where possible) to better position the contribution within the literature.

5. Include a discussion of computational complexity and scalability to larger networks and datasets.

The paper's theoretical contribution is sound and novel, but its practical value is limited by the strong constraints. With more extensive experiments on diverse datasets and better justification of the parameter selection process, this work could make a more significant contribution to the field. As it stands, the method is interesting but not sufficiently general to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
