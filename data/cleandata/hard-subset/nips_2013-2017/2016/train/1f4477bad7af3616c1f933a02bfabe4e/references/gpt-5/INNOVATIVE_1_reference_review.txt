Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method that globally and linearly converges to the unique optimum for training a class of nonnegative generalized polynomial neural networks. The network uses one (and is extended to two) hidden layer(s) with activations of the form (u^T x)^αl for αl ≥ 1 (not necessarily integers), nonnegative weights, and nonnegative inputs. The optimization objective is nonstandard: it maximizes the average of negative cross-entropy plus the sum of outputs, with an additional arbitrarily small positive term to ensure strict positivity of gradients, and with p-norm constraints on layers. The core algorithm is a normalized gradient map GΦ defined on the positive cone; by leveraging multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, the Thompson metric, and Banach’s fixed-point theorem, the authors derive a simple checkable sufficient condition for global convergence: the spectral radius ρ(A) of a (K+1)×(K+1) (or (K+2)×(K+2)) nonnegative matrix constructed from model parameters must be less than 1. They also show one can always make this true by choosing sufficiently large p exponents, albeit conservatively. Experiments on small UCI datasets indicate competitive performance against ReLU networks trained by SGD and RBF SVMs, and demonstrate linear convergence and speed.

The work is positioned against: Haeffele & Vidal (global optimality via structured factorizations but hard to use in practice) and Janzamin et al. (tensor methods requiring score functions and density knowledge). Here the method needs only nonnegativity and a spectral-radius check, independent of the data distribution.

Strengths
- Bold, novel conceptual direction: a practically implementable globally convergent algorithm for a nontrivial NN class, with a clear, verifiable condition (ρ(A)<1) and linear convergence rate. This is rare and valuable.
- Elegant theoretical framework grounded in nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction mappings on cones. The mapping, metric, and Lipschitz analysis are well-motivated.
- Simple algorithm with no learning-rate tuning; per-iteration cost is transparent.
- Clear extension to two layers with analogous guarantees.
- Preliminary experiments support fast convergence and nontrivial classification performance.

Weaknesses and questions
- Modeling constraints limit scope: inputs and weights must be nonnegative, activations are monotone powers, and layer norms are fixed. While many modalities (pixels, counts) are nonnegative, common pipelines use centering/whitening producing negative features. Discuss practical preprocessing pipelines and impact on performance.
- Objective is nonstandard: maximizing −cross-entropy + sum_r f_r(x) (+ ε-weight sums). Please clarify the exact form and the role/sign/scale of ε (its symbol appears to be missing in places). How does the added sum-of-outputs interact with cross-entropy (which includes log-sum-exp)? A small ablation would help.
- Expressivity: the network is monotone in inputs and uses nonnegative weights; moreover, αl must be mutually distinct to avoid symmetry-induced triviality. Provide a more systematic expressivity discussion: classes of decision boundaries realizable, limits relative to ReLU/polynomial nets; and practical guidance on choosing α.
- Condition ρ(A)<1: bounds are acknowledged as conservative and grow with hidden units, pushing toward large p. How large do p values become in practice? Any numerical stability issues? Report empirical gap between predicted ρ(A) and observed contraction rates.
- Experiments are modest in scale and performance is mixed vs. SVM/RELU. Given the claim of practicality, a slightly larger nonnegative dataset (e.g., MNIST without mean subtraction) would strengthen the case. Also consider a polynomial kernel baseline or polynomial network baseline for a closer comparison.
- Clarity/presentation: there are typos and formatting issues (e.g., missing ε in objective, stray figure axis annotations in the main text, duplicated/conflicting definitions of ρx with different norms, some references with missing years/venues). The loss L is referenced but not fully specified (assumed softmax cross-entropy); please define explicitly.

Quality
- The theoretical contribution appears technically sound, with a coherent proof strategy (gradient positivity in the cone, fixed point characterization, contraction under Thompson metric). Some key bounds are deferred to the supplement; given their centrality (Ψ function), ensuring they are complete and tight would be important.
- Experimental validation is proof-of-concept and honest about limitations; more breadth would help but does not negate the core theoretical contribution.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, but polish is needed. Define L explicitly; ensure ε appears where intended; reconcile ρx definitions; improve notation consistency; fix references.

Originality
- High. The combination of multi-homogeneous nonlinear spectral theory with NN training to obtain global optimality under checkable conditions and a simple iterative algorithm is novel.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: introduces a new global-optimality paradigm for a constrained yet nontrivial NN class. Even if immediate practical impact is limited, this can catalyze further work on globally convergent methods under structure (e.g., monotone/conic architectures, convex surrogates for deep models).

Pros
- Clear, bold new idea with rigorous guarantees and a practical check.
- Extensible framework (two layers shown).
- Parameter-free iteration with linear convergence.
- First to demonstrate globally optimal NN training with nontrivial empirical results.

Cons
- Strong modeling and objective constraints; limited expressivity relative to unconstrained nets.
- Conservative conditions may hinder scalability to larger/wider networks.
- Limited empirical scope; clarity/typo issues.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Rationale: Despite limitations and modest experiments, the paper introduces a fresh and well-founded global optimization approach for neural networks that is easy to verify and implement. Given the novelty and potential to open a new line of work, I am willing to overlook the preliminary empirical section and some presentation issues.

Confidence: 3/5

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and motivate the modified objective; include ablations on the sum-of-outputs term and the ε term.
- Provide guidance and analysis on selecting α (and possibly learning α?) and ρu, ρw.
- Report empirical ρ(A), contraction rate vs. observed convergence, and sensitivity to p.
- Add a larger nonnegative dataset (e.g., MNIST without centering) and a polynomial kernel baseline.
- Improve notation consistency and complete the bibliography. Consider releasing code for reproducibility.
