Summary
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of a constrained class of neural networks: generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights, with p-norm normalization constraints. The objective is nonstandard: maximize an average of “negative cross-entropy + sum of outputs,” plus small positive linear terms in the parameters to enforce strict positivity of the gradient. The training algorithm is a fixed-point iteration inspired by nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous maps; its convergence to a unique global maximizer is guaranteed when the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small (K+1)-by-(K+1) nonnegative matrix is < 1. The authors give versions for one and two hidden layers, linear convergence rate bounds, and proof templates using Thompson metrics, Banach’s fixed-point theorem, and Lipschitz bounds derived from gradient structure. Experiments on several small UCI datasets suggest competitive accuracy with ReLU MLPs and slightly worse than RBF SVMs, and faster optimization than SGD on their modified objective.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on globally optimal methods for structured nonnegative/multihomogeneous problems and connects to recent work on identifiability/optimality in deep models (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.). Compared to tensor methods that require score functions or density estimation, this method requires only nonnegativity and the spectral radius condition. The analysis is grounded in nonlinear PF theory and fixed-point contraction arguments.

Strengths
- Clear and principled algorithmic framework: a concrete fixed-point operator GΦ with a checkable spectral-radius condition and a linear convergence guarantee.
- Novel application of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory to a neural network training objective; the “no hyperparameters” claim (once the model is fixed) is appealing.
- Theoretical developments are generally rigorous at a high level (mapping positivity, contraction in Thompson metric, uniqueness via Banach).
- Extension to two hidden layers with a structured block matrix A.
- Provides the first empirical demonstration (albeit small-scale) of a globally optimal training method that yields nontrivial classifiers.

Weaknesses and concerns
Major technical/methodological:
- Objective mismatch and unclear probabilistic model: “negative cross-entropy” is invoked, but the exact loss is not defined. There is no explicit mapping from f(x) to class probabilities (softmax? one-vs-all logistic?). Adding ∑r fr(x) to the objective when maximizing can bias outputs upward; how does this interact with cross-entropy? The paper must precisely define L and the prediction rule.
- The ε-terms: The linear terms in weights (and the statement “ε > 0 can be arbitrarily small”) are crucial to strict positivity of ∇Φ and uniqueness. Yet there is no guidance, ablation, or sensitivity analysis. How small is acceptable in practice? How do ε and sample size interact (e.g., when gradients are near zero)?
- Nonnegativity and normalization constraints: The method requires nonnegative inputs and weights, and fixed p-norm constraints (‖wi‖pw=ρw, ‖u‖pu=ρu). This substantially changes the hypothesis class and scale geometry relative to standard training. The title/claims should better emphasize this restricted setting and modified objective; otherwise the connection to “training neural networks” is potentially misleading.
- Distinct α per hidden unit: The claim that equal α’s + uniqueness implies trivial rank-1 solutions rests on permutation invariance; however, the provided argument is informal and unconvincing. Either provide a formal theorem (with assumptions precisely stated) or temper the claim. Requiring each αl to be distinct is an unusual architectural constraint and should be better justified empirically/theoretically.
- ρ(A)<1 condition: While “always possible by taking pw,pu large enough” is stated, this changes the contraction metric rather than the model, and the bounds in Eqns (4)/(9) are very conservative. The paper should report actual ρ(A) values used, the chosen γ eigenvector, and the resulting bounds on iterations R, as well as per-iteration complexity. Currently, the practical tightness of the condition is unclear.
- Scalability: No complexity analysis or wall-clock comparisons beyond the toy figures. The method involves full gradients over the dataset; how does it scale with n, d, K, n1, n2? Can mini-batching be incorporated while preserving guarantees?
- Reproducibility: No code, no details on preprocessing to ensure nonnegative inputs (many UCI features can be negative; how were features transformed?), no reporting of ε, ρw,ρu choices beyond random sampling ranges, and limited details on stopping criteria and tolerance τ.

Clarity and presentation:
- Missing/unfinished equations and definitions: The exact cross-entropy loss is not presented after “defined as,” leaving a gap. Several equations and references appear truncated or malformed.
- Figure 2: y-axis “(p* − f)/|p*|” is not defined; p* is not explained. Axis labels are small/unclear.
- Algorithm box appears in the middle of the Experiments section, with notational dependencies not explained there.
- Many typos and reference issues: “th Conference…” appears mid-text; numerous references lack years, venues, or authors are garbled (“and Y, A Daniely, R. Frostigy, Singer.”; “general edition”; “radius basis function” instead of “radial”). Several references lack page numbers or proper formatting.

Experimental evaluation:
- Small datasets, modest or inferior performance to SVM on several benchmarks; no statistical tests, no confidence intervals, no multi-seed reporting.
- Baselines: ReLU MLP trained with a fixed-step SGD seems suboptimal; modern optimizers (Adam, momentum, schedules) and early stopping should be used for a fair comparison.
- Preprocessing and feature nonnegativity not described; potential leakage or scaling choices can affect polynomial features drastically.
- No ablations: effect of distinct vs equal α, of ε, of ρ(A) magnitude, of p-values.

Questions for the authors
1) Precisely define L (with equations) and the prediction rule used at test time. How does the ∑r fr(x) term affect calibration/decision boundaries?
2) What ε values are used? How sensitive is performance to ε? Can strict positivity be ensured without the linear term?
3) Report ρ(A), γ, and R for all experiments, with iterations and wall-clock times. How tight are the bounds?
4) Provide a formal proof or counterexample for the “distinct α” necessity; otherwise, justify this design choice empirically.
5) Describe preprocessing to enforce x ≥ 0 on each dataset; how does performance change if features are shifted/rescaled differently?
6) Can mini-batch variants preserve convergence (perhaps as nonexpansive maps in expectation)?

Pros
- Interesting and principled theory connecting fixed-point contractions to a novel training procedure.
- Checkable condition (ρ(A)<1) and linear convergence under that condition.
- First steps toward practical global optimization in a neural-like model.

Cons
- Objective and modeling choices deviate substantially from standard NN training and are insufficiently justified/defined.
- Clarity issues, missing definitions, and numerous formatting/reference errors.
- Weak/limited empirical validation and fairness concerns in baselines.
- Key architectural constraint (distinct α) not rigorously supported.

Recommendation: Reject
While the theoretical framework is elegant and potentially impactful, the paper in its current form has significant clarity issues, methodological gaps (loss definition, ε, preprocessing), unconvincing empirical validation, and numerous presentation/formatting problems. With substantial revisions, clearer problem framing, rigorous definitions, and stronger experiments, this line of work could be suitable for a future submission.
