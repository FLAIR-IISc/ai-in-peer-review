This paper presents a globally optimal training algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks with one or two hidden layers, leveraging nonlinear spectral methods. The core idea is to reformulate the training problem as a maximization problem on a convex cone, benefiting from the Perron-Frobenius theory to guarantee global optimality under certain conditions (non-negativity of weights and data, a constraint on the spectral radius of a related matrix). The authors demonstrate competitive performance on UCI datasets, showcasing the practical feasibility of their approach.

The paper builds upon recent work aiming for global optimality in neural network training, specifically citing Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015).  Unlike those works which either have practical limitations (Haeffele & Vidal) or rely on estimating the data generating measure (Janzamin et al.), this paper proposes an algorithm requiring only non-negativity of the data, a readily verifiable condition.  The method is presented as an alternative to stochastic gradient descent, offering guaranteed convergence to a global optimum without the need for parameter tuning. The extension to two hidden layers is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Global Optimality Guarantee:** The primary strength lies in the theoretical guarantee of finding the global optimum under the stated conditions. This is a significant contribution, as it addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning: escaping local minima.
*   **Clear Mathematical Formulation:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical framework, defining the optimization problem, the nonlinear spectral method, and the conditions for convergence.  The notation, while dense, is generally consistent.
*   **Novel Approach:** The use of nonlinear spectral methods and connection to Perron-Frobenius theory in the context of neural network training represents a novel approach.
*   **Extension to Two Hidden Layers:** Demonstrating the applicability of the method to networks with two hidden layers increases its potential relevance.
*   **Competitive Experimental Results:** While not state-of-the-art, the results on UCI datasets demonstrate that the method is viable and achieves competitive performance with other established techniques.
*   **Linear Convergence:** The stated linear convergence rate offers a significant advantage over the typically slower convergence of stochastic gradient descent.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Assumptions:** The requirement of non-negativity on both data and weights is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, its practical impact on the applicability of the method to real-world datasets needs further discussion. Many datasets naturally contain negative values.
*   **Conservatism of Bounds:** The authors acknowledge that the derived bounds on the spectral radius of the matrix A are likely conservative. This conservatism can lead to more stringent conditions for global optimality than are actually necessary, potentially limiting the method’s applicability.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  While the algorithm avoids the learning rate tuning of SGD, it introduces other parameters (e.g., *p*, *ρ*, *α*) whose optimal selection isn’t fully explored. The dependence of performance on these parameters and guidance for their selection would be valuable.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets. Evaluating the method on larger, more complex datasets would be crucial to assess its scalability and performance in realistic scenarios.
*   **Objective Function Modification:** The modification of the standard objective function (adding the sum of outputs) feels somewhat ad-hoc and requires stronger justification beyond simply ensuring a strictly positive gradient.
*   **Proof Complexity:**  The proof, while thorough, is very dense and relies heavily on numerous lemmas and theorems.  Improving the readability of the proof, potentially by moving more details to the supplementary material, would aid comprehension.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to globally optimizing neural network training.
*   The authors provide a clear and rigorous mathematical formulation of their method.
*   The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate the feasibility and competitiveness of the proposed algorithm.
*   The linear convergence rate is a significant advantage over standard training methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The strong assumptions regarding data and weight non-negativity significantly limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The conservatism of the derived bounds on the spectral radius may restrict the method's usefulness.
*   The limited scope of the experiments hinders a comprehensive evaluation of the method's performance and scalability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of neural network optimization. The guarantee of global optimality is a significant achievement. However, the strong assumptions and limited experimental evaluation raise concerns about the practical applicability of the method.  Despite these limitations, the theoretical results are compelling and warrant publication.  The authors should be encouraged to address the limitations in future work, particularly by exploring techniques to relax the non-negativity constraints and by conducting more extensive experiments on larger and more complex datasets.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity and experiment limitations in a revised version. Adding a discussion of the potential challenges and future directions for relaxing the non-negativity constraint would also be beneficial.
