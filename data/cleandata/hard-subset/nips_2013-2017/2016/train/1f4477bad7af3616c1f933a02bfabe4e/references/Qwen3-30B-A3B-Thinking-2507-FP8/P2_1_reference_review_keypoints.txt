- Proposes a novel globally optimal training method for Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks using nonlinear spectral methods.
- Introduces a modified optimization with nonnegative weights/data and proves global convergence under spectral radius conditions.
- Main theorem is rigorously proven using fixed-point theory, Thompson metric, Banach fixed point, and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory.
- Algorithm claims linear convergence and requires no hyperparameters.
- Strong restrictive assumptions: nonnegative data and weights, α_i ≠ α_j, and conservative spectral radius bounds.
- Authors acknowledge limitations but do not convincingly justify necessity or relaxation of these constraints.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to small, low-dimensional UCI datasets and serves only as proof-of-concept.
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, with consistent notation and clear pseudocode.
- Motivation for the modified objective (to ensure strictly positive gradient) lacks intuitive explanation.
- The requirement α_i ≠ α_j is stated but not fully justified in practical terms.
- Original contribution: applying Perron-Frobenius/multihomogeneous nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training.
- Claim of being the first "practically feasible" globally optimal algorithm is debatable given the strong constraints.
- Significance: meaningful theoretical advance for the specific network class but limited practical applicability to real-world problems.
- Overall: technically sound and elegant within its constrained framework, but practical impact is questionable.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
- Suggested revisions: expand experiments to more diverse datasets, discuss extensions or data transforms to handle nonnegativity, clarify practical implications of constraints (including α_i ≠ α_j), and provide a more intuitive motivation for the modified objective.
