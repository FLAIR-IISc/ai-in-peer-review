

# Review for "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights. The authors claim their method provides the first practically feasible algorithm for global optimality in neural networks, with a linear convergence rate and a condition for global optimality that depends on the spectral radius of a small matrix. The method requires nonnegative weights and a modified objective function that adds the sum of outputs to the loss. The paper includes theoretical guarantees for one-hidden-layer networks and extends to two-hidden-layer networks. Experiments on UCI datasets show competitive performance compared to SVM and ReLU networks.

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a theoretically grounded approach to global optimization of neural networks, which is a significant contribution to a challenging problem.
2. The theoretical framework is well-developed with clear theorems and proofs, and the connection to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory is well-motivated.
3. The paper correctly identifies limitations of previous approaches (e.g., tensor methods requiring data-generating measure) and provides a concrete alternative.
4. The experimental section provides a proof-of-concept showing the method can achieve non-trivial classification results.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies
The paper contains numerous formatting and notational inconsistencies that undermine its technical credibility:

1. Inconsistent mathematical notation: The paper uses "R+" for nonnegative reals (should be $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ or $\mathbb{R}_+$), "R++" for positive reals (should be $\mathbb{R}_{>0}$), and inconsistent subscripting like "‖u‖pu" (should be $\|u\|_{p_u}$).

2. In the definition of the function class (equation 1), the paper writes "n1∑ l=1" instead of the standard summation notation $\sum_{l=1}^{n_1}$.

3. The matrix A is defined as "A ∈ R (K+1)×(K+1) ++" which is confusing notation. It should be "A ∈ \mathbb{R}^{(K+1)\times(K+1)}_{++}".

4. The paper uses "p′" instead of "p'" or "p^*" consistently, which is confusing.

5. The paper references "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" but no figures are included in the provided text.

### Theoretical and Methodological Issues

1. The spectral radius condition ρ(A) < 1 appears extremely conservative. The paper states "we think that our current bounds are sub-optimal so that this choice is quite conservative," but doesn't provide any empirical evidence of how conservative these bounds are. The suggested parameter choices (equation 4) would require extremely large p_w, p_u values that would likely make the method impractical for real-world applications.

2. The claim that "it is always possible to find pu, pw large enough so that ρ(A) < 1" is technically true but misleading. The paper doesn't provide any practical guidance on how to choose these values, and the required values would likely be so large as to make the method unusable.

3. The requirement for nonnegative weights is a strong constraint that significantly limits the expressiveness of the model. The paper claims "the resulting classifier is still expressive enough to create complex decision boundaries," but provides only a single toy example (Figure 1, which is missing in the text) to support this claim.

4. The paper claims "up to our knowledge, we show for the first time a globally optimal algorithm for neural networks that leads to non-trivial classification results," but this claim is questionable. The paper doesn't properly compare with [15] (which analyzes polynomial neural networks) or more recent work on global optimization of neural networks.

### Experimental Limitations

1. The experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, which doesn't reflect the complexity of modern deep learning applications. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't address how the method would scale to high-dimensional problems.

2. The comparison with ReLU networks is unfair because ReLU networks don't have the nonnegativity constraint. The paper doesn't explore how to incorporate nonnegativity into ReLU networks for a fair comparison.

3. The paper states "the other papers which have up to now discussed global optimality for neural networks [11, 8] have not included any results on real datasets," but this is not entirely accurate. [8] does include some experiments, though they're limited.

4. The paper claims "competitive performance" but Table 1 shows only marginal improvements over SVM (e.g., 96.4% vs. 95.7% for Cancer dataset), which is not a strong claim for a novel approach.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents an interesting theoretical approach to global optimization of neural networks with a novel mathematical framework. However, the numerous notational inconsistencies, the extremely conservative bounds for the spectral radius condition, and the limited experimental validation undermine the paper's contribution. The method's practical applicability is questionable due to the requirement for extremely large p_w, p_u values to satisfy the spectral radius condition.

While the theoretical framework is sound, the paper fails to demonstrate that the method is practically feasible for real-world applications. The experimental results are weak and don't justify the claim of being "the first practically feasible algorithm."

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper has significant technical flaws and the experimental results are insufficient to support the strong claims made in the paper. The notational inconsistencies and mathematical errors make it difficult to assess the validity of the theoretical claims. While the theoretical contribution is potentially interesting, the practical limitations and lack of convincing empirical evidence prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS.
