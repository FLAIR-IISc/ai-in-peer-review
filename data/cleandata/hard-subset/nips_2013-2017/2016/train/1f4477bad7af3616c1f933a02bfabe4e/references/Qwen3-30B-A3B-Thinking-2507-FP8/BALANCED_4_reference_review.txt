

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel algorithm for globally optimal training of neural networks under specific constraints. The authors develop a "nonlinear spectral method" that guarantees global convergence to the optimum under conditions that can be checked via the spectral radius of a small matrix. The approach requires nonnegative weights and nonnegative data, and modifies the objective function by adding the negative sum of all outputs. The paper builds on recent work in global optimality for neural networks (Janzamin et al., Haeffele & Vidal), but introduces a novel connection to Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices and multihomogeneous problems.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a well-structured proof of global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The conditions for global optimality (Theorem 1) are practical to check (via spectral radius of a small matrix), which is a significant improvement over previous work that is "challenging to apply in practice" (Haeffele & Vidal).

2. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear notation, logical flow, and careful explanations of the proof strategy. The connection to fixed point theory and Thompson metric is elegantly presented.

3. **Novel Approach**: The use of nonlinear spectral methods inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory is a fresh perspective on neural network optimization. The paper successfully bridges theoretical matrix analysis with practical neural network training.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The authors provide a proof-of-concept on UCI datasets, demonstrating that their method achieves competitive performance with standard approaches (SVM, ReLU networks) while guaranteeing global optimality.

## Weaknesses
1. **Restrictive Assumptions**: The nonnegativity constraint on weights and data is extremely limiting. Most real-world data (e.g., images, text) requires negative values, and standard neural networks use both positive and negative weights. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this constraint is acceptable for practical applications.

2. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The experiments are confined to low-dimensional UCI datasets. The paper doesn't demonstrate scalability to larger, more complex datasets where neural networks typically excel. The claim that this is the "first practically feasible algorithm" for global optimality is weakened by the limited experimental validation.

3. **Parameter Selection Ambiguity**: The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to choose the parameters (α, n1, ρw, ρu) for real-world applications. The authors simply "choose the parameters out of 100 randomly generated combinations," which is not a practical approach.

4. **Comparison with Prior Work**: While the paper references previous global optimality work (Janzamin et al., Haeffele & Vidal), it doesn't sufficiently address why their method is more practical than these approaches. The paper claims "up to our knowledge this is the first practically feasible algorithm," but doesn't provide a detailed comparison with existing methods.

## Arguments for and against acceptance
**For acceptance**: The theoretical contribution is sound and novel, with a clear proof of global optimality under practical conditions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network training, and the method has a linear convergence rate. The clear writing and well-structured proofs are commendable.

**Against acceptance**: The restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights/data) significantly limit the practical applicability of the method. The experimental validation is insufficient to demonstrate the method's utility for real-world problems. The paper doesn't adequately address how to select parameters for real applications, making it difficult to implement.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the understanding of global optimality in neural network training. However, the extreme restrictions on the model (nonnegative weights and data) and the limited experimental validation prevent it from being a significant contribution to the field of machine learning as practiced. The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the assumptions severely limit its practical relevance.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. The authors should explore whether their method can be extended to handle negative values (perhaps through a transformation or modified activation functions) to increase practical applicability.

2. More extensive experiments on larger datasets (including image or text data) would strengthen the paper significantly. The current UCI results are insufficient to claim practical feasibility.

3. The paper should provide more guidance on parameter selection (α, n1, etc.) for real-world applications, rather than relying on random search.

4. A more detailed comparison with previous global optimality approaches (Janzamin et al., Haeffele & Vidal) would better justify the claim of being "practically feasible."

5. The authors should explicitly discuss the trade-offs between the global optimality guarantee and the restrictive assumptions, helping readers understand when this method might be preferable to standard approaches.

The paper demonstrates a valuable theoretical insight, but the practical limitations make it difficult to recommend for acceptance at NIPS. With more extensive experiments on real-world data and a better justification for the restrictive assumptions, this work could become a significant contribution to the field.
