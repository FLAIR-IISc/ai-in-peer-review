- Proposes a novel algorithm for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods.  
- Requires nonnegative weights and a modified objective (adds negative total sum of outputs) to ensure positive gradients.  
- Proves linear convergence to the global optimum when the spectral radius of a derived matrix A is < 1.  
- Extends the method and provides explicit convergence conditions for networks with one and two hidden layers.  
- Leverages nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory with a rigorous, technically sound proof and careful derivation of matrix A and its bounds.  
- Provides explicit bounds allowing the spectral radius condition to be checked without running the algorithm.  
- Lacks thorough guidance on selecting hyperparameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) for practical performance.  
- The nonnegative weights constraint is a strong limitation that reduces practical applicability and isn’t fully explored for trade-offs.  
- Experimental validation is limited to UCI datasets and small-scale tasks, lacking large or high-dimensional benchmarks.  
- Does not compare against other methods that enforce nonnegative weights, making relative benefit unclear.  
- Motivation for the modified objective function is brief and needs stronger justification.  
- Mathematical notation and exposition are clear, and the main theorem is well stated.  
- Novel application of nonlinear spectral/Perron-Frobenius theory to neural network training distinguishes the work from prior literature.  
- Significance is mainly theoretical: fills a gap by providing a practical, globally optimal algorithm without free parameters and with linear convergence, but practical impact is limited.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address practical limitations and better justify/compare the modified objective and parameter choices.
