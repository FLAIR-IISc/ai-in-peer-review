Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) to train a class of generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative data. The network computes class scores as sums of powered linear forms: fr(x)=∑l wr,l(∑m ulm xm)αl with αl≥1 (and pairwise distinct), w≥0,u≥0. The training problem is a maximization of a modified objective: negative cross-entropy plus a sum of outputs and small positive linear terms in the parameters, under norm constraints ‖wi‖pw=ρw and ‖u‖pu=ρu. The core contribution is a fixed-point iteration GΦ built from normalized gradients under a Thompson-type metric; if the spectral radius ρ(A) of an explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix (depending on architecture and data norms) is <1, then GΦ is a contraction with unique fixed point, which is the unique global maximizer of the training objective; linear convergence follows. The approach is extended to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees. Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy versus small ReLU networks and slightly below RBF-SVM, with very fast convergence in epochs.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical framework grounded in nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and Banach fixed-point theorem; the map GΦ is well-motivated and the contraction analysis via a weighted Thompson metric is elegant.
- Checkable sufficient condition for global optimality (ρ(A)<1) that depends only on architecture hyperparameters and a bound on the data norm; practical in that it can be evaluated pre-training.
- Extension to two hidden layers, with explicit constants, demonstrates the generality of the analysis.
- Linear convergence rate and uniqueness of the global optimum are strong guarantees (for the modified objective).
- First empirical attempt (to my knowledge) to demonstrate globally optimal training procedures producing nontrivial classifiers on real datasets.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/modeling:
- The global optimality pertains to a modified objective (negative cross-entropy plus ∑r fr(xi) plus ε-linear terms) under nonnegativity and norm constraints, not standard training. The added terms are introduced for technical reasons (positivity of gradients), but they change the optimization target. There is no analysis of calibration or how solutions relate to those minimizing cross-entropy. The claim of “practically feasible” globally optimal training is thus overstated.
- ε (“arbitrarily small >0”) is never specified in experiments; sensitivity to ε is not studied. If ε must be strictly positive to ensure ∇Φ∈V++, how small is small enough in finite precision?
- The requirement that each hidden unit uses a distinct αl to break permutation symmetry is unusual; α is not learned, and there is no principled guidance beyond random sampling. The “Choice of α” argument is informal and, as presented, not rigorous (e.g., the inference that permutation invariance plus uniqueness implies rank-one degeneracy needs a clean proof or a precise statement).
- The sufficient conditions (ρ(A)<1) rely on very conservative bounds; authors admit this. In practice they choose large p’s from (4)/(9) to force ρ(A)<1, but the implications of very large p on optimization geometry and generalization are not discussed.
- Expressivity under nonnegative weights is limited: each class score is monotone in inputs (with u≥0). The paper claims complex decision boundaries but provides no theoretical analysis of what decision regions are representable when all weights are nonnegative and activations are monotone powers; differences of monotone class scores can be non-monotone, but a discussion/characterization is missing.
- Computational cost per iteration: GΦ requires full gradients over the dataset. Complexity and scaling with n,d,K,n1 are not analyzed. The “faster than SGD” claim is based on epochs, not wall-clock.

Assumptions and data:
- The method requires nonnegative data. Many UCI datasets have signed features; preprocessing is not described (shifting/scaling?). The empirical section must state how nonnegativity was enforced.
- The matrix A depends on ρx=maxi‖xi‖; whether this uses ℓ1 or ℓp′u norms varies across sections (ρx vs ρ̃x); this inconsistency should be fixed and its effect on A clarified.

Clarity/organization:
- Many notational and formatting issues: e.g., stray “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)…” line in the middle of the introduction; inconsistent notation (R n1×d +, p′ vs p′w, ρx vs ρ̃x); missing definition of the cross-entropy loss; Figure references (Figure 1) without inclusion; axes in Figure 2 are unclear; equation numbering jumps; several equations use undefined symbols on first appearance (e.g., ψp without immediate recap).
- The exposition of Lemma 5 (positivity of ∇Φ) is crucial but its proof is deferred; at least a sketch with explicit bounds should be in the main text.
- The derivation of A is dense; the role and tightness of Ψαp,q are not intuitively explained; no empirical values of ρ(A) or comparison between Theorem 1 vs Theorem 4 bounds are reported.

Experiments:
- Small-scale, limited to a few low-dimensional UCI datasets; no experiments on canonical nonnegative data like MNIST (pixel intensities) or text (counts). The method’s sweet spot (nonnegative features) is not showcased convincingly.
- Hyperparameter selection: α (and β) are sampled randomly; ρw,ρu (and ρv) in (0,1] are also random. This seems ad hoc. The chosen p’s from (4)/(9) are not reported per dataset.
- Baselines: ReLU training uses SGD with a broad step-size grid up to 1e2 and regularization up to 1e4; there is no mention of learning rate schedules, momentum, or Adam. The comparison to “Batch-SGD” for optimizing (2) uses 5% mini-batches, while NLSM uses full-batch gradients; epoch-wise comparisons are not equitable; wall-clock timing is missing.
- Claims of being “competitive/slightly better than ReLU” are not supported by Table 1 (ReLU1/2 equal or better on multiple datasets; SVM dominates several). No statistical tests or variance across splits are reported.

Related work and citations:
- References are inconsistent/incomplete (missing years, author names garbled, e.g., “and Y, A Daniely, R. Frostigy, Singer.”; “general edition” for a monograph). Some relevant lines of work are not discussed (e.g., convex neural networks / positively constrained nets; calibration of modified losses). The positioning against [8,11,15] is fair but the “first practically feasible” claim needs tempering.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide explicit ε values used and a sensitivity study; report actual ρ(A) and contraction constants for each experiment.
- Clarify preprocessing to ensure nonnegativity; discuss how to handle general real-valued data.
- Analyze expressivity under nonnegativity and fixed α; can one learn α? Is there a convex reformulation in special cases?
- Fair baseline: full-batch gradient ascent on the same constrained manifold; report wall-clock and FLOPs; include modern optimizers for ReLU.
- Tighten A’s bounds or provide an empirical certificate that smaller p’s still yield contraction; include ablations varying p and ρ(A).
- Fix notation, define the loss explicitly, clean up figures, and correct the bibliography.

Pros
- Solid and interesting theory with a clear contraction argument.
- Checkable precondition independent of data labels.
- Extension to two layers.
- Linear convergence and uniqueness guarantees.

Cons
- Strong modeling constraints and modified objective; unclear practical relevance to standard training.
- Experimental evidence is weak and comparisons are not entirely fair.
- Many presentation/notation/reference issues.
- Important implementation details missing (ε, preprocessing, complexity).

Recommendation
Reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting, but the paper in its current form suffers from numerous clarity and methodological issues, limited and unconvincing experiments, and overclaims regarding practical feasibility. With substantial revision, stronger empirical validation on appropriate nonnegative datasets, and clearer positioning, this line of work could be impactful. My confidence is high.
