

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "nonlinear spectral method" for globally optimal training of neural networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative data. The authors claim their method achieves global optimality with linear convergence under certain conditions (specifically, that the spectral radius of a derived matrix is less than 1). The method requires a modified objective function and imposes severe constraints: nonnegative weights, nonnegative data, and a generalized polynomial activation function with real powers. The authors demonstrate competitive results on small UCI datasets but acknowledge the method is limited to low-dimensional problems.

## Strengths
The paper demonstrates technical soundness within its constrained setting, with a clear theoretical framework connecting nonlinear eigenproblems to neural network optimization. The proofs for the one-hidden-layer case appear rigorous, and the authors make an effort to connect their work to Perron-Frobenius theory and nonlinear spectral methods. The supplementary material provides additional proof details, and the paper acknowledges limitations in the theoretical bounds.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Severe Constraints Limit Applicability
The paper's core claim of "global optimality" applies only to a highly constrained setting: nonnegative weights (a severe limitation for neural networks), nonnegative data (rare in practice), and a modified loss function. As the authors themselves note, "nonnegativity of the weights is a strong constraint," yet they fail to adequately address how this constraint affects representational capacity. The authors acknowledge that ReLU networks (which allow negative weights) can achieve zero test error on Iris and Banknote datasets, while their constrained method cannot. This undermines their claim of "competitive performance" since their method cannot match the representational capacity of standard neural networks.

### 2. Limited Experimental Validation
The experimental validation is insufficient for a NIPS submission. The authors test only on small, low-dimensional UCI datasets (max 7 features), which do not reflect the scale of modern deep learning applications. The paper provides no evidence that the method scales to high-dimensional data (e.g., images) or complex tasks. The claim of "fast convergence" is based on a few iterations, but the paper doesn't show how the method would perform on larger datasets or with more hidden units (which the authors acknowledge would increase the spectral radius).

### 3. Theoretical Bounds Are Too Conservative
The authors explicitly state that their bounds for the spectral radius are "very conservative" and "sub-optimal." In fact, the conditions for convergence (e.g., Equation 4) require extremely large values of p_w, p_u to satisfy ρ(A) < 1, making the method impractical. The paper acknowledges that "the constant R in Theorem 1 can be explicitly computed when running the algorithm," but fails to show how this computation is feasible in practice. The authors' claim that the method is "practically feasible" is not supported by evidence.

### 4. Lack of Comparison to Relevant Work
The paper cites prior work on global optimality (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.) but fails to properly compare to recent methods that address similar challenges. For example, there are several recent papers (e.g., [15] cited in the paper) that provide theoretically grounded optimization methods for neural networks that don't require nonnegative weights. The paper doesn't explain why their approach is superior to these alternatives in terms of practicality or performance.

### 5. Misleading Claims of Novelty
The authors claim this is "the first practically feasible algorithm to achieve global optimality for a non-trivial neural network model." However, this claim is misleading. The paper's model is highly constrained (nonnegative weights, nonnegative data), making it trivial in the broader context of neural network optimization. The authors don't demonstrate how their method outperforms standard approaches on realistic tasks, which is the key criterion for "practical feasibility."

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a technically sound contribution within a severely constrained setting, but the constraints are so severe that the method has little practical relevance for real-world deep learning applications. The authors' claims of "global optimality" and "practical feasibility" are misleading given the nonnegativity constraints and the conservative theoretical bounds. The experimental validation is limited to small datasets and fails to address how the method would scale to realistic problems.

The paper would be more convincing if the authors demonstrated:
1) How the nonnegativity constraint could be relaxed while maintaining global optimality guarantees
2) Results on larger, more complex datasets
3) A clear comparison to other theoretically grounded optimization methods that don't require nonnegative weights

Without these, the paper's contribution is limited to a niche mathematical problem rather than advancing the field of deep learning optimization. The method's severe constraints and lack of practical validation make it unsuitable for publication at NIPS, where we expect work to address real-world challenges with sufficient generality.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper's claims of practical feasibility and global optimality are not supported by the evidence provided. The severe constraints on the model architecture and data make the method largely irrelevant for most deep learning applications. The experimental validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims, and the theoretical bounds are too conservative to be practically useful. The paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way for the broader deep learning community.
