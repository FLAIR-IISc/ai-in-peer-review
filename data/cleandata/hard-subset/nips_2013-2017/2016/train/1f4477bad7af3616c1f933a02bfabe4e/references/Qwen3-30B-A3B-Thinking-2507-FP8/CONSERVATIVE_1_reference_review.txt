

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel "nonlinear spectral method" for training neural networks with global optimality guarantees under restrictive assumptions. The authors claim to provide the first practically feasible algorithm for achieving global optimality in neural network training, requiring only nonnegative data and weights, and providing a linear convergence rate. The theoretical foundation builds on Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems.

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically rigorous contribution by establishing convergence guarantees for a specific neural network architecture under well-defined conditions. The proof strategy using fixed-point theory and Thompson metric is mathematically sound, and the authors carefully establish the necessary conditions for global optimality. The experimental section provides some validation on standard UCI datasets, which is a positive step beyond purely theoretical work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overly restrictive assumptions**: The requirement for nonnegative weights and nonnegative data severely limits the applicability of this method. The authors claim "all sorts of count data such as images, word frequencies" are nonnegative, but standard image data (even in pixel form) requires normalization to [0,1] or [-1,1], and word frequencies are typically nonnegative but not directly used as inputs without extensive preprocessing. More critically, the restriction to nonnegative weights fundamentally changes the representational capacity of neural networks, which typically rely on both positive and negative weights to model complex decision boundaries.

2. **Lack of meaningful comparison**: The experimental results compare only to simple baselines (ReLU networks and SVMs) without comparing to modern deep learning approaches. The authors claim to show "competitive performance" but fail to demonstrate how their method compares to state-of-the-art optimization techniques like Adam or L-BFGS on standard vision or language tasks. The comparison to SGD with different learning rates (Figure 2) is insufficient, as SGD is not the standard baseline for such theoretical work.

3. **Questionable practical value**: The paper claims "global optimality" but the model is fundamentally constrained (nonnegative weights) and the theoretical conditions (ρ(A) < 1) are very conservative. The authors themselves note their bounds are "sub-optimal" and "quite conservative," suggesting the practical applicability is limited. The need to add the term "sum of all outputs" to the objective for technical reasons further undermines the practical relevance.

4. **Novelty concerns**: The paper builds on established Perron-Frobenius theory, and the authors themselves cite work by Gautier et al. on "The Perron-Frobenius Theorem for Multi-Homogeneous Maps." The core idea of using spectral methods for neural networks is not new, with Haeffele and Vidal (2015) having previously explored similar connections. The authors acknowledge their approach is "challenging to apply in practice," which undermines their claim of providing a "practically feasible" algorithm.

5. **Limited experimental scope**: The experiments are confined to low-dimensional UCI datasets with small networks (2-20 hidden units). The authors acknowledge they "have limited ourselves in the experiments to low-dimensional datasets" due to the spectral radius growing with hidden units. This severely limits the relevance to contemporary deep learning applications, which typically involve high-dimensional data and deep architectures.

## Recommendation

While the theoretical contribution is mathematically sound, the paper's practical relevance is questionable due to its restrictive assumptions and limited experimental validation. The claims about "global optimality" for neural networks are misleading in the context of standard neural network training, where nonnegative weights are not a practical constraint. The authors fail to demonstrate how their method compares to modern deep learning approaches or explain why the restrictive nonnegativity constraint is justified for real-world applications.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more thorough comparison to recent optimization methods (e.g., adaptive gradient methods)
2. Experiments on more challenging datasets (CIFAR-10, ImageNet)
3. A discussion of how the nonnegativity constraint affects representational capacity
4. More realistic experimental validation of the convergence claims

Given these limitations, I recommend rejection. While the theoretical results are interesting, they do not advance the state of the art in a way that would be valuable to the broader machine learning community. The paper's claims of "global optimality" for neural networks are misleading without addressing the severe practical constraints that make the method inapplicable to standard neural network training scenarios.
