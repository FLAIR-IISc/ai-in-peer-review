Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that provably converges to a unique global optimum for training a restricted class of neural networks: generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative inputs, under Lp norm constraints on each layer. The method optimizes a modified objective that is the negative cross-entropy plus the sum of raw outputs (and an arbitrarily small positive linear term in the weights to ensure strictly positive gradients on the cone). The core algorithm is a fixed-point iteration GΦ defined via normalized gradients with componentwise ψp′ maps. The main theorem shows linear convergence to the global maximizer provided the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small nonnegative matrix A(K+1×K+1 for one hidden layer; K+2×K+2 for two layers), which depends only on architectural and normalization parameters, is strictly less than 1. The authors provide conservative sufficient bounds and a practical recipe to pick pw, pu (and pv) large enough to satisfy ρ(A) < 1. They extend the framework to two hidden layers and claim generalization to arbitrary depth.

Technically, the proofs leverage multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and the Thompson metric to show GΦ is a contraction on a product of positive cones, yielding uniqueness and linear convergence via the Banach fixed-point theorem. A key modeling detail is requiring distinct exponents αl ≥ 1 per hidden unit to break permutation symmetries that would otherwise force trivial solutions. Experiments on several small UCI datasets demonstrate very fast linear convergence and competitive accuracy relative to (carefully tuned) ReLU networks trained by SGD, though typically below RBF SVM.

This work relates to global optimality results for neural networks based on structured matrix factorization (Haeffele & Vidal) and tensor methods (Janzamin et al.), but differs by offering a practically checkable sufficient condition (ρ(A) < 1) that depends only on model parameters rather than data score tensors or difficult non-degeneracy conditions. It also connects to classic Perron–Frobenius theory and recent extensions to multi-homogeneous maps.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The mathematical framework is carefully constructed. The mapping GΦ, the choice of metric with learned weights γ from the Collatz–Wielandt characterization, and the derivation of contraction constants via a nonnegative matrix A are coherent and standard in the nonlinear PF literature. The existence/uniqueness and linear convergence claims are well supported under the stated assumptions. The reliance on strictly positive gradients is explicitly handled by the additive output/weight terms.
- Completeness: The one- and two-layer cases are worked out, with explicit A matrices and conservative bounds. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement. Complexity per iteration and implementation details are only briefly discussed.
- Empirics: Proof-of-concept experiments are appropriate given the theory focus. However, datasets are small/low-dimensional, and the method underperforms RBF SVM in most cases. The effect of the nonstandard objective and distinct-α constraint is not isolated via ablations.

Clarity
- The core ideas and results are presented clearly at a high level, and the fixed-point perspective is well motivated.
- There are clarity issues: the cross-entropy loss is referenced but not explicitly defined in the text; references are incomplete; there are minor typos; details on handling logits/softmax with positive-only outputs are not fully specified. The role and choice of the small ε term, and its practical effect, could be explained better.
- The rationale for distinct α’s is discussed but somewhat informally; a more formal statement of the degeneracy with identical α would help.

Originality
- Using nonlinear spectral methods with the Thompson metric to obtain global optimality and linear convergence for training a class of neural networks appears novel in the ML literature. The spectral radius condition depending only on architecture/normalization is also a distinctive contribution. The shift to generalized polynomial activations in a nonnegative cone is known, but the specific combination here is new.

Significance
- The theoretical contribution is meaningful: a practically checkable global optimality condition and an algorithm with linear convergence for a nontrivial (though restricted) neural architecture.
- Practical impact is limited by strong modeling constraints: nonnegative inputs and weights, fixed Lp norms at each layer, distinct exponents per hidden unit, and a modified training objective that adds the sum of outputs. The sufficient condition becomes more restrictive with more hidden units, which may hinder scaling. Empirical results on small datasets do not surpass strong baselines like RBF SVM.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, rigorous theoretical framework yielding global optimality and linear convergence under verifiable conditions.
- Novel application of multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory to NN training.
- Simple, parameter-free iteration (no step-size tuning) with fast convergence in practice.
- Extension to two layers and a path toward deeper networks.

Cons for acceptance
- Restricted applicability: requires nonnegative inputs/weights, fixed norms, and distinct exponents; no biases.
- Trains a modified objective (loss plus sum of outputs), with limited justification for its statistical properties.
- Sufficient condition ρ(A) < 1 can be conservative and worsens with network size; resorting to very large p-norm exponents may have unintended effects.
- Limited empirical validation on small datasets; performance generally below RBF SVM; lack of ablations (e.g., effect of ε or sum-of-outputs term).
- Clarity issues (incomplete loss definition, references).

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely define the loss (including whether f is passed through a softmax) and discuss how the added sum-of-outputs term impacts the classifier and calibration; include an ablation removing this term (or replacing it) to quantify necessity.
- Discuss preprocessing for negative-valued features (e.g., shifts), and include datasets with inherent negative inputs to assess robustness.
- Provide computational complexity per iteration and wall-clock comparisons to SGD across a range of sizes; report the typical number of iterations and how it relates to ρ(A).
- Analyze the tightness of the A bounds: empirical ρ(A) versus the conservative estimates; study how guarantees degrade with width.
- Explore alternatives to distinct α’s or show a formal result on degeneracy with identical α’s; provide guidance for choosing α in practice.
- Clarify the role and magnitude of ε and its effect on the solution in finite precision.
- Improve and complete references and fix minor typos.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a rigorous and interesting theoretical framework with a novel perspective and provable guarantees, but the modeling restrictions, deviation from standard training objectives, conservative sufficient conditions that scale poorly, and limited empirical validation reduce practical significance at this stage. Strengthening clarity and empirical support, and further relaxing or justifying the constraints, would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
