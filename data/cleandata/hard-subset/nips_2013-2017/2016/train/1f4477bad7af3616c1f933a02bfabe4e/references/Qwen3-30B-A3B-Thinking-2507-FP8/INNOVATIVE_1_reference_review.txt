

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper introduces a novel approach to training neural networks with global optimality guarantees, which is a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The authors propose a "nonlinear spectral method" that leverages Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems to achieve global convergence for a specific class of neural networks. The key insight is that by restricting to nonnegative weights and modifying the objective function (adding the negative sum of outputs), they can transform the training problem into one that can be solved with a fixed-point iteration that converges linearly to the global optimum.

The paper makes a bold claim: this is the first practically feasible globally optimal algorithm for training neural networks. This is a significant departure from the standard stochastic gradient descent approach that dominates deep learning practice. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation, proving that under certain conditions (specifically, when the spectral radius of a constructed matrix is less than 1), their algorithm converges linearly to the global optimum. This is a major theoretical advance compared to previous work on global optimality (e.g., Janzamin et al. [11] and Haeffele and Vidal [8]), which either lacked practical applicability or required conditions that were difficult to verify.

The core novelty lies in the theoretical framework connecting structured matrix factorization to neural network training through nonlinear spectral methods. The authors develop a comprehensive theoretical foundation, including a characterization of the Lipschitz constant of the mapping GΦ and a proof of convergence via the Banach fixed point theorem. The paper's most innovative aspect is the way it transforms the neural network training problem into a nonlinear eigenproblem on convex cones, enabling the use of powerful tools from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory.

The experimental results, while limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, provide compelling evidence of the approach's viability. The authors show that their method (NLSM1 and NLSM2) achieves competitive performance compared to ReLU networks and SVMs, with the added benefit of global optimality guarantees. The linear convergence rate demonstrated in Figure 2 is particularly impressive compared to SGD variants, which require careful hyperparameter tuning.

I find the paper's approach refreshingly bold. The authors are not afraid to impose constraints (nonnegative weights) that might seem restrictive at first glance, but they carefully justify why these constraints are necessary for their theoretical guarantees. The paper also thoughtfully addresses the limitations of their approach, noting that the current bounds on the spectral radius might be conservative, and that the method is currently limited to low-dimensional datasets.

The main weaknesses I see are:
1. The nonnegative weights constraint significantly limits the applicability of the method to domains where negative weights are essential (though the authors argue for count data applications).
2. The experimental validation is limited to small-scale datasets, and the paper doesn't address scalability to larger, more complex problems.
3. The choice of α (the exponents in the generalized polynomial activation) is somewhat arbitrary in the experiments, and the paper doesn't provide strong guidance on how to select these parameters for optimal performance.
4. The theoretical guarantees require checking the spectral radius of a matrix, which might be computationally expensive for large networks.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical contributions over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution. The core idea of using nonlinear spectral methods for global optimality in neural networks is genuinely novel and could inspire new research directions in both theoretical and practical deep learning. The paper's theoretical foundation is sound, and the experimental results, while limited, are sufficient to demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from theoretical motivation to algorithm development to experimental validation. The authors have also thoughtfully addressed related work and positioned their contribution within the broader context of deep learning theory.

In conclusion, while the method has limitations in terms of applicability (nonnegative weights constraint) and experimental validation (limited to small datasets), the paper's theoretical novelty and bold approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The authors have introduced a genuinely new perspective on neural network training that could have significant long-term impact, even if the immediate practical applications are somewhat limited.

**Recommendation: Accept**
