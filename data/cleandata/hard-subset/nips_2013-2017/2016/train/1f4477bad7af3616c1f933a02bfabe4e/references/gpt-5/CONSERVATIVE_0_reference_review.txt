Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) to train a class of generalized polynomial neural networks with guaranteed convergence to a unique global optimum under checkable conditions. The model assumes nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights, with first- or two-hidden-layer architectures using power activations (∑m u_lm x_m)^α_l with α_l ≥ 1 and distinct across units. The training objective is non-standard: maximize negative cross-entropy plus a sum of outputs, with additional small positive terms to ensure strictly positive gradients. Parameters lie on fixed ℓ_p spheres. The algorithm is a fixed-point iteration formed by normalizing ψp′ of the gradient; global convergence with linear rate follows by viewing the update as a contraction in the Thompson metric and invoking Banach’s fixed-point theorem. A sufficient condition is ρ(A) < 1 for an explicit small nonnegative matrix A depending on architecture and norm parameters; similar results extend to two hidden layers. Empirically, on small UCI datasets, NLSM is competitive with (but usually below) RBF SVM and comparable to ReLU networks trained by SGD, and it converges rapidly in the objective on toy plots.

Strengths
- Offers a clear, mathematically rigorous pathway to global optimality for a nontrivial neural network class. The link between multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory, Thompson metric contractions, and a normalized-gradient fixed point is elegant.
- The ρ(A) < 1 condition is explicit and easily checkable; the extension to two layers is mechanically clear.
- Simple, parameter-free iteration (no learning rate) with linear convergence under the stated condition.
- First to provide a practically implementable global training algorithm with actual experiments (albeit on small datasets), improving on earlier tensor-factorization approaches that require score function estimation.

Weaknesses
- The model and objective deviate substantially from standard neural network training:
  - Nonnegativity constraints on weights and inputs, fixed ℓ_p norm constraints, distinct power activations, and the addition of a sum-of-outputs term plus ε-positivity terms fundamentally change the hypothesis class and learning objective.
  - The paper admits the extra terms are added “for technical reasons” (to ensure strictly positive gradients), but there is little justification that the modified objective remains aligned with standard cross-entropy minimization. The effect of the added sum-of-outputs (with seemingly unit coefficient) on classification behavior is not analyzed.
- The empirical evaluation is limited:
  - Small, low-dimensional UCI datasets; no image/text benchmarks where nonnegativity is natural (e.g., MNIST with nonnegative pixels, BoW features).
  - SVM typically matches or outperforms NLSM; ReLU baselines are not exhaustively tuned and do not include stronger optimizers or regularization strategies; no wall-clock comparisons beyond one toy figure; no ablations on the necessity/impact of the additional objective terms or nonnegativity constraints.
- Practicality of the ρ(A) < 1 condition:
  - The paper relies on conservative bounds and often achieves ρ(A) < 1 by taking pw, pu very large. The impact of these choices on the update dynamics, optimization landscape, and representational capacity is not explored. The dependence of ρ(A) on hidden units suggests the condition becomes harder as models scale, leaving scalability uncertain.
- Clarity and presentation issues:
  - Several citation inconsistencies and incomplete references; missing definitions and typos (e.g., specification of L, the exact coefficient multiplying the added terms, which appears inconsistent with the text about “arbitrarily small ε”).
  - Important parts of the proofs are deferred; the key reasons why the added sum-of-outputs is needed deserve more intuitive explanation and ablation evidence.

Quality
The theoretical development appears sound and self-consistent: the mapping is well-defined on S++, the contraction is carefully controlled via a weighted Thompson metric, and the spectral radius criterion is clear. However, the practical relevance hinges on a modified objective; the paper does not sufficiently quantify how much performance/behavior deviates from standard training or how robust the method is to the (conservative) parameter choices needed to enforce ρ(A) < 1.

Clarity
The technical exposition is generally well-structured, but clarity suffers from missing or inconsistent constants (ε), incomplete references, and insufficient motivation for key modeling choices. More intuition and examples would help. Pseudocode is helpful but complexity per iteration is not discussed.

Originality
The combination of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory, Thompson metric contractions, and neural network training is novel. Requiring distinct α per hidden unit to avoid permutation-induced degeneracy is an interesting observation. However, the price is a rather specialized hypothesis class and objective.

Significance
Theoretical significance is decent: an explicit, checkable sufficient condition for global optimality with linear convergence for a class of networks. Practical significance is limited at present due to strong modeling restrictions, nonstandard objective, and modest empirical results on small datasets. Claims of being “first practically feasible” seem overstated relative to the constraints and performance.

Questions for authors
1. What is the exact coefficient on the added output and weight-sum terms? Is it ε or 1? Please reconcile the text and equations and quantify sensitivity to ε.
2. How does adding the sum-of-outputs affect classification calibration and decision boundaries? Any ablation removing/weighting this term?
3. What are typical values of pw, pu used in practice to enforce ρ(A) < 1? How sensitive are results to these choices?
4. Can you provide wall-clock time comparisons and complexity per iteration?
5. Can you evaluate on at least one larger nonnegative dataset (e.g., MNIST) and include comparisons to nonnegative NMF-based classifiers or monotone/positivity-constrained networks?

Pros
- Solid, rigorous theory with a simple fixed-point algorithm and explicit global optimality condition.
- Interesting application of nonlinear spectral methods to NN training.
- Extension to two layers presented.

Cons
- Nonstandard model/objective; limited practical relevance currently demonstrated.
- Weak empirical validation; SVM often wins; no strong baselines or ablations.
- Conservative conditions may restrict scalability; unclear impact of large p.
- Presentation issues and missing clarifications.

Recommendation
Reject. The theoretical contribution is elegant but the modeling constraints and nonstandard objective significantly limit practical utility, and the empirical validation is too narrow to justify acceptance at NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to (i) clarify the objective and ε, (ii) provide ablations and stronger baselines, (iii) demonstrate scalability and robustness, and (iv) better motivate the added terms’ effect on classification.
