Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of a class of generalized polynomial neural networks under nonnegativity constraints. The key idea is to recast the training objective into a multi-homogeneous map on the positive orthant and to apply a Perron–Frobenius–type fixed-point iteration with respect to the Thompson metric. For one hidden layer, the authors define an update map GΦ that normalizes ψp′ of the gradient and prove linear convergence to a unique global maximizer whenever the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small (K+1)×(K+1) nonnegative matrix A is < 1. The condition is model- and data-scale–dependent but can be checked a priori. They extend the framework to two hidden layers (matrix size K+2), again with a computable sufficient condition for global optimality and linear convergence. The method requires: nonnegative inputs, nonnegative weights, power activations with exponents αl ≥ 1 (distinct across units), norm constraints on layers, and a modified objective that adds the sum of outputs and a small L1-type term on weights to ensure strict positivity needed by the theory. Proofs follow from contraction in the Thompson metric and the Banach fixed-point theorem, with constructive bounds on Lipschitz constants via a matrix inequality. Experiments on small UCI datasets show fast linear convergence and competitive accuracy compared to ReLU networks trained by SGD and an RBF-SVM.

Relation to prior work
This builds on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and multi-homogeneous maps, connecting them to network training. It contrasts with globally optimal guarantees via structured matrix factorizations (Haeffele & Vidal) or tensor methods (Janzamin et al.), which either require strong identifiability conditions or access to score-function tensors. The present approach is novel in giving a practically checkable spectral condition and a simple parameter-free iteration (given a feasible architecture) that converges globally for a nontrivial network class.

Strengths
- Conceptually bold: reframes NN training as a nonlinear spectral problem with global optimality guarantees under clear, verifiable conditions.
- Practically checkable certificate: ρ(A) < 1 depends on architecture/normalization/data scale; computing the spectral radius of a tiny matrix is trivial.
- Linear convergence with an explicit rate; convergence is very fast in practice.
- Extension to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees.
- First, to my knowledge, to demonstrate globally optimal NN training with experiments on real datasets, beyond purely theoretical guarantees.
- No learning-rate tuning; the iteration is simple to implement.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Modified objective: maximizing −cross-entropy + Σr fr(x) plus a small L1 term materially changes the learning target. The Σr fr(x) term is not scaled by ε; its statistical meaning relative to cross-entropy is unclear, and could bias solutions. An ablation or justification of its effect on classification/calibration is needed.
- Strong constraints on model/data: nonnegative inputs and weights, power activations with αl ≥ 1, distinct α per unit, and fixed Lp norm constraints. This limits applicability and comparability to standard architectures.
- Conservativeness: bounds leading to A can be very loose; ensuring ρ(A) < 1 often requires large p’s, which may degrade effective modeling. Scaling with hidden units is unfavorable (ρ(A) tends to grow with n1,n2), restricting network size and possibly limiting performance on higher-dimensional tasks.
- Empirics are modest: small low-dimensional UCI datasets only; no tests on standard nonnegative domains like MNIST or bag-of-words text; no ablations on the necessity of Σr fr(x) or distinct α’s; limited comparisons to polynomial networks/NMF-like baselines.
- Clarity/polish: several typos/incomplete references; the “ε” multiplying the L1 term seems missing in Eq. (2), though referenced in the text; the definition of the loss is elided in places; figures and formatting issues reduce readability.

Quality
The theoretical development is careful and appears sound; the contraction argument and use of the Thompson metric are appropriate, and the mapping between critical points and fixed points is well motivated. However, some constants are likely very conservative, and practical guidance on picking (pu,pw,ρu,ρw,α) to balance feasibility (ρ(A)<1) and expressivity is thin. Experiments support fast convergence but are too small to establish broad competitiveness; still, they serve as a proof of concept.

Clarity
Well organized at a high level, but hard to read in places due to dense notation and missing details/typos. The role and size of the ε term, and the impact of the Σr fr(x) addition, should be clarified. A short intuitive explanation of why the Σr fr(x) term ensures strict positivity of gradients would help.

Originality
High. The combination of multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and NN training with a computable spectral certificate for global optimality is novel, and the extension to depth-two under similar principles is rare in the literature.

Significance
Moderate-to-high as a new direction. Even if restricted, having a globally convergent, practical, and checkable method for a nontrivial class of networks is valuable and could inspire further work on spectral/contractive viewpoints, tighter bounds, and broader activation/constraint sets.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please clarify Eq. (2): is the L1 term multiplied by ε? The text suggests so.
- Empirically, what is the effect of the Σr fr(x) term? Show results without it (or with diminishing coefficient) to quantify trade-offs.
- Provide guidance for choosing (pu,pw,ρu,ρw,α), beyond the conservative bounds; can data-dependent tighter A reduce conservativeness?
- Can you preprocess nonnegative inputs from general data (e.g., shifting/scaling) without harming guarantees?
- Complexity: per-iteration cost vs SGD; scalability to larger n1/n2 and datasets; monotonicity of Φ along iterates?
- Compare against polynomial networks and NMF-inspired classifiers.

Pros
- Bold, original theoretical framework with a practical, verifiable certificate.
- Global optimality with linear convergence; simple iteration.
- First real-data demonstration for such guarantees.

Cons
- Objective deviates from standard cross-entropy; unclear statistical implications.
- Strong nonnegativity and architectural constraints; scaling limitations.
- Conservative bounds; limited experiments; clarity/polish issues.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Given the novelty and potential to open a new line of globally optimal training methods with practical certificates, I would accept despite the experimental limitations and objective modification. With improved clarity and additional empirical analysis (especially on the Σr fr(x) term), this could be an influential paper.

Confidence: Medium-high
