- Proposes a globally optimal training algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods (Perron-Frobenius); claims practical feasibility, linear convergence, and no hyperparameters, but requires nonnegative weights and adds the sum of outputs to the loss.

- Builds on Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015), claiming to avoid their impracticalities by not needing the data-generating measure and only requiring nonnegative data.

- Strength: Provides a rigorous proof of global optimality with linear convergence and a spectral radius condition that can be checked before training.

- Strength: Practical implementation needs no learning-rate hyperparameter and shows fast empirical convergence.

- Strength: Delivers the first empirical results for a globally optimal neural-network training algorithm on real (UCI) datasets.

- Weakness: The nonnegativity constraint on weights is highly restrictive and limits applicability beyond count-like data.

- Weakness: The modified objective (adding outputs) lacks theoretical justification and may reduce model expressiveness; empirical gains over SVM are marginal.

- Weakness: Spectral radius bounds are overly conservative (e.g., requiring very large p_w), making the method potentially impractical.

- Weakness: Paper does not explain how to select α_i parameters or the number of hidden units, undermining practical feasibility.

- Weakness: Experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets and do not demonstrate scalability to modern high-dimensional tasks.

- Overall: Strong theoretical/mathematical contribution but practical limitations significantly undermine real-world applicability.

- Suggested improvements: justify nonnegative-weight assumption, provide a principled α_i selection method, tighten spectral-radius bounds, and test on higher-dimensional datasets.

- Recommendation: Weak accept — important theoretical advance but currently limited practical utility.

- Pro/Con summary: Pros — rigorous global guarantees, linear convergence, empirical demonstration, no hyperparameters; Cons — restrictive nonnegative weights, unmotivated objective change, conservative bounds, unclear parameter selection.
