Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a restricted class of feedforward neural networks with guaranteed global optimality under checkable conditions. The networks use generalized polynomial activations with nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights. The training problem is reformulated as maximizing a modified objective: average negative cross-entropy plus a positive linear term that sums the network outputs, along with an arbitrarily small positive linear term over the weights to ensure strict positivity of gradients. The authors construct a fixed-point map based on normalized gradients and prove that, if the spectral radius of a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix A is < 1, then: (i) the objective has a unique critical point in the positive orthant, (ii) this point is the unique global maximizer, and (iii) the fixed-point iteration converges linearly from any positive initialization. They extend the analysis from one to two hidden layers and give conservative sufficient conditions on norms and radii that ensure ρ(A) < 1. Experiments on several small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy to ReLU networks and RBF SVMs and substantiate the linear convergence claim.

This work is positioned against recent global-optimality results for neural networks. Compared to tensor methods (Janzamin et al.) that require score functions and non-degeneracy, and to matrix factorization–based formulations (Haeffele & Vidal) that can be hard to apply, the proposed method relies on nonnegativity and norm constraints plus a checkable spectral radius condition. The analysis builds on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous maps, the Thompson metric, and Banach’s fixed-point theorem.

Quality
- The theoretical contribution is substantial and, to the best I can judge, technically sound. The chain of reasoning—move maximum into the interior via strictly positive gradients, identify critical points with fixed points of GΦ, show GΦ is a contraction on a complete metric space with an explicit Lipschitz bound controlled by matrix A, then apply Banach—fits together cleanly. The dependence of ρ(A) on model hyperparameters is carefully discussed, and the conservativeness of bounds is acknowledged.
- The assumptions are strong and clearly stated: nonnegative data and weights, p-norm normalization constraints, αℓ ≥ 1 with all α distinct, and the addition of output-sum and ε-weight-sum terms to ensure strict positivity and contraction. The authors are honest about these being non-standard and about the conservativeness of their sufficient conditions.
- The extension to two hidden layers mirrors the one-layer case, with appropriately expanded A. While general depth is claimed as future work, the two-layer case is nontrivial and well worked out.
- Empirically, the experiments are modest but appropriate for a proof-of-concept. They validate linear convergence and show competitive accuracy. However, they are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, and there is no ablation on the effect of the added output/weight terms or the distinct-α requirement. Baselines are reasonable, but the objective differs from standard cross-entropy, making comparisons somewhat indirect.

Clarity
- Overall, the paper is readable for an expert familiar with nonlinear spectral theory and fixed-point methods. The main theorems, definitions, and algorithm are clearly laid out.
- Some notational and expository issues hinder clarity: the negative cross-entropy definition is elided; the role and placement of the small ε are notationally inconsistent (“> 0” appears instead of a symbol like ε), and the practical magnitude choice is not discussed. The motivation for, and implications of, adding the sum of outputs to the objective deserve more explanation and empirical probing. The need for distinct α per unit is explained, but the practical cost of this restriction (vs. standard activations) could be clarified.
- Several proofs are deferred to the supplement, which is fine, but a brief intuition for the construction of A and the Ψ function would help.

Originality
- High. Applying multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory with Thompson metric contractions to derive globally convergent training of NN-like models, with a verifiable spectral condition, is novel. The construction of a parameter-free fixed-point map from normalized gradients and the explicit dependence on a small matrix A are original in the neural network training context.
- The restrictions (nonnegativity, generalized polynomial activations, added output term) are unusual, making the model depart from conventional NNs, but that does not diminish the novelty of the optimization approach.

Significance
- Scientifically meaningful: it provides a practically checkable and provably globally convergent training method for a nontrivial network family, a rare result in deep learning optimization. The contraction-based analysis and the link to the spectral radius condition are likely to inspire follow-ups.
- Practical impact is limited by the restrictions: nonnegative data/weights, distinct exponents, and a modified loss. It is unclear how well the approach scales to higher-dimensional tasks or how much the output-sum term biases classifiers. The bounds ensuring ρ(A) < 1 become more restrictive with more units, which may constrain expressivity in higher dimensions.

Strengths
- Strong, well-structured theory with explicit, verifiable conditions and linear convergence rate.
- Novel use of nonlinear spectral methods and multi-homogeneous contractions for NN training.
- Extension to two hidden layers and clear dependence of conditions on architecture/hyperparameters.
- Transparent discussion of assumptions and limitations; simple parameter-free iteration.

Weaknesses
- Model restrictions (nonnegativity, distinct α, added output and ε terms) make it atypical and may limit applicability.
- Conservative bounds; ρ(A) increases with network size, potentially limiting scalability and expressivity.
- Limited empirical evaluation on small datasets; no ablations quantifying the effect of the added terms or the distinct-α requirement; unclear performance on higher-dimensional problems.
- Some clarity issues (definition of loss, notation for ε, practical guidance for α selection and numerical stability for large p).

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please clarify the exact form of the loss (softmax cross-entropy?) and how the positive output-sum term interacts with it. Can you provide ablations showing accuracy with and without that term and with varying ε?
- How sensitive are results to the choice and spread of α? Any principled way to set α beyond random selection?
- Can you report experiments on higher-dimensional data (even synthetic) to illustrate how ρ(A) and convergence behave with more units, and whether tighter, data-dependent bounds on A yield less conservative ρ(A)?
- Numerical stability: when choosing large p to enforce ρ(A) < 1, do you observe conditioning issues in practice?
- Could the nonnegativity requirement be relaxed to signed weights via reparameterizations or cone decompositions?

Recommendation
- Score: 6 (weak accept)
- Justification: The paper offers a rigorous and original optimization framework with clear global optimality and convergence guarantees for a constrained but nontrivial class of neural networks. While the empirical section is limited and the model restrictions curb immediate practical impact, the theoretical contribution is strong, relevant to NeurIPS, and likely to stimulate further research on globally optimal training under structural assumptions. Strengthen clarity around the modified objective and broaden/ablate experiments if possible.
