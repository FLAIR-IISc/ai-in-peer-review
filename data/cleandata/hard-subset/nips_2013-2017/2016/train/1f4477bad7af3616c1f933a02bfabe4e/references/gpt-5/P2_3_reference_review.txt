Summary
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that provably converges to a unique global optimum for training a constrained class of neural networks: generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative inputs. The method reformulates learning as a fixed-point problem on a convex cone and applies multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and the Thompson metric to show that an explicit, normalization-based gradient mapping GΦ is a contraction whenever the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 for one hidden layer; K+2 for two layers) is less than one. Under this condition, the algorithm enjoys linear convergence to the unique maximizer of a modified objective (negative cross-entropy plus a sum of outputs and small linear terms in weights), with no step-size tuning. The authors extend the framework to two hidden layers and provide proof sketches with the core technical elements in the main text. Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive performance against ReLU networks and RBF SVMs.

Relation to prior work
The work directly tackles nonconvex neural network training via global optimality guarantees, building on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory. It contrasts with Haeffele and Vidal (global optimality via structured factorizations but limited practicality) and Janzamin et al. (tensor methods requiring score functions and nondegeneracy assumptions). The novelty here is an implementable fixed-point scheme with a simple, architecture-dependent check (ρ(A) < 1) that does not require data distributional knowledge, at the cost of strong nonnegativity constraints and a modified loss.

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The fixed-point construction, contraction proof in Thompson metric, and link to global optimality via uniqueness of fixed points are technically solid and well-motivated. The positivity of gradients ensured by adding sum-of-outputs and small linear terms is a key technical device that is clearly explained. The derivation of matrix A and the spectral radius condition is systematic; the authors are appropriately conservative about bounds.
- Assumptions and scope: The guarantees require (i) nonnegative inputs, (ii) nonnegative, p-norm-normalized weights, (iii) generalized power activations with αl ≥ 1 and αl all distinct, and (iv) a modified objective that includes the sum of outputs. These are substantial departures from standard practice and restrict applicability. The authors argue that α diversity avoids permutation invariances leading to trivial solutions; however, α is not learned and becomes a hyperparameter that strongly shapes expressivity.
- Objective design: The inclusion of the sum of outputs is nonstandard. For softmax cross-entropy, adding a constant to all logits is loss-invariant, so this additional term introduces a competing objective that may bias solutions independent of the classification loss (the norm constraints limit but do not remove this effect). An ablation clarifying its empirical impact would help. The authors explain it is needed to ensure strict positivity of gradients; alternative constructions that ensure positivity without changing the objective (e.g., smoothing near the boundary) should be discussed.
- Completeness: Many intermediate proofs are deferred to the supplement but the main proof strategy and key lemmas are present. Computational complexity per iteration is not analyzed in detail.

Clarity
- The paper is mathematically dense but generally coherent. However, several presentation issues impede readability: missing or malformed equations (e.g., the loss definition), inconsistent notation, and some typos in references. The role of ε (the small positive constant) and its interaction with constraints could be explained more cleanly. The algorithm description is helpful, but complexity and implementation details (e.g., computing ∇Φ efficiently) should be elaborated.

Originality
- The combination of nonlinear spectral methods, Thompson metric contraction, and a per-architecture spectral radius condition for global optimal training is novel for neural networks. While related in spirit to prior global optimality results, the theoretical pathway and the checkable sufficient condition are original.

Significance
- Conceptually, the paper advances our understanding of when global optimality is algorithmically achievable for neural networks, and it connects deep learning to a rich body of spectral theory. Practically, the constraints (nonnegativity, fixed α exponents, modified objective) and the conservativeness of the ρ(A) < 1 condition limit immediate impact on mainstream architectures and datasets. The experiments are proof-of-concept on small UCI datasets; performance is competitive but not state-of-the-art, and the benefits of the theoretical guarantee are not clearly realized on larger or more challenging tasks. Still, as a theoretical contribution with a working algorithm, this is meaningful.

Pros
- Clear, rigorous theoretical framework with a simple, checkable condition for global optimality and linear convergence.
- Algorithm has no learning-rate tuning and is fast in practice under the condition.
- Extension to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees.
- First empirical demonstration (to my knowledge) of nontrivial classification from a globally optimal NN training algorithm.

Cons
- Strong modeling constraints (nonnegative inputs/weights, fixed α, normalization) and a nonstandard objective that adds sum of outputs.
- Global optimality condition appears conservative; feasibility may degrade quickly with network width/dimension.
- Limited empirical evaluation: small datasets, modest gains, no ablation on the added output-sum term or sensitivity to α, p, ρ.
- Presentation issues (missing equations, typos) reduce clarity and reproducibility.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Clarify precisely the cross-entropy used and the invariance of adding a constant to all logits; quantify how the sum-of-outputs term affects solutions. Provide an ablation without this term and discuss alternative ways to ensure gradient positivity.
- Discuss preprocessing for nonnegativity on generic data, and the effect on performance.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for α choices, p, and ρ, and report how often ρ(A) < 1 holds in practice as model size grows (using the tighter Theorem 4 bounds).
- Compare against nonnegative matrix factorization and nonnegative neural nets with multiplicative updates.
- Improve clarity: fix missing equations, notation, and references; add complexity analysis per iteration.

Recommendation
Borderline reject (leaning weak reject). The theoretical contribution is elegant and original, but the gap to practical significance is substantial: the model restrictions and modified objective limit applicability, and the empirical section is too narrow to support impact claims. With clearer exposition, deeper empirical analysis (including ablations), and discussion on relaxing the added-output term while preserving guarantees, this could be a strong theoretical paper. Confidence: medium.
