Summary
The paper proposes a globally convergent training algorithm for a constrained class of feedforward neural networks with nonnegative parameters and generalized polynomial activations. The core idea is to reformulate training as finding a fixed point of a multihomogeneous, order-preserving mapping derived from the gradient of a modified objective. Using nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and the Thompson metric, the authors show that, under an explicit spectral radius condition on a small (K+1)-by-(K+1) nonnegative matrix A (K = number of classes), the map is a contraction with a unique fixed point in the positive orthant. This yields convergence to a unique global maximizer with a linear rate. The guarantees extend to two hidden layers. Empirically, the method is evaluated on small UCI datasets and compared to ReLU networks trained by SGD and RBF SVMs; the proposed method is competitive with ReLU and somewhat below SVMs.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against (i) tensor-based identifiability/global training for shallow nets (Janzamin et al.) requiring score functions, and (ii) structured matrix/tensor factorization views of neural training with global guarantees (Haeffele & Vidal), often impractical. The paper leverages nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous maps (Lemmens & Nussbaum; Gautier et al.) and provides a concrete neural setting where the contraction can be checked a priori. Conceptually, the model class resembles posynomials in geometric programming (positive variables and positive coefficients), which could be cited as related mathematical structure.

Quality and technical soundness
- The fixed-point framework is technically solid: mapping GΦ is defined using normalized gradients via ψp and p-norm constraints, and the contraction analysis via the Thompson metric is appropriate. The Lipschitz characterization through a nonnegative matrix A and the use of Collatz–Wielandt to select metric weights is elegant.
- The assumptions needed for the global guarantee are strong but explicit: nonnegative data and weights, strictly positive gradients on S+, fixed p-norm constraints on each layer, and a modified objective that adds the sum of outputs and a small positivity-ensuring term. The paper is careful to ensure the maximizer lies in the interior (Lemma 1) and that critical points coincide with fixed points (Lemma 2), then invokes Banach’s theorem.
- The spectral radius condition is checkable, but the bounds used to build A (ξ1, ξ2) appear conservative, which the authors acknowledge. The extension to two layers follows similarly with larger A.
- Key caveats:
  - The objective is not standard cross-entropy; it is cross-entropy plus an extra sum-of-outputs term and an ε-weight sum to force strictly positive gradients. The extra term breaks the translation invariance of the softmax loss and, together with norm constraints, prevents trivial unboundedness. This is central but under-explained: what classifier is optimal for this modified objective and how does it relate to standard training?
  - The representational class is quite restricted: nonnegative weights and nonnegative inputs imply monotone models; no biases are included; to avoid degeneracy the exponents αl must all differ. While generalized polynomials are expressive, the constraints are substantial and their implications for decision boundary complexity deserve a deeper treatment.
  - Practicality hinges on choosing many hyperparameters (n1, α, pw, pu, ρw, ρu, and ε). The paper claims “no free parameter” due to absence of a learning rate, but the method still requires extensive hyperparameter selection; the experiments use random search over 100 combinations, which contradicts the simplicity claim.

Clarity
- The overall structure and high-level idea are clear, but the exposition has rough edges: missing or truncated formulas (the cross-entropy definition), numerous typographical issues in references, and some leaps (e.g., why the positivity of gradients rules out boundary maxima) are deferred to the supplement.
- Important modeling choices (handling of biases, data preprocessing to enforce nonnegativity, choice of ε, numerical stability for large p, computational cost per iteration) are not discussed in sufficient detail.
- The requirement that all αl differ to avoid permutation-induced degeneracy is interesting but could be documented more rigorously, with guidance on how to choose α in practice.

Originality
- The application of multihomogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius fixed-point theory to obtain global convergence guarantees for a neural-like model is original in this precise form. The explicit spectral-radius condition and linear convergence rate are notable.
- The model class (nonnegative, posynomial-like nets with fixed p-norms) is nonstandard for deep learning, which is both a strength (tractable global training) and a limitation (reduced scope).

Significance
- The theoretical contribution is conceptually significant: a practically checkable, globally convergent algorithm for a nontrivial, albeit constrained, neural model. This can stimulate work on globally trainable architectures via monotonicity/positivity and homogeneity.
- The empirical significance is limited. Experiments are on small, low-dimensional UCI datasets; results are comparable to small ReLU nets and below RBF SVMs in several cases. There is no demonstration on higher-dimensional data (e.g., MNIST) where positivity constraints might still hold. The comparison to SGD is on the modified objective and does not show wall-clock time nor fairness (full-batch gradients vs. minibatch SGD).

Pros
- Clear, checkable condition (ρ(A) < 1) leading to global optimality and linear convergence.
- Solid fixed-point analysis in a nonconvex setting.
- Extension to two hidden layers.
- First empirical demonstration (to my knowledge) of global training for a neural-like classifier beyond synthetic setups.

Cons
- Strong modeling constraints (nonnegative inputs/weights, no biases, fixed norms, modified objective).
- Practical utility unclear beyond small problems; bounds appear very conservative and may require large p, raising numerical concerns.
- Many hyperparameters to tune; claim of “no free parameter” is misleading.
- Clarity issues and incomplete references; key definitions missing in the text.

Questions for the authors
1. How are biases handled? Can biases be incorporated without breaking the fixed-point framework?
2. What preprocessing was applied to ensure xi ≥ 0? Does shifting/scaling preserve performance and theory?
3. What ε was used in practice, and how sensitive are results to ε?
4. Can you report actual ρ(A) values and iteration counts across datasets, and whether empirical contraction factors match theory?
5. How does the added sum-of-outputs term affect classification compared to standard cross-entropy under the same norm constraints?
6. Any path toward learning α (or selecting it more systematically) while retaining guarantees?

Suggestions
- Improve exposition: include explicit loss formula, clarify the role of the added terms, and polish references.
- Provide a principled procedure for choosing α and radii (ρw, ρu) and discuss numerical stability for large p.
- Add experiments on a higher-dimensional nonnegative dataset (e.g., MNIST with scaled pixels) and report wall-clock time.
- Explore adding biases and/or input duplication (x+, x−) to relax monotonicity while maintaining positivity structure.

Recommendation
Borderline. The theory is interesting and novel for NeurIPS, but the modeling limitations, conservative conditions, and modest empirical evidence limit impact. Strengthening clarity and experiments would improve the case for acceptance.
