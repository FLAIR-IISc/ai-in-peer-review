

# Peer Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach for globally optimal training of a specific class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activation functions. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method that guarantees convergence to the global optimum under certain conditions, primarily involving the spectral radius of a small matrix. The method differs significantly from standard stochastic gradient descent by requiring nonnegative weights and a modified objective function. The authors prove linear convergence rates and demonstrate competitive performance on several low-dimensional UCI datasets. The work builds on recent advances in structured matrix factorization and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory, but distinguishes itself from prior work by Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015) by providing a practically feasible algorithm for global optimality.

## Strengths

**Quality**: The theoretical contribution is strong, with rigorous proofs of global convergence and linear rates. The paper clearly establishes the conditions under which global optimality is guaranteed (Theorem 1), and the proof strategy is well-structured, building on established concepts from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach (nonnegative weights constraint, limited to low-dimensional datasets) and provide explicit conditions for convergence. The experimental validation on UCI datasets is reasonable for a proof-of-concept paper, showing competitive performance against kernel SVMs and ReLU networks.

**Originality**: The paper makes a novel contribution by applying nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training, which is distinct from prior approaches to global optimality. The specific constraint of nonnegative weights to enable global optimality is an interesting and non-obvious approach that differentiates this work from previous research. The paper clearly positions itself against related work, acknowledging limitations of prior approaches while demonstrating how their method overcomes these limitations.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning - global optimality in training - with a method that has no hyperparameters to tune (unlike SGD). This is a significant practical advantage. The authors claim this is the first practically feasible algorithm for global optimality in neural networks, which, if true, would be a major contribution. The linear convergence rate is also a significant theoretical advantage over SGD.

## Weaknesses

**Quality**: The nonnegative weights constraint is highly restrictive and limits applicability to real-world deep learning problems, which typically require negative weights. The experimental validation is limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, which don't demonstrate scalability to larger, more complex problems. The paper doesn't adequately address how the method would perform on high-dimensional data like images or text. The theoretical bounds (e.g., ξ1, ξ2 in Theorem 1) are conservative, making the spectral radius condition difficult to satisfy in practice, as noted in the paper.

**Clarity**: While the mathematical presentation is rigorous, some key connections between theory and practice are unclear. The paper mentions that "the effective spectral radius is typically much smaller" than the bounds suggest, but doesn't provide empirical evidence to support this. The relationship between the choice of α (activation exponents) and network performance isn't thoroughly explored, despite being crucial to the method.

**Significance**: The method's applicability is limited to a very specific class of neural networks (nonnegative weights, generalized polynomial activations), which may not be relevant for most deep learning applications. The experimental results show only marginal improvements over kernel SVMs, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this method would be preferred over existing approaches for practical applications. The paper doesn't compare to more recent methods that might have addressed similar problems (e.g., recent advances in global optimization of neural networks).

## Recommendation

This paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to global optimality in neural network training, with a rigorous proof of linear convergence. The work is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network training. However, the restrictive nonnegative weights assumption and limited experimental validation significantly reduce its practical significance for the broader deep learning community.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More comprehensive experimental validation on larger datasets
2. A thorough discussion of how the method compares to more recent approaches to global optimality
3. A better explanation of the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and practical applicability
4. More concrete guidance on how to choose α and other parameters for practical use

## Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pro (accept):**
- Theoretically sound contribution to global optimality in neural networks
- First practically feasible algorithm for global optimality in neural networks (as claimed)
- Linear convergence rate with no hyperparameters to tune
- Clear proof strategy and well-structured theoretical development

**Con (reject):**
- Highly restrictive nonnegative weights constraint limits applicability
- Limited experimental validation on small, low-dimensional datasets
- Theoretical bounds are overly conservative, making the convergence condition difficult to satisfy
- Doesn't convincingly demonstrate advantages over existing methods for practical applications

## Final Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding neural network training, but its practical significance is limited by the restrictive assumptions and lack of validation on more complex datasets. The authors could strengthen the paper substantially by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing more comprehensive experimental results and a clearer justification for the practical relevance of their method. The theoretical contribution is strong, but the practical relevance for the NIPS community is questionable given the current state of deep learning practice.
