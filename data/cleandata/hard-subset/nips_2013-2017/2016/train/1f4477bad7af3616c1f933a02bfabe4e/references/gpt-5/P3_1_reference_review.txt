Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) to train a restricted class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with linear convergence. The networks use nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, no biases, and generalized polynomial activations of the form (∑m u_lm x_m)^{α_l}, with distinct α_l ≥ 1 per hidden unit. The training objective is a modified maximization: negative cross-entropy plus (i) a sum over outputs across classes and (ii) an ε-weight sum term, under Lp-norm constraints on each layer’s weights. The update is a normalized, positive “gradient map” defined on the positive cone; using Perron–Frobenius-type arguments in the Thompson metric, the authors derive a sufficient spectral-radius condition (ρ(A)<1 for a small (K+1)-by-(K+1) nonnegative matrix A) that guarantees uniqueness of a fixed point (hence global optimality) and linear convergence. They provide an extension to two hidden layers and small-scale experiments on UCI datasets, showing competitive accuracy with ReLU networks and kernel SVMs, and faster convergence than SGD on their modified objective.

Relation to prior work
The paper complements prior “global training” results based on (i) structured matrix/tensor factorization with nonconvex regularization (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal) and (ii) moment/tensor methods for shallow networks (Janzamin et al.). Unlike those, the proposed method is iterative, practical, and yields a checkable precondition (ρ(A)<1). It also connects the training dynamics to nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and multi-homogeneous maps (Lemmens & Nussbaum; the authors’ own in-prep work). The approach is also reminiscent of classical NMF/multiplicative updates and Hilbert/Thompson metric contractions, which should be discussed more fully.

Strengths
- Clear, theoretically grounded convergence guarantee: uniqueness of the global optimum and linear rate under a tractable spectral condition.
- Pre-checkable condition ρ(A)<1 depending on architecture and norm parameters only; no tuning of learning rates.
- Nonlinear spectral viewpoint is original in the neural network training literature and technically interesting (use of Thompson metric, multi-homogeneous structure).
- Extension to two hidden layers shows the framework’s generality.
- Proof strategy is well organized: interior maximizer, fixed-point characterization, contraction mapping.
- Empirical results, though modest, demonstrate feasibility and stability.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope of “global optimality”: The guarantee holds for a heavily constrained model (nonnegative inputs and weights, normalized Lp norms, no biases, heterogeneous fixed α per unit) and a modified objective that adds the sum of outputs and an ε-weight sum. This is quite far from standard neural networks. Claims of “globally optimal training of neural networks” should be tempered.
- Objective modification: Adding ∑r f_r(x_i) materially changes the optimization landscape for cross-entropy (it is not a harmless constant shift under softmax). The paper states it is added to ensure strictly positive gradients but does not analyze its effect on classification risk or decision boundaries. An ablation is needed (with/without this term, behavior as ε→0).
- Practicality/expressivity: Requiring distinct α_l is an unusual design choice to avoid permutation-invariance-induced degeneracy; these exponents are not learned and may restrict expressivity/robustness. Discussion on how to choose α_l and the impact on approximation power is limited.
- Conservativeness/scaling: The spectral-radius bound appears very conservative and grows with the number of hidden units, effectively restricting network size (authors acknowledge). No experiments probe the tightness of the bound or behavior when ρ(A)≥1.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: small UCI datasets; no high-dimensional tasks; minimal baselines; no wall-clock comparisons; ReLU baselines likely underpowered (simple SGD, small hyperparameter search); no sensitivity analysis to pw, pu or α. Results do not convincingly demonstrate practical superiority.
- Missing or incomplete related work: Classical NMF/multiplicative updates (Lee & Seung) and related contraction analyses in projective metrics should be cited and contrasted. There is sparse discussion of convex neural network formulations/positive-homogeneous models and their guarantees.
- Clarity/notation issues: Some formulae are missing/garbled (explicit cross-entropy not shown); typos and incomplete references (e.g., “th Conference…”, [7] “in preparation” is pivotal), inconsistent notation (ρx vs ρ̃x), and limited guidance on computational cost per iteration.

Questions for the authors
1. What is the precise loss L used (softmax cross-entropy?) and how does adding ∑r f_r(x) alter the Bayes-consistent objective? Any theoretical guarantee that the added term does not harm classification in the limit ε→0?
2. Can α_l be learned (e.g., via bilevel optimization) while preserving contraction? Is there a tractable condition on α_l under which ρ(A)<1 still holds?
3. How tight are the A bounds in practice? Can data-dependent (tighter) bounds be computed to allow larger n1, n2?
4. How to handle negative-valued inputs (e.g., z-scored features)? Is a simple shift/scaling sufficient, and how does this affect the guarantees?
5. Can biases be incorporated while retaining nonnegativity and contraction (e.g., by augmenting inputs)?
6. Runtime: per-iteration complexity vs. SGD/Adam; wall-clock convergence on larger datasets.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and elegant application of nonlinear spectral methods to derive global convergence for a class of neural networks.
- Solid, self-contained theory with a checkable condition and linear rates.
- Opens a potentially fertile line connecting PF theory and NN optimization.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical impact is limited by strong modeling constraints and a modified objective with unclear statistical implications.
- Experimental evidence is weak for NIPS standards; benchmarks are small and baselines limited.
- Clarity and completeness issues (notation, references, role of the added terms) hinder reproducibility and assessment.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper overstates its practical relevance, lacks sufficient empirical validation, and needs clearer positioning and analysis of the modified objective and modeling constraints. Strengthening experiments, tightening/clarifying the analysis (especially the role of the output-sum and ε terms), and expanding related work would significantly improve the paper.
