## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel algorithm for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – generalized polynomial neural networks with non-negative weights – to a globally optimal solution. The core idea is to reformulate the training problem as a maximization problem leveraging concepts from nonlinear spectral theory and the Perron-Frobenius theorem.  The authors demonstrate that under certain conditions (specifically related to the spectral radius of a constructed matrix), the algorithm guarantees convergence to the global optimum with a linear convergence rate.  The work distinguishes itself from recent advances in globally optimal neural network training by avoiding the need to estimate data-generating measures or check complex non-degeneracy conditions.  Initial experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate competitive performance.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing body of work aiming for global optimality in neural network training. It acknowledges and contrasts its approach with recent contributions by Haeffele & Vidal (2016) and Janzamin et al. (2016).  The authors clearly articulate the limitations of those prior works – practical difficulty and reliance on data distribution estimation – and present their method as an alternative addressing those shortcomings. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory and related concepts in nonlinear spectral theory is well-explained, providing a solid theoretical foundation.  The reference to related work on polynomial neural networks (e.g., [15]) is also appropriate.

**Strengths:**

* **Global Optimality Guarantee:** The strongest aspect of this work is the theoretical guarantee of global optimality under relatively mild conditions (non-negativity of weights and a check on the spectral radius).  This is a significant contribution, as achieving global optima in neural network training is notoriously difficult.
* **Practicality:**  The condition for global optimality (checking the spectral radius of a small matrix) is computationally tractable, unlike methods relying on data distribution estimation.
* **Linear Convergence:**  The linear convergence rate is a major advantage over iterative methods like stochastic gradient descent, promising faster training times.
* **Clear Presentation of Theory:**  The paper provides a detailed, step-by-step development of the theoretical framework, making the underlying ideas accessible.
* **Novelty:**  The application of nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training is a novel approach, and the connection to Perron-Frobenius theory is insightful.
* **Complete Metric Space Formulation**: The rigorous treatment using Thompson metric and Banach Fixed-Point theorem is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Strong Non-Negativity Constraint:** The requirement for non-negative weights is a significant limitation. While justified by the theoretical framework, it restricts the expressiveness of the model and may hinder performance in applications where negative weights are crucial. The authors acknowledge this but could explore potential workarounds or extensions to mitigate this constraint.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments on UCI datasets are a good starting point, they are limited in scope. The authors should include more comprehensive evaluations on larger and more complex datasets, and compare their method against stronger baselines, including modern deep learning architectures.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:**  The authors admit that the bounds derived for ensuring ρ(A) < 1 are likely conservative, potentially limiting the applicability of the method. Further investigation into tightening these bounds would be valuable.
* **Choice of α:** The necessity of distinct α values for each hidden unit, while theoretically motivated, raises practical concerns.  The impact of this constraint on model performance and its potential for overfitting should be investigated.
* **Objective Function Modification:** The modification to the standard objective function (addition of the sum of outputs) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more thorough justification for this modification, beyond its necessity for the theoretical framework, would be helpful.
* **Lack of Ablation Study:** It would be beneficial to see an ablation study on the proposed objective modification to understand its specific contribution to the performance.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel and theoretically sound method for achieving global optimality in training a specific class of neural networks.
* Offers a practical advantage over existing methods for global optimization by avoiding complex estimation procedures.
* Demonstrates competitive performance on UCI datasets.
* The work is clearly written and well-motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong non-negativity constraint limits the applicability of the method.
* The empirical evaluation is limited and needs to be expanded.
* The conservatism of the bounds for ensuring convergence needs to be addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The guarantee of global optimality and the linear convergence rate are compelling advantages. While the non-negativity constraint and limited empirical evaluation are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. The paper offers a valuable contribution to the field of neural network optimization and is worthy of acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised above, particularly expanding the experimental evaluation and investigating potential methods to relax the non-negativity constraint.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions** - The authors should address the weaknesses identified above, particularly expanding the experimental evaluation and providing a more thorough discussion of the implications of the non-negativity constraint.
