## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel algorithm, the Nonlinear Spectral Method (NLSM), for globally optimally training feedforward neural networks with one (and extending to two) hidden layers. Unlike gradient-based methods, NLSM guarantees convergence to a global optimum under specific conditions – namely, non-negativity of weights and a modified objective function including a regularization term. The authors leverage the Perron-Frobenius theory and demonstrate a linear convergence rate. Crucially, checking the global optimality condition involves computing the spectral radius of a small matrix, making it computationally feasible.  The paper includes experimental validation on UCI datasets, demonstrating competitive performance.

**Strengths:**

* **Global Optimality Guarantee:** This is the most significant contribution. Obtaining global optimality for non-trivial neural network training is a notoriously difficult problem, and this work provides a theoretical guarantee under reasonable constraints.
* **Computational Feasibility:**  The authors cleverly frame the problem to allow for a check of the optimality condition via spectral radius calculation, which is computationally efficient.
* **Linear Convergence:**  A linear convergence rate is excellent and promises fast training compared to gradient descent methods.
* **Clear Connection to Mathematical Theory:** The grounding of the method in Perron-Frobenius theory is well-explained and provides a solid foundation.
* **Non-Standard but Motivated Objective:** The modified objective function is justified by the theoretical framework and provides the necessary properties for the algorithm to work.
* **Experimental Validation:**  While on limited datasets, the authors demonstrate that their method performs competitively with established techniques (SVM, ReLU networks) and showcase the speed advantage of NLSM.
* **Detailed Theoretical Development:** The paper meticulously develops the necessary theoretical framework, including lemmas and theorems, providing a rigorous justification for the algorithm. The supplementary material will be useful for a deep understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Strong Non-Negativity Constraint:** The requirement of non-negative weights is a significant limitation.  While the authors acknowledge it, it restricts the expressiveness of the network and may not be suitable for all applications. This constraint is especially problematic for datasets where negative weights would naturally arise (e.g., representing inhibitory connections). The claim that it is only needed for count data feels too strong.
* **Modified Objective Function:** The added regularization term (sum of outputs) feels somewhat ad-hoc and its impact on the network's representational power isn't fully explored. It’s not immediately clear why this specific term is crucial for the spectral method’s success.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge this, but the theoretical bounds (and practical performance) likely suffer in high-dimensional spaces due to the growing spectral radius of the associated matrix A with increased hidden units.  Further investigation into mitigating this issue is needed.  The provided bounds appear conservative, and the potential for tighter bounds should be investigated.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are performed on a small set of low-dimensional UCI datasets.  The performance on more complex, high-dimensional datasets (e.g., image classification) would be crucial to assess the practical applicability of the method.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** Choosing appropriate α values for the hidden layer units is crucial (requiring distinct values). The impact of the choice of α on performance and convergence rate is not sufficiently discussed.
* **Comparison to other Globally Optimal Methods:** The paper positions itself against Janzamin et al. [11], but a more detailed comparison of the practical limitations of both approaches (computational cost, scalability, data requirements) would be valuable.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound and original paper that tackles a fundamental problem in deep learning – global optimization. The theoretical guarantees and linear convergence rate are highly appealing.  However, the strong non-negativity constraint and scalability concerns limit its immediate practical impact.  Despite these limitations, the work represents a significant contribution to the field, offering a novel perspective on neural network training.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Provides a rigorous theoretical framework for globally optimal training of a specific class of neural networks.
* Offers a computationally feasible method for checking the optimality condition.
* Demonstrates competitive performance on UCI datasets.
* Fills a gap in the literature by presenting a practical algorithm for a previously intractable problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The non-negativity constraint is a significant limitation.
* Scalability to high-dimensional datasets remains a concern.
* Limited experimental validation on complex datasets.

**Rating:**

**7/10 (Accept with Minor Revisions)**

**Recommendations:**

* **Expand Experimental Evaluation:**  The authors should evaluate their method on more challenging datasets, including image classification tasks.
* **Address Scalability Concerns:**  Investigate techniques to mitigate the impact of the spectral radius growth with increasing hidden units, potentially through regularization or approximate spectral methods.
* **Discuss Parameter Sensitivity:**  Provide a more in-depth discussion on the influence of the α values on network performance and convergence.
* **Compare to Alternatives:**  Include a more comprehensive comparison with other globally optimal training methods, highlighting the trade-offs between different approaches.
* **Investigate Tighter Bounds:** Explore if the bounds for ρ(A) can be improved, potentially extending the applicability of the method to larger networks.
* **Clarify Objective Function:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of why the added regularization term is necessary and its effect on the learned representations.
