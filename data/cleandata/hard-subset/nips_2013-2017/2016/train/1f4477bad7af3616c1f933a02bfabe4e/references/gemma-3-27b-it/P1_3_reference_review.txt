## Peer Review for "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to training feedforward neural networks with one (and extended to two) hidden layers, guaranteeing global optimality under certain conditions.  The key innovation lies in formulating the training problem as a maximization problem amenable to nonlinear spectral methods, inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory.  Unlike recent work on global optimality (e.g., [8, 11]), this method avoids estimating the data generating measure and relies on checking the spectral radius of a relatively small matrix, easily computable *a priori*.  The authors impose non-negativity constraints on the network weights and introduce a modified loss function to achieve these guarantees. Experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate competitive performance, and the authors highlight the method's linear convergence rate.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper directly addresses the challenging problem of globally optimizing neural network training, a contrast to the typically stochastic and non-convex optimization landscape of deep learning. It builds on recent advances in globally optimal training [8, 11] but aims to overcome their practical limitations.  [8]’s tensor-based approach is noted as difficult to apply, while [11] requires knowledge of the data generating measure. This work elegantly sidesteps those issues by leveraging spectral methods and non-negativity constraints. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory and matrix analysis (referencing [3, 10]) is a strong theoretical foundation, and the authors correctly position their work within the broader context of non-convex optimization and neural network expressivity [1, 5, 13, 16]. The discussion regarding polynomial neural networks [15] is relevant and justifies the choice of generalized polynomial activations.

**Strengths:**

* **Guaranteed Global Optimality:** This is the primary and most significant strength.  The rigorous theoretical framework and the clear condition for global convergence (ρ(A) < 1) are compelling. The ability to check this condition *before* training is a substantial advantage.
* **Practicality Compared to Existing Global Optimality Methods:**  The avoidance of data density estimation and the reliance on a simple spectral radius calculation make the approach considerably more practical than [8, 11].
* **Linear Convergence:** The demonstrated linear convergence rate is impressive and suggests fast training times, as supported by the experiments.
* **Clear Theoretical Development:** The paper carefully develops the necessary mathematical machinery, with proofs (some moved to supplementary material) providing confidence in the results.
* **Novel Approach:**  The connection to nonlinear spectral methods and Perron-Frobenius theory for neural network optimization is a genuinely novel idea.
* **Reasonable Experimental Validation:** While limited, the experiments on UCI datasets show competitive performance and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Strong Non-Negativity Constraint:** The requirement for non-negative weights is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, the implications for expressivity and applicability to real-world datasets where negative weights are often crucial are substantial.  A more thorough discussion of the impact of this constraint on network capacity would be beneficial.
* **Modified Objective Function:** The addition of the sum of outputs to the objective function, while theoretically motivated, is non-standard and requires justification beyond simply ensuring a strictly positive gradient.  Its impact on the learned representation is unclear.
* **Conservative Bounds:**  The authors admit the bounds used to determine convergence are likely conservative. This means the actual region of parameter space where global optimality is guaranteed might be larger than currently estimated. Further investigation into tighter bounds is warranted.
* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets.  Demonstrating performance on more complex, high-dimensional datasets (e.g., image classification) would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The connection between the model parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu, p, q) and the spectral radius, and thus convergence, is complex. More guidance on selecting these parameters in practice would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost of Matrix A:** While smaller than computing the data generating measure, the computational cost of constructing and evaluating the spectral radius of the matrix A may still be non-trivial for large networks.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning: globally optimal training.
* The method offers a practical advantage over existing global optimization techniques by avoiding complex computations.
* The linear convergence rate is a significant benefit.
* The theoretical development is rigorous and well-presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong non-negativity constraint severely limits the applicability of the method.
* The limited experimental evaluation weakens the claim of practical utility.
* The conservative bounds may restrict the range of architectures for which global optimality is guaranteed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. While the non-negativity constraint is a substantial limitation, the theoretical guarantees, the practical advantages over existing methods, and the novelty of the approach are compelling. The authors have provided a solid foundation for future research. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions**

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Expand Discussion on Non-Negativity:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of how the non-negativity constraint affects the expressivity and representational capacity of the network.
*   **Justify the Modified Objective:**  Offer a more intuitive explanation for the inclusion of the sum of outputs in the objective function.  Perhaps analyze its effect on the learned representations.
*   **Investigate Tighter Bounds:** Explore methods for deriving tighter bounds on the spectral radius, potentially expanding the range of architectures for which global optimality is guaranteed.
*   **Perform More Extensive Experiments:**  Evaluate the method on more challenging, high-dimensional datasets, such as image classification tasks.
*   **Provide Parameter Selection Guidance:** Offer more practical guidance on selecting the model parameters to ensure convergence and achieve good performance.
