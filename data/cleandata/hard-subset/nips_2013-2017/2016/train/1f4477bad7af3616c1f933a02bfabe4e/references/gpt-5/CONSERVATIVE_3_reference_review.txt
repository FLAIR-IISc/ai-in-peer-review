Summary
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a class of generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights. The activations are monomials with possibly non-integer exponents αl ≥ 1, and all layers are p-norm normalized. The objective is nonstandard: maximize minus the cross-entropy loss plus the sum of all outputs, plus an arbitrarily small positive linear term in the weights to ensure strictly positive gradients. The authors construct an iterative mapping GΦ based on normalized gradients and analyze it using multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction mappings under the Thompson metric. The main theorem states that if the spectral radius of a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 for one hidden layer; K+2 for two hidden layers) is less than 1, then the algorithm converges linearly to a unique global maximizer. They argue the condition can be checked a priori and can be enforced (conservatively) by choosing large p exponents. Limited experiments on small UCI datasets show accuracy roughly comparable to simple ReLU networks and below RBF SVM.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical framework: fixed-point characterization, contraction via Thompson metric, and a verifiable spectral radius condition for global convergence with a linear rate.
- Extends to two hidden layers under analogous conditions.
- The check for ρ(A) < 1 reduces to a small matrix and can be done before training.
- Deterministic algorithm with no learning-rate tuning; iteration complexity is transparent.
- An interesting connection between multi-homogeneous nonlinear maps and neural network training.

Weaknesses
- Practical relevance is limited by strong modeling constraints: nonnegative inputs, nonnegative weights, per-unit monomial activations with distinct exponents, norm-normalized layers, and a modified objective that includes a sum of outputs and an epsilon linear term. These changes substantially depart from standard neural nets and cross-entropy training and may undermine applicability.
- The “global optimum” claim pertains to the modified objective under constraints, not to standard cross-entropy or standard architectures. The paper does not clarify the implications for conventional models or how to interpret the added sum-of-outputs term under softmax cross-entropy.
- The conditions for contraction (ρ(A) < 1) appear conservative and degrade with number of hidden units and data norms, leading the authors to limit experiments to small, low-dimensional datasets. This raises scalability concerns and questions the feasibility on typical NIPS-scale tasks.
- Empirical evaluation is weak: only small UCI datasets; performance generally below RBF SVM and only sometimes competitive with simple ReLU baselines; no ablation on the impact of α choices, nonnegativity constraints, or the added output-sum term; no larger or structured datasets (vision, text), despite the initial motivation (images, word counts).
- Comparisons may be unfair or insufficient: ReLU training settings (SGD with fixed step sizes) are limited; the proposed method also involves many hyperparameters (n1, α vector, ρw, ρu, p exponents), selected by random search with cross-validation, which can be at least as involved as tuning SGD.
- Several technical dependencies are not fully self-contained (e.g., reference [7] “in preparation”) and bounds are acknowledged to be loose; proofs of key lemmas are deferred to the supplement.
- Presentation issues: missing or unclear definitions (precise form of L is not explicitly written), references are incomplete, typos, and layout artifacts. The requirement that αl be all distinct to avoid triviality is important but leaves practical guidance unclear.

Quality
The mathematical development appears careful and leverages established tools (Banach fixed-point theorem, Thompson metric, Collatz–Wielandt). However, the global optimality pertains to a heavily modified, constrained problem, and the key contraction condition is enforced by making p large, with unclear impact on modeling capacity and numerical stability. Empirical evidence is too limited to support claims of practical feasibility; the method underperforms strong baselines on standard small datasets and is not demonstrated at scale.

Clarity
The paper is notation-heavy but the high-level flow (optimization → fixed point → contraction) is understandable. However, important definitions (exact cross-entropy form, prediction rule at test time) are not explicit. The role and impact of the sum-of-outputs term and epsilon need clearer justification and empirical analysis. References are incomplete and formatting has numerous glitches.

Originality
The application of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and multihomogeneous contraction analysis to this training setup is novel. Prior work on tensor methods and factorization-based guarantees is discussed, but the present approach is different. That said, the model restrictions and objective modifications are unconventional.

Significance
As a theoretical contribution, the existence of a simple, checkable condition for linear convergence to a unique global maximizer is interesting. However, given the restrictive assumptions and weak empirical performance, I am not convinced this advances practical training of neural networks or will see adoption. The claim of “first practically feasible algorithm” seems overstated.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely define L and the prediction rule used at test time. How does adding sum_r f_r to the objective interact with softmax cross-entropy?
- How are datasets with negative features handled? What preprocessing ensures nonnegativity?
- What value of epsilon was used in experiments? How sensitive are results to it?
- Provide ablations: effect of distinct vs. equal αl, effect of nonnegativity constraints, and the impact of increasing p to satisfy ρ(A) < 1.
- Discuss computational cost per iteration vs. SGD and provide wall-clock comparisons.
- Can you show results on at least one moderately high-dimensional dataset, or convincingly argue why the method cannot scale due to ρ(A)?

Pros
- Solid and self-consistent theoretical framework with a verifiable condition.
- Extension to two hidden layers.
- Deterministic, linearly convergent algorithm under stated conditions.
- Novel use of multi-homogeneous PF theory.

Cons
- Strong, arguably impractical modeling constraints and a nonstandard objective.
- Weak empirical validation and performance; no large-scale experiments.
- Conservative bounds limiting model size; unclear path to scalability.
- Clarity and reference issues; reliance on in-prep citation.

Recommendation
Reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting but, under the current modeling assumptions and experimental evidence, the work does not meet the bar for practical impact or validation expected at NIPS. Strengthening the empirical section, clarifying the learning objective’s implications, tightening bounds (or demonstrating less conservative practice), and showing applicability beyond small nonnegative datasets would substantially improve the paper.

Confidence: High.
