- Presents an algorithm for globally optimizing single-layer (and extended multi-layer) generalized polynomial neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods and Perron-Frobenius theory.  
- Provides theoretical global optimality guarantees under non-negativity of weights/data and a linear convergence rate; avoids learning-rate hyperparameter tuning.  
- Proofs and full theoretical development are provided (some details in the supplement).  
- Positions itself relative to Haeffele & Vidal (2016) and Janzamin et al. (2016), claiming a less restrictive requirement (only non-negativity) and a novel PF/theoretical connection.  
- Strength: rare global optimality guarantee for a class of neural networks.  
- Strength: linear convergence suggesting fast training.  
- Strength: avoids learning-rate and similar hyperparameter tuning.  
- Strength: novel methodological perspective via nonlinear spectral methods.  
- Strength: clear theoretical exposition.  
- Strength: initial empirical validation on UCI datasets showing competitive performance.  
- Weakness: strong non-negativity constraint limits expressiveness and real-world applicability.  
- Weakness: addition of the sum-of-outputs term to the objective is non-standard and feels ad-hoc.  
- Weakness: spectral radius bounds are likely conservative, so conditions may be stricter than necessary.  
- Weakness: experiments limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets; scalability and robustness unclear; comparisons to ReLU may be unfair.  
- Weakness: parameter selection relies on satisfying spectral radius condition and lacks guidance (e.g., choice of α).  
- Weakness: potential computational/implementation complexity for larger networks.  
- For acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, linear convergence, and new research direction merit publication.  
- Against acceptance: practical limitations (non-negativity, limited experiments) and specialized theory may limit impact.  
- Overall: recommend acceptance with minor revisions, requesting expanded experiments, better parameter-selection strategies, intuitive justification for the objective modification, and discussion of computational cost.
