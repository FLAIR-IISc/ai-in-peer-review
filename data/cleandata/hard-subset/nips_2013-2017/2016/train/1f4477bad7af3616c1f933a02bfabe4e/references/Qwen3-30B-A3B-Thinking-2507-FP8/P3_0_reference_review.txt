

# Peer Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a globally optimal training algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors claim to provide the first practically feasible globally optimal algorithm for neural networks, with linear convergence rate and no hyperparameter tuning required. The method requires nonnegative weights and a modified objective function that adds the sum of outputs to the loss.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on recent work by Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015), which provided globally optimal algorithms but were impractical due to computational complexity or requiring the data-generating measure. The authors argue their method overcomes these limitations by requiring only nonnegative data (common for count data like images or word frequencies) and a spectral radius condition that can be checked before training. Unlike previous approaches, their method doesn't require knowledge of the data-generating distribution.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous proof of global optimality with linear convergence rate, which is a significant theoretical advance. The convergence condition (spectral radius < 1) is checkable before training, a practical advantage over methods requiring empirical validation.

2. **Practical Implementation:** The algorithm has no hyperparameters (no learning rate to tune) and converges quickly in practice (as shown in Figure 2). This addresses a key pain point in deep learning training.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The authors provide the first empirical results for a globally optimal neural network training algorithm on real datasets (UCI), which is a significant contribution compared to previous theoretical work that lacked experiments.

## Weaknesses

1. **Strong Nonnegativity Constraint:** The requirement of nonnegative weights is extremely restrictive for most neural network applications. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't convincingly argue why this constraint is acceptable for general applications. Most real-world data has both positive and negative features, and weights can naturally be negative. The paper's focus on count data is too narrow to be generally applicable.

2. **Lack of Justification for Modified Objective:** The authors add the sum of outputs to the objective function to ensure gradient positivity, but provide no theoretical justification for why this modification doesn't compromise the model's expressiveness. The experimental results (Table 1) show only marginal improvements over SVM (e.g., 96.4% vs. 95.7% on Cancer), which doesn't justify the strong constraint.

3. **Overly Conservative Bounds:** The bounds for the spectral radius condition (Equations 4 and 9) are extremely conservative. For example, Equation 4 requires p_w > 4(K+1)ξ_1 + 3, which for small datasets would require p_w to be very large (e.g., 100+), making the method impractical. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide a way to tighten these bounds.

4. **Parameter Selection Challenges:** The paper claims to choose parameters through cross-validation, but doesn't explain how to choose the α_i (which must be distinct to avoid trivial classifiers) or the number of hidden units. This is a significant practical limitation that undermines the "practical feasibility" claim.

5. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, which doesn't demonstrate the method's scalability to modern deep learning tasks. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't address how to extend to high-dimensional data.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to the field of globally optimal neural network training. The mathematical framework is sound, and the convergence guarantees are rigorous. However, the practical limitations of the method (nonnegative weights constraint, conservative bounds, lack of justification for modified objective) significantly undermine its applicability to real-world problems.

The experimental results show competitive performance on UCI datasets, but the marginal improvements over SVM and ReLU networks don't justify the restrictive assumptions. For instance, on the Cancer dataset, NLSM1 achieves 96.4% accuracy while SVM achieves 95.7%, a 0.7% improvement that doesn't warrant the strong constraints of the method.

The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could:
1. Provide a more convincing argument for why nonnegative weights are acceptable in general applications
2. Show how to choose the α_i parameters without exhaustive search
3. Tighten the theoretical bounds for the spectral radius condition
4. Extend the experiments to higher-dimensional datasets

## Recommendation

Given the significant theoretical contribution but substantial practical limitations, I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes an important theoretical advance, but the practical utility is limited by the restrictive assumptions. With further development addressing the weaknesses noted above, this could become a highly impactful paper. The authors should address the parameter selection issue and provide more convincing justification for the nonnegative weight constraint to make their method more broadly applicable.

**Pro Acceptance:** Rigorous global optimality guarantee with linear convergence rate; first empirical results for globally optimal neural network training on real datasets; no hyperparameter tuning required.

**Con Acceptance:** Nonnegative weights constraint is too restrictive for general applications; modified objective lacks justification; theoretical bounds are extremely conservative; parameter selection is not well-justified.
