- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a restricted class of feedforward nets with checkable global optimality conditions.  
- Networks use generalized polynomial activations with nonnegative inputs and weights, and training maximizes negative cross-entropy plus small positive output- and weight-sum terms.  
- Constructs a fixed-point map from normalized gradients and an explicit nonnegative matrix A; shows if ρ(A) < 1 then the map is a contraction.  
- Under ρ(A) < 1 they prove a unique positive critical point, uniqueness of the global maximizer, and linear convergence from any positive initialization.  
- Analysis extends from one to two hidden layers with appropriately expanded A; deeper networks are left for future work.  
- Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy with ReLU and RBF SVM and substantiate linear convergence, but are modest in scope.  
- Positions contribution against tensor and matrix-factorization global-opt results, relying instead on nonnegativity, norm constraints, and a spectral condition.  
- Theoretical contribution is substantial and technically sound; reasoning chain (interiorization, fixed points, contraction, Banach) is coherent.  
- Dependence of ρ(A) on hyperparameters is carefully discussed; authors acknowledge conservativeness of sufficient conditions.  
- Assumptions are strong and explicit: nonnegative data/weights, p-norm constraints, distinct αℓ ≥ 1, and added output/ε terms for strict positivity.  
- Clarity is overall good for experts, but notation/exposition issues: unclear negative cross-entropy definition, inconsistent ε notation, and limited discussion of practical ε choice.  
- Motivation and empirical effect of adding the output-sum term, and the practical cost of distinct α per unit, need more explanation and ablation.  
- Originality is high: novel application of multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and Thompson-metric contractions to NN training.  
- Scientific significance is meaningful (provable, checkable global convergence), but practical impact is limited by restrictions and conservative scaling with network size.  
- Strengths: explicit verifiable conditions, linear convergence rate, novel spectral-method approach, extension to two layers, transparent assumptions.  
- Weaknesses: atypical model restrictions, conservative bounds that worsen with size, limited empirical evaluation, missing ablations, and some notation/practical guidance gaps.  
- Key author questions: clarify exact loss form and interaction with output-sum term; provide ablations for output/ε and α choices; show higher-dimensional/synthetic experiments and data-dependent bounds; report numerical stability for large p; discuss relaxing nonnegativity.  
- Recommendation: weak accept (score 6) — strong and original theory with limited empirical/practical reach; improve clarity and broaden experiments.
