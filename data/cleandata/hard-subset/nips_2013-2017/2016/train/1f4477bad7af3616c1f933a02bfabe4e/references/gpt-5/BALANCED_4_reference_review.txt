Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of a constrained class of feedforward neural networks with generalized polynomial activations. Inputs and weights are constrained to be nonnegative; each layer’s weights are normalized in ℓp norms; activations are monomials with exponents αl ≥ 1 (distinct across hidden units). The training objective maximizes negative cross-entropy plus an extra “sum of outputs” term and a small positive linear term in the parameters to ensure strictly positive gradients on the positive orthant.

The key idea is to construct a multi-homogeneous, order-preserving update mapping GΦ based on normalized (componentwise) powers of the gradient, and to analyze GΦ using nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory on cones, the Thompson metric, and the Banach fixed point theorem. The main result gives a simple, checkable sufficient condition for global optimality: the spectral radius of a (K+1)×(K+1) nonnegative matrix A, built from model hyperparameters and simple data bounds, must be < 1. Under this condition, GΦ is a contraction, there is a unique fixed point in the positive interior, and the iterates converge linearly to the global maximizer. The framework is extended to two hidden layers (matrix size K+2). Experiments on small UCI datasets compare NLSM to ReLU networks trained with SGD and to RBF-SVMs; NLSM is competitive with ReLU and somewhat below SVM overall; convergence to the global optimum of the modified objective is rapid.

This work sits at the intersection of structured nonconvex optimization with global guarantees (e.g., Haeffele & Vidal; Janzamin et al.) and polynomial networks (e.g., Livni et al.), and builds on recent multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory. Relative to tensor methods (e.g., Janzamin et al.), the guarantee does not require a score function tensor or nondegeneracy, but imposes positivity, normalization, and an altered objective.

Strengths
- A novel and technically solid application of nonlinear spectral methods and contraction mappings to neural network training, with a clear fixed-point algorithm.
- Checkable a priori sufficient condition for global optimality (ρ(A) < 1) and linear convergence rate; no step-size tuning.
- Extension to two hidden layers and explicit dependence of ρ(A) on architecture and hyperparameters.
- First empirical demonstration (to my knowledge) of a globally optimal training method for a nontrivial NN producing nontrivial decision boundaries; convergence plots clearly show linear rate.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical restrictions: nonnegative data and weights, monomial activations with fixed exponents, ℓp normalization constraints, and an added “sum of outputs” term in the objective. These make the model and objective significantly deviate from standard neural networks and may limit applicability/performance.
- The global guarantee relies on conservative bounds; satisfying ρ(A) < 1 often requires choosing large pw, pu and small networks, potentially harming expressivity. The paper acknowledges conservativeness but does not quantify how tight A is in practice.
- The distinct-α requirement to avoid permutation invariance (and consequent trivial rank-one solutions) is unusual. α are treated as fixed hyperparameters rather than learned, further constraining the model.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets where SVMs excel; results are mixed and often below SVM. No experiments on higher-dimensional nonnegative data (e.g., MNIST) where the positivity constraint would be natural.
- Limited ablations: the necessity and impact of the additional output-sum and small ε terms are not quantified; no sensitivity analysis for ρw, ρu, pw, pu, and α. The extension to two layers is only lightly tested.
- Some clarity issues: the ε term appears in prose but is not consistently displayed in the objective; intuition for the added terms and their effect on classification/probability calibration is brief.

Quality
- The theoretical development appears careful and correct, with proofs relying on established tools (Thompson metric, Collatz–Wielandt, Banach fixed point). Some technical proofs are deferred to the supplement.
- The empirical section is adequate as a proof of concept but does not convincingly demonstrate practical utility or scalability.

Clarity
- Generally well structured, though notation is heavy. The role of the added terms (sum of outputs and ε-regularization) deserves clearer motivation and empirical validation. Provide consistent notation for ε in equations. Including more intuition and small illustrative examples would help.

Originality
- High on the optimization side: the specific use of multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory to obtain a contraction for a NN training map is novel. The architectural and objective constraints are unusual.

Significance
- As a theoretical contribution showing a feasible globally convergent algorithm for a constrained neural model, it is interesting. Practical impact seems limited at present due to strong constraints and modest empirical performance.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Quantify tightness: report the actual ρ(A) (and the optimal Lipschitz constant from ATγ/γ) across experiments, and compare with empirical convergence rates. Explore less conservative A (Theorem 4) in practice.
- Ablations: remove or vary the output-sum term and ε to measure their effect on accuracy and calibration; analyze what the modified objective optimizes relative to cross-entropy.
- Sensitivity/selection: provide guidance or an adaptive scheme (e.g., continuation on pw, pu or ρw, ρu) to achieve ρ(A) < 1 while maintaining accuracy; study sensitivity to α and test learning α under constraints.
- Expressivity: discuss approximation power under nonnegativity and monomial activations (beyond citing polynomial networks), and show decision boundaries vs ReLU on synthetic tasks.
- Broaden experiments to higher-dimensional nonnegative data (e.g., MNIST with [0,1] pixels, bag-of-words text), report wall-clock vs SGD, and include comparisons to nonnegative/monotone networks or NMF-like baselines.
- Clarity: ensure ε appears explicitly in all displayed formulae; add more intuition for GΦ and the Thompson metric; provide code or pseudocode with complexity details.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical framework with clear, checkable global optimality and linear convergence.
- Simple algorithm; extends beyond one layer; first empirical demonstration of a globally optimal NN training method.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong modeling constraints and modified objective; limited practicality demonstrated.
- Conservative sufficient conditions; unclear trade-offs and parameter selection.
- Narrow, small-scale experiments with mixed performance.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current empirical validation and practical scope are too limited for NeurIPS. Strengthening the empirical section, clarifying the objective’s implications, and providing tighter/less conservative conditions or adaptive strategies would significantly improve the paper.
