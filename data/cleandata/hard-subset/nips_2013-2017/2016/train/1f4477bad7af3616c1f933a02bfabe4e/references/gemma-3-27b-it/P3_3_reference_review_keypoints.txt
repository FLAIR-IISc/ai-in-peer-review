- Summary: Proposes Nonlinear Spectral Method (NLSM) to globally optimally train 1–2 layer feedforward networks under non-negativity and a modified objective, with linear convergence and spectral-radius-based optimality check; validated on UCI datasets.

- Strength: Provides a theoretical global optimality guarantee for a non-trivial class of neural networks.

- Strength: Optimality check reduces to computing the spectral radius of a small matrix, making it computationally feasible.

- Strength: Demonstrates linear convergence rate, promising faster training than standard gradient methods.

- Strength: Strong mathematical grounding via Perron-Frobenius theory and detailed theoretical development.

- Strength: Uses a motivated (though non-standard) modified objective that enables the theoretical framework.

- Strength: Experimental results show competitive performance and speed advantages on selected UCI datasets.

- Weakness: Requires non-negative weights, a restrictive constraint that limits expressiveness and applicability.

- Weakness: The added regularization term (sum of outputs) seems ad-hoc and its representational impact is not fully explored.

- Weakness: Scalability concerns—spectral radius and theoretical/practical performance likely degrade in high-dimensional or larger hidden-layer settings.

- Weakness: Limited experimental scope confined to small, low-dimensional UCI datasets; no evaluation on complex tasks like image classification.

- Weakness: Sensitivity to chosen α values for hidden units is under-discussed; distinct α selection is crucial but unclear.

- Weakness: Comparison to other globally optimal methods (e.g., Janzamin et al.) lacks depth on practical trade-offs like cost and scalability.

- Overall assessment: Technically sound and original with strong theoretical contributions, but practical impact is limited by non-negativity and scalability issues.

- Argument for acceptance: Offers a rigorous, computationally feasible framework for global optimization in a previously intractable setting.

- Argument for acceptance: Shows competitive empirical performance and fills a literature gap with a practical algorithm and checks for global optimality.

- Argument against acceptance: The non-negativity constraint significantly limits applicability.

- Argument against acceptance: Unclear scalability to high-dimensional, real-world datasets and limited experimental validation.

- Rating: 7/10 (Accept with Minor Revisions).

- Recommendation: Expand experiments to more challenging datasets, including image classification.

- Recommendation: Investigate methods to mitigate spectral-radius growth and address scalability (regularization or approximate spectral methods).

- Recommendation: Provide deeper analysis of α parameter sensitivity and guidance on selecting α values.

- Recommendation: Include a more comprehensive comparison with other globally optimal training methods, highlighting trade-offs.

- Recommendation: Explore tighter theoretical bounds for ρ(A) and clarify the necessity and effect of the added regularization term.
