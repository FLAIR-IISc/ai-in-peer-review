- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) to train a restricted class of feedforward nets to global optimality with linear convergence under a spectral condition.
- Model constraints: nonnegative inputs and weights, no biases, Lp-norm constraints per layer, and per-unit generalized polynomial activations (distinct α_l ≥ 1).
- Objective is a modified maximization: negative cross-entropy plus a sum over outputs term and an ε-weight sum term.
- Updates use a normalized positive “gradient map” on the positive cone; contraction shown via Perron–Frobenius-type arguments in the Thompson metric.
- Main theoretical guarantee: uniqueness of fixed point and linear convergence if ρ(A)<1 for a small (K+1)-by-(K+1) nonnegative matrix A.
- Framework extended to two hidden layers; experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy and faster convergence than SGD on the modified objective.
- Complements prior global-training work (structured factorization, moment/tensor methods) by offering an iterative, practical algorithm with a checkable precondition.
- Connects training dynamics to nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and multi-homogeneous maps; should discuss links to NMF/multiplicative updates and projective-metric contractions more fully.
- Strength: clear, theoretically grounded convergence guarantee with a tractable spectral condition and no learning-rate tuning.
- Strength: original nonlinear spectral viewpoint and technically interesting use of Thompson metric and multi-homogeneous structure.
- Strength: proof strategy is organized (interior maximizer → fixed-point → contraction) and empirical results show feasibility and stability.
- Weakness: “global optimality” applies only to a heavily constrained, nonstandard model and a modified objective far from typical neural networks.
- Weakness: Added sum-of-outputs term and ε-weight change the optimization landscape; paper lacks analysis of their effect on classification risk and decision boundaries.
- Weakness: Requiring distinct, fixed α_l per unit is unusual, may reduce expressivity, and lacks guidance on choosing α_l or learning them.
- Weakness: Spectral-radius bound appears conservative and scales poorly with hidden-unit count; no experiments probing tightness or behavior when ρ(A)≥1.
- Weakness: Experimental evaluation is limited (small datasets, few baselines, no wall-clock or sensitivity analyses), so practical superiority is unproven.
- Weakness: Missing or incomplete related work citations (classical NMF/multiplicative updates, projective metric analyses, convex NN formulations) and sparse discussion.
- Weakness: Clarity and notation problems (missing formulas, typos, incomplete references, inconsistent notation) and limited detail on per-iteration computational cost.
- Key question: What is the precise loss used and does adding ∑r f_r(x) preserve Bayes consistency or harmlessness as ε→0?
- Key question: Can α_l be learned while preserving contraction, and is there a tractable condition on α_l for ρ(A)<1?
- Key question: How tight are the A bounds in practice and can data-dependent tighter bounds permit larger networks?
- Key question: How to handle negative-valued inputs (e.g., z-scoring) and how do input shifts affect guarantees?
- Key question: Can biases be incorporated while retaining nonnegativity and contraction (e.g., via augmented inputs)?
- Key question: What is per-iteration complexity vs. SGD/Adam and wall-clock convergence on larger datasets?
- Pros for acceptance: novel, elegant application of nonlinear spectral methods, solid self-contained theory, and opens a fertile connection between PF theory and NN optimization.
- Cons for acceptance: limited practical impact due to strong constraints and modified objective, weak experimental evidence, and clarity/reproducibility issues.
- Recommendation: Borderline reject; paper needs stronger experiments, clearer analysis of the added terms and constraints, tighter/tighter-justified bounds, and improved clarity and related-work coverage.
