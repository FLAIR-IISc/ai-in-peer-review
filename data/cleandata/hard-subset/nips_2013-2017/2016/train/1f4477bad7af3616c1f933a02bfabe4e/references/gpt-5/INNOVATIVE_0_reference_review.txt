Summary
This paper proposes a bold and original optimization framework for training a class of neural networks to global optimality using nonlinear spectral methods grounded in Perron–Frobenius theory on cones and fixed-point analysis in the Thompson metric. The model is a multi-class network with one (and an extension to two) hidden layer(s), nonnegative inputs and weights, and “generalized polynomial” activations with exponents αl ≥ 1 (distinct across hidden units). The training objective is modified: maximize negative cross-entropy plus a sum of outputs, along with small positive linear terms in the parameters to ensure strictly positive gradients on the cone. The core algorithm is a normalized-gradient power-like map GΦ that is shown to be a contraction under conditions that can be checked a priori by computing the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small nonnegative matrix A determined by the architecture, norms, and data bounds. If ρ(A) < 1, the method converges linearly from any positive initialization to the unique global maximizer. The authors provide theory, an extension to two hidden layers, and proof-of-concept experiments on several UCI datasets, with speed advantages over SGD and competitive accuracy with ReLU networks and RBF-SVM in some cases.

Relation to prior work
The work directly tackles global optimization of (restricted) neural networks, building on: (i) global optimality and factorization perspectives (Haeffele & Vidal) that are insightful but “challenging to apply,” and (ii) tensor methods (Janzamin et al.) requiring score-function tensors and strong non-degeneracy assumptions. In contrast, the present approach requires only nonnegativity and architectural choices satisfying a spectral-radius condition, with an implementable fixed-point iteration and linear rates. The analysis leverages Nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and Birkhoff/Thompson metrics (Lemmens & Nussbaum), and is reminiscent of multiplicative-update/normalized power methods in NMF, but generalized to multi-homogeneous maps with explicit contraction constants.

Strengths
- Originality: A genuinely novel, conceptually clean route to globally optimal training via nonlinear spectral methods and contraction mappings. The spectral-radius check and linear convergence are elegant and practically verifiable.
- Theoretical depth: Clear pathway from constrained optimization to a fixed-point map; identification of a weighted Thompson metric that minimizes the Lipschitz bound via the Collatz–Wielandt eigenvector; conditions for uniqueness and rates; extension to two hidden layers.
- Practicality relative to prior global-optimality work: Requires only nonnegative data and weights and an a priori architectural check (computing ρ(A) of a (K+1)- or (K+2)-dimensional matrix).
- Algorithmic simplicity: Parameter-free iteration (no learning rate), with explicit rate bound driven by ρ(A).
- Experiments: Though small-scale, they demonstrate nontrivial decision boundaries, competitive accuracy with standard baselines on some datasets, and faster convergence than batch-SGD on the modified objective.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling gap to standard practice: The objective adds a sum-of-outputs term (and ε-linear parameter terms) to make gradients strictly positive. This deviates from the standard cross-entropy objective and can bias the classifier; the impact on calibration and generalization is not analyzed. An ablation isolating this term’s effect would help.
- Restrictive assumptions: Nonnegative inputs/weights and polynomial activations with fixed exponents limit applicability. While suitable for count data and image intensities after preprocessing, many tasks will require nontrivial transformations. Discussion of practical pre-processing pipelines (e.g., shifts, rescalings) and their effect on guarantees would be useful.
- Hyperparameter selection: Performance depends on α, p-norms (pu, pw, pv), radii (ρu, ρw, ρv), and n1,n2. The paper acknowledges conservative bounds for ensuring ρ(A)<1 (often requiring large p), but provides little guidance on choosing these in practice beyond random search and cross-validation.
- Expressivity and invariances: The need for distinct α across hidden units to avoid degeneracies highlights delicate invariance issues; the practical trade-offs and representational capacity relative to ReLU/tanh networks are not fully explored.
- Experimental scope: Datasets are low-dimensional; comparisons are limited and do not explore scaling, larger architectures, or higher-dimensional vision/NLP tasks where the assumptions may still hold (e.g., nonnegative pixel intensities, BoW features). Also, the multiclass loss specification and output-to-probability mapping are under-specified in the main text.
- Clarity/Presentation: Some definitions (the exact cross-entropy form, role and magnitude of ε) are incomplete in the main text; a compact pseudocode with complexity per iteration and a practical recipe for setting α, p, and ρ would improve readability.

Quality
The theoretical development appears sound and carefully tied to known fixed-point and PF theory. Some proofs are deferred; the assumptions (distinct α, ε>0, positivity of gradients) are clearly stated. The experimental section is honest about scope and supports feasibility, though not definitive on accuracy.

Clarity
Generally well structured, but dense. Improving the exposition of the objective (precisely define the loss), providing intuition for the added output-sum term, and offering practitioner guidelines would help. A figure illustrating the contraction geometry and the role of A would aid understanding.

Originality
High. The nonlinear spectral perspective with a concrete, checkable spectral-radius condition and linear-rate fixed-point iteration for globally optimal training is, to my knowledge, new in ML.

Significance
Conceptually significant: provides an actionable path to global optima for a nontrivial NN class, likely to inspire follow-up work (e.g., monotone networks, convolutional variants on cones, better A bounds, stochastic/online versions). Immediate empirical impact is modest due to modeling constraints and limited experiments.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide an ablation on the added sum-of-outputs/ε terms: effect on training dynamics, calibration, and decision boundaries.
- Offer a practical recipe for choosing α, p, and ρ to both satisfy ρ(A)<1 and avoid overly conservative regimes. Can adaptive schemes tighten A online?
- Clarify the exact multiclass loss, how outputs map to probabilities, and whether biases are included/excludable.
- Discuss preprocessing to enforce nonnegativity in common modalities, and how this interacts with guarantees.
- Report iteration complexity, memory footprint, and wall-clock comparisons to strong optimizers on moderate-size datasets.
- Explore higher-dimensional synthetic tasks to probe scalability and the dependence of ρ(A) on width/depth.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical framework with clear global optimality and linear-rate guarantees.
- Practically verifiable condition via a small spectral-radius computation.
- Opens a compelling direction connecting PF theory and NN training.

Cons for acceptance
- Nonstandard objective and restrictive assumptions; limited empirical validation and guidance.
- Conservative sufficient conditions may limit usable regimes; unclear scalability.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. Despite imperfections and modest experiments, the conceptual novelty and crisp global-optimality guarantees under a verifiable condition make this a valuable and inspiring contribution for NeurIPS.
