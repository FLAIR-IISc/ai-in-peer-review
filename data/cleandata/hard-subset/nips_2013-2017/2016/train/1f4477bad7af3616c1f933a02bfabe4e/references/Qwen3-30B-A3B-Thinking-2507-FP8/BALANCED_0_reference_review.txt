

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method for training generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights and real-power activations, proving linear convergence to the global optimum under certain conditions. The key insight is that by restricting to nonnegative weights and data (which holds for count data like images and word frequencies), the optimization problem can be transformed into a nonlinear eigenproblem on the positive orthant, allowing application of Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors prove that the spectral radius of a small matrix (computable before training) determines convergence, and provide a globally optimal algorithm without hyperparameters.

The work builds on recent advances in globally optimal neural network training (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.), but addresses their practical limitations: the spectral method avoids the need for unknown data distributions required by tensor approaches and provides a concrete algorithm with convergence guarantees. The authors correctly distinguish their work from prior literature, noting that previous approaches are "challenging to apply in practice" or require conditions difficult to verify.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical contribution is significant: the paper provides the first practically feasible globally optimal algorithm for neural networks with provable convergence guarantees. The convergence rate is linear, and the spectral radius condition can be checked without running the algorithm.
2. The mathematical development is rigorous, with careful proofs of key lemmas and theorems (though some proofs are relegated to supplementary material).
3. The authors demonstrate the approach on UCI datasets, showing competitive performance compared to SVM and ReLU networks, which is notable since prior global optimality papers lack empirical validation.
4. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate technical depth for the NIPS audience.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The nonnegativity constraint on weights is extremely restrictive and limits applicability to real-world problems where negative weights are essential (e.g., most natural image data). The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this constraint is acceptable for practical applications.
2. The modified objective function (adding the negative sum of outputs) lacks clear motivation and appears ad-hoc. This modification is necessary for the theoretical guarantees but may degrade performance in practice.
3. The experimental results show only marginal improvements over baseline methods, with several datasets where the proposed method underperforms. The paper doesn't provide sufficient explanation for these performance differences.
4. The parameter selection process (choosing α, n1, ρw, ρu) is poorly justified. The authors randomly sample from parameter spaces and select the best performer via cross-validation, but provide no guidance for practitioners on how to choose these parameters for new datasets.
5. The theoretical bounds for the spectral radius are conservative, and the paper acknowledges this, limiting the range of architectures that can be trained (the authors restrict to low-dimensional datasets).

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- First practically feasible globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks with provable convergence guarantees
- Theoretical framework is elegant and extends Perron-Frobenius theory to neural network training
- Demonstrates the first empirical results for globally optimal neural networks on real datasets
- Linear convergence rate and no hyperparameters make the method theoretically appealing

*Con:*
- Nonnegativity constraint on weights severely limits practical applicability
- Modified objective function lacks theoretical justification
- Experimental results show only marginal performance gains over standard methods
- Parameter selection lacks principled guidance
- Theoretical bounds are conservative, restricting the method's applicability

**Recommendation:**

While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the field of neural network optimization, the restrictive assumptions significantly limit its practical impact. The nonnegativity constraint on weights is a major limitation that would prevent adoption in most real-world applications. The paper would benefit from either:
1) A more convincing argument for why nonnegative weights are acceptable in practice, or
2) A discussion of how to relax the constraint while maintaining global optimality guarantees.

The experimental results are adequate but not compelling enough to overcome the theoretical limitations. The authors should address the parameter selection issue more thoroughly and provide a clearer explanation for why the modified objective function is necessary.

Given NIPS's focus on high-impact contributions that advance the field, this paper's theoretical novelty is offset by its limited practical relevance. I recommend rejection, but with the hope that addressing these limitations would make the work suitable for a future venue.
