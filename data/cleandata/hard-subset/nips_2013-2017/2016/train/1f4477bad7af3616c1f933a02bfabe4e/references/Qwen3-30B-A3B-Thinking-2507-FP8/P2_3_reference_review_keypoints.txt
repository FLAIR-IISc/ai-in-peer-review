- Proposes a globally optimal training algorithm for polynomial-activation networks with nonnegative weights using a nonlinear spectral method inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory.  
- Main theoretical result: linear convergence to the global optimum when the spectral radius of a certain matrix is < 1, with a checkable condition.  
- Strong mathematical development linking optimization to fixed-point theory and use of Banach fixed point theorem; proofs are well-structured and sound.  
- Theoretical bounds are very conservative, potentially limiting practical applicability even when conditions hold.  
- Method requires nonnegative data and weights, a major constraint not thoroughly analyzed or justified for real-world data.  
- Experimental validation is limited to small UCI datasets and low-dimensional settings, reducing practical relevance.  
- Scalability concerns: matrix A’s entries grow with hidden units, suggesting poor scaling to larger networks.  
- Missing comparisons to the most relevant prior work (e.g., [11]) on real datasets, weakening empirical claims of advantage.  
- Clarity issues: motivation for the modified objective (adding sum of outputs) is buried and not clearly explained.  
- Important constraint αi ≠ αj is downplayed in a parenthetical rather than emphasized as critical.  
- Insufficient explanation of why Perron-Frobenius/nonlinear spectral methods are preferable to alternative approaches for readers unfamiliar with the area.  
- Originality: an interesting and meaningful theoretical contribution, but not clearly distinguished from tensor or structured matrix factorization approaches.  
- Significance limited: global optimality is theoretically valuable but not shown to solve a meaningful practical problem given constraints and limited experiments.  
- Recommended improvements: analyze nonnegativity impact on expressivity, compare to prior work on real datasets, better justify practical feasibility, and run larger/more challenging experiments.  
- Overall recommendation: reject due to limited practical significance, strong constraints, and insufficient experimental validation despite solid theoretical work.
