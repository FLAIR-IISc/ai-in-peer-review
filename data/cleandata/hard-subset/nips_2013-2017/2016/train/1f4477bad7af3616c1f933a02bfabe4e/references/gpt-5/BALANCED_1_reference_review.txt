Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) to train a class of feedforward neural networks with provable convergence to a unique global optimum. The networks use nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and generalized polynomial activations with unit-specific exponents αl ≥ 1 (and required to be distinct). Training is posed as a maximization of a modified objective: negative cross-entropy plus the sum of class outputs, with small positive linear terms added to weights for technical reasons. Parameters are constrained to fixed Lp norms. The algorithm iterates a normalized positive-gradient mapping GΦ whose contraction is established in the Thompson metric via multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory. A sufficient condition for global convergence is that the spectral radius of a (K+1)×(K+1) nonnegative matrix A is < 1; this condition depends only on architecture and norm parameters and can be checked before training. The approach extends to two hidden layers (with a (K+2)×(K+2) A), and the authors present small UCI experiments suggesting competitiveness with shallow ReLU networks and RBF-SVM.

This work connects to: (i) guarantees for nonconvex NN training via structured factorization (Haeffele & Vidal) and tensor methods (Janzamin et al.), where the latter require score-function tensors and non-degeneracy conditions; (ii) nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction mappings in cones (Lemmens & Nussbaum, Thompson metric). The key novelty is integrating multihomogeneous PF theory with a specific NN architecture to yield a checkable global optimality condition and a practical fixed-point algorithm.

Quality
- Theory: The fixed-point/contraction framework is carefully developed. The mapping GΦ is well-motivated by positivity and multi-homogeneity, and the linear convergence rate follows from a clear Banach fixed-point argument. The use of a small matrix A to capture Lipschitz bounds is elegant. However, the bounds used to construct A are acknowledged to be conservative; the practical tightness and scaling with depth/width are unclear.
- Assumptions: The guarantees require (i) nonnegative inputs; (ii) nonnegative weights; (iii) generalized polynomial activations with exponents ≥ 1 and pairwise distinct; and (iv) a modified objective that includes a sum over outputs (not scaled by ε). These are strong modeling changes relative to standard NNs and likely reduce expressivity or shift the learning target. The necessity and implications of the added “sum-of-outputs” term for classification are insufficiently analyzed.
- Empirics: Experiments are proof-of-concept on small UCI datasets, with limited detail on preprocessing to enforce nonnegativity. The method is sometimes competitive with ReLU and SVM, but overall results are modest and do not demonstrate clear advantages or scalability. The reliance on random hyperparameter search over many architectural and norm parameters weakens the claim that the method is “parameter-free.” Comparisons to relevant nonnegative/monotone models (e.g., NMF-based classifiers, monotone/positivity-constrained NNs) are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but dense. Core ideas (positivity, Thompson metric, contraction) are conveyed, yet several definitions are missing or imprecise (e.g., the exact cross-entropy form used with logits; preprocessing for nonnegative features). The role and necessity of the sum-of-outputs term are not fully justified. There are typos and incomplete references. Figures and table captions could be clearer about settings and preprocessing.

Originality
High on the theoretical side: a novel combination of multi-homogeneous nonlinear PF theory with NN training to obtain global optimality guarantees under checkable conditions. The extension to two layers is noteworthy. The objective and architectural constraints are unusual in the NN literature.

Significance
Moderate to low for the NeurIPS audience in the current form. While the analysis is interesting and potentially influential for a niche (positive networks, spectral optimization, fixed-point methods), impact on mainstream deep learning is limited due to restrictive assumptions (nonnegativity, modified objective, polynomial activations with distinct exponents) and weak empirical evidence. Strengthening the practical relevance and demonstrating scalability are needed.

Pros
- Clear, rigorous fixed-point framework with linear convergence and a checkable spectral-radius condition.
- Novel application of multihomogeneous Perron–Frobenius and Thompson metric to NN training.
- Extension from one to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees.
- Simple algorithm; no step-size tuning.

Cons
- Strong and atypical modeling constraints: nonnegative data and weights; generalized polynomial activations with distinct exponents; fixed-norm constraints.
- Modified training objective includes a sum of outputs, changing the learning problem; no ablation or calibration analysis.
- Conservative conditions may hinder scaling; empirical validation limited to small datasets.
- Heavy hyperparameter search over architecture/norms contradicts the “no tuning” narrative.
- Missing comparisons to nonnegative/monotone baselines; incomplete experimental and preprocessing details; references have issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled justification and analysis of the “sum-of-outputs” term: calibration effects, relationship to standard cross-entropy (noting shift-invariance of softmax), and ablations without this term.
- Clarify preprocessing to ensure nonnegative inputs; explore domains where this assumption is natural (e.g., text counts, images with [0,1] scaling) and include stronger datasets in those domains.
- Analyze expressivity under nonnegativity and polynomial activations; justify the “distinct α” requirement and study sensitivity to α, pw, pu, ρw, ρu. Provide guidance for selecting these parameters beyond conservative bounds.
- Report computational complexity and wall-clock time vs strong baselines (full-batch methods), and scale experiments to medium-size tasks.
- Compare against nonnegative/monotone NN baselines and NMF-based classifiers.
- Tighten the bounds used to construct A, or empirically estimate effective Lipschitz constants to argue scalability.
- Improve clarity: define losses precisely, clean up references, and relocate key proofs/results from the supplement if space allows. Release code.

Recommendation
Reject in the current form. The theoretical contribution is interesting and original, but the modeling constraints and modified objective limit significance for NeurIPS, and the empirical validation is too weak to support claims of practical feasibility. With stronger experiments, clearer justification of the objective, and tighter conditions, this line of work could be a good fit in the future.
