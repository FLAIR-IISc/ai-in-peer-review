

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a "nonlinear spectral method" (NLSM) for training neural networks with global optimality guarantees under specific constraints. The authors claim to provide the first practically feasible algorithm for achieving global optimality in neural network training, requiring only nonnegative weights and a modified objective function. The method builds on Perron-Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices and claims linear convergence rates.

**Strengths**: The theoretical foundation using nonlinear spectral methods is well-motivated and the convergence guarantees are mathematically rigorous under their specific constraints. The paper clearly identifies the theoretical limitations of previous approaches [8, 11] and attempts to address them. The experimental section provides some validation on UCI datasets, which is a step beyond prior work that claimed global optimality but lacked empirical results.

**Weaknesses**: The paper's main weakness is the highly restrictive nonnegativity assumption on weights, which severely limits its applicability to real-world neural network problems. The authors claim nonnegativity "is true for all sorts of count data such as images, word frequencies," but this conflates input data with network weights - image pixels may be nonnegative, but neural network weights are not constrained to be nonnegative. This constraint makes the approach fundamentally incompatible with standard neural network architectures that require both positive and negative weights to model complex patterns.

The experimental validation is insufficient. The authors only test on low-dimensional UCI datasets (e.g., Iris, Cancer, Banknote), which are trivial problems where even simple methods can achieve high accuracy. The comparison with standard methods (ReLU networks, SVMs) is limited to one hidden layer, while modern deep learning relies on multiple layers. The paper shows NLSM achieves "competitive performance" but actually performs worse than SVMs in most cases (Table 1), and the gains over ReLU networks are marginal at best.

The paper's claim of "practical feasibility" is questionable. The method requires checking the spectral radius of a matrix (Theorem 1) to ensure convergence, which adds significant computational overhead. The authors acknowledge their bounds are "very conservative," suggesting the method may not be practically useful even when the convergence guarantee holds. The paper also fails to address how to handle cases where the spectral radius condition isn't satisfied.

The theoretical contribution appears to be a specific application of established Perron-Frobenius theory to a highly constrained neural network setting, rather than a novel methodological advance. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why their approach is superior to existing methods for the problems it can solve.

**Major concerns**: The paper makes several misleading claims. It states "up to our knowledge this is the first practically feasible algorithm to achieve global optimality for a non-trivial neural network model," but this ignores the fact that the "non-trivial" model is severely constrained by nonnegativity. The authors cite [8, 11] as having "challenging to apply in practice" methods, but don't provide evidence that their approach is actually more practical. The paper also claims to "achieve competitive performance," yet Table 1 shows their method is consistently outperformed by SVMs and often by ReLU networks.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A thorough discussion of why nonnegativity is acceptable for real-world applications
2. Experiments on larger, more challenging datasets (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR-10)
3. Comparison with standard optimization methods on standard benchmarks
4. Analysis of how the nonnegativity constraint impacts model expressiveness

**Conclusion**: While the paper presents a mathematically rigorous approach to a specific constrained neural network training problem, the severe restrictions on the model (nonnegative weights) and the limited experimental validation make the contribution too narrow to be of significant value to the broader deep learning community. The method is not practically useful for standard neural network applications, and the theoretical contribution appears to be a specialized application of established mathematics rather than a novel methodological advance. The paper's claims of "global optimality" and "practical feasibility" are overstated given the constraints.

The paper does not advance the state of the art in a meaningful way for the deep learning community. It would be more appropriate for a more specialized journal focusing on matrix theory or constrained optimization rather than NIPS. I recommend rejection.
