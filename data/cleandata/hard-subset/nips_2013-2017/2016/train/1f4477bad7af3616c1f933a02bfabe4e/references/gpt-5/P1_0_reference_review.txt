Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of a restricted class of neural networks: generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative inputs. The hidden units compute powered linear responses (∑m u_lm x_m)^{α_l} with exponents α_l≥1, and each α_l must be distinct to avoid degeneracy. The training objective is nonstandard: maximize the average negative cross-entropy plus the sum of all class outputs, with an additional small positive linear term in the parameters to ensure strict positivity of gradients. The core algorithm is a fixed-point iteration GΦ based on normalized ψ_{p′} of the gradients under L_p constraints on weights. Using multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and the Thompson metric, the authors derive sufficient conditions—expressed as the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A depending only on architecture and norm parameters—for existence/uniqueness of a global maximizer and linear convergence from any positive initialization. They provide results for one hidden layer (K+1 dimensional A) and extend to two hidden layers (K+2 dimensional A). Experiments on small UCI datasets show that the method is competitive with ReLU networks trained by SGD and somewhat worse than RBF-SVM, while converging very rapidly.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against recent theory guaranteeing global optimality for special network classes, notably Haeffele & Vidal’s structured factorization view and Janzamin et al.’s tensor methods. Unlike those, NLSM does not rely on score functions or density estimation and gives a practical fixed-point procedure with a checkable contraction condition. The approach is conceptually related to classical Perron–Frobenius theory for nonnegative matrices/tensors and to multiplicative updates in NMF; the authors leverage multi-homogeneous maps and contraction in the Thompson metric. The paper also acknowledges NP-hardness for general networks and aims for a tractable, constrained setting.

Strengths
- Novel theory: A clear application of nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory to neural network training, with an explicit fixed-point map and linear-rate convergence under an easily checkable spectral radius condition.
- Practicality of the condition: The contraction check reduces to computing ρ(A) of a small matrix determined by architecture and norms; no iterative tuning (e.g., learning rates) is required.
- Extension to two hidden layers: The framework scales beyond single-hidden-layer networks, with explicit constants and conditions.
- Empirical feasibility: Proof-of-concept experiments demonstrate nontrivial decision boundaries, competitive accuracy on several UCI datasets, and much faster convergence than SGD on the modified objective.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Scope of optimality: “Global optimality” holds for a heavily modified objective (negative cross-entropy plus sum of outputs and a small positive linear parameter term), not the standard cross-entropy. The paper should more clearly analyze how adding the sum of outputs changes the classifier learned (e.g., invariances with softmax/logit shifts) and its statistical implications.
- Strong modeling constraints: Nonnegative data and weights, powered activations with distinct exponents per unit, and norm normalizations may substantially limit expressivity compared to standard architectures.
- Conservativeness and practicality of conditions: The bounds leading to ρ(A)<1 are acknowledged as conservative; the paper provides no empirical study of how often ρ(A)<1 holds for useful architectures on realistic datasets, nor an exploration of tightness.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: Only small, low-dimensional datasets; no ablations isolating the effect of the “sum of outputs” term; no comparison to closely related nonnegative/tensor/NMF-like models; limited analysis of sensitivity to α, (p_u, p_w), and radius parameters. The “competitive with ReLU” claim is weak given ReLU’s small-scale tuning and strong constraints for NLSM.

Clarity
- Several presentation issues: The definition of the loss L is referenced but not explicitly written; the “arbitrarily small ε>0” multiplying the linear parameter sum is used in the text but missing in the displayed objective; notation overloading (α both as vector and scalar); occasional typos and missing reference details; some claims (e.g., rank-one argument for distinct α’s) deserve a more careful, self-contained justification.

Originality
- The combination of multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius fixed-point theory with neural network training and an explicit contraction analysis is original and interesting. However, the relationship to known monotone/positive networks, NMF multiplicative updates, and positive polynomial networks could be better contextualized.

Significance
- The theoretical contribution may stimulate further work on globally convergent training under monotonicity/positivity constraints. That said, the applicability is constrained, and empirical evidence is too limited to claim practical impact beyond a proof-of-concept.

Detailed comments and questions for authors
- Clarify the objective: Please provide the precise loss definition and include the ε term in the displayed objective. Discuss how adding ∑_r f_r(x) interacts with softmax cross-entropy (e.g., invariance to adding constants to logits), whether it biases toward uniformly high logits, and how the norm constraints prevent trivial blow-up.
- Choice of α: The argument that uniqueness plus permutation invariance forces rank-one seems too terse. Could you formalize the claim or provide a counterexample to show why distinct exponents are necessary for nontrivial solutions?
- Practicality of ρ(A)<1: Report empirical values of ρ(A) (and the eigenvector γ) for the architectures used; show how often random parameter draws meet the condition, and whether the tighter A in Theorem 4 materially improves feasibility over Theorem 1.
- Tightness: Can you provide per-instance Lipschitz estimates or adaptive bounds leading to less conservative A during training?
- Expressivity: Include synthetic experiments comparing decision boundaries and accuracy to ReLU/NMF models under nonnegativity constraints. How sensitive is performance to α’s magnitudes and spacing?
- Complexity: Provide per-iteration complexity and memory versus SGD; discuss scalability to higher-dimensional data.
- Extensions: Could the method handle signed inputs via simple shifts or feature splits? Are there variants that relax nonnegativity of weights while preserving contraction?
- Reproducibility: Please release code and exact hyperparameters; clarify initialization, stopping, and how R in Theorem 3 is computed in practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, rigorous application of nonlinear spectral theory yielding a simple fixed-point algorithm with linear convergence.
- Clear, checkable global optimality condition dependent only on architecture/norms.
- First to demonstrate practical globally convergent training with empirical results, albeit limited.

Cons for acceptance
- Objective differs from standard practice; unclear statistical implications.
- Strong constraints (nonnegativity, distinct exponents) and conservative conditions may limit practicality and expressivity.
- Limited and small-scale experiments; insufficient ablations and comparisons.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting and potentially impactful within monotone/positive networks, but the empirical validation and clarity around the modified objective and practicality need strengthening for NeurIPS. Confidence: Medium.
