This paper presents a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks, aiming for global optimality using a nonlinear spectral method. The core idea is to reformulate the training problem into a maximization problem solvable via iterative updates based on the spectral radius of a matrix derived from network parameters. The authors claim their method is significantly more efficient and guarantees global optimality under specific conditions (non-negativity of weights) compared to existing approaches like those by Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015). They demonstrate the feasibility of the approach with theoretical analysis and limited experiments on UCI datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of utilizing nonlinear spectral methods to achieve global optimality in neural network training is genuinely novel. It diverges from the standard gradient-based optimization techniques and offers a potentially attractive alternative under specific constraints.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation with a clear convergence theorem (Theorem 1) guaranteeing global optimality under relatively mild conditions. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory provides a solid mathematical basis.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The authors clearly define the problem, including the function class and optimization objective, making it accessible to the reader. The extension to two hidden layers is a welcome addition.
*   **Linear Convergence:** The reported linear convergence rate is a significant advantage over stochastic gradient descent-based methods.
*   **Detailed Proof Strategy:** The paper outlines a comprehensive proof strategy, albeit with some details deferred to the supplementary material, which is acceptable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Assumptions:** The requirement of non-negativity on weights is a significant constraint and limits the applicability of the method to scenarios where this assumption is valid or can be reasonably enforced.  The paper acknowledges this, but the practical implications are substantial.
*   **Conservatism of Bounds:** The authors admit that the bounds derived for ensuring ρ(A) < 1 are conservative, potentially restricting the applicability of the method. The discussion of Equation (4) suggests the need for tighter bounds.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets. While they demonstrate the feasibility of the approach, it is unclear how well it will scale to more complex, high-dimensional problems commonly encountered in deep learning. The comparison with ReLU networks is also limited by their different constraints (non-negativity).
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the authors claim there are no free parameters, determining appropriate values for ρw, ρu, and α can be non-trivial and likely requires careful tuning.
*   **Clarity of Notation:** Some notation, especially in Section 3 related to the metric spaces and Lipschitz constants, could be improved for clarity. Defining terms like "B++" more explicitly earlier in the paper would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison against Batch-SGD focuses primarily on convergence speed. A more comprehensive comparison of generalization performance (e.g., test accuracy) with various learning rates would strengthen the analysis.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous mathematical treatment of the problem. The proofs appear logically structured and well-motivated. However, the conservatism of the derived bounds and the limited experimental validation raise concerns about the practical applicability and scalability of the method.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but some sections (particularly Section 3) could benefit from further clarification. The mathematical notation is mostly standard, but could be more consistently used and explained.

**Originality:** The approach is highly original and represents a significant departure from conventional neural network training methods. The connection to nonlinear spectral theory and Perron-Frobenius theory is novel and insightful.

**Significance:**  If the practical limitations associated with the non-negativity constraint and conservative bounds can be addressed, this method has the potential to be a significant contribution to the field. The guarantee of global optimality is highly desirable, and the linear convergence rate could offer substantial speedups over existing algorithms.  However, the current results suggest that the method may be more suited for specific applications where the non-negativity constraint is naturally satisfied.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to global optimization of neural networks.
*   Clear convergence guarantees and a linear convergence rate.
*   Well-structured and logically presented proof strategy.
*   Provides a valuable contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Strong and limiting assumption of non-negativity on weights.
*   Conservative bounds on the spectral radius, potentially restricting applicability.
*   Limited experimental validation on low-dimensional datasets.
*   Potential difficulty in parameter tuning (choosing α, ρw, ρu).



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the theoretical contribution is strong and the approach is novel, the significant constraints and limited experimental validation raise concerns about its practical impact. I believe the paper warrants acceptance due to its originality and potential, but the authors should address the limitations in future work. Specifically, exploring methods to relax the non-negativity constraint and deriving tighter bounds are crucial steps towards broader applicability.
