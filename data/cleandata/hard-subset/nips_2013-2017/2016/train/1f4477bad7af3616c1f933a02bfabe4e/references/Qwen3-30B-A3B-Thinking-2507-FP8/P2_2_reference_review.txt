

# Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to training neural networks with global optimality guarantees using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors propose a novel algorithm that requires nonnegative weights and a modified objective function (adding the negative total sum of outputs), proving that under certain conditions (specifically, when the spectral radius of a derived matrix A is less than 1), their method converges linearly to the global optimum. The paper extends this approach to networks with one and two hidden layers, providing explicit conditions for convergence in each case.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and presents a rigorous theoretical proof of global optimality. The authors successfully leverage nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory to establish their convergence guarantees, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The derivation of matrix A and the conditions for its spectral radius being less than 1 is well-executed, with careful attention to the mathematical details. The authors provide explicit bounds for the matrix A and demonstrate how to check the convergence condition without running the algorithm.

However, there are some concerns about the completeness of the theoretical analysis. The paper doesn't thoroughly address how to select the parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) for optimal performance in practice, which is crucial for any machine learning method. The requirement of nonnegative weights is a strong constraint that significantly limits practical applicability, yet the paper doesn't sufficiently explore the trade-offs between this constraint and the benefits of global optimality.

The experimental results on UCI datasets are limited in scope. While the method achieves competitive performance with other approaches, the authors don't compare against methods that also enforce nonnegative weights (if such methods exist), making it difficult to assess the relative advantage of their approach. The paper also doesn't explore the performance on larger, more challenging datasets that would better demonstrate the method's practical utility.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to experiments. The main theorem (Theorem 1) is clearly stated with precise conditions for convergence. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout the paper.

The motivation for the modified objective function (adding the sum of outputs) could be better explained. The authors briefly mention that this is necessary to ensure the gradient is strictly positive, but a more detailed justification would strengthen the paper. The relationship between the spectral radius condition and practical implementation could also be clarified.

## Originality

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network training. The use of nonlinear spectral methods to achieve global optimality is novel and builds on Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, which is not commonly applied in deep learning. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from previous work (e.g., Janzamin et al. [11] and Haeffele and Vidal [8]) by highlighting the practical feasibility of their algorithm.

The constraint of nonnegative weights is a strong assumption, but the authors provide a clear theoretical justification for it. They also note that the method can be extended to networks with two hidden layers, which adds to the novelty of their approach. The paper effectively situates itself within the broader literature on global optimality in neural networks.

## Significance

The significance of this work is somewhat limited by the strong constraint of nonnegative weights. While the paper provides a theoretically sound method with global optimality guarantees, it's unclear how this would be useful in most real-world applications where negative weights are essential for modeling complex functions. The experimental results on UCI datasets are promising but not particularly groundbreaking, as the method achieves competitive performance with other approaches without clearly demonstrating why it's better suited for the problems it's addressing.

That said, the paper does fill an important theoretical gap by providing a globally optimal algorithm that is actually practical to implement (unlike previous work that required unknown data distributions). The absence of free parameters (like learning rates) is a significant advantage over stochastic gradient descent, and the linear convergence rate is a notable theoretical contribution.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically sound contribution to the field of neural network training with global optimality guarantees. While the strong constraint of nonnegative weights limits practical applicability, the method is novel, well-justified, and has a clear convergence proof.

**Pro:**
- Rigorous theoretical proof of global optimality with linear convergence rate
- Practical implementation (unlike previous work that required unknown data distributions)
- Clear exposition of the theoretical framework
- Novel application of nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory to neural network training

**Con:**
- Strong constraint of nonnegative weights limits practical applicability
- Limited experimental validation on real-world, high-dimensional datasets
- Lack of clear guidance on parameter selection for optimal performance
- Modified objective function lacks thorough justification

I believe this paper would be a good fit for NIPS as it makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to the field. The main weakness is the practical limitation of the nonnegative weight constraint, which should be more thoroughly discussed in the paper. With minor revisions to address these limitations, this paper would be a strong contribution to the literature.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
