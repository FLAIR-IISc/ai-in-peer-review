This paper presents a globally optimal training algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks based on nonlinear spectral methods. The core idea is to formulate the training problem as a maximization problem on a convex cone, leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory to guarantee global optimality under certain conditions. While the concept of achieving global optimality for neural network training is highly appealing and represents a significant theoretical advance, the paper is riddled with a concerning number of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and overly conservative bounds, ultimately hindering its impact and practical usability.

The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the challenges of training deep neural networks. However, it relies heavily on referencing other works without fully synthesizing their contributions. The justification for the modified objective function – the addition of the sum of outputs – feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks deeper intuition beyond the need for a strictly positive gradient.

The mathematical presentation is generally sound, but suffers from a lack of conciseness and clarity. Definitions are often introduced without sufficient motivation, and the notation, while mathematically correct, is dense and can be difficult to follow. For example, the introduction of ψp and its application feels abrupt. The multiple definitions of sets (S+, S++, V+, V++, B+, B++) seem unnecessary and contribute to cognitive load. The justification for maximizing instead of minimizing the loss, while explained, feels somewhat weak.

A major weakness lies in the conservative nature of the derived bounds. The authors themselves acknowledge this ("As our bounds for the matrix A are very conservative…"), but underestimate the extent of its impact. The conditions required for global optimality (specifically, ensuring ρ(A) < 1) appear restrictive, particularly considering the dependence on the number of hidden units and the data scale (ρx). The derived bounds (4) and (9) for ensuring ρ(A) < 1 are likely overly pessimistic and would severely limit the practical applicability of the method. The empirical evidence provided (Section 5) is insufficient to demonstrate the practical viability of the approach. The experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets and lack a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods beyond a cursory table. The statement that “our nonlinear spectral methods achieve slightly worse performance than kernel SVM” is a significant drawback, especially considering the theoretical guarantee of global optimality. The observation that ReLU networks can achieve zero test error underscores the trade-off between optimality guarantees and empirical performance. The discussion on the effect of α being large feels anecdotal and lacks strong justification.

The two-hidden layer extension in Section 4.1 feels rushed and less thoroughly developed than the single-hidden layer case. The notation becomes even more cumbersome, and the implications of the added complexity are not fully explored.

Several minor issues detract from the overall polish of the paper:

*   Throughout, there are instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors.
*   The figure caption in the introduction references "Figure 1" but no figure is included.
*   The citation style is inconsistent.
*   The concluding remarks regarding "large changes in the parameter space" are vague and lack quantitative support.
*   The phrase “up to our knowledge” appears several times and feels unnecessary.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The theoretical result of providing a globally optimal training algorithm for a specific class of neural networks is novel and potentially significant.
*   The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory is interesting and provides a unique perspective on neural network training.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The overly conservative bounds significantly limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The empirical evaluation is weak and does not demonstrate competitive performance.
*   The paper suffers from numerous minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of clarity.
*   The theoretical framework appears overly complex for the demonstrated benefits.
*   The lack of a well-defined and sufficiently compelling application domain.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the theoretical novelty of the work, I recommend **rejection**. While the idea of globally optimal training is attractive, the practical limitations imposed by the conservative bounds and the lack of compelling empirical results outweigh the theoretical contributions. The numerous minor flaws further detract from the paper's quality. A significant overhaul, addressing the issues outlined above, is required before this work could be considered for publication at NIPS. Specifically, the authors need to focus on tightening the bounds, demonstrating competitive performance on more challenging datasets, and improving the clarity and conciseness of the presentation. The current manuscript feels more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and impactful contribution to the field.
