

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel "nonlinear spectral method" for training generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights. The authors claim to provide the first practically feasible algorithm that achieves global optimality for neural networks, with linear convergence guarantees under specific conditions. The approach requires nonnegativity constraints on weights and a modified objective function, and the convergence guarantee depends on the spectral radius of a small matrix that can be checked without running the algorithm.

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by connecting neural network training to nonlinear eigenvalue problems and Perron-Frobenius theory. The proof framework using the Banach fixed point theorem is well-structured and provides a clear convergence analysis. The experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate that the method can achieve competitive performance, which is notable given the lack of such results in prior global optimality work.

## Weaknesses

Despite the interesting theoretical approach, the paper contains numerous technical and presentation issues that significantly undermine its quality:

1. **Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous formatting errors, including inconsistent use of mathematical notation (e.g., "fr(x) = fr(w, u)(x)" vs. "f(w, u)(x)"), inconsistent capitalization ("Nonlinear Spectral Method" vs. "nonlinear spectral method"), and a typographical error in the conference name ("th Conference" instead of "The Conference").

2. **Theoretical Gaps**: The paper claims "for every (w0, u0) ∈ S++, there exists R > 0" for the convergence bound, but fails to explain how to compute R, despite claiming it "can be explicitly computed" (Theorem 3). The paper also incorrectly states that "the matrix A in Theorem 1 has a smaller spectral radius than that of Theorem 1" - this is a circular reference.

3. **Misleading Claims**: The paper claims "to our knowledge this is the first practically feasible algorithm to achieve global optimality for a non-trivial neural network model" without adequately addressing the practical limitations of their approach (nonnegative weights constraint) compared to prior work [8, 11]. The authors acknowledge their constraint is strong but don't sufficiently justify why this is acceptable for practical applications.

4. **Experimental Limitations**: The experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, and the paper doesn't provide a convincing argument for why these results are representative of the method's practical utility. The comparison with ReLU networks is flawed since ReLU networks allow negative weights while the proposed method does not, which is a fundamental architectural difference.

5. **Theoretical Justification Gaps**: The explanation for requiring "αi ≠ αj for every i, j" to avoid trivial classifiers is incomplete. The authors claim that "for any permutation matrix P, Aσ(Bx) = APTPσ(Bx) = APTσ(PBx)" implies "A = APT and B = PB for all permutation matrices P," but this inference is not mathematically sound.

6. **Reference Issues**: The paper cites reference [7] as "The Perron-Frobenius Theory for Multi-Homogeneous Maps" but this reference is not listed in the bibliography. The paper also contains grammatical errors such as "NP hard" (should be "NP-hard").

## Recommendation

While the theoretical framework is interesting, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, misleading claims, and experimental limitations significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The paper fails to adequately address the practical implications of the nonnegativity constraint, and the theoretical claims are not fully supported by the provided analysis.

The paper's most significant flaw is its failure to properly contextualize its approach relative to prior work on global optimality in neural networks. The authors cite [8, 11] as having "challenging to apply in practice" results but don't clearly demonstrate why their approach is substantially more practical, especially given the strong nonnegativity constraint.

For a NIPS paper, which should be of high technical quality and clear presentation, this paper falls short in multiple critical aspects. The minor but pervasive technical errors and theoretical gaps are particularly concerning for a paper claiming to provide rigorous global optimality guarantees.

I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from significant revision to address the theoretical gaps, improve presentation consistency, provide a more balanced comparison with prior work, and clarify the practical implications of the nonnegativity constraint.
