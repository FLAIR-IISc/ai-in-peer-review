This paper presents a globally optimal training algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks using a nonlinear spectral method. The authors address a significant challenge in deep learning – the non-convexity of the optimization landscape – by proposing a method that guarantees convergence to a global optimum under certain conditions, namely non-negativity of weights and a modified objective function. The work builds on recent advances in globally optimal training of neural networks [8, 11] but aims to overcome practical limitations of those approaches, particularly the need to estimate data-generating measures or check non-degeneracy conditions.

The core idea is appealing: leveraging the Perron-Frobenius theory to find a globally optimal solution. The authors provide a theoretical framework with a convergence guarantee expressed in terms of the spectral radius of a small matrix. They also present initial experimental results on UCI datasets, demonstrating competitive performance compared to standard methods like SVM and ReLU networks. The inclusion of a detailed proof strategy and a discussion on the dependence of the spectral radius on model parameters strengthens the paper. The extension to two hidden layers, while briefly discussed, is a positive step.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the imposed constraint of non-negativity on weights is quite strong and limits the expressiveness of the model. While the authors attempt to justify this choice through the theoretical framework, a more thorough discussion of the impact on practical applications is needed.  The claim of achieving “competitive performance” on UCI datasets needs further scrutiny. The table shows mixed results; the performance is not consistently better than existing methods and sometimes worse, particularly concerning the ReLU networks. The hyperparameter tuning strategy for the baselines (SVM and ReLU) is also briefly mentioned but lacks detail. It is unclear how carefully these baselines were optimized for fair comparison.

Secondly, the theoretical analysis, while mathematically sound, is quite involved. While the authors provide a high-level overview, the complexity might be a barrier for readers not deeply familiar with spectral theory and nonlinear eigenvalue problems. The bounds on the spectral radius, while theoretically useful, may be overly conservative, as acknowledged by the authors.  A more practical characterization of these bounds and guidelines for choosing model parameters would significantly improve the paper’s utility. 

Thirdly, the experiments are limited in scope. The datasets are low-dimensional, and the results are not particularly compelling. The comparison to SGD is only briefly presented in a figure, without detailed analysis of the convergence speed and other relevant metrics. A more extensive experimental evaluation, including higher-dimensional datasets and comparisons to more sophisticated optimization algorithms, is crucial to validate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The claim of being "the first practically feasible algorithm" is a strong one and requires much stronger empirical evidence.

Finally, the writing style, while generally clear, occasionally suffers from dense mathematical notation and lengthy paragraphs.  Streamlining the presentation and providing more intuitive explanations would enhance readability.  For example, the explanation of the added term in the objective function feels somewhat tacked on and could benefit from further motivation.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning – global optimization.
*   Provides a theoretical guarantee of convergence to a global optimum under specific conditions.
*   Presents a novel approach based on nonlinear spectral methods and Perron-Frobenius theory.
*   Demonstrates initial experimental results on UCI datasets, showing competitive performance.
*   Includes a detailed proof sketch and analysis of the theoretical framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The non-negativity constraint significantly limits model expressiveness.
*   Experimental results are not compelling and lack sufficient scope and detail.
*   Theoretical analysis is complex and potentially difficult to apply in practice.
*   The claim of being the "first practically feasible" algorithm is not adequately supported.
*   Writing style could be improved for clarity and readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, its practical limitations and the lack of compelling experimental evidence outweigh its strengths. The paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, but it requires significant revisions and further experimentation to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should focus on exploring ways to relax the non-negativity constraint, conducting more extensive experiments on diverse datasets, and providing more practical guidelines for parameter selection. A more accessible and streamlined presentation of the theoretical framework would also be beneficial.
