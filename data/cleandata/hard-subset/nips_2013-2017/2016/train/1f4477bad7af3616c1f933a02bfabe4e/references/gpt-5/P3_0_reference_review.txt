Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a restricted class of neural networks to global optimality with linear convergence. The model is a generalized polynomial network with one or two hidden layers, nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights, and activations of the form (u^T x)^α with α ≥ 1 (distinct across hidden units). Training solves a modified maximization objective: negative cross-entropy plus a positive sum over outputs, along with tiny positive linear terms in the weights to ensure strictly positive gradients on the cone. The algorithm is a fixed-point iteration GΦ that normalizes the (componentwise) ψp′ of the gradients of Φ, akin to a power method on the positive orthant. Under a checkable condition—spectral radius ρ(A) < 1 of a (K+1)×(K+1) nonnegative matrix A that depends on network hyperparameters and a bound on data norms—the mapping is a contraction in the Thompson metric, guaranteeing a unique global maximizer in the interior (S++) and linear convergence rate ρ(A). The approach is extended to two hidden layers with analogous guarantees (matrix size K+2). Proofs leverage multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction arguments. Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy with simple ReLU baselines and slightly worse performance than RBF-SVM; the method converges rapidly.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to: (i) global optimality via structured factorization (Haeffele & Vidal [8]) and tensor methods (Janzamin et al. [11])—both provide guarantees under restrictive assumptions; (ii) polynomial networks [15]; and (iii) nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory on cones [14], including the Thompson metric. The novelty here is a practical fixed-point procedure with a small, pre-checkable spectral-radius condition and linear convergence for a nontrivial (albeit restricted) NN class.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical framework: a concrete fixed-point map, explicit contraction condition, and linear convergence. The reliance on a small spectral-radius check is appealing and actionable.
- Solid use of nonlinear PF theory and the Thompson metric for multi-homogeneous maps; the theory is internally consistent and extends to two hidden layers.
- Algorithm has no step-size tuning and is simple to implement (full-gradient plus normalization).
- Proof-of-concept experiments demonstrate the method can learn nontrivial decision boundaries and converge quickly in practice.

Major concerns and weaknesses
Quality/technical
- Strong modeling restrictions: nonnegative inputs and weights; fixed p-norm constraints on each layer; nonstandard activations with per-unit α ≥ 1; and a modified objective that adds the sum over outputs plus ε-weight sums. The global optimality is for this modified problem, not standard cross-entropy training. The added ∑r fr(x) term is not softmax-translation invariant and biases the logits upward subject to norm constraints; its effect on the classifier is under-discussed.
- The ρ(A) < 1 condition depends on conservative bounds (and on data scaling via ρx), and the paper suggests one can always choose large p to shrink (p′−1) and force ρ(A) < 1. This raises concerns about practicality and sensitivity: large p changes the geometry of ψp′ and may degrade optimization behavior or expressivity; guidance is limited.
- The claim that equal α across hidden units forces trivial (rank-one) solutions due to uniqueness/permutation symmetry is not rigorously established (the provided argument is heuristic). A formal statement, conditions, or a counterexample would be helpful.
- Complexity and scalability are not analyzed: each iteration appears to require full-gradient computation; wall-clock comparisons versus competitive full-batch methods are absent; feasibility on higher-dimensional data is unclear (the authors note the spectral-radius bound degrades with width).

Clarity
- The manuscript has numerous typos and inconsistencies that hinder readability: incomplete or malformed references; missing explicit definition of the loss L; inconsistent definitions of ρx (ℓ1 vs ℓp′ norms); mis-indexing (e.g., αi ≥ 1 with i ∈ [K] in the intro); figure and table references appear garbled. Some proofs are deferred, but enough detail is present to follow the main line.
- The effect of the additive ∑r fr term on the decision rule (and whether it changes argmax predictions under softmax-like losses) should be discussed explicitly.

Originality
- The combination of multi-homogeneous PF-based contraction analysis with a practical fixed-point algorithm and a simple spectral-radius check for global convergence appears novel for neural network training (even under restrictions). Relative to [8, 11], the method is arguably more “plug-and-check” and less reliant on data generative assumptions or spectral tensor decompositions.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: provides a rare, implementable global-optimality guarantee for a nontrivial NN class with linear convergence and a verifiable precondition.
- Practical impact is currently limited: restrictions (nonnegativity, fixed norms, special activations, modified objective) and conservative conditions curtail applicability; empirical results are on small datasets and underperform kernel SVMs. The work is a promising step, but more evidence is needed to influence practice.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Precisely define the loss L (softmax cross-entropy?), and analyze the impact of the +∑r fr term on the decision boundaries. Is there a way to choose a compensating term that preserves softmax invariances?
- Provide runtime/complexity per iteration and wall-clock comparisons versus full-batch gradient methods. Report empirical convergence factors and relate them to ρ(A).
- Report how often randomly sampled architectures satisfy ρ(A) < 1, and whether the best-performing models are filtered out by the condition. Include sensitivity to data scaling and recommended preprocessing.
- Formalize the “distinct α to avoid triviality” claim with a theorem or counterexample.
- Discuss possible routes to relax nonnegativity (e.g., signed inputs via x = x+ − x−) or to handle standard activations. Any prospects for partial negativity or alternative cones?
- Clean up notation, references, and figures; ensure consistency of ρx and indices.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant and rigorous theory with a simple, verifiable condition and linear-rate convergence.
- Novel and concrete link between PF theory and NN training with an implementable algorithm.
- First practical global-optimality result that goes beyond toy proofs and includes real (albeit small) datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy modeling restrictions and training a modified objective limit practical relevance.
- Experimental evaluation is weak (small datasets, below SVM performance) and lacks scalability/complexity analysis.
- Clarity issues and missing details (loss definition, rigorous symmetry argument) hinder assessment.

Overall
This is an interesting and thoughtful theoretical contribution, but the practical scope and empirical evidence are limited, and the paper needs polishing. I lean toward a weak reject, with encouragement to strengthen experiments, clarify the impact of the modified objective, formalize key claims, and improve presentation.
