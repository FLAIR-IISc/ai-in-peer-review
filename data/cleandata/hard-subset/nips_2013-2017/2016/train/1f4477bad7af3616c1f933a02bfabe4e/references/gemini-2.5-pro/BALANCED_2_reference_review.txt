### Summary

This paper presents a novel "nonlinear spectral method" for training a specific class of neural networks, termed Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs), to a global optimum. The method is applicable to networks with non-negative weights and non-negative input data, using generalized polynomial activation functions of the form $x^\alpha$ for $\alpha \ge 1$. The authors formulate a modified optimization problem that involves maximizing a sum of the negative cross-entropy loss and the total network output. The core theoretical contribution is to show that an iterative algorithm based on this objective function is a contraction mapping with respect to the Thompson metric. This key result, drawing from the Perron-Frobenius theory for nonlinear maps, guarantees convergence to a unique global optimum at a linear rate. A practical strength of the method is that the condition for guaranteed convergence—that the spectral radius of a small, well-defined matrix is less than one—can be verified *a priori* based on the network's architecture. The authors extend their framework from one to two hidden layers and present experimental results on several UCI datasets, demonstrating competitive performance and faster convergence compared to SGD on their objective. This work is related to a recent line of research on globally optimal training of neural networks (e.g., Janzamin et al., 2015; Haeffele and Vidal, 2017), but distinguishes itself by proposing a more direct and computationally feasible algorithm that does not require estimating the data distribution or performing tensor decompositions.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant and Novel Contribution:** The paper addresses the fundamental challenge of non-convex optimization in neural networks. Providing a practical algorithm with a provable guarantee of convergence to a unique global optimum is a highly significant achievement. The connection established between neural network training and the theory of nonlinear Perron-Frobenius operators via the Thompson metric is original and elegant.

2.  **Rigorous Theoretical Foundation:** The work is technically sound and well-supported by a sophisticated mathematical framework. The authors clearly lay out their proof strategy, proceeding from the optimization problem to the application of the Banach fixed-point theorem in a complete metric space. The main results (Theorems 1 and 3) are powerful, providing not only a guarantee of optimality but also a linear convergence rate.

3.  **Practical Guarantees and Simplicity:** A major strength is the *a priori* check for convergence. The ability to compute the spectral radius of a small matrix `A` to determine if global optimality is guaranteed for a given architecture is a significant practical advantage. Furthermore, the resulting algorithm is iterative and simple, avoiding the need to tune learning rates, which is a common challenge in training neural networks with SGD.

4.  **First Empirical Demonstration:** Unlike some prior theoretical works on this topic, the authors provide empirical results on real-world datasets. While presented as a "proof of concept," these experiments are crucial as they demonstrate that the proposed model, despite its constraints, can learn non-trivial decision boundaries and achieve competitive classification accuracy.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Restrictive Model Assumptions:** The primary weakness of the work lies in its strong and non-standard assumptions, which significantly limit its general applicability.
    *   **Non-negativity of Data and Weights:** The requirement for both input data and network weights to be non-negative is a major constraint. This excludes a vast range of common machine learning tasks and preprocessing techniques (e.g., data standardization).
    *   **Modified Objective Function:** The objective function is modified by adding the sum of all network outputs. This term is included for technical reasons—to ensure the gradient remains in the positive cone—rather than being motivated by a learning principle. The impact of this term on the properties of the learned classifier is not well-explored. It is unclear if minimizing loss while maximizing total output is always desirable.
    *   **Specialized Activations:** The use of generalized polynomial activations with the constraint that each exponent $\alpha_l$ must be distinct is also restrictive compared to standard activations like ReLU or sigmoid.

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The condition for convergence, $\rho(A) < 1$, appears to become harder to satisfy as the network size (number of hidden units $n_1$), data norm, and other architectural parameters increase. The authors explicitly state that $\rho(A)$ is increasing with respect to these parameters. This raises serious doubts about the method's scalability to the larger and deeper networks commonly used in modern deep learning. The experiments are confined to small, low-dimensional UCI datasets, which does not allay these concerns.

3.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the inclusion of experiments is a strength, the evaluation could be more comprehensive. The performance, while competitive, does not consistently outperform a standard baseline like an RBF-kernel SVM. It would be beneficial to understand if this is an inherent limitation of the constrained model class or a matter of hyperparameter tuning for the proposed NLSM model.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Discussing the Limitations:** The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of its limitations in the main text. Addressing the non-negativity constraint and its implications for practical use would provide important context for the reader. It would also be valuable to speculate on potential avenues for relaxing these assumptions in future work (e.g., by splitting features into positive and negative parts or by developing a similar theory for different convex cones).

2.  **Intuition for the Objective Function:** Providing more intuition behind the modified objective function would be helpful. For example, an analysis of what kind of solutions are encouraged by maximizing the sum of outputs alongside minimizing the loss would clarify the inductive bias of the model. An ablation study showing the effect of this term (e.g., by weighting it with a hyperparameter) could also be insightful.

3.  **Analysis of Scalability:** A more detailed theoretical or empirical analysis of how the spectral radius $\rho(A)$ scales with key parameters (e.g., number of hidden units, data dimension) would be a valuable addition. The authors note their bounds are likely not tight; demonstrating this with an example where the condition $\rho(A)<1$ is violated but the algorithm still converges would motivate further research into tightening these bounds.

4.  **Broader Experimental Context:** To better assess scalability, applying the method to a slightly larger dataset that still fits the non-negativity constraint (e.g., MNIST) would be very informative.

### Decision

This paper introduces a novel and theoretically deep approach to the globally optimal training of a constrained class of neural networks. The connection to nonlinear spectral theory is a significant and elegant contribution to the field. However, the practical utility of the proposed method is currently hampered by strong assumptions on the data, weights, and objective function, as well as significant concerns about scalability.

Despite these limitations, the novelty and technical quality of the theoretical results are high. The work opens a new and promising research direction. Therefore, I believe the contribution is valuable to the NeurIPS community.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept.
