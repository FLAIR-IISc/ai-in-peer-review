## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel algorithm for globally optimally training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) – with a single hidden layer. The core contribution lies in formulating the training problem as a maximization problem amenable to analysis using Perron-Frobenius theory, leveraging non-negativity constraints on network weights. The authors demonstrate theoretical guarantees for convergence to a global optimum under certain conditions (related to the spectral radius of a small matrix) and showcase preliminary experimental results on UCI datasets. The work attempts to bridge the gap between recent theoretical advances in globally optimal neural network training and practical applicability, a point specifically acknowledged as a limitation in prior work (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.).  The approach extends to networks with two hidden layers.

**Strengths:**

* **Global Optimality Guarantee:** The most significant strength is the provision of a *provably* globally optimal training algorithm for a non-trivial neural network architecture.  This is a rare and valuable contribution in the field of deep learning, where optimization is notoriously challenging.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is grounded in solid mathematical theory – Perron-Frobenius theory, spectral analysis, and fixed-point theory. The authors clearly articulate the connection between these concepts and the training algorithm.
* **Clear Algorithm:** The proposed nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) is clearly defined and appears relatively straightforward to implement, differing significantly from tensor-based approaches that require estimation of data generating measures.  The algorithm's independence from hyperparameter tuning (like learning rates) is also a notable advantage.
* **Extension to Multiple Layers:** Demonstrating the applicability of the method to two hidden layers expands the practical relevance of the work.
* **Novel Objective Function:** The modified objective function, incorporating a regularization term based on network outputs, is a clever way to facilitate the application of Perron-Frobenius theory.
* **Detailed Proof Strategy:** The paper provides a detailed outline of the proof strategy, including important lemmas and theorems. The inclusion of details in the supplementary material is a good practice.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Strong Non-Negativity Constraint:** The requirement of non-negative weights is a significant limitation.  While the authors acknowledge this, the impact on expressiveness (despite Figure 1) and applicability to real-world datasets is substantial. Most real-world data and network architectures do not naturally adhere to this constraint. It's unclear how easily this constraint can be relaxed or how much performance is sacrificed by imposing it.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:**  The authors acknowledge that the bounds on the spectral radius (ρ(A)) are likely conservative. This suggests that the algorithm may converge faster than predicted by the theory, but the tightest achievable bounds remain an open question.  This limits the practical usability in the short term.
* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are limited to relatively small UCI datasets. While competitive, they do not demonstrate performance on more complex, high-dimensional problems where the benefits of global optimality might be more pronounced. The comparison to ReLU networks is somewhat unfair given the constraints imposed.
* **Choice of α:** The requirement that αi be distinct for each hidden unit seems arbitrary and potentially restrictive. The rationale provided – preventing permutation invariance and ensuring uniqueness – feels somewhat hand-wavy and could benefit from more thorough justification.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The description of the algorithm doesn’t explicitly address scalability to larger networks and datasets. Computing the spectral radius of even a modestly sized matrix A could become computationally expensive. This is not discussed.
* **Missing Connection to Existing Work:** While the authors reference Haeffele & Vidal and Janzamin et al., a more detailed discussion of how their approach differs from and builds upon these works would be beneficial. Specifically, a comparison regarding computational complexity would be insightful.



**Originality:**

The work is original in its application of Perron-Frobenius theory to globally optimize GPNN training. While the underlying mathematical tools are established, the specific formulation and algorithm are novel. The combination of these techniques to achieve global optimality in a neural network setting is a significant contribution.

**Significance:**

The potential significance of this work is high. A demonstrably globally optimal training algorithm, even for a restricted network architecture, is a substantial advance. It offers a theoretical benchmark against which other optimization methods can be evaluated. However, the practical impact hinges on addressing the limitations related to the non-negativity constraint and scalability. If these issues can be mitigated, the work could have a lasting impact on the field.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a strong theoretical contribution with a novel algorithm for globally optimally training a specific class of neural networks.  The strengths of the work – the theoretical guarantees, clear algorithm, and strong mathematical foundation – outweigh the weaknesses, but the non-negativity constraint and limited experimental validation represent significant hurdles.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a provably globally optimal training algorithm, a rare and valuable achievement.
* Grounded in solid mathematical theory.
* Clear and well-defined algorithm.
* Demonstrates potential for applicability to more complex networks (two hidden layers).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Strong non-negativity constraint limits practical applicability.
* Limited experimental validation on small datasets.
* Potential scalability concerns.
* Conservative bounds on the spectral radius.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should address the following points:

1.  **Discuss Relaxations of Non-Negativity:** Explore potential strategies for relaxing the non-negativity constraint, even if it compromises the global optimality guarantee.
2.  **Scalability Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm, particularly regarding the spectral radius calculation.
3.  **Expand Experimental Evaluation:** Conduct experiments on larger, more complex datasets to better assess the algorithm's performance and scalability.
4.  **Detailed Comparison to Related Work:** Provide a more in-depth comparison to Haeffele & Vidal and Janzamin et al., focusing on computational complexity and practical considerations.
