This paper proposes a globally optimal training algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks based on nonlinear spectral methods. The core idea is to constrain the network weights to be nonnegative and modify the objective function to facilitate the application of Perron-Frobenius theory, leading to a globally convergent algorithm. While the concept of globally optimal training is highly desirable, and the mathematical foundations presented appear rigorous, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet pervasive, issues that collectively detract from its overall quality and, in my assessment, justify rejection.

The introduction, while setting the stage, is somewhat verbose and lacks focus. Several citations are dropped without sufficient contextualization (e.g., [1], [5], [6], [9]). The claim that the proposed method only requires data to be nonnegative is overstated; the data itself isn’t necessarily nonnegative, but the *inputs* to the activation functions must be. This is a subtle but important distinction. The assertion that existing global optimization methods are “challenging to apply in practice” (referencing Haeffele & Vidal) feels dismissive without a more detailed comparative analysis.

Section 2 presents the main result, but the mathematical notation is occasionally cumbersome. The use of both `fr(x)` and `fr(w, u)(x)` for the same function is confusing. The motivation for the modified objective function, specifically the addition of the sum of outputs, is initially vague and only clarified later. The equation for Φ(w, u) lacks sufficient spacing, making it difficult to parse visually. The explanation of S+ and S++ is clear, but the justification for using maximization instead of minimization is weak – citing the Perron-Frobenius theory feels like post-hoc rationalization. The introduction of ψp is mathematically correct but could benefit from a more intuitive explanation for a broader audience.

The theorem statement itself is dense and relies heavily on numerous undefined or poorly defined variables (e.g., ξ1, ξ2, ρx). The definition of matrix A, while seemingly correct, is exceptionally complex and lacks clear physical or intuitive meaning. The conditions for convergence (ρ(A) < 1) are stated, but the practicality of checking this condition, especially for larger networks, remains questionable. The claim of linear convergence is positive, but the constant R in the convergence guarantee is merely stated to exist without any discussion of its dependence on the problem parameters.

Section 3 delves into the proof strategy, which, while theoretically sound (leveraging Banach fixed-point theorem and Thompson metric), is presented in an overly formal and abstract manner. The lemmas are stated without sufficient motivation. The metric definitions, while mathematically correct, are dense and likely inaccessible to readers not already deeply familiar with functional analysis. Lemma 4’s derivation of the Lipschitz constant feels disconnected from the main results. The choice of γ weights feels ad-hoc without a more rigorous justification.

Section 4 expands the method to two hidden layers. While the extension is logical, it introduces even more complexity without providing further intuition. The equations become increasingly unmanageable. Equation (9) presents bounds on pw, pv, and pu, but it's unclear how these bounds translate into practical guidance for model selection.

The experiments in Section 5 are insufficient. The claim that experiments are merely a “proof of concept” is a significant weakness. The parameter selection process (random generation and cross-validation) is described but lacks detail.  The table comparing NLSM1/2 with ReLU and SVM is inconclusive; the performance differences are marginal, and the authors acknowledge that ReLU can achieve zero test error (implying that the non-negativity constraint is detrimental in some cases). The comparison with Batch-SGD in Figure 2, while demonstrating faster convergence, doesn’t provide a fair comparison (different optimization goals and parameter settings). The discussion of α’s influence on performance is insightful but raises concerns about the method's sensitivity to hyperparameter choices. The conclusion emphasizes the novelty of a “practically feasible” algorithm, yet the experimental results and extensive mathematical complexity cast doubt on this claim.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Overly Complex:** The mathematical formalism, while rigorous, is excessively complex and hinders readability.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experiments are limited, lack detail, and don’t demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   **Minor Flaws Accumulate:** A large number of minor issues (notation inconsistencies, vague explanations, lack of intuitive motivation) collectively diminish the paper’s quality.
*   **Questionable Practicality:** The convergence condition (ρ(A) < 1) and the sensitivity to hyperparameters raise concerns about the method's practical applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of applying nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training is novel.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical foundations appear sound and the global optimality guarantees are appealing.
*   **Potential:** The concept of globally optimal training, if made practical, could be a significant advancement.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the theoretical novelty and rigor, the numerous flaws in presentation, experimental validation, and potential practicality lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper feels more like a preliminary exploration of a complex mathematical framework than a polished and impactful contribution to the field. A substantial revision, with a focus on simplification, improved experimental design, and a clearer articulation of practical considerations, would be necessary for reconsideration.
