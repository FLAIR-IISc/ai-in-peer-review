

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a "nonlinear spectral method" for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights. The authors claim to provide the first practically feasible algorithm for achieving global optimality in non-trivial neural network models, with a linear convergence rate and no hyperparameter tuning. The main contribution is a fixed-point iteration method that converges to the global optimum under certain conditions on the spectral radius of a matrix derived from the network architecture.

## Major Technical Concerns

The most significant issue is a fundamental contradiction in the paper's claims. The authors state in the introduction that "the condition which guarantees global optimality just depends on the parameters of the architecture of the network and boils down to the computation of the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix. The condition can be checked without running the algorithm." However, in Theorem 1, the matrix A depends on ξ1, ξ2, and ρx = maxi ||xi||1, which are all data-dependent quantities. Therefore, the condition ρ(A) < 1 cannot be checked without seeing the data, directly contradicting the authors' claim. This is a severe methodological flaw that undermines the paper's core contribution.

The paper also contains several technical inconsistencies:
- The optimization problem is stated as a maximization (equation 2), but the gradient is computed as if it were a minimization problem (with the negative loss), creating confusion.
- The authors claim their method "requires no free parameter," but they actually have to choose multiple parameters (n1, α, pw, pu, ρw, ρu, ε) and use cross-validation to select them, contradicting their claim.
- The comparison with ReLU networks is unfair because the authors' method requires nonnegative weights while ReLU networks allow negative weights, which is a fundamental difference in model class, not just an optimization method.

## Formatting and Presentation Issues

The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism:
- Inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "n1∑ l=1" should be "∑_{l=1}^{n1}").
- The phrase "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems" is clearly a typo.
- Mathematical notation is inconsistent (e.g., using both "fr" and "f" for the network output).
- The paper claims to provide a "proof" of the main result but moves all key technical details to supplementary material, making it impossible to verify the claims without consulting additional material.

## Weak Experimental Validation

The experimental section is severely limited:
- The authors test only on low-dimensional UCI datasets, despite claiming their method could handle "images" as nonnegative data.
- The performance improvements over baseline methods (SVM, ReLU) are marginal (e.g., 96.4% vs 95.7% on Cancer dataset).
- The comparison with ReLU networks is not fair due to the weight sign constraint.
- The authors claim "linear convergence rate" but Figure 2 shows SGD with step-size 0.1 having a similar convergence rate, making this claim less impressive.

## Minor Technical Issues

- The choice of α (requiring αi ≠ αj for all i ≠ j) is not well-justified and seems arbitrary.
- The function Ψαp,q(δ,t) in equation (6) is extremely complex and not explained intuitively.
- The paper claims to be for "generalized polynomial neural networks" but the activation functions are not polynomials in the traditional sense (since αl are real numbers, not necessarily integers).
- The reference list contains formatting inconsistencies (inconsistent use of "et al.", incomplete journal information).

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses an interesting problem (global optimality for neural networks), the technical flaws are substantial enough to undermine the contribution. The fundamental contradiction regarding the data-dependent nature of the convergence condition, combined with the limited experimental validation and unfair comparisons, significantly weaken the paper's claims. The paper also suffers from numerous formatting inconsistencies that suggest a lack of attention to detail.

The authors' claim that this is "the first practically feasible algorithm to achieve global optimality for a non-trivial neural network model" is unsubstantiated given the data-dependent convergence condition and the limited experimental results. The paper would be much stronger if the authors could address the contradictions in their claims and provide more thorough experimental validation on datasets where the nonnegativity constraint is meaningful.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection due to substantial methodological flaws and formatting inconsistencies that prevent the claims from being verified. The core technical contradiction regarding the data-dependent convergence condition is particularly concerning, as it undermines the paper's central contribution. While the problem is interesting, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims.
