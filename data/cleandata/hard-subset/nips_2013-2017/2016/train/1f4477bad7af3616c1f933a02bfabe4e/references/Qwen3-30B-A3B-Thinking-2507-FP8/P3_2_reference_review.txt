

# Peer Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary
This paper proposes a globally optimal training algorithm for a specific class of neural networks - generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights and inputs. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory, providing theoretical guarantees of global optimality under certain conditions. The key contribution is a practical algorithm with linear convergence rate that achieves global optimality for a restricted neural network class, contrasting with prior work that is either theoretically sound but impractical ([8]) or requires knowledge of data-generating distributions ([11]).

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound theoretical framework with well-structured proofs. The main theorem (Theorem 1) establishes clear conditions for global optimality via a linear convergence rate, which is a significant contribution to the literature on global optimality in neural networks. The authors correctly acknowledge that the nonnegativity constraint on weights is a strong limitation but argue it's necessary for their theoretical approach.

However, several quality concerns exist:
1. The modified objective function (adding the sum of outputs) is necessary for the theoretical guarantees but lacks practical justification. The authors don't convincingly explain why this modification doesn't significantly impact generalization performance.
2. The nonnegativity constraint on weights severely limits the model's expressiveness. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't thoroughly explore how this constraint affects the types of functions that can be learned or how it compares to standard neural networks.
3. The experimental section is underdeveloped. The results on UCI datasets show competitive performance, but the authors don't provide enough detail on parameter selection or why their model performs well despite the nonnegativity constraint.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the authors do a good job explaining the key insights behind their approach. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory is clearly articulated, and the proof strategy is well-motivated.

Some areas could be clearer:
1. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why nonnegativity is required for their approach, beyond the mathematical convenience.
2. A clearer comparison of how their method differs from Haeffele & Vidal ([8]) and Janzamin et al. ([11]) in terms of practical applicability would strengthen the contribution.
3. The experimental methodology lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility, particularly regarding how parameters were selected for the UCI datasets.

## Originality

The use of nonlinear spectral methods inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory for neural network training is novel and represents an interesting approach to global optimization. The authors successfully connect their method to established theory in nonlinear eigenproblems, which is a valuable contribution.

However, the originality is somewhat limited by the restrictive nature of the model (nonnegative weights and inputs). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work on global optimality by emphasizing why their approach is practically feasible where others aren't. The key difference appears to be the nonnegativity constraint, which is a significant limitation rather than a general improvement.

## Significance

The significance of this work is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of the model. While global optimality is theoretically important, the practical relevance is questionable for most real-world applications where negative weights are essential for good performance. The paper acknowledges this by stating the results are "a proof of concept" and that they don't yet have a good understanding of parameter selection.

The experimental results on UCI datasets are promising but limited. The authors correctly note that they're the first to show global optimality with non-trivial classification results, but the results don't demonstrate scalability to more complex tasks or datasets. The comparison with ReLU networks is limited, as the ReLU networks can have negative weights while the authors' model cannot, making a fair comparison difficult.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the literature on global optimality in neural networks, but the practical significance is limited by the restrictive nonnegativity constraint. The theoretical framework is well-developed, but the paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of the limitations of the nonnegativity constraint and better justification for the modified objective function.

The paper is well-written and the mathematical proofs appear correct, but the experimental results lack sufficient depth to demonstrate the practical value of the approach. The authors should address the limitations more honestly and provide more insight into how their method could be extended beyond the restrictive setting they consider.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions for improvement:

1. The authors should more thoroughly discuss the limitations imposed by the nonnegativity constraint and how this affects the model's expressiveness.
2. They should provide a better justification for the modified objective function (adding the sum of outputs).
3. The experimental section should be expanded to include more details on parameter selection and implementation.
4. The comparison with prior work on global optimality should be more precise, clearly explaining why their approach is "practically feasible" where others aren't.

While the theoretical contribution is valid, the practical relevance of the method is questionable for most real-world applications. The paper would be stronger if the authors acknowledged these limitations more directly and provided a clearer path toward addressing them.
