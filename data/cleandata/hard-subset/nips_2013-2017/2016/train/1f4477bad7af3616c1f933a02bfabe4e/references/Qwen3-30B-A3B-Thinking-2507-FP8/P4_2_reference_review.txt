

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel approach for achieving global optimality in training a specific class of neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors consider feedforward networks with one or two hidden layers, where the activation functions are generalized polynomials (real powers) and weights are constrained to be nonnegative. The key contribution is a globally optimal training algorithm that guarantees linear convergence to the global optimum under certain conditions, which can be verified by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix.

The paper builds on recent work on global optimality in neural networks, particularly the tensor-based approach by Janzamin et al. (2015) and the structured matrix factorization approach by Haeffele and Vidal (2015). Unlike these works, the proposed method requires only nonnegativity of the data (which holds for many real-world datasets like images and word frequencies) and avoids the need to estimate the data-generating measure. The authors connect their work to Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, which provides a theoretical foundation for their nonlinear spectral method.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The main theorem (Theorem 1) is rigorously proven using fixed point theory and the Banach fixed point theorem. The proof strategy is well-structured, with intermediate lemmas that build toward the main result. The authors carefully address the conditions for global optimality and provide a convergence rate analysis. The experimental results on UCI datasets show competitive performance compared to ReLU networks and SVMs, with the authors correctly noting these as proof-of-concept results. However, the restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, nonnegative data, specific activation functions) limit the practical applicability of the method, as most real-world neural networks use ReLU activations with mixed-sign weights. The bounds on the spectral radius are acknowledged to be conservative, which may limit the practical utility of the method.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the key concepts (such as the nonlinear spectral mapping GΦ) are clearly defined. The experimental section provides sufficient detail about the setup, including how parameters were selected. However, some parts of the theoretical exposition could be clearer, particularly the connection between the matrix A and the convergence conditions in Theorem 1. The authors could better explain how the theoretical guarantees translate into practical implementation details, such as how to choose the parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) for real-world applications.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a theoretically sound algorithm for global optimality in neural networks, which is rare in the literature. The use of Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems to develop a nonlinear spectral method is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on global optimality (Janzamin et al. 2015, Haeffele and Vidal 2015) by providing a practically feasible approach with verifiable conditions for convergence. The requirement of nonnegative weights and data is a novel constraint that enables the theoretical guarantees, though it does limit the applicability to standard neural networks. The paper does not duplicate previous NIPS papers on global optimality, as it addresses a different class of networks with a distinct theoretical foundation.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning: the lack of theoretical guarantees for global optimality in neural network training. The proposed method provides a theoretically sound approach to achieving global optimality for a specific class of networks, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, the restrictive assumptions significantly limit the practical significance of the work. The experimental results on UCI datasets show competitive performance, but the method does not scale to larger, more complex problems due to the nonnegativity constraints. The paper acknowledges these limitations but does not provide a clear path for extending the approach to standard neural network architectures. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the practical significance is limited by the restrictive assumptions.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Provides the first practically feasible algorithm for achieving global optimality in neural networks with verifiable conditions
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with clear convergence guarantees
- Demonstrates competitive performance on small UCI datasets
- Novel application of Perron-Frobenius theory to neural network training
- Clear connection between theoretical analysis and practical implementation

*Con:*
- Restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, nonnegative data, generalized polynomial activations) limit applicability to standard neural networks
- Experimental results are limited to small, low-dimensional UCI datasets
- Theoretical bounds on spectral radius are conservative, potentially limiting practical utility
- No clear path for extension to standard ReLU networks with mixed-sign weights
- The "modified objective function" with the sum of outputs may not be intuitive to practitioners

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization, but its practical significance is limited by the restrictive assumptions. While the results are technically sound and novel, the method's applicability to standard neural network training is questionable. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the method might be extended to more general settings, and stronger justification for why the restrictive assumptions are acceptable for practical applications.

Given the strong theoretical foundation and the importance of global optimality in neural networks, I recommend acceptance, but with the caveat that the practical implications of the method are limited by its restrictive assumptions. The paper represents a significant theoretical advance that could inspire future work on global optimality in neural networks, even if the specific approach has limited direct applicability.
