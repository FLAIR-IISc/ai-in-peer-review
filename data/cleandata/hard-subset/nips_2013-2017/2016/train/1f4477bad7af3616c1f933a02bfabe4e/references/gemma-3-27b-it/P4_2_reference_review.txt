This paper proposes a novel approach for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks, termed the “nonlinear spectral method.” The core idea is to recast the training problem as a maximization problem over a specific set, leveraging the Perron-Frobenius theory and related spectral properties to guarantee convergence to a global optimum under certain conditions, primarily non-negativity of weights. The authors present theoretical results, including a convergence theorem and an analysis of conditions for global optimality, and demonstrate preliminary experimental results on UCI datasets.

The paper builds upon recent work addressing global optimality in neural network training, such as Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015).  It distinguishes itself by avoiding the density estimation required by Janzamin et al. and offering a more practical approach than the matrix factorization-based method of Haeffele & Vidal. It draws connections to Perron-Frobenius theory and spectral analysis, leveraging these tools for the optimization process.  The work extends previous results by considering two hidden layers and providing a practical algorithm with a linear convergence rate.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of utilizing nonlinear spectral methods and the connection to Perron-Frobenius theory for neural network training is a novel contribution. The attempt to provide a globally optimal training algorithm is significant.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a substantial theoretical framework, including a convergence theorem (Theorem 1) and a detailed proof strategy. The analysis of the spectral radius condition for global optimality is well-developed.  The extension to two hidden layers (Theorem 5) is a valuable addition.
* **Clarity (within technical depth):** While the mathematical notation is dense, the paper strives for clarity in presenting the algorithm and theoretical results. The proof strategy is outlined effectively, even though detailed proofs are in the supplementary material.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The authors outline a concrete algorithm based on their theoretical findings, offering a potentially useful method for training neural networks under specific constraints.
* **Experiments:**  The inclusion of experiments, even if preliminary and on small datasets, demonstrates the feasibility of the approach and offers a baseline for comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Strong Constraints:** The requirement of non-negative weights is a significant limitation. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't fully address the impact on expressiveness or explore ways to mitigate this constraint. The impact of this constraint on practical applications is not thoroughly discussed.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The computational complexity of the spectral analysis and the requirement to compute and check the spectral radius of a matrix (even of size K+1) may pose scalability issues for large networks and datasets. This concern isn't adequately addressed.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:** The authors acknowledge that the bounds derived for ensuring ρ(A) < 1 are likely conservative, potentially limiting the applicability of the method. Further investigation into tighter bounds is needed.
* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are limited in scope, focusing on low-dimensional UCI datasets. A more comprehensive evaluation, including larger datasets and comparison with state-of-the-art methods, is crucial to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The choice of parameters and the cross-validation process are briefly described, but more detail would be beneficial.
* **Objective Function Modification:** The modification to the objective function (adding the sum of outputs) is justified as a technical necessity but its impact on the learned representation and generalization performance warrants further investigation.
* **Dependency on α:** The choice of α values is crucial. The paper notes that α values must be distinct, but provides limited guidance on selecting appropriate values in practice.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to globally optimal neural network training.
* The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory and spectral analysis is insightful and potentially opens up new avenues for research.
* The algorithm is well-defined and offers a practical implementation of the theoretical results.
* The experimental results, while preliminary, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The strong constraint of non-negative weights significantly limits the applicability of the method.
* Scalability concerns due to the spectral analysis and matrix computations may hinder practical use.
* The experimental evaluation is limited and doesn’t provide strong evidence of superior performance compared to existing methods.
* The conservative bounds on the spectral radius could restrict the applicability of the method in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of neural network optimization. The theoretical results are solid, and the proposed algorithm is novel. However, the strong constraints, potential scalability issues, and limited experimental evaluation raise concerns about its practical applicability.

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.**  I recommend accepting this paper with the understanding that further research is needed to address the limitations outlined above. Specifically, the authors should investigate methods to relax the non-negativity constraint, explore techniques to improve scalability, and conduct a more comprehensive experimental evaluation on larger datasets.  Addressing these issues will significantly enhance the impact and practical relevance of this work.
