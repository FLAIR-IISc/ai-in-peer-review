- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method based on Perron-Frobenius theory that guarantees global convergence under a verifiable spectral radius condition.  
- Method requires nonnegative weights, a modified objective (sum of outputs), and generalized polynomial activations with exponents α_i ≥ 1.  
- Main theorem proves linear convergence to the global optimum under specified, verifiable conditions; proof strategy is complete and technically sound.  
- Practical applicability is limited by strong constraints: nonnegative weights, requirement α_i ≠ α_j for all i ≠ j, and conservative theoretical bounds.  
- Experiments show competitive performance on low-dimensional UCI datasets but do not demonstrate scalability to high-dimensional tasks like images.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written with consistent notation and a well-explained proof strategy.  
- Needs clearer motivation for the nonnegative-weight constraint and a principled method for choosing α values in practice.  
- The connection between conservative theoretical bounds and practical parameter selection is underexplained; more guidance is needed.  
- Original contribution: novel application of nonlinear Perron-Frobenius methods to neural network training, distinguishing it from prior global-optimality work.  
- Some aspects are not entirely novel (nonnegative constraints relate to NMF; polynomial activations build on prior work).  
- Significance: provides the first practically feasible, theoretically grounded globally optimal training algorithm under verifiable conditions.  
- Broader impact is limited by architecture constraints and conservative conditions, reducing applicability to general deep learning.  
- Strengths: theoretical novelty, verifiable global optimality, linear convergence, demonstration on real datasets, clear exposition.  
- Weaknesses: restrictive assumptions, overly conservative bounds, limited experimental scope, no parameter-selection strategy, arbitrary α distinctness.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, verifiable conditions, competitive experiments, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical applicability, conservative bounds, unclear scalability and extension to standard architectures.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions; advise adding guidance on parameter choices, tighter bounds, and discussion of limitations and future extensions.
