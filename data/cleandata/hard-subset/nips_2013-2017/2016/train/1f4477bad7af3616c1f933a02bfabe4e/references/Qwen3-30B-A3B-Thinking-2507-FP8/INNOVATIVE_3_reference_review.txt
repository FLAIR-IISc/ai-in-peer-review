

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel approach to training neural networks with global optimality guarantees, which is a highly significant contribution to the field. The authors introduce a "nonlinear spectral method" that provides a globally optimal training algorithm for generalized polynomial neural networks under specific constraints. The key novelty lies in transforming the neural network training problem into a nonlinear eigenproblem on convex cones, leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems. Unlike prior work on global optimality (e.g., [11] with tensor methods or [8] with structured matrix factorization), the proposed method avoids the need to compute score function tensors or make assumptions about the data-generating distribution, and it offers a linear convergence rate.

The main technical contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes conditions under which a simple iterative algorithm (GΦ) converges to the unique global maximizer of the modified objective function. The conditions for convergence depend solely on the spectral radius of a small matrix, which can be checked without running the algorithm. This is a major practical advantage over previous globally optimal approaches, which the authors correctly note are "challenging to apply in practice." The method also eliminates the need for learning rate tuning, as there are no free parameters in the algorithm.

The paper makes several bold assumptions that enable this novel approach: nonnegative weights and data (which restricts applicability to count data like images and word frequencies), and a modified objective function that includes a sum of outputs. The authors convincingly argue that these constraints are necessary to ensure the uniqueness of the global optimum and to enable the nonlinear spectral approach. They also provide a careful analysis of how the spectral radius depends on model parameters, showing that for any fixed architecture, there exist parameter choices (large enough p_w, p_u) that guarantee convergence.

The experimental results serve as a compelling proof of concept. The authors test their method (NLSM1 and NLSM2) on several UCI datasets, demonstrating competitive performance compared to ReLU networks and SVMs. Crucially, unlike previous papers on global optimality [8, 11], this work provides empirical results on real datasets, showing that the theoretically optimal solution can achieve practical performance. The convergence behavior (Figure 2) clearly demonstrates the linear convergence rate claimed in the theory, which is significantly faster than SGD with various learning rates.

The paper is well-structured and the mathematical presentation is rigorous, though the notation can be dense. The authors carefully distinguish their work from prior approaches and properly acknowledge limitations. The supplementary material contains proofs of intermediate results, which is appropriate for a conference paper.

The main limitations of the work are:
1. The nonnegativity constraint on weights and data is quite restrictive, limiting applicability to specific data types (count data, images, etc.)
2. The parameter selection process (choosing α, n1, etc.) is not well understood and requires extensive cross-validation
3. The theoretical bounds on the spectral radius are conservative, requiring potentially large values for p_w and p_u
4. The experiments are limited to low-dimensional datasets, and the authors acknowledge the difficulty of scaling to higher dimensions

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold directions over methodological perfection, I find these limitations to be acceptable given the paper's significant contribution. The authors have successfully demonstrated that global optimality for neural networks can be achieved with a practical algorithm, which is a major step forward in understanding neural network training.

The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning (global optimality) with a novel approach that is theoretically grounded and practically implementable. The paper opens up new directions for research in neural network optimization by connecting it to nonlinear spectral theory. While the constraints are restrictive, the authors clearly identify the scope of their approach ("nonnegative data such as images, word frequencies, etc.") and provide evidence that the resulting classifiers can still be expressive enough for practical problems.

In conclusion, this paper presents a highly novel and significant contribution to the field. The authors have developed a theoretically sound, practically implementable algorithm for globally optimal neural network training, which is a major advancement over previous work. While the constraints are notable, the paper's core contribution is sufficiently bold and novel to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

**Strengths:**
- First practically feasible algorithm for globally optimal neural network training
- Novel connection between neural network training and nonlinear spectral theory
- Clear theoretical guarantees with a checkable condition (spectral radius)
- Practical convergence behavior demonstrated in experiments
- Proof of concept on real datasets (unlike prior global optimality papers)

**Weaknesses:**
- Nonnegativity constraints limit applicability to specific data types
- Parameter selection remains somewhat ad hoc
- Theoretical bounds may be conservative
- Limited to low-dimensional datasets in experiments

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning, with significant theoretical contributions and promising empirical results. The constraints are acknowledged and reasonable for the scope of the work, and the paper's bold approach to global optimality makes it a strong candidate for NIPS.
