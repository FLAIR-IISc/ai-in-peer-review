This paper presents a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks, achieving global optimality under certain conditions using a nonlinear spectral method. The core idea – framing the training problem as a nonlinear eigenproblem on a convex cone – is strikingly original and potentially impactful. While the methodology appears complex, the promise of a globally optimal training algorithm, circumventing the typical NP-hardness and convergence issues of neural network optimization, is significant. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing globally optimal training methods (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.) and position their work as a practical alternative, requiring only non-negativity of data and weights.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach is genuinely novel, diverging from standard gradient-based methods and existing global optimization techniques for neural networks. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory and nonlinear spectral methods is a creative insight.
*   **Global Optimality Guarantee:** The theoretical guarantee of global optimality, even if under specific constraints (non-negativity, checking spectral radius), is a major strength. This is a rare claim in deep learning.
*   **Linear Convergence:** The claim of linear convergence is particularly attractive, promising fast training times compared to iterative methods.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of training neural networks and the challenges associated with it.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper demonstrates considerable mathematical sophistication, attempting a rigorous development of the method and its convergence properties.
*   **Initial Experimental Validation:** The UCI dataset results, while limited, provide preliminary evidence that the method is viable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Constraints:** The requirement for non-negative weights is a significant limitation, potentially restricting the expressiveness of the network. The authors acknowledge this but don’t fully explore the impact on practical applications.
*   **Conservatism of Bounds:** The authors admit the bounds derived for ensuring ρ(A) < 1 are likely conservative. This raises concerns about the practical applicability of the method, as unnecessarily strict constraints might hinder performance.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are limited in scope. More extensive evaluation on diverse datasets, comparing to state-of-the-art methods, is crucial. The comparisons provided are not exhaustive.
*   **Complexity and Readability:** While thorough, the paper is mathematically dense and can be challenging to follow. Some proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, which hinders reproducibility and complete understanding.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of α, pu, pw, ρu, and ρw significantly impacts the convergence guarantee. The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how to effectively choose these parameters in practice, particularly beyond the constraints derived for ρ(A) < 1.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The computational cost of calculating the spectral radius of the matrix A, especially for larger networks, is not discussed. This is a critical factor for real-world applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to training neural networks with a global optimality guarantee.
*   The linear convergence claim, if validated, could offer a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   The connection to nonlinear spectral methods and Perron-Frobenius theory opens up new avenues for research in neural network optimization.
*   Even with its limitations, this work presents a valuable contribution to the field, pushing the boundaries of what is currently possible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong constraints on weights may severely limit the applicability of the method.
*   The limited experimental evaluation and lack of comprehensive comparisons raise concerns about practical performance.
*   The complexity and density of the paper could hinder adoption and further research.
*   The potential scalability issues and lack of discussion on parameter selection could limit the impact of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a bold and innovative paper. While the constraints and limited experimental validation are concerns, the novelty and theoretical significance are compelling. The potential for a globally optimal, linearly convergent training algorithm is highly attractive. I am inclined to **accept** the paper, recognizing that further research and experimentation are needed to fully realize its potential. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above in future work. In particular, exploring techniques to relax the non-negativity constraints, developing more efficient methods for calculating the spectral radius, and conducting more extensive experiments are crucial next steps. The reviewers should carefully look at supplementary materials, and the mathematical formalism. The novelty is very high and the ideas presented are interesting.
