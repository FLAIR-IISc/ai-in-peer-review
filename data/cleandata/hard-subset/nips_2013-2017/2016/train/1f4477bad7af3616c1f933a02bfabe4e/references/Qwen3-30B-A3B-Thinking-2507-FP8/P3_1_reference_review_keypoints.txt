- Proposes a globally optimal training algorithm for generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative weights and real exponents using a nonlinear spectral method.

- Uses a modified objective (adding negative sum of outputs) and weight constraints to enable a Perron–Frobenius–style fixed-point approach, with linear convergence provable by checking ρ(A) < 1.

- Theoretical contribution: establishes global optimality and leverages multihomogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory in an elegant, well-executed proof.

- Provides a clear, checkable convergence condition (ρ(A) < 1) and a linear convergence rate, improving on prior impractical approaches.

- Empirical validation: first demonstration of globally optimal training on real UCI datasets, with comparisons to SGD and ReLU networks.

- Well-organized presentation with separated theory/experiments and supplementary proofs.

- Major limitation: nonnegative weight requirement is highly restrictive and undermines applicability to standard neural networks.

- Experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets and lack direct comparisons to other globally optimal methods [8, 11].

- Practical adoption is hindered by sensitive parameter tuning (pw, pu, ρw, ρu) needed to satisfy ρ(A) < 1 and conservative theoretical bounds.

- The added negative-sum objective lacks explanation of its impact on the learning objective and generalization.

- Scope is limited: the considered “non-trivial” networks are narrowly constrained and the two-layer extension is minor.

- Practical relevance is questionable because nonnegativity removes expressive capacity needed for complex decision boundaries.

- Empirical gains are small or negligible versus SVM/ReLU, so global optimality does not demonstrably yield better real-world performance.

- The connection to count-data applications is weak and not convincingly justified.

- Conservative spectral bounds suggest convergence may be slower in practice than claimed "very fast" behavior.

- Recommendation: clarify practical relevance of nonnegativity, expand experiments to harder datasets, and compare with other global methods.

- Recommendation: provide practical parameter-selection guidance and justify/explain the objective modification's effect on generalization.

- Overall assessment: strong theoretical result but limited practical impact; reviewer recommends weak reject unless constraints are relaxed or stronger empirical evidence is provided.
