

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel method for achieving globally optimal training of neural networks, specifically generalized polynomial networks with nonnegative weights and inputs. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method that converges to the global optimum with a linear convergence rate, providing theoretical guarantees that can be verified by checking the spectral radius of a small matrix. The approach differs from previous globally optimal methods [8, 11] by avoiding the need for data-generating measure knowledge and offering a practically implementable algorithm. The paper establishes a connection to Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, which enables the theoretical guarantees.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Quality (High):** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with rigorous proofs for the main results. The authors successfully connect neural network training to nonlinear spectral methods and fixed point theory, providing clear conditions for global optimality. The proof strategy is well-structured, building on established mathematical frameworks (Perron-Frobenius theory, Thompson metric, Banach fixed point theorem). The conditions for global optimality (Theorem 1) are explicitly verifiable by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix, which is a practical advantage over previous approaches.

**Novelty (High):** The work is genuinely novel in combining nonlinear spectral methods with neural network training. The authors clearly identify limitations of previous globally optimal approaches (e.g., [11] requires knowledge of the data-generating measure) and provide a solution that avoids these limitations. The nonnegative weight constraint, while restrictive, is shown to be necessary for the uniqueness of the global optimum.

**Experimental Results (Moderate):** The experiments provide a proof of concept for the practical feasibility of the approach. The method achieves competitive results on UCI datasets compared to SVM and ReLU networks, with the two-hidden-layer variant (NLSM2) outperforming others on several datasets. The comparison with SGD shows the method's linear convergence rate, which is a significant advantage.

## Weaknesses

**Practical Limitations (Major):** The nonnegative weight constraint is a severe limitation for real-world applications, as most successful neural networks require negative weights to model complex relationships. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't adequately address how to handle datasets with negative values (which are common in practice). The claim that the method is "practically feasible" seems overstated given this constraint.

**Experimental Scope (Moderate):** The experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets. The authors acknowledge this limitation, noting that "the number of required hidden units grows with the dimension of the datasets," but don't provide any experiments on high-dimensional data where neural networks typically excel.

**Theoretical Assumptions (Moderate):** The paper assumes nonnegative inputs and weights, which is restrictive. While the authors argue this is valid for "count data" like images and word frequencies, many modern applications (e.g., normalized images, continuous features) don't satisfy this constraint. The paper doesn't explore how to relax this assumption while maintaining the theoretical guarantees.

**Comparison with Baselines (Minor):** The comparison with ReLU networks is somewhat unfair since ReLU networks allow negative weights while the proposed method does not. The paper doesn't adequately explain why the nonnegative constraint doesn't significantly degrade performance on the tested datasets.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network training, providing the first practically verifiable globally optimal algorithm for neural networks. The theoretical framework is sound and well-motivated, with clear connections to established mathematical theories. However, the severe practical limitation of the nonnegative weight constraint significantly undermines the potential impact of the work.

The paper meets the quality criterion with strong theoretical foundations, but the significance is limited by the restrictive assumptions. While the method is elegant and theoretically sound, it may not be widely applicable to the vast majority of neural network applications that require negative weights. The experimental results are promising for the constrained setting but don't demonstrate the method's broader applicability.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, meeting the clarity criterion. The novelty is substantial within the constrained problem space, though the practical relevance is limited by the nonnegative weight constraint.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following caveats:

1. The paper's theoretical contribution is strong and novel, making it a valuable addition to the literature on neural network optimization.
2. The practical limitations must be clearly acknowledged in the final version, particularly the nonnegative weight constraint and its implications for real-world applications.
3. The authors should consider expanding the experimental section to include a discussion of how to handle negative inputs (e.g., through data transformation) and potentially provide results on a small high-dimensional dataset to demonstrate broader applicability.

The paper represents an important step in understanding the theoretical foundations of neural network training, but its practical impact will be limited without addressing the nonnegative weight constraint. Future work could explore ways to relax this constraint while maintaining the theoretical guarantees, which would significantly increase the paper's significance.

The authors have made a valuable theoretical contribution, but the practical constraints mean the work is more of a theoretical curiosity than a practical alternative to SGD for most applications. For NIPS, this paper is likely to be of interest to researchers in optimization and theoretical machine learning, but its broader impact on the field is limited.
