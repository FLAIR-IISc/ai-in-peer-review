- Presents a novel algorithm that provably trains Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) to global optimality for a single hidden layer.  
- Frames training as a maximization problem solvable via Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point spectral analysis.  
- Requires non-negativity constraints on network weights as a core assumption for the theory.  
- Provides theoretical convergence and global optimality guarantees under conditions tied to the spectral radius of a small matrix.  
- Extends the approach to networks with two hidden layers.  
- Proposes a modified objective with an output-based regularizer to enable Perron-Frobenius analysis.  
- Introduces a clear, easy-to-implement nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that avoids typical hyperparameter tuning like learning rates.  
- Supplies detailed proof sketches and supplementary material with lemmas and theorems.  
- Main strength: a rare, provably globally optimal training result grounded in solid mathematical theory.  
- Additional strength: novelty in applying Perron-Frobenius and spectral methods to neural network training.  
- Limitation: the strong non-negativity weight constraint significantly restricts expressiveness and real-world applicability.  
- Concern: the provided spectral radius bounds are conservative, leaving practical tightness unclear.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited to small UCI datasets and may not reflect performance on high-dimensional or complex tasks.  
- Design choice requiring distinct αi for hidden units is inadequately justified and may be arbitrary or restrictive.  
- Scalability is not addressed; computing spectral radii and scaling to larger networks/datasets could be expensive.  
- Lacks detailed comparison to closely related work (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.), especially regarding computational complexity.  
- Overall originality is high due to the novel formulation and algorithm despite using established mathematical tools.  
- Significance is potentially high as a theoretical benchmark, but practical impact depends on relaxing constraints and improving scalability.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: discuss relaxations of non-negativity, provide scalability/complexity analysis, expand experiments to larger datasets, and give a more detailed comparison to related work.
