Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that provably finds a unique global optimum for training a class of generalized polynomial neural networks under specific constraints. The networks have nonnegative inputs and nonnegative weights, use per-hidden-unit power activations with exponents αl ≥ 1 (and distinct across units), and are trained on a modified objective: average negative cross-entropy plus the sum of all outputs, with norm constraints on each layer and a small positive bias term to ensure strictly positive gradients. The core idea is to recast training as finding a fixed point of a multihomogeneous map GΦ, and to prove that, under an easily checkable spectral radius condition ρ(A) < 1 for a (K+1)×(K+1) nonnegative matrix A (K classes), GΦ is a contraction in the Thompson metric. This yields uniqueness of the critical point in the positive orthant S++ and linear convergence to the global optimum. The approach generalizes to two hidden layers with an analogous condition on a (K+2)×(K+2) matrix. Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive accuracy to ReLU networks and somewhat below RBF-SVM, with faster convergence than SGD for their objective.

This work extends recent lines of guaranteed training approaches: Haeffele & Vidal’s global optimality via structured factorizations (but with limited practicality), and Janzamin et al.’s tensor methods for single-hidden-layer networks that require access to score functions. Polynomial networks have been studied for efficiency and expressivity in prior NIPS work (e.g., Livni et al.). The paper is novel in applying nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory (Thompson metric, multi-homogeneous maps) to derive a practical, globally convergent training algorithm for a nontrivial neural class.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical advance: A simple, checkable spectral radius condition guarantees uniqueness and linear convergence of a fixed-point iteration to the global optimum.
- Conceptual novelty: Bridges nonlinear spectral theory (Thompson metric, multihomogeneous maps) with neural network training.
- Practicality relative to prior guarantees: Requires only nonnegative data and weights, and avoids estimating score functions or verifying delicate tensor identifiability.
- Extension to two hidden layers (with explicit A) and a principled discussion of how model parameters affect ρ(A).
- Algorithm is parameter-free in the optimization sense (no learning rate), with deterministic, linear-rate convergence when the condition holds.

Weaknesses and evaluation
Quality
- The global optimality guarantee hinges on several restrictive modeling choices: nonnegative inputs and weights, per-unit distinct power activations, hard p-norm constraints per layer, and an altered objective that adds the sum of outputs (and a small positive weight-sum term). The paper gives intuition, but the statistical consequences of these choices (biases, calibration, generalization) are not well analyzed.
- The spectral-radius bounds are admitted to be conservative; the paper asserts the “effective” ρ(A) is much smaller but provides no quantitative study. A tighter, data-dependent analysis or empirical study of ρ(A) vs. n1, norms, and K would strengthen the claim of practicality.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to small UCI datasets with modest architectures. Baselines are reasonable (ReLU, RBF-SVM), but comparisons are not apples-to-apples in runtime (full-gradient fixed-point vs. mini-batch SGD) and the objective differs from standard cross-entropy. No ablations (impact of the output-sum term, α choices, norm radii, p exponents) are provided.
- Reproducibility is hindered by missing details and code; the cross-entropy form is not explicitly stated; several proofs are deferred; reference list has incomplete entries.

Clarity
- The paper is mathematically dense but broadly well structured. However, notation is heavy and some definitions (e.g., the exact loss L) and motivations (effect of the “sum of outputs” and ε-terms) should be made explicit. Several typos and incomplete references reduce readability.

Originality
- High. Applying multihomogeneous Perron–Frobenius contraction arguments to derive globally optimal training for a nontrivial network family appears new, and the fixed point operator based on normalized gradients is interesting.

Significance
- Mixed. The theoretical contribution is elegant and potentially influential, but practical impact may be limited by modeling constraints and the modified objective. Demonstrations beyond toy/low-dimensional settings are needed to assess broader applicability.

Questions for authors and suggestions
- Please state the exact cross-entropy used. For softmax cross-entropy, adding a constant to all logits is invariant inside the loss; how does the extra “sum of outputs” outside the loss affect optimization and calibration? Provide an ablation without this term and a discussion of its empirical effect.
- Can the “nonnegativity” cone be generalized (e.g., to signed weights via positive/negative parts) while preserving the contraction proof? Are there variants using alternative cones to relax constraints?
- Distinct αl are required to avoid permutation symmetries. How sensitive is performance to these choices? Can α be learned or regularized, and what happens if αl are close?
- Provide empirical study of ρ(A): how it scales with n1, K, norms, and data norms; whether the conservative bounds are indeed far from the observed Lipschitz constant; and how this correlates with iteration counts.
- Compare wall-clock time and total data passes vs. tuned full-batch or large-batch optimizers for the same (modified) objective.
- Explore larger nonnegative datasets (e.g., MNIST with [0,1] pixels), and report calibration metrics and robustness.
- Clarify the role of ε and its numerical stability; does taking ε→0 preserve the guarantees in practice?

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant and novel theory giving global optimality and linear convergence.
- Clear connection between architecture/hyperparameters and a verifiable spectral condition.
- First practically implementable global-optimal training method for a nontrivial NN class with empirical validation.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong modeling constraints and modified objective raise concerns about practical relevance.
- Conservative conditions and lack of quantitative analysis of ρ(A) vs. model capacity.
- Limited experiments and missing ablations; unclear real-world scalability.
- Clarity issues and incomplete references.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The work is theoretically strong and novel, but the empirical validation and analysis of the practical implications of the constraints are not yet convincing for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to strengthen the empirical section (larger datasets, ablations, runtime), clarify the loss/objective and the effect of the added terms, provide a quantitative study of ρ(A), and improve clarity and references. With these improvements, the paper could make a valuable contribution.
