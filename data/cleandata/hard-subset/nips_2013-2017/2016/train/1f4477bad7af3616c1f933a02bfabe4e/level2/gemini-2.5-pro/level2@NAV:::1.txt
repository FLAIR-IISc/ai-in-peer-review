This paper presents a novel and theoretically significant contribution to the optimization of neural networks. The authors propose a "nonlinear spectral method" for training a specific class of feedforward networks with generalized polynomial activations. The main claim is that, under checkable conditions, this method converges linearly to the unique global optimum of a modified objective function. This work relates to recent efforts on globally optimal training (e.g., tensor methods) but distinguishes itself by proposing a method that is demonstrated to be feasible on real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical result on a notoriously difficult non-convex problem. The authors provide a rare, practical guarantee of global optimality. The approach, which elegantly reduces the optimization problem to finding a unique fixed point of a contraction mapping, is both novel and well-motivated by Perron-Frobenius theory. A key advantage is the *a priori* check for guaranteed convergence, which depends on the spectral radius of a small, easily constructed matrix. Unlike much of the preceding theoretical work in this area, the authors validate their method with experiments on several UCI datasets. These experiments serve as a valuable proof-of-concept, demonstrating that the constrained model can achieve competitive performance against standard baselines like SVMs and ReLU networks, and confirming the claimed linear convergence rate.

**Weaknesses:**
The method's practical applicability is constrained by several strong assumptions. The requirements of non-negative input data and non-negative network weights significantly limit the model's scope compared to standard neural networks. Furthermore, the algorithm optimizes a modified objective function that includes penalty terms in addition to the classification loss. While these terms are technically necessary for the theoretical guarantees, their impact on the final classifier's performance on the original task is not fully explored. The paper also notes that the condition for convergence becomes harder to satisfy for larger networks, which raises questions about the method's scalability to the high-dimensional, complex problems where deep learning typically excels.

**Conclusion and Suggestions:**
This is a high-quality paper that presents a clear and valuable advancement in our understanding of the neural network optimization landscape. While the practical constraints are significant, the theoretical guarantees are powerful. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion on the implications of using the modified objective function. Additionally, a theoretical analysis of how the convergence condition scales with network size (depth and width), even if based on loose bounds, would provide better insight into the method’s potential for more complex architectures.
