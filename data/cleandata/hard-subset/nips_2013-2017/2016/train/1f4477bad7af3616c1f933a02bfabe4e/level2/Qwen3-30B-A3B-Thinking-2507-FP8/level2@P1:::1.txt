

This paper proposes a novel method for globally optimal training of a specific class of neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors consider feedforward neural networks with generalized polynomial activation functions (real powers) and nonnegative weights, and prove that under certain conditions (nonnegative data, specific parameter choices), their method achieves global optimality with linear convergence rate. The key insight is to reformulate the training problem as a fixed point problem in the positive orthant and apply results from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for one and two hidden layer networks, with the convergence condition being checkable without running the algorithm.

Strengths: The theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed. The paper provides rigorous proofs for global optimality and linear convergence, which is rare in neural network training literature. The method is practically feasible compared to prior global optimization approaches (e.g., Janzamin et al. [11] requires estimating the data-generating measure, which is difficult in high dimensions). The authors clearly articulate the conditions under which their method works and provide explicit ways to verify them. The experimental results on UCI datasets support their theoretical claims, showing competitive performance compared to standard methods like ReLU networks and SVMs, with the added benefit of global optimality guarantees.

Weaknesses: The nonnegative weights constraint significantly limits the applicability of the method to real-world problems where negative weights are essential for learning complex patterns. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide sufficient justification for why this constraint doesn't severely impact the model's expressiveness. The experiments are restricted to low-dimensional UCI datasets, which doesn't adequately demonstrate the method's potential on high-dimensional data common in modern applications. The paper could benefit from more detailed discussion about the practical implications of the parameter choices (e.g., why the specific form of the modified objective function is necessary) and why the authors chose generalized polynomial activations instead of more standard ones.

The paper's main contribution is theoretically sound and represents a meaningful step toward understanding global optimization in neural networks. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous problems is novel and well-executed. However, the practical impact is somewhat limited by the restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, low-dimensional datasets). The authors make a strong case for the theoretical novelty and practical feasibility of their method compared to prior global optimization approaches, but the paper would be strengthened by more discussion on how to overcome the nonnegative weights constraint or how the method might be extended to more general networks.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient technical detail for experts to follow the proofs. The experimental section is appropriately focused on demonstrating the theoretical claims rather than pushing state-of-the-art performance. The authors provide a useful algorithmic description and clear guidelines for implementing their method.

I believe this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization. While the practical applicability is currently limited by the nonnegative weights constraint, the method provides an important stepping stone toward globally optimal training. The paper is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers on neural network optimization, as it offers a practically feasible approach to global optimality rather than just theoretical guarantees.

For future work, the authors might explore ways to relax the nonnegative weights constraint or extend their method to deeper networks while maintaining theoretical guarantees. It would also be valuable to test the method on larger, more complex datasets to better understand its scalability.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of neural network training. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear distinction from prior work. While the practical limitations are notable, the theoretical insights are valuable to the community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better address the limitations and discuss the practical implications of the nonnegative weights constraint.
