- Introduces a "nonlinear spectral method" to train Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with provable linear-rate convergence to the unique global optimum.  
- Model: one- or two-hidden-layer feedforward networks with generalized polynomial activations (powers α_i ≥ 1) and non-negative inputs and weights.  
- Training objective: cross-entropy loss plus a sum-of-outputs term and small regularizer to ensure strictly positive gradients.  
- Main theoretical result (Theorem 1): a checkable sufficient condition (spectral radius ρ(A) < 1) guarantees global optimality and linear convergence.  
- Proof technique: reframes optimization as finding a unique fixed point of a contraction on a convex cone using nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and the Banach fixed-point theorem with the Thompson metric.  
- Compared to prior global-optimality work (e.g., tensor methods), the approach requires no data-generating distribution and has simpler verifiable conditions.  
- Empirical validation: includes experiments on UCI datasets demonstrating practical implementability and observed linear convergence.  
- Strength — originality/significance: novel application of nonlinear Perron-Frobenius and fixed-point analysis to neural network training.  
- Strength — technical quality: rigorous, elegant proofs and a clean, checkable condition (ρ(A) < 1).  
- Strength — practicality: provides real-data experiments rather than purely theoretical results.  
- Weakness — restrictive assumptions: non-negative data and weights limit applicability to many real-world problems with centered or negative features.  
- Weakness — unconventional objective: adding the sum-of-outputs term is technical and its effect on decision boundaries and generalization is not fully explored.  
- Weakness — scalability concerns: ρ(A) depends on model/data (hidden units, max data norm), likely limiting scaling to larger models and high-dimensional data; provided bounds seem conservative.  
- Questions for authors: clarify the role/impact of the sum-of-outputs term, ways to handle negative-valued data (e.g., splitting features), practical effects of large p_u/p_w values, and guidance on choosing distinct α exponents.  
- Conclusion: significant theoretical advance with a practical proof-of-concept, but limited applicability due to strong assumptions; reviewer recommends acceptance.
