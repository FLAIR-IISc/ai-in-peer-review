### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel approach for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, guaranteeing convergence to the global optimum of a modified objective function. The core problem this work addresses is the highly non-convex nature of the standard neural network training objective, for which prevalent methods like SGD offer no guarantee of global optimality.

The proposed "nonlinear spectral method" applies to networks with non-negative weights, non-negative input data, and generalized polynomial activation functions. The training procedure is framed as finding a fixed point of an iterative map derived from the gradient of a modified objective function. The paper's main theoretical contribution is to show that, under certain conditions, this map is a contraction mapping with respect to the Thompson metric. This allows the application of the Banach fixed-point theorem to prove the existence of a unique global optimum and the linear convergence of the iterative algorithm to it.

A key feature of the method is that the condition for guaranteed convergence—that the spectral radius of a small, well-defined matrix is less than one—can be checked a priori, based on the network architecture and data properties. The framework is developed for single-hidden-layer networks and extended to two-hidden-layer networks. To demonstrate practical feasibility, the paper presents experimental results on several UCI datasets, comparing its performance to SVMs and standard ReLU networks trained with SGD.

**Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper presents a high-quality, theoretically sound piece of work. The mathematical development, which leverages concepts from nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed-point theory on convex cones, is rigorous and well-executed. The proof strategy of reformulating the optimization problem as a fixed-point problem and proving the iterative map is a contraction is elegant. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the provided lemmas and theorems. The authors are commendably transparent about the limitations of their approach, such as the conservative nature of their theoretical bounds and the restrictive model assumptions. The inclusion of experiments on real data, which is often missing from highly theoretical papers on this topic, strengthens the work by providing a proof of concept.

A point for consideration is the modified objective function. It includes terms (`+ sum(outputs) + epsilon * sum(weights)`) that are added for technical reasons—namely, to ensure the gradient remains in the positive orthant. While this is necessary for the theoretical machinery to work, a deeper discussion on the implications of optimizing this surrogate objective for the classification task would be beneficial. For example, how does maximizing this objective relate to minimizing the classification error? The experiments provide some empirical validation, but a more formal connection would enhance the work.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The inclusion of a "Proof Strategy" section at the end of Section 2 is very effective, as it provides readers with a high-level map of the technical arguments before they delve into the details of the proofs. The main results are stated upfront, which improves readability.

The topic is inherently technical and relies on mathematical tools that may be unfamiliar to a broad machine learning audience. While the density is necessary, the manuscript could be made more accessible. For instance, providing a more intuitive, high-level explanation for why the Thompson metric is the natural choice for this problem could help readers better appreciate the technical approach. The notation is dense but consistent. The algorithm is presented clearly, making the proposed method understandable.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The application of nonlinear spectral methods, and specifically the Thompson metric, to prove global convergence for neural network training is a novel and creative contribution. This approach provides a completely new perspective on the neural network optimization problem, distinct from the more common lines of research involving landscape analysis or tensor decomposition methods. The paper properly situates its contribution with respect to prior work on guaranteed training (e.g., Janzamin et al., 2015; Haeffele and Vidal, 2015) and clearly articulates its advantages in terms of practical feasibility and the simplicity of its required conditions.

**Significance:** The theoretical significance of this paper is substantial. It is one of the first works to present a practically implementable algorithm for training a non-trivial neural network that is guaranteed to converge to a global optimum at a linear rate. This is a powerful result that challenges the prevailing reliance on heuristics for non-convex optimization in deep learning. It opens up a new and promising research direction for developing algorithms with theoretical guarantees.

The immediate practical significance is more limited due to the restrictive assumptions of the model: non-negative data, non-negative weights, generalized polynomial activations, and a non-standard objective function. The experimental results, while valuable as a proof of concept, show performance that is competitive with but not superior to existing methods on small datasets. Furthermore, the scalability of the approach is a concern, as the condition `rho(A) < 1` becomes more difficult to satisfy for larger networks. However, these limitations do not detract from the importance of the foundational theoretical contribution. Future work building on these ideas could potentially relax some of these constraints.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novelty:** Proposes a highly original method for guaranteed global optimization of neural networks by connecting the problem to nonlinear spectral theory.
*   **Theoretical Strength:** Provides a rigorous proof of global optimality and linear convergence under checkable, a priori conditions.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, with a clear exposition of a complex theoretical argument.
*   **Impact:** Presents a significant theoretical advance that could inspire new research directions in optimization for machine learning.
*   **Completeness:** Includes an extension to two-layer networks and experimental validation on real datasets, demonstrating feasibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Practical Limitations:** The proposed model is restrictive (non-negativity constraints, specific activations) and may not be widely applicable in its current form.
*   **Performance:** Experimental results do not demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, though they are competitive.

**Conclusion:**
This is a strong paper that makes a significant and original theoretical contribution to the field. The establishment of a practical algorithm with guarantees for globally optimal training is a noteworthy achievement. While the practical applicability is currently limited by the model's constraints, the novelty and theoretical depth of the work make it an excellent fit for the conference. It is likely to stimulate further research into guaranteed optimization for neural networks.
