- Summary: Proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) with a fixed-point GΦ and spectral radius test ρ(A)<1 to globally train nonnegative-weight feedforward nets with polynomial activations; proves existence, uniqueness, and linear convergence for 1–2 hidden layers and shows fast convergence on small UCI datasets.
- Correctness: Analysis is careful and grounded in nonlinear Perron–Frobenius and contraction theory; technical conditions and mapping definitions are coherent and internally consistent.
- Empirical support: Experiments are limited to small, low-dimensional UCI datasets but demonstrate feasibility and linear convergence; performance is competitive with ReLU nets and below RBF-SVM.
- Clarity: Generally clear though dense; high-level flow and role of assumptions are explained, but test-time classification rule and effect of the added output-sum term need clearer exposition.
- Originality: Novel connection of multi-homogeneous PF theory to global training guarantees for a constrained NN family, with an unusual use of unit-specific activation exponents to break symmetry.
- Significance: Conceptually and methodologically valuable for globally trainable architectures, but constrained assumptions and modest empirical scope limit immediate practical impact.
- Strength: Provides the first practical global optimality guarantee for a nontrivial NN class with an explicit, checkable spectral condition and linear convergence.
- Strength: Extension to two hidden layers and explicit dependence of ρ(A) on hyperparameters with a recipe to enforce ρ(A)<1.
- Weakness: Strong modeling constraints (nonnegative inputs/weights, distinct power activations, modified objective) may harm statistical fidelity to standard setups.
- Weakness: Experimental scope is small with no medium/high-dimensional or image/text benchmarks despite claimed nonnegativity rationales.
- Weakness: ρ(A)<1 condition relies on conservative bounds; feasibility and trade-offs for larger networks/datasets are unclear, especially when forcing it via large pu, pw.
- Weakness: Lacks ablation studies (no comparison without output-sum term) and sensitivity analyses for scaling, α distribution, and norm radii.
- Weakness: Practical details missing on per-iteration cost, bias handling, preprocessing to nonnegativity, and numerical stability for real-power activations.
- Questions for authors: Show ablations removing the output-sum and ε-weight terms to quantify impact on accuracy and convergence.
- Questions for authors: How sensitive are ρ(A) and performance to feature scaling, and does rescaling materially improve convergence/accuracy?
- Questions for authors: Can biases be incorporated (e.g., constant positive input) without breaking guarantees?
- Questions for authors: How do choices of pu, pw (especially large values) affect optimization dynamics and final accuracy?
- Questions for authors: What is per-iteration complexity versus full-batch gradient descent, and any memory/numerical stability issues for large α or deeper nets?
- Questions for authors: Can the αl ≠ αm requirement be relaxed while preserving uniqueness (via alternative symmetry-breaking)?
- Relation to prior work: Positions well against tensor methods and structured factorization; should also discuss monotone/nonnegative network models and multiplicative-update methods (e.g., NMF) to clarify differences.
- Reproducibility: Algorithm and condition are specified and checkable; providing code, preprocessing details, and exact hyperparameters per dataset is needed for reproduction.
- Pros for acceptance: Solid, original theoretical contribution with a practical verification test and fast algorithm likely to inspire follow-up work.
- Cons for acceptance: Strong constraints, modified objective, and modest experiments limit practical impact; key empirical questions remain open.
- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept — strong theoretical novelty and rigor but needs stronger empirical validation, ablations, and clarity on statistical implications to be more compelling.
