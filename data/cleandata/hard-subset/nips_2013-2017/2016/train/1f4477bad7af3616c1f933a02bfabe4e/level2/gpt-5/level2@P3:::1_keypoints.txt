- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method that trains nonnegative, generalized polynomial feedforward networks to global optimality with linear convergence under a verifiable spectral condition.  
- Models require nonnegative inputs/weights, monomial activations (exponents αl ≥ 1), normalization, and a modified objective (negative cross-entropy + output-sum + small linear parameter term).  
- Iteration is a normalized gradient fixed-point map derived from multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction in the Thompson metric.  
- Global uniqueness and linear convergence hold when the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small nonnegative matrix A (size depends on layers) is < 1; framework extends to two hidden layers.  
- Theoretical development is careful and technically solid, with explicit assumptions and use of Collatz–Wielandt characterization to control Lipschitz constants.  
- The spectral condition is conservative but directly checkable; dependence on architecture and normalization parameters is analyzed.  
- Experiments are proof-of-concept: show linear convergence and non-trivial classification on several UCI datasets, but limited to low-dimensional data.  
- Empirical evaluation omits deeper analysis of the modified objective’s effects on probability calibration and decision boundaries.  
- Paper is generally clear mathematically, with an explicit algorithm, but missing explicit definitions for loss and prediction rule and could better explain the small linear term’s role.  
- Originality stems from combining multi-homogeneous nonlinear spectral theory with neural training to obtain a practical fixed-point algorithm and verifiable sufficient condition.  
- Significance: advances provable global training for a tractable neural subclass, but constraints limit direct applicability to mainstream architectures.  
- Pros: rigorous global optimality and linear convergence, practical step-size-free algorithm, extension to two layers, first empirical demonstration of globally optimal NN training on real data.  
- Cons: restrictive model constraints, nonstandard modified objective with unexplored implications, conservative spectral bounds limiting scalability, limited baselines/ablations and missing reported spectral radii.  
- Essential suggestions: explicitly state loss and prediction rule, ablate/remove the output-sum/linear term, report actual ρ(A) values and relate them to convergence, and clarify data preprocessing to ensure nonnegativity.  
- Important-but-optional suggestions: test at least one moderate-dimensional nonnegative dataset or provide complexity/timing analysis, add relevant baselines (polynomial-kernel SVM, nonnegative logistic regression), and provide sensitivity analyses for α and normalization parameters.  
- Recommend discussing possibilities for tightening spectral bounds or deriving data-dependent bounds to enable larger networks.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution with a practical, tunable-free algorithm and verifiable global guarantees for a constrained neural class; needs clarification and broader experiments to strengthen empirical claims.
