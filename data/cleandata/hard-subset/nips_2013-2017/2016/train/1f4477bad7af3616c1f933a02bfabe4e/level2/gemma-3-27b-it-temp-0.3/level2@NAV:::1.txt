## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) – with a guarantee of global optimality and a linear convergence rate. The core idea is to leverage nonlinear spectral methods, requiring non-negativity constraints on weights and a modified objective function. The authors address a significant challenge in deep learning: the non-convexity of the optimization landscape and the lack of guarantees for standard training algorithms like SGD.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical contribution. Providing a *provably* globally optimal training algorithm, even for a restricted network architecture, is a noteworthy achievement. The authors clearly articulate the assumptions required for their method to work (data non-negativity, network architecture parameters) and provide a relatively straightforward check (spectral radius calculation) to verify these conditions. The extension to two hidden layers, while briefly discussed, demonstrates the potential for broader applicability. The experimental results, though limited to UCI datasets, provide initial validation of the approach and show competitive performance compared to standard methods like SVM and ReLU networks. The comparison with SGD, illustrating the faster convergence of the proposed method, is also compelling.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The restriction to non-negative weights is a significant limitation, and the authors acknowledge this. While they argue it doesn’t severely impact expressiveness, the practical implications for many applications are unclear. The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, is dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with spectral methods to fully grasp. The experimental section feels somewhat underdeveloped. The choice of datasets is limited, and a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those designed for non-negative data, would strengthen the results. The discussion of the tightness of the bounds on the spectral radius and the impact of the number of hidden units could be expanded.  The paper also lacks a discussion of computational complexity, which is crucial for assessing the practicality of the method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to globally optimal training.
*   Clear identification of conditions for global optimality and a practical check for these conditions.
*   Initial experimental validation demonstrating competitive performance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Significant restriction to non-negative weights.
*   Limited experimental evaluation and lack of comparison with relevant baselines.
*   Dense theoretical analysis may limit accessibility.
*   Lack of discussion regarding computational complexity.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neural network optimization. While the limitations are significant, the theoretical results are compelling and warrant further investigation. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should expand the experimental section, provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity, and consider clarifying the theoretical analysis for a broader audience. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
