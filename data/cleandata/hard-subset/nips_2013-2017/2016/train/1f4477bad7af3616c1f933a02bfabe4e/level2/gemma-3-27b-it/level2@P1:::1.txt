This paper presents a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks, aiming for global optimality using a nonlinear spectral method. The core idea is compelling: by imposing non-negativity constraints on the weights and modifying the objective function, the authors achieve a provably globally optimal solution with a linear convergence rate – a significant result, especially given the generally non-convex nature of neural network training. The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction to the problem and related work, then delving into the theoretical foundations, and finally presenting experimental results.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantee:** The most significant strength is the theoretical guarantee of global optimality with linear convergence. This is a substantial contribution, distinguishing this work from methods relying on stochastic gradient descent, which often get stuck in local minima.
*   **Novel Approach:** The use of nonlinear spectral methods for neural network training is a fresh perspective and a departure from standard optimization techniques.
*   **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors attempt to break down the complex theoretical aspects into manageable sections.
*   **Experimental Validation:** Although limited in scope, the experiments demonstrate the feasibility of the method and competitive performance on UCI datasets, validating the theoretical findings.
*   **Detailed Analysis:** The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's dependence on model parameters and derive conditions for convergence.  The discussions regarding the bounds and their conservativeness are valuable.
*   **Extension to Two Hidden Layers:** The authors show how their approach can be extended to networks with two hidden layers, increasing the practical relevance of their work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Non-Negativity Constraint:** The requirement of non-negative weights is a significant limitation.  While justified by the theoretical framework, it restricts the expressiveness of the network and may hinder performance on datasets where negative weights are beneficial.  The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of the impact on representational power would be helpful.
*   **Modified Objective Function:** Adding the sum of outputs to the loss function is unusual and could potentially affect the learning dynamics. The justification provided is reasonable, but further analysis of its impact on performance and generalization would be valuable.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The spectral method’s computational complexity isn’t explicitly addressed. The authors mention challenges with high-dimensional feature spaces and the computational burden of the score function tensor in related work. It’s unclear how well this method scales to larger datasets and more complex networks. The current experiments focus on low-dimensional UCI datasets.
*   **Conservative Bounds:** The authors themselves admit that the derived bounds on the spectral radius are conservative. This suggests potential for improvement in the algorithm's efficiency and applicability to more complex architectures.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** While comparisons to SVM and ReLU networks are provided, a more in-depth comparison to state-of-the-art optimization methods (e.g., Adam, RMSprop) on the same datasets would strengthen the evaluation.  The limited performance gain over SVM is somewhat underwhelming.
*   **Clarity in Proofs:** The proofs are dense and rely heavily on notation. While mathematically sound, they could benefit from more intuitive explanations and intermediate steps.  The reliance on supplementary material for some proofs makes it harder to fully assess the technical correctness.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to training neural networks with a guarantee of global optimality – a significant advancement.
*   The experimental results, though limited, demonstrate the feasibility and competitive performance of the proposed method.
*   The detailed analysis and extension to two hidden layers showcase the authors’ thorough understanding of the problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The non-negativity constraint limits the practical applicability of the method.
*   The scalability concerns and conservative bounds raise questions about the algorithm's performance on larger and more complex datasets.
*   The comparison to baselines could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The guarantee of global optimality is a compelling result that sets this work apart. However, the limitations related to the non-negativity constraint and scalability are important considerations.  I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding scalability and providing a more detailed discussion of the impact of the non-negativity constraint.  Addressing the comparison to more modern optimizers would also strengthen the paper.



**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
