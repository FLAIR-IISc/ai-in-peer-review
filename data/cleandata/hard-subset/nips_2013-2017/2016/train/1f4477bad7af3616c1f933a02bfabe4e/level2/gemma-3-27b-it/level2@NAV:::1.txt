## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) – with a guarantee of global optimality and a linear convergence rate. The authors address a significant challenge in deep learning: the non-convexity of the optimization landscape. They achieve this by restricting the weights to be non-negative and formulating a modified objective function solvable via a “nonlinear spectral method.” The work builds upon recent advances in globally optimal neural network training, notably [8, 11], but aims to provide a more practical and readily applicable solution.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its theoretical guarantee of global optimality, a rare achievement in the field. The authors clearly outline the conditions under which this guarantee holds, relating them to the spectral radius of a relatively small matrix. This is a significant contribution, offering a potential path toward reliable and efficient training.  The experimental validation, though limited to UCI datasets, demonstrates that the method achieves competitive performance, and importantly, validates the theoretical findings. The inclusion of a comparison to SGD methods with varying learning rates (Figure 2) is particularly insightful.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The strong constraint of non-negativity on weights seems restrictive and may limit the expressiveness of the network. While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough investigation of the impact on performance and potential mitigation strategies would be beneficial.  The proofs, while presumably correct, are dense and rely heavily on notation, making them difficult to follow without a strong background in spectral theory and optimization. Furthermore, the dependency of the convergence guarantees on parameters like α and the selection of p, q (in the two-hidden-layer extension) isn't fully explored regarding practical implementation.  The comparison to other methods, while present, is not extensive; a comparison with more state-of-the-art techniques would strengthen the claim of usefulness. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*  Novelty: Provides the first practically feasible method for globally optimal training of a non-trivial neural network model.
*  Theoretical Rigor: The method is supported by a strong theoretical framework and a proven linear convergence rate.
*  Initial Experimental Validation: Demonstrates competitive performance on standard datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*  Restricted Applicability: The non-negativity constraint limits the flexibility of the model.
*  Proof Complexity:  Dense mathematical notation makes the technical details inaccessible to a broad audience.
*  Limited Scope of Experiments: The experimental validation is limited to low-dimensional datasets.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field.  Despite its limitations, the theoretical results and initial experimental success are promising. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, I suggest the authors provide more intuitive explanations of the key theoretical concepts, elaborate on the practical implications of the non-negativity constraint, and consider expanding the experimental evaluation with more complex datasets and comparisons.
