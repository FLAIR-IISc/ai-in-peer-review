This paper presents a method for globally optimally training generalized polynomial neural networks using a nonlinear spectral method. The central claim is that, under relatively weak assumptions, the proposed method achieves linear convergence to a global optimum, a result not previously demonstrated in a practically feasible manner. The approach restricts itself to one and two hidden layer networks and demonstrates competitive performance on UCI datasets.

The work is technically sound and addresses a significant challenge in deep learning: the non-convexity of the optimization problem. The authors build upon recent work in globally optimal training of neural networks [8, 11], but importantly, avoid the practical difficulties associated with those approaches, specifically the need for density estimation or checking complex non-degeneracy conditions. The introduction clearly positions the work within the current research landscape and motivates the proposed solution. The mathematical development, while dense, appears rigorous and builds logically toward the main theorem. The inclusion of a proof sketch in the main body and deferred proofs to supplementary material is reasonable. The experiments, although limited in scope (low-dimensional UCI datasets), provide initial validation of the theoretical claims and demonstrate that the method achieves competitive performance.

A significant strength of the paper lies in the theoretical guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence, a rare achievement in neural network training. The authors clearly articulate the assumptions required for this guarantee and provide a method for verifying these assumptions before running the algorithm. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory provides a solid theoretical foundation. The comparison to SGD in Figure 2 effectively highlights the speed of convergence achieved by the proposed method.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The non-negativity constraint on weights is a significant limitation and the justification for it (compatibility with the spectral method) feels somewhat unsatisfying. While the authors acknowledge this constraint, a more thorough discussion of its potential impact on expressivity and generalizability would be beneficial. The choice of activation functions (generalized polynomials) also feels restrictive. Although justified by the need for non-negativity, the paper doesn't thoroughly explore the impact of this choice on performance. The discussion of the bounds on pu and pw in relation to Theorem 1 and 5, while present, appears complex and could be simplified. The experimental evaluation is limited. The datasets are small and the comparison with other methods is not comprehensive. A more extensive evaluation on larger, more complex datasets, and a comparison with state-of-the-art optimization algorithms (e.g., Adam, RMSprop) would strengthen the claims. The reliance on cross-validation to select hyperparameters, while standard, makes it difficult to directly assess the benefit of avoiding hyperparameter tuning claimed in the introduction. Specifically, the statement "no careful tuning of the learning rate is required" is misleading as the parameters for the spectral method still require tuning.  Finally, the notation is heavy and could be simplified in certain sections, enhancing readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretically grounded method for globally optimally training a specific class of neural networks.
*   Offers a practical algorithm with a linear convergence rate.
*   Addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning optimization.
*   Clear presentation of the theoretical framework and assumptions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The non-negativity constraint on weights is a significant limitation.
*   Limited experimental validation on small datasets.
*   The mathematical notation is dense and could be simplified.
*   Claims of “no tuning required” are somewhat overstated.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neural network optimization. The theoretical results are strong and the proposed method offers a promising approach to achieving global optimality. However, the limitations related to the non-negativity constraint, restricted activation functions, and limited experimental validation raise concerns.  

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Provide a more thorough discussion of the impact of the non-negativity constraint on model expressivity and generalizability.
2.  Expand the experimental evaluation to include larger, more complex datasets and a comparison with state-of-the-art optimization algorithms.
3.  Simplify the notation and improve the clarity of the mathematical derivations, particularly regarding the bounds on pu and pw.
4.  Clarify the statement regarding hyperparameter tuning, acknowledging that the spectral method still requires parameter selection.
