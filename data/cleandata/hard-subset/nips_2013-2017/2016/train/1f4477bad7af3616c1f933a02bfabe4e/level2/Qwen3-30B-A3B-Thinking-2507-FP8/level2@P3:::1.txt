

This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for training a specific class of neural networks with global optimality guarantees under certain assumptions. The work addresses the non-convex optimization problem inherent in neural network training, which typically requires careful parameter tuning with no guarantee of reaching global optima. The authors claim their method is the first practically feasible algorithm providing global optimality guarantees for non-trivial neural network models.

The paper introduces a generalized polynomial neural network architecture with nonnegative weights and real-power activation functions, restricting to one or two hidden layers. The key theoretical contribution is a convergence guarantee showing the method achieves global optimality with linear convergence rate under conditions that can be verified by computing the spectral radius of a small matrix. The approach requires a modified objective function that includes a penalty term to ensure strict positivity of the gradient, and it imposes nonnegativity constraints on both weights and data.

The main strength of the paper lies in its theoretical contribution. The proof strategy, based on fixed point theory and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory, provides a rigorous foundation for the convergence guarantee. The authors successfully establish that the spectral radius condition can be checked without running the algorithm, which is a significant practical advantage over previous approaches. The method's lack of free parameters (compared to SGD with learning rate tuning) is also a notable benefit.

The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on UCI datasets, with the method outperforming ReLU networks on several datasets (Cancer, Haberman, Pima) while achieving results comparable to SVMs. The training speed is notably faster than SGD with linear convergence, as shown in Figure 2. The authors also provide a clear algorithm description and implementation details for both one and two hidden layer networks.

However, several limitations should be noted. The restriction to nonnegative weights is quite strong, potentially limiting applicability to many real-world problems where negative weights are essential. The generalized polynomial activation functions with real powers are unusual in deep learning, and the paper does not sufficiently explain why these are preferable to standard activation functions. The experimental scope is limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, which may not reflect the challenges of modern deep learning applications. The paper also lacks guidance on how to select the model parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) for optimal performance, making practical application difficult.

The paper's novelty is somewhat constrained by its specific architecture. While the theoretical approach is interesting, the restriction to nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations makes the method less applicable to the broader class of neural networks. The connection to previous work on tensor methods (Janzamin et al.) and structured matrix factorization (Haeffele & Vidal) is well-established, but the paper doesn't sufficiently address how its approach differs from or improves upon these in practical terms.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with a logical flow from theoretical development to algorithmic implementation. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the proofs are well-structured, though some technical details are relegated to the supplementary material. The experimental section is adequately described but could benefit from more detailed analysis of why certain parameter choices lead to better performance.

In terms of significance, the work represents an important theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization. However, its practical impact may be limited by the restrictive assumptions. The authors acknowledge this by noting their experiments should be viewed as a "proof of concept" and that they restrict to low-dimensional datasets due to the growth of the spectral radius with hidden units.

The paper's main contribution is the theoretical guarantee of global optimality for a specific class of neural networks with a practical algorithm. While the assumptions (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions) are restrictive, the work provides a valuable alternative perspective on neural network optimization that could inspire further research into structured optimization methods. The approach is particularly relevant for applications where count data is involved (e.g., images, word frequencies), as noted by the authors.

Overall, the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network optimization. The main limitation is the restrictive nature of the assumptions, which may limit practical applicability. The work would be strengthened by more thorough discussion of parameter selection, broader experimental validation on higher-dimensional datasets, and clearer articulation of the trade-offs between the theoretical guarantees and practical flexibility.
