Summary
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of a class of nonnegative feedforward neural networks with generalized polynomial activations. The networks use positive weights and nonnegative inputs; each hidden unit computes a positively weighted monomial with a unit-specific exponent αl ≥ 1 (distinct across units). The training problem maximizes a modified objective: negative cross-entropy plus an additive sum of all class outputs, together with a small positive linear term in the parameters to ensure strictly positive gradients on the positive orthant. Parameters are constrained to lie on fixed p-norm spheres, ensuring compactness. The main result shows that an explicit, checkable condition—spectral radius ρ(A) of a small nonnegative matrix built from network and data parameters—implies that a simple fixed-point iteration GΦ (a normalized positive gradient map) is a contraction in the Thompson metric, yielding a unique global maximizer with linear convergence. Extensions to two hidden layers are provided. Experiments on several UCI datasets show competitive accuracy relative to small ReLU networks and RBF SVMs, and faster convergence than SGD when optimizing the modified objective.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory for multi-homogeneous maps and contraction mappings in cones, bringing this machinery to NN training. Compared to tensor-based global methods for single-hidden-layer networks with square loss (e.g., Janzamin et al.) and global optimality via convex surrogates/regularization (Haeffele & Vidal), this work targets classification with cross-entropy-like objectives but under stringent structural constraints (nonnegativity, fixed-norm layers, polynomial activations, modified objective). Polynomial networks have been studied before (e.g., Livni et al.), but without global convergence guarantees. The paper positions itself well relative to [8, 11, 15], and to Perron-Frobenius references; a discussion of links to nonnegative matrix/tensor factorization and multiplicative-update style methods would further contextualize the approach.

Quality
– Theory: The fixed-point framework, use of Thompson metric, and multi-homogeneous PF theory are sound and well-motivated. The construction of A via derivative bounds and the contraction argument is plausible and carefully laid out. The requirement ρ(A) < 1 is explicit and checkable. However, the bounds used to build A are acknowledged to be conservative; as a result, the guarantees may apply only to relatively small networks (as also reflected by the experiments).
– Modeling assumptions: The guarantees hinge on (i) nonnegative inputs and weights, (ii) distinct αl ≥ 1, (iii) fixed p-norm constraints, and (iv) a modified training objective including a positive sum of outputs term (with non-negligible weight). These are substantial departures from standard NN training. The optimization guarantee is therefore for a different learning problem with restricted hypothesis class.
– Correctness of claims: The paper claims global optimality and linear convergence for the modified objective under ρ(A) < 1; this appears correct. The claim of “first practically feasible method” should be moderated: while computationally light and parameter-free at the iteration level, expressivity and data assumptions limit applicability, and multiple architectural hyperparameters still require CV.
– Empirics: Experiments are small-scale and meant as proof-of-concept. They demonstrate convergence speed and nontrivial performance, but do not convincingly establish practical superiority or scalability.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, with a clean separation of algorithm, main theorem, and proof strategy. Notation is heavy but mostly consistent. Some details are missing or hard to find:
– The exact cross-entropy form used should be written explicitly to clarify the interaction with the added sum-of-outputs term.
– The role and practical choice of ε > 0 should be discussed (numerical stability; sensitivity).
– Reporting actual values of ρ(A), iteration counts, and wall-clock times would make the convergence claims more tangible.
Minor typos and references need polishing.

Originality
The use of nonlinear spectral methods and contractions in the Thompson metric to obtain global convergence for NN-like models is novel. The specific architectural constraints and the new training objective differentiate it from other globally optimal approaches.

Significance
The work provides an interesting optimization perspective with rigorous guarantees and a checkable sufficient condition, potentially inspiring further research on globally convergent methods for constrained NN classes. However, the practical impact is limited by:
– Restricted data domain (nonnegative inputs) and positive weights.
– Necessity of distinct exponents per hidden unit.
– Modified objective that encourages large outputs across classes, whose statistical implications are unclear.
– Conservative spectral conditions that appear to limit network size.

Strengths
– Clear, rigorous theoretical framework with linear convergence to a unique global optimum.
– Explicit, a priori verifiable condition via spectral radius of a small matrix.
– Extension to two hidden layers.
– Empirical demonstration that the method trains nontrivial classifiers and converges much faster than SGD on the same objective.

Weaknesses
– Significant deviation from standard neural network training: altered objective, positivity constraints, fixed-norm constraints, and specialized activations.
– Limited empirical scope; no large-scale datasets; no runtime comparisons beyond epochs; no ablations (e.g., effect of sum-of-outputs term).
– Guarantees rely on conservative bounds; unclear scalability as n1, n2 grow.
– Requirement of distinct αl is an unnatural architectural constraint; guidance on choosing α is ad hoc.
– The “no free parameter” claim is at odds with the need to cross-validate α, numbers of units, and norm radii ρ.

Questions for the authors
1) Please write the exact cross-entropy used and derive the combined objective to clarify how the sum-of-outputs term interacts with class separation. Why is its coefficient fixed at 1 rather than tuned?
2) How sensitive is performance to ε? Can you quantify a safe practical range?
3) Can you report typical values of ρ(A), iteration counts to reach a given tolerance, and wall-clock times versus SGD (for both your objective and standard cross-entropy)?
4) How do you preprocess datasets with negative features? Is shifting/scaling acceptable, and how does it affect performance?
5) What is the effect of the distinct-α constraint? Do closely spaced α lead to numerical issues or near-non-uniqueness?

Suggestions for improvement
– Add ablation studies: remove or downweight the sum-of-outputs term; vary ε; compare with/without nonnegativity constraints (if feasible).
– Report the computed ρ(A), the chosen p’s, and discuss the tightness of the bounds; explore tighter problem-specific bounds to enable larger networks.
– Provide guidance on choosing α (e.g., a principled grid or distribution) and analyze sensitivity to α.
– Include runtime comparisons and scalability experiments (e.g., larger nonnegative datasets such as MNIST with pixel intensities).
– Discuss connections to NMF/multiplicative updates and other positive-map fixed point methods.
– Release code to facilitate reproducibility.

Pro/Con arguments
Pro:
– Solid theoretical contribution with clear, verifiable conditions and linear convergence.
– Novel application of nonlinear spectral methods to NN training.
– Simple, stable iteration without learning-rate tuning.
– First empirical demonstration (to my knowledge) of a globally optimal NN method producing nontrivial classifiers.

Con:
– Substantial modeling restrictions and a nonstandard objective limit applicability.
– Empirical evaluation is small-scale with modest accuracy; no clear advantage over strong baselines.
– Conservative conditions likely restrict network size; limited evidence of scalability.
– Hyperparameter selection (α, ρ’s) remains heuristic.

Overall assessment
Interesting and technically sound theoretical work that brings a fresh perspective to NN optimization under strong structural constraints. As a NeurIPS contribution, the significance is tempered by the departure from standard training objectives and limited empirical validation. I lean toward a weak reject in its current form, but would be enthusiastic if the authors can better justify the modified objective, demonstrate scalability, and provide stronger empirical evidence.
