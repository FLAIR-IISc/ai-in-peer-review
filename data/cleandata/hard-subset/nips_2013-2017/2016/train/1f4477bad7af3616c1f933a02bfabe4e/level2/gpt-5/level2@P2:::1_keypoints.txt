- Proposes NLSM for globally optimal training of nonnegative feedforward nets with positive weights, nonnegative inputs, and unit-specific polynomial activations under fixed p-norm constraints.  
- Uses a modified objective: negative cross-entropy + sum of class outputs + small positive linear term to ensure strictly positive gradients.  
- Main result: if the spectral radius ρ(A)<1 for a constructed nonnegative matrix A, the normalized positive-gradient fixed-point map is a Thompson-metric contraction with a unique global maximizer and linear convergence.  
- Provides an extension of the method to two hidden layers.  
- Experiments on UCI datasets show competitive accuracy with small ReLU nets and RBF SVMs and faster convergence than SGD on the modified objective.  
- Positions work relative to nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory and prior tensor/convex global methods, while noting distinct structural constraints.  
- Recommends further discussion of connections to NMF and multiplicative-update style positive-map methods.  
- Theoretical framework and contraction proof are sound and well-motivated, with an explicit, checkable condition ρ(A)<1.  
- Bounds used to construct A are conservative, so guarantees likely apply only to relatively small networks.  
- Strong modeling assumptions (nonnegative inputs/weights, distinct αl≥1, fixed-norms, modified objective) substantially restrict applicability.  
- Claims of global optimality and linear convergence for the modified objective under ρ(A)<1 appear correct.  
- The assertion of being the “first practically feasible method” should be tempered due to expressivity limits and hyperparameter needs.  
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale and proof-of-concept, not demonstrating practical superiority or clear scalability.  
- Paper is well-organized but notation-heavy; key details (exact cross-entropy form, role/choice of ε, reported ρ(A), iteration counts, and runtimes) are missing.  
- The application of Thompson-metric nonlinear spectral methods to NN training is novel and original.  
- Practical impact is limited by nonnegative-data requirement, need for distinct per-unit exponents, modified objective that increases outputs, and conservative spectral conditions.  
- Strengths: rigorous theory with linear convergence, an a priori verifiable spectral condition, extension to two layers, and empirical evidence of rapid convergence.  
- Weaknesses: nonstandard objective and constraints, limited empirical scope, conservative bounds limiting scalability, unnatural distinct-α constraint, and remaining hyperparameter tuning.  
- Questions for authors: specify exact cross-entropy and combined objective, sensitivity and practical choice of ε, report typical ρ(A)/iterations/wall-clock times, how negatives are preprocessed, and effects of distinct-α.  
- Suggested improvements: ablations on sum-of-outputs and ε, report computed ρ(A) and p choices, provide guidance and sensitivity analysis for α, include runtime/scalability experiments (e.g., MNIST), discuss NMF links, and release code.  
- Pro: solid, verifiable theoretical contribution, novel method, stable parameter-free iteration step, and first empirical global-opt NN producing nontrivial classifiers.  
- Con: substantial modeling restrictions, small-scale empirical results with modest accuracy, conservative conditions likely restricting network size, and heuristic hyperparameter selection.  
- Overall: technically interesting and rigorous but of limited practical significance in current form; reviewer leans to weak reject pending stronger justification of the objective, scalability, and empirical evidence.
