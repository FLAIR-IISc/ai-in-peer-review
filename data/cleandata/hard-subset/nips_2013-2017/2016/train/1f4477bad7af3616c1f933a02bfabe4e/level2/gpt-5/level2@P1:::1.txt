Summary:
This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for globally optimal training of a class of feedforward neural networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations. The networks operate on nonnegative features and optimize a modified objective consisting of negative cross-entropy plus the sum of class outputs and small linear terms in the weights. The main technical contribution is a fixed-point iteration GΦ, derived from normalized gradients on the positive cone, whose contraction is established under a verifiable spectral radius condition ρ(A) < 1 for a small, explicitly constructed nonnegative matrix A. Using the Thompson metric and multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory, the authors prove existence, uniqueness, and linear convergence to the global maximizer for one hidden layer, with an extension to two hidden layers. Experiments on small UCI datasets show competitive performance versus ReLU networks and somewhat below RBF-SVM; convergence is much faster than SGD on the modified objective.

Quality:
- Correctness: The analysis is careful and builds on established nonlinear Perron–Frobenius and contraction mapping theory. The mapping GΦ is well-defined on S++ with the technical additions ensuring strictly positive gradients; the use of ψp′ and normalization is coherent with the geometry of the cone. The matrix bounds and spectral radius condition are explicit, and the dependence on model/data parameters is discussed (monotonicity and conservative lower bounds). While some intermediate proofs are deferred, the structure appears sound and internally consistent.
- Empirical support: The experiments are limited but demonstrate feasibility and linear convergence in practice. The scope is small (low-dimensional UCI datasets), aligning with the conservativeness of current bounds.

Clarity:
- Generally clear, though dense. The high-level flow from optimization to fixed-point theory to contraction is well laid out. The role of each assumption (nonnegativity, α ≥ 1, distinct αl across units) is explained. A more explicit statement of the classification rule at test time (e.g., argmax over outputs) and a brief discussion of how the added output-sum term changes the loss landscape would improve readability.

Originality:
- The paper offers a novel training paradigm for a constrained yet nontrivial NN family, connecting multi-homogeneous PF theory and fixed-point contractions to global training guarantees. Relative to tensor-based methods (Janzamin et al.) and structured matrix factorization perspectives (Haeffele & Vidal), this method is different in assumptions, is more directly applicable, and provides a practical verification test (ρ(A) < 1) with linear convergence. The requirement of unit-specific activation exponents to break permutation symmetry is unusual and interesting.

Significance:
- The main value is conceptual and methodological: a practically checkable global optimality guarantee with a fast algorithm for a class of nonlinear networks. The constraints (nonnegative inputs and weights, modified objective, fixed-norm constraints, distinct αl) limit immediate applicability, and performance lags SVM on some tasks. However, this could seed further work on globally trainable architectures and improved bounds. Extending beyond small datasets and relaxing constraints would increase impact.

Strengths:
- Clear, rigorous fixed-point framework with explicit spectral radius condition and linear convergence rate.
- First practical global optimality guarantee for a (nontrivial) NN class under weak data assumptions (nonnegativity) and checkable model conditions.
- Extension to two hidden layers and detailed dependence of ρ(A) on hyperparameters; recipe to enforce ρ(A) < 1.
- Empirical evidence of fast convergence and reasonable accuracy.

Weaknesses:
- Modeling constraints: nonnegative inputs and weights, generalized power activations with distinct exponents, and a modified objective that adds the sum of outputs. The effect of these choices on statistical fidelity versus standard cross-entropy is insufficiently quantified.
- Experimental scope is small; no medium/high-dimensional benchmarks or image/text tasks despite the nonnegativity rationale (e.g., grayscale images, bag-of-words).
- The condition ρ(A) < 1 relies on conservative bounds; practical feasibility for larger networks/datasets remains unclear. The trade-offs when choosing very large pu, pw to force ρ(A) < 1 are underexplored.
- Lack of ablations: no comparison to the same model trained without the output-sum term; no sensitivity analysis to scaling of inputs (ρx), α distribution, and norm radii ρu, ρw.
- Practical details: computational cost per iteration, handling of biases, strategies to preprocess general data to nonnegative domains, and numerical stability for real-power activations are not fully discussed.

Questions for the authors:
1. How does adding the sum of outputs to the objective affect calibration and decision boundaries? Can you show ablations removing this term (and the ε-weight sum) to quantify its impact on accuracy and convergence?
2. How sensitive is ρ(A) and performance to feature scaling? If features are rescaled to reduce ρx, does this materially tighten the condition and improve convergence/accuracy?
3. Can biases be incorporated (e.g., via an additional constant positive input) without breaking the guarantees?
4. How do choices of pu, pw (especially very large values used to force ρ(A) < 1) affect optimization dynamics and final accuracy?
5. What is the per-iteration complexity versus full-batch gradient descent on the same model? Any memory or numerical stability issues with large α or deep extensions?
6. Can the requirement αl ≠ αm be relaxed while preserving uniqueness (e.g., with alternative symmetry-breaking constraints)?

Relation to prior work:
- The paper positions itself well relative to tensor approaches (Janzamin et al.) and structured factorization (Haeffele & Vidal). It would be helpful to mention related monotone/nonnegative network models and classical multiplicative-update methods (e.g., NMF), clarifying differences in guarantees and applicability.

Reproducibility:
- The algorithm is specified and the condition is checkable. Providing code, details on preprocessing to ensure nonnegativity, and exact parameter selections per dataset would greatly aid reproduction.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and rigorous global training framework with a practical verification test and linear convergence.
- Conceptual contribution likely to inspire follow-on work.
- Clear extension to two hidden layers and explicit parameter dependence.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong modeling constraints and modified objective limit practical impact relative to standard architectures.
- Experimental evaluation is modest; no demonstrations on larger or more representative domains.
- Some key empirical questions (effect of output-sum term, sensitivity to scaling/parameters) remain open.

Overall recommendation:
A borderline accept. The theoretical contribution is solid and original, and the method is practically checkable and fast, albeit for a restricted model class. Strengthening the empirical section with ablations and at least one higher-dimensional task, and clarifying the statistical implications of the modified objective, would make this a stronger submission.
