

This paper presents a theoretically grounded method for achieving globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights. The main claim is that their nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) provides the first practically feasible approach to achieve global optimality guarantees for neural networks, with linear convergence rates and no hyperparameter tuning required. The authors prove theoretically that under certain conditions (nonnegative data, specific weight constraints), their method converges to the unique global optimum for one and two hidden layer networks.

The paper's strengths lie in its strong theoretical foundation, building on Perron-Frobenius theory for nonlinear eigenproblems. The proofs for Theorems 1 and 5 are rigorous, with clear conditions for convergence (spectral radius of a small matrix < 1) that can be checked before training. The experimental results on UCI datasets demonstrate competitive performance against ReLU networks and SVMs, with NLSM1 and NLSM2 achieving strong results on several benchmarks like Cancer and Banknote datasets. The linear convergence rate shown in Figure 2 is particularly compelling compared to SGD variants.

However, the paper has notable limitations. The requirement for nonnegative weights significantly restricts applicability, as most real-world neural networks require negative weights to model complex patterns. This constraint makes the method less practical for many standard deep learning applications. The experimental section is limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets, with the authors acknowledging that their bounds grow with hidden units, making scaling to high-dimensional problems challenging. The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of how to select optimal parameters for real-world applications.

While the theoretical contribution is novel and significant for understanding neural network optimization, the practical limitations reduce the paper's impact. The authors could strengthen their case by exploring ways to relax the nonnegativity constraint or demonstrating performance on higher-dimensional datasets. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with proper references to related work in non-convex optimization and neural networks. Overall, this is a technically sound contribution with important theoretical insights, but its practical applicability is limited by the nonnegativity assumption. The paper would benefit from more extensive experiments and discussion of how to overcome this constraint.
