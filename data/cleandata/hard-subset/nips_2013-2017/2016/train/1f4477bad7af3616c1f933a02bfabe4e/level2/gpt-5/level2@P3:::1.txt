Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method that trains a class of nonnegative, generalized polynomial feedforward neural networks to global optimality with a linear convergence rate under a verifiable spectral condition. The models use nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and activations that are generalized monomials with exponents αl ≥ 1, and optimize a modified objective: negative cross-entropy plus a sum of outputs and a small positive linear term in the parameters to ensure strict positivity of gradients. The iteration is a normalized gradient map derived from multi-homogeneous nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction in the Thompson metric; global uniqueness and convergence follow when the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 for one hidden layer; K+2 for two layers) is strictly less than 1. The framework extends to two hidden layers, with analogous guarantees. Experiments on several UCI datasets demonstrate non-trivial classification performance and linear convergence compared to SGD.

This work situates itself among recent efforts providing global guarantees for neural network training. Compared with tensor-based methods for one-hidden-layer networks that require access to score functions and non-degeneracy conditions (Janzamin et al.), and convex-relaxation/structured-factorization results with limited practical instantiations (Haeffele & Vidal), the proposed approach offers a practical fixed-point algorithm with a directly checkable sufficient condition and observed competitive accuracy on real data. The approach builds on nonlinear Perron–Frobenius and multi-homogeneous maps, relating to a growing literature on spectral methods and contraction mappings in cones.

Quality:
The theoretical development is careful and technically solid. The path from the constrained optimization to a fixed-point map, the use of the Thompson metric, and the Lipschitz constant control via the Collatz–Wielandt characterization are well-motivated. The main assumptions and their roles are explicit: nonnegativity, distinct exponents to avoid permutation-induced degeneracies, and small linear positivity terms. The spectral condition is conservative but verifiable; the dependence of the spectral radius on architectural and normalization parameters is analyzed. The extension to two hidden layers mirrors the one-layer case.

On the empirical side, the experiments are clearly positioned as proof-of-concept. They verify linear convergence and provide a sanity check on expressivity and accuracy. However, the experimental scope is limited to low-dimensional datasets, and the modified objective’s effect on probability calibration and decision boundaries is not deeply examined.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear in its mathematical presentation, with helpful summaries of the fixed-point machinery and an explicit algorithm. Some places would benefit from clearer exposition: the exact definition of the negative cross-entropy loss and the prediction rule are not shown in the excerpt; the role and magnitude of the small linear term could be emphasized with more intuition. There are minor typos and formatting issues, and the rationale for choosing αl all distinct is important and well explained but could be highlighted earlier.

Originality:
The combination of multi-homogeneous nonlinear spectral theory with neural network training to obtain a globally optimal, linearly convergent algorithm under a simple spectral-radius condition appears novel. The construction of the iteration map and the verifiable sufficient condition through a small matrix A are original and practically appealing.

Significance:
The results advance understanding of globally optimal training in nonconvex neural models by identifying a tractable subclass with provable guarantees and a practical algorithm. The constraints (nonnegativity, generalized polynomial activations, normalization, and modified objective) limit direct applicability to mainstream architectures. Even so, the contribution is significant for theory and for broadening the design space of provably trainable networks.

Pros:
- Clear, rigorous theory culminating in global optimality and linear convergence with a checkable condition.
- Practical fixed-point algorithm with no step-size tuning; fast convergence in practice.
- Extension to two hidden layers; analysis of parameter dependence of the spectral condition.
- First empirical demonstration (to the best of current knowledge) of globally optimal NN training yielding non-trivial classification on real datasets.

Cons:
- Model constraints (nonnegative inputs and weights, generalized polynomial activations, distinct exponents, normalization) limit general applicability.
- The modified objective (adding sum of outputs and a small linear term) is nonstandard; implications for calibration and decision boundaries are underexplored.
- Conservative spectral bounds can limit scalability with number of hidden units; experiments restricted to low-dimensional datasets.
- Limited baselines and ablations; missing comparison to polynomial-kernel SVMs or nonnegative logistic regression, and missing reporting of the actual spectral radii used.

Suggestions for improvement:
Essential:
- Precisely state the loss and prediction rule (e.g., whether a softmax is applied) and discuss how the added output-sum term affects optimization and probability calibration. Include an ablation removing this term to illustrate its necessity for theory vs. its empirical effect.
- Report actual spectral radii ρ(A) for the selected models and relate them to observed convergence rates.
- Clarify data preprocessing to ensure nonnegativity (e.g., feature scaling to [0,1]) and discuss applicability beyond inherently nonnegative data.

Important but optional:
- Expand experiments to at least one moderate-dimensional nonnegative dataset (e.g., images normalized to [0,1]) to test scalability, or provide timing/complexity analysis with respect to units and classes.
- Add baselines closer to the hypothesis class: polynomial-kernel SVMs; nonnegative logistic regression; constrained nonnegative networks, to contextualize accuracy trade-offs.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for α, normalization radii (ρw, ρu, ρv), and pw, pu, pv, and show how these affect ρ(A), convergence, and accuracy.
- Discuss potential tightening of bounds leading to smaller A entries, enabling larger networks, and outline whether data-dependent bounds are feasible.

Overall assessment:
A strong theoretical contribution with a practical, tunable-free algorithm and verifiable global optimality for a well-defined, albeit constrained, neural class. The empirical section is modest but adequate for demonstrating feasibility and convergence. With clarifications on the loss/prediction and more thorough experimental characterization or tighter bounds, this work would be a compelling addition to NeurIPS.
