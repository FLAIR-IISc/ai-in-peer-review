- Novel method: introduces globally optimal training for a class of neural nets using nonlinear spectral methods by leveraging Perron–Frobenius theory.  
- Key innovation: restricts to nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial (real-power) activations to make the theory work.  
- Theoretical strength: provides a rigorous proof of global convergence with linear rates under specified conditions (nonnegativity, activation type, spectral-radius constraints).  
- Elegant proof strategy: connects neural-net optimization to fixed-point theory on convex cones and is technically careful.  
- Experimental results: competitive performance and faster training than SGD on several low-dimensional UCI datasets, but experiments are limited.  
- Scalability concern: paper does not address how the method scales to high-dimensional tasks (e.g., image recognition).  
- Major limitation: strong architectural constraints (nonnegative weights, specific activations) exclude standard models like ReLU networks and may limit expressiveness.  
- Insufficient justification of expressiveness: claim of adequate expressiveness supported only by a single toy example.  
- Practicality issue: parameter selection is done via random search and cross-validation (n1, α, ρw, ρu), which is impractical for real-world use without guidance.  
- Claim of being “first practically feasible” is overstated and not convincingly contrasted with prior work (Haeffele & Vidal 2015; Janzamin et al. 2015).  
- Missing analyses: needs deeper study of the relation between spectral-radius condition and performance, computational complexity, and clearer, less dense notation for broader accessibility.
