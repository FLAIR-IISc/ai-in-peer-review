- Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for training feedforward neural networks claiming global optimality and linear convergence under specific conditions.  
- Claims to be the first practically feasible method achieving global optimality for neural networks, addressing SGD's lack of global guarantees.  
- Method demonstrated on one- and two-hidden-layer networks and validated on multiple UCI datasets.  
- Strength: Novel theoretical contribution leveraging fixed-point theory and spectral radius analysis with rigorous convergence proofs.  
- Strength: More practically feasible than prior theoretically-guaranteed methods; demonstrated on real (low-dimensional) datasets.  
- Strength: Clear, rigorous mathematical foundation with articulated proofs and conditions for global optimality.  
- Strength: Experiments show competitiveness with SGD and kernel SVMs, linear convergence, and reduced hyperparameter tuning.  
- Strength: Authors explicitly discuss limitations, showing awareness of practical constraints.  
- Weakness: Experiments are limited to low-dimensional datasets, reducing generalizability to high-dimensional/complex tasks.  
- Weakness: Method requires nonnegativity constraints on weights and specific parameter choices (e.g., α), limiting broader applicability.  
- Weakness: Performance trade-offs—on some datasets (Iris, Banknote) unconstrained ReLU nets outperform and achieve zero test error.  
- Weakness: Theoretical framework is dense and may be hard for practitioners to implement or extend due to spectral radius and fixed-point reliance.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions focusing on expanded experiments, practical parameter guidance, and scalability discussion.  
- Requested revisions: expand validation to higher-dimensional datasets, provide practical parameter-selection advice and scalability strategies, and simplify theoretical exposition for accessibility.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solves a critical optimization problem with a novel, theoretically sound approach and provides a foundation for future globally optimal training methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow experimental scope and architectural/complexity constraints raise doubts about real-world applicability despite theoretical strengths.
