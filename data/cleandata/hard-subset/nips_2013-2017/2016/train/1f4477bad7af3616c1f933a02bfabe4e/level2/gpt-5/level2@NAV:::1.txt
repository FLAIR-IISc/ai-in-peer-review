Summary and relation to prior work: This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method to train a class of feedforward neural networks with generalized polynomial activations, nonnegative inputs, and nonnegative weights. The key claim is a globally optimal training algorithm with linear convergence under a checkable condition: the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A must be less than one. The method is framed via nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and contraction in the Thompson metric; it yields a unique global optimum and extends to two hidden layers. Empirically, the approach converges quickly and achieves competitive accuracy on small UCI datasets. The work is positioned against tensor-based global methods (e.g., Janzamin et al.) and structured factorization (Haeffele & Vidal), and relates to prior NIPS work on polynomial networks (Livni et al.) and optimization guarantees.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and mostly self-contained. The fixed-point mapping, contraction argument, and pre-run check via ρ(A) are solid, with proofs outlined and details deferred appropriately. However, guarantees hinge on strong modeling constraints (nonnegativity; added output-sum and epsilon terms; distinct exponents), and bounds appear conservative, potentially requiring large p that may affect model behavior. Experiments are limited in scale and scope.

Clarity: The paper is mathematically dense but generally well organized, with a clear proof roadmap and algorithm description. Some notation is heavy, references are inconsistently formatted, and practical guidance for choosing α, ρ, and p is limited.

Originality: High. Applying nonlinear spectral methods and multi-homogeneous PF theory to globally optimal NN training is novel, and the checkable linear-convergence condition is an attractive contribution.

Significance: Conceptually significant, demonstrating a pathway to practical global optimality beyond shallow special cases. Practical impact is currently constrained by modeling assumptions and limited empirical validation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel fixed-point training with global optimality and linear convergence.
- Simple, parameter-free iteration with a priori verifiable conditions.
- Extension to two layers; clear theoretical framework connecting to PF theory.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong restrictions (nonnegative data/weights, modified objective) and requirement of distinct exponents.
- Conservative conditions; unclear trade-offs from large p and normalization constraints.
- Empirical evaluation small-scale; performance often below SVM; limited analysis and ablations; incomplete references.

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide ablations on the output-sum term and epsilon; analyze sensitivity to α, p, ρ and tightness of A-bounds.
- Explore reparameterizations to handle signed data (e.g., shifts, monotone decompositions) and relax nonnegativity.
- Evaluate on larger nonnegative datasets (e.g., MNIST, bag-of-words) and compare to NMF/monotone networks and constrained-positive ReLU baselines.
- Automate parameter selection and release code for reproducibility.
