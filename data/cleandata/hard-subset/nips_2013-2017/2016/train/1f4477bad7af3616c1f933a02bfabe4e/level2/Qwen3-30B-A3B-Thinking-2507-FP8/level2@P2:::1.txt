

This paper presents a novel method for globally optimal training of a specific class of neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors claim to provide the first practically feasible algorithm that achieves global optimality guarantees with linear convergence rates for neural networks. The key innovation lies in restricting to nonnegative weights and using generalized polynomial activation functions (real powers), which allows leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory for nonlinear eigenproblems.

The paper's main strength is its theoretically sound contribution. The authors develop a rigorous proof showing that under certain conditions (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions, and constraints on the matrix spectral radius), their nonlinear spectral method converges globally to the optimum with linear rate. The proof strategy is elegant, connecting neural network optimization to fixed-point theory on convex cones. The theoretical framework is well-constructed, with careful attention to the technical details required for the convergence proof.

The experimental results are promising but limited. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on several UCI datasets (Table 1), showing that their constrained model can achieve good results despite the nonnegative weight constraint. The training speed (Figure 2) is notably faster than SGD with various learning rates, which aligns with the linear convergence guarantee. However, the experiments are restricted to low-dimensional datasets, and the paper doesn't address how the method scales to high-dimensional problems like image recognition, which is where neural networks typically shine.

The paper's primary weakness is its strong constraint on the network architecture (nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations). The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately justify why it's acceptable for practical applications. Standard neural networks with ReLU activations (which can model complex functions) are excluded by this constraint. The paper should more thoroughly address how the expressiveness of this constrained model compares to standard neural networks. The claim that "the resulting classifier is still expressive enough to create complex decision boundaries" (Figure 1) is supported only by a single toy example.

Another significant limitation is the lack of practical guidance for parameter selection. The authors randomly search over parameter spaces (n1, α, ρw, ρu) and select the best configuration via cross-validation. This approach isn't feasible for real-world applications where parameter search would be prohibitively expensive. The paper should provide concrete guidelines for choosing these parameters in practice.

The claim that this is "the first practically feasible method which achieves such a guarantee" needs stronger justification. The paper mentions prior work by Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015) but doesn't sufficiently explain why their methods aren't "practical" in the same way. The authors should more clearly articulate the practical advantages of their method over these alternatives.

The paper is technically sound but could be improved in several ways. The experimental section needs more depth regarding the relationship between the spectral radius condition and model performance. The paper also doesn't discuss computational complexity, which is crucial for practical adoption. The notation is dense and could be made more accessible to readers unfamiliar with fixed-point theory on convex cones.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization. The global convergence guarantee is significant, and the proof is technically rigorous. However, the strong architectural constraints limit the practical applicability of the method, and the experimental validation is insufficient for claiming broader significance. The paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of the practical limitations, better justification for the architectural constraints, and more comprehensive experimental evaluation on larger datasets.

The paper is well-written but suffers from excessive technical density in the proofs. Moving some of the intermediate results to the supplementary material is appropriate, but the main text could better emphasize the practical implications of the theoretical results.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting theoretical advance with limited practical applicability due to strong architectural constraints. While the global optimality guarantee is significant, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this method offers advantages over standard neural network training in practical settings. The claim of being "the first practically feasible method" is overstated given the severe constraints on the model architecture. The work would be significantly stronger if it addressed how to mitigate these constraints or demonstrated performance on more challenging problems.
