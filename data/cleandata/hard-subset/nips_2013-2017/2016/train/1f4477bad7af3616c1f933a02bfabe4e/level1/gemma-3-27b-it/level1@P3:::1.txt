This paper presents a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks, aiming for global optimality via a nonlinear spectral method. The core contribution lies in identifying a class of networks, under fairly mild assumptions (non-negativity of weights and data), for which training can be achieved with a linear convergence rate. This contrasts sharply with the typical challenges of non-convex optimization in deep learning. The paper builds on recent work attempting to provide global optimality guarantees for neural network training, like Haeffele & Vidal (2016) and Janzamin et al. (2016), but argues their methods are less practical due to computational complexity or restrictive assumptions.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction outlining the problem and positioning the work within the existing landscape. The mathematical development, while dense, appears rigorous and logically presented. The authors carefully lay out the necessary conditions for global optimality – a key strength. The experimental section, though limited in scope, provides initial evidence supporting the theoretical claims and demonstrates competitiveness with standard methods.  The inclusion of a detailed proof strategy outlining the steps taken is also appreciated. The extension to two hidden layers is a welcome addition.

**Strengths:**

* **Global Optimality Guarantee:** The primary strength is the theoretical guarantee of global optimality under reasonable assumptions. This is a significant departure from the typical heuristic nature of deep learning training.
* **Linear Convergence:**  The method’s linear convergence rate is a substantial advantage over iterative methods like SGD, which can suffer from slow convergence or getting stuck in local minima.
* **Clear Mathematical Formulation:** The problem is meticulously formulated, and the conditions for global optimality are clearly stated. The derivation appears mathematically sound.
* **Novelty:** The nonlinear spectral method represents a novel approach to neural network training. It's distinct from gradient-based methods and tensor decompositions.
* **Practical Feasibility (Claim):** The authors argue the method is more practically feasible than existing approaches with global optimality guarantees, a claim partially supported by the experiments.
* **Extension to Two Layers:** Demonstrating applicability beyond a single hidden layer improves the method's potential.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Non-Negativity Constraint:** The requirement of non-negative weights is a significant limitation. While justified in the context of the mathematical framework, it restricts the expressiveness of the network and potentially limits practical applicability. ReLU networks, while not directly comparable due to the different objective function, can model more complex functions. The authors acknowledge this, but its impact on real-world performance needs further investigation.
* **Modified Objective Function:** The addition of the sum of outputs to the loss function is somewhat ad-hoc. While theoretically motivated by the need for a strictly positive gradient, its practical effect on the learned representations and performance deserves more scrutiny.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to relatively small UCI datasets. While demonstrating proof-of-concept, they don’t provide sufficient evidence of scalability or performance on more challenging real-world problems (e.g., image classification, natural language processing). The comparison to SVM is relevant, but more comprehensive benchmarks are needed.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:** The authors acknowledge that the derived bounds for ensuring ρ(A) < 1 are likely conservative. This suggests there might be a larger region of the parameter space where global optimality is guaranteed, but currently unexplored.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t explicitly discuss the computational cost of computing the spectral radius and applying the nonlinear spectral method, especially for larger networks.  While claiming faster convergence than SGD, the per-iteration cost might be significant.
* **Choice of α:** The requirement for distinct α values for each hidden unit, while theoretically justified, seems somewhat restrictive and could limit the model's flexibility.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to neural network training with a strong guarantee (global optimality).
* The linear convergence rate is a significant advantage.
* The mathematical development is rigorous and clearly presented.
* The extension to two hidden layers demonstrates the potential for broader applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The non-negativity constraint is a significant limitation.
* The limited experimental evaluation doesn’t fully justify the claims of practicality.
* The computational cost of the method needs to be better characterized.
* The restricted choice of activation function and parameters (α) may hinder performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The authors have made a compelling case for the potential of nonlinear spectral methods to achieve global optimality in neural network training. However, the practical limitations (non-negativity constraint, limited experimental validation) raise concerns about its widespread applicability. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should:

1. **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include experiments on larger, more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, MNIST) to demonstrate scalability and performance.
2. **Analyze the impact of the non-negativity constraint:** Investigate how the performance is affected by this constraint and explore potential workarounds (e.g., using a slight regularization term to allow small negative values).
3. **Discuss computational complexity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the method, including the time complexity of computing the spectral radius.
4. **Investigate tighter bounds:** Explore methods for tightening the bounds on ρ(A) to expand the region of the parameter space where global optimality is guaranteed.
5. **Clarify the role of the added term:** Provide more intuition on how the summation of outputs affects the learning process and potentially explore alternative methods to ensure a positive gradient.
