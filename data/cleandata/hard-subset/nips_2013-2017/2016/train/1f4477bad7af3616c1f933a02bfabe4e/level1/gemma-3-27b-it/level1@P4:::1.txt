This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks. The core idea is to impose non-negativity constraints on the network weights and modify the objective function to ensure global optimality with a linear convergence rate, under certain conditions. The authors provide a theoretical analysis and preliminary experimental results on UCI datasets.

The paper clearly builds upon recent work attempting to provide global optimality guarantees for neural network training, specifically citing Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015). It positions itself as offering a more practically feasible approach than these prior works by avoiding the need to estimate the data-generating measure or check complex non-degeneracy conditions. It also acknowledges the broader context of deep learning optimization challenges and the lack of theoretical understanding of stochastic gradient descent. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory and nonlinear spectral methods is clearly stated as motivation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach, combining non-negativity constraints and a modified objective function with spectral methods, appears novel. The guarantee of global optimality with a linear convergence rate is a significant claim.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a substantial theoretical analysis, including lemmas and theorems, to support its claims. The derivation of conditions for global optimality and the analysis of the convergence rate demonstrate a strong mathematical foundation. The extension to two hidden layers is also a positive aspect.
*   **Clarity (in parts):** The main ideas are initially well-presented in the introduction and the core concepts of the method are relatively clear.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experiments on UCI datasets, despite being limited in scope, provides some initial validation of the approach. The comparison with SGD and SVM provides a baseline for evaluating performance.
*   **Detailed Proof Strategy:** The authors clearly outline their proof strategy, providing a roadmap for readers interested in the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity (overall):** While the core ideas are understandable, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The notation is dense and often lacks sufficient explanation. The flow of the mathematical derivations can be difficult to follow, even for readers familiar with the relevant background. Several jumps in logic occur without sufficient justification.
*   **Conservatism of Conditions:** The conditions for global optimality (specifically, the spectral radius constraint) may be overly conservative, as acknowledged by the authors. This raises concerns about the practical applicability of the method. The tight bounds on `pu` and `pw` seem overly restrictive and may limit the flexibility of the model.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to low-dimensional datasets and do not demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods like SVM. The limited parameter tuning described is also concerning. The statement about ReLU achieving zero test error needs further investigation to understand if it is a result of overfitting.
*   **Justification for Modifications:** The rationale behind adding the term summing the outputs to the objective function is not fully convincing and feels somewhat ad-hoc.
*   **α constraint:** The constraint that αi are distinct is strong and potentially limiting. The explanation given is insightful but it doesn’t address how to overcome this limitation.
*   **Missing References:** The paper could benefit from more thorough referencing. For example, the Thompson metric is introduced without substantial context.
*   **Lack of Discussion on Scalability**: The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the method and its scalability to higher-dimensional datasets and larger networks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to achieving global optimality in neural network training.
*   The mathematical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the conditions for convergence.
*   The experimental results, while limited, provide some evidence of the feasibility of the method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from significant clarity issues that hinder understanding.
*   The conditions for global optimality may be overly conservative, limiting practical applicability.
*   The experimental results do not demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The limited scope of experiments and parameter tuning raises concerns about the robustness of the findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but its lack of clarity and limited experimental validation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The novelty of the approach and the theoretical rigor are commendable, but the practical limitations and readability issues are significant drawbacks. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the clarity concerns, conducting more extensive experiments, and discussing the scalability of the method. The authors should also provide more justification for the modifications to the objective function and explore ways to relax the constraint on the α values.
