### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the highly non-convex problem of training neural networks, for which standard methods like stochastic gradient descent (SGD) offer no guarantees of finding a global optimum. The authors propose a "nonlinear spectral method" for training a specific class of feedforward neural networks, which they term "Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks." The core contribution is a provably convergent algorithm that, under checkable conditions, finds the unique global optimum of a modified objective function at a linear convergence rate.

The proposed network model uses generalized polynomial activation functions, requires non-negative weights and input data, and constrains the norms of the weight vectors. The optimization problem is reformulated to maximize a modified objective that includes the negative cross-entropy loss plus terms that sum the network's outputs and weights. This modification is crucial for the theoretical analysis.

The theoretical foundation of the method is drawn from the Perron-Frobenius theory for non-negative matrices and its extension to nonlinear maps on convex cones. The training algorithm is an iterative map, and the authors prove it is a contraction mapping with respect to the Thompson metric on the space of non-negative weights. By the Banach fixed-point theorem, this guarantees the existence of a unique fixed point, which corresponds to the global optimum. The condition for convergence, `ρ(A) < 1`, depends on the spectral radius of a small, explicitly defined matrix `A` whose entries are determined by the network's hyperparameters.

This work is positioned as a practically feasible alternative to prior attempts at globally optimal training, such as the tensor-based methods of Haeffele and Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015), which are noted to have significant practical challenges (e.g., requiring estimation of the unknown data-generating density). The authors provide experimental results on several UCI datasets as a proof of concept, demonstrating competitive performance with ReLU networks.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper's primary strength lies in its technical quality. The theoretical development is rigorous, and the proof strategy is elegant. The authors successfully connect the problem of training a neural network to the established mathematical framework of nonlinear eigenproblems and fixed-point theory on convex cones. The derivation of the conditions under which the iterative map `GΦ` becomes a contraction appears sound. The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) is powerful, as it provides an *a priori* check for guaranteed global convergence. The extension to two hidden layers is also provided, demonstrating the generality of the framework.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-structured and generally clear, especially given the technical nature of the material. The authors begin with the main result and algorithm, then systematically build the necessary theoretical machinery in Section 3 before applying it to their neural network model in Section 4. The proof strategy is laid out clearly at the end of Section 2. The notation, while dense, is consistent. However, the intuition behind the modified objective function could be explained more thoroughly. The inclusion of the `sum(outputs)` term is a significant departure from standard objectives, and a deeper discussion of its effect on the learned decision boundary and generalization properties would strengthen the paper.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. While the underlying mathematical tools (Thompson metric, Banach fixed-point theorem, nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory) are not new, their application to establish a globally optimal training algorithm for a class of neural networks is a novel and significant contribution. This approach provides a completely different perspective on the neural network optimization landscape, moving away from local gradient-based methods towards global fixed-point iterations. The proposed generalized polynomial network architecture, while specific, is also an interesting model class to analyze.

**Significance:**
The paper presents a significant theoretical breakthrough. It offers one of the first practically executable algorithms for training a non-trivial neural network with a guarantee of finding the global optimum. This is a fundamental problem in the field, and any progress is noteworthy. The linear convergence guarantee is also a very strong result compared to the sub-linear rates often seen for convex optimization or the lack of guarantees for non-convex problems.

However, the practical significance is tempered by several strong constraints on the model:
1.  **Non-negativity:** The requirement of non-negative data and weights severely restricts the model's applicability. Many standard datasets require feature normalization (like zero-centering) that would violate this assumption.
2.  **Modified Objective:** The objective function is non-standard. Minimizing loss is the typical goal, but this model also maximizes the sum of the network's outputs. The tension between these two goals and its impact on classification performance is not fully explored.
3.  **Scalability:** The authors rightly note that the condition for convergence (`ρ(A) < 1`) becomes harder to satisfy as the number of hidden units or layers increases. The experimental section is limited to small, low-dimensional datasets, which leaves the scalability of the approach as a major open question.

The experimental evaluation serves as a proof of concept but is not extensive. The performance is competitive but not superior to standard baselines like SVMs on most tasks. A more thorough empirical analysis exploring the trade-offs between the hyperparameters (e.g., `pw`, `pu`) required for the guarantee and the resulting model performance would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Theoretical Contribution:** The paper introduces a novel and powerful theoretical framework for analyzing and solving the neural network optimization problem, which could inspire significant follow-up research.
2.  **Guaranteed Global Optimality:** It provides a rare and valuable result—a practical algorithm with a guarantee of finding the global optimum for a non-trivial neural network model.
3.  **Linear Convergence:** The proof of a linear convergence rate is a strong theoretical property.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Restrictive Assumptions:** The constraints on the data (non-negativity) and the objective function (non-standard) may limit the model's practical impact and general interest.
2.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments are conducted on a small scale and do not thoroughly investigate the limitations or scalability of the proposed method. The performance, while reasonable, does not demonstrate a clear practical advantage over existing methods.

**Conclusion:**
This paper makes a very strong theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization. The originality of applying nonlinear spectral methods to guarantee global optimality is high, and the technical execution is solid. While the practical applicability of the specific model proposed is limited by its strong assumptions and un-explored scalability, the novelty and significance of the theoretical results are substantial. This work opens a new and promising research direction. Therefore, despite the practical limitations, the paper is a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
