This paper presents a novel approach to training Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) with a nonlinear spectral method, guaranteeing global optimality under certain conditions. The core idea is to impose non-negativity constraints on the network weights and modify the objective function to enable the application of Perron-Frobenius theory. The authors claim this leads to a linear convergence rate, a significant advantage over stochastic gradient descent (SGD) based methods. The work is well-motivated, addressing a key challenge in deep learning – the non-convexity of the optimization landscape.

The paper clearly builds upon existing research. It correctly identifies the NP-hardness of training even single-hidden-layer networks and references recent work aiming for global optimality, specifically citing Haeffele & Vidal (2016) and Janzamin et al. (2016).  It positions itself as a practical alternative to these prior approaches, arguing that their methods are either difficult to apply in practice (Haeffele & Vidal) or require estimating the data generating measure (Janzamin et al.). The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory and the related nonlinear eigenproblems is a central and novel aspect, offering a strong theoretical foundation.  The authors also correctly acknowledge related work in deep learning theory (e.g., Arora et al., 2016).

**Strengths:**

* **Global Optimality Guarantee:** The primary strength is the theoretical guarantee of global optimality under stated conditions. This is a significant contribution, as finding globally optimal solutions for neural networks is extremely challenging.
* **Linear Convergence:** The claimed linear convergence rate is another major advantage, suggesting rapid training compared to SGD.
* **Clear Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical grounding by leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory. The development of the fixed-point theory and the detailed analysis leading to Theorem 1 and 5 is commendable.
* **Novelty:** The combination of non-negativity constraints, a modified objective function, and the application of spectral methods is novel and offers a fresh perspective on neural network training.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experiments, albeit on relatively small UCI datasets, demonstrates the feasibility of the approach and provides a baseline for performance.  The comparison to SGD is insightful.
* **Extension to Two Hidden Layers:** Presenting initial results for two hidden layers expands the scope of the work and suggests potential for further generalization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Non-Negativity Constraint:** The requirement of non-negative weights is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, the impact on expressiveness and practical applicability needs further investigation. ReLU activations are not compatible, and it is unclear how this constraint affects performance on datasets where negative weights are beneficial.
* **Conservatism of Bounds:** The authors admit that the derived bounds for ensuring convergence (Equations 4 and 9) are likely conservative. This implies that the actual achievable performance might be better than indicated by the theoretical analysis.  Investigating tighter bounds would be valuable.
* **Scalability:** The computational cost of computing the spectral radius and performing the spectral iterations is not discussed in detail.  For larger networks and datasets, this could become a bottleneck. The computational complexity should be analyzed.
* **Limited Experiments:** The experiments are performed on relatively small, low-dimensional datasets. Scaling the method to more complex, high-dimensional datasets is crucial to demonstrate its practical value.
* **Objective Function Modification:** The addition of the sum of outputs to the objective function seems somewhat ad-hoc.  A more intuitive justification for this term would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to SGD and SVM is present, a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning models trained with advanced optimization techniques (e.g., Adam, RMSprop) would strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for globally optimal training of a specific class of neural networks.
* The guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence is a significant contribution.
* The work is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical foundations.
* The experimental results, though limited, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The non-negativity constraint significantly limits the applicability of the method.
* The scalability of the method to larger datasets and networks is unclear.
* The experimental evaluation is limited and does not include comparisons to stronger baselines.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling theoretical contribution. While the non-negativity constraint and potential scalability issues are limitations, the guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence is a significant achievement. The paper is well-written and clearly presents the underlying ideas. I recommend **acceptance**, but with the caveat that the authors should address the scalability concerns and provide more extensive experimental validation in future work.  I would also encourage them to explore ways to relax the non-negativity constraint, potentially by introducing a regularization term that penalizes negative weights.  The supplementary material should be carefully reviewed, and the clarity of proofs is crucial.
