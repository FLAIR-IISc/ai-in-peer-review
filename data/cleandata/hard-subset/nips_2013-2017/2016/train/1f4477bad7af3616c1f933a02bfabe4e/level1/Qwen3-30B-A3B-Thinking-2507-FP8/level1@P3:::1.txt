

# Peer Review: Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods

This paper presents a novel approach to training neural networks with global optimality guarantees, a significant challenge in deep learning. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method that achieves global convergence for a specific class of neural networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations. The approach builds upon Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, differing from prior work by Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2015) in that it doesn't require knowledge of the data-generating distribution, only nonnegativity of the data (which holds for many real-world applications like images and text), and provides a linear convergence rate. The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes conditions for global optimality with linear convergence.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical advance by providing the first practically feasible globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks. The proof strategy is sound, leveraging fixed-point theory and the Thompson metric to establish convergence guarantees. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory is well-motivated and well-executed.

2. **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The main theorem is stated precisely with all necessary conditions, and the proof strategy is logically presented. The authors effectively use supplementary material to handle technical proofs while keeping the main paper accessible.

3. **Practicality:** The method has no hyperparameters to tune (unlike SGD), and the convergence condition (ρ(A) < 1) can be checked before training. The experimental results demonstrate that the method converges rapidly (linear convergence rate) compared to SGD, which is a significant practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Strong Constraints:** The nonnegativity constraint on weights is a significant limitation that substantially reduces the model's expressiveness compared to standard neural networks. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper doesn't sufficiently address how this constraint impacts the model's ability to represent complex functions, especially for high-dimensional data.

2. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are restricted to low-dimensional UCI datasets, which are not representative of modern deep learning challenges. The paper doesn't demonstrate how the method scales to more complex tasks like image classification (e.g., CIFAR-10 or ImageNet). The competitive performance on UCI datasets is noteworthy, but the authors don't provide clear evidence that the global optimality guarantee translates to meaningful improvements in practice.

3. **Theoretical Bounds:** The bounds for the matrix A in Theorem 1 are conservative (as the authors acknowledge), which limits the practical applicability of the method. The authors note that "the 'effective' spectral radius is typically much smaller," but they don't provide a systematic way to determine when these bounds are tight.

4. **Parameter Selection:** The paper doesn't provide a principled approach for selecting the model parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu), relying instead on random search over a limited parameter space. This undermines the claim of being "practically feasible" since it requires extensive hyperparameter tuning.

**Significance and Originality:**
The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization, establishing a framework for global optimality that is both theoretically sound and practically implementable. The approach is sufficiently different from previous work to be considered original, particularly in its use of nonlinear spectral methods rather than tensor decomposition or structured matrix factorization.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by the strong constraints of the model. While the method provides global optimality guarantees, it's unclear whether these guarantees translate to practical advantages for real-world deep learning problems. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why global optimality is more important than other factors like model expressiveness, scalability, or generalization performance on large-scale problems.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach that advances our understanding of neural network optimization. While the constraints of the model limit its practical applicability, the method is a meaningful contribution to the theoretical foundations of deep learning. The paper is well-written and the proofs are sound.

The main weakness is the limited scope of the experiments and the strong constraints of the model, which prevent the authors from demonstrating the practical significance of their theoretical results. With more extensive experiments on larger, more challenging datasets and a better justification for the model's constraints, this paper would be significantly stronger.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Theoretical contribution is significant, well-motivated, and sound. Provides the first practically feasible globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks. Clear and well-organized presentation.

*Con:* Strong nonnegativity constraints limit model expressiveness. Limited experimental scope on small UCI datasets. Conservative theoretical bounds make practical application challenging. No principled approach for parameter selection.

Given the theoretical contribution and the paper's clear presentation, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should strengthen the discussion of the model's limitations and provide more concrete evidence of the practical significance of their method, particularly regarding the trade-offs between global optimality and model expressiveness.
