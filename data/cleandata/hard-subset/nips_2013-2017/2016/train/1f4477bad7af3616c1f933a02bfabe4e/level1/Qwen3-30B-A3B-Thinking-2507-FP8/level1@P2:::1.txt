

# Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to training a specific class of neural networks with global optimality guarantees. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method that, under certain conditions, achieves global optimality for feedforward neural networks with one or two hidden layers, using generalized polynomial activation functions with nonnegative weights. The work builds on recent NIPS papers by Janzamin et al. (2016) who proposed a tensor-based globally optimal algorithm but noted practical challenges, and Haeffele and Vidal (2015) who provided theoretical insights but with limited practical applicability. The authors' approach differs significantly by requiring only nonnegativity of weights (a common assumption for certain data types like count data) and using a modified objective function, avoiding the need for explicit data density estimation required by tensor methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation with a well-structured proof strategy. The main theorem (Theorem 1) establishes global optimality with linear convergence rate under verifiable conditions (checking spectral radius of a small matrix). The proof leverages advanced fixed point theory and nonlinear Perron-Frobenius theory, demonstrating technical depth.

2. **Novel Methodology:** The nonlinear spectral method represents a genuinely novel approach to neural network training, distinct from standard SGD and previous globally optimal methods. The authors successfully connect neural network training to nonlinear eigenvalue problems, providing a fresh perspective on the optimization landscape.

3. **Clear Experimental Validation:** The paper provides meaningful experimental results on standard UCI datasets, demonstrating competitive performance compared to ReLU-based networks and SVMs. The comparison with SGD variants shows clear advantages in convergence speed, as illustrated in Figure 2.

4. **Honest Assessment of Limitations:** The authors are transparent about the restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions) and acknowledge these limitations while showing their methods can still achieve meaningful results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Restricted Assumptions:** The nonnegativity constraint on weights and the specific activation functions (real powers) significantly limit the model's expressiveness compared to standard neural networks. The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate why these constraints aren't prohibitive for real-world applications. The claim that "these models are rich enough to achieve good performance" is somewhat weakly supported by the experimental results, which show only competitive (not superior) performance.

2. **Limited Practical Applicability:** While the paper claims to be "practically feasible," the requirement to compute the spectral radius of a matrix (A) for each model architecture adds complexity that may not be practical for real-world deep learning applications. The experimental section also notes the authors had to restrict to low-dimensional datasets due to computational constraints.

3. **Underdeveloped Connection to Practice:** The paper doesn't adequately address how practitioners would select parameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) for new datasets. The authors admit they "do not have yet a good understanding of how one should pick the parameters," which undermines the practical utility of their method.

4. **Comparative Analysis:** The comparison with ReLU networks is somewhat unfair, as ReLU networks allow negative weights while the authors' model does not. The paper would benefit from a more balanced comparison that acknowledges this fundamental difference in representational capacity.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong paper with a novel theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network optimization. The authors successfully establish a globally optimal training algorithm for a specific class of networks with verifiable convergence guarantees. However, the restrictive assumptions significantly limit the practical significance of the work.

*Pro-acceptance arguments:*
- Provides the first practically feasible globally optimal training method for neural networks with verifiable convergence guarantees.
- The theoretical contribution is substantial and well-executed, advancing our understanding of the optimization landscape.
- The experimental validation demonstrates that the method is not merely theoretical but produces meaningful results on real datasets.

*Con-acceptance arguments:*
- The restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, specific activation functions) substantially limit the applicability to standard neural network architectures.
- The practical utility is questionable given the need to compute spectral radii and the lack of clear parameter selection guidelines.
- The performance on standard benchmarks is only competitive, not superior, to existing methods.

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of neural network optimization. While the practical applicability is limited by its restrictive assumptions, the theoretical insight is significant and could inspire future work on globally optimal training methods. The paper meets the quality and originality criteria but falls somewhat short on significance due to the constrained model class. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better clarify the limitations and potential future directions for this line of work. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the restrictive assumptions might be relaxed in future work and a more nuanced comparison with standard neural network approaches that acknowledges the representational capacity differences.
