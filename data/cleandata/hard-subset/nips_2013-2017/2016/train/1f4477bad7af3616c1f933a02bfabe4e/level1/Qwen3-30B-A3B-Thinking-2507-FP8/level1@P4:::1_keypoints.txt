- Proposes a novel globally optimal training algorithm for feedforward networks with generalized polynomial activations and nonnegative weights using nonlinear spectral methods.
- Guarantees global convergence with a linear convergence rate under specific parameter conditions via Perron–Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems.
- Introduces a modified objective and nonnegative-weight constraint to enable practical implementation with theoretical guarantees.
- Theoretical development is rigorous: main theorem (Theorem 1) states clear conditions for global optimality and linear convergence, with well-structured proofs.
- Relates and differentiates from prior global-optimality work (Janzamin et al., Haeffele & Vidal), highlighting practical applicability and fewer assumptions.
- Provides extensions to deeper architectures (two hidden layers) demonstrating generality of the approach.
- Experimental results on UCI datasets support theoretical claims as proofs-of-concept and show competitive performance versus standard methods.
- Carefully discusses limitations, including the nonnegative weights constraint and restriction to low-dimensional datasets.
- Notes practical limitations: spectral radius grows with hidden units, limiting scalability to higher dimensions.
- Lacks a principled parameter-selection strategy (e.g., for α, n1), relying on random search in experiments.
- The modified objective (adding sum of outputs) requires clearer justification and practitioner-oriented explanation.
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides sufficient mathematical context for the intended audience.
- Claims novelty as the first practically feasible method providing global optimality guarantees for this class of networks.
- Significance lies in addressing fundamental training challenges and offering a theoretically grounded alternative to SGD with linear convergence and less hyperparameter tuning.
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, rigorous guarantees, novelty, and supportive empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: strong nonnegative-weight constraint, limited scalability, and lack of theoretical guidance for parameter tuning.
