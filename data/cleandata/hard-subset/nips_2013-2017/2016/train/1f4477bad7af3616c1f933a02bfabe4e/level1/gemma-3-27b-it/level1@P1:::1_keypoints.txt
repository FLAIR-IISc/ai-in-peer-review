- Presents a novel training method for Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) using a nonlinear spectral method with Perron-Frobenius theory.  
- Imposes non-negativity constraints and modifies the objective to enable spectral analysis and fixed-point theory.  
- Claims global optimality guarantees under stated conditions, addressing non-convexity in neural network training.  
- Claims linear convergence rate, offering faster training than SGD-based methods.  
- Builds on prior work (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.) and positions itself as a more practical alternative.  
- Provides solid theoretical development (Theorems 1 and 5) linking nonlinear eigenproblems to training.  
- Novel combination of constraints, objective modification, and spectral methods offering a fresh perspective.  
- Includes experiments on small UCI datasets that demonstrate feasibility and baseline performance versus SGD.  
- Presents initial extension/results for two hidden layers, suggesting potential generalization.  
- Major limitation: non-negativity constraint on weights restricts expressiveness and rules out ReLU compatibility.  
- Theoretical convergence bounds (Eqs. 4 and 9) are likely conservative and could be tightened.  
- Scalability concerns: computational cost of spectral radius computation and iterations is not analyzed.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited to small, low-dimensional datasets and lacks scaling studies.  
- Objective modification (adding sum of outputs) appears ad-hoc and needs better justification.  
- Lacks comparisons to stronger optimization baselines (e.g., Adam, RMSprop) and state-of-the-art models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound method with global optimality and linear convergence.  
- Arguments against acceptance: applicability limited by non-negativity constraint, unclear scalability, and limited experiments.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with caveats—authors should address scalability, expand experiments, explore relaxing non-negativity, and ensure supplementary proofs are clear.
