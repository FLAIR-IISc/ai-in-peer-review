This paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of a restricted but nontrivial class of feedforward neural networks with generalized polynomial activations and nonnegative data/weights. The core idea is to cast training as a fixed-point problem on a positive cone and to use multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and the Thompson metric to obtain a contraction mapping whose unique fixed point is the global optimizer. The global optimality and linear convergence rate hold under a checkable condition that the spectral radius of a small nonnegative matrix A (dimension K+1 or K+2) is less than 1. The paper treats one- and two-hidden-layer networks, requires distinct exponents per hidden unit, and optimizes a modified objective (cross-entropy plus a sum-of-outputs term and a small strictly positive regularizer to ensure positivity of gradients). Empirically, the method is competitive on small UCI datasets and converges in few iterations. The work is positioned against tensor-based global methods (Janzamin et al.) and matrix-factorization-based guarantees (Haeffele & Vidal), and relates to prior analyses of polynomial networks (Livni et al.).

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically solid, with a clear path from optimization to fixed-point theory and explicit Lipschitz bounds tied to ρ(A). The assumptions are strong: nonnegative inputs/weights, distinct exponents, normalization constraints, and a modified objective whose statistical implications are not fully analyzed. Bounds appear conservative, limiting applicability to small models; experiments confirm this.

Clarity: The manuscript is generally well written for a mathematically inclined audience; the proof roadmap is helpful. Some choices need clearer motivation and guidance (how to choose α, the role and limit of ε→0, practical computational cost per iteration, and tighter, data-dependent bounds on A).

Originality: The use of nonlinear spectral methods to deliver practically checkable global optimality and linear rates for neural network training appears novel and differentiates this work from tensor methods that require score functions or structured factorizations.

Significance: The contribution advances our understanding of globally optimal training under structural constraints and may spur further work on monotone/nonnegative networks. Practical impact is currently limited by model restrictions and modest empirical scope.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, rigorous theory with verifiable conditions and linear convergence
- Novel application of Perron–Frobenius tools to NN training
- Extension to two layers; algorithm is parameter-free once the model is fixed

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong constraints (nonnegativity, distinct exponents) and modified objective
- Conservative bounds restrict model size; limited empirical evaluation
- Missing discussion/comparison to related monotone/nonnegative models and NMF-based classifiers
- Limited guidance on hyperparameter selection and effect on expressivity/generalization
