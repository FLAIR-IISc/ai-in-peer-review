

# Review: Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods

This paper presents a novel approach for globally optimal training of neural networks using nonlinear spectral methods. The authors focus on feedforward neural networks with generalized polynomial activation functions (real powers) and nonnegative weights. The key contribution is a theoretically grounded algorithm that guarantees global convergence with linear convergence rate under certain conditions on the problem parameters. The authors establish conditions for global optimality by leveraging Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems and nonlinear eigenvalue problems on convex cones.

The paper relates to recent work on global optimality guarantees for neural networks, particularly the tensor-based approach by Janzamin et al. (NIPS 2015) and the structured matrix factorization approach by Haeffele and Vidal (ICML 2015). The authors clearly differentiate their work by noting that previous approaches either require knowledge of the data-generating measure (Janzamin et al.) or are "challenging to apply in practice" (Haeffele and Vidal). The key innovation here is the use of a modified objective function with nonnegative weights, which allows for a practical implementation with theoretical guarantees.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical foundation. The main theorem (Theorem 1) provides clear conditions for global optimality and linear convergence, and the proof strategy is well-structured. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach (nonnegative weights constraint, applicability to low-dimensional datasets) and provide concrete conditions for the spectral radius of a matrix to be less than 1. The experimental results on UCI datasets support the theoretical claims, though the authors acknowledge these are "proof of concept" results. The paper demonstrates appropriate care in evaluating both strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and consistent presentation. The main theorem is presented with appropriate supporting definitions, and the proof strategy is logically explained. The supplementary material appears to contain necessary technical details. Some readers unfamiliar with nonlinear spectral methods may find the mathematical background challenging, but the authors provide sufficient context for the intended audience.

**Originality:** This work represents a significant departure from standard neural network training approaches. The requirement for nonnegative weights and the modified objective function are novel constraints that enable the theoretical guarantees. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work on global optimality, claiming it is the first "practically feasible method" that achieves global optimality guarantees. The extension to two hidden layers (Section 4.1) further demonstrates the generality of the approach. The paper appropriately cites relevant NIPS and other conference proceedings to establish novelty.

**Significance:** The contribution addresses a fundamental challenge in neural network training—achieving global optimality with theoretical guarantees. While the method is currently limited to low-dimensional datasets due to the growth of the spectral radius with hidden units, it provides a theoretically sound foundation that could inspire future work. The experimental results show competitive performance with standard methods on UCI datasets, and the linear convergence rate offers a practical advantage over SGD-based methods that require careful hyperparameter tuning. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural network training.

**Strengths:**
- Provides the first practically feasible globally optimal training algorithm for neural networks with theoretical guarantees
- Demonstrates linear convergence rate without requiring careful learning rate tuning
- Clear mathematical framework with rigorous proof of convergence conditions
- Competitive experimental results on benchmark datasets
- Well-structured theoretical development with appropriate references to related work

**Weaknesses:**
- The nonnegative weights constraint is a significant limitation that may limit practical applicability
- Current implementation is limited to low-dimensional datasets due to spectral radius growth
- The choice of parameters for the model (α, n1, etc.) lacks a principled strategy beyond random search
- The modified objective function (adding sum of outputs) requires additional explanation for practitioners

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The theoretical contribution is significant and addresses a fundamental problem in neural network training
- The paper provides rigorous conditions for global optimality with linear convergence
- The approach is novel compared to previous work on global optimality for neural networks
- The experimental results demonstrate practical viability on benchmark datasets
- The paper is technically sound with clear presentation of mathematical concepts

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The nonnegative weights constraint is a strong limitation that may prevent practical adoption
- The current scope is limited to low-dimensional datasets, which restricts the practical impact
- The parameter selection strategy lacks theoretical justification and relies on random search
- The modified objective function may be difficult to justify to practitioners

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of neural network training. While the nonnegative weights constraint and current dimensionality limitations present practical challenges, the theoretical guarantees and linear convergence rate represent a significant advance. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the literature on global optimality in neural networks. The authors have provided a solid foundation for future work that could address the current limitations. The work aligns well with NIPS's focus on theoretical advances in machine learning and provides a meaningful step toward bridging the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical neural network training.
