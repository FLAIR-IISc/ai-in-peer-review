- Presents a theoretically grounded nonlinear spectral method with global optimality guarantees for certain feedforward networks using generalized polynomial activations and nonnegative weights.  
- Main theorem (Theorem 1) proves global optimality with linear convergence under verifiable conditions (spectral radius check of a small matrix).  
- Methodologically novel: connects neural network training to nonlinear eigenvalue problems, distinct from SGD and prior tensor methods.  
- Avoids explicit data density estimation required by tensor approaches, relying only on nonnegativity of weights.  
- Experimental validation on UCI datasets shows competitive performance versus ReLU networks, SVMs, and faster convergence than SGD variants.  
- Honest about limitations: restrictive assumptions (nonnegative weights, specific real-power activations) reduce expressiveness relative to standard networks.  
- Practical applicability is limited: computing spectral radius for each architecture adds complexity and experiments were confined to low-dimensional datasets.  
- Underdeveloped practical guidance: no clear method for selecting hyperparameters (n1, α, ρw, ρu) for new datasets.  
- Comparative analysis issues: comparisons to ReLU networks are imperfect since ReLU allows negative weights, affecting representational parity.  
- Pro-accept: first practically feasible globally optimal training method with verifiable guarantees and substantial theoretical contribution.  
- Pro-accept: method is not purely theoretical—demonstrates meaningful empirical results on real datasets.  
- Con-accept: restrictive assumptions and unclear practical workflow limit applicability to standard deep learning.  
- Con-accept: need to compute spectral radii and lack of parameter selection guidelines weaken practical utility.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to better clarify limitations, future directions for relaxing assumptions, and provide a more nuanced comparison to standard architectures.
