

# Peer Review: "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper proposes a novel approach for globally optimal training of neural networks with theoretical guarantees. The authors develop a nonlinear spectral method that achieves global optimality for feedforward neural networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activation functions (i.e., real powers rather than standard activations like ReLU). The key contribution is a theoretically grounded algorithm that provides linear convergence to the global optimum under specific conditions, which the authors show can be verified via the spectral radius of a small matrix. The method differs significantly from prior work by Haeffele & Vidal (2014) and Janzamin et al. (2015), which either lack practical applicability or require assumptions about the data-generating measure that are difficult to verify.

The paper makes important contributions to the understanding of neural network training, which has been largely dominated by heuristic approaches like SGD despite the well-known non-convexity of the optimization problem. Previous work has shown that training even single-layer networks is NP-hard (as cited in the paper), making the authors' approach to providing global optimality guarantees particularly valuable. Their method stands out as being both theoretically sound and practically feasible, which is a significant advance over prior work.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-structured theoretical foundation. The main theorem (Theorem 1) provides a rigorous proof of global optimality with linear convergence rate under reasonable conditions. The authors carefully establish the necessary mathematical framework, including connections to Perron-Frobenius theory and fixed point theory. The proof strategy is sound, using Banach fixed point theorem on the Thompson metric space. The experimental results on UCI datasets provide empirical validation, though the authors appropriately acknowledge these as "proof of concept." The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the nonnegative weight constraint and the focus on low-dimensional datasets. One minor weakness is that the paper doesn't fully explore the relationship between the choice of α (the activation exponents) and network performance, though this is noted as a direction for future work.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation. The authors effectively explain the motivation for their approach and how it differs from previous work. The theoretical development is logically structured, though the dense mathematical notation may challenge some readers. The presentation of the algorithm (in the appendix) is clear, and the experimental section provides useful visualizations (Figure 2) showing the linear convergence rate. A minor improvement would be to provide more intuitive explanations for why nonnegative weights and the specific objective function are necessary for their approach.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant original contribution by providing the first practically feasible method for globally optimal training of neural networks. The use of nonlinear spectral methods inspired by Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems is novel. The constraint of nonnegative weights is a key departure from standard practice, but the authors provide a compelling theoretical justification for this choice. The paper distinguishes itself clearly from previous work (citing Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al., and others) and shows how their approach overcomes practical limitations of prior methods.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. Global optimality in neural network training has been a long-standing challenge, and while previous work has provided theoretical insights, this is the first method that appears practically applicable. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on standard UCI datasets, which is notable given that prior globally optimal approaches (like Janzamin et al.) didn't include real-world results. The method's parameter-free nature (no learning rate tuning required) is particularly valuable for practitioners. However, the paper's significance would be strengthened by more extensive experiments on larger or more complex datasets, though the authors appropriately acknowledge this as a limitation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides the first practically feasible globally optimal training method for neural networks
- Theoretical guarantees of linear convergence to global optimum
- Conditions for global optimality are computable and practical
- Demonstrates competitive performance on standard datasets
- Clear theoretical framework connecting neural networks to nonlinear spectral methods

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The nonnegative weight constraint severely limits network expressiveness
- Experiments limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets (not addressing scalability)
- The relationship between α and performance is not well understood
- The spectral radius condition (ρ(A) < 1) might be too conservative in practice
- Limited comparison with state-of-the-art training methods beyond basic SGD

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of neural network training. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel and rigorous approach. While the nonnegative weight constraint is restrictive, the authors provide a compelling theoretical justification for it, and the method's practicality is demonstrated through experiments. The paper is well-positioned to be a significant contribution to NIPS, advancing both the theoretical understanding and practical applicability of globally optimal neural network training. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scalability concerns and provide more detailed discussion of the limitations of the nonnegative weight constraint.
