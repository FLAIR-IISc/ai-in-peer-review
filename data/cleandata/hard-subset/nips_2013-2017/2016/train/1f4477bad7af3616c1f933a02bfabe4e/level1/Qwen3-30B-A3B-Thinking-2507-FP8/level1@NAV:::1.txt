

This paper introduces a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of generalized polynomial neural networks with nonnegative weights and nonnegative data, presenting a theoretically grounded approach with linear convergence guarantees. The authors build upon recent work on global optimization of neural networks [8, 11], but significantly advance the field by providing the first practically feasible algorithm that guarantees global optimality under weak conditions (nonnegative data), whereas prior methods either required impractical conditions or lacked implementation details. The key insight connects neural network training to Perron-Frobenius theory for multihomogeneous problems, enabling a convergence analysis based on spectral properties of a small matrix.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with rigorous proofs establishing global convergence under easily verifiable conditions (spectral radius < 1), and the method has no free parameters unlike SGD. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured exposition and sufficient detail for reproduction. The authors properly contextualize their work against relevant NIPS literature [8, 11] and clarify how their approach differs from prior contributions.

Originality is high, as this represents the first practical globally optimal training method for neural networks. The significance is substantial for theoretical machine learning, though the restriction to nonnegative weights and data limits immediate applicability to standard deep learning problems. The experimental results on UCI datasets show competitive performance (slightly worse than SVMs but better than ReLU networks on some tasks), which is notable since prior global optimization work lacked empirical validation.

Strengths: First practical globally optimal neural network training method; strong theoretical foundation; clear presentation; empirical validation on real datasets. Weaknesses: Restrictive nonnegativity assumptions limit applicability; performance doesn't significantly outperform standard methods; limited to one/two hidden layers; unclear how nonnegative constraint affects model expressiveness for complex tasks.

The paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of neural network optimization. While the practical impact is currently limited by its restrictive assumptions, the method opens promising new directions for research in globally optimal training. Given NIPS's focus on high-quality scientific contributions, this paper should be accepted for its theoretical significance and rigorous approach, though the practical limitations should be acknowledged in the final version.
