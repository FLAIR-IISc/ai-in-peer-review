Introduces a novel nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) for training feedforward neural networks with guarantees of global optimality and linear convergence under certain conditions.  
Imposes nonnegativity constraints on weights and modifies the objective; proven for one- and two-hidden-layer networks.  
Theoretical results rely on fixed-point theory and the Perron-Frobenius framework.  
Experiments on UCI datasets show competitive performance vs SGD and kernel SVMs, with faster convergence and no hyperparameter tuning.  

Strength: Provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for global optimality in nonconvex neural network training.  
Strength: Method is computationally feasible and more practical than some prior global-optimality approaches.  
Strength: Experimental validation, though limited, demonstrates efficacy and linear convergence.  
Strength: Theoretical exposition and proofs are clear and accessible.  

Weakness: Experiments are limited to low-dimensional datasets, restricting generalizability to high-dimensional/real-world problems.  
Weakness: Architectural constraints (nonnegativity and specific generalized polynomial activations) reduce flexibility versus standard architectures.  
Weakness: Comparisons omit other global optimization methods and advanced neural training techniques.  
Weakness: Spectral radius condition and provided bounds are conservative and parameter-dependent, limiting scalability.  

Argument for acceptance: Significant theoretical contribution addressing a longstanding optimization challenge with practical implications.  
Argument for acceptance: Method is efficient, validated on real data, and well-presented.  
Argument against acceptance: Unclear applicability to high-dimensional/deeper networks and limited experimental scope.  
Argument against acceptance: Architectural restrictions and conservative parameter bounds may hinder practical adoption.  

Recommendation: Recommend acceptance, but authors should address scalability and model flexibility in future work.
