## Review of “Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods”

This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) – with a guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence. The authors tackle the challenging non-convex optimization landscape of neural network training by imposing constraints on the network’s weights (non-negativity) and modifying the objective function. This allows them to leverage nonlinear spectral methods, rooted in Perron-Frobenius theory, to find the globally optimal solution. The work builds on recent advances in globally optimal training (e.g., [8, 11]) but addresses the practical limitations of existing methods. 

The paper begins with a clear introduction outlining the motivation and contribution. The core technical development, detailing the nonlinear spectral method and the associated convergence theorem (Theorem 1), is presented rigorously. The extension to networks with two hidden layers (Theorem 5) demonstrates the method's potential for greater complexity. Finally, the experimental section provides preliminary results on UCI datasets, showing competitive performance and illustrating the fast convergence of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary strength is the proof of global optimality with linear convergence, a significant result in the field.
* **Novel Approach:** The application of nonlinear spectral methods to neural network training is a unique and promising direction.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with the theoretical derivations clearly explained.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge and address the practical challenges of applying their method, including parameter selection and convergence checks.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of experiments, even on limited datasets, provides initial evidence of the method’s effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Restricted Network Architecture:** The requirement for non-negative weights and the use of generalized polynomials limit the expressiveness and applicability of the method compared to standard neural networks with ReLU activations.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The matrix computations involved in the spectral method may become computationally expensive for larger networks and datasets, although this is only hinted at.
* **Conservative Bounds:** The authors acknowledge their bounds on convergence might be conservative and suggest potential for improvement. This raises questions about the true performance gains achievable.
* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** While the results are promising, the experiments are limited in scope, lacking comparisons to more complex datasets and state-of-the-art methods beyond SVM and ReLU networks. 



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution with strong guarantees.
* The proposed method offers a novel approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning.
* The initial experimental results are encouraging and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The limitations on network architecture restrict the practical applicability of the method.
* Concerns about scalability and computational cost need to be addressed.
* The experimental evaluation is limited and doesn't fully demonstrate the method's advantages.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a strong contribution with the potential to advance the field of neural network optimization. The theoretical results are impressive and the proposed method is a valuable addition to the toolkit for training specific types of neural networks. However, the limitations on network architecture and scalability require further investigation. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and a more thorough experimental evaluation, potentially including larger datasets and comparisons to more advanced training techniques.
