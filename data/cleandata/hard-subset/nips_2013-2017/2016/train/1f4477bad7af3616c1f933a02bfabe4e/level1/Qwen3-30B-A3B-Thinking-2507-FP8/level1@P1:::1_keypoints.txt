- Proposes a nonlinear spectral method for globally optimal training of feedforward networks with nonnegative weights and generalized polynomial activations.  
- Main contribution: a theoretically grounded algorithm with provable linear convergence to the global optimum under specific verifiable conditions.  
- Global optimality conditions can be checked via the spectral radius of a small matrix (ρ(A) < 1).  
- Method departs from prior work (Haeffele & Vidal, Janzamin et al.) by being more practically feasible and not requiring hard-to-verify data-measure assumptions.  
- Strong technical quality: rigorous proofs using Perron–Frobenius theory and Banach fixed point on the Thompson metric.  
- Theorem 1 provides a formal global optimality guarantee and linear convergence rate.  
- Experimental validation on UCI datasets demonstrates the method as a proof-of-concept and shows empirical linear convergence.  
- Method is parameter-free (no learning-rate tuning), which is practically attractive.  
- Key limitation: restriction to nonnegative weights reduces network expressiveness and deviates from standard practice.  
- Scalability concerns: experiments are limited to low-dimensional UCI datasets and do not address larger/complex datasets.  
- The relationship between activation exponents α and performance is not fully explored.  
- The spectral radius condition (ρ(A) < 1) may be conservative in practice.  
- Limited empirical comparison to state-of-the-art training methods beyond basic SGD.  
- Overall recommendation: strong theoretical contribution worthy of acceptance with minor revisions to address scalability and discuss nonnegative-weight limitations.
