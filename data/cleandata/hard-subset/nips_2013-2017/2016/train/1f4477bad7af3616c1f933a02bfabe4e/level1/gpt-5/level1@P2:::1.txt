Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) that provably trains a class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with linear convergence under a checkable sufficient condition. The networks use nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and generalized polynomial activations with exponents αl ≥ 1 (and pairwise distinct across hidden units). The objective departs from standard practice: it maximizes negative cross-entropy plus a sum over outputs and small positive linear terms in the weights, chosen to ensure strict positivity of gradients on the positive orthant. The central algorithm is a multihomogeneous fixed-point iteration GΦ that normalizes gradient directions with respect to Lp norms; convergence and uniqueness follow by showing GΦ is a contraction in a Thompson metric if the spectral radius ρ(A) of a small nonnegative matrix A (size K+1 for one-hidden-layer, K+2 for two) is <1. The authors provide explicit (albeit conservative) constructions of A and show how to pick pw, pu (and pv for two layers) to enforce ρ(A) < 1. They extend the framework to two hidden layers. Experiments on low-dimensional UCI datasets show competitive performance with small ReLU networks and slightly worse than RBF-SVM, and demonstrate faster, linear-rate convergence versus SGD variants.

This work connects to: globally optimal training via tensor methods [Janzamin et al., 2015] and through structured factorizations [Haeffele & Vidal], both of which have strong guarantees but limited practical reach; polynomial networks and their learnability [Livni et al., NIPS 2014]; and nonlinear Perron–Frobenius theory and contractions on cones. The novelty is a practically implementable spectral iteration with a simple, pre-checkable condition.

Quality:
- The theoretical development is careful and technically solid. The proof strategy (attainment in interior, critical points as fixed points of GΦ, contraction in Thompson metric, Banach fixed-point) is appropriate and well-structured. The dependencies in A are derived with attention to bounding derivatives via multihomogeneous structure; the refinement in Theorem 4 versus Theorem 1 is appreciated.
- Assumptions are clear: nonnegative data, nonnegative weights, αl ≥ 1 and distinct, norm constraints, and the modified objective. The authors are honest about the conservativeness of bounds and the role of the added terms in the objective.
- Some proofs are relegated to the supplement; from what is presented, the chain of lemmas is plausible. The existence/uniqueness and rate statements are precise with computable constants.
- Empirical evaluation is modest. While the method converges rapidly, the datasets are small and low-dimensional; no ablations quantify the impact of the added “sum of outputs” term or the positivity constraints on accuracy. Runtime comparisons and scaling behavior are not deeply explored.

Clarity:
- Overall clearly written for a mathematically inclined audience. The fixed-point map and metric choices are motivated. The dependence of ρ(A) on architecture and norms is discussed, with useful monotonicity insights and explicit conservative thresholds.
- Some presentation issues: the cross-entropy definition is incomplete in the typeset excerpt; several references are missing years or have typos; notation is heavy and occasionally inconsistent (e.g., switching between ℓ1 and p′u norms for ρx across sections). The need for distinct αl to avoid triviality is important and well explained.
- Algorithm box is helpful, but more implementation details (e.g., cost per iteration, handling of zero entries, data preprocessing to ensure nonnegativity) would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
- High. Applying nonlinear spectral methods and multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory to obtain a globally optimal training algorithm for (a nontrivial) class of neural networks with a linear rate and a simple pre-check is novel. The requirement of distinct exponents and positivity is unconventional but technically insightful. Compared to [11] and [8], this appears more directly implementable.

Significance:
- Conceptually significant: offers a new lens on nonconvex NN training with strong guarantees and a concrete, fast algorithm. The spectral-radius condition depending only on architecture is appealing.
- Practical significance is currently limited: the model class is constrained (nonnegative inputs and weights; specific activations; modified objective), and the contraction condition becomes harder to meet as hidden units grow, limiting scalability and expressivity. The empirical results, on small datasets, do not demonstrate advantages over strong baselines (SVM), though they are competitive with small ReLU networks.

Strengths:
- Clear, rigorous theoretical contribution with linear-rate global convergence under explicit, checkable conditions.
- Extension to two hidden layers.
- Simple algorithm without learning-rate tuning; fast empirical convergence.
- Good discussion of parameter dependence and conditions.

Weaknesses:
- Restricted setting: nonnegative inputs/weights, distinct α, modified objective; unclear how close the learned classifier is to standard cross-entropy solutions.
- Conservative bounds make ρ(A) < 1 more restrictive as network size grows; limited guidance on designing expressive yet contractive architectures.
- Limited experiments: small UCI datasets, no higher-dimensional images (e.g., MNIST with simple nonnegative preprocessing), no ablations on the added terms or positivity constraints, no wall-clock/runtime scaling study.
- Presentation/typos in references and some missing details.

Suggestions:
- Provide ablations isolating the impact of the “sum of outputs” and ε terms on both guarantees and accuracy.
- Demonstrate on at least one moderately high-dimensional dataset (e.g., MNIST with pixel intensities normalized to [0,1]), reporting whether practical ρ(A) values allow convergence and what accuracies result.
- Analyze the tightness of A and explore less conservative Lipschitz bounds to relax the contraction condition, perhaps data-adaptive rather than worst-case.
- Clarify the exact cross-entropy form used, preprocessing for nonnegativity, and the computational complexity per iteration versus SGD.
- Improve and complete references.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and rigorous approach to globally optimal training with linear convergence.
- Clear, principled use of nonlinear spectral theory; potentially influential direction.
- Simple, parameter-free iteration that converges fast.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical impact is not convincingly demonstrated; narrow applicability due to constraints.
- Experimental evaluation is too limited for NIPS standards.
- Conservative conditions may preclude more expressive architectures.

Recommendation: Borderline reject. The theory is elegant and original, but the empirical section and practical applicability need strengthening to meet the bar. With more substantial experiments, tighter bounds, and clearer discussion of the objective’s deviation from standard practice, this work could be impactful.
