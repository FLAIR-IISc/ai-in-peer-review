This paper presents a novel approach to training generalized polynomial neural networks (GPNNs) with a nonlinear spectral method, aiming for global optimality with a linear convergence rate. The core idea is to impose non-negativity constraints on the network weights and modify the objective function to enable the application of Perron-Frobenius theory. This is a significant attempt to address the non-convexity inherent in neural network training.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation and theoretical foundation. It builds upon recent work attempting to provide global optimality guarantees for neural network training (e.g., [8, 11]), but addresses the practical limitations of those methods. The authors acknowledge the challenges of estimating data generating measures ([11]) and propose a method relying only on data non-negativity, a weaker and more practical assumption. The connection to Perron-Frobenius theory is explained well, and the derivation of the main theorem is reasonably clear, though dense. The extension to two hidden layers is a welcome addition. The experimental section, while limited in scope, demonstrates the feasibility of the approach and provides a comparison to standard techniques like ReLU networks and SVMs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed nonlinear spectral method and the use of Perron-Frobenius theory in this context are novel and represent a potentially significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis and a guarantee of global optimality under specific conditions. The linear convergence rate is also a strong result.
*   **Practical Feasibility:** The method requires a relatively weak assumption (data non-negativity) and avoids the computationally expensive steps required by other globally optimal training algorithms (e.g., tensor decomposition in [11]).
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical derivations are presented in a logical order, although they are complex.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, although limited, demonstrate that the method can achieve competitive performance on UCI datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Assumptions:** The non-negativity constraint on weights is restrictive and may limit the expressiveness of the network. While the authors acknowledge this, the practical implications aren’t fully explored.
*   **Scalability:**  The matrix A in Theorem 1 and 5 grows with the number of hidden units, and the spectral radius calculation becomes a bottleneck.  The authors acknowledge this, and claim the bounds are conservative, but this needs further investigation.  The current experiments are limited to low-dimensional datasets.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of parameters (e.g., α, ρw, ρu) can significantly impact performance, and the guidelines for parameter selection are not fully developed. The paper mentions randomly generating and selecting the best configuration, but a more systematic approach is needed.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with other methods is somewhat limited. While SVM and ReLU networks are included, a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art techniques on larger, more complex datasets would be beneficial.
*   **Mathematical Density:** The theoretical sections are mathematically dense, making them challenging to follow for readers not specialized in spectral theory and matrix analysis. While the authors provide references, a more intuitive explanation of some concepts would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning – achieving global optimality in training.
*   The theoretical guarantees are strong and well-supported.
*   The method is practically feasible and offers a potential alternative to stochastic gradient descent.
*   The experimental results, while preliminary, are encouraging.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions (non-negativity constraint) may limit the applicability of the method.
*   The scalability concerns and parameter sensitivity require further investigation.
*   The mathematical complexity may make the paper inaccessible to a wider audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field and warrants acceptance. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the potential for practical application outweigh the limitations. However, the authors should address the scalability concerns, provide more guidance on parameter selection, and consider expanding the experimental evaluation in a revised version.  I rate this as a **Strong Accept** contingent on minor revisions to address the identified weaknesses, particularly providing more discussion and exploration around scalability and parameter selection.
