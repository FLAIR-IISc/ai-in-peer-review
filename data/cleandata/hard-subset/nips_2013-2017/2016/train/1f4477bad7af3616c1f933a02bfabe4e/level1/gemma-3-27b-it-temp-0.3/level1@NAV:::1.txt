## Review of "Globally Optimal Training of Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks with Nonlinear Spectral Methods"

This paper presents a novel approach to training a specific class of feedforward neural networks – Generalized Polynomial Neural Networks (GPNNs) – with guaranteed global optimality and a linear convergence rate. The authors leverage nonlinear spectral methods, building upon recent work in tensor decomposition and Perron-Frobenius theory to achieve this result. The core idea is to constrain weights to be non-negative and modify the objective function to enable global optimization via a spectral method. The paper clearly identifies the limitations of standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and positions its contribution as a significant step towards theoretically grounded, globally optimal neural network training.

The work builds upon and distinguishes itself from prior research like Haeffele & Vidal (2015) and Janzamin et al. (2016) by avoiding the computational challenges of tensor decompositions or the need to estimate data-generating measures. Instead, it relies on a readily checkable condition based on the spectral radius of a small matrix. The extension to two hidden layer networks is a welcome addition, demonstrating the potential for broader applicability. The experimental results, while limited to UCI datasets, provide initial evidence that the proposed method can achieve competitive performance despite the non-standard constraints.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantee:** The primary strength is the provision of a globally optimal training algorithm with a proven linear convergence rate – a significant contribution in a field often lacking such guarantees.
*   **Novel Approach:** The use of nonlinear spectral methods for neural network training is innovative and offers a fresh perspective on the optimization problem.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, with a clear explanation of the mathematical foundations and algorithmic steps.
*   **Practical Feasibility:** Unlike some prior work on global optimality, the authors claim and demonstrate (through experiments) practical feasibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Constraint on Non-Negativity:** The requirement of non-negative weights is a significant limitation, potentially restricting the expressiveness of the network and hindering performance on certain datasets.
*   **Limited Scalability:** The computational cost of the spectral method, particularly the matrix computations, may become prohibitive for large-scale datasets and complex architectures.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to relatively small UCI datasets. More extensive evaluation on larger, more challenging benchmarks is needed to demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the method.
*   **Conservative Bounds:** The authors acknowledge that the bounds derived for ensuring convergence may be conservative, potentially leading to unnecessarily restrictive parameter choices.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a fundamental problem in deep learning – optimization.
*   The guarantee of global optimality and linear convergence is a significant contribution.
*   The method is practically feasible and demonstrates competitive performance on benchmark datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The non-negativity constraint significantly limits the applicability of the method.
*   The scalability of the method to larger datasets and more complex architectures is uncertain.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited and does not fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neural network optimization. While the non-negativity constraint and scalability concerns are legitimate, the theoretical guarantee of global optimality and the demonstrated feasibility of the method warrant acceptance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically encouraging the authors to explore techniques for relaxing the non-negativity constraint and to conduct more extensive experiments on larger datasets.
