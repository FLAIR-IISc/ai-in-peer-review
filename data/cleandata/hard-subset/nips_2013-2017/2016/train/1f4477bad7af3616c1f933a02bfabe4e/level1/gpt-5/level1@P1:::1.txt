Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a nonlinear spectral method (NLSM) to train a class of feedforward neural networks to global optimality with linear convergence under verifiable conditions. The networks use nonnegative weights, nonnegative inputs, and generalized polynomial activations: each hidden unit applies a positive linear combination of inputs followed by an elementwise power with exponent αl ≥ 1, and the output layer is linear with nonnegative weights. The training objective modifies standard negative cross-entropy by adding (i) the sum of network outputs over samples and (ii) small positive linear terms in the weights; with Lp norm constraints on each layer’s weights, the method maximizes this objective.

The key technical contribution is to recast stationarity as a fixed-point equation GΦ(w,u)=(w,u) involving normalized gradients, and to prove that GΦ is a contraction on a product cone with the Thompson metric. A nonnegative matrix A, whose entries depend only on network hyperparameters (number of units, α, p-norms and radii) and coarse bounds on input norms, controls the Lipschitz constant. If the spectral radius ρ(A)<1, then (i) a unique critical point exists in the positive orthant, (ii) it is the global maximizer, and (iii) the iteration converges to it linearly from any positive initialization. The construction extends to two hidden layers with an analogous matrix A. The condition ρ(A)<1 can be checked a priori; conservative lower bounds on pw,pu (and pv for two layers) ensure the condition.

This connects to: (a) multi-homogeneous Perron–Frobenius theory and nonlinear eigenproblems (e.g., Lemmens & Nussbaum; Gautier et al.), (b) globally optimal approaches for specific networks (Janzamin et al. via score-function tensors; Haeffele & Vidal via structured factorization), and (c) polynomial neural networks (Livni et al.). Compared to [8,11], the proposed method is more practical: it avoids estimating score tensors and yields a simple fixed-point iteration without tuning. The price is a constrained architecture (nonnegativity; distinct α exponents) and a modified objective.

Strengths
- Novelty: A practically implementable, globally convergent training algorithm for a nontrivial NN class, with a priori, checkable sufficient conditions.
- Theoretical depth and rigor: Careful use of Thompson metric, Banach fixed-point theorem, and multi-homogeneous PF theory. Clear delineation of the role of positivity and normalization. Linear convergence with explicit rate bound ρ(A).
- Generality: Extension from one to two hidden layers; the framework appears extensible in principle to deeper nets.
- Practical aspects: No step-size tuning; convergence in few iterations in experiments; condition ρ(A)<1 depends only on architecture/data bounds and can be enforced by choosing large enough p’s.

Weaknesses
- Modeling constraints and modified objective: Requires nonnegative inputs and weights, power activations with all αl≥1 and pairwise distinct, norm constraints, and an additional “sum-of-outputs” term plus ε>0 linear terms to enforce strict positivity of gradients. The statistical meaning of this modified loss relative to standard cross-entropy is underexplored; potential bias introduced by the additive output term is not ablated.
- Conservativeness of conditions: The matrices A are upper-bounded via loose inequalities, likely forcing large p and small norm radii, potentially shrinking hypothesis class capacity. No empirical report of actual ρ(A) values vs. bounds.
- Limited empirical validation: Only small, low-dimensional UCI datasets; performance often below kernel SVM; no comparisons to polynomial kernel SVM, explicit polynomial features, or nonnegative/multiplicative-update baselines (e.g., NMF-inspired nets). No experiments on medium-scale tasks to test scalability.
- Clarity/Presentation gaps: Some equations (e.g., exact cross-entropy form) are missing or poorly rendered; the role and magnitude of ε, and how normalization constraints interact with classification calibration, deserve more discussion. Complexity per iteration and wall-clock comparisons are not provided.
- Practicality of input nonnegativity: For generic datasets with signed features, required preprocessing (e.g., feature splitting/offsets) is not discussed; negative inputs are problematic for non-integer α.

Evaluation against criteria
- Quality: The math appears sound; assumptions are stated; proofs are sketched with key steps and references, with some details deferred to supplementary. Experimental section is a proof of concept rather than a thorough study.
- Clarity: Generally well organized; exposition of the fixed-point framework and the metric is clear. However, the paper would benefit from cleaner equation rendering, a concise algorithm box with complexity, and more intuition for why the additive output term ensures positivity/contraction.
- Originality: High. Bringing nonlinear PF theory to global training of NNs with explicit, checkable conditions and linear-rate guarantees is novel.
- Significance: Potentially impactful theoretically, opening a path to globally optimal training under structural constraints. Practical significance is limited in current form due to architectural and loss constraints, conservativeness, and small-scale validation, but the idea could inspire follow-up work (tighter bounds, richer activations, better preprocessing).

Suggestions for improvement
- Report actual ρ(A) and the tighter A (Theorem 4) vs. coarse bounds, and show how they scale with architecture; include ablation on p-norm choices and norm radii.
- Compare against polynomial kernel SVM and explicit polynomial feature models with nonnegative constraints; include NMF-like multiplicative update baselines.
- Provide guidance for handling signed data (e.g., feature splitting), and discuss the effect of ε and the output-sum term; add an ablation removing this term to show necessity vs. performance impact.
- Include runtime and iteration counts vs. SGD for the same objective; show decision boundary plots beyond toy examples.
- Clarify the cross-entropy expression used, inclusion of biases, and how distinct α’s are chosen in practice.

Arguments for acceptance
- First practical global-optimality method for a class of NNs with linear convergence and a priori verifiable condition.
- Strong, elegant theoretical framework that may generalize and stimulate further research.
- Simple algorithm with no tuning and fast convergence.

Arguments against acceptance
- Architectural and objective constraints limit applicability; unclear statistical implications of the modified loss.
- Conservative conditions likely restrict capacity; no demonstration on higher-dimensional problems.
- Empirical evaluation is modest and lacks key baselines; performance often below strong nonparametric baselines.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept for its theoretical novelty and potential to inspire new globally convergent training methods, conditional on improved clarity and a more convincing empirical section.
