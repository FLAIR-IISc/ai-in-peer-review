Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time, first-order dynamics for convex optimization under Lipschitz gradients and convex constraints. It introduces a two-parameter family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) ODEs with generalized averaging weights, AMDw,η, where w(t) governs averaging of a mirror-mapped dual trajectory and η(t) controls gradient accumulation. The main technical device is a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), for a prescribed rate function r(t), yielding sufficient conditions on w and η to guarantee f(X(t))−f* = O(1/r(t)). The paper also proposes an “energy” function Er that decreases under the same conditions, provides primal-space formulations and examples (positive orthant and simplex, leading to an “accelerated replicator” ODE), and suggests an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts weights to further decrease Lr. A discrete algorithm for r(t)=t^2 is presented and compared empirically to gradient and speed restarts on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems.

The work is closely related to (i) the ODE viewpoints on accelerated methods (Su-Boyd-Candès), (ii) accelerated mirror descent in continuous/discrete time (Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett), and (iii) the Bregman Lagrangian / variational perspective (Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan), where families of ODEs achieve target rates via time-varying scalings. The paper’s generalized averaging perspective and the specific Lyapunov construction are in the same spirit; the conditions linking r, w, η mirror the α,β scheduling in [23]. The adaptive averaging heuristic is conceptually akin to existing restart strategies but operates by modifying the weight schedule.

Strengths
- Unified Lyapunov-based analysis giving simple sufficient conditions to realize a desired continuous-time rate 1/r(t). The connection r′/r ≤ w/W and η = r w/W is clean and recovers known quadratic rates as special cases.
- Clear primal/dual formulations; the accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex are interesting and potentially of independent interest to the game-theory community.
- The energy function Er provides additional intuition and may be useful beyond this work.
- Empirical comparisons suggest that adaptive averaging can outperform standard restarts on some toy problems, while preserving a decreasing Lyapunov in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The key sufficient condition (Theorem 2, condition 2) depends on the trajectory via ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w) ≤ 0 and is not verifiable a priori. The corollary that circumvents it enforces exact cancellation (η = r w/W), which removes adaptivity. The proposed adaptive rule (Sec. 4) departs from this exact condition and is only loosely justified.
- The discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is the critical object for practice, yet the main text contains no explicit theorem stating its convergence rate, assumptions, constants, or how the adaptive changes to ak preserve O(1/k^2). The connection between the continuous-time condition and the discrete trigger f(tilde xk+1) ≤ f(tilde xk) is heuristic. Without a clear, rigorous discrete-time analysis, it is difficult to endorse the method.
- Experiments are limited to R^3 simplex constraints and small toy objectives (quadratics, linear, KL), with performance measured in iterations. There is no study on higher-dimensional problems, standard machine learning losses (e.g., logistic regression, L1-regularized problems), wall-clock time, robustness to step size/β, or sensitivity to regularizer choices. Comparisons to strong baselines (FISTA/AFBS variants, other mirror-prox style accelerations) are absent.
- Several mathematical inconsistencies/typos reduce confidence. Example 1 appears to take a(t)=β/t (consistent with r′/r=2/t), but the text writes “a(t)=β t” and later computes integrals as if a(t)=β/t. Similarly, in Sec. 4, “η(t)r(t) = β t” when r(t)=t^2 and η(t)=β t is dimensionally inconsistent (η(t)r(t)=β t^3). These are nontrivial and should be corrected.
- The novelty over Wibisono et al. is incremental. The family AMDw,η with conditions on r, w, η and the Lyapunov argument are very close to the variational/Bregman Lagrangian perspective, which already delivers families of ODEs achieving target rates and higher-order discretizations. The main new ingredient is the specific adaptive averaging heuristic, which remains theoretically underdeveloped in discrete time.

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis is mostly sound and clearly presented. However, the trajectory-dependent condition and the reliance on a corollary for implementable schedules limit its practical impact. The discrete algorithm’s guarantees are deferred to the supplement and not summarized. The experimental methodology is too narrow to substantiate strong claims.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with helpful intuition. Nonetheless, the noted typos/inconsistencies in the key examples (a(t), η(t)) must be fixed. The mapping between continuous and discrete parameters (t=k√s, role of λk, γ, R) needs to be stated precisely in the main text, along with a theorem for Algorithm 1.

Originality
- Moderate. The generalized averaging perspective and accelerated replicator instantiation are nice, but the overall framework and the Lyapunov approach closely follow recent ODE/Bregman-Lagrangian work. The adaptive heuristic is a modest extension conceptually similar to restarts.

Significance
- Potentially useful if a rigorous and broadly applicable discrete-time analysis is provided and if empirical benefits extend to realistic problems. As is, the impact is limited by the scope of experiments and the lack of firm discrete guarantees.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, unified continuous-time analysis; recovers known schedules and offers a lens on acceleration via averaging.
- Accelerated replicator dynamics may interest communities beyond optimization.
- Adaptive averaging is a plausible practical idea and seems competitive with restarts on the reported tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient discrete-time theory for the proposed adaptive method; key claims not established in the main text.
- Experiments are small-scale, not representative, and lack rigorous ablations and strong baselines.
- Notational/mathematical inconsistencies.
- Incremental novelty relative to contemporaneous ODE/variational formulations.

Questions for the authors
1. Please provide a precise theorem (with assumptions and proof sketch) for Algorithm 1’s convergence rate in the main text. Under what regularity does the adaptive rule preserve O(1/k^2)?
2. Can you clarify and correct Example 1/2 (is a(t)=β/t?) and the statements in Sec. 4 (η(t)r(t))?
3. How should β and s be chosen/tuned in practice? What is the sensitivity?
4. Can you report results on higher-dimensional problems (e.g., logistic regression with simplex or orthant constraints) and compare against FISTA and fixed-schedule AMD?
5. Is there any guarantee for strongly convex objectives (e.g., o(1/k^2) or linear rates via adaptivity)?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The continuous-time perspective is solid and the adaptive idea is interesting, but the current discrete-time theory and experimental validation are not sufficient for NeurIPS. Strengthening the discrete guarantees, fixing inconsistencies, and expanding experiments would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
