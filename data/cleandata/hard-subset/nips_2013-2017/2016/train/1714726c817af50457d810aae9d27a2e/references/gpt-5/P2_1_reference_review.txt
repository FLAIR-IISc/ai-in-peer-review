Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated first-order dynamics for minimizing a smooth convex function over a closed convex set. It generalizes the accelerated mirror descent (AMD) ODE by introducing two positive weight functions, w(t) for averaging and η(t) for gradient accumulation, leading to a family AMDw,η. Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*) with a user-chosen rate function r(t), the authors derive simple sufficient conditions under which Lr decreases and obtain f(X(t)) − f* = O(1/r(t)). They also provide an “energy” function Er(t) that decreases under similar conditions. The paper gives a primal-only formulation, applies the framework to positive-orthant and simplex constraints, and identifies an “accelerated replicator” dynamic. Finally, it proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts w(t) along the trajectory to further decrease Lr and provides a discrete algorithm that aims to preserve the accelerated rate, with experiments comparing to gradient and speed restarting heuristics.

This work builds on and extends the continuous-time view of accelerated methods (e.g., Su-Boyd-Candès; Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan), the authors’ prior AMD ODE, and restart heuristics (O’Donoghue-Candès). Its angle is to decouple the averaging and gradient accumulation schedules and to design Lyapunov-based conditions that both unify and extend these flows, together with an adaptive averaging mechanism with guarantees.

Quality
- Theory: The main derivation—computing d/dt Lr, obtaining the bound in equation (3), and identifying sufficient conditions via η ≥ r′ and r − ηW/w interacting with ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩—is sound and transparent. The existence-uniqueness argument is standard under the stated Lipschitz assumptions. The corollary (η = a r with a = w/W and a ≥ r′/r) cleanly recovers O(1/t2) as a special case and allows r(t) = t^p. The energy function Er is well motivated and its monotonicity proof is plausible.
- Clarity/consistency issues: There are important notational inconsistencies and apparent typos that can confuse or compromise reproducibility:
  - Section 2 defines a(t) = w(t)/W(t). In Theorem 3’s proof, the relation ∇ψ*(Z) − X = (1/a) Ẋ is consistent with this definition, but later in Section 4 the coefficient is written as r(t) − η(t)a(t), whereas in (3) it is r(t) − η(t)W(t)/w(t) = r − η/a. To zero this term, one needs a(t) = η(t)/r(t), not a(t) = η(t) r(t) as written.
  - Example 1 for r(t) = t^2: the text states a(t) = β t, but the subsequent integral is computed as if a(t) = β/t. The latter is the correct choice since r′/r = 2/t.
  - In Section 4, “η(t) r(t) = β/t” is dimensionally inconsistent for η(t) = β t and r(t) = t^2.
  These should be fixed and the a(t) definition kept consistent throughout.
- Discretization: The discrete algorithm is only sketched in the main text. Important elements (choice/role of R, mapping t = k√s, conditions ensuring the discrete Lyapunov decrease and rate) are deferred to the supplement. As presented, it is difficult to fully verify the claimed rate preservation or to reimplement.
- Experiments: The experiments are illustrative but limited (small-dimensional simplex, synthetic objectives). They do confirm monotonicity of Lr for adaptive averaging and show improved empirical performance over restarts in the examples shown, especially in the strongly convex case. However, broader validation (higher dimensions, non-simplex constraints, larger-scale convex ML tasks) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with clear motivation, a unifying Lyapunov-based analysis, and helpful primal forms and examples. However, the notational slips around a(t), η(t), and the sign of the key coefficient materially hinder readability and should be corrected. Reference formatting is inconsistent in places.

Originality
- The unifying AMDw,η family with two independent weight functions and the Lyapunov design that yields a target O(1/r(t)) rate is a neat generalization of prior ODE viewpoints.
- The identification and analysis of an accelerated replicator ODE under the mirror map for the simplex is interesting and likely of independent appeal to communities working with replicator dynamics.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic that provably preserves the Lyapunov decrease (and thus the target rate) is conceptually simple yet, to my knowledge, novel relative to standard restarts, which typically lack such guarantees in the non-strongly convex case.

Significance
The framework offers a clean way to derive and analyze accelerated flows with generalized averaging, potentially informing new discrete algorithms. The adaptive averaging idea is a practical contribution that can complement or replace restarts. While the current experiments are modest, the conceptual advance is likely to be of interest to optimization researchers and may inspire further algorithmic developments, including beyond first order.

Pros
- Clear and general Lyapunov framework yielding O(1/r(t)) rates under simple conditions.
- Flexible design knobs (w, η) with constructive examples for r(t) = t^p.
- Primal formulations and concrete constrained examples; accelerated replicator is appealing.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantee to preserve the designed rate.
- Empirical comparisons to common restart heuristics show promising improvements.

Cons
- Notational inconsistencies and typos in critical places (a vs 1/a; example parameters).
- Discrete algorithm description and guarantees are not self-contained.
- Experimental scope is limited; no large-scale or stochastic evaluations.
- Assumptions (global Lipschitz ∇ψ*) may exclude common mirror maps on domains with boundaries unless carefully handled.

Questions and suggestions
- Please correct the a(t) and Example 1 inconsistencies and keep the definition a = w/W uniform. State explicitly the condition needed to zero the (r − ηW/w) term.
- Provide a more self-contained description of Algorithm 1 and its convergence proof in the main text, or at least a precise theorem statement with assumptions.
- Discuss how to choose β and sensitivity to this hyperparameter; include an ablation.
- Expand experiments to higher dimensions and to other constraint sets (e.g., box, l1-ball), and include at least one standard convex ML task.
- Clarify the domain assumptions ensuring ∇ψ* is Lipschitz and how boundary behavior is handled.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a useful generalization and a simple, theoretically grounded heuristic that appears effective. With the suggested clarifications and expanded empirical validation, it would make a solid contribution to the NeurIPS optimization track.

Confidence: Medium.
