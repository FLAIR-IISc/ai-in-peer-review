Summary
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) through the lens of generalized averaging. It introduces a two-parameter family of ODEs AMDw,η where w(t) controls the averaging weights in the primal and η(t) scales gradient accumulation in the dual. Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors provide sufficient conditions under which Lr is non-increasing, yielding a convergence guarantee f(X(t))−f* ≤ const./r(t). They show how to construct w and η to achieve a target rate r(t) (e.g., t^2). They also introduce a second “energy” function Er that decreases under similar conditions, and provide a primal representation of AMD that encompasses replicator dynamics on the simplex and generalizes prior ODE families (e.g., Wibisono et al.). Finally, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts the averaging weights online to decrease the Lyapunov derivative, and present a discrete algorithm whose experiments (in low-dimensional simplex problems) show favorable comparisons to gradient- and speed-based restart heuristics.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on continuous-time views of acceleration (Su-Boyd-Candès, Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan) and on AMD (Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett). The novelty is to parametrize acceleration via generalized averaging and to design an adaptive rule for w(t) that preserves Lyapunov decrease (and hence the accelerated rate) without restarts. The primal forms connect to classical mirror descent ODEs (Nemirovski & Yudin) and to replicator dynamics. The paper appropriately cites Attouch et al. on inertial systems and the common restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès; Su et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: The parametrization AMDw,η with Lyapunov Lr gives a clean template linking target rates r(t) to weight schedules via a(t)=w/W.
- Constructive conditions: Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 yield explicit recipes (η=a r, a≥r’/r) to ensure Lyapunov decrease and attain rate 1/r(t); the reparameterization via a(t)=w/W is elegant and useful.
- Primal representation and examples: Clear derivations for orthant and simplex; the accelerated replicator dynamics are of interest to evolutionary game theory and constrained optimization.
- Adaptive averaging: The proposed heuristic is principled (drives down d/dt Lr) and preserves the rate guarantee in continuous time; the discrete scheme is reasonable and compared empirically to standard restarts.
- Energy function: Er provides additional insight into the dynamics as potential+kinetic energy and appears robust empirically across methods.

Weaknesses and points to clarify
- Notational inconsistencies/typos that affect understanding:
  • Section 2, Examples 1–2: a(t) must satisfy a(t) ≥ r’(t)/r(t) = p/t. The text says “a(t)=β t” but all subsequent algebra uses a(t)=β/t. Please correct.
  • Section 4: To nullify the trajectory-dependent term in Theorem 2, one needs η W/w = r, i.e., η = a r with a=w/W. The text states “set a(t)=η(t) r(t)” which contradicts earlier definitions; it should be a(t)=η(t)/r(t) or equivalently η=a r.
  • The proof of Theorem 3 uses ∇ψ*(Z)−X = (1/a) Ẋ, consistent only if a=w/W. Please standardize this throughout.
  • Algorithm 1: The expression for λk is unclear (“λk = √(s a_k) + √(s a_k)”). Likely λk = √(s a_k)/(1+√(s a_k)) as in prior accelerated mirror descent; please fix.

- Scope of guarantees:
  • The continuous-time analysis is solid, but the rate guarantee for the discrete adaptive algorithm is deferred to the supplement. Given that the paper’s contribution hinges on adaptive averaging, a concise main-text theorem for the discrete scheme (even with simplified assumptions) would strengthen the work.
  • The trajectory-dependent condition in Theorem 2 is addressed in Corollary 1 by choosing η=a r. The adaptive rule in Section 4 switches a(t) based on the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩; in discrete time this is approximated by testing f(x̃(k+1)) ≤ f(x̃(k)). A justification bridging this test to the sign of ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩ (or to a descent inequality ensuring discrete Lyapunov decrease) would make the heuristic more principled.

- Experimental evaluation:
  • Experiments are limited to R^3 simplex problems and qualitative plots. Please include quantitative metrics (best objective vs iterations, time; number of restarts; area under curve), sensitivity to step size and β, and higher-dimensional tests (e.g., l1-ball or box constraints, Euclidean mirror map) to support broader applicability.
  • Fairness of hyperparameter tuning across methods (step sizes, restart thresholds) should be specified.
  • Since the claim is that adaptive averaging preserves rates without sacrificing robustness, a strongly convex case with linear-rate targets r(t)=e^{μ t} (and an appropriate discrete analogue) would be compelling, or at least a discussion of why the current framework recovers linear rates in continuous time.

- Relation to variational/Bregman Lagrangian perspective:
  • You state AMDp generalizes Wibisono et al.’s ODE with specific α,β choices. It would be helpful to explicitly map a(t), r(t), η(t) to α(t), β(t), and to comment on whether your adaptive rule has a variational interpretation (e.g., time-varying damping) and how it compares to restart schedules in that framework.

- Minor technical comments:
  • Existence/uniqueness: You assume ∇ψ* Lipschitz. Clarify the required smoothness and strong convexity of ψ ensuring this, especially on domains with boundaries (simplex/orthant). You partially address it, but a precise assumption block would help.
  • Choice of z*: The Lyapunov term Dψ*(Z,z*) depends on z*. For multiple minimizers, Lr need not go to zero; discussing how this affects the discrete diagnostics would be useful.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear theoretical framework linking averaging schedules to accelerated rates with Lyapunov guarantees.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with rate preservation; practical alternative to restarts.
- Insightful connection to replicator dynamics; broadened perspective on accelerated dynamics.
- Clean, reusable analysis (Theorem 2/Corollary 1) that others can build upon.

Cons for acceptance
- Several notational errors/confusions that obscure key conditions.
- Discrete-time guarantees are not prominent in the main text; experimental scope is limited.
- Incremental relative to existing continuous-time acceleration frameworks; novelty centers on adaptive averaging rather than new rates.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. With corrections to notation, a succinct discrete-time theorem, and strengthened experiments, this would be a solid contribution. Even as is, the adaptive averaging idea is interesting and practically relevant, and the continuous-time analysis is technically sound and clearly presented.
