

# Review: Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics

## Summary

This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated convex optimization, building on the recent trend of interpreting discrete optimization algorithms as discretizations of continuous-time ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The authors generalize the Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics introduced in Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015), introducing a family of ODEs parameterized by two weight functions w(t) and η(t) that control the averaging scheme and gradient accumulation rate, respectively. The paper provides a unified Lyapunov function analysis to establish convergence rates under appropriate conditions on these weight functions. A key contribution is the introduction of an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts the weight function to improve performance while preserving the convergence rate guarantees. The authors demonstrate their approach on specific examples, including an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics for simplex constraints.

The paper builds on important prior work by Su et al. (NIPS 2014) on continuous-time analogues of Nesterov's acceleration, and Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) on the connection between acceleration and averaging. The current paper extends these ideas by providing a more general framework for adaptive averaging, which is a significant improvement over the fixed-weight schemes previously studied.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs that follow standard mathematical conventions in optimization. The Lyapunov function approach is appropriately applied to establish convergence rates, and the authors carefully justify the conditions under which their results hold.

The main strength of the paper is its unified framework for analyzing a broad class of accelerated dynamics. The authors successfully generalize previous work on accelerated mirror descent (Krichene et al., 2015) by introducing a flexible weight function w(t) and providing sufficient conditions for convergence. The adaptive averaging heuristic is particularly well-motivated and theoretically grounded, as it preserves the Lyapunov function property while adapting to the trajectory's progress.

The numerical experiments are adequate for the scope of the paper, though limited to a few examples in R^3. The experiments properly compare the adaptive averaging heuristic against existing restarting heuristics (O'Donoghue & Candès, 2015; Su et al., 2014) and demonstrate that adaptive averaging outperforms these methods in the tested examples, particularly for strongly convex problems. However, the paper would have been strengthened by more extensive numerical validation across diverse problem types and higher dimensions.

One minor weakness is that the connection to discrete-time algorithms is somewhat brief, though the authors mention a discrete version in the supplementary material. The paper could have provided more explicit discussion of how the continuous-time results translate to practical discrete algorithms, especially for readers interested in implementation.

## Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the flow from introduction to theoretical results to examples is logical. The introduction provides excellent context for the problem and clearly articulates the paper's contributions.

The authors effectively use examples (positive-orthant and simplex constraints) to illustrate their framework, particularly the connection to replicator dynamics for the simplex constraint, which is a nice application to evolutionary game theory. The adaptive averaging heuristic is described with sufficient detail to understand its operation.

The paper is dense with mathematical content, which is appropriate for a theoretical contribution, but some readers might find certain proofs (e.g., Theorem 1) challenging without more intuitive explanation. A brief discussion of the intuition behind the Lyapunov function choice would have been helpful. The supplementary material appears to contain additional details that would aid understanding, which is a good practice.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on continuous-time optimization dynamics. While building on prior work by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) and others, it provides a novel generalization of the averaging scheme in accelerated mirror descent. The key innovation is the adaptive averaging heuristic, which dynamically adjusts the weight function to improve performance while preserving convergence guarantees.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by providing a unified framework that encompasses previous fixed-weight schemes as special cases and introducing a new adaptive mechanism. The connection to replicator dynamics for simplex constraints is particularly novel and demonstrates the broad applicability of the framework.

The authors have adequately referenced related work, including the key papers on continuous-time acceleration (Su et al., 2014; Krichene et al., 2015). The paper would have been stronger with a more detailed comparison of the adaptive averaging heuristic to other adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam, though these are not directly comparable as they target different optimization contexts.

## Significance

The significance of this work lies in its theoretical contribution to understanding accelerated optimization methods through a continuous-time lens, and the practical implications of the adaptive averaging heuristic. The unified framework provides deeper insight into the relationship between averaging and acceleration, which has been observed in prior work (Flammarion & Bach, 2015) but not systematically analyzed in continuous time.

The adaptive averaging heuristic addresses an important practical challenge: how to improve the performance of accelerated methods without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. The paper demonstrates that this heuristic outperforms existing restarting heuristics in the tested examples, which is promising for practitioners. The connection to replicator dynamics also opens up potential applications in evolutionary game theory and other domains where these dynamics are studied.

While the paper's primary contribution is theoretical, the adaptive heuristic has clear potential for practical application in accelerated optimization algorithms. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the growing body of work on continuous-time perspectives of optimization, which has been an active area at NIPS in recent years.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of accelerated optimization methods through continuous-time dynamics. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the literature. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and well-justified, with promising empirical results.

The main limitations are the limited scope of numerical experiments and the somewhat brief connection to discrete-time algorithms. However, these are relatively minor issues for a theoretical paper that primarily contributes a unified framework and a new heuristic for continuous-time dynamics.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of optimization theory with clear theoretical advances and practical implications. The work is well-executed, properly contextualized within the literature, and makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is particularly noteworthy for its theoretical guarantees and promising empirical performance.

I would recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016, as it meets the quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria for the conference. The paper provides valuable theoretical insights that will be of interest to the optimization and machine learning communities.
