This paper explores adaptive averaging schemes within the framework of accelerated mirror descent (AMD), building upon previous work connecting acceleration and averaging in optimization. The authors aim to identify conditions under which different averaging schemes can achieve, or even improve upon, existing convergence rates. They present a generalized AMD dynamic parameterized by weight functions, derive sufficient conditions for convergence via a Lyapunov analysis, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic grounded in minimizing the rate of decrease of the Lyapunov function. Numerical experiments demonstrate promising results, especially in strongly convex settings.

Despite the potentially significant contributions of this work, a multitude of issues, ranging from notational inconsistencies to insufficiently justified claims, lead me to recommend rejection. While the core idea of adaptive averaging is intriguing, the presentation lacks the rigor and polish expected of a NIPS submission.

**Strengths:**

*   **Interesting Core Idea:** The exploration of adaptive averaging as a means to optimize convergence rates in AMD is a valuable direction.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature on accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics.
*   **Lyapunov Analysis:** The use of a Lyapunov function provides a principled approach to analyzing the convergence of the proposed dynamics.
*   **Numerical Validation:** The numerical experiments offer preliminary evidence supporting the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging heuristic.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Lack of Clarity:** There are numerous instances of unclear notation. For example, the subscript 'w,η' in AMDw,η is inconsistently used – sometimes appearing, sometimes omitted.  The meaning of ‘r’ is also often unclear; is it a function of time, a constant, or both? The notation Ẋ is used without consistent definition, it seems to be a derivative of X, but sometimes is used as part of the averaging integral. This makes it difficult to follow the derivations and understand the precise conditions for convergence.
*   **Insufficient Justification of Claims:** The jump from the general AMD dynamics to the specific conditions in Theorem 2 feels abrupt. The connection between the conditions imposed on η and w and the guarantee of a decreasing Lyapunov function is not fully elucidated. The role of the second condition in Theorem 2 (regarding the sign of the gradient product) remains unclear.  Why is this condition sufficient?
*   **Overly Strong Claims & Lack of Nuance:** The claim that the heuristic “preserves the 1/r(t) convergence rate” feels overstated. While the Lyapunov analysis guarantees a decreasing Lyapunov function, the rate of decrease is not explicitly shown to be maintained, and the influence of the adaptation rule isn’t precisely quantified. The discussion of existing heuristics lacks nuance – simply stating they “provide few guarantees” is an oversimplification.
*   **Mathematical Sloppiness:** In the proof of Theorem 1, the assertion that “W(t) is non-decreasing” is trivially true but could lead to issues if w(t) is allowed to be negative. A more careful phrasing would be needed.  The handling of the Bregman divergence in the Lyapunov function derivative is also not entirely clear, and lacks sufficient detail.
*   **Primal Representation Issues:**  The jump from the second-order ODE in the primal representation (AMD′w,η) to the first-order system (AMDpw,η) in section 3 appears unjustified and mathematically suspect. The authors state "Taking the time derivative of Ž(t) = ∇ψ∗(Z(t))”, but this ignores the fact that Ž(t) is a function of X(t) as well through the averaging scheme, introducing a further derivative and making the connection unclear.
*  **Algorithm 1 Implementation detail**: In Algorithm 1, the choice of βmax is unexplained and arbitrary. What is the motivation behind this upper bound?
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments, while promising, are limited in scope. The parameter settings and sensitivity analysis are not sufficiently detailed to draw strong conclusions about the robustness of the proposed method. The comparison with existing heuristics feels superficial.
*   **Minor Formatting Issues**: The reference list includes a paper from "NIPS 2016" which is an anachronism for a review of a current submission.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The core idea of adaptive averaging is novel and could potentially lead to improved optimization algorithms.
*   The Lyapunov analysis provides a theoretical framework for understanding the convergence behavior of the proposed dynamics.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The lack of mathematical rigor and clarity significantly detracts from the paper's quality.
*   The insufficiently justified claims and mathematical sloppiness raise concerns about the validity of the results.
*   The limited scope of the experiments and the superficial comparison with existing heuristics weaken the empirical evidence.
*   Numerous notational inconsistencies and a lack of polish make the paper difficult to follow.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the authors address an important problem and present an interesting idea, the numerous deficiencies in the presentation, mathematical analysis, and experimental validation outweigh the strengths. The paper, in its current form, falls short of the standards expected for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend rejection, with encouragement for the authors to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit in the future. A thorough revision addressing the points above is required before this work can be considered a substantial contribution to the field.
