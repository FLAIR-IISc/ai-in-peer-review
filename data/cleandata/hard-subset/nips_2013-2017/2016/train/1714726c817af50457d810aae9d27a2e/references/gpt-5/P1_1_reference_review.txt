Summary
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated first-order dynamics for smooth convex optimization over a closed convex set X. It generalizes the “averaging view” of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) by introducing a family of ODEs parameterized by two positive weight functions: w(t) for averaging the primal path and η(t) for accumulating gradients in the dual variable. The key Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*) yields a unifying analysis: if η and w satisfy simple conditions, then Lr decreases and f(X(t)) − f* = O(1/r(t)). This recovers the classical 1/t^2 rate and enables other rates r(t) = t^p, p ≥ 1. The paper also derives a second “energy” function that decreases under the same conditions. A primal representation shows the role of the mirror metric, and examples include the positive orthant and the simplex, leading to an accelerated replicator ODE. Building on the Lyapunov analysis, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) = w/W on the fly to further decrease d/dt Lr without sacrificing the rate. A discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) for r(t)=t^2 is proposed and empirically compared to gradient and speed restarting heuristics on small constrained problems; adaptive averaging shows competitive or improved performance and a monotone Lyapunov proxy.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to and extends: (i) Su–Boyd–Candès (2016) and Attouch–Peypouquet on inertial ODEs for acceleration; (ii) the authors’ prior AMD paper (NIPS 2015), which interprets acceleration via averaging; and (iii) Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan’s Bregman Lagrangian (variational perspective) that also yields families of accelerated ODEs with prescribed rates. The adaptive restart heuristics of O’Donoghue–Candès and Su et al. are the empirical baselines. The new angle here is to generalize the averaging weights, provide simple sufficient conditions for rate guarantees via Lyapunov functions, identify an accelerated replicator ODE, and propose adaptive averaging with guaranteed Lyapunov decrease (in continuous time) as an alternative to restart.

Strengths
- Clear and elegant continuous-time framework that unifies several accelerated dynamics via a simple Lyapunov function. The sufficient conditions (η ≥ r′ and a sign condition involving ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩) are transparent.
- Constructive recipe (Corollary 1) for choosing w and η to achieve any desired polynomial rate r(t) = t^p, recovering known weightings as special cases.
- The additional energy function provides physical intuition (potential vs kinetic) and helps interpret behavior under restart vs adaptive averaging.
- Primal formulation illuminates metric structure via ∇^2ψ*∘∇ψ; examples for R^n_+ and the simplex are instructive. The accelerated replicator dynamics are novel and of independent interest to game theory/viability communities.
- Adaptive averaging is conceptually simple, grounded in Lyapunov decrease, and empirically competitive with restarts.
- Existence/uniqueness and feasibility preservation are discussed carefully (initialization at t0, Lipschitz assumptions).

Weaknesses
- Relationship to Wibisono et al. is underdeveloped. Their variational framework also prescribes rates through time-varying scaling; your Corollary 1 (η = a r, w/W = a) appears closely related. Please delineate what is genuinely new beyond that (e.g., the averaging interpretation, the specific Lyapunov and adaptive scheme).
- The key condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on the unknown trajectory. Corollary 1 circumvents this but at the cost of a specific coupling. The adaptive rule (making ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w) ≤ 0) is appealing, yet the continuous-time heuristic requires access to ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩; in discrete time the proposed surrogate (checking f(x̃) decrease) is heuristic. The discrete-time theorem is deferred to the supplement; at least a main-text theorem statement and proof sketch would strengthen the paper.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: very low-dimensional (n=3) toy objectives, no large-scale studies, no wall-clock comparisons, and no stochastic gradients. Comparisons omit widely used baselines such as FISTA with adaptive restart on composite problems.
- Some clarity/notation issues: Algorithm 1 mixes x̃, ž, x with a nonstandard λk expression; the roles of R and γ are not well motivated; bibliographic entries are incomplete. The choice of β, step size s, and sensitivity are not discussed.
- Strongly convex case: the text mentions potential for faster rates but provides no theory or experiments demonstrating linear convergence using, say, r(t)=e^{ct}, nor a discrete counterpart.
- Technical details: Theorem 1’s global existence relies on global Lipschitzness; clarifying the precise assumptions on ψ (smooth, strongly convex) and f would avoid ambiguity.

Quality
Technically sound at the level presented; the Lyapunov derivations are correct and informative. However, the main practical contribution—adaptive averaging—would benefit from a clearer, self-contained discrete-time convergence guarantee in the main body and stronger experiments.

Clarity
Overall clear mathematically, but notation is heavy. The paper would benefit from streamlining Algorithm 1, defining all symbols locally, and improving reference formatting.

Originality
Moderate. The generalized averaging/Lyapunov view and accelerated replicator ODE are nice contributions. The adaptive averaging heuristic is simple and well-motivated, but its relation to existing variational/ODE formulations and to adaptive restarts should be clarified.

Significance
Potentially useful for understanding and designing accelerated methods and dynamics, with a conceptually simple heuristic that seems to work well. Current empirical scope limits impact; stronger experiments and discrete-time guarantees would boost significance.

Pros
- Unified Lyapunov analysis yielding flexible rates and recovering known accelerated dynamics.
- New accelerated replicator ODE and insightful energy interpretation.
- Simple adaptive averaging with monotone Lyapunov in continuous time and promising empirical behavior.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to variational formulations; needs clearer positioning.
- Discrete-time theory is offloaded to the supplement; experiments are small-scale.
- Clarity issues in algorithmic presentation and references.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a main-text theorem (with sketch) for the discrete algorithm’s rate guarantee and precise conditions.
- Expand experiments to higher-dimensional, real datasets (e.g., logistic regression with simplex/orthant constraints), compare with FISTA + adaptive restart, and report wall-clock and sensitivity to β,s.
- Clarify relationship to Wibisono et al. and to inertial/Hessian-damped ODEs; highlight what is unique here.
- Clean up Algorithm 1 notation, define λk precisely, and complete citations.
- Discuss strongly convex case and whether r(t)=e^{ct} yields linear rates in continuous and discrete time.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical framework is clean and the adaptive averaging idea is interesting and practically relevant; with improved discrete-time analysis and broader experiments, this would be a solid contribution. Confidence: medium.
