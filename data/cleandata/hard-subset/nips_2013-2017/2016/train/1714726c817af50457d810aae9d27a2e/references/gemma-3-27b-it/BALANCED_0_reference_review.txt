This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated mirror descent, extending previous work connecting Nesterov’s acceleration to ODEs (Su et al. 2016, Krichene et al. 2016). The authors introduce a generalized family of AMD dynamics parameterized by weight functions and provide sufficient conditions for quadratic convergence. A key contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to maintain the convergence rate by dynamically adjusting these weights based on progress along the solution trajectory. The paper rigorously analyzes the dynamics and supports the proposed heuristic with numerical experiments, including a connection to replicator dynamics.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds upon a solid foundation of continuous-time optimization theory. The Lyapunov analysis provides rigorous guarantees about the convergence rate under specific conditions on the weight functions.
* **Generality & Flexibility:**  The generalized AMD framework allows for a wide range of averaging schemes. This generality is a key strength, potentially encompassing existing accelerated methods as special cases.
* **Adaptive Averaging Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a valuable contribution. The intuition behind adaptively adjusting weights based on progress is sound, and the provided guarantees (maintaining the convergence rate) are significant.
* **Connection to Replicator Dynamics:** Demonstrating a connection to the replicator equation is a nice result, showcasing the applicability of the framework to a different field (evolutionary game theory).
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. Mathematical derivations are presented with reasonable clarity. The inclusion of the primal representation aids understanding.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments offer support for the proposed heuristic, demonstrating its performance compared to existing restarting methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conditions for Convergence:** The sufficient conditions for convergence (Theorems 2 and Corollary 1) are somewhat restrictive and may be difficult to verify in practice. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of how these conditions might be relaxed or approximated would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of the Heuristic:** While theoretically sound, the adaptive averaging heuristic (Algorithm 1) appears relatively complex to implement compared to simpler restarting strategies. A more thorough analysis of its computational cost would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The numerical experiments, while illustrative, are relatively limited. Expanding these to include a wider range of objective functions and problem sizes, and comparing against more recent adaptive optimization algorithms (e.g., variants of Adam) would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Lack of Discussion on Practical Initialization:**  The initialization at t0 > 0 is crucial for theoretical results but might be less intuitive in a practical setting.  More discussion regarding the selection of t0 and its impact on performance would be helpful.
* **Relation to Existing Adaptive Methods:**  The authors briefly mention Adagrad and Adam, but a more detailed comparison and discussion of the differences (and potential synergies) between these methods and the proposed adaptive averaging would be insightful.  Are the underlying principles fundamentally different, or is this more of a different perspective on adaptive optimization?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of a generalized framework for accelerated mirror descent dynamics.
* The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution with theoretical guarantees.
* The connection to replicator dynamics highlights the potential for applications in diverse fields.
* The paper is well-written and clear.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The convergence conditions are somewhat restrictive and may limit practical applicability.
* The numerical experiments are limited in scope.
* The practical complexity of the heuristic is not fully addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and theoretically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a promising idea with rigorous guarantees. However, the restrictive convergence conditions and limited scope of the numerical experiments prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance.

**Recommendation:**  **Weak Reject**.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Relax Convergence Conditions:** Investigate possibilities for relaxing the convergence conditions (Theorems 2 & Corollary 1) or providing guidance on how to approximate them in practice.
* **Expand Numerical Evaluation:** Conduct more extensive numerical experiments with a wider range of problems and comparisons to state-of-the-art adaptive optimization algorithms.
* **Analyze Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the adaptive averaging heuristic.
* **Discuss Practical Initialization:** Elaborate on the choice of t0 and its influence on performance.
* **Deepen Comparison with Existing Methods:**  Provide a more detailed comparison between the proposed adaptive averaging and existing adaptive optimization algorithms like Adam, highlighting similarities and differences.
* **Explore Alternative Lyapunov Functions:** Investigate if alternative Lyapunov functions can provide stronger convergence guarantees or lead to more practical algorithms.
