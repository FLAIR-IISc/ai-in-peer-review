

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary
This paper studies a generalization of Nesterov's accelerated method through continuous-time dynamics with adaptive averaging. The authors propose a family of ODEs called Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) with generalized averaging, and provide theoretical guarantees using Lyapunov functions. They also propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates while potentially improving performance. The paper connects their approach to the replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory, showing how to accelerate it through their framework.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear connection between acceleration and averaging in optimization, and the theoretical framework is well-structured. The adaptive averaging heuristic is an interesting extension to existing work on accelerated methods. The connection to replicator dynamics is novel and potentially valuable for applications in game theory.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
1. **Notation and Formatting Errors**: The paper consistently uses "f?" to denote the minimum value of f, which should be "f*" (asterisk). Similarly, "z?" appears throughout where "z*" should be used. This is a critical formatting error that appears in the abstract, equations, and throughout the paper.

2. **Theorem 1 Proof**: The authors state "w(t)/W(t) ≤ w(t)/W(t0)" but this is incorrect since W(t) ≥ W(t0) (as W is non-decreasing), so the inequality should be reversed. This is a fundamental error in the proof.

3. **Corollary 1**: The condition η(t) = w(t)r(t)W(t) is incorrectly stated. From Theorem 2, the correct condition should be η(t) = w(t)r(t)/W(t) to satisfy the conditions.

4. **Equation (3)**: The inequality contains a typo with "≤" written as "≤" with a space before it, which is inconsistent with standard mathematical notation.

5. **Theorem 2 Proof**: The proof does not address the case where ⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩ = 0, which is necessary for completeness.

### Theoretical and Explanatory Issues
1. **Incomplete Theoretical Support**: The paper claims the adaptive averaging heuristic "preserves the convergence rate" but provides no formal proof for this claim, only an intuitive argument.

2. **Misattribution of Key Concepts**: The paper doesn't properly acknowledge that the continuous-time interpretation of Nesterov's method was first introduced in [20] (Su et al.), which is a seminal work in this area.

3. **Inadequate Comparison with Prior Work**: The paper fails to adequately distinguish its adaptive averaging heuristic from existing restarting heuristics in [17, 20]. It doesn't clearly explain how adaptive averaging differs from these methods in a way that justifies its novelty.

4. **Missing Details in Algorithm 1**: The algorithm references a regularizer R(x̃, x(k)) but doesn't define it, making the algorithm impossible to reproduce.

### Experimental and Presentation Issues
1. **Missing Figures**: The paper references "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" but neither is included in the provided text, making it impossible to evaluate the experimental results.

2. **Incomplete Experimental Results**: The paper states "the experiments indicate that adaptive averaging compares favorably" but provides no specific metrics, statistical significance, or comparison with baselines beyond qualitative descriptions.

3. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper uses "restarting" and "adaptive averaging" interchangeably in some sections, creating confusion about the relationship between these concepts.

4. **Unsubstantiated Claims**: The paper claims "a significant improvement in some cases" without providing quantitative evidence to support this assertion.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting extension to the continuous-time interpretation of accelerated optimization methods. However, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and incomplete theoretical and experimental support undermine the paper's contribution. The paper contains critical mathematical errors (such as the incorrect condition in Corollary 1) that would prevent others from building on the work. The experimental section is incomplete without the figures and specific metrics.

While the connection to replicator dynamics is potentially valuable, the paper fails to clearly articulate how this work advances the state of the art beyond previous continuous-time interpretations of accelerated methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is presented as novel, but it doesn't clearly differentiate itself from existing restarting heuristics.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these fundamental issues, but as submitted, it contains too many technical errors and omissions to be considered for publication at NIPS. The quality of the work is compromised by these significant flaws, and the paper does not meet the standards expected for NIPS.

## Recommendation
Reject
