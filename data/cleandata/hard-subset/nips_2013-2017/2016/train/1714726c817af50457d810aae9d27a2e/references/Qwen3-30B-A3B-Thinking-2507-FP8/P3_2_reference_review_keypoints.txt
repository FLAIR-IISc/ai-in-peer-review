- Paper studies generalized continuous-time Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics with flexible weight functions w(t) and η(t), and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic.

- Theoretical rigor: provides existence/uniqueness proofs and Lyapunov-based sufficient conditions for convergence, extending prior work.

- Novel generalization: extends averaging schemes beyond polynomial weights, offering a broader continuous-time framework for acceleration.

- Practical motivation: proposes a simple adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates and aims to improve empirical performance over restart heuristics.

- Clear examples: includes insightful primal representations, notably accelerated replicator dynamics for simplex constraints linking to evolutionary game theory.

- Comprehensive experiments: tests on varied problem types and constraints with trajectory and energy visualizations.

- Limited discrete connection: main focus is continuous-time; discrete implementations and how continuous results translate to discrete algorithms are under-analyzed.

- Weak comparison to adaptive methods: does not compare to widely used adaptive optimizers like Adagrad or Adam or discuss relations to them.

- Computational complexity unclear: paper omits discussion of extra costs or gradient evaluations required by the adaptive heuristic.

- Underdeveloped related work discussion: insufficiently clarifies differences from or builds on Flammarion and Bach and broader continuous-time literature.

- Non-convex analysis missing: focuses on convex settings with no theoretical or empirical study of non-convex behavior relevant to ML.

- Theorem 2 concern: one condition depends on trajectory X(t), which may be hard to verify in practice; Corollary 1 is a limited special case.

- Discretization details lacking: adaptive heuristic is continuous-time but experiments use a discrete approximation without detailed discretization method or guarantees.

- Statistical significance absent: claims of favorable comparisons lack statistical testing to validate observed performance differences.

- Relation to faster rates unclear: statement about discretizing ODEs for faster rates r(t)=t^p (p>2) is not clearly connected to the adaptive heuristic or prior discrete methods.

- Overall assessment: valuable theoretical contribution with a novel adaptive heuristic and strong experiments, but practical impact limited by discrete-time and complexity gaps.

- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions focused on stronger discrete-time connection, comparisons to Adagrad/Adam, complexity analysis, and clearer relations to related work.

- Pro: unified theoretical framework, novel adaptive heuristic preserving rates, rigorous proofs and experiments, and interesting links to evolutionary game theory.

- Con: insufficient practical implementation discussion and complexity analysis, weak discrete-time justification, limited adaptive-method comparisons, and no non-convex analysis.
