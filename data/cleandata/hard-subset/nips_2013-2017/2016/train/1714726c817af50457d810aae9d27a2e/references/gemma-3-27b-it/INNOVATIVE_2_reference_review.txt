This paper presents a fascinating and broadly applicable generalization of accelerated mirror descent dynamics, framing it within a continuous-time ODE framework. The core idea—exploring different averaging schemes beyond the standard acceleration—is genuinely novel and insightful. The connection to replicator dynamics is a particularly nice touch, demonstrating the potential for applying these insights to other fields. While the technical details are somewhat dense, the authors clearly articulate the motivation and implications of their work. The adaptive averaging heuristic proposed in Section 4 is a strong contribution, offering a practical method for improving convergence without sacrificing the theoretical guarantees.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The generalization of AMD dynamics and the exploration of adaptive averaging are genuinely novel. This isn't incremental work; it opens up a new direction for research.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The Lyapunov analysis provides a rigorous theoretical basis for the proposed methods. The convergence guarantees are a significant strength.
*   **Generality:** The framework is quite general, applicable to a wide range of convex problems and mirror maps.
*   **Connections to Other Fields:** The link to replicator dynamics highlights the broader relevance of the work beyond optimization.
*   **Practical Heuristic:** The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a concrete way to improve convergence in practice.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated and clearly explains the connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density & Accessibility:** The mathematical notation is quite dense, potentially making the paper challenging for readers unfamiliar with the continuous-time optimization literature. A more gradual introduction of the notation and concepts would improve accessibility.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the numerical experiments are encouraging, they are somewhat limited in scope. More extensive experimentation with a wider range of problem types and datasets would strengthen the claims. The visualization provided (Figure 2) is good, but more details on the experimental setup would be helpful.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The authors don’t sufficiently discuss the potential limitations of their approach. For instance, the computational cost of evaluating the gradient and Hessian in the primal representation (Section 3) could be a significant bottleneck for large-scale problems.
*   **Comparison to Existing Adaptive Methods:** The paper briefly mentions Adagrad and Adam but doesn’t offer a detailed comparison, particularly concerning why their approach is fundamentally different or superior for *accelerated* methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant new perspective on accelerated optimization.
*   The theoretical results are solid and provide strong guarantees.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic is a valuable contribution that could have practical impact.
*   The connection to replicator dynamics expands the scope of the work.
*   The potential for further research in this area is high.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The density of the mathematical notation may limit accessibility.
*   The empirical validation is somewhat limited.
*   The discussion of limitations is lacking.
*   While novel, the practical benefits over existing accelerated methods, outside of the specific test cases, are not definitively demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a very strong paper that pushes the boundaries of our understanding of accelerated optimization. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the potential for practical impact outweigh the limitations. I am inclined to recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors should consider expanding the empirical validation and providing a more detailed discussion of the limitations in a revised version. The boldness of the approach, prioritizing a new concept over methodological perfection, is highly commendable.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
