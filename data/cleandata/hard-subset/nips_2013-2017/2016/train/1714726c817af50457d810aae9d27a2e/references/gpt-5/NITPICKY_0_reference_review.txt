Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for convex optimization and proposes a generalized “accelerated mirror descent” (AMD) ODE parameterized by two positive weight functions w(t) (averaging weights) and η(t) (gradient accumulation). A Lyapunov family Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) is analyzed, yielding sufficient conditions under which Lr is nonincreasing and f(X(t))−f* ≤ Lr(X(t0),Z(t0),t0)/r(t). This unifies and extends prior ODE views of accelerated methods, connects acceleration with averaging, and recovers t^2 rates with appropriate choices of w,η. The paper also provides a primal-only representation, instantiates dynamics on the positive orthant and simplex, and introduces an “adaptive averaging” heuristic that adjusts w via a(t)=w/W depending on the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩, with a discrete-time algorithm and experiments comparing against restart heuristics.

This work builds on: (i) ODE interpretations of Nesterov acceleration (e.g., Su-Boyd-Candès 2014; Attouch-Peypouquet et al.; Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan 2016), (ii) mirror descent and Bregman geometry (Nemirovski & Yudin; Krichene et al. 2015), and (iii) restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès; Su et al.). The AMD family is close in spirit to the variational framework of [Wibisono et al., 2016], and the replicator/entropy examples connect to well-known primal forms.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: the link between averaging weights and acceleration is sharpened. The Lyapunov function Lr yields an elegant and simple sufficient condition for 1/r(t) rates, and the energy Er provides additional intuition.
- Generality: the AMD family with arbitrary positive w,η and target rate r(t) encompasses several known dynamics; the primal representations and examples (positive orthant, simplex/replicator) make the framework concrete.
- Adaptive averaging idea: choosing a(t) to drive down the Lyapunov derivative is natural, and the heuristic is plausibly competitive with restart approaches. The continuous-time logic is clean.
- Existence/uniqueness and feasibility are addressed (initialization at t0>0 is a sensible technical fix).

Weaknesses and issues
I list these carefully, because several are substantive and impede reproducibility.

Technical/correctness
1) Core inconsistency between a(t), w/W, and Corollary 1:
- Theorem 2 condition (2) involves r(t)−η(t)W(t)/w(t). Corollary 1 sets η(t)=w(t)r(t)/W(t), which zeros this factor.
- Section 4 states: “In Corollary 1, we simply set a(t)=η(t)r(t) so that ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩(r−ηa)≡0.” Here a(t)=w/W. To zero the factor, one must set a(t)=η(t)/r(t), not a(t)=η(t)r(t). This appears to be a serious typo that propagates to the heuristic description.

2) Example 1 and 2: wrong expression for a(t).
- For r(t)=t^2, r′/r=2/t. The text claims “take a(t)=β t,” but then computes exp(∫a)=exp(β ln(t/t0)), which corresponds to a(t)=β/t. Similarly in Example 2. This inconsistency is critical because it changes the growth of W and w and the resulting η.

3) Theorem 1 proof has a likely typo: the vector field term is written “w(t)W(t)(∇ψ*(Z)−X)” instead of w(t)/W(t). The subsequent bounds use w/W, so the printed w·W is misleading.

4) Algorithm 1 is currently not reproducible:
- λk definition is garbled: “λk = √(s ak) + √(s ak)” (likely missing a denominator and/or a 1+ term).
- γ is used in step 4 but never defined.
- R is introduced as a “regularizer” without specifying its role, regularity, or how it ties to theory.
- βmax appears in step 6 but is not defined; step 6 and 7 conflict (ak set twice).
- The objective in step 3 uses Dψ (not Dψ*), but consistency with the continuous-time model and the discretization is not explained.
- The mapping from continuous-time a(t), η(t) to ak, step sizes, and parameter schedules is not derived here (relegated to supplement); as written, the algorithm cannot be implemented.

5) Discrete-time guarantees are deferred to the supplement, but are essential; without them, claims that the heuristic “preserves the convergence rate” in discrete time are not substantiated.

6) The existence/uniqueness argument claims a global solution on [t0,∞) because “T is arbitrary,” but this usually requires a continuation argument; as written it is a bit too terse. Given Lipschitz vector field in (X,Z) uniformly on bounded t-intervals, standard continuation applies, but stating it precisely would avoid confusion.

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous typos and formatting remnants (e.g., “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)”; author footnote inside the main text; inconsistent spacing/parentheses; “AMD′w,η ”; mixed use of X̌, Ž with and without hats).
- Several symbols introduced without definition or ambiguous: γ, R, βmax; also occasionally ∇ψ* is assumed Lipschitz without specifying the norm; “Lipschitz function that maps into X” would be clearer as “∇ψ* is Lψ-Lipschitz (w.r.t. a chosen norm) and maps E* into X.”
- The primal derivations assume twice differentiability of ψ*; elsewhere only Lipschitz ∇ψ* is assumed. The precise regularity assumptions should be consolidated.
- The experiments refer to Figure 2; settings, step sizes, choice of β, s, γ, R, and stopping criteria are unspecified; no numerical tables or reproducibility details.

Originality and relation to prior work
- The AMD family and Lyapunov analysis are close to the variational formulation of [Wibisono et al., 2016], where target rates are enforced via a Lagrangian. The paper should more clearly position what is new beyond that (e.g., the specific Lyapunov Lr with Bregman in dual space; the condition r−ηW/w; the adaptive averaging heuristic).
- The adaptive averaging concept seems related in spirit to adaptive restarts and to time reparameterizations used in ODE views; a more explicit comparison and theoretical distinction would help.
- Citations have many incomplete entries (missing years/venue/pages) and a few assertions (e.g., “preserve the original convergence rate” under heuristic) need either references or careful hedging.

Significance
- The continuous-time framework is interesting, and the adaptive averaging idea is potentially useful. However, without a correct and reproducible discretization, limited experiments (mostly 3D), and with key typos in the core formulas, the impact is limited in its current form.

Pros
- Clean, unified Lyapunov analysis; flexible accelerated ODE family.
- Intuitive adaptive averaging proposal; connections to replicator dynamics.
- Potentially valuable perspective complementary to restart heuristics.

Cons
- Multiple serious typos affecting core relationships (a(t), Example 1/2) and a misleading statement in Sec. 4.
- Non-reproducible algorithm (missing/inconsistent definitions).
- Experimental section lacks details and breadth; figures not included here.
- Incomplete references and numerous formatting issues.
- Limited discrete-time theory presented in the main paper.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea has merit, but the paper in its current form contains critical inconsistencies and insufficiently specified algorithms/experiments. I encourage the authors to fix the a(t)/η/w/W inconsistencies, clean up Theorem/Algorithm statements, move essential discrete-time guarantees into the main text, improve experimental rigor (higher dimensions, ablations, parameter reporting), and tighten the positioning relative to [Wibisono et al.] and restart literature. With these corrections, the contribution could be valuable. Quality: low-to-moderate (due to errors). Clarity: below threshold (due to typos/omissions). Originality: moderate. Significance: moderate if fixed.
