### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order convex optimization. Building on prior work that connects Nesterov's acceleration to second-order ODEs with a specific averaging scheme [20, 11], this work proposes and analyzes a much broader family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics. This family, denoted `AMDw,η`, is parameterized by a generalized averaging weight function `w(t)` and a gradient accumulation rate `η(t)`.

The main theoretical contribution is a unified Lyapunov analysis for this family of ODEs. The authors introduce a candidate Lyapunov function `Lr` and derive simple, sufficient conditions on the functions `w`, `η`, and a target rate function `r(t)` that guarantee `Lr` is non-increasing. This directly implies a convergence rate of `f(X(t)) - f* ≤ O(1/r(t))`. The framework is shown to be a proper generalization, recovering the O(1/t²) rate from [11] as a special case. The authors also derive a primal-space representation of the dynamics and show that for optimization over the probability simplex, this framework yields an accelerated version of the well-known replicator dynamics from evolutionary game theory.

The key practical contribution is a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic derived from the Lyapunov analysis. The insight is that the convergence can be sped up by adaptively choosing the weights `w(t)` to make the time derivative of the Lyapunov function as negative as possible, while still satisfying the sufficient conditions that guarantee the baseline convergence rate. A discrete-time algorithm based on this heuristic is proposed and empirically compared against popular restarting heuristics on several simplex-constrained problems.

### Strengths

1.  **Theoretical Generalization and Unification:** The paper provides an elegant and significant generalization of the continuous-time view of acceleration. Moving from a single fixed averaging scheme (e.g., `w(t) ∝ t`) to a general family parameterized by `w(t)` and `η(t)` deepens our understanding of the role of averaging in acceleration. The unified Lyapunov analysis (Theorem 2) is clean, insightful, and provides a clear recipe for designing dynamics with a desired convergence rate.

2.  **Novel and Well-Grounded Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a key strength. Unlike many ad-hoc heuristics, it is directly derived from and justified by the continuous-time theoretical analysis. The guarantee that it preserves the O(1/t²) convergence rate (by construction, as it ensures the Lyapunov function is non-increasing) is a strong theoretical advantage over restart schemes, for which such guarantees are not always straightforward, especially in constrained settings.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the connection to prior work is well-established, and the theoretical development progresses logically from the general ODE to the Lyapunov analysis and the resulting adaptive algorithm. The examples, particularly the derivation of accelerated replicator dynamics, are illustrative and help ground the abstract framework.

4.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The numerical experiments, while limited in scale, effectively demonstrate the potential of adaptive averaging. The method consistently performs on par with or significantly better than established gradient and speed-based restarting heuristics. The plots of the Lyapunov and energy functions provide compelling visual confirmation of the method's theoretical properties, highlighting a key difference from restart schemes which can cause the Lyapunov function to increase.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Theoretical Analysis of the Heuristic's Improvement:** While the paper guarantees that the adaptive heuristic preserves the baseline `O(1/r(t))` rate, it offers no theoretical analysis of *when* or *by how much* it can improve upon this rate. For instance, restart heuristics are known to achieve linear convergence for strongly convex functions. It is unclear if adaptive averaging can achieve a similar speed-up. The experiments suggest a significant benefit for strong convexity, but a theoretical justification is missing.

2.  **Gap between Continuous and Discrete Analysis:** The core theoretical results are for continuous-time ODEs. The proposed Algorithm 1 is a discretization, but its analysis is largely deferred to the supplementary material. The connection between the condition for adapting the weights in continuous time (`〈∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)〉 > 0`) and its discrete counterpart (`f(x̃(k+1)) > f(x̃(k))`) could be more formally justified in the main text. This would strengthen the link between the theory and the practical algorithm.

3.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are confined to 3D simplex-constrained problems. While excellent for visualization, this makes it difficult to assess the heuristic's performance and scalability on higher-dimensional problems more typical of modern machine learning. Demonstrating its effectiveness on a standard benchmark, such as logistic regression or SVMs on a real-world dataset, would significantly bolster the paper's practical claims.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Justifying the Discrete Heuristic:** A brief formal argument connecting the continuous and discrete conditions for adaptation would be valuable. For instance, noting that `f(x̃(k+1)) - f(x̃(k)) ≈ s〈∇f(x̃(k)), x̃̇(k)〉` via a Taylor expansion could help bridge this gap for the reader.

*   **Expanding Experiments:** To improve the paper's impact, I would strongly encourage the authors to include at least one experiment on a higher-dimensional, standard machine learning problem. This would help demonstrate that the observed benefits are not an artifact of the low-dimensional setting.

*   **Refining Claims on Restarting:** The introduction states that restart heuristics provide "few guarantees" and may not "preserve the original convergence rate". This claim could be softened or made more precise. For example, the work of O'Donoghue and Candès [17] does provide rate-preserving guarantees for their restart scheme on unconstrained convex problems. The authors could clarify that such guarantees are less established for the general constrained settings considered in this paper.

*   **Discussion on Strong Convexity:** In the discussion or conclusion, it would be insightful for the authors to speculate on why adaptive averaging appears to work so well for strongly convex problems. Is it because the condition `〈∇f(X), Ẋ〉 > 0` is a better indicator of non-progress in this setting? This could motivate the future theoretical work mentioned.

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution to our understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The generalization of the continuous-time dynamics and the principled derivation of the adaptive averaging heuristic are both significant. While the theoretical analysis of the heuristic's improvement and the experimental scope could be expanded, the existing contributions are solid, original, and well-presented. The work is likely to be of high interest to the optimization and machine learning theory community at NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance.
