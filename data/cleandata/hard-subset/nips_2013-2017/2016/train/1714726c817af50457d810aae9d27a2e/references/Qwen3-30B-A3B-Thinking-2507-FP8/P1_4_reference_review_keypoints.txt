- Introduces a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) continuous-time dynamics parameterized by averaging weight w and gradient accumulation rate η.  
- Provides a unified Lyapunov-based analysis to establish convergence rates and sufficient conditions.  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts weights based on the trajectory, preserving convergence rates while improving empirical performance.  
- Derives examples for specific constraint sets (positive orthant, simplex) and connects to known dynamics like the replicator equation.  
- Connects continuous-time analysis to discrete optimization algorithms and provides practical improvements in experiments.  
- Theoretical claims are technically sound with rigorous Lyapunov analysis and careful treatment of existence and uniqueness (Theorem 1).  
- Numerical experiments are well-designed and show adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics across multiple problem types.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with a clear technical progression and accessible Lyapunov framework.  
- Supplementary material addresses implementation details for the discrete algorithm.  
- Minor clarity suggestions: discuss computational complexity of the adaptive heuristic and contrast explicitly with adaptive methods like Adagrad.  
- Original contribution: generalizes averaging schemes and introduces a novel adaptive heuristic that preserves rates and improves empirical results.  
- Novel and meaningful connection to replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained problems with applications in evolutionary game theory.  
- Results have both theoretical and practical significance and could guide future algorithm design; adaptive heuristic is a rare case of theory+empirical gain.  
- Strengths: clear Lyapunov framework, novel adaptive heuristic, replicator connection, strong numerical validation, good theory–practice balance.  
- Weaknesses: adaptive heuristic limited to r(t)=t² with limited discussion of generalization, incomplete treatment of discrete-time translation beyond the specific case.  
- Additional weaknesses: computational complexity of the heuristic is not discussed and comparison to other adaptive methods (Adagrad/Adam) is lacking.  
- Recommendation: strong contribution, recommended for acceptance due to theoretical insights, novel heuristic, and compelling empirical evidence.  
- Counterpoint: scope of the adaptive heuristic is narrow (specific rate) and discrete-time implementation beyond that case is not fully addressed.
