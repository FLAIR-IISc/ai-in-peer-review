

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary
This paper studies a generalized family of accelerated mirror descent dynamics with adaptive averaging, extending previous work by Krichene et al. [11] on continuous-time accelerated methods. The authors introduce a unified framework using a Lyapunov function to guarantee convergence rates, propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence guarantees, and demonstrate its effectiveness on constrained optimization problems, including an accelerated replicator dynamics for the simplex. The work connects continuous-time dynamics to discrete optimization methods, with potential applications in machine learning and game theory.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear contribution to the growing literature on continuous-time interpretations of accelerated optimization methods. The theoretical framework is well-motivated, and the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel in its approach to preserving convergence guarantees while improving empirical performance. The connection to replicator dynamics for the simplex constraint is particularly interesting and well-explained. The numerical experiments provide useful empirical validation of the theoretical claims.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Errors in Equations and Proofs**: 
   - Equation (1) contains a critical typo: "X(t) = X(t0)W (t0)+ ∫ t t0 w(τ)∇ψ∗(Z(τ))dτ W (t)" should be "X(t) = [X(t0)W(t0) + ∫_t0^t w(τ)∇ψ∗(Z(τ))dτ]/W(t)".
   - In equation (3), the derivation contains a sign error: "〈 −η(t)∇f(X(t)), X(t) + W(t) w(t) Ẋ(t)− x? 〉" should be "〈 −η(t)∇f(X(t)), X(t) − W(t) w(t) Ẋ(t)− x? 〉" based on the correct expression for Ẋ(t).
   - Theorem 1's proof incorrectly states "w(t)/W(t) ≤ w(t)/W(t0)" without justification for why this inequality holds (it requires w(t) to be non-negative, which isn't established).

2. **Mischaracterization of Related Work**:
   - The paper claims that the gradient restart heuristic in [17] restarts "whenever the trajectory forms an acute angle with the gradient," which is incorrect. The heuristic restarts when the angle is obtuse (indicating progress), not acute.
   - Reference [17] is cited with an incorrect title and publication venue. The correct citation is "Adaptive restart for accelerated gradient methods" (not "schemes") in Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015.

3. **Inconsistent Terminology and Notation**:
   - The paper uses non-standard symbols like "X̌" and "Ž" without clear explanation, which could confuse readers.
   - The paper claims "quadratic convergence rate" for O(1/t²), which is a misnomer (quadratic convergence would be O(1/2^t) or similar).
   - In the adaptive averaging section, the paper claims "a constant a(t) corresponds to an exponential increase in w(t)," but equation (4) shows it corresponds to a polynomial increase.

4. **Missing Details and Supplementation**:
   - The paper repeatedly references "the supplementary material" for critical details (e.g., mirror map properties, discrete counterpart of the Lyapunov function), but the supplementary material is not provided. This is unacceptable for a submission.
   - The numerical experiments section references "Figure 2" but the figure is not included in the submission, making it impossible to verify the claims about performance comparisons.

5. **Formatting Issues**:
   - The conference name is incorrectly written as "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" instead of "The Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)".
   - References have inconsistent formatting (e.g., some with page numbers, some without).
   - Missing spaces in equations (e.g., "∫ t 0 τ r−1" should be "∫_t0^t τ^{r-1}").

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to accelerated optimization dynamics, the numerous technical errors, mischaracterizations of prior work, and missing details significantly undermine its credibility. The equation errors in the main theorems are particularly concerning, as they could lead to incorrect conclusions about the convergence guarantees. The paper also fails to provide necessary supplementary materials that are essential for verifying the claims.

The minor technical issues are not just superficial; they represent fundamental errors in the mathematical presentation that would make it difficult for readers to properly understand and build upon the work. Given the stringent standards of NIPS, these issues outweigh the paper's theoretical interest and empirical results.

I recommend rejection due to these substantial methodological errors and incomplete presentation. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmission.
