- Paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with generalized averaging via weight functions w and η, proving Lyapunov stability and 1/r(t) convergence under sufficient conditions.  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts weights based on the Lyapunov derivative to preserve or improve convergence, with numerical comparisons to restarting heuristics.

- Strong theoretical foundation linking optimization algorithms to ODEs, with rigorous Lyapunov analysis and clear convergence conditions.  
- Generalizes AMD into a unified framework that encompasses existing accelerated methods and enables new averaging schemes.  
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantees and demonstrated empirical benefits.  
- Interesting application to the probability simplex yielding an accelerated replicator dynamic.  
- Comprehensive experiments with visualizations comparing adaptive averaging to restarting heuristics.

- Core theoretical contribution is incremental, relying on existing Lyapunov techniques; convergence conditions may be conservative.  
- Discretization of the adaptive heuristic is underdeveloped and lacks justification of chosen schemes.  
- Experiments lack comparisons to modern adaptive optimizers (e.g., Adam) and thus miss key baselines.  
- Limited discussion and empirical evidence regarding scalability to higher-dimensional problems.

- Quality: technically sound but would benefit from refined analysis and broader experiments.  
- Clarity: generally well-written, but some sections (notably discretization) need more detail.  
- Originality: adaptive averaging and the replicator connection are novel, but core analysis is derivative of prior work.

- Pros: rigorous theory, novel adaptive heuristic with guarantees, well-designed experiments, clear exposition.  
- Cons: limited theoretical novelty, weak discretization discussion, missing comparisons to state-of-the-art adaptive methods, limited scalability analysis.

- Overall assessment: reviewer recommends strong accept, noting the paper is a valuable contribution that would be improved by addressing discretization, broader baselines, and scalability.
