Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies continuous-time, first-order dynamics for minimizing smooth convex functions over convex sets, with a focus on accelerated mirror descent (AMD). The authors generalize the averaging viewpoint of AMD by introducing a family of ODEs parameterized by two positive weight functions: w(t) for averaging and η(t) for gradient accumulation. They introduce a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*) and provide sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) under which Lr decreases, yielding a convergence rate O(1/r(t)). They also present an “energy” function Er combining potential and kinetic terms that is non-increasing under the same conditions. The framework recovers known accelerated rates (e.g., O(1/t^2)) and allows other polynomial rates, and it connects to the variational framework of Wibisono et al. [23], Su–Boyd–Candès [20], and prior AMD interpretations [11]. The paper offers a primal representation (via ∇^2ψ*∘∇ψ), and concrete examples, including accelerated dynamics on the positive orthant and on the simplex where it yields an accelerated replicator ODE. Finally, it proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts w(t) to reduce the derivative of Lr along the trajectories, with a discrete algorithm and experiments comparing favorably to gradient and speed restarts [17,20].

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Clear Lyapunov-based analysis yielding a simple and general condition for accelerated rates via r(t), covering and extending prior ODE-based interpretations of Nesterov/AMD [11,20,23].
- Adaptive averaging idea: A principled, easy-to-implement heuristic that provably preserves the target rate by directly controlling the Lyapunov decrease, with a discrete counterpart.
- Useful primal view and examples: The primal representation clarifies geometric structure through ∇^2ψ*∘∇ψ and connects to accelerated replicator dynamics, which is interesting beyond optimization (evolutionary game theory).
- Energy function: Offers additional intuition and diagnostics for dynamics and experiments.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/typos in key derivations: Several notational inversions appear to be errors and may confuse readers:
  • In Example 1–2, to satisfy a(t) ≥ r′(t)/r(t) with r(t)=t^p, one should take a(t)=β/t (β≥p), not a(t)=β t. Subsequent formulas for w and η are consistent with a(t)=β/t.
  • In Section 4, to make r(t)−η(t)W(t)/w(t)=0, one needs η(t)=a(t)r(t), not a(t)=η(t)r(t). The adaptive rule written as a(t)=η(t)r(t) when ⟨∇f,Ẋ⟩>0 appears inverted; to enforce ⟨∇f,Ẋ⟩(r−η/a)≤0 with fixed η, one needs a(t)≥η(t)/r(t) when ⟨∇f,Ẋ⟩>0 and a(t)≤η(t)/r(t) when ⟨∇f,Ẋ⟩<0.
  • Algorithm 1: λk is likely λk = √(s ak)/(1+√(s ak)); as written it repeats the numerator. Line 6 also seems to contain a typo (“βmax”).
- Experimental scope: Experiments are largely 3D simplex visualizations; stronger empirical support (higher-dimensional problems, standard benchmarks, robustness to step sizes, wall-clock comparisons) would improve significance.
- Discrete-time guarantees: The discrete adaptive scheme is only briefly sketched; the promised bound is relegated to the supplement. A clear statement in the main text (with assumptions) would help.
- Limited discussion of strong convexity: The framework could naturally handle r(t)=e^{μt} and yield linear rates; the paper does not develop such results theoretically.
- Related work positioning: The connections to inertial dynamics with vanishing damping and Hessian-driven damping (e.g., Attouch–Peypouquet–Redont) could be discussed more explicitly to sharpen novelty and boundaries.

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis via Lr and the corollary giving constructive a(t), w(t), η(t) for a target r(t) are sound and recover known accelerated rates. The energy function Er is a nice addition.
- The existence/uniqueness argument is reasonable under the stated (global) Lipschitz assumptions and the t0>0 initialization; the extension to [t0,∞) is justified since the vector field is globally Lipschitz on any finite horizon and the trajectory remains in X.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is principled; however, the text contains inversions that should be corrected to avoid implementation errors.
- Discrete-time theory is underdeveloped in the main paper.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written, but the noted typos in Sections 2 and 4, and in Algorithm 1, are significant and should be corrected. Some notation (Ž, X̌) could be streamlined, and the role/choice of R and γ in Algorithm 1 should be clarified.

Originality
- The generalized averaging formulation and the adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves Lyapunov decrease appear novel and complementary to restart schemes. The accelerated replicator example is a nice, original application of the framework.
- The core ODE perspective is firmly grounded in prior work [11,20,23], and the contribution is an incremental but meaningful extension.

Significance
- The framework is useful for understanding and designing accelerated dynamics, and the adaptive averaging idea could influence practical accelerated methods. However, broader empirical validation and stronger discrete-time guarantees would increase the practical impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, general Lyapunov framework that unifies and extends accelerated dynamics with generalized averaging.
- Simple, principled adaptive heuristic with guaranteed preservation of target rate.
- Insightful primal representation and illustrative examples (accelerated replicator).
- Empirical results suggest benefits over popular restart heuristics.

Cons for acceptance
- Several typographical/inversion errors in key formulas and pseudocode.
- Limited experimental scope; lack of higher-dimensional and standard benchmarks.
- Discrete-time theory largely deferred; no strong-convexity rate guarantees developed.
- Related work on inertial dynamics could be discussed more thoroughly.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the inversions: use a(t)=w/W; for r=t^p choose a(t)=β/t; enforce η(t)=a(t)r(t); fix Algorithm 1 (λk, line 6).
- State the discrete-time convergence guarantee in the main text, with assumptions.
- Explore r(t)=e^{μt} (and other rates) to obtain theoretical linear convergence under strong convexity; compare with restart-based linear rates.
- Expand experiments: higher-dimensional problems (e.g., sparse logistic regression with simplex or other constraints), sensitivity to stepsizes, comparisons against well-tuned restarts and fixed-weight AMD, and report wall-clock time.
- Discuss connections and differences with inertial ODEs with vanishing viscosity and Hessian-driven damping, and with the variational framework [23].

Recommendation: Weak accept
