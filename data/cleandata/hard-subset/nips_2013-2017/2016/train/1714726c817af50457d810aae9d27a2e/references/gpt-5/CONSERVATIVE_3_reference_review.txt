Summary
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for smooth convex optimization over a convex set X. Building on the averaging view of accelerated mirror descent (AMD), the authors introduce a two-parameter family of ODEs, AMDw,η, where w(t) controls averaging and η(t) scales gradient accumulation, and analyze them via a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ∗(Z,z*). They give sufficient conditions on (w,η,r) for Lr to be nonincreasing and obtain f(X(t))−f* ≤ C/r(t). They present a primal representation, specialize to orthant and simplex constraints, and interpret the simplex case as an “accelerated replicator” dynamics. They propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that selects a(t)=w/W along the trajectory to further decrease Lr without sacrificing the nominal rate, and give a discrete algorithm (with details delegated to the supplement). Small-scale experiments on simplex-constrained problems compare the proposed adaptive averaging to restart heuristics, with reported improvements.

Relation to prior work
The paper fits into the burgeoning line connecting Nesterov-style acceleration with ODEs (Su et al. 2014; Wibisono et al. 2016), including the authors’ prior AMD work (Krichene et al. 2015). The Lyapunov-based analysis and the rate f(X(t))−f* = O(1/r(t)) under structural conditions is in the spirit of [23], which parameterizes families via a variational formulation (α,β schedules), and of Attouch–Peypouquet–Redont on inertial systems. The discrete comparisons relate to adaptive restart (O’Donoghue & Candès). The replicator connection is classical for mirror descent; the “accelerated replicator” is a straightforward consequence of the general framework.

Strengths
- Clear and reasonably self-contained continuous-time analysis with a simple, uniform Lyapunov argument. The existence/uniqueness discussion is careful.
- Unified parametrization that recovers known schedules (e.g., Nesterov-like rate with r(t)=t^2) and allows other r(t).
- Primal form derivations and concrete examples (orthant, simplex) aid intuition; the “accelerated replicator” is an appealing interpretation.
- The adaptive averaging idea is natural: choose a(t) to make the Lyapunov derivative as negative as possible; the condition preserves the nominal rate in continuous time.
- Experiments, albeit small, visualize trajectories and report that the Lyapunov function decreases under the heuristic.

Weaknesses
- Novelty over [11,23] appears incremental. The main rate guarantee f(X(t))−f* ≤ C/r(t) depends on a trajectory-dependent condition (Theorem 2, condition 2) that is generally not checkable; the usable corollary imposes a tight coupling η = w r/W and w/W ≥ r’/r. This largely recovers known schedules. The new “adaptive averaging” is a heuristic; its continuous-time guarantee relies on conditions that the algorithm enforces by construction, but the discrete-time algorithm’s guarantees are deferred to the supplement and appear to only preserve standard O(1/k^2) rates.
- Discretization gap. As with many ODE papers, the interesting continuous-time properties do not automatically carry to discrete time. The paper acknowledges that faster-than-quadratic continuous rates require higher-order discretizations [23]. For the proposed first-order discrete method, theoretical guarantees, step-size restrictions, and stability conditions are not established in the main text; empirical evidence is limited.
- Empirical evaluation is small scale (R^3 simplex) and uses synthetic convex objectives. No medium/large-scale convex benchmarks, no comparisons to strong baselines like FISTA with monotone variants, Nesterov with adaptive restart, or modern adaptive-accelerated methods. No sensitivity analysis to β, s, and regularizer R, and no stochastic or sparse-gradient settings where “adaptive” methods are often motivated.
- Claims about “preserving the Lyapunov function” and “preserving the rate” for the heuristic are continuous-time statements; the discrete algorithm that practitioners would use is only sketched. Important details (proofs, algorithmic constants, convergence statements) are relegated to the supplement.
- Some technical imprecision/typos (see below), which matter for clarity (e.g., a(t) should be β/t, not β t).

Quality
Technically sound at the ODE level; the Lyapunov computations and existence/uniqueness are standard and appear correct. However, the core contribution that would matter to a NeurIPS audience—the discrete algorithm with adaptive averaging—lacks a complete, in-main-text convergence theory and robust empirical validation.

Clarity
Generally well written, with helpful intuition. However, several notational slips and minor inaccuracies (e.g., missing “/” in a(t)=β/t), and heavy reliance on the supplement for key algorithmic details, detract from reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. The generalized averaging perspective and energy function are nice unifications, but close to existing ODE frameworks. The adaptive averaging heuristic is conceptually simple but not deeply substantiated theoretically in discrete time.

Significance
Currently limited. Without stronger discrete-time guarantees and broader experiments, it is unclear that practitioners will adopt the method over established accelerated schemes with adaptive restart.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify the conditions under which the discrete adaptive averaging algorithm provably preserves O(1/k^2). Are step sizes fixed or line-searched? What assumptions on ψ, R are needed?
2. Can you provide a monotone variant (like MFISTA) that preserves Lyapunov decrease in discrete time?
3. How sensitive is performance to β and the step size s? Any automatic tuning?
4. Can the heuristic yield provably faster (e.g., linear) rates under strong convexity?
5. How does the method fare on higher-dimensional simplex problems and unconstrained problems with Euclidean prox? Comparisons to FISTA, APG with adaptive restart?

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, unified ODE analysis; connects acceleration and averaging with a general schedule.
- Intuitive adaptive averaging idea; interesting “accelerated replicator” viewpoint.
- Potentially useful Lyapunov/energy framework for designing schedules.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental over established ODE acceleration literature; main novelty is heuristic.
- Discrete-time theory not in main paper; empirical validation limited and not against strong baselines.
- Reproducibility concerns due to reliance on supplement.

Typos/notes
- Example 1–2: a(t) should be β/t (not β t); otherwise dimensions and subsequent calculations are inconsistent.
- “MD can be interpreted as a limit of AMD with w a Dirac at t”: this is heuristic; consider rephrasing more rigorously.
- Ensure all cited results (e.g., discrete convergence bound for Algorithm 1) are stated precisely in the main text.

Overall recommendation: 5 (borderline). I encourage the authors to strengthen the discrete-time analysis in the main paper and substantially expand experiments; with those additions, the work could be impactful. Confidence: 4/5.
