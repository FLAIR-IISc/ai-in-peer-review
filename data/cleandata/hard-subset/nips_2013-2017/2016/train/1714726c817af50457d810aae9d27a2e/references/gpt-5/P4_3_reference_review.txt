Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics and generalizes prior ODE formulations by introducing two time-dependent weight functions: w(t), which governs averaging, and η(t), which scales the gradient accumulation in the dual variable. Building on the averaging interpretation of accelerated methods (e.g., Krichene et al. [11]) and the ODE perspective on Nesterov’s acceleration (Su et al. [20], Wibisono et al. [23]), the authors:

- Propose a family of AMD ODEs parameterized by w(t), η(t), and analyze them using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ∗(Z, z*), where r(t) is a target rate.
- Provide sufficient conditions linking r, w, η under which Lr decreases, implying f(X(t)) − f* = O(1/r(t)). A constructive recipe (via the ratio a(t) = w(t)/W(t)) yields choices of w and η to achieve prescribed rates r(t) (e.g., r(t) = tp).
- Give a second decreasing “energy” Er(t) = f(X(t)) + (1/r(t))Dψ∗(Z, ∇ψ(X)) that interprets kinetic/potential energy and is guaranteed to decrease under the same conditions.
- Provide a primal formulation and examples, including positive-orthant and simplex constraints. For the simplex with entropy mirror map, they identify an “accelerated” replicator dynamics.
- Introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) online to further decrease Lr whenever possible, preserving the O(1/r(t)) rate. A discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is proposed for r(t) = t2 and compared empirically to gradient and speed restarts [17, 20] on low-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems.

The work unifies and extends several strands: the averaging view of acceleration [8, 11], inertial/variational ODEs [20, 23], and adaptive schemes (related in spirit to AdaGrad/Adam [7, 10], though those target step-size adaptation rather than acceleration).

Quality (technical soundness)
- Continuous-time analysis appears sound. Existence/uniqueness (Theorem 1) is justified via Cauchy–Lipschitz under Lipschitz ∇f and ∇ψ∗, and positivity of W ensures bounded w/W. The Lyapunov derivative bound (Eq. 3) correctly leverages convexity to control the inner products. The sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) are clear and recover known rates as special cases (Corollary 1). The construction via a(t) and Eq. (4) is useful and explicit. The energy function decrease (Theorem 3) follows from monotonicity of ∇ψ∗ and the same condition.
- The primal representations are consistent; the examples for orthant and simplex lead to intuitive dynamics (mirror descent becomes mass-matrix scaled gradient flow; replicator is recovered, with an accelerated variant).
- The adaptive averaging is well-motivated: choosing a(t) ≥ η(t)r(t) and increasing it when ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ ≤ 0 can only lower dLr/dt while preserving rate guarantees.
- Discrete-time claims rely on supplementary proofs. In the main text, guarantees for Algorithm 1 (e.g., preserved O(1/k2) rate) are stated but not shown; the connection between the continuous-time sign condition ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩ and the discrete acceptance test f(x̃(k+1)) ≤ f(x̃(k)) could be stated more explicitly. Stability and step-size conditions are not fully discussed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, with clear derivations and good intuition (Lyapunov/energy viewpoints, examples). The relation to [23] via α(t), β(t) is helpful.
- Some minor clarity issues and typos:
  - In Example 1, a(t) should be β/t (consistent with r′/r = 2/t) rather than β t; the subsequent formulas (w(t) ∝ (t/t0)β−1, η(t) = β t) match a(t) = β/t.
  - Algorithm 1, line 6: “βmax” is undefined; please clarify whether this is a cap on β or a typo for βk/√s.
  - Notation alternates between Ž and Ž(t) and uses both ψ and ψ∗ strong smoothness assumptions; the exact smoothness/strong convexity requirements on ψ, ψ∗ should be collected in one place.
  - References lack complete bibliographic details.

Originality
- The generalized averaging framework and simple sufficient conditions for achieving a desired rate r(t) in AMD ODEs are a meaningful extension of [11, 20, 23].
- The decreasing energy function Er and its kinetic/potential interpretation add insight.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic that provably preserves the target rate (by decreasing Lr) is novel and practically relevant. The accelerated replicator dynamics connection is also of interest.

Significance
- Conceptually, the paper offers a unifying and flexible way to design accelerated mirror descent dynamics with prescribed rates and an adaptive mechanism to improve practical performance while preserving theoretical guarantees in continuous time.
- Empirically, the experiments are limited (R3 simplex problems), but indicate benefits over popular restart heuristics. Demonstrations on higher-dimensional, large-scale convex tasks (e.g., logistic regression with entropy/Euclidean mirror maps), and comparisons to more baselines (e.g., FISTA with adaptive restarts, Catalyst, and step-size-adaptive methods) would strengthen practical significance.
- A discrete-time theory beyond r(t) = t2 and beyond smooth deterministic gradients would increase impact (e.g., strongly convex rates, stochastic gradients, composite objectives).

Pros
- Clear and general continuous-time framework with constructive conditions to achieve O(1/r(t)).
- Lyapunov and energy analyses are simple, reusable, and insightful.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with continuous-time guarantees; intuitive and effective in experiments.
- Connection to replicator dynamics and constrained settings.

Cons
- Discrete-time guarantees are deferred to the supplement; the main text lacks formal statements (assumptions, rates, step-size conditions).
- Experiments are small-scale and limited to simplex constraints; broader benchmarks and ablations are missing.
- Minor technical/typographical issues (a(t) typo; Algorithm 1 ambiguity; incomplete references).
- Condition (2) in Theorem 2 is trajectory-dependent; while the corollary sidesteps it, discussion of its tightness/necessity would help.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please correct the a(t) typo in Example 1 and clarify Algorithm 1 line 6 (“βmax”).
- Provide, in the main text, a theorem summarizing the discrete-time rate and assumptions for Algorithm 1, and explain the link between the continuous-time sign condition and the discrete acceptance test.
- Discuss parameter sensitivity (β, s), computational overhead, and choices of ψ, R. Can the approach handle composite objectives with proximal steps?
- Extend experiments to higher dimensions and standard convex benchmarks; compare to FISTA with adaptive restarts, Catalyst, and, where appropriate, step-size adaptive methods.
- For strongly convex f, can the adaptive averaging be shown to yield faster rates (e.g., linear in continuous time) with a suitable r(t)?

Overall, the paper provides a solid and elegant continuous-time analysis with a practical adaptive idea. Strengthening the discrete-time theory and empirical evaluation would significantly improve its impact.
