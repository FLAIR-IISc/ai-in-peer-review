Summary
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with generalized averaging. It introduces a two-parameter family of ODEs (weights w(t) for averaging and η(t) for gradient accumulation) and analyzes them via a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), where r(t) encodes a target rate. The main result gives simple sufficient conditions on (w,η,r) that ensure Lr is decreasing, yielding f(X(t))−f* = O(1/r(t)). A second “energy” function Er(t) = f(X(t)) + (1/r(t))Dψ*(Z,∇ψ(X)) is also shown to be non-increasing under the same condition. The paper provides a primal representation, applies it to orthant/simplex constraints, yielding an “accelerated replicator” dynamics, and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts the weighting online to further decrease Lr while preserving the target rate. A discrete-time algorithm is sketched and empirically compared to gradient and speed restarts on small simplex-constrained problems.

Relation to prior work
- Su, Boyd, Candès (Nesterov ODE) and Krichene et al. previously established AMD with t^2 rate via a specific averaging schedule. This paper generalizes to arbitrary r(t) and weight schedules satisfying a simple inequality, recovering the classical t^2 case.
- Wibisono, Wilson, Jordan’s “Bregman Lagrangian” also parameterizes continuous-time accelerated flows for arbitrary rates (via α,β schedules) and derives energy dissipation/ideal scaling conditions. The present work is closely related but framed via averaging and dual/primal couplings; the corollary η = (w r)/W and w/W ≥ r′/r essentially matches the ideal-scaling requirement in [23]. The connection is noted but could be made more explicit.
- Restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès; Su et al.) are used as baselines; here the proposed adaptive averaging offers a principled way to ensure Lyapunov decrease without restarts.

Strengths
- Clean, unified Lyapunov analysis that recovers and extends known accelerated flows to a broad class via weight design; simple sufficient conditions are easy to check/construct (through a(t)=w/W).
- The “energy” function Er adds intuitive kinetic/potential interpretation and may be of independent interest.
- Existence/uniqueness and feasibility are handled carefully by initializing at t0>0 and using Lipschitz assumptions.
- The accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex is a nice conceptual contribution that ties optimization and evolutionary dynamics, with clear derivations.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is simple, grounded in the Lyapunov derivative, and in continuous time provably preserves the intended rate.
- Experiments qualitatively support reduced oscillations and improved performance versus restarts; Lyapunov/energy plots are informative.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate relative to [23] (Bregman Lagrangian) and [11]: the main continuous-time conditions and achievable rates largely mirror the ideal-scaling framework; the averaging viewpoint is appealing but arguably a reparameterization. Please clarify the precise technical advantage versus [23], beyond the averaging representation and specific examples.
- The key sufficient condition (Theorem 2, item 2) is trajectory-dependent and not verifiable a priori; the main usable corollary enforces η = (w r)/W and w/W ≥ r′/r, which limits adaptivity unless one departs from equality. The proposed adaptive rule ensures the condition, but the discrete version does not directly test ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ and instead uses function decrease on x̃; the theoretical bridge is deferred to the supplement. The main paper would benefit from a concise discrete-time guarantee and its assumptions.
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale (R^3 simplex) and deterministic; no large-scale, noisy, or stochastic-gradient settings are explored, and no wall-clock or oracle complexity comparison is provided. Comparisons to other adaptive methods (Adagrad/Adam) are discussed conceptually but not tested.
- Some technical assumptions are glossed over: for entropy on the simplex with an indicator, ψ is non-smooth; the Er analysis assumes differentiable ψ (stated “can be relaxed” but not shown). Clarify the exact conditions under which Er is decreasing with entropy/barrier choices common in mirror descent.
- Clarity issues/typos: In Example 1 and 2, a(t) should be β/t (consistent with r′/r = p/t), but the text writes β t in places; please correct. The expression for w(t) in (4) is scale-invariant, but constants are casually handled; be explicit about w(t0). References lack full bibliographic details.

Questions for the authors
1) Please make the link to Wibisono et al. precise: can your conditions be derived as a special case of their ideal-scaling (with a = e^α, r = e^β, η = a r)? Is there a setting where your framework yields strictly new schedules or Lyapunov constructions not covered there?
2) For the entropy/simplex case, under which precise assumptions on ψ (with indicator) do the Er results hold? If not, can you provide a version using Bregman divergences with subgradients?
3) Discrete algorithm: What assumptions and step-size rules ensure the stated rate for adaptive averaging? Can you give the main theorem and proof sketch in the paper (at least for smooth convex f and a standard ψ) rather than deferring entirely to the supplement?
4) How sensitive is performance to β and s? Is there an automatic tuning? Any guarantees under stochastic gradients?
5) Can the heuristic yield improved rates for strongly convex f (e.g., linear convergence) with an appropriate r(t), and do experiments reflect this?

Suggestions for improvement
- State a succinct discrete-time theorem for Algorithm 1 in the main text, with assumptions and rate (constants and dependence on initial Bregman divergence).
- Expand the discussion contrasting your Lyapunov-based generalized averaging to the Bregman Lagrangian framework, highlighting what is genuinely new (e.g., the adaptive averaging principle and its guarantees).
- Fix the a(t) typos; clarify the Lipschitz assumptions on ∇ψ* for the chosen ψ.
- Add at least one higher-dimensional experiment and a stochastic setting; include tuning details and runtime.
- Consider comparing to monotone adaptive restart variants and to Nesterov’s composite acceleration when R is nonzero.

Overall evaluation
- Quality: solid continuous-time analysis; discrete-time guarantees need to be clearer in the main text; experiments are limited.
- Clarity: generally good, but some typos and missing details.
- Originality: modest; the averaging perspective and adaptive heuristic are interesting but closely related to prior continuous-time acceleration frameworks.
- Significance: potential to influence how practitioners tune accelerated methods; current empirical evidence is preliminary.

Recommendation: Borderline accept (weak accept if discrete-time guarantees are clarified in the main text and typos fixed). Confidence: high.
