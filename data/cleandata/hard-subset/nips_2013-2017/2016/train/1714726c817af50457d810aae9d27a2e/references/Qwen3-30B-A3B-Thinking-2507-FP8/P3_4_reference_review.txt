

# Peer Review: "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary
This paper studies continuous-time accelerated optimization dynamics, generalizing the Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) framework through a flexible averaging scheme parameterized by weight functions w and η. The authors introduce a unified Lyapunov function approach to establish convergence rates, prove sufficient conditions for the dynamics to achieve a desired rate 1/r(t), and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts the weights while preserving the convergence guarantee. The paper connects this framework to the replicator dynamics for simplex constraints, and provides numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging to existing restart heuristics.

The work builds on the influential continuous-time interpretation of Nesterov's acceleration by Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11], extending their framework to a broader class of dynamics with generalized averaging. The adaptive heuristic represents a novel approach to acceleration that differs from existing restart heuristics by dynamically adjusting weights rather than resetting the trajectory.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by providing a unified framework for analyzing accelerated dynamics with generalized averaging. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 establish clear sufficient conditions for achieving a given convergence rate, which is a significant advancement over previous work.

2. **Theoretical Rigor**: The mathematical analysis is thorough and well-executed. The Lyapunov function approach is appropriately applied, and the connection between the continuous-time dynamics and discrete-time algorithms is theoretically grounded.

3. **Novel Application**: The connection to the replicator dynamics for simplex constraints (Section 3.2) is both theoretically interesting and potentially impactful for fields like evolutionary game theory, demonstrating the framework's applicability beyond standard optimization.

4. **Practical Heuristic**: The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated by the Lyapunov analysis and provides a theoretically grounded alternative to existing restart heuristics, which often lack convergence guarantees for non-strongly convex problems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The numerical experiments (Section 5) are restricted to R^3 simplex problems, which are too simple to demonstrate the method's potential on real-world machine learning problems. More comprehensive experiments on larger-scale problems would strengthen the claims.

2. **Discrete-Time Connection**: The transition from continuous-time dynamics to discrete-time Algorithm 1 is not clearly articulated in the main text. The paper mentions a "discretization" in the supplementary material, but the theoretical justification for how this discretization preserves the convergence rate needs to be more explicit.

3. **Comparison with Existing Adaptive Methods**: While the paper correctly notes that adaptive averaging differs from methods like Adagrad and Adam, it doesn't sufficiently compare with more recent adaptive acceleration methods, such as those in [16, 23], which could provide a stronger basis for claiming novelty.

4. **Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't analyze the computational overhead of the adaptive heuristic compared to simpler restart heuristics, which is a practical concern for implementation.

## Originality and Significance

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the growing literature on continuous-time interpretations of optimization algorithms. While the Lyapunov function approach itself is not novel, the generalization to a broad class of averaging schemes and the development of an adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence guarantees is significant. The connection to replicator dynamics is particularly noteworthy.

However, the significance could be strengthened by better articulating how adaptive averaging improves upon existing restart heuristics in practical machine learning contexts. The experiments show improvement, but the magnitude of the improvement is not substantial enough to clearly establish a significant advance over existing methods.

## Specific Comments

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the adaptive heuristic (Equation 8) is discretized in Algorithm 1. The connection between the continuous-time analysis and the discrete algorithm needs to be more explicit.

2. The authors claim their heuristic "preserves the convergence rate" (Section 4), but don't provide a discrete-time convergence rate bound for Algorithm 1. This would strengthen the theoretical claims.

3. The comparison with existing restart heuristics (Section 5) is limited to two methods [17, 20]. Including a comparison with more recent adaptive methods would provide a more complete picture.

4. The supplementary material contains important details (e.g., the discrete-time convergence bound), but the main paper should reference these key results more prominently.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, and the connection to replicator dynamics is a nice application. However, the limited experimental validation and insufficient articulation of the discrete-time algorithm's theoretical guarantees prevent me from recommending acceptance at NIPS.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More comprehensive numerical experiments on larger-scale problems
2. A clearer theoretical connection between continuous-time dynamics and the discrete algorithm
3. A more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the adaptive heuristic
4. A stronger comparison with recent adaptive acceleration methods

With these improvements, the paper would make a compelling contribution to the field. As it stands, it represents a solid but incremental advance that would be better suited for a specialized optimization journal or conference.

## Final Assessment

**Quality**: 7/10 (Sound theory, but limited experimental validation)
**Clarity**: 6/10 (Well-structured but lacks clarity on discrete-time connection)
**Originality**: 7/10 (Meaningful extension of existing framework)
**Significance**: 6/10 (Theoretical contribution is strong, but practical impact needs more demonstration)

**Overall**: Weak Accept (Borderline for NIPS) - The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution that advances the understanding of accelerated optimization, but the limited empirical validation and insufficient articulation of the discrete-time algorithm prevent it from being a strong NIPS contribution.
