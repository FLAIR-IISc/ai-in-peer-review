

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies continuous-time accelerated optimization methods with a generalized averaging scheme, extending the authors' prior work on accelerated mirror descent [11]. The authors introduce a family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, and provide sufficient conditions for convergence rates using a Lyapunov function approach. They propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates while potentially improving performance, with numerical experiments demonstrating its advantages over existing restarting heuristics.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a well-structured theoretical framework with clear proofs of sufficient conditions for convergence. The Lyapunov function approach is appropriately applied to establish convergence rates, with Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 providing precise conditions under which the convergence rate is guaranteed. The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is well-explained.

2. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. Mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the connection between continuous-time analysis and discrete algorithms is clearly articulated. The examples for positive-orthant and simplex constraints (particularly the replicator dynamics application) are particularly well-executed and insightful.

3. **Significance of Results:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is a meaningful contribution that preserves convergence rates while improving performance across multiple problem types. The numerical experiments (Section 5) clearly demonstrate that adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics in all tested cases, with significant improvements in the strongly convex case.

4. **Connection to Prior Work:** The paper clearly situates itself within the literature on continuous-time optimization, building on and extending the work of Su et al. [20] and the authors' own previous work [11], while providing a unified framework for analyzing accelerated methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental Nature of Contribution:** While the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, the core theoretical framework represents a natural extension of the authors' prior NIPS paper [11]. The paper could better articulate how this work differs from and advances beyond their previous contribution, particularly in terms of the adaptive mechanism.

2. **Comparison to Other Adaptive Methods:** The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish adaptive averaging from other adaptive methods like Adagrad [7] and Adam [10]. It would be valuable to clarify how adaptive averaging differs from these methods, particularly in the context of accelerated optimization, and why the proposed approach is better suited for this specific problem.

3. **Practical Significance Justification:** While the numerical experiments are promising, the paper could better justify the practical significance of the results for large-scale machine learning applications. The connection to real-world machine learning problems could be strengthened.

4. **Discrete-Time Translation:** The paper briefly mentions the translation to discrete-time algorithms but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the continuous-time results generalize beyond the specific case they consider. A more detailed discussion of the discrete-time implementation and its computational complexity would be valuable.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the theory of accelerated optimization. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and demonstrated to be effective through numerical experiments. The paper's main weakness is that the contribution is somewhat incremental compared to the authors' prior work, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from other adaptive optimization methods.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. More explicitly articulate how this work advances beyond their prior NIPS paper [11].
2. Provide a clearer distinction between adaptive averaging and other adaptive optimization methods like Adagrad and Adam.
3. Strengthen the justification for the practical significance of the results in machine learning contexts.
4. Offer a more thorough analysis of the discrete-time translation of the continuous-time results.

The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the relationship between adaptive averaging and existing adaptive methods, and a clearer articulation of the practical advantages of the proposed approach for machine learning practitioners.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear convergence rate guarantees
- Novel adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence rates while improving performance
- Well-executed numerical experiments across multiple problem types
- Clear connection to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory

*Con:*
- Contribution is somewhat incremental compared to authors' prior work
- Insufficient distinction from other adaptive optimization methods
- Limited justification for practical significance in machine learning contexts
- Superficial treatment of discrete-time translation

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theory of accelerated optimization methods, and with the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
