This paper studies accelerated mirror descent dynamics, generalizing previous work connecting Nesterov acceleration to continuous-time ODEs. The core contribution lies in analyzing a broad family of AMD dynamics parameterized by weight functions *w* and *η*, and providing sufficient conditions for quadratic convergence. A novel adaptive averaging heuristic is proposed, aiming to preserve convergence rates by dynamically adjusting the weights based on the Lyapunov function’s derivative. Numerical experiments demonstrate favorable performance compared to existing restarting heuristics.

While the paper builds upon a solid foundation of prior work (Su et al., Nesterov, etc.), and the idea of generalized averaging is natural and potentially valuable, the presentation suffers from a plethora of minor, yet distracting, issues that undermine the overall impact. The mathematical formalism, while correct, is often unnecessarily convoluted, hindering clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The generalization of AMD dynamics and the introduction of adaptive averaging represent genuine contributions, offering a new perspective on acceleration techniques.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The Lyapunov analysis provides a rigorous framework for understanding the convergence behavior of the proposed dynamics.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively bridges the gap between continuous-time dynamics and discrete-time algorithms, extending earlier results.
*   **Numerical Validation:** The experiments provide empirical support for the adaptive averaging heuristic, demonstrating its competitiveness with existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is densely written and lacks sufficient explanation of key concepts. The notation is often cumbersome, and the transitions between ideas are abrupt. For instance, the motivation for initializing the ODE at t0 > 0 is stated but not adequately explained beyond ensuring existence, leaving the reader wondering about the practical implications.
*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:** Several minor errors and inconsistencies detract from the paper’s rigor. For example, the statement “if f? is the minimum of f over the feasible set” is imprecise. f? usually denotes the *value* of the minimum, not the set of minimizers S. Similarly, the use of the term ‘mirror map’ feels unnecessarily vague without consistent adherence to standard convex analysis terminology.
*   **Overly Detailed, Yet Insufficiently Explanatory Derivations:** While derivations are provided, they often lack intuitive explanations. The leap from the primal form to the example dynamics (simplex constrained) isn't clearly delineated for readers not intimately familiar with replicator dynamics.
*   **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The experiments are restricted to relatively simple scenarios. The impact of the adaptive averaging heuristic on more complex, high-dimensional problems remains unexplored. The comparative analysis with restarting heuristics feels superficial, lacking a thorough investigation of parameter sensitivity.
*   **Redundancy:** The derivation of the primal form appears largely redundant, as it doesn’t unlock substantially new insights.
*   **Formatting Issues:** Minor formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper, like uneven spacing and inconsistent capitalization, are distracting. (e.g., "Lr" is sometimes written as "Lr", and sometimes as "Lr".)

**Specific Concerns:**

*   Equation (1) should clearly define *W(t)* as the integral from *t0* to *t*. The current presentation is ambiguous.
*   The discussion surrounding the Bregman divergence lacks sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with the concept.
*   The condition `r(t) − η(t)W(t)w(t)` in Theorem 2 appears problematic, potentially leading to negative values. The authors should carefully examine this condition.
*   The link between the energy function *Er* and kinetic energy, while intuitively appealing, needs further justification.
*   Algorithm 1 uses `ak = min(ak−1, βk√s)`, which feels strangely constrained; the maximum value of a is not utilized.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel theoretical framework for analyzing accelerated mirror descent.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic shows promise and warrants further investigation.
*   The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete-time algorithms is valuable for algorithm design.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The numerous minor flaws and lack of clarity significantly detract from the paper’s impact.
*   The limited scope of the numerical experiments reduces confidence in the practical benefits of the proposed heuristic.
*   The presentation requires substantial revision to improve readability and accessibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the inherent novelty and theoretical soundness, the paper falls short of NIPS standards due to its lack of clarity, numerous minor flaws, and limited experimental evaluation. While the ideas are promising, the presentation needs substantial polishing to ensure that the contribution is fully appreciated by the community. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns raised in this review. A significant rewrite, focusing on clarity and rigor, is essential. The authors should also expand the numerical experiments and provide a more in-depth analysis of the adaptive averaging heuristic.
