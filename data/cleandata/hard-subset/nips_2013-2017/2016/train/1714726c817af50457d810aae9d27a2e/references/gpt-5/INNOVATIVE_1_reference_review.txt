Summary
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated optimization through the lens of generalized averaging. It introduces a family of Accelerated Mirror Descent dynamics AMDw,η parameterized by two positive weight functions w(t) (for averaging) and η(t) (gradient accumulation), and analyzes them via a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*). The main result (Theorem 2) provides simple sufficient conditions on (w,η,r) ensuring Lr is non-increasing, yielding convergence rate O(1/r(t)). A constructive recipe (via a(t)=w/W) produces weights for a target rate r(t), recovering known 1/t^2 rates and generalizing to r(t)=t^p. A second “energy” function Er that decreases under the same conditions is also established.

The paper further gives a primal representation of the dynamics, with examples on the positive orthant and the simplex, yielding an “accelerated replicator” ODE. The key algorithmic contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) along the trajectory to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative while preserving the same rate. A discrete-time scheme (Algorithm 1) is proposed, and experiments on simplex-constrained problems compare adaptive averaging to popular restarting heuristics, showing consistently favorable performance and monotone Lyapunov decrease.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on ODE interpretations of acceleration (Su et al.; Wibisono et al.) and on Krichene et al.’s accelerated mirror descent formulation linking acceleration and averaging. Compared to the Bregman Lagrangian view, the present contribution isolates the role of averaging weights in a unified AMD ODE and introduces an adaptive mechanism with continuous-time guarantees for general convex objectives (not only strongly convex). The accelerated replicator example connects optimization dynamics to evolutionary game theory, extending prior links for non-accelerated replicator dynamics.

Strengths
- Novel perspective and unification: Casting acceleration as generalized averaging with two weight functions is clean and broadly applicable; it subsumes earlier formulations and clarifies when a given target rate is achievable.
- Simple, checkable conditions: The Lyapunov analysis provides intuitive sufficient conditions and a constructive recipe to realize r(t), including t^p with p≥1. The energy function Er adds further insight.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic: New idea tailored to accelerated methods that provably preserves the Lyapunov decrease and the intended rate, contrasted with heuristic restarts that lack such guarantees in weak convexity.
- Interesting example: An accelerated replicator ODE on the simplex is derived naturally from the framework.
- Empirical evidence: On several simplex-constrained problems, the adaptive averaging compares favorably with gradient and speed restarts, while ensuring monotone Lyapunov decrease.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scope of experiments: Results are primarily in 3D simplices with entropy mirror map. The empirical section would be stronger with higher-dimensional problems, other constraint sets (e.g., ℓ1-ball, spectrahedron), alternative mirror maps, and non-smooth settings (prox).
- Discretization guarantees: While the continuous-time guarantees are clear, the discrete-time Algorithm 1’s convergence proof is deferred to the supplement and seems to show preservation of the 1/k^2 rate for p=2. It would help to make precise when discretization preserves the continuous-time rate for general r(t) and how step sizes/parameters are chosen.
- Strengthening rates: For strongly convex objectives, the paper hints at the possibility of faster rates but does not provide continuous-time or discrete-time results (beyond experiments). A formal result (e.g., linear convergence with appropriate w,η or via adaptive choices) would substantially increase impact.
- Condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on the trajectory, hence hard to verify a priori. While the corollary removes this via η=a r, it would be valuable to discuss the suboptimality, if any, of this conservative choice.
- Clarity and typos in formulas: There appear to be inconsistencies. For example, in Theorem 2’s condition (2) the term involves r(t) − η(t) W(t)/w(t); to make it identically zero one needs η(t) = a(t) r(t) with a(t)=w(t)/W(t). Some places write η(t)=w(t) r(t) W(t) (missing division), and Example 1 alternates between a(t)=β/t and a(t)=β t, while subsequent derivations align with a(t)=β/t. These should be corrected.

Questions for the authors
- Can you characterize when adaptive averaging yields strictly better constants (or even better asymptotic rates) than the baseline η=a r choice? Any conditions under which a(t) > η r is systematically beneficial?
- How sensitive is Algorithm 1 to the choice of β, s, and the regularizer R? Could you provide ablations or guidelines?
- Can the heuristic be extended to stochastic settings or composite objectives with proximal operators, and does the Lyapunov argument still go through?
- For strongly convex f, can you prove faster continuous-time rates (e.g., r(t) exponential) with suitable w,η and an adaptive mechanism?

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Fix the notational inconsistencies around a(t), w/W, and η. Explicitly restate the mapping η=a r and cross-check Example 1/2.
- Expand experiments: include higher dimensions, different feasible sets and mirror maps, and comparisons to adaptive restart variants; report wall-clock vs iterations and sensitivity plots.
- Bring key parts of the discrete-time analysis into the main text for p=2 (and discuss p>2), clarifying when the rate is preserved and the trade-offs vs higher-order discretizations (e.g., cubic regularization).
- Discuss connections and differences with the Bregman Lagrangian viewpoint more explicitly, and with recent continuous-time inertial systems (Attouch and co-authors), to position the contribution.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and unifying view of acceleration via adaptive averaging, with clear theoretical guarantees.
- Introduces a simple, practical adaptive mechanism that preserves Lyapunov decrease and outperforms common restarts in experiments.
- Bridges optimization and evolutionary dynamics with an accelerated replicator ODE.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental scope is limited; discrete-time guarantees could be made more prominent.
- Some presentation issues and formula inconsistencies.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper proposes a fresh, broadly applicable idea—adaptive averaging in accelerated dynamics—with solid theory in continuous time, a reasonable discretization, and promising empirical behavior. Despite minor clarity issues and limited experiments, the conceptual advance and potential impact justify acceptance.

Confidence
High for the theoretical aspects; medium for the empirical claims pending broader evaluation.
