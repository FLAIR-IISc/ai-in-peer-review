Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time, first-order dynamics for minimizing a smooth convex function over a convex set, focusing on the accelerated mirror descent (AMD) viewpoint where the primal variable is an average of a transformed dual trajectory. The authors introduce a broad two-parameter family of ODEs, AMDw,η, in which the averaging weights w(t) and the gradient accumulation rate η(t) are arbitrary positive functions. They analyze these dynamics via a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) for a target rate r(t), and give simple sufficient conditions on w, η that guarantee d/dt Lr ≤ 0 and hence f(X(t))−f* = O(1/r(t)). A second “energy” function Er(t) = f(X(t)) + (1/r(t))Dψ*(Z,∇ψ(X)) is also shown to decrease under the same conditions, providing a potential/kinetic interpretation.

The work generalizes prior ODE perspectives on Nesterov acceleration (e.g., Su, Boyd, and Candès 2014; Wibisono, Wilson, Jordan 2016) and the authors’ earlier AMD formulation (Krichene, Bayen, Bartlett 2015). It recovers classical t^2 rates and extends to r(t)=t^p (p≥1), with explicit choices of w and η. Equivalent primal forms are derived, with examples for the positive orthant and the simplex; the latter yields an “accelerated replicator” ODE, linking acceleration to evolutionary dynamics. Motivated by the Lyapunov derivative, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic: choose a(t)=w/W to make the extra term in d/dt Lr as negative as possible while maintaining the Lyapunov conditions. A discrete algorithm is offered (Algorithm 1), and experiments on simplex-constrained problems compare the adaptive scheme to gradient and speed restarting heuristics, showing favorable performance and monotonic decrease of the Lyapunov function.

Quality
- Theory: The continuous-time derivations are sound and clearly grounded in convex analysis and ODE theory. The Lyapunov argument provides transparent sufficient conditions for a target rate 1/r(t), and the existence/uniqueness proof (via Picard–Lindelöf/Cauchy–Lipschitz) is appropriate. The energy function Er is a nice, independently useful invariant.
- Generality vs checkability: One condition (η ≥ r′ and a sign condition involving ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ and r−ηW/w) is trajectory dependent and hard to verify a priori; the corollary provides a convenient sufficient choice (η = r a and a ≥ r′/r with a=w/W), restoring checkability.
- Discretization: The adaptive averaging is rigorously justified in continuous time. The discrete algorithm is appealing but its theoretical guarantees are deferred to the supplement; in the main paper, a precise discrete-time rate guarantee for the adaptive variant is not established. Algorithm 1 contains some ambiguities (see Clarity).
- Empirics: Experiments are clean, visualize trajectories, and include multiple convex objectives. However, they are limited to low-dimensional simplex constraints; step-size/parameter selection and sensitivity are not discussed, and comparisons are limited to restarting variants rather than widely used accelerated prox/MD baselines.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear motivation and derivations. The primal forms and examples help intuition.
- Notational issues/typos:
  - Example 1 appears to have a typo: for r(t)=t^2, r′/r=2/t, so a(t) should be β/t (not β t). The subsequent expressions w(t)∝(t/t0)^(β−1) and η(t)=β t match a(t)=β/t.
  - Algorithm 1: λk is written as λk = √s ak/(+√s ak). Please correct the formula. Also define γ in Step 4 and specify the role/assumptions on R (strong convexity parameter, norm).
  - Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues).
- The discrete heuristic’s connection to the continuous-time condition ⟨∇f(X),Ẋ⟩(r−ηW/w)≤0 is discussed only qualitatively; making that mapping explicit would improve readability.

Originality
- The generalized averaging AMD family and the Lyapunov template for arbitrary r(t) are a clear and useful synthesis beyond prior specific ODEs. The adaptive averaging idea—choosing a(t) online to decrease the Lyapunov derivative—appears novel in the accelerated mirror-descent context and distinct from restarting schemes and per-coordinate step-size methods (Adagrad/Adam).
- The accelerated replicator dynamics and the energy interpretation add fresh perspectives linking optimization and evolutionary dynamics.

Significance
- The work offers a unifying lens on accelerated dynamics with flexible rates and a practical heuristic with monotonic Lyapunov decrease, potentially informing the design of adaptive accelerated algorithms beyond the simplex. The adaptive scheme compares favorably to well-known restarts and could be adopted in practice.
- Broader empirical validation (higher dimensions, other constraint sets, proximal/nonsmooth settings, stochastic gradients) would further establish impact.

Pros
- Clean, general Lyapunov-based analysis yielding O(1/r(t)) under simple conditions.
- Conceptually appealing adaptive averaging grounded in the Lyapunov derivative; preserves rate in continuous time.
- Primal formulations and concrete examples (accelerated replicator).
- Monotone energy function providing additional insight.
- Empirical improvements over popular restarting heuristics.

Cons
- Discrete-time theory for the adaptive variant is not presented in the main text; Algorithm 1 specification is incomplete/ambiguous.
- Experiments are limited in scope and baselines; parameter selection and robustness are not systematically addressed.
- The key condition in Theorem 2 is trajectory dependent; practical checks rely on corollaries or heuristics.
- Some typos and notation issues (especially in Example 1 and Algorithm 1) that can confuse readers.

Questions and suggestions
- Please provide the exact discrete Lyapunov function and the convergence rate guarantee for Algorithm 1 in the main paper (even as a theorem statement), and specify assumptions on ψ, R, and step sizes.
- Clarify the mapping from the continuous-time condition to the discrete trigger f(x̃(k+1))≤f(x̃(k)); can you bound a discrete analogue of ⟨∇f,Δx⟩?
- Discuss sensitivity to β and s, and provide guidelines or adaptive rules for choosing them. Report wall-clock or gradient-evaluation counts.
- Include experiments on higher-dimensional problems, other feasible sets (e.g., ℓ1-ball, spectrahedron), and with stochastic gradients.
- Compare to standard accelerated mirror-descent baselines without restarts and to FISTA-type methods when applicable.

Minor comments
- Replace “Cauchy-Lipschitz” with “Picard–Lindelöf” or mention both.
- Ensure all references have complete bibliographic information.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper provides a solid theoretical contribution that generalizes and clarifies accelerated dynamics via adaptive averaging, with a conceptually novel heuristic backed by continuous-time guarantees and promising empirical results. Addressing the clarity issues and strengthening the discrete-time theory/exposition would further enhance the paper’s impact.

Confidence
High on the ODE/optimization aspects; moderate on the completeness of the discrete-time claims pending supplemental details.
