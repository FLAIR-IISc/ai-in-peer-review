Summary
The paper studies continuous-time, first-order dynamics for minimizing a smooth convex function over a convex set via an “accelerated mirror descent” (AMD) viewpoint. The key contribution is to generalize the averaging scheme in AMD by introducing two positive weight functions: w(t) for averaging the “mirror” trajectory and η(t) for the rate of accumulation of gradients in the dual variable. The authors define a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), and derive simple sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) that guarantee Lr is nonincreasing and that f(X(t))−f* decays as O(1/r(t)). A convenient sufficient condition (Corollary 1) selects η(t) = w(t)r(t)/W(t) with W(t) = ∫ w and requires w(t)/W(t) ≥ r′(t)/r(t); this recovers previously known O(1/t^2) rates and extends to more general rates r(t) = t^p. The paper also presents an “energy” function Er that decreases under the same conditions.

The authors provide a primal representation of the dynamics and work out examples for the positive orthant (entropy mirror map leading to diagonal scaling) and the simplex (entropy leading to the replicator equation). They then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) = w(t)/W(t) along the trajectory to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative whenever possible, with the aim of preserving the theoretical rate while improving practical performance. A discrete algorithm (variant of accelerated mirror descent) with an adaptive schedule ak is proposed; experiments on small simplex-constrained problems compare this adaptive averaging to popular restarting heuristics (gradient and speed restarts).

Relation to prior work
The work builds on the continuous-time perspective on acceleration (Su-Boyd-Candès, 2016), on earlier AMD analysis (Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett, 2015), and on the variational/Bregman Lagrangian framework (Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan, 2016). It connects restarts (O’Donoghue-Candès, 2015) and adaptive first-order methods (AdaGrad, Adam) conceptually, while focusing specifically on accelerated mirror dynamics. The primal AMD form is shown to generalize a family of ODEs studied in Wibisono et al. (by parameterizing a(t), r(t), η(t)), but the present work emphasizes averaging weights and an adaptive rule that directly controls the Lyapunov derivative.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and unification: The generalized AMD_{w,η} and the Lyapunov template Lr provide a clean way to synthesize many accelerated ODEs and rates via simple conditions on w and η. The corollary gives a constructive recipe.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic: The idea to modulate a(t) based on the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ to ensure/strengthen Lyapunov decrease is simple and appealing, and it preserves the nominal rate in continuous time.
- Examples and connections: The primal forms and the examples (orthant, simplex/replicator) are illuminating and of interest beyond optimization (e.g., links to evolutionary dynamics).
- Technical soundness (continuous time): The existence/uniqueness argument, Lyapunov analysis, and energy function derivation are standard but carefully executed.

Weaknesses and questions
- Discrete-time guarantees are not in the main text: The usefulness of the heuristic depends on the discretization (Algorithm 1). The paper states a bound is in the supplement; however, the main text does not provide a theorem guaranteeing an O(1/k^2) rate (or any precise rate) for the adaptive scheme. Since comparisons to restarts are empirical, a formal discrete-time result would substantially strengthen the contribution.
- Scope and scale of experiments: Experiments are limited to low-dimensional simplex problems (R^3) and a few objective types. It would be valuable to include higher-dimensional benchmarks (e.g., logistic regression with constraints, portfolio, structured norm constraints), and to show robustness of the adaptive rule to step-size choices, noise, and problem conditioning. Comparisons to widely used accelerated methods with restart/backtracking (e.g., FISTA with adaptive restart) would be informative.
- Trajectory-dependent condition and its proxy: Condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩, while the discrete heuristic uses f(x̃(k+1)) ≤ f(x̃(k)) as a trigger. The connection between these two is intuitive but not formally justified in the main text. A short lemma relating these conditions under the chosen discretization would clarify when the discrete trigger indeed implies the Lyapunov decrease proxy.
- Assumptions on ψ, norms, and domains: The exposition would benefit from clarifying the norm choices under which ∇ψ* is Lipschitz (e.g., if ψ is µ-strongly convex w.r.t. a norm, then ∇ψ* is 1/µ-Lipschitz w.r.t. the dual norm). For the entropy mirror map on the simplex (with an indicator), smoothness holds in the interior; explicitly noting the interior invariance of trajectories and the norm assumptions would avoid ambiguity.
- Relation to variational framework: While the primal AMD form is shown to encompass the ODE family in Wibisono et al., it would help to position the novelty: the generalized averaging perspective and adaptive schedule are the distinct contributions here; stating explicitly what can and cannot be recovered from the Bregman Lagrangian view would avoid the impression of overlap.
- Presentation and references: Some references are incomplete and contain typos; cleaning these up is important. Algorithm 1 could explain choices of R and γ, the prox setup on the simplex, and how β and s are selected. A brief complexity/cost discussion (per iteration) would help.

Quality
The continuous-time theory appears sound and clearly presented. The adaptive averaging idea is well-motivated and tied to a Lyapunov argument. The discrete-time analysis (central for practical impact) needs to be surfaced in the main paper with a precise theorem and proof sketch. Experiments are correct but small-scale.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized; notation is heavy but standard for this literature. Adding the discrete theorem, clarifying assumptions on ψ and norms, and improving references would improve clarity.

Originality
Moderately high. The generalized averaging and the adaptive Lyapunov-based rule are novel and complementary to restart heuristics and to the variational ODE framework. The acceleration of replicator dynamics via averaging is a nice contribution.

Significance
Potentially impactful as a unifying and design perspective, and as a practical heuristic that competes with restarts. Current experimental evidence is promising but limited; stronger discrete-time theory and broader experiments would raise significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, general Lyapunov framework that unifies and extends accelerated ODEs via averaging weights.
- Simple, theoretically grounded adaptive heuristic that preserves rates in continuous time.
- Interesting connections to replicator dynamics and mirror maps.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing discrete-time convergence theorem in the main paper for the adaptive algorithm.
- Limited experimental scope; no large-scale or noisy settings; comparisons could be broader.
- Some technical assumptions and norm choices are implicit.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. If the authors can include a clear discrete-time convergence guarantee for Algorithm 1 in the main text and expand/strengthen experiments, this would be a solid accept.

Confidence
Medium.
