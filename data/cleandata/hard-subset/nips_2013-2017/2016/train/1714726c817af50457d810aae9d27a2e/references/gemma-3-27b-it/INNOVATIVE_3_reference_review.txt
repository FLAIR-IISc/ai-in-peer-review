This paper presents a fascinating and broadly applicable generalization of accelerated mirror descent dynamics, moving beyond the specific choices explored in prior work and providing a unified framework for analysis. The core idea – parameterizing the dynamics with arbitrary averaging weights – is genuinely novel and offers a compelling connection between continuous-time analysis and discrete acceleration techniques. The authors successfully demonstrate that a wide range of averaging schemes can achieve quadratic convergence, and, crucially, propose an adaptive heuristic that *preserves* this rate while potentially speeding up convergence in practice. This is a significant contribution, as many existing acceleration heuristics lack theoretical guarantees.

The strengths of the paper are considerable. The mathematical analysis appears sound, building upon existing Lyapunov function techniques. The exploration of primal representations and the derivation of accelerated replicator dynamics for the simplex constraint provides concrete examples and highlights the practical relevance of the framework. The adaptive averaging heuristic, with its theoretical guarantees, is a particularly strong point. The numerical experiments, while not exhaustive, provide initial evidence supporting the efficacy of the approach. The authors clearly articulate the motivation, connections to prior work, and potential impact of their research.

However, the paper isn’t without limitations. The analysis, while rigorous, feels somewhat abstract. The conditions for convergence (Theorem 2) are stated but the practicality of *checking* these conditions a priori is not fully addressed. The experiments, although promising, are limited in scope – only a few objective functions and constraints are considered. A more comprehensive evaluation, particularly comparing against state-of-the-art adaptive methods like Adam in various settings, would strengthen the claims. The discussion of the adaptive averaging heuristic lacks detailed pseudocode, making it harder to immediately implement and test. The connection to existing restart heuristics, while acknowledged, could be explored in greater depth. While the authors prove convergence rate preservation, demonstrating *faster* convergence in practice—even in specific cases—would elevate the contribution further. Finally, the paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the computational cost associated with the proposed adaptive averaging scheme.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The generalized averaging framework and the adaptive heuristic represent a genuinely new approach to accelerated optimization.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis is mathematically rigorous and builds upon established techniques.
*   **Significance:** The preservation of convergence rates under adaptation is a significant result, addressing a key limitation of existing heuristics.
*   **Potential Impact:** The framework has broad applicability and could inspire new algorithm design and analysis.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively bridges continuous-time dynamics with discrete acceleration methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope and don’t provide a definitive demonstration of performance gains.
*   **Practical Verifiability:** The convergence conditions are stated but their practical checkability is unclear.
*   **Lack of Detailed Implementation:** The absence of detailed pseudocode hinders immediate implementation and testing.
*   **Abstractness:** The analysis is somewhat abstract and could benefit from more concrete examples and interpretations.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor shortcomings, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the potential impact outweigh the limitations. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a particularly compelling result, and the paper’s exploration of the connections between continuous-time dynamics and discrete acceleration is insightful. While more extensive experimental validation and a deeper discussion of implementation details would be beneficial, the paper, as it stands, warrants acceptance. I’m willing to overlook the incomplete validation given the boldness and originality of the idea. This paper sets a new direction in accelerated optimization and could spur significant further research.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
