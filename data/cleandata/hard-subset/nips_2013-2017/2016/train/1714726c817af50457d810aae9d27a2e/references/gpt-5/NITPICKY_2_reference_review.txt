Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for convex optimization, introducing a two-parameter family of accelerated mirror descent ODEs with generalized averaging weights (w(t), η(t)). A Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) is proposed, and sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) ensure monotonicity of Lr, yielding convergence f(X(t)) − f* ≤ const/r(t). The framework recovers classical t^2 rates and extends to r(t)=t^p. A primal representation connects the dynamics to mirror descent and to Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan’s variational ODEs. For simplex constraints, the dynamics reduce to an “accelerated replicator” ODE. The paper further proposes an “adaptive averaging” heuristic that adjusts weights online to decrease the Lyapunov derivative, with a discrete-time algorithm and empirical comparisons versus gradient and speed restarts.

This builds on prior connections between Nesterov acceleration and second-order ODEs (Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono et al.), mirror descent ODEs (Nemirovski–Yudin; Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett), and restart heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès). The paper’s angle is to make averaging explicit in the dynamics, characterize weight schedules ensuring a target rate, and propose a Lyapunov-controlled adaptive weighting.

Strengths
- Clear, general Lyapunov construction that unifies several accelerated ODEs and yields rate f(X(t))−f* = O(1/r(t)) under simple inequalities on (w,η,r).
- Primal reformulation and concrete examples for R^n_+ (entropy) and the simplex lead to recognizable dynamics (replicator), aiding intuition.
- The adaptive averaging idea is principled: choose weights to decrease d/dt Lr and preserve rates.
- Empirical section compares against common restart heuristics and inspects both objective and proposed energies/Lyapunov, which is informative.

Weaknesses and technical issues
- Assumption inconsistency: Theorem 1 assumes ∇ψ* is Lipschitz. In the R^n_+ example with ψ*(z)=∑(e^{z_i}−1), ∇ψ*(z)=exp(z) is not globally Lipschitz, contradicting the assumption used for existence/uniqueness. Local Lipschitz would suffice for local existence; global existence then needs additional bounds (e.g., coercivity or a priori boundedness). This mismatch should be fixed with precise conditions (e.g., ψ strongly convex with Lipschitz gradient, or restricting to compact invariant sets) and the examples adapted accordingly.
- Core derivation error in the adaptive rule: From inequality (3), the critical factor is ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w) = ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − η/a) with a = w/W. To make this term identically zero, one needs η = a r (equivalently a = η/r). However, Section 4 states “set a(t) = η(t) r(t)” and later uses that choice to claim the term is zero—this is dimensionally and algebraically incorrect. The correct zeroing condition is a(t) = η(t)/r(t); with inequality directions reversed depending on the sign of ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩. This mistake affects both the continuous-time heuristic (8) and its interpretation.
- Systematic typo in Examples 1–2: You require a(t) ≥ r′(t)/r(t) = p/t, but the text sets a(t)=β t (β≥p) and then integrates as if a(t)=β/t, yielding e^{∫ a} = (t/t0)^β. The integral shown (β ln(t/t0)) only matches a(t)=β/t. This is not a minor typo—it propagates to the expressions for w and η.
- Algorithm 1 is underspecified and contains multiple errors/undefined symbols:
  - Step 4 uses γ without defining it.
  - Step 5 defines λk = √(s a_k) + √(s a_k) (nonsensical); presumably λk = √(s a_k)/(1+√(s a_k)) or similar.
  - Steps 6–8: “ak = min(ak−1, βmax k √s)” has undefined “βmax”; line 8 switches ak to βk/√s or βk√s? The text earlier sets a1=β√s. Dimensional consistency and rationale are unclear.
  - The mapping t = k√s is asserted but the derivation is deferred; the discrete Lyapunov proof is not included.
  - Notation alternates between x, x̃, ž without clear definitions of which iterate is used to evaluate the gradient in each step (step 3/4 refer to ∇f(x(k)) although x(k) is defined only after those steps).
- Theorem 1 proof relies on global Lipschitz of ∇ψ*, not guaranteed in key examples (entropy on R^n_+). If only local Lipschitz holds, one needs a non-explosion argument; this is missing.
- Condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on the trajectory ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩ and may be hard to guarantee; the corollary addresses this with η = a r and a ≥ r′/r, but the adaptive section misstates the relation between a and η/r (see above), undermining the main heuristic.
- Relation to Wibisono et al. (Bregman Lagrangian) is underplayed. The primal ODE (6) can be viewed as a special case of their family via time-dilation and parameter choices; this overlap should be clarified, and the distinct contribution emphasized rigorously.
- Experiments: Only R^3 simplex problems are shown, with no quantitative tables or hyperparameter settings (β, s, γ, regularizers), no variance analysis, and no code. Claims that adaptive averaging “compares favorably in all examples” are not substantiated rigorously.
- Numerous formatting/referencing issues:
  - “th Conference on NIPS 2016” missing the number; inconsistent venue naming.
  - Many references lack years/venues/pages; inconsistent styles.
  - Footnote placement and equation numbering are irregular; several in-text cross-references (“Section 2/3/4”) do not match exact content; a stray “2” superscript after “first-order accelerated method2.”
  - Minor notation issues (e.g., AMD′w,η typesetting, missing parentheses, inconsistent bold/roman vectors, E vs E* notational clarity).

Quality
The continuous-time analysis is largely sound aside from the above errors, and the Lyapunov arguments are standard and correct when conditions are met. However, the adaptive section has algebraic mistakes, and the discrete algorithm is too error-prone to assess. Experimental evaluation is insufficient for NeurIPS standards.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but marred by many typos, undefined symbols, and mismatches that impede reproducibility and trust.

Originality
Generalized weighting/averaging and the explicit Lyapunov construction are useful, but close to known ODE formulations (Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono et al.; Krichene et al.). The “adaptive averaging” idea could be novel, but requires a correct formulation and solid discrete-time theory.

Significance
Potentially interesting if the adaptive mechanism were fixed and validated; as is, the impact is limited.

Pros
- Unifying Lyapunov analysis with tunable rates r(t).
- Connection to replicator dynamics is nice and pedagogical.
- Energy function Er(t) adds intuition.

Cons
- Key algebraic/typographical errors (a vs η/r) in the adaptive section and examples.
- Algorithm 1 incomplete/incorrect.
- Assumption mismatch for existence/uniqueness with entropy mirror map.
- Weak and non-reproducible experiments.
- Poorly formatted/incomplete references.

Questions for authors
- Please correct the definition of a(t) in Examples 1–2 and in the adaptive rule (8). Which is intended: a=η/r or a=η r?
- Under what precise conditions on ψ (global vs local Lipschitz of ∇ψ*) does Theorem 1 hold? How do you handle entropy on R^n_+?
- Provide a complete, typo-free Algorithm 1 with defined parameters and a discrete Lyapunov proof.
- Clarify the novelty relative to the Bregman Lagrangian framework.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper has promise, but the accumulation of technical and presentation errors (some substantive) and the incomplete discrete-time development do not meet NeurIPS standards at this time.
