Summary
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for minimizing a smooth convex function over a closed convex set. Building on the “averaging view” of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) [11] and the ODE interpretations of acceleration [20, 23], the authors introduce a family of accelerated mirror descent dynamics with generalized averaging weights, AMDw,η, parameterized by two positive functions: w(t) for averaging and η(t) for gradient accumulation. They analyze these ODEs via a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), where r(t) encodes a target convergence rate. The key result (Theorem 2) provides sufficient conditions on w, η, r under which Lr is decreasing, yielding f(X(t))−f* ≤ const/r(t). A simple corollary gives a constructive recipe for w, η achieving O(1/r(t)) for a wide class of r (e.g., t^p).

They also present a second “energy” function Er that decreases under the same conditions, derive a primal form of the dynamics and examples for the positive orthant and simplex, yielding an “accelerated replicator” dynamics. Motivated by the trajectory-dependent term in the Lyapunov derivative, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t)=w/W to decrease Lr whenever possible, and present a discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) for r(t)=t^2. Experiments on low-dimensional simplex problems compare adaptive averaging to gradient and speed restart heuristics [17, 20], showing favorable or comparable performance and monotonic Lr.

Relation to prior work
The work extends the continuous-time acceleration literature connecting Nesterov’s methods to ODEs [20] and to variational/Lagrangian viewpoints [23]. It generalizes the averaging formulation of accelerated mirror descent [11] by allowing a broad family of weight schedules and giving Lyapunov-based sufficient conditions for arbitrary r(t). The adaptive averaging heuristic is distinct from restart heuristics [17, 20] and from adaptive step-size methods like Adagrad/Adam [7, 10], which are not tailored to accelerated averaging or Lyapunov control. The replicator example ties to evolutionary dynamics [19, 22].

Strengths
- Conceptual generality: A clean and unifying Lyapunov-based framework for AMD with generalized averaging. The r(t)-indexed Lyapunov function is elegant and yields transparent sufficient conditions.
- Constructive recipes: Corollary 1 and the w-parameterization via a(t)=w/W make it easy to synthesize weights for desired rates (e.g., O(t^p) continuous-time).
- Clear connections: Primal forms and concrete examples (orthant and simplex) make the dynamics interpretable; the accelerated replicator is appealing and appears novel.
- Adaptive averaging: Simple, intuitive heuristic tied directly to Lyapunov decrease; preserves the continuous-time rate by design.
- Experiments: Empirical comparisons to standard restarts showing monotone Lyapunov behavior and favorable performance.

Weaknesses and questions
- Trajectory-dependent condition: Theorem 2’s condition (2) depends on X(t) via ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ and can be hard to verify or enforce a priori; the main generic guarantee comes from the more restrictive Corollary 1 (η=a·r).
- Discretization gap: The theoretical guarantees are continuous-time; discrete-time guarantees are deferred to the supplement and presented only for r(t)=t^2. It would strengthen the paper to include main-text theorems for Algorithm 1, including explicit constants and assumptions on s, β, ψ, R.
- Scope of experiments: Mostly 3D simplex toy problems; no higher-dimensional or real ML tasks, and no comparison against tuned FISTA/Nesterov with modern adaptive restarts. Effects of noise/stochastic gradients are not studied.
- Practical guidance: Limited guidance for choosing r(t) beyond t^2 in practice, for tuning β, step-size s, or for selecting ψ and R. Stability/robustness under misspecification is unclear.
- Notational/algorithmic clarity: Algorithm 1 has possible typos (e.g., line 5 λk formula simplifies to 1/2 as written; line 6 “βmax k √s” unclear). The role of the extra regularizer R alongside ψ could be explained better, as well as projections/constraints handling.
- Assumptions: Lipschitzness of ∇ψ* on E* is assumed; for entropy/softmax, norm dependence matters—clarify the norms and constants. Twice differentiability of ψ* is assumed in the primal derivations.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound and clearly argued using Lyapunov analysis. The existence/uniqueness discussion is careful. The adaptive heuristic is logically tied to reducing Lr and Er. However, discrete-time theory is not fully presented in the main text, and experiments, while illustrative, are limited in scope. Some algorithmic details/typos need fixing.

Clarity
Overall well organized, with a clear flow from dynamics to Lyapunov analysis, primal forms, and applications. Figures (referenced) and examples help. Improve clarity by:
- Fixing Algorithm 1 typos and providing a self-contained statement of its convergence guarantee in the main text.
- Explicitly stating norms and constants for Lipschitz assumptions of ∇ψ* and ∇f.
- Providing more intuition for choosing r, w, η and for the adaptivity threshold in discrete time.

Originality
The generalized averaging framework and adaptive averaging heuristic are novel and nontrivial extensions of [11, 20, 23]. The accelerated replicator dynamics is a neat contribution.

Significance
The work advances the understanding of acceleration via averaging in continuous time and proposes a practical adaptive mechanism. If the discrete-time theory and broader experiments are strengthened, this could influence algorithm design beyond restarts.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, general theoretical framework with simple sufficient conditions for target rates.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with Lyapunov-based rationale.
- Interesting application to replicator dynamics and constrained settings.
- Clear relation to and extension of prominent recent work.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited discrete-time theoretical results in the main paper; experiments are small-scale.
- Trajectory-dependent condition limits direct applicability unless specialized to Corollary 1 or the heuristic.
- Some presentation issues and minor ambiguities.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a main-text theorem for Algorithm 1’s O(1/k^2) rate with precise assumptions, and discuss stability.
- Add higher-dimensional experiments (e.g., logistic regression on simplex constraints, portfolio optimization) and compare against tuned FISTA with adaptive restart.
- Clarify Algorithm 1 (fix λk, define “βmax k √s”, specify R and its role).
- Discuss extension to strongly convex f (linear rates?) and to stochastic gradients.
- Elaborate on norms/Lipschitz constants for ∇ψ* (e.g., entropy/softmax case).

Overall
A solid and insightful theoretical contribution with promising ideas for adaptivity. With improved discrete-time analysis and broader experiments, it would be a strong NeurIPS paper. As is, I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing clarity and empirical scope.
