Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time viewpoints of accelerated first-order methods for convex optimization with constraints. It introduces a two-parameter family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by weight functions w(t) (averaging) and η(t) (gradient accumulation). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors derive sufficient conditions on w, η, and a target rate r(t) under which Lr is decreasing and f(X(t))−f* = O(1/r(t)). They show how prior ODE formulations of Nesterov/accelerated mirror descent arise as special cases (e.g., r(t)=t^2). They also provide a “primal” representation, connect to known dynamics (replicator dynamics on the simplex), and introduce a decreasing “energy” E_r that has an intuitive kinetic/potential interpretation.

The more novel part is an “adaptive averaging” heuristic that adjusts a(t)=w/W online to further decrease d/dt Lr when possible. A discrete-time algorithm is proposed for r(t)=t^2, with empirical comparisons against gradient and speed restart heuristics on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems. The paper positions itself relative to continuous-time acceleration (Su et al., Wibisono et al.), proximal/MD perspectives, and restart heuristics.

Strengths
- Clear and unified continuous-time analysis: The Lyapunov template cleanly recovers known accelerated dynamics via specific choices of w, η, r and provides an understandable condition (Corollary 1) that trivializes one of the nontrivial inequalities.
- Existence/uniqueness and feasibility guarantees for the ODE are carefully noted.
- The primal representation is helpful for intuition and for connections to evolutionary dynamics; the accelerated variant of replicator dynamics is of independent interest.
- The energy function E_r adds interpretability and appears to decrease under the same conditions as the Lyapunov function.
- The adaptive-averaging idea is simple and well-motivated from the bound on d/dt Lr.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited discretization theory: The primary contributions are in continuous time; the discrete method (Algorithm 1) is only sketched with claims deferred to the supplement. For NeurIPS, I expect a rigorous and self-contained discrete-time convergence theorem and proof, at least for the main algorithmic proposal (adaptive averaging with mirror maps). In particular, the statement that the heuristic “preserves the rate” is not established in the main text for the discrete algorithm, and the conditions used in continuous time (e.g., the trajectory-dependent sign condition) do not carry over transparently to the discretization as presented.
- Heuristic-to-implementation gap: The adaptive rule in continuous time uses the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩. The discrete rule in Algorithm 1 switches a_k based on whether f(tilde x) decreased, which is only a loose surrogate. There is no analysis of how this surrogate enforces a discrete analog of the Lyapunov decrease.
- Experimental evidence is limited: Experiments are small-scale (R^3 simplex) and do not test robustness, higher dimension, different mirror maps, or noisy/stochastic gradients. Baselines are limited to two restart heuristics; missing comparisons to widely used adaptive restarts (e.g., FISTA with O’Donoghue–Candès adaptive restart on Euclidean problems), and to strong baselines in the mirror setting. Tuning details and computational overhead are not discussed.
- Practical guidance is underspecified: Choices of ψ*, R, γ, step size s, and β are not motivated or justified, and Algorithm 1 has unclear lines (e.g., “βmax_k” in step 6). The potential for numerical instability when a(t) is increased (potentially making w grow exponentially on intervals) is not discussed.
- Novelty over prior ODE frameworks may be incremental: The parameterized Lyapunov framework is elegant but appears close in spirit to the Bregman Lagrangian/variational viewpoints (Wibisono et al.) and prior AMD ODEs (Krichene et al.). The adaptive-averaging idea is new in this form, but currently lacks discrete-time theoretical backing.
- Related work and references: Several references are incomplete; some pertinent literature on discrete accelerated methods and restarts is not discussed in depth. The paper would benefit from a more thorough positioning vs. inertial MD variants and discrete mirror-prox/accelerated mirror frameworks.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Provide a formal, self-contained convergence theorem and proof for Algorithm 1 in the main paper, including conditions linking s, β, ψ, and R that ensure the advertised O(1/k^2) rate and Lyapunov decrease.
- Clarify line 6 of Algorithm 1 and define all constants (γ, R, βmax, etc.). How are these chosen in experiments?
- How sensitive is performance to the aggressiveness of a_k updates? Are there safeguards to prevent overly large weights that destabilize the method?
- Can you demonstrate results on Euclidean unconstrained problems and higher dimensions, and compare to FISTA with adaptive restarts (and to tuned classical restarts)?
- Does the adaptive heuristic extend to stochastic gradients? If so, what happens to Lyapunov/energy decrease?

Minor comments and typos
- In Example 1, please check the typesetting of a(t): to obtain w(t) ∝ (t/t0)^{β−1} you need a(t) = β/t (the current text is ambiguous).
- Check the statement “η(t) = w(t) r(t)/W(t)”: dimensional consistency and derivation should be explicit.
- Several references are missing years/venues; please complete and standardize citations.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Solid continuous-time derivations; discrete methodology and guarantees are insufficiently developed in the main text; experiments are limited. Moderate.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but the discrete algorithm and experimental setup need more detail; some notational/typographical issues. Moderate.
- Originality: The generalized averaging perspective is a useful unification; the adaptive averaging idea is interesting but currently heuristic in discrete time; otherwise incremental relative to existing ODE frameworks. Moderate.
- Significance: Potentially useful if the discrete theory and empirical validation are strengthened. As is, the impact is unclear beyond the continuous-time perspective. Limited-to-moderate.

Pros
- Clean, general Lyapunov framework unifying accelerated ODEs.
- New interpretive energy function; nice connections to replicator dynamics.
- Simple adaptive idea motivated by Lyapunov decrease.

Cons
- Main algorithmic contribution (adaptive averaging) lacks rigorous discrete analysis in the paper.
- Experiments are small-scale and do not establish practical advantages.
- Some ambiguity in algorithm description and parameter choices.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. With a rigorous discrete-time analysis for Algorithm 1, clearer algorithmic specification, and stronger empirical validation on standard benchmarks and baselines, this work could be much more compelling.
