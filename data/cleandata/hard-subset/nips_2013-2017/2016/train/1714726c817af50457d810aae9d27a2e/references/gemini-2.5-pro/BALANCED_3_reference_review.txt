### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a generalization of the continuous-time dynamics for Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD). Building upon prior work that connects Nesterov's acceleration to second-order ODEs with a specific averaging scheme [11, 20], the authors introduce a broad family of dynamics, `AMDw,η`, parameterized by a generalized averaging weight function `w(t)` and a gradient accumulation rate `η(t)`.

The core theoretical contribution is a unified Lyapunov-based analysis for this family of ODEs. The authors identify a Lyapunov function `Lr` and derive simple, sufficient conditions on `w(t)` and `η(t)` that guarantee a convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))` for a chosen rate function `r(t)`. This framework is shown to recover existing results as special cases and provides a constructive method for designing new accelerated dynamics. The paper also derives an equivalent primal-space representation of these dynamics, offering interesting connections to existing models; for instance, it shows how to derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics used in evolutionary game theory.

Capitalizing on their theoretical analysis, the authors propose a novel and practical "adaptive averaging" heuristic. This heuristic dynamically adjusts the averaging weights `w(t)` to further decrease the Lyapunov function, effectively speeding up convergence. A key advantage of this adaptive scheme is that, by construction, it is guaranteed to preserve the theoretical `O(1/r(t))` convergence rate, a property that is often not guaranteed for popular restart heuristics [17, 20] on general convex problems. The paper concludes with numerical experiments comparing a discrete implementation of the adaptive averaging heuristic against standard AMD and two restart schemes, demonstrating its superior or competitive performance.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is rigorous and elegant. The derivation of the time derivative of the Lyapunov function in equation (3) is the cornerstone of the analysis, and the resulting sufficient conditions in Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 are clear and well-justified. The proposed adaptive heuristic is directly and cleverly motivated by this theoretical analysis, making it a principled rather than an ad-hoc approach. The claims are well-supported by the continuous-time theory, and the experiments provide good evidence for the practical utility of the proposed discrete algorithm.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of building intuition, starting from the general `AMDw,η` formulation, moving to the unified analysis, and culminating in the practical adaptive algorithm. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the structure is logical. The connection between the continuous-time analysis and the discrete-time heuristic is made clear. Figure 1 provides a helpful illustration of the dynamics.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on the established ODE perspective of acceleration, it makes several novel contributions:
1.  The generalization of AMD dynamics to a family parameterized by `w(t)` and `η(t)` is new and provides a unified viewpoint.
2.  The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel idea. It differs fundamentally from adaptive learning rate methods (like Adagrad) and from restart schemes. The core idea of adapting the averaging schedule based on the trajectory's alignment with the gradient is insightful.
3.  The guarantee that this heuristic preserves the worst-case convergence rate is a significant and novel advantage over existing heuristics.
4.  The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics is an interesting and original connection to another field.
The paper is well-situated within the literature and clearly articulates its relationship to and departure from prior work.

**Significance:** This paper has the potential for significant impact on both the theory and practice of optimization.
*   **Theoretically,** it deepens our understanding of acceleration by formalizing its connection to a wide range of averaging schemes. The unified Lyapunov framework is a powerful analytical tool that could be used to design and analyze other accelerated methods.
*   **Practically,** the adaptive averaging algorithm offers a compelling alternative to existing restart heuristics. It is simple to implement, has strong empirical performance, and comes with a valuable theoretical guarantee. Researchers and practitioners working with first-order methods are likely to find this work useful and may adopt or build upon the proposed adaptive scheme.

### **Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper introduces and thoroughly analyzes a general class of accelerated dynamics, providing a unified framework that is a significant contribution to the optimization literature.
2.  **Novel and Principled Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a key contribution. It is novel, well-motivated by the theory, and empirically effective.
3.  **Rate-Preservation Guarantee:** A major advantage of the adaptive heuristic is its guaranteed preservation of the `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate, addressing a known limitation of many popular restart schemes for general convex optimization.
4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is a model of clarity, making complex ideas accessible and providing valuable intuition.
5.  **Insightful Connections:** The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics demonstrates the breadth of the framework and provides a novel link between optimization and evolutionary game theory.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Continuous-to-Discrete Gap:** The main theoretical results are for continuous-time ODEs. While a discrete algorithm is presented and its rate is claimed to be bounded in the supplement, the main text would benefit from a more explicit discussion of this transition. For example, does the discrete Lyapunov counterpart also provably decrease at each step under the adaptive scheme? Clarifying how tightly the discrete algorithm mimics the properties of its continuous counterpart would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments are illustrative and compelling but are limited to low-dimensional (R^3) problems. The paper's impact would be bolstered by demonstrating the effectiveness of adaptive averaging on larger-scale, standard machine learning benchmarks (e.g., logistic regression or SVMs on datasets like CIFAR-10 or MNIST), where the behavior of optimization algorithms can differ.
3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The proposed algorithm introduces a parameter `β` (and relies on a step-size `s`). The paper states `β >= 2` is required for the `t^2` rate, but a brief discussion on the practical choice of `β` and the algorithm's sensitivity to it would be a valuable addition for practitioners seeking to apply this method.
4.  **Comparison with Adaptive Step-Rate Methods:** The paper distinguishes itself from Adagrad/Adam by noting they are not for accelerated methods. While this is largely true, momentum is a key component of both acceleration and methods like Adam. A slightly more detailed discussion, or even an empirical comparison to Adam (which is a common baseline), could help better position the contribution within the broader landscape of adaptive optimization algorithms.

Despite these minor points for improvement, the paper is outstanding. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. It presents a beautiful blend of deep theory and practical algorithm design.

**Final Decision:** Accept.
