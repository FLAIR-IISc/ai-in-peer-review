Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for convex optimization under Lipschitz gradients and convex constraints. Building on the “averaging yields acceleration” perspective of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) (e.g., Krichene et al. NIPS’15) and the ODE/variational viewpoints (Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan), the authors introduce a two-parameter family of accelerated mirror descent ODEs, AMDw,η, that decouples: (i) how gradients are accumulated in a dual variable via η(t), and (ii) how a primal iterate X(t) is formed by averaging a mirror map of the dual trajectory using a weight function w(t). They analyze this broad family with a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ∗(Z, z*), giving simple sufficient conditions under which Lr is decreasing, yielding a convergence bound f(X(t)) − f* ≤ O(1/r(t)). They also give a “primal” formulation, show special cases (positive orthant; simplex) including an accelerated replicator dynamics, and introduce a second “energy” function Er(t) that is also decreasing under the same conditions. Motivated by the Lyapunov derivative, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that increases weights on trajectory segments making progress; the continuous-time rule preserves the Lyapunov decrease and hence the convergence rate. A discretization for r(t)=t^2 is given (Algorithm 1) and empirically compared to gradient and speed restarts on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems.

Quality
- Theory: The Lyapunov analysis for the generalized AMD family is sound and aligns well with existing ODE-based analyses. The sufficient conditions are transparent, and the construction of w,η from a desired rate r(t) is useful. The decreasing “energy” Er adds a physically intuitive quantity whose monotonicity is guaranteed by the same condition. The existence/uniqueness argument for t0>0 is careful and standard.
- However, there are important typographical issues in key formulas that should be corrected for correctness and clarity:
  - Corollary 1 should set η(t) = a(t) r(t) with a(t) = w(t)/W(t), equivalently η(t) = (w(t)/W(t)) r(t), to make the coefficient r(t) − η(t)W(t)/w(t) vanish. The text currently states η(t) = w(t) r(t) W(t), which is dimensionally inconsistent and does not zero the coefficient.
  - Examples 1–2: for r(t)=t^2 (and more generally r(t)=t^p), to meet a(t) ≥ r′/r, one needs a(t) = β/t (β≥2 or β≥p), not a(t)=β t. The subsequent expressions for w(t) and η(t) and the conclusion η(t)=β t (or β t^{p−1}) do match a(t)=β/t, so the “β t” occurrences appear to be typos.
- Discretization: The discrete adaptive scheme is plausible and consistent with the continuous-time insight, and the authors state a discrete Lyapunov-based rate in the supplement. In the main text, however, rigorous guarantees for Algorithm 1 are only sketched. The criterion used in practice (monotonicity of f(x̃)) is a surrogate for the continuous-time sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩; more discussion of when this surrogate preserves the intended Lyapunov decrease would strengthen the work.
- Experiments: The empirical evaluation is clear but limited to low-dimensional simplex problems (R^3) and a few objectives. Claims that adaptive averaging compares favorably to restarts are supported on these cases, but broader validation (higher dimensions, different ψ, unconstrained Euclidean cases, stochastic gradients, wall-clock costs) would help substantiate practical significance.

Clarity
- Overall the paper is well-organized, with a clean derivation of the Lyapunov bound, primal forms, and examples. The physical interpretation via energy is helpful.
- The above-noted typos in crucial formulas (Corollary 1; Examples 1–2) are potentially confusing and should be corrected. Some notation is heavy (X,Z,Ž, X̌, a=W′/W, etc.); a short notation table would help.
- The relationship to prior ODE families (e.g., mapping of AMDpw,η to Wibisono et al. via a(t)=e^{α}, r=e^{β}, η=a r) is useful; expanding this connection and any differences in admissible rates and discretizations would improve context.

Originality
- The generalized “weighted averaging” AMD family with a simple Lyapunov certificate for arbitrary desired rates r(t) is a neat unification and modest extension of earlier AMD/ODE work. The explicit construction of w,η from r(t) and the identification of conditions under which both a Lyapunov function and an energy function decrease are novel in this formulation.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic guided by the Lyapunov derivative is, to my knowledge, new for accelerated mirror descent; it contrasts with restart heuristics by preserving the continuous-time rate. The accelerated replicator dynamics example is a nice conceptual contribution.
- That said, the work sits close to existing literature on ODE views of acceleration and variational frameworks; the main novelty is the adaptive averaging rule with guarantees in continuous time.

Significance
- Conceptually, the paper clarifies how acceleration can be driven by averaging with time-varying weights, offers a broad class of AMD dynamics with rate guarantees, and provides a practically motivated adaptive averaging mechanism with principled justification. This is likely to be of interest to researchers working on optimization dynamics and accelerated first-order methods.
- Practical impact would be strengthened by more extensive experiments and by firmer discrete-time guarantees for Algorithm 1 beyond the supplement. Demonstrations on higher-dimensional problems and comparisons to other adaptive schemes (e.g., adaptive step sizes; stochastic settings) would broaden appeal.

Pros
- Clean unifying Lyapunov analysis for generalized weighted averaging AMD with rate 1/r(t).
- Constructive design of w,η for desired rates; decreasing “energy” function.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with a principled criterion; preserves continuous-time rate.
- Clear primal forms and illustrative examples, including accelerated replicator dynamics.
- Empirical gains over restarts on tested problems.

Cons
- Critical typos in corollary and examples that could mislead readers.
- Discrete-time analysis of the adaptive scheme is largely deferred; surrogate condition may not always reflect the continuous-time criterion.
- Limited experimental scope; no large-scale or stochastic tests; no wall-clock comparisons.
- Incremental relative to prior ODE/variational work; significance hinges on adaptive averaging.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the typographical errors: Corollary 1 should set η = (w/W) r; Examples 1–2 should use a(t)=β/t.
- Bring a succinct discrete-time theorem (with assumptions) for Algorithm 1 into the main text, clarifying when the f(x̃)-based trigger ensures Lyapunov decrease.
- Expand experiments: higher dimensions, different ψ and constraints, Euclidean unconstrained cases, robustness to step sizes, runtime comparisons, and possibly stochastic gradients.
- Clarify relations to Wibisono et al.’s Bregman Lagrangian and to restart schemes; discuss when adaptive averaging is preferable.

Overall evaluation
Technically solid and clearly motivated, with a nice unifying analysis and a simple, principled adaptive averaging idea. Novelty is moderate but meaningful; significance is promising yet would benefit from stronger discrete-time guarantees and broader experiments. I lean toward a weak accept if the formula errors are corrected and discrete-time results are clarified; otherwise borderline.
