

# Peer Review: "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper studies continuous-time accelerated optimization dynamics through the lens of generalized averaging schemes. The authors extend the Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) framework by introducing a broad family of dynamics parameterized by two weight functions w and η, where w controls the averaging scheme and η determines the rate at which the dual variable accumulates gradients. The core contribution is a unified Lyapunov analysis showing that under specific conditions on w, η, and the desired convergence rate r(t), the solution trajectories converge at rate 1/r(t). The authors then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts w(t) to ensure the Lyapunov function decreases, preserving the convergence rate. They demonstrate the approach on the replicator dynamics for simplex constraints and provide numerical experiments showing adaptive averaging outperforms standard restarting heuristics.

The work builds on a growing line of research connecting continuous-time dynamics to accelerated optimization methods, particularly extending Krichene et al. [11] and Su et al. [20] who established connections between Nesterov's methods and continuous-time ODEs. The paper also relates to the "averaging" perspective on acceleration from Flammarion & Bach [8], though the current work provides a more general framework.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical analysis is technically sound. The Lyapunov function approach is well-executed, and Theorem 2 provides clear sufficient conditions for convergence at rate 1/r(t).
- The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is well-motivated, with the adaptive averaging heuristic providing a principled approach that preserves the convergence rate.
- The examples, particularly the simplex-constrained replicator dynamics, are well-chosen and demonstrate the practical relevance of the theory.

**Weaknesses:**
- The discrete counterpart of the continuous-time method (Algorithm 1) lacks a rigorous convergence analysis. While the paper mentions "the supplementary material" contains a bound, this is insufficient for a NIPS submission. The discrete algorithm is presented without formal guarantees.
- The paper does not address non-smooth optimization, which is a major application domain for accelerated methods. The assumption that f is differentiable with Lipschitz gradient limits the applicability.
- The numerical experiments, while well-designed, do not provide statistical significance testing or detailed computational complexity analysis. The paper claims "significant improvement" in some cases without quantifying how much better the adaptive method is compared to alternatives.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic (equation 8) is described as "simple," but the paper doesn't provide a clear justification for why this particular adaptive strategy is optimal or how it compares to other adaptive schemes.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the key concepts are introduced progressively.
- The connection between the continuous-time dynamics and the discrete algorithm is well-motivated, with the primal representation (Section 3) providing valuable intuition.

**Weaknesses:**
- The relationship between the continuous-time ODE and the discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is not sufficiently explained. The paper mentions "the supplementary material" for details, which suggests the main text is too dense.
- The energy function Er (Theorem 3) is introduced without sufficient intuition. A more descriptive explanation of why this energy function captures "natural measure of energy" would help readers.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic (Section 4) could be better motivated by comparing it to existing adaptive methods like Adagrad or Adam, which are mentioned in the introduction but not directly contrasted with the proposed approach.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The generalized averaging scheme extends prior work by Krichene et al. [11] and provides a more flexible framework for understanding acceleration through averaging.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel in that it dynamically adjusts the weights to ensure the Lyapunov function decreases, rather than using fixed schedules or restarting heuristics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from Flammarion & Bach [8] ("From averaging to acceleration, there is only a step-size"), which is cited but not thoroughly contrasted. The current work seems to build directly on [8] without clearly articulating the key differences.
- The connection to the replicator dynamics (Section 3) is interesting but not particularly novel in itself. The replicator dynamics have been studied extensively in evolutionary game theory [22], and the paper doesn't clearly explain how the accelerated version provides new insights beyond the standard replicator dynamics.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The continuous-time perspective on optimization has gained significant traction in recent years, and this paper contributes meaningfully to that line of work.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic has potential practical value, as it preserves the convergence rate while potentially improving empirical performance.
- The paper's examples, particularly the replicator dynamics, connect optimization theory to applications in game theory and viability theory, broadening the appeal of the work.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the proposed method would be applied in real-world machine learning problems. The numerical experiments are limited to simple examples in R^3, which may not reflect the complexity of practical ML problems.
- The significance of the adaptive heuristic over other heuristics (like restarting) is not fully established. The paper claims "significant improvement" but doesn't provide a clear metric for what constitutes "significant" in practice.
- The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the adaptive averaging heuristic compared to fixed schedules or restarting heuristics, which is critical for practical adoption.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1. The theoretical framework is sound and extends prior work on continuous-time accelerated optimization.
2. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and provides a principled way to improve convergence without sacrificing the theoretical guarantee.
3. The connection to the replicator dynamics provides a valuable bridge between optimization theory and applications in evolutionary game theory.
4. The numerical experiments are well-designed and provide evidence that the proposed method outperforms existing heuristics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1. The discrete counterpart of the continuous-time method lacks formal convergence analysis, which is a significant omission for a NIPS submission.
2. The paper doesn't adequately distinguish its approach from related work, particularly [8].
3. The numerical experiments are limited in scope and don't provide sufficient statistical evidence of improvement.
4. The paper doesn't address non-smooth optimization, which limits its applicability to a major class of optimization problems in machine learning.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the growing literature on continuous-time optimization dynamics. The theoretical framework is well-developed, and the adaptive averaging heuristic is promising. However, the lack of a rigorous discrete convergence analysis and the limited scope of the numerical experiments are significant weaknesses that prevent the paper from being a strong candidate for NIPS.

I would recommend rejection with the following major revisions:
1. Provide a formal convergence analysis for the discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1).
2. Expand the numerical experiments to include more challenging problems and provide statistical significance testing.
3. Better distinguish the work from Flammarion & Bach [8] and other related papers.
4. Address non-smooth optimization cases to broaden the applicability.

The paper has strong theoretical foundations and could be a valuable contribution with these revisions. As it stands, it's a solid but incomplete work that doesn't fully meet the standards for a NIPS submission.

## Specific Recommendations

1. **Discrete Analysis:** The paper should provide a formal convergence rate analysis for Algorithm 1. This is critical for a NIPS submission, as the discrete algorithm is the practical implementation of the continuous-time theory.

2. **Comparison with [8]:** The paper should explicitly contrast its adaptive averaging heuristic with the "step-size" approach in Flammarion & Bach [8], explaining how the current work extends or differs from that perspective.

3. **Non-smooth Optimization:** The paper should discuss how the framework could be extended to handle non-smooth functions, which would significantly broaden its applicability.

4. **Computational Cost:** The paper should include a detailed comparison of the computational complexity of the adaptive heuristic versus fixed schedules and restarting heuristics.

5. **Supplementary Material:** The paper should move more details from the supplementary material to the main text, particularly the connection between continuous-time and discrete algorithms.

6. **Statistical Significance:** The numerical experiments should include statistical significance testing to support claims of "significant improvement."

This paper has potential to be a strong contribution to the field, but it requires significant revisions to address the theoretical and empirical shortcomings noted above before it can be considered for publication at NIPS.
