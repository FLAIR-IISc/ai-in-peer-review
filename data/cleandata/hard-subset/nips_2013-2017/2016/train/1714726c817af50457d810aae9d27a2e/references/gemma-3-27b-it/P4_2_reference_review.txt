This paper investigates accelerated mirror descent dynamics with a focus on generalized averaging schemes. The authors build upon their previous work connecting Nesterov's accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs, introducing a broader family of dynamics parameterized by weight functions. They provide sufficient conditions for convergence and demonstrate how to construct these weights to achieve a desired rate. A novel adaptive averaging heuristic is proposed, guaranteeing convergence rate preservation, and numerical experiments demonstrate its performance against existing methods.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the mathematical framework. The connection to existing literature on accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics is thoroughly established, referencing relevant works like [6, 9, 5, 20, 2, 1, 3, 11, 23, 14, 15]. The introduction effectively motivates the research and highlights the contributions. The derivations are presented with reasonable detail, and the theorems are clearly stated. The inclusion of primal representations and examples, particularly the derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamic, adds to the paper’s value. The numerical experiments provide evidence supporting the proposed method, and the comparison to established heuristics is helpful.

A major strength lies in the theoretical results. The sufficient conditions for Lyapunov function behavior, provided in Theorem 2, and the conditions for rate preservation are well-defined. The adaptive averaging heuristic, while intuitively appealing, is supported by the theoretical framework. The analysis using the Lyapunov and energy functions is a solid approach. The exploration of the primal representation, and the application to the simplex-constrained problem leading to the accelerated replicator dynamics, represent novel contributions.

However, the paper suffers from some limitations. The assumption of differentiability of ψ is stated but not extensively justified. The numerical experiments, while providing some validation, could be more comprehensive. The choice of experimental settings and datasets feels somewhat limited. A more rigorous statistical analysis of the results would strengthen the conclusions. Furthermore, the discussion around the limitations of the restarting heuristics (e.g. [17, 20]) feels a bit superficial. While the authors state lack of guarantees when the objective function is not strongly convex, a more detailed exploration of why these heuristics might fail in such scenarios would be beneficial.  Additionally, the connection between the proposed method and other adaptive gradient methods (mentioned briefly) could be deepened. The link between the Lyapunov/Energy analysis and the performance gains observed in the numerical experiments could be strengthened.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous mathematical analysis of the proposed dynamics and the adaptive averaging heuristic, backed by theorems and proofs.
*   **Novelty:** The generalized averaging framework and the adaptive heuristic represent a novel contribution to the field of accelerated optimization.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, making the complex concepts accessible to readers familiar with the area.
*   **Potential Impact:** The adaptive averaging heuristic could offer practical improvements in the convergence speed of accelerated methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope and lack rigorous statistical analysis.
*   **Superficial Discussion of Related Heuristics:** The critique of existing restarting heuristics could be more in-depth.
*   **Assumptions:** The reliance on differentiability assumptions without extensive justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the theory of accelerated optimization. The mathematical analysis is sound, the proposed method is novel, and the paper is clearly written. While the numerical experiments could be more comprehensive, the theoretical results and the potential impact of the adaptive averaging heuristic justify acceptance.  The work addresses a relevant problem and offers a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
