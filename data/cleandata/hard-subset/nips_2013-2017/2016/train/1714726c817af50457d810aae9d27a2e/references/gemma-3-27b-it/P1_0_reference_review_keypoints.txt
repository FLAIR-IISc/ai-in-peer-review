- Paper studies continuous-time accelerated first-order dynamics, generalizing accelerated mirror descent with weight functions w and η.  
- Establishes sufficient conditions and a Lyapunov analysis guaranteeing a 1/r(t) convergence rate for the generalized dynamics.  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic to dynamically adjust w(t) while preserving convergence guarantees.  
- Derives an accelerated replicator dynamic as an application, showing broader framework applicability.  
- Numerical experiments show the adaptive heuristic outperforms fixed-weight schemes and restarting heuristics on a few test problems.  
- Builds on the authors’ prior AMD work and situates results within continuous-time optimization and adaptive-method literature.  
- Connects to replicator dynamics and evolutionary game theory in the simplex-constrained case.  
- Strong theoretical foundation and clear, consistent presentation with reasonable mathematical derivations.  
- Framework is flexible via w and η parameters, enabling exploration of different averaging schemes.  
- Adaptive heuristic is principled with provable guarantees, an advantage over ad-hoc restarts.  
- Empirical validation is present but limited to a small set of examples (quadratic, linear, KL) and mainly simplex problems.  
- Implementation complexity and parameter tuning (e.g., β) may hinder practical adoption; more discussion needed.  
- Lacks direct comparison to state-of-the-art discrete accelerated methods (e.g., Nesterov, FISTA).  
- Convergence proof for Algorithm 1 is in the supplement, which the reviewer could not verify.  
- Primal representation derivation feels rushed; assumptions (e.g., twice differentiability of ψ*) need clearer explanation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel generalization, strong theory, adaptive heuristic with guarantees, and interesting replicator connection.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experimental scope, implementation complexity, and weak comparison baseline.  
- Overall assessment: strong accept with minor revisions; recommends broader experiments, discrete-method comparisons, and clearer practical guidance.  
- Reviewer rating: 8/10 (Strong Accept).
