

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies a generalized family of continuous-time accelerated optimization dynamics, extending the connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The authors build upon their previous work [11] and foundational studies by Su et al. [20] that established the continuous-time interpretation of accelerated methods. The paper introduces a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two weight functions w and η, and provides a unified Lyapunov analysis to guarantee convergence rates. The key contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate while potentially improving performance.

The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions. Theorem 2 provides sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing, guaranteeing a convergence rate of 1/r(t). The authors derive a primal representation of the dynamics (Section 3) and demonstrate its application to specific constraint sets, including the positive orthant and simplex. For the simplex constraint, they show how the replicator dynamics can be accelerated, which is a novel application connecting optimization with evolutionary game theory. The adaptive averaging heuristic (Section 4) is particularly noteworthy, as it preserves the convergence rate while adaptively computing weights to improve performance, addressing limitations of previous restart heuristics [17, 20].

The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The proofs are rigorous, with careful attention to existence and uniqueness of solutions (Theorem 1). The Lyapunov analysis is well-constructed, and the conditions for convergence rates are clearly articulated. The numerical experiments (Section 5) provide empirical validation of the adaptive heuristic, showing favorable performance compared to existing restart heuristics across various objective functions on the simplex. The experiments also confirm that the Lyapunov function decreases under the adaptive heuristic, as theoretically predicted.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from theoretical foundations to practical applications. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the examples provide concrete illustrations of the general framework. The supplementary material appears to contain additional details, including the discrete version of the adaptive heuristic.

Regarding originality, the paper extends previous NIPS work [11, 20] by generalizing the averaging scheme to a broader family of dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a novel approach to improving accelerated methods, distinct from other adaptive methods like Adagrad [7] and Adam [10], which are not designed for accelerated methods. The connection to replicator dynamics for the simplex constraint is particularly novel, as it provides a continuous-time interpretation of an important dynamics in evolutionary game theory.

The significance of the work is demonstrated through both theoretical and empirical contributions. The unified framework for analyzing accelerated dynamics with generalized averaging represents an advance in understanding continuous-time optimization. The adaptive heuristic provides a practical improvement to existing accelerated methods, with experimental results showing significant gains in some cases. The paper opens avenues for further theoretical exploration of adaptive averaging, particularly for strongly convex functions.

Weaknesses include the limited scope of numerical experiments (only simplex-constrained problems), which could be expanded to demonstrate broader applicability. The connection to discrete-time algorithms is somewhat brief, with the discrete version only mentioned in the supplementary material. While the paper is highly technical, this is appropriate for the NIPS audience focused on optimization theory.

In summary, the paper makes a strong contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods through continuous-time dynamics. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the adaptive heuristic is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The paper advances the state of the art in optimization theory and provides a framework that could be applied to various constrained optimization problems.

Pro:
- Rigorous Lyapunov analysis for a broad family of accelerated dynamics
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates
- Practical application to replicator dynamics on the simplex
- Comprehensive theoretical and experimental validation
- Clear connection to previous NIPS work [11, 20]

Con:
- Limited numerical experiments (only simplex constraints)
- Discrete-time connection is not fully developed in main text
- Highly technical, potentially challenging for some NIPS readers
- Theoretical results could be strengthened with more discussion on practical implementation considerations

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. It is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of optimization. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a promising direction that could influence future work on accelerated optimization methods. I recommend acceptance.
