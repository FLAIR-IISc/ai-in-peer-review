

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies continuous-time accelerated descent dynamics with a generalized averaging scheme, extending the authors' previous work on accelerated mirror descent dynamics. The main contribution is a unified framework for analyzing a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, using a Lyapunov function approach to establish convergence rates. The paper also proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate while empirically improving performance over existing restarting heuristics. The work builds on prior NIPS contributions, particularly the authors' own work on continuous-time accelerated methods [11] and Su et al.'s connection between Nesterov's method and ODEs [20].

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed. The authors successfully generalize previous work on accelerated methods by introducing a unified Lyapunov function approach that establishes sufficient conditions for convergence rates. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 provide clear, generalizable conditions for convergence.

2. The connection between the continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is thoughtfully developed. The paper properly acknowledges the relationship between continuous-time ODEs and their discrete-time discretizations, which is crucial for the field.

3. The examples are well-chosen and demonstrate the practical relevance of the approach. The accelerated replicator dynamics for simplex constraints (Section 3) is particularly compelling, as it connects optimization theory to evolutionary game theory and viability theory, with potential applications in multiple domains.

4. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The paper convincingly shows that this heuristic preserves the convergence rate while improving empirical performance, as demonstrated in the numerical experiments.

5. The paper is clearly written with consistent notation. The logical flow from theoretical development to examples to empirical validation is well-structured and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper lacks sufficient discussion of the practical implications of the adaptive averaging heuristic for discrete-time algorithms. While the continuous-time analysis is strong, the connection to specific discrete implementations needs more detail. The authors mention a discrete version in Section 4 but don't clearly explain how it differs from or improves upon existing adaptive methods like Adagrad or Adam, which also adjust learning rates based on gradient information.

2. The numerical experiments, while suggestive, are limited in scope. The experiments are confined to R^3 simplex problems, which doesn't fully capture the behavior in more complex, high-dimensional settings where accelerated methods are typically applied. The paper would benefit from additional experiments on more challenging optimization problems.

3. The significance of the adaptive averaging heuristic compared to existing techniques is not fully articulated. The paper mentions that adaptive averaging "compares favorably" but doesn't clearly establish how it differs from or improves upon existing adaptive methods in the optimization literature, which is a missed opportunity to highlight the paper's contribution.

4. The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of the adaptive heuristic. While the theoretical convergence rate is preserved, the authors don't discuss whether the adaptive mechanism introduces significant computational overhead that might negate the benefits in practice.

5. The connection to the broader optimization literature could be strengthened. While the paper references relevant work, it could better situate its contributions relative to recent advances in adaptive optimization methods (e.g., [7, 10]) and accelerated methods (e.g., [16, 23]).

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated descent dynamics, with a well-executed Lyapunov function analysis that generalizes previous work. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a promising approach that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance. However, the paper's practical significance could be strengthened by better articulating the relationship to discrete algorithms and addressing the computational cost of the adaptive mechanism.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper is technically sound, makes a clear theoretical contribution, and provides empirical evidence supporting the proposed method. The weaknesses identified are addressable with relatively minor revisions.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Expand Section 4 to more clearly explain how the adaptive averaging heuristic differs from and improves upon existing adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam, particularly in the context of accelerated methods.

2. Include additional numerical experiments on higher-dimensional problems and more complex objective functions to better demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.

3. Add a discussion of the computational cost of the adaptive averaging heuristic, including how it compares to the standard accelerated method in terms of computational overhead.

4. Clarify the relationship between the continuous-time analysis and specific discrete implementations, perhaps by including a brief discussion of discretization schemes that preserve the convergence properties.

5. Strengthen the discussion of the paper's significance by more explicitly stating how the adaptive averaging heuristic advances the state of the art in accelerated optimization methods.

The paper's theoretical contribution is significant, and with these minor revisions, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The adaptive averaging heuristic is particularly promising as it provides a principled way to improve accelerated methods while preserving convergence guarantees.
