

# Peer Review: "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary

This paper studies continuous-time optimization dynamics, extending the connection between accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs. The authors introduce a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two weight functions w and η, which govern the averaging scheme and gradient accumulation rate, respectively. They provide a unified Lyapunov analysis to characterize convergence rates and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate while improving empirical performance. The paper connects to the NIPS literature through its extension of Su et al.'s work [20] on Nesterov's method as a continuous-time ODE, and Krichene et al.'s earlier work [11] on accelerated mirror descent. It builds on the broader theme of understanding optimization algorithms through continuous-time dynamics, which has been a fruitful line of research at NIPS (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 11, 20]).

The key novelty lies in the adaptive averaging heuristic, which dynamically adjusts the weight function w(t) to preserve the convergence rate while potentially improving performance. The authors demonstrate this approach on the replicator dynamics (a key ODE in evolutionary game theory) and provide numerical experiments showing favorable comparison to existing restarting heuristics.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Technical Quality**: The paper provides a rigorous Lyapunov analysis for the generalized AMD dynamics, establishing sufficient conditions for convergence at a given rate. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 are technically sound and build naturally on the authors' prior work [11]. The proof techniques are well-executed and follow standard practices in continuous-time optimization analysis.

2. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-organized, with clear transitions between theoretical results and applications. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the technical sections are logically structured. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to follow the mathematical derivations, including the primal representation of the dynamics and specific examples for simple constraint sets.

3. **Originality**: While the paper builds on previous work in continuous-time optimization dynamics, the adaptive averaging heuristic represents a meaningful extension. The idea of adaptively computing the weights w(t) to preserve the convergence rate while improving performance is novel and addresses a practical gap in the literature. The connection to replicator dynamics (Section 3.2) is particularly insightful, showing how acceleration can be applied to a fundamental ODE in evolutionary game theory.

4. **Significance**: The paper addresses an important practical issue in accelerated optimization - how to improve convergence without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a principled approach that outperforms existing heuristics in experiments. The results are significant for both theoretical understanding of accelerated methods and practical implementation, with potential applications in machine learning and optimization.

5. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The numerical experiments (Section 5) are well-designed, comparing the adaptive averaging heuristic against multiple restarting heuristics across different objective functions and constraint sets. The results demonstrate clear advantages for adaptive averaging, especially in strongly convex cases. The authors also provide visualizations of solution trajectories and energy functions, which enhance the interpretability of the results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation**: While the discrete version of the algorithm is presented in Algorithm 1, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of computational complexity and practical considerations for implementing adaptive averaging. For instance, it's unclear how the adaptive choice of a_k affects the per-iteration cost compared to fixed-weight schemes.

2. **Comparative Analysis with Other Adaptive Methods**: The paper briefly mentions Adagrad [7] and Adam [10] as similar but not designed for acceleration, but doesn't thoroughly compare the adaptive averaging approach to these methods. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the significance argument.

3. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper establishes that adaptive averaging preserves the 1/r(t) convergence rate, but doesn't prove it achieves a faster rate. The authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 6 ("it would be interesting to prove faster rates, e.g. for strongly convex functions"), but this is a notable theoretical gap. For strongly convex functions, accelerated methods typically achieve exponential convergence rates, but the paper doesn't address whether adaptive averaging can achieve this.

4. **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments**: While the experiments are well-designed, they're limited to R^3 for visualization purposes. The supplementary material mentions additional experiments in higher dimensions, but the main paper doesn't discuss these results. More comprehensive numerical evaluation across a wider range of problem sizes and dimensions would strengthen the empirical claims.

5. **Connection to Discrete Accelerated Methods**: The paper discusses the connection to Nesterov's method and discrete accelerated methods, but could more explicitly connect the adaptive averaging heuristic to discrete-time algorithms. The discrete version in Algorithm 1 is presented without sufficient context about how it relates to known accelerated methods.

## Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
- The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization dynamics
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, well-motivated, and empirically superior to existing methods
- The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical results
- The connection to replicator dynamics is insightful and shows broad applicability
- The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates practical significance

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The theoretical results don't prove adaptive averaging achieves faster rates (e.g., for strongly convex functions)
- The practical implementation details and computational complexity are not thoroughly addressed
- The numerical experiments could be more comprehensive (e.g., higher dimensions, larger problem sizes)
- The connection to discrete accelerated methods could be strengthened

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The theoretical contributions are significant, the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and well-justified, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. While there are some limitations in the theoretical scope and experimental coverage, these do not undermine the core contribution of the paper.

The paper represents a strong contribution to the growing literature on continuous-time optimization dynamics at NIPS, extending previous work with a practical and theoretically sound heuristic that has the potential to improve both the understanding and implementation of accelerated optimization methods.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the theoretical section, explicitly state the rate improvement (if any) for strongly convex functions when adaptive averaging is applied.

2. Include a brief discussion of computational complexity for the adaptive averaging heuristic in Algorithm 1, comparing it to fixed-weight schemes.

3. Expand the numerical experiments to include higher-dimensional problems in the main paper (or provide more details about the supplementary experiments).

4. Strengthen the connection between the continuous-time adaptive averaging heuristic and discrete-time accelerated methods, perhaps by showing how the discrete algorithm relates to known accelerated methods.

5. Consider adding a brief comparison to other adaptive methods (like Adagrad or Adam) applied to accelerated settings, to better position the contribution relative to existing work.

## Final Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of accelerated optimization dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic is both theoretically grounded and empirically effective, addressing a practical gap in the literature. While there are some limitations in the theoretical scope and experimental coverage, these do not detract from the paper's significant contribution to the field. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and represents an important step toward understanding and improving accelerated optimization methods. I believe this work would be of interest to the NIPS community and would make a valuable addition to the conference proceedings.
