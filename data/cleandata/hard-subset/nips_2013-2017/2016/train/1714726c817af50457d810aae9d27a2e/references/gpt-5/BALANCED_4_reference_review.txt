Summary
This paper studies a broad family of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two positive weight functions: w(t) for averaging in the primal and η(t) for gradient accumulation in the dual. The authors define a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), where r(t) is a target rate, and give simple sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) under which Lr is nonincreasing, yielding a convergence guarantee f(X(t))−f* ≤ Lr(X(t0),Z(t0),t0)/r(t). They also introduce a decreasing “energy” function Er combining potential and kinetic terms that decreases under the same conditions. The framework unifies and generalizes prior ODE views of acceleration (e.g., Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan) and recovers classical O(1/t^2) rates as special cases. The paper also derives a primal-only form of the dynamics, with concrete expressions for important feasible sets (positive orthant and simplex), yielding an accelerated replicator dynamics. Building on the Lyapunov analysis, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts w(t) online to further decrease Lr without sacrificing the target rate r(t), and give a discrete-time algorithm that preserves the continuous-time intuition. Experiments on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems compare adaptive averaging with gradient and speed restarts; adaptive averaging appears competitive and often superior, while maintaining a decreasing Lyapunov function.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to and extends (i) Su et al.’s ODE for Nesterov’s method and restart heuristics; (ii) Wibisono et al.’s variational perspective, showing AMDpw,η recovers their family with a particular parameterization; (iii) Lyapunov analyses of inertial dynamics (Attouch et al.). It complements practical restart heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès) by providing a rate-preserving adaptive rule in continuous time.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and unification: The Lyapunov template Lr with simple conditions on (w, η, r) elegantly connects averaging schedules to accelerated rates and encompasses several known ODEs as special cases.
- Generality: The framework handles generic mirror maps and convex constraint sets, with explicit primal forms for the orthant and simplex. The accelerated replicator dynamics is of independent interest to communities beyond optimization.
- Constructive design: The mapping a(t) = w(t)/W(t) provides a recipe to synthesize (w, η) for a desired r(t), including r(t) = t^p, p ≥ 2. The adaptive heuristic follows directly from the Lyapunov derivative bound and provably preserves the rate in continuous time.
- Empirical insight: Experiments, albeit small-scale, show adaptive averaging reduces oscillations and compares favorably to popular restart rules, while maintaining monotone Lr.

Weaknesses
- Discrete-time theory: The main rate guarantees are in continuous time. The discrete algorithm is only sketched, with theoretical details deferred to the supplement. It is unclear under which practical conditions (e.g., step sizes, proximal errors) the O(1/k^2) rate is preserved with the adaptive rule; no proof is in the main text.
- Experimental scope: Experiments are 3D and mainly illustrative. There are no large-scale comparisons on standard tasks (e.g., high-dimensional logistic regression, constrained L1/L∞ problems) or with strong baselines like FISTA with adaptive restarts, modern line-search variants, or Adam/Adagrad in constrained settings.
- Assumptions and technical clarity: The existence/uniqueness proof assumes ∇ψ* is Lipschitz; this may not hold globally for standard mirror maps without specifying norms and domains. Some notational inconsistencies/typos may confuse readers (see below).
- Relation to prior continuous-time acceleration: While the paper cites Wibisono et al., a deeper comparison of the condition η(t) = a(t)r(t) to the Bregman Lagrangian perspective, and how adaptive averaging compares to time-dilation/energy shaping there, would bolster the novelty.

Quality
The continuous-time analysis appears technically sound and uses standard convex-analytic tools. The sufficient conditions in Theorem 2 are transparent and lead to explicit constructions. The additional energy function is a nice interpretative device. However, the lack of main-text discrete-time convergence proofs for the adaptive heuristic limits practical impact. Some derivations (Examples 1–2) contain typographical inconsistencies that should be corrected.

Clarity
Overall well written, with clear statements of dynamics and conditions. The primal derivations and examples are valuable. However:
- In Examples 1–2, a(t) should be β/t (not β t) to satisfy a(t) ≥ r′/r and match subsequent formulas w(t) ∝ t^{β−1} and η(t) = a(t)r(t) = β t^{p−1}.
- The algorithmic description (Algorithm 1) uses additional parameters (γ, R) without full specification in the main text; Step 6 “βmax k √s” is unclear.
- The mapping between continuous time t and iteration k (t = k√s) is nonstandard; a brief rationale and sensitivity discussion would help.

Originality
The generalized averaging viewpoint with a tunable Lyapunov and the adaptive averaging heuristic that provably preserves the continuous-time rate are novel and insightful. The accelerated replicator dynamics is a neat byproduct. The work is incremental relative to the existing ODE acceleration literature but offers a distinctive angle with practical implications.

Significance
Potentially useful for designing rate-preserving adaptive variants of accelerated methods, and for understanding acceleration in constrained domains. The significance would be strengthened by solid discrete-time guarantees and broader empirical validation.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, general Lyapunov-based framework linking averaging schedules and rates
- Simple, principled adaptive heuristic that preserves rate (in continuous time)
- Interesting accelerated replicator dynamics and primal forms
- Clear connections to and generalizations of prior ODE work

Cons for acceptance
- Discrete-time theory for the adaptive rule is not established in the main text
- Experiments are small-scale; lacks strong baselines and high-dimensional tasks
- Some technical assumptions and notational issues reduce clarity

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a main-text discrete-time convergence analysis for the adaptive scheme (e.g., O(1/k^2) under mild conditions), including stability under misspecified β and adaptive step sizes.
- Extend theory to strongly convex settings (linear rates) with adaptive averaging, or clarify why this may or may not be possible.
- Clarify assumptions on ψ, ψ*, and norms ensuring Lipschitzness and existence/uniqueness, especially for entropy/softmax cases.
- Correct the a(t) typos in Examples 1–2; specify all algorithmic constants (γ, R) and Step 6 precisely; add guidance for choosing β, s.
- Broaden experiments: higher dimensions, standard datasets, comparisons to FISTA with adaptive restarts, line-search variants, and projection-free baselines if relevant; include sensitivity and ablation on the adaptive rule.
- Explore whether choosing a(t) by minimizing the instantaneous upper bound on dLr/dt leads to an “optimal” schedule, and connections to time-dilation in the Bregman Lagrangian.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Conceptually solid and potentially impactful, but would benefit from stronger discrete-time theory and broader empirical validation.
