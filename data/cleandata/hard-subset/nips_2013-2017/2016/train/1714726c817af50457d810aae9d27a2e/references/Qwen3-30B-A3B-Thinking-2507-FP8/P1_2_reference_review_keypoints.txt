- Summary: Introduces generalized Accelerated Mirror Descent dynamics with adaptive averaging (weights w, η), provides Lyapunov-based convergence analysis, and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic with favorable empirical results and connection to replicator dynamics.

- Strength 1 (Theoretical): Rigorous Lyapunov analysis (Theorem 2, Corollary 1) giving sufficient conditions for preserved convergence rates.

- Strength 2 (Clarity): Well-organized paper with clear motivation, derivations, primal representation, and illustrative examples.

- Strength 3 (Originality): Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts weights to preserve rates while improving performance; insightful link to replicator dynamics.

- Strength 4 (Significance): Addresses practical issue of improving accelerated methods without losing guarantees, with potential ML/optimization applications.

- Strength 5 (Experiments): Comprehensive, well-designed numerical comparisons and visualizations showing adaptive averaging outperforming restarting heuristics, especially in strongly convex cases.

- Weakness 1 (Implementation): Lacks detailed discussion of computational complexity and per-iteration cost of adaptive weight selection in the discrete algorithm.

- Weakness 2 (Comparisons): Insufficient comparative analysis with popular adaptive methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) and their relation to acceleration.

- Weakness 3 (Theoretical limits): Shows preservation of 1/r(t) rates but does not prove faster rates (e.g., exponential for strongly convex problems).

- Weakness 4 (Empirical scope): Main experiments confined to R^3 for visualization; higher-dimensional results are only in supplementary material and not discussed in the main text.

- Weakness 5 (Discrete connection): The link between the continuous adaptive averaging heuristic and discrete accelerated algorithms is underdeveloped; Algorithm 1 lacks contextualization with known methods.

- Arguments for acceptance: Makes clear theoretical contributions, presents a novel and empirically effective heuristic, is technically sound, connects to replicator dynamics, and demonstrates practical significance.

- Arguments against acceptance: Does not prove faster convergence rates for adaptive averaging, omits implementation complexity analysis, needs broader experiments, and should better relate to discrete accelerated methods.

- Recommendation: Accept — paper is a strong, technically sound contribution with novel heuristic and convincing empirical results despite some limitations.

- Suggestion 1: State explicitly any rate improvements (if present) for strongly convex functions under adaptive averaging.

- Suggestion 2: Add discussion of computational complexity and per-iteration cost comparing adaptive vs. fixed-weight schemes.

- Suggestion 3: Expand main-paper experiments to higher-dimensional problems or summarize supplementary higher-dimensional results.

- Suggestion 4: Strengthen the connection between the discrete Algorithm 1 and known discrete accelerated methods to clarify relations.

- Suggestion 5: Include brief empirical or theoretical comparisons to other adaptive optimizers (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) in accelerated settings.

- Final assessment: Strong theoretical and empirical contribution to continuous-time accelerated dynamics with a novel adaptive averaging heuristic deserving acceptance.
