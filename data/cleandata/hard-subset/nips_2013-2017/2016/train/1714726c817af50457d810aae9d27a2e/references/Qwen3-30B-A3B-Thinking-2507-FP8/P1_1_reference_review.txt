

# Peer Review: "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for convex optimization, extending previous work on the connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs. The authors introduce a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two weight functions w(t) and η(t), which govern the averaging scheme and the rate at which gradients accumulate, respectively. The key contribution is a unified Lyapunov function analysis that provides sufficient conditions for guaranteeing specific convergence rates. The authors then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes the weight functions w(t) to preserve the convergence rate while potentially improving performance. The paper demonstrates this approach on specific constraint sets (positive orthant and simplex), connecting it to established dynamics like the replicator equation. Numerical experiments show that adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics.

The work builds on prior NIPS contributions such as Su et al. [20] (who connected Nesterov's method to a second-order ODE) and Krichene et al. [11] (who studied the proximal version of Nesterov's method in continuous time). It also relates to the broader literature on continuous-time optimization dynamics [1, 2, 3, 9, 23].

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs of all theoretical claims. The authors provide a clear Lyapunov function analysis that establishes sufficient conditions for convergence rates, which is a significant contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. Theorem 2 and its corollary provide precise conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing, guaranteeing the desired convergence rates. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, with the authors showing that it preserves the convergence rate (unlike some existing heuristics).

The numerical experiments are well-designed, comparing adaptive averaging to established restarting heuristics across multiple problem types (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, and KL divergence). The results demonstrate that adaptive averaging consistently outperforms the other methods, particularly in the strongly convex case. The supplementary material provides additional theoretical analysis and algorithm details.

The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations, noting that the second condition in Theorem 2 depends on the solution trajectory, which can be difficult to verify a priori. The authors also clearly distinguish their approach from previous work on restarting heuristics, which often lack theoretical guarantees for non-strongly convex problems.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear and detailed, with consistent notation throughout. The progression from problem setup to theoretical results to examples and experiments is logical and easy to follow. The examples (positive orthant and simplex) effectively illustrate the theoretical results in concrete settings.

The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the relationship between the continuous-time dynamics and their discrete-time counterparts. While the authors mention that the discrete version is provided in the supplementary material, a concise overview in the main text would help readers understand how the continuous-time insights translate to practical algorithms.

The figures mentioned in the text (e.g., Figure 1, Figure 2) would be valuable for visualizing the dynamics and experimental results, though they are not included in the provided excerpt.

## Originality Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on continuous-time optimization dynamics. The generalized averaging scheme extends previous work on accelerated methods, and the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel. Unlike previous restarting heuristics (e.g., [17, 20]), which lack theoretical guarantees for non-strongly convex problems, the authors' approach preserves the convergence rate while adapting to the trajectory.

The connection to the replicator dynamics (a well-known ODE in evolutionary game theory) is particularly insightful and demonstrates the broader applicability of the framework. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, including the authors' own previous NIPS contribution [11], by providing a more general framework with adaptive weights.

The paper does not appear to be a trivial extension of previous work. The authors provide a unified analysis that encompasses previous special cases while introducing new theoretical insights and practical improvements.

## Significance Assessment

The significance of this work is substantial. The theoretical insights into accelerated optimization dynamics have the potential to inform the design of more efficient algorithms. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a practical improvement that could benefit a wide range of optimization problems. The connection to replicator dynamics opens up new possibilities for applying optimization techniques to problems in evolutionary game theory and related fields.

The numerical experiments demonstrate clear practical benefits over existing methods, with significant improvements in the strongly convex case. The paper advances the state of the art in understanding accelerated optimization methods, both theoretically and practically.

The work is likely to be influential for researchers studying continuous-time optimization dynamics and for practitioners seeking to implement accelerated methods with better convergence properties.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is technically sound, well-written, original, and significant. It makes a clear contribution to the field of optimization, particularly in the context of continuous-time dynamics and accelerated methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a valuable addition to the optimization toolkit with both theoretical guarantees and practical benefits.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. **Technical details**: The proof of Theorem 1 (existence and uniqueness) is concise but could benefit from a brief discussion of why initializing at t0 > 0 is necessary (beyond the technical point about W(0) = 0).

2. **Discrete-time connection**: While the discrete version is in the supplementary material, a brief overview of the relationship between the continuous-time dynamics and their discrete-time counterparts in the main text would be helpful for readers less familiar with the field.

3. **Experimental details**: The paper mentions experiments in R^3 for visualization, but it would be valuable to include a brief discussion of computational complexity or how the adaptive averaging heuristic scales with problem dimension.

4. **Comparison to other adaptive methods**: While the authors mention Adagrad and Adam, it might be worth briefly discussing how their adaptive averaging differs from these methods, particularly since they're designed for accelerated methods rather than standard first-order methods.

5. **Theoretical limitations**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the cases where the adaptive averaging heuristic might not provide significant benefits compared to the standard accelerated method.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides a unified framework for analyzing accelerated descent dynamics with generalized averaging
- Theoretical guarantee that the adaptive averaging heuristic preserves the convergence rate
- Demonstrates clear practical benefits over existing heuristics through well-designed experiments
- Makes a novel connection between optimization and replicator dynamics
- Well-written with clear mathematical derivations and organization

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The adaptive averaging heuristic might be seen as a minor extension of existing methods
- The numerical experiments are limited to specific problem types (though they are well-chosen)
- The paper could more explicitly discuss how the adaptive averaging compares to other adaptive methods in the literature

## Conclusion

This paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods through the lens of continuous-time dynamics. The theoretical results are rigorous, the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and well-motivated, and the numerical experiments provide strong evidence of its practical benefits. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to the NIPS community. I believe this work would be of interest to researchers working on optimization algorithms, continuous-time dynamics, and their applications in machine learning.
