

# Peer Review: "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary
This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for optimization, specifically focusing on accelerated descent methods. The authors introduce a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two weight functions w and η, which control the averaging scheme and gradient accumulation rate. Building on previous work by Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11], they provide a unified Lyapunov-based analysis to establish convergence rates. The key contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts weights based on the solution trajectory, preserving the convergence rate while improving empirical performance. The paper includes examples for specific constraint sets (positive orthant and simplex), where they derive accelerated versions of known dynamics (e.g., replicator equation). The work connects continuous-time analysis to discrete optimization algorithms while providing practical improvements.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-justified theoretical claims. The Lyapunov function analysis is rigorous and clearly demonstrates sufficient conditions for achieving desired convergence rates. The authors provide a careful treatment of existence and uniqueness of solutions (Theorem 1), which is often overlooked in similar works. The numerical experiments are appropriately designed and comparative, showing adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics across multiple problem types (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, KL divergence). The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the focus on r(t)=t² for the adaptive heuristic, and properly references related work.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear motivation for studying continuous-time dynamics and the specific problem of adaptive averaging. The technical development follows a logical progression from general formulation to specific examples. The use of the Lyapunov function framework (Equation 2) provides a unifying perspective that makes the analysis accessible. The supplementary material appears to address implementation details for the discrete algorithm. Minor suggestions for improvement include adding a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the adaptive heuristic and explicitly contrasting it with other adaptive methods like Adagrad.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of accelerated methods through continuous-time dynamics. While previous work [20, 11] established connections between acceleration and averaging, this paper generalizes the averaging scheme and introduces a novel adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance. The connection to replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained problems (Section 3) is particularly novel and meaningful, as it provides a concrete application in evolutionary game theory. The adaptive heuristic (Section 4) represents a clear advance over existing restarting heuristics, as it does not require external conditions for restarting and guarantees the convergence rate is preserved.

## Significance
The results have both theoretical and practical significance. The continuous-time analysis provides deeper insight into acceleration mechanisms, which could guide future algorithm design. The adaptive averaging heuristic is particularly valuable as it preserves theoretical guarantees while improving empirical performance - a rare combination in the optimization literature. The experiments demonstrate significant improvements over existing heuristics, especially for strongly convex problems. The connection to replicator dynamics opens potential applications in evolutionary game theory and viability theory, broadening the paper's impact beyond standard optimization.

## Strengths
- Clear theoretical framework using Lyapunov functions to analyze convergence rates
- Novel adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance
- Interesting connection to replicator dynamics with meaningful applications
- Well-designed numerical experiments demonstrating consistent improvements over existing methods
- Good balance between theoretical analysis and practical insights

## Weaknesses
- The adaptive heuristic is limited to the specific rate r(t)=t², with limited discussion of generalization to other rates
- The paper doesn't fully address how the continuous-time analysis translates to discrete-time algorithms beyond the specific case
- Computational complexity of the adaptive heuristic is not discussed
- The connection to other adaptive methods (Adagrad, Adam) could be more explicitly contrasted

## Recommendation
This is a strong contribution to the field of optimization, with clear theoretical insights and practical improvements. The paper addresses an important problem (improving accelerated methods without sacrificing convergence guarantees) with a novel approach that has both theoretical and empirical merit. While the adaptive heuristic is limited to r(t)=t², this is a reasonable starting point for a new approach, and the paper provides a clear path for future work.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding accelerated methods through continuous-time dynamics, introduces a novel adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance, and provides compelling numerical evidence of its superiority over existing methods. The connection to replicator dynamics is particularly valuable for applications in evolutionary game theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The scope of the adaptive heuristic is limited to a specific convergence rate, and the discrete-time implementation details beyond this specific case are not fully addressed.

**Overall:** This is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The theoretical results are sound, the empirical validation is thorough, and the practical implications are clear. I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS.
