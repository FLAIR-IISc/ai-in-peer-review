

# Peer Review: "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper studies continuous-time accelerated optimization dynamics with a generalized averaging scheme. The authors propose a family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two weight functions w and η, where w is used in the averaging and η determines the rate at which the dual variable accumulates gradients. The main theoretical contribution is a unified analysis using a Lyapunov function that establishes sufficient conditions for achieving a specified convergence rate. The paper also introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes the weights w along solution trajectories, preserving the convergence rate while potentially improving empirical performance.

The work builds upon and extends prior research by Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11], which connected Nesterov's accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs. The authors generalize these results to a broader class of averaging schemes and provide a unified framework for analyzing the convergence properties of these dynamics. A key application is to the replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained optimization problems, where the authors show how to accelerate the standard replicator dynamics through adaptive averaging.

## Quality

The paper presents technically sound theoretical analysis with rigorous proofs. The authors derive sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing, which guarantees the desired convergence rate. The analysis is well-structured, building logically from the continuous-time dynamics to the discrete implementation.

Key strengths include:
- Clear definition of the Lyapunov function and its properties
- Thorough derivation of sufficient conditions (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1)
- Well-motivated examples for specific constraint sets (positive orthant, simplex)
- Empirical validation of the adaptive heuristic against existing restarting methods

However, there are some quality concerns:
- The continuous-time analysis is not sufficiently justified as necessary for the discrete algorithm, which is the practical contribution. The paper states that the continuous-time approach "provides intuition and insight into the discrete process" (p.1), but doesn't convincingly argue why this particular continuous-time framework was needed to develop the adaptive heuristic.
- The paper doesn't fully explore how the adaptive averaging heuristic relates to existing adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam, which also adjust parameters based on observed gradients. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
- The numerical experiments are limited to R^3 (simplex in 3 dimensions), which doesn't fully demonstrate the scalability of the method for larger-scale machine learning problems.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The main theorems and proofs are clearly presented, and the examples help ground the abstract framework in concrete applications.

Areas where clarity could be improved:
- The motivation for the continuous-time perspective could be strengthened. The authors state that "continuous-time dynamics for optimization have been studied for a long time" but don't clearly articulate why this particular continuous-time framework is needed for their adaptive heuristic, which is ultimately implemented in discrete time.
- The connection between the continuous-time analysis and the discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) could be made more explicit. The supplementary material contains the discrete version, but it would be helpful to have a more detailed discussion of how the continuous-time analysis directly informs the discrete algorithm.
- The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the Bregman divergence and its role in the Lyapunov function, particularly for readers who may not be optimization experts.

## Originality

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the literature on accelerated optimization dynamics. The generalized averaging scheme extends previous work by Krichene et al. [11] and Su et al. [20]. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel in its specific implementation and theoretical grounding in the Lyapunov function.

However, the degree of novelty is somewhat limited:
- The connection between acceleration and averaging was already explored in [8] and [11], and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its generalization from these prior works.
- The adaptive heuristic itself (decreasing the time derivative of the Lyapunov function) is conceptually similar to existing adaptive methods like Adagrad, which adjust learning rates based on historical gradient information. The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how this approach differs from or improves upon these existing methods.
- The paper references [23] (Wibisono et al.), which also studies accelerated dynamics with a focus on discretization. It would be helpful to more clearly position this work relative to that paper.

## Significance

The significance of the paper is somewhat limited:
- While the theoretical contribution is solid, it's largely incremental rather than transformative. The convergence rate results are consistent with prior work, and the adaptive heuristic doesn't seem to offer a major improvement over existing methods beyond what's already known.
- The practical implications aren't fully explored. The paper shows the heuristic works well on small-scale problems, but doesn't provide evidence that it would be particularly useful for large-scale machine learning problems, which are the main application domain for accelerated methods.
- The connection to replicator dynamics is interesting, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this would impact real-world applications in evolutionary game theory or other fields.
- The paper doesn't address how the adaptive heuristic would compare to other adaptive methods like Adagrad or Adam in terms of both theoretical guarantees and empirical performance on standard machine learning benchmarks.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of continuous-time optimization dynamics. It generalizes prior work on accelerated mirror descent to a broader class of averaging schemes and provides a unified theoretical analysis. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and shows promising empirical results in the limited experiments provided.

However, the novelty is somewhat incremental, and the significance is limited by the lack of a strong argument for why the continuous-time framework was necessary for the adaptive heuristic. The practical impact on machine learning applications isn't sufficiently demonstrated, and the connection to existing adaptive methods could be better clarified.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear sufficient conditions for convergence
- Well-motivated examples, particularly the connection to replicator dynamics
- Empirical validation showing adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics in the tested examples
- Clear presentation of the main theorems and proofs

### Weaknesses:
- Limited justification for the continuous-time perspective when the main contribution is a discrete algorithm
- Insufficient comparison with existing adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam
- Limited empirical evaluation (only R^3 experiments)
- Not sufficiently clear how the work differs from or improves upon prior research by Krichene et al. [11] and Wibisono et al. [23]

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The work is technically sound and makes a reasonable contribution to the literature, but the novelty is somewhat incremental and the significance is limited by the lack of a strong argument for the continuous-time approach and the limited empirical validation.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A stronger justification for why the continuous-time analysis was necessary for developing the adaptive heuristic
2. More comprehensive empirical evaluation on larger-scale problems
3. A more detailed comparison with existing adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam
4. A clearer articulation of how this work differs from and improves upon prior research by Krichene et al. [11] and Wibisono et al. [23]

With these improvements, the paper would be a stronger contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a solid technical contribution that would be appropriate for publication in a journal but falls short of the most significant contributions expected at NeurIPS.
