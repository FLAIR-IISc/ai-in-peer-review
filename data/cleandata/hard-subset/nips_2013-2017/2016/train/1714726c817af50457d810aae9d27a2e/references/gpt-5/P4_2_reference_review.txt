Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for convex optimization with Lipschitz gradients over a closed convex set X. It introduces a family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) ODEs parameterized by two positive, continuous weight functions: an averaging weight w(t) and a gradient-accumulation rate η(t). The dynamics are given in dual form by Ż(t) = −η(t)∇f(X(t)) and X(t) being a weighted average of the mirror map ∇ψ∗(Z(τ)) with weights w(τ). A Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f∗) + Dψ∗(Z, z∗) is proposed, where r(t) is a desired rate function and Dψ∗ is the Bregman divergence. The paper derives sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) ensuring Lr is nonincreasing, implying f(X(t)) − f∗ ≤ const/r(t). A corollary gives a convenient condition: choosing η(t) = w(t)r(t)/W(t) and w(t)/W(t) ≥ r′(t)/r(t) guarantees the rate 1/r(t). The authors provide example constructions, including r(t) = tp with p ≥ 1, yielding η(t) = βtp−1 and w(t) ∝ tβ−1 for β ≥ p, which recovers known quadratic rates when p = 2.

A second “energy” function Er(t) = f(X(t)) + 1/r(t) Dψ∗(Z(t), ∇ψ(X(t))) is shown to be nonincreasing under the same conditions. The paper also derives a primal-only formulation and shows that for common ψ (entropy on the positive orthant and simplex), the dynamics reduce to interpretable ODEs, e.g., an accelerated replicator equation on the simplex. Building on the Lyapunov analysis, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) := w/W along the trajectory to decrease d/dt Lr whenever possible, preserving the continuous-time rate. A discrete algorithm is proposed for r(t) = t2, and experiments (mostly in R3) compare adaptive averaging to gradient and speed restart heuristics [17, 20], showing favorable performance and monotonicity of the discrete analogue of the Lyapunov function.

This work connects to: inertial/accelerated ODEs illuminating Nesterov’s methods [20, 23]; mirror descent and accelerated mirror descent [13, 11]; and heuristic restarts [17, 20]. The averaging perspective also relates to observations of connections between averaging and acceleration [8].

Strengths
- Unified Lyapunov framework: Clear sufficient conditions yielding a convergence rate 1/r(t) for a broad family of AMD dynamics, with a simple corollary that recovers known accelerated rates.
- Generality and interpretability: The decoupling into gradient accumulation η and averaging weights w clarifies the role of averaging in acceleration and unifies several continuous-time formulations, including a mapping to the variational family in [23].
- Primal forms and examples: The derivations for positive orthant and simplex are explicit and show how classical dynamics (replicator) can be accelerated via the proposed scheme.
- Energy function: The additional nonincreasing energy provides intuition and diagnostic value beyond the Lyapunov function.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic: Conceptually simple, preserves the continuous-time rate by design, and empirically competitive with popular restart heuristics.

Weaknesses
- Trajectory-dependent condition: The Lyapunov decrease condition (Theorem 2, item 2) is trajectory-dependent and not verifiable a priori; the corollary imposes structural constraints that may be conservative.
- Discrete-time theory: The rate for the discrete adaptive algorithm is deferred to the supplement; the main text lacks a detailed, self-contained discrete analysis or comparison with optimal discrete rates.
- Experimental scope: Experiments are low-dimensional and deterministic; there is no assessment with large-scale or stochastic gradients, where adaptive schemes (e.g., Adam/Adagrad) are commonly applied.
- Clarity issues/typos: In Example 1, a(t) is stated as βt but calculations correspond to a(t) = β/t; this can confuse readers. Some notation choices (initialization at t0, assumptions on ∇ψ∗ Lipschitzness) could be clarified for common ψ on constrained sets.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound and internally consistent. Existence/uniqueness follows from standard ODE theory given the initialization at t0 > 0 and Lipschitz assumptions. The Lyapunov derivative calculations (inequality (3)) are standard and the conditions are sufficient. The corollary and constructive recipe via a(t) are helpful. The energy argument is correct and provides an additional monotonic quantity. The discrete algorithm appears to be a reasonable discretization of the continuous heuristic, but the manuscript’s main body lacks proofs of discrete convergence and robustness to practical issues (step-size choice, noise). The experiments support the claims qualitatively, though they are limited in scope.

Clarity
Overall the paper is clearly written and well organized. The separation between dual and primal representations, and the examples, aid readability. Notation is heavy but mostly consistent. Specific suggestions:
- Correct the typo in Example 1: a(t) should be β/t (not βt), consistent with the subsequent integrals and η(t) = βt.
- Explicitly state the conditions under which ∇ψ∗ is Lipschitz and maps E∗ to X for the entropy-based ψ on constrained domains.
- Summarize the discrete Lyapunov function and the main discrete convergence bound in the main text.

Originality
The generalized averaging viewpoint with flexible w and η, together with a Lyapunov condition that yields any prescribed rate r(t), is a notable conceptual contribution. The adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the Lyapunov decrease is, to the best of my knowledge, new relative to restart heuristics [17, 20]. The mapping to and generalization of the variational ODE family [23] is insightful. While the continuous-time acceleration literature is large, this paper’s emphasis on adaptive averaging and its unifying conditions appears original.

Significance
The results provide a coherent framework for understanding and designing accelerated dynamics via averaging, which is relevant to both theory (continuous-time analysis) and algorithm design. The adaptive averaging heuristic is potentially impactful, especially if supported by stronger discrete-time analysis and broader experiments. Current empirical evidence is promising but limited; hence the practical significance is moderate at this stage.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, general Lyapunov framework with constructive conditions recovering and extending known accelerated rates.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with guaranteed Lyapunov decrease and good empirical behavior.
- Useful primal representations and examples (including accelerated replicator dynamics).
- Conceptual unification with variational formulations [23].

Arguments against acceptance
- Discrete-time guarantees for the adaptive method are not developed in the main text; experiments are small-scale.
- The key sufficient condition is trajectory-dependent (requiring corollary conditions that may be restrictive).
- Minor but important clarity/typo issues that could mislead readers.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concise discrete-time theorem in the main text: state assumptions, the Lyapunov function, and the rate for Algorithm 1; clarify step-size selection and robustness.
- Extend experiments to higher-dimensional problems, include stochastic gradients, and report wall-clock or gradient-evaluation metrics.
- Discuss choices of ψ and regularizer R in practice, and any computational overhead of maintaining adaptive weights.
- Clarify the initialization at t0 > 0 and its implications for discretization; add guidance on setting β and a(t) caps.
- Fix the a(t) typo in Example 1 and ensure consistency of assumptions on ∇ψ∗.

Overall, the paper presents a technically solid and conceptually interesting contribution to accelerated optimization dynamics with an adaptive averaging perspective.
