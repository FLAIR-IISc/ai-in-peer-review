## NIPS 2016 Review: Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics

**Summary:**

This paper investigates continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order optimization, building upon the connection established in recent work (Su et al., Krichene et al.) between Nesterov's acceleration and ODEs. The authors generalize the Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics by introducing a broad family of averaging schemes parameterized by weight functions *w* and *η*. They provide sufficient conditions on *w* and *η* for a Lyapunov function to guarantee convergence at a rate of 1/r(t), and demonstrate this with specific examples including an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics. Crucially, they propose a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts the weights *w(t)* to preserve the convergence rate, unlike existing restarting heuristics. Numerical experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, particularly in strongly convex settings.

**Related Work & Context:**

The paper sits firmly within a growing body of research connecting discrete optimization algorithms with their continuous-time counterparts.  The lineage is clearly established, referencing key works like Nesterov’s original acceleration [14, 15], the work of Su et al. [20] linking acceleration to ODEs and restarting heuristics, and the authors' prior work [11] on proximal AMD. The focus on *continuous-time analysis* is a valuable approach as it often simplifies theoretical analysis and provides intuition for discrete algorithms. The comparison to other adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam [7, 10] is appropriate, highlighting the distinction that their method is specifically tailored for accelerated schemes.  The acknowledgment of the broader field of restarting heuristics ([17, 20]) and their limitations provides a clear motivation for the adaptive approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The Lyapunov analysis is rigorous and provides a solid guarantee for convergence under the stated conditions. The derivation of sufficient conditions on *w* and *η* is clear and well-motivated.
* **Generalization of AMD:**  The generalized AMD framework allows for a flexible exploration of different averaging schemes, going beyond the established approach in [11].
* **Adaptive Averaging Heuristic:** The proposed heuristic is the key contribution. Its ability to preserve the convergence rate – a critical advantage over existing restarting methods – is a significant theoretical result.
* **Connection to Replicator Dynamics:**  Deriving an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics is a nice demonstration of the framework’s applicability and relevance to other fields like game theory.
* **Convincing Empirical Results:**  The numerical experiments support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging heuristic, particularly in the strongly convex case.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Empirical Validation:** While the experiments are promising, they are limited to relatively simple scenarios. Expanding the experiments to a broader range of problems (e.g., non-convex optimization, larger datasets, different constraint sets) would significantly strengthen the claims.  The experiments don’t clearly illustrate *when* the adaptive averaging truly outperforms the baseline methods beyond the strongly convex case.
* **Implementation Details:** The description of Algorithm 1 is relatively high-level.  More details on the practical implementation (e.g., line search, parameter tuning) would be helpful for reproducibility.
* **Computational Cost of Adaptive Step:**  The adaptive step, where *a(t)* is updated, introduces a computational cost. While likely small, it's not explicitly discussed or analyzed in relation to the overall complexity of the algorithm.
* **Convergence Rate in Non-Strongly Convex Cases:** The paper focuses primarily on preserving the convergence rate. Investigating whether the adaptive averaging can *improve* the convergence rate in non-strongly convex settings would be a valuable extension.
* **Discussion of Regularization Parameter:**  Algorithm 1 mentions a regularizer R, but its role and impact on the overall performance aren’t fully elaborated.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to accelerating first-order optimization methods.
* The adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution, offering a guaranteed convergence rate that surpasses existing methods.
* The connection to replicator dynamics highlights the broader applicability of the framework.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical validation is limited and doesn't fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach across a wider range of problems.
* Some implementation details are missing, hindering reproducibility.
*  The paper doesn't explore potential improvements to the convergence rate in non-strongly convex settings.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The theoretical results are rigorous, the adaptive averaging heuristic is insightful, and the empirical validation is encouraging. While the experiments could be more comprehensive and the implementation details more explicit, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I believe this paper is of high quality and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions to address the suggestions regarding experimental scope and implementation details).  I recommend that the authors consider expanding the numerical experiments and providing more detail on the practical implementation of Algorithm 1.
