## Peer Review: Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics

**Summary:**

This paper investigates continuous-time dynamics for accelerated optimization methods, specifically focusing on Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD). Building on prior work connecting Nesterov’s acceleration to continuous ODEs, the authors introduce a generalized AMD framework parameterized by weight functions controlling averaging and gradient accumulation. They establish sufficient conditions for Lyapunov stability and demonstrate a convergence rate of 1/r(t), where r(t) is a desired convergence rate function.  Importantly, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts the weighting scheme based on the Lyapunov function’s derivative, aiming to preserve the convergence rate and outperform existing restart heuristics. The paper also provides primal representations of the dynamics and demonstrates applications to specific constraint sets (positive orthant and simplex), deriving an accelerated replicator dynamics for the simplex case.  Numerical experiments support the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds upon a well-established line of work connecting discrete optimization algorithms to continuous-time dynamics, providing a solid theoretical basis for the proposed generalized AMD framework. The Lyapunov analysis is rigorous and clearly presented.
* **Generalization and Flexibility:** The introduction of weight functions `w` and `η` provides a flexible framework for exploring different averaging schemes, moving beyond the fixed schemes of previous work.
* **Adaptive Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution.  The intuition behind it—dynamically adjusting the averaging to maximize progress—is compelling, and the theoretical guarantee of preserving the convergence rate is valuable.
* **Concrete Examples and Applications:** The derivation of the accelerated replicator dynamics for the simplex constraint is a nice illustration of the framework’s applicability to real-world problems and draws connections to evolutionary game theory.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments, while not extensive, provide initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging heuristic compared to existing restart strategies.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the theoretical development and understand the main ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. They primarily focus on the simplex constraint and a few objective functions.  More comprehensive experiments, including higher-dimensional problems and different constraint sets, would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Heuristic Justification, Not Proof:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is presented as a strategy to *preserve* the convergence rate. While the theoretical analysis supports this, it doesn’t inherently prove that this is *optimal* or that it outperforms other adaptive strategies. A deeper theoretical justification for why this specific adaptive scheme is beneficial would be valuable.
* **Connection to Existing Adaptive Methods:** While the authors acknowledge related work like AdaGrad and Adam, the connection could be explored further. It would be helpful to explicitly discuss how adaptive averaging differs in its approach and potential advantages over these methods, especially in the context of accelerated methods.  
* **Complexity of Implementation:** The adaptive averaging scheme, while theoretically elegant, introduces additional complexity to the algorithm. A discussion of the practical implications of this complexity—e.g., computational overhead—would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Discussion on Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of initial weight functions and parameters (e.g., `β` in Algorithm 1) might significantly impact performance. The paper lacks a discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm to these parameters.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound generalization of accelerated mirror descent dynamics.
* The adaptive averaging heuristic is a promising approach to improving the performance of accelerated methods, with a theoretical guarantee of preserving the convergence rate.
* The connections to replicator dynamics and other areas like evolutionary game theory are interesting and broaden the potential impact of the work.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The numerical experiments are limited in scope and could benefit from more comprehensive validation.
* The theoretical justification for the specific adaptive averaging scheme could be strengthened.
* The complexity of implementation and parameter sensitivity are not adequately addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The generalized AMD framework and adaptive averaging heuristic are novel and well-motivated. While the numerical experiments could be more extensive and the theoretical justification could be refined, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I believe this paper is a worthy contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions, specifically encouraging more extensive numerical validation and a deeper discussion of parameter sensitivity and connections to existing adaptive methods).



**Confidence:** 8/10. While I have confidence in the quality of the work, the limited scope of the experiments leads to some uncertainty.
