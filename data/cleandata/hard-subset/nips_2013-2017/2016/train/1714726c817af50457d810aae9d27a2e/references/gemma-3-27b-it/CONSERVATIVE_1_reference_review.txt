This paper explores adaptive averaging schemes within the framework of accelerated mirror descent (AMD), building upon previous work connecting acceleration and averaging in optimization. The authors generalize a prior ODE formulation and propose a heuristic for adaptively computing averaging weights to preserve convergence rates. While the continuous-time analysis and connection to replicator dynamics are interesting, the paper lacks the rigorous validation and comparative analysis expected at NIPS, and relies heavily on intuition without comprehensive empirical support.

The core idea – adapting averaging weights based on Lyapunov function decrease – is reasonable. However, the theoretical guarantees, while stated, feel limited. The conditions for Lyapunov function decrease (Theorem 2) are quite strong and likely difficult to verify in practice. The discussion around energy function decrease (Theorem 3) is also interesting, but doesn’t fundamentally strengthen the claims regarding convergence. The leap from continuous-time analysis to practical algorithm design (Algorithm 1) feels insufficiently motivated and the convergence claims for the discrete version, referenced to supplementary material, are not presented here for evaluation.

A key weakness is the limited experimental evaluation. The visualizations are helpful, but the setup (R3, simple objectives) is constrained. The comparison against existing heuristics (gradient/speed restarting) feels cursory. While the authors claim favorable comparisons, the presentation lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., convergence curves, number of iterations to a given accuracy). It's unclear *how much* better adaptive averaging performs, and under what conditions. Furthermore, the lack of comparison to standard, well-established first-order methods (e.g., Nesterov's accelerated gradient, FISTA) is a significant omission. Demonstrating improvement over a basic accelerated method is crucial before claiming superiority over more sophisticated heuristics.  The statement that the Lyapunov function is non-decreasing under adaptive averaging, but "will not necessarily converge to 0" is a bit of a red flag, suggesting a potential subtlety the authors haven’t fully addressed.

The connection to replicator dynamics is a potentially interesting application, but this feels somewhat tangential to the main contribution. The paper hints at potential applications in evolutionary game theory, but doesn’t delve deeply into these areas. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper explores a potentially fruitful direction in adaptive averaging schemes.
*   The continuous-time analysis provides some theoretical justification for the proposed approach.
*   The connection to replicator dynamics suggests potential applications in other domains.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical guarantees are limited and potentially difficult to verify in practice.
*   The experimental evaluation is weak and lacks quantitative comparisons to standard methods.
*   The paper relies heavily on intuition without comprehensive empirical support.
*   The connection to replicator dynamics feels underdeveloped.
*   The overall contribution does not represent a substantial advance in the field, lacking a compelling empirical demonstration of performance.

**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be a moderately interesting exploration of adaptive averaging, but ultimately not of sufficient quality for acceptance to NIPS. The theoretical contributions are incremental, and the lack of rigorous experimental validation significantly weakens the claims. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a complete, impactful contribution. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the experimental section, provide a more thorough comparison to established methods, and offer stronger theoretical guarantees to warrant acceptance. I recommend **rejection**.
