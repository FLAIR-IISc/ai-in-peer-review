- Studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with a generalized averaging scheme parameterized by weight functions w and η.  
- Provides sufficient conditions on w, η, and r(t) for AMD to achieve convergence rate 1/r(t).  
- Develops a unified Lyapunov-function analysis (Theorems 2 and 3) that establishes convergence and decreasing energy.  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves theoretical convergence rates and can improve empirical performance.  
- Demonstrates application of adaptive averaging to replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained problems.  
- Connects continuous-time dynamics to discrete optimization algorithms and provides a discrete algorithm in the supplement.  
- Numerical experiments show adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics across several objective types.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous with careful proofs and attention to required conditions.  
- Presentation is clear, well-organized, with consistent notation and helpful examples for different constraint sets.  
- Supplementary material provides additional derivations, but some adaptive-averaging intuition could be moved to the main text.  
- Originality: extends prior continuous-time accelerated-method work by unifying generalized averaging and introducing adaptive averaging.  
- Novel connection to replicator dynamics is interesting and potentially impactful for evolutionary/game-theoretic applications.  
- Weakness: numerical experiments are limited to R^3 simplex problems and may not generalize to higher-dimensional or complex settings.  
- Weakness: computational cost of the adaptive averaging heuristic relative to simpler restarts is not fully explored.  
- Weakness: adaptive averaging is a refinement of existing ideas and the paper doesn’t explore non-quadratic rates beyond r(t)=t^2.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution with solid empirical support; recommendation to accept.  
- Pros for acceptance: unified theoretical framework, novel adaptive heuristic, rigorous proofs, compelling experiments, clear relation to prior work.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited experimental scope, unclear computational overhead, and less explicit translation to practical discrete algorithms.
