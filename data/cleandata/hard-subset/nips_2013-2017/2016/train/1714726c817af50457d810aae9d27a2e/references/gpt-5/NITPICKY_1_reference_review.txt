Summary
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with generalized averaging. The authors define a family of ODEs AMDw,η parameterized by two positive weight functions: w(t) for averaging and η(t) for gradient accumulation. Using the Lyapunov candidate Lr(X,Z,t)=r(t)(f(X)−f*)+Dψ*(Z,z*), they give sufficient conditions under which Lr is decreasing, yielding a convergence guarantee f(X(t))−f* ≤ const/r(t). A second “energy” Er(t)=f(X(t))+(1/r(t))Dψ*(Z,X̌) is also shown to decrease under the same condition. The paper provides a primal form of the dynamics, connects the construction to Wibisono et al.’s variational flows, and instantiates the dynamics for the positive orthant (entropy geometry) and the simplex, where an “accelerated” replicator dynamics emerges. Finally, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that selects a(t)=w/W online to further decrease d/dt Lr and present discrete-time experiments comparing to gradient- and speed-restart heuristics.

Relation to prior work
This builds on: (i) Su, Boyd, Candès (NIPS’16) ODE for Nesterov; (ii) Krichene et al. (NIPS’15) continuous/discrete AMD with averaging; (iii) Wibisono et al. variational perspective; (iv) restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès; Su et al.); and adaptive gradient methods (Adagrad, Adam). The paper’s main novelty is the generalization to arbitrary weight schedules with a Lyapunov analysis and an adaptive scheme that preserves the continuous-time rate.

Strengths
- Clear, unified Lyapunov framework: simple sufficient conditions (η≥r′ and sign condition involving r−ηW/w) guarantee a target rate 1/r(t).
- Generalized averaging perspective that recovers several known cases (e.g., quadratic rate with polynomial weights) and connects to Wibisono et al.’s flows.
- Insightful primal formulations for common geometries; the accelerated replicator dynamics is interesting and likely of independent interest.
- The adaptive averaging idea is natural (choosing a(t) to reduce d/dt Lr) and can preserve the rate in continuous time; empirical results suggest practical benefits over restarts in some settings.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and consistency:
- Major inconsistency in Examples 1–2. The corollary requires a(t)=w/W ≥ r′/r. For r(t)=t^2, r′/r=2/t, so one needs a(t)=β/t. The text incorrectly states a(t)=β t (with dimensionally incorrect choice), yet derives w(t)∝(t/t0)^{β−1}, which corresponds to a(t)=β/t. This is an important error that propagates to Example 2.
- Misstatement around Corollary 1 and the adaptive rule: to zero out the term (r−ηW/w) one needs η=a r, i.e., a=η/r. The manuscript says “we simply set a(t)=η(t)r(t)” (Section 4), which is the inverse. This also affects Eq. (8), which proposes thresholds based on “η(t)r(t)” instead of η(t)/r(t).
- Existence and uniqueness (Theorem 1) rely on Lipschitzness of ∇ψ*. However, key examples (e.g., ψ*(z)=∑ e^{zi}−1 on R^n for the positive orthant) are not globally Lipschitz. The proof claims global C^1 solutions on [t0,∞), but assumes globally Lipschitz right-hand sides or bounded coefficients; neither is verified for unbounded feasible sets (R^n_+) and non-Lipschitz ∇ψ*. Local existence is standard; global existence requires additional growth bounds or bounded X. This needs careful revision or stronger assumptions (e.g., bounded X, or coercivity ensuring bounded trajectories).
- The use of the symbol r is overloaded: earlier r≥2 is a fixed parameter in prior AMD, later r(t) is the target-rate function. This is confusing and should be disentangled.

Discrete-time algorithm and experiments:
- Algorithm 1 is not implementable as written. There are garbled/missing terms: λk formula is incomplete (“λk = √s ak + √s ak”), the update “ak = min(ak−1, βmax k √s)” is undefined (what is “max k”?), the gradient evaluation point x(k) is not clearly defined initially, and steps 3–5 mix Dψ and a separate regularizer R without a clear rationale. The mapping from the continuous-time adaptive rule to the discrete parameters is relegated to the supplement; without a correct, reproducible algorithm in the main text, the experimental claims are hard to assess.
- Claims that the discrete scheme preserves rates are deferred to the supplement, but given the notational errors above (a vs η/r), it is unclear if the discrete Lyapunov proof is consistent.
- Experiments are small-scale (R^3) and mostly qualitative; details (step sizes, β, ψ/R choices, number of restarts) are insufficient for reproducibility. It is unclear whether the improvements persist in higher dimensions or under noise/stochastic gradients.

Clarity and presentation:
- Several equations and references have formatting or content errors (see Typos below). The manuscript would benefit from a thorough pass to fix notation and ensure all conditions are dimensionally and conceptually correct.
- The dependence of constants in the rate (e.g., Lr(X(t0),Z(t0),t0)) is not discussed; some guidance on choosing r(t) beyond t^2 and on tradeoffs between w, η, r would be helpful.

Significance and originality
Moderate. The general Lyapunov condition and adaptive averaging idea are interesting and build a bridge between acceleration and averaging. However, similar continuous-time Lyapunov analyses exist (Su et al.; Wibisono et al.), and the discrete-time contribution—the part most relevant to practice—is currently too underspecified and error-prone to be impactful. The novelty over Krichene et al. (2015) is incremental unless the adaptive method is made precise and theoretically supported in discrete time.

Questions for the authors
1) Please correct the definitions of a(t) in Examples 1–2 and Section 4; specify clearly whether a=η/r or a=η r and fix all downstream formulas.
2) Under what assumptions on ψ* (global vs local Lipschitz, bounded X) does Theorem 1 hold? How do you handle the entropy geometry on R^n_+ where ∇ψ* is not Lipschitz?
3) Provide a complete, unambiguous description of Algorithm 1 (all parameters, λk, ak update), and a self-contained discrete Lyapunov proof in the main text.
4) Can you quantify the improvement due to adaptive averaging on higher-dimensional problems and with stochastic gradients?
5) How sensitive is the heuristic to the choice of β and step size s?

Typos and formatting (non-exhaustive)
- “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems” missing the number.
- Overloaded r: parameter r≥2 vs function r(t); rename one.
- Eq. (4) derivation: fine, but the choice a(t)=β/t must be stated.
- Section 4: “In Corollary 1, we simply set a(t)=η(t)r(t)” should be a(t)=η(t)/r(t).
- Eq. (8): thresholds should use η(t)/r(t), not η(t)r(t).
- Algorithm 1: λk formula incomplete; “βmax k √s” undefined; inconsistent use of ψ vs ψ*; missing definition of x(k) at k=0.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (missing years/venues/pages; capitalization; some CoRR entries; “Optimization and dynamical systems” vs “Ordinary differential equations…”). Please normalize to NeurIPS style.

Pros
- Conceptually clean continuous-time framework; connections to prior ODE-based acceleration.
- Interesting accelerated replicator dynamics.
- Adaptive averaging is a promising idea with a sound continuous-time rationale.

Cons
- Multiple technical inconsistencies/typos affecting core formulas.
- Insufficiently specified discrete algorithm; reproducibility concerns.
- Existence/uniqueness proof assumptions not aligned with provided examples.
- Limited and small-scale experiments.

Recommendation
Reject. While the main ideas are interesting, the manuscript contains too many technical and presentation issues to be accepted in its current form. With careful correction of the core inconsistencies, a precise and analyzable discrete algorithm, and stronger empirical validation, the paper could become a solid contribution.

Confidence
High (familiar with ODE-based views of accelerated methods and mirror descent).
