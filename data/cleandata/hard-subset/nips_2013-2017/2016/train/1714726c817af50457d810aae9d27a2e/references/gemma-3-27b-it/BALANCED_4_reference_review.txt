This paper investigates accelerated mirror descent dynamics, introducing a generalized averaging scheme parameterized by two weight functions. The authors provide sufficient conditions for convergence, demonstrate an equivalent primal representation, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to preserve the convergence rate. The work builds upon the connection between Nesterov’s accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs, a growing area of research highlighted by references to Su et al. [20], Krichene et al. [11], and others. The investigation into replicator dynamics as a special case is a particularly interesting application.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed dynamics, including conditions for convergence based on Lyapunov function arguments. The proof of existence and uniqueness is also well-handled.
* **Generality:** The framework is quite general, allowing for different averaging schemes and applicable to constrained optimization problems via mirror descent.
* **Practical Heuristic:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is a valuable contribution, aiming to improve convergence without sacrificing the guaranteed rate. The provided discretization for implementation is a plus.
* **Connections to Existing Literature:** The paper effectively connects to relevant literature in optimization, dynamical systems, and game theory, demonstrating awareness of prior work. The link to replicator dynamics is nicely explored.
* **Numerical Validation:** The numerical experiments provide some support for the proposed heuristic, showing its competitive performance compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Numerical Scope:** The numerical experiments, while present, feel somewhat limited. Focusing primarily on the simplex-constrained case, and only testing a few objective functions, doesn't provide a comprehensive assessment of the heuristic’s performance across different problem settings. More diverse examples (e.g., different constraint sets, higher dimensions, different conditioning) would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Analysis:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, it’s also quite dense and could be more accessible. Some steps could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The reliance on a Lyapunov function, while standard, might be a barrier to entry for readers not deeply familiar with dynamical systems.
* **Heuristic’s Practicality:** The adaptive averaging heuristic, while theoretically sound, introduces additional parameters and computations in the discrete implementation (Algorithm 1). The overhead introduced by adaptively computing `ak` is not discussed, and it’s unclear if it outweighs the potential gains in convergence speed in practice. A discussion of the computational cost of the heuristic would be valuable.
* **Limited Discussion of Related Adaptive Methods:** The paper mentions Adagrad and Adam [7, 10] briefly, but doesn’t fully explore how they relate to the proposed adaptive averaging scheme, or why this approach might be more suited for accelerated methods. A deeper comparison would strengthen the paper.
* **Lack of Discussion on Initialization:** The initialization of the ODE at t0 > 0 is mentioned as crucial for existence and uniqueness, but a more detailed discussion of the impact of t0 on convergence behavior would be helpful.  Is there an optimal choice for t0?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated mirror descent dynamics.
* The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic offers a potentially valuable improvement to existing methods.
* The work is well-written (despite some density) and demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited scope of the numerical experiments weakens the empirical validation of the proposed heuristic.
* The complexity of the theoretical analysis may limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
* The practical benefits of the adaptive averaging heuristic (compared to its computational cost) remain unclear.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the adaptive averaging heuristic is a promising avenue for further research. However, the limited scope of the numerical experiments and the potential computational overhead of the heuristic raise some concerns.  I believe the paper, as it stands, is borderline.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit.**

I recommend rejection primarily due to the limited empirical evaluation.  The authors should significantly expand the numerical experiments to include a wider range of problem settings and demonstrate a clear practical advantage of their adaptive averaging heuristic.  A discussion of the computational cost of the heuristic and a more thorough comparison to existing adaptive methods would also strengthen the paper. Addressing these weaknesses would significantly improve the quality of the submission and increase its potential impact. The theoretical contribution is strong enough to warrant reconsideration after revision.
