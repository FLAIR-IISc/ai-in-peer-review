- Paper generalizes accelerated mirror descent (AMD) continuous-time dynamics using weight functions w and η and analyzes convergence via a Lyapunov function.
- Demonstrates that different averaging schemes can achieve quadratic convergence and introduces an adaptive heuristic for w(t) that provably preserves convergence rate.
- Provides primal representations of the dynamics and applies them to constrained sets, deriving an accelerated replicator dynamic on the simplex.
- Numerical experiments show the adaptive averaging heuristic is competitive with existing restarting heuristics and visualize trajectories and Lyapunov/Energy behavior.
- Builds on Su et al. (2016) and Krichene et al. (2016), situating the work in the continuous-time analysis literature and relating to adaptive methods like Adagrad/Adam while emphasizing differences.
- Strength: rigorous generalization of AMD dynamics with clear convergence conditions via Lyapunov analysis.
- Strength: novel adaptive averaging heuristic with provable rate-preservation and intuitive Lyapunov-based motivation.
- Strength: useful primal derivations and a noteworthy accelerated replicator dynamic application.
- Strength: solid experimental validation and informative visualizations.
- Strength: clear writing and logical organization aiding readability of technical details.
- Weakness: experiments are limited to low-dimensional problems (R^3), leaving scalability to high dimensions untested.
- Weakness: theoretical analysis is dense and may be inaccessible to readers without strong convex analysis/ODE background.
- Weakness: discussion of the choice and implications of the rate function r(t) is limited and could be expanded beyond examples like r(t)=t^2.
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to adaptive learning-rate methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) and potential combinations with adaptive averaging are not explored.
- Weakness: discretization procedure (section 4) is described abruptly and lacks detail on its impact on convergence guarantees.
- Argument for acceptance: significant theoretical advance in continuous-time accelerated optimization and a provably effective adaptive heuristic supported by analysis and experiments.
- Argument against acceptance: limited experimental scope and potential accessibility issues due to theoretical complexity.
- Overall assessment: strong paper with noteworthy theoretical and practical contributions; minor weaknesses do not outweigh merits.
- Recommendation: Accept, with minor revisions to expand experiments and clarify theoretical exposition.
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
