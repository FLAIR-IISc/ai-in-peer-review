Summary and contributions
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for convex optimization and introduces a generalized Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) family with two time-varying weights: w(t) controlling averaging and η(t) controlling gradient accumulation. The central technical device is a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*) parameterized by a target rate r(t). The authors provide simple sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) under which Lr is decreasing along trajectories and f(X(t)) − f* ≤ const/r(t). A key sufficient condition is η(t) ≥ r′(t) and r(t) = η(t)W(t)/w(t), with W(t) = ∫0^t w(τ)dτ; equivalently, choosing a(t) = w/W ≥ r′/r and η = a r cancels a trajectory-dependent term. The paper also presents an “energy” function Er(t) = f(X(t)) + (1/r(t))Dψ*(Z,X̌) that decreases under similar conditions, gives primal-space representations (including an accelerated replicator ODE on the simplex), and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) to decrease the Lyapunov derivative. A discrete algorithm is provided (Algorithm 1) and experiments on simplex-constrained problems compare adaptive averaging against gradient and speed restarts.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Su-Boyd-Candès (inertial ODE for Nesterov), Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett (accelerated mirror descent in continuous/discrete time), and Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan (Bregman Lagrangian and general ODE framework). It also connects to inertial dynamics with vanishing damping (Attouch et al.) and to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory. The paper appropriately references these, and positions its contribution as a unifying Lyapunov view for generalized averaging and a new adaptive averaging mechanism that retains the target rate in continuous time. The observation that rates faster than 1/t^2 are available in continuous time but require higher-order discretizations (as in [23]) is correctly noted.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The a(t) = w/W reparameterization and the Lyapunov derivative computation cleanly isolate the role of averaging vs gradient accumulation. The conditions in Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 are simple and interpretable.
- Generality: The framework covers mirror descent geometries, constrained sets, and produces concrete examples (positive orthant, simplex), including an “accelerated replicator” ODE.
- Energy interpretation: The Er function provides additional intuition about potential/kinetic tradeoffs and ties to physical analogies common in ODE analyses.
- Adaptive heuristic: The proposed rule targets directly the sign of the Lyapunov derivative term, giving a plausible mechanism to reduce oscillations while preserving the continuous-time rate.
- Clarity of continuous-time analysis: Existence/uniqueness, feasibility preservation, and the careful initialization at t0 > 0 are well discussed.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Trajectory-dependent condition and practical checkability: The sufficient condition in Theorem 2 involves ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w) ≤ 0. While the corollary eliminates it by equality, the motivation for adaptivity is to make the term negative; however, the practical discrete check used in Algorithm 1 (f(x̃(k+1)) ≤ f(x̃(k))) is only a surrogate for the sign of ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩. The theoretical link between the discrete test and the continuous Lyapunov decrease is deferred to the supplement; the main text does not provide a discrete-time Lyapunov guarantee. This weakens the main algorithmic claim.
- Discretization gaps and guarantees: For the r(t) = t^2 case, a convergence bound for Algorithm 1 is said to be in the supplement, but the main paper provides no theorem, no step-size conditions in terms of L, and no explicit constants. For p > 2, the text acknowledges that provably preserving rates requires higher-order methods (per [23]). Overall, the discrete-time story—the piece most relevant to NIPS practitioners—is underdeveloped.
- Smoothness/Lipschitz assumptions: The existence/uniqueness proof assumes Lipschitz ∇f and Lipschitz ∇ψ*. For common mirror maps (e.g., entropy/softmax), global Lipschitzness depends on the choice of norms; the paper should state the norms and constants explicitly and clarify whether the Lipschitz property is global on E* or only on bounded sets visited by the trajectory.
- Use of unknown x*: The Lyapunov Lr depends on z* with ∇ψ*(z*) = x*, which is unknown. While this is fine for analysis, it complicates the empirical reporting of Lr (what x* is used when the minimizer set is non-unique?). The paper mentions this subtlety but should standardize reporting.
- Experimental scope: Experiments are small (R^3 simplex) with entropy mirror map. It is unclear how the method performs on higher-dimensional, large-scale problems (e.g., logistic regression with simplex constraints; portfolio optimization) or in Euclidean settings. Hyperparameter selection (β, s, γ, regularizer R) and fair tuning for baselines (restart thresholds) are not detailed. The evidence that adaptive averaging consistently outperforms restarts is therefore limited.
- Computational aspects: Algorithm 1 requires two proximal-like steps per iteration (w.r.t. Dψ and R). The cost and choice of R are not discussed; practical guidance on how to pick R and its impact on performance is missing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Move a discrete-time convergence theorem for Algorithm 1 (assumptions, step-size bounds, rates, and constants) from the supplement into the main text. In particular, clarify under what conditions the discrete Lyapunov function decreases.
- Replace the f(x̃(k+1)) − f(x̃(k)) test with a more faithful discrete analogue of the inner-product test ⟨∇f(x(k)), x(k+1) − x(k)⟩ when possible, and analyze its effect on the discrete Lyapunov.
- State explicitly the norm in which ∇f and ∇ψ* are Lipschitz and provide the constants; clarify whether trajectories remain in regions where these constants are controlled.
- Provide experiments in higher dimensions and with different geometries (Euclidean, ℓ1-ball, spectrahedra), including real datasets. Report sensitivity to β, s, and to the initialization. Ensure fair and tuned baselines for restarts.
- Discuss applicability to strongly convex f. Your framework allows choosing r(t) with exponential growth (e.g., r ∝ e^{μ t}) by taking a(t) ≥ μ; it would be valuable to work out the continuous-time guarantees and a practical discrete algorithm with provable linear rate.
- Clarify computational cost of Algorithm 1, including choices for R and whether the two proximal steps can be merged or simplified in standard settings.

Pros
- Clean and general Lyapunov framework unifying averaging schedules and rates.
- Novel and appealing adaptive averaging idea targeted at Lyapunov decrease.
- Interesting accelerated replicator dynamics and clear primal/dual interpretations.
- Well-situated within recent ODE-based acceleration literature.

Cons
- Main algorithmic claims for the discrete method lack in-text guarantees.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks scale and tuning details.
- Some assumptions (Lipschitzness) and practical checks (inner-product surrogate) need clarification.
- The dependence on unknown x* in Lr complicates empirical interpretation.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The continuous-time theory is sound, clear, and potentially influential, and the adaptive averaging idea is interesting and plausibly useful. However, strengthening the discrete-time analysis and expanding the experiments would materially improve the paper’s impact and readiness for a broad NIPS audience.
