This paper investigates accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics, proposing a generalized averaging scheme and an adaptive heuristic for computing averaging weights. The authors establish conditions for convergence and demonstrate, through both theoretical analysis and numerical experiments, that their adaptive averaging approach can outperform existing restarting heuristics. The work builds upon a growing body of research connecting Nesterov’s acceleration to continuous-time ODEs, providing a deeper understanding of the interplay between averaging and acceleration in optimization.

The paper is well-structured and presents a clear mathematical formulation of the AMD dynamics. The theoretical results, particularly Theorems 2 and 3, are valuable contributions to the understanding of these dynamics, offering sufficient conditions for Lyapunov function decrease and convergence rate preservation. The primal representation derived in Section 3 is a useful addition and the application to the simplex-constrained dynamics (replicator equation) is insightful. The inclusion of numerical experiments strengthens the paper’s claims, demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic.

However, despite the novel contributions, I find several shortcomings that significantly detract from the overall quality of the paper. My primary concern is the meticulousness of the mathematical treatment and the lack of rigorous justification for several claims.

1. **Notational Inconsistency & Clarity:** The notation is inconsistent. For example, X(t) is sometimes used to represent the primal variable trajectory and other times the weighted average. The definition of W(t) is also slightly confusing and could be clarified. The prime notation (’) for derivatives is used inconsistently.

2. **Theorem 2's Condition 2:** The condition 〈 ∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t) 〉( r(t)− η(t)W (t)w(t) ) ≤ 0 in Theorem 2 is presented as sufficient but its practical verifiability is questionable. It depends directly on the solution trajectory X(t), making it difficult to assess *a priori*. While the authors provide a specific case in Corollary 1 where the condition is trivially met, the general applicability of Theorem 2 is limited without a clearer understanding of how to guarantee this condition. The authors acknowledge this but do not offer further discussion on alternative ways to ensure it holds.

3. **Energy Function Analysis (Theorem 3):** While Theorem 3 demonstrates a decreasing energy function, its interpretation feels somewhat tacked on and lacks a strong connection to the convergence rate. The claim that the energy function represents potential and kinetic energy is a loose analogy that requires further justification, especially considering the non-Euclidean geometry induced by the mirror map. The discussion feels superficial.

4. **Algorithm 1 & Discretization:** The description of the discrete adaptive averaging heuristic (Algorithm 1) lacks sufficient detail regarding the parameters and the practical implementation. How is 's' chosen? What is the optimal selection of beta? The authors claim the discretization preserves the rate, but provide a reference to supplementary material without detailing the proof within the main body. This feels incomplete.

5. **Numerical Experiments - Limited Scope:** The numerical experiments, while providing some validation, are limited in scope. The parameter settings are not fully justified and a more extensive evaluation on different objective functions and constraint sets would be beneficial. The visualization of trajectories lacks detailed explanation, and the quantitative comparison with other methods could be more robust (e.g., with confidence intervals).

6. **Related Work:** The paper mentions related work, but lacks a critical assessment of how the proposed approach differs from existing adaptive methods like Adagrad or Adam, beyond simply stating they are "not designed for accelerated methods." A deeper comparison of the algorithmic mechanisms and theoretical properties would strengthen the contribution. Specifically, the connection of the gradient restart heuristic [17] to the current work isn’t well-explored.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel generalization of AMD dynamics.
*   Theoretical guarantees on convergence rate under specific conditions.
*   Insightful connection to replicator dynamics.
*   Empirical evidence of performance improvement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity and consistency in notation.
*   Impractical verifiability of key conditions in Theorem 2.
*   Weak justification for the energy function analysis.
*   Insufficient detail regarding the discretization and algorithm.
*   Limited scope of numerical experiments.
*   Inadequate comparison to existing adaptive methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents interesting ideas and a solid theoretical foundation, the numerous minor flaws, the lack of practical guidance for verifying key conditions, and the limited scope of the experimental validation raise significant concerns. The paper feels somewhat incomplete and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS publication. I recommend **rejection** at this time. I believe a significant revision addressing the points raised above would be necessary for reconsideration. Specifically, the authors need to address the issues regarding the practical verifiability of Theorem 2, expand the numerical experiments, and provide a more rigorous comparison to related work.
