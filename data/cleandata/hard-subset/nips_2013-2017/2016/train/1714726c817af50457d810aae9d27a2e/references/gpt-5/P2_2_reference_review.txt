Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated first-order methods via continuous-time dynamics, building on the mirror-descent/accelerated ODE viewpoint. It introduces a broad family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two positive weight functions w(t) (for averaging) and η(t) (for gradient accumulation), together with a target rate function r(t). A Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) is constructed, and the paper gives sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) under which Lr is nonincreasing, yielding a convergence guarantee f(X(t))−f* ≤ const./r(t). This unifies known accelerated ODEs (e.g., Su-Boyd-Candès; Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett) and the variational/Bregman Lagrangian perspective (Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan) via a simple “averaging and accumulation” parameterization. The paper also:
- Derives a primal representation of the AMD dynamics, including explicit forms for positive-orthant and simplex constraints, yielding an “accelerated replicator” ODE.
- Introduces a second “energy” function Er that is also nonincreasing under the same conditions, giving a potential/kinetic-energy interpretation.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t)=w/W online to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative while preserving the rate in continuous time; a discrete algorithm is proposed for r(t)=t^2 and compared empirically to restart heuristics (gradient and speed restart).

The work connects closely to prior results on accelerated ODEs (Su et al.), accelerated mirror descent (Krichene et al.), restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès; Su et al.), inertial systems (Attouch et al.), and the Bregman Lagrangian view (Wibisono et al.), citing these appropriately and situating the contribution as a generalization with a new adaptive averaging mechanism.

Quality
- Theory: The Lyapunov analysis is clear and technically sound. The key inequality isolating the two terms in d/dt Lr (a rate term and an averaging-induced term) is illuminating and yields simple sufficient conditions. The constructive choice of w via a(t)=w/W ≥ r′/r and η = a r is neat and recovers known accelerated schedules (e.g., r=t^p gives polynomial weights). The existence/uniqueness result is standard but complete.
- Novelty in analysis includes: a unified energy/Lyapunov perspective for generalized averaging; an accelerated replicator dynamics derivation; and an adaptive rule that guarantees Lyapunov decrease and preserves the target rate in continuous time.
- Discrete-time: The paper sketches a discretization for r(t)=t^2 and claims a rate bound in the supplement via a discrete Lyapunov. In the main text, this is not fully detailed. The discrete heuristic (adapting ak based on function decrease) is plausible and computationally simple, but a clear, self-contained convergence guarantee and practical guidance on parameters (β, s, regularizer R) would strengthen the paper.
- Experiments: The comparisons to gradient and speed restarts on simplex-constrained problems are thoughtful, visualize trajectories, and report Lyapunov and energy quantities. However, most shown results are low-dimensional (R^3), and it’s unclear how broadly the empirical benefits carry to standard high-dimensional benchmarks (logistic regression, lasso, constrained softmax regression). Run-time/iteration tradeoffs and sensitivity to β and step-size are not explored in the main text.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. The continuous-time argument and conditions are easy to follow. The connection to prior ODEs is clearly explained. The primal forms for common constraints are helpful and pedagogical.
- Some aspects could be clearer:
  - Condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on the trajectory and is hard to verify a priori; the paper addresses this via Corollary 1 and the adaptive rule—highlighting this design tradeoff earlier would help.
  - The discrete algorithm references a regularizer R (strongly convex, smooth) and the specific Bregman distances (Dψ, R) but gives limited guidance on concrete choices and parameter tuning in practice.
  - References have missing bibliographic details; tightening them up would aid readers.

Originality
- The generalized averaging formulation of AMD with explicit Lyapunov conditions and constructive schedules is a nice unification/generalization. The accelerated replicator dynamics and the adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves continuous-time rates are, to my knowledge, novel contributions beyond prior restart heuristics and the Bregman Lagrangian literature.
- The second energy function and its monotonicity give an appealing physical interpretation that has not been emphasized in earlier AMD work.

Significance
- Conceptually, the work sharpens our understanding of how averaging schedules interact with acceleration, offering a clean recipe for designing accelerated ODEs across mirror maps and constraints.
- Practically, the adaptive averaging heuristic is simple to implement and compares favorably to common restart heuristics in the presented tasks, with promise for broader adoption.
- The significance would be higher with stronger discrete-time guarantees and more extensive empirical validation in higher dimensions and on standard ML tasks.

Strengths
- Clear, unified continuous-time analysis with simple, verifiable (or enforceable) conditions.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with principled Lyapunov grounding; preserves the target rate in continuous time.
- New accelerated replicator dynamics and energy interpretation.
- Consistent empirical improvements over restarts in shown examples and good diagnostics (Lyapunov, energy).

Weaknesses
- Discrete-time theory and practical guidance are limited in the main text; key proofs in the supplement.
- Experiments are mostly low-dimensional/simplex; limited evidence on scalability and competitiveness on standard benchmarks.
- Sensitivity to parameters (β, step-size, choice of R) and computational cost per iteration vs restarts are not investigated.
- Condition (2) of Theorem 2 is trajectory-dependent; while the adaptive rule addresses it, the gap between continuous- and discrete-time guarantees should be discussed more explicitly.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a concise discrete-time theorem in the main paper with assumptions, constants, and a proof sketch.
- Provide guidance and ablations on choosing β, step-size, and R; report sensitivity analyses.
- Add higher-dimensional experiments on standard convex problems (e.g., L1/L2-regularized logistic regression) and compare wall-clock as well as iterations.
- Clarify how to compute or approximate the inner-product condition in practice and its discrete proxy.
- Improve bibliography completeness and formatting.

Overall assessment
- Quality: good
- Clarity: good
- Originality: good to very good
- Significance: moderate to good

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and elegant theoretical contribution that unifies and extends accelerated ODEs.
- Simple, principled adaptive heuristic with promising empirical behavior.
- Useful connections (replicator dynamics, energy interpretation) that broaden impact.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited discrete-time analysis in the main text and modest experimental scope; practical impact not yet fully demonstrated at scale.

Recommendation: lean accept. Confidence: medium.
