Summary:
- Introduces a two-parameter family of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent ODEs with weights w(t) and η(t) and analyzes them via a Lyapunov Lr to get f(X(t))−f* = O(1/r(t)).
- Shows a second energy Er is non-increasing under the same condition, gives a primal representation (including accelerated replicator on the simplex), proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic, and sketches a discrete algorithm with small-scale experiments.

Relation to prior work:
- Recovers classical t^2 Nesterov rate and generalizes Su–Boyd–Candès and Krichene et al. via arbitrary r(t) and weight schedules.
- Closely related to Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan’s Bregman Lagrangian; main conditions essentially match their ideal-scaling but presented via averaging/dual-primal coupling.
- Positions adaptive averaging as a principled alternative to restart heuristics.

Strengths:
- Clean, unified Lyapunov analysis with simple, checkable sufficient conditions for accelerated rates.
- The Er energy gives an intuitive kinetic/potential interpretation that may be independently useful.
- Careful handling of existence/uniqueness and feasibility with t0>0 and Lipschitz assumptions.
- Accelerated replicator dynamics is a nice conceptual tie between optimization and evolutionary dynamics.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic is simple, Lyapunov-grounded, and provably preserves rates in continuous time.
- Experiments suggest reduced oscillations and improved performance versus restarts with informative Lyapunov/energy plots.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is moderate relative to the Bregman Lagrangian framework; averaging may be largely a reparameterization.
- Theorem 2’s key sufficient condition is trajectory-dependent and not verifiable a priori; corollary restricts adaptivity to an equality choice.
- Discrete adaptive rule does not directly test the continuous inner product condition and theoretical discrete guarantees are deferred to the supplement.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to small deterministic R^3 simplex problems; lacks large-scale, stochastic, or runtime comparisons and comparisons to adaptive optimizers.
- Some technical assumptions are glossed over (non-smooth ψ with indicator, differentiability in Er analysis) and need clarification.
- Clarity issues and typos (e.g., a(t) miswritten), casual handling of scale constants, and incomplete bibliographic details.

Questions for the authors:
- Can you precisely relate your conditions to Wibisono et al.’s ideal-scaling and show if your framework yields genuinely new schedules or Lyapunovs?
- For entropy + indicator on the simplex, under exactly what ψ assumptions does Er decrease; can results be extended using Bregman divergences/subgradients?
- What discrete-time assumptions and step-size rules ensure the adaptive averaging rate; include a main theorem and proof sketch for smooth convex f and standard ψ.
- How sensitive is performance to hyperparameters (β, s), is there automatic tuning, and any guarantees under stochastic gradients?
- Can the heuristic give improved rates for strongly convex f (e.g., linear convergence) and do experiments support this?

Suggestions for improvement:
- State a concise discrete-time theorem for Algorithm 1 in the main text with assumptions, rates, and dependence on initial Bregman divergence.
- Expand the discussion contrasting generalized averaging to the Bregman Lagrangian, emphasizing genuinely new contributions (e.g., adaptive averaging).
- Fix a(t) typos, clarify Lipschitz assumptions on ∇ψ*, and make w(t0)/scale constants explicit.
- Add higher-dimensional and stochastic experiments, include tuning and runtime details, and compare to monotone adaptive restarts and adaptive optimizers.

Overall evaluation:
- Quality: solid continuous-time analysis but discrete-time guarantees need clearer presentation.
- Clarity: generally good but marred by typos and missing technical details.
- Originality: modest—averaging perspective and adaptive heuristic are interesting but closely related to prior work.
- Significance: potential practical impact if discrete guarantees and stronger experiments are added.
- Recommendation: Borderline accept (weak accept if discrete-time guarantees and typos are fixed).
