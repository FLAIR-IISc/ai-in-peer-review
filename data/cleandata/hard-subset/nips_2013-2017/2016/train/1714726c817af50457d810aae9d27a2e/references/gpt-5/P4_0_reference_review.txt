Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies first-order continuous-time dynamics for minimizing a smooth convex function over a convex closed set X. Building on the accelerated mirror descent (AMD) view that couples a dual variable Z with a primal average X, the authors generalize the averaging mechanism by introducing time-varying weights w(t) (for averaging) and η(t) (for gradient accumulation). They analyze the resulting family of ODEs, AMDw,η, via a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), where r(t) encodes a target convergence rate. They provide sufficient conditions under which Lr is nonincreasing and f(X(t)) − f* ≤ const/r(t). They also present a companion “energy” function Er that decreases under the same conditions.

The work connects to: (i) Su, Boyd, and Candès’ interpretation of Nesterov’s acceleration as a second-order ODE; (ii) the authors’ prior work on continuous/discrete accelerated mirror descent using averaging; (iii) the Bregman Lagrangian/variational framework of Wibisono et al., which also yields general families of accelerated ODEs with target rates; (iv) restart heuristics such as gradient restart and speed restart; and (v) adaptive first-order methods (AdaGrad/Adam) in spirit, though not designed for acceleration.

Beyond analysis, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic: choose a(t) = w(t)/W(t) to decrease the time derivative of Lr when possible, subject to η(t) ≥ r′(t), thereby preserving the 1/r(t) rate. A discrete algorithm for r(t)=t^2 is given (Algorithm 1), with a discrete Lyapunov-based rate guarantee deferred to the supplement. The paper derives a primal representation of the dynamics, exhibits examples for the positive orthant (entropy mirror map) and the simplex, and identifies an accelerated replicator dynamics variant. Experiments on simplex-constrained problems in R^3 compare adaptive averaging with restart heuristics, showing competitive or improved performance and monotonic Lyapunov decrease.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The continuous-time analysis is sound and standard in style: Lipschitz conditions imply well-posedness; the Lyapunov derivative bound is derived using convexity and mirror map properties; sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) and a constructive corollary give a recipe to achieve a desired r(t). The construction via a(t)=w/W and η= a r is clean and consistent.
- The Er energy function is well-motivated and its monotonicity follows from the same condition as in Theorem 2.
- The primal formulations and examples are correct and helpful; the replicator dynamics specialization is derived correctly.
- The main caveat is that the key condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on the trajectory; while the corollary removes this by choosing η = a r and a ≥ r′/r, the price is that the sufficient condition becomes somewhat tautological (fixed by construction), and the design space becomes narrower.
- Discrete-time guarantees for Algorithm 1 are not in the main text (claimed in the supplement), and the algorithmic specification contains parameters (e.g., γ, βmax, R) that are not fully defined in the main body.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear, with a consistent notation for primal/dual maps and averaged trajectories, and the examples aid intuition.
- There appear to be typographical issues that should be fixed for precision: in Corollary 1 and surrounding text, the intended relation is η(t) = a(t) r(t) with a(t) = w(t)/W(t) (equivalently, η(t) = r(t) w(t)/W(t)); as written, the division sometimes appears as multiplication by W(t), which would contradict the derivation. Also, some minor language issues and undefined symbols in Algorithm 1 (e.g., γ, βmax, R) should be clarified. Reference entries are incomplete.
- The discussion of initializing at t0>0 is appropriate, but the dependence on W(t0), w(t0) could be stated more explicitly.

Originality
- The Lyapunov-based unification of generalized averaging with target rate r(t) is a useful and mostly incremental extension of prior AMD analyses. The adaptive averaging heuristic—choosing a(t) to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative while preserving the rate—is a novel and simple idea with a clear theoretical guarantee in continuous time.
- The primal accelerated replicator dynamics is a nice, concrete instance, likely new to the NeurIPS audience.
- Relative to the variational perspective of Wibisono et al., the contribution is complementary: it provides a different construction (via averaging and Lyapunov functions) leading to similar families of accelerated dynamics and rates, and an adaptive mechanism tied to the Lyapunov decrease.

Significance
- On the theory side, the framework gives a simple recipe to achieve a prescribed rate in continuous time and clarifies the role of averaging weights in acceleration. This is conceptually valuable and may inform algorithm design.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic could be practically useful as an alternative to restart schemes; the empirical results are promising but limited to low-dimensional simplex problems.
- The discrete-time impact would be stronger with a fully specified algorithm, complete convergence guarantees in the main text, and larger-scale experiments.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, unified continuous-time analysis with constructive sufficient conditions for achieving target rates via generalized averaging.
- Simple, principled adaptive averaging heuristic with guaranteed Lyapunov decrease and preserved rate; connects naturally to practical tuning.
- Insightful primal formulations and concrete accelerated replicator dynamics example.
- Empirical evidence showing advantages over restart heuristics in several settings; Lyapunov monotonicity observed in practice.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Novelty over prior AMD and variational ODE work is moderate; main guarantees hinge on design choices that make the Lyapunov condition vacuous by construction.
- Discrete-time algorithm and guarantees are not fully presented in the main paper; experimental evaluation is small-scale and limited in scope.
- Some clarity/notation issues and incomplete definitions/references that should be addressed for rigor and reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify Corollary 1: confirm that η(t) = a(t) r(t) with a(t)=w(t)/W(t), ensuring r(t) − η(t) W(t)/w(t) = 0.
- In Algorithm 1, define γ, βmax, and the role of R versus ψ; specify assumptions (e.g., strong convexity/smoothness constants) and how the subproblems are solved.
- Can you include the discrete-time convergence theorem and its proof sketch in the main text for r(t)=t^2? What conditions on β, step size s, and regularizers are required?
- Does adaptive averaging yield provably faster rates under strong convexity without restart (e.g., linear rate), or is the benefit primarily empirical?
- How sensitive is the method to noise/stochastic gradients? Can the adaptive rule be made robust in stochastic settings?

Suggestions
- Fix typographical/notation issues; unify and fully define all symbols in the algorithm; complete references.
- Add comparisons to the Bregman Lagrangian approach, highlighting similarities/differences in assumptions and guarantees.
- Expand experiments to higher dimensions and additional constraint sets, include ablations of the adaptive rule, and report computational costs.
