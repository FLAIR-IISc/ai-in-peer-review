

# Peer Review: "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated convex optimization, extending the work of Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016) on Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics. The authors introduce a generalized family of AMD dynamics parameterized by two weight functions w and η, where w controls the averaging scheme and η governs the rate of gradient accumulation. The key contribution is a unified Lyapunov analysis that provides sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate 1/r(t), and an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts weights while preserving the convergence rate.

The paper builds on the seminal connection between continuous-time dynamics and accelerated methods established by Su et al. (NIPS 2014), and extends the NIPS 2016 work that linked Nesterov's accelerated method to a specific second-order ODE. The authors demonstrate how their framework generalizes previous results and provides a systematic approach to designing accelerated dynamics with different averaging schemes.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a clear and well-motivated Lyapunov analysis framework (Theorem 2) that establishes sufficient conditions for convergence. The constructive method for building weight functions (Corollary 1) is elegant and generalizable. The connection to replicator dynamics (Section 3) is particularly valuable, showing how accelerated optimization can be applied to evolutionary game theory.

2. **Novel Heuristic**: The adaptive averaging heuristic (Section 4) is well-justified and addresses a significant gap in the literature. Unlike existing restarting heuristics (which often lack theoretical guarantees for non-strongly convex problems), this approach preserves the convergence rate while improving practical performance.

3. **Experiments**: The numerical experiments (Section 5) are well-designed and demonstrate clear advantages of adaptive averaging across multiple problem classes (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, KL divergence). The visualizations of trajectories and energy functions provide valuable intuition.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Framework**: While the paper generalizes previous work, the theoretical foundation is largely based on the NIPS 2016 paper. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to clarify what's truly new beyond the previous work. The adaptive heuristic is the main novelty, but the theoretical framework itself is not groundbreaking.

2. **Lack of Discrete-Time Convergence Analysis**: The paper claims the adaptive heuristic "preserves the convergence rate" (Theorem 2), but the discrete-time implementation (Algorithm 1) lacks a formal convergence rate analysis. The supplementary material mentions a bound, but this omission is significant for a NIPS paper that should provide complete theoretical guarantees.

3. **Practical Implementation Concerns**: The heuristic relies on computing ⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩, which requires approximating the derivative Ẋ(t) in practice. The paper provides no guidance on efficient implementation, which is a practical limitation for adoption.

4. **Missing Comparison to Adaptive Methods**: While the paper mentions Adagrad and Adam, it doesn't compare their performance to adaptive averaging. Given that adaptive methods are common in optimization, this comparison would strengthen the paper's position.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theory of accelerated optimization, particularly with its adaptive averaging heuristic. The theoretical framework is sound, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach. However, the core theoretical framework is not sufficiently novel for NIPS, and the lack of a formal convergence rate analysis for the discrete-time adaptive algorithm is a significant weakness.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The adaptive averaging heuristic is promising and could be a useful tool for practitioners, and the theoretical foundation is solid. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more detailed comparison to the NIPS 2016 paper to clarify the novelty
2. Include a formal convergence rate analysis for the discrete-time adaptive algorithm in the main paper
3. Add a comparison to other adaptive methods (like Adagrad) in the experiments
4. Discuss practical considerations for implementing the adaptive heuristic

The paper's strengths in theoretical contribution and practical demonstration outweigh its weaknesses, especially with the suggested revisions. The adaptive averaging approach represents a meaningful improvement over existing heuristics, particularly for non-strongly convex problems where restarting heuristics often lack guarantees.

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Solid theoretical framework for accelerated dynamics
- Novel adaptive heuristic with practical benefits
- Well-designed experiments showing clear advantages
- Connection to replicator dynamics (significant application in game theory)

**Arguments for Rejection**:
- Limited novelty in core theoretical framework
- Lack of discrete-time convergence rate analysis
- Insufficient discussion of practical implementation
- Missing comparison to other adaptive optimization methods

The paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS with the suggested revisions, advancing our understanding of accelerated optimization through a novel adaptive heuristic that preserves theoretical guarantees while improving practical performance.
