### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a generalized family of continuous-time dynamics for accelerated convex optimization, termed Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD_w,η). This framework extends prior work that connected Nesterov's accelerated methods to second-order ODEs (e.g., Su et al., 2015; Krichene et al., 2015) by parameterizing the dynamics with two continuous weight functions: `w(t)` for averaging the primal trajectory and `η(t)` for controlling the rate of gradient accumulation. The central contribution is a unified analysis of this broad class of dynamics using a Lyapunov function `Lr`. The authors derive simple, sufficient conditions on `w(t)` and `η(t)` that guarantee a convergence rate of `f(X(t)) - f* <= C/r(t)` for a desired rate function `r(t)`.

Building on this theoretical framework, the paper develops a novel adaptive heuristic for choosing the averaging weights `w(t)`. The key idea is to adaptively adjust the weights to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function whenever possible, which has the significant benefit of preserving the theoretical `1/r(t)` convergence rate. This is contrasted with popular restarting heuristics (e.g., O'Donoghue & Candès, 2015), which often lack such guarantees for general convex functions. The paper presents a discrete-time version of this "adaptive averaging" heuristic and provides numerical experiments comparing its performance against non-adaptive accelerated mirror descent and two common restarting heuristics. The paper also illustrates the generality of its framework by deriving an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics, an important ODE in evolutionary game theory.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The use of a Lyapunov function to analyze the generalized `AMDw,η` dynamics is elegant and effective. The derivation in Section 2, culminating in Theorem 2, is clear and provides a straightforward recipe for designing dynamics with a guaranteed convergence rate. The proposed adaptive heuristic is well-motivated by this analysis, with the goal of tightening the bound on the time derivative of the Lyapunov function. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis. The experimental results, while limited in dimension, appear to support the claims about the practical performance of the adaptive heuristic. The authors are careful to position their heuristic against others, noting its unique rate-preservation guarantee.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent context, clearly stating the motivation and summarizing the contributions. The progression from the general ODE framework to the specific conditions for acceleration and finally to the adaptive heuristic is logical and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is consistent and the derivations are presented with sufficient detail. The distinction between the primal and dual space dynamics, and the connections between them, are explained effectively. Figure 2 is particularly helpful for visualizing the behavior of the different methods and a key claim of the paper—that the Lyapunov function is non-increasing for the proposed method but not necessarily for restarting heuristics.

**Originality:** The work is original and builds naturally on a recent, active line of research connecting continuous-time dynamics and accelerated optimization. While the general idea of using ODEs to understand optimization is not new, the formulation of the `AMDw,η` family is a novel and significant generalization of existing models (e.g., Krichene et al., 2015; Wibisono et al., 2016). The main original contribution is the adaptive averaging heuristic. While adaptive methods for optimization exist (e.g., Adagrad), this work proposes a fundamentally different mechanism based on adapting the averaging schedule rather than the learning rate, and is directly derived from a continuous-time Lyapunov analysis for accelerated methods. The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics is also a nice, original contribution that highlights the framework's utility.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are significant for several reasons.
1.  **Theoretical Insight:** The generalized framework provides a deeper, more unified understanding of the role of averaging in accelerated methods. It offers a principled way to design and analyze new accelerated dynamics.
2.  **Practical Heuristic with Guarantees:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is a practical contribution. Its main advantage over existing restarting schemes is the formal guarantee that it preserves the worst-case convergence rate. This provides a level of safety that is often absent in other heuristics, making it an attractive option for practitioners.
3.  **Connections to Other Fields:** The paper strengthens the bridge between optimization and dynamical systems, and the example of the accelerated replicator dynamics may spur interest and new applications in fields like game theory.

Despite these strengths, there are some weaknesses:
1.  **Limited Theoretical Analysis of the Heuristic:** While the paper proves that the adaptive heuristic *preserves* the convergence rate, it does not provide a theoretical analysis of *when* or *by how much* it improves upon it. For instance, for strongly convex functions, it is known that restarting can achieve a linear rate. It is unclear from the current analysis if adaptive averaging can also achieve this, though numerical results on a strongly convex quadratic are promising.
2.  **Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments are conducted in a low-dimensional space (R3) to facilitate visualization. While illustrative, this makes it difficult to assess the heuristic's performance and scalability on larger, more complex problems typical of modern machine learning. Including results on a higher-dimensional problem in the main paper would strengthen the empirical claims.
3.  **Continuous-to-Discrete Gap:** The proposed discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is motivated by the continuous-time heuristic, but the link is not fully formalized in the main text. The condition `f(x̃(k+1)) > f(x̃(k))` is used as a proxy for `⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩ > 0`. A more detailed discussion of this discretization and its implications would be valuable.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant generalization of continuous-time dynamics for accelerated optimization.
*   The Lyapunov-based analysis is technically sound and provides new insights.
*   Proposes a new, practical adaptive heuristic that, unlike many alternatives, comes with a formal guarantee of preserving the worst-case convergence rate.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical analysis of the adaptive heuristic is incomplete, as it does not prove any rate *improvement*.
*   The empirical validation is performed on low-dimensional toy problems, which may not be representative of real-world performance.

In conclusion, the paper's strengths, particularly its novel theoretical framework and the well-motivated, rate-preserving heuristic, significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The work represents a solid and interesting contribution to the literature on accelerated optimization.
