- Paper extends Accelerated Mirror Descent with generalized averaging via weight functions w and η and proves convergence rate 1/r(t) via a unified Lyapunov analysis.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts w(t) to ensure Lyapunov decrease and preserves the convergence rate.
- Demonstrates the approach on simplex-constrained replicator dynamics and shows adaptive averaging outperforms standard restarting heuristics in experiments.

- Theoretical analysis is technically sound and Theorem 2 gives clear sufficient conditions for 1/r(t) convergence.
- The continuous-to-discrete motivation is well-explained and the primal representation offers useful intuition.
- Examples, especially replicator dynamics, are well-chosen and practically relevant.

- The discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented without a rigorous convergence proof, a major omission for a NIPS submission.
- The work assumes smoothness (Lipschitz gradient) and does not address non-smooth optimization, limiting applicability.
- Numerical experiments lack statistical significance testing and detailed computational complexity comparisons.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is not justified as optimal and is not compared to other adaptive schemes.

- The paper is generally well-organized with consistent notation and progressive concept introduction.
- The connection between the continuous ODE and the discrete algorithm is insufficiently explained in the main text.
- The energy function Er is introduced without enough intuition about why it captures a natural energy measure.
- The adaptive heuristic should be better motivated by direct comparison to methods like Adagrad or Adam.

- The generalized averaging scheme meaningfully extends prior AMD work and provides a flexible acceleration framework.
- The adaptive averaging idea is novel in dynamically ensuring Lyapunov decrease rather than using fixed schedules or restarts.

- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from Flammarion & Bach [8] and lacks a clear articulation of key differences.
- The accelerated replicator dynamics connection is interesting but not shown to offer novel insights beyond existing literature.

- The continuous-time perspective and adaptive heuristic have potential practical and theoretical significance.
- The replicator dynamics example broadens appeal by linking optimization to evolutionary game theory.

- The experiments are limited (simple R^3 examples) and do not demonstrate applicability to realistic ML problems.
- The claimed "significant improvement" over heuristics is not quantified, and computational cost of the adaptive heuristic is not analyzed.

- For acceptance: sound theoretical framework, novel adaptive heuristic, useful replicator dynamics connection, and promising empirical evidence.
- Against acceptance: missing discrete convergence proof, insufficient distinction from related work, limited and unquantified experiments, and lack of non-smooth treatment.

- Overall assessment: technically solid and promising but incomplete; recommend rejection with major revisions focusing on discrete analysis and stronger empirical validation.

- Recommendation 1: Provide a formal convergence analysis for the discrete Algorithm 1.
- Recommendation 2: Explicitly contrast and clarify differences with Flammarion & Bach [8].
- Recommendation 3: Extend the framework or discuss extensions to non-smooth optimization.
- Recommendation 4: Include detailed computational complexity comparisons for the adaptive heuristic.
- Recommendation 5: Move key supplementary material (continuous-discrete connection) into the main text.
- Recommendation 6: Expand experiments to harder problems and include statistical significance testing.
