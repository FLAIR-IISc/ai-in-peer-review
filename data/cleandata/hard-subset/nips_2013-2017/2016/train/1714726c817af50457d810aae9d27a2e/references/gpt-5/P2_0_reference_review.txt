Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for convex optimization and introduces a two-parameter family of accelerated mirror descent ODEs with generalized averaging (AMDw,η). The state Z(t) accumulates gradients at rate η(t) while the primal iterate X(t) is a weighted average of ∇ψ∗(Z(·)) with weights w(t). The key analytical tool is a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f∗) + Dψ∗(Z, z∗), where r(t) encodes a target convergence rate. Theorem 2 provides sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) such that Lr is nonincreasing, yielding f(X(t)) − f∗ ≤ Lr(X(t0),Z(t0),t0)/r(t). A corollary gives a constructive recipe via a(t) = w/W and η = r a ensuring the condition. The paper also presents a second energy function Er that decreases under the same condition, develops a primal-only representation, and works out examples for positive-orthant and simplex constraints, yielding an “accelerated replicator” ODE. Finally, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative and discretize it (for r(t)=t^2) into a practical algorithm compared empirically against restart heuristics.

This builds on and connects several threads: Su-Boyd-Candès’ ODE view of Nesterov’s acceleration, Krichene et al.’s continuous-time AMD, and the variational/Bregman Lagrangian perspective of Wibisono et al. The novelty here is the generalized averaging parametrization (w, η) with explicit Lyapunov-based conditions and an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the target rate while empirically improving performance versus restarts.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: unifies a family of accelerated ODEs via generalized averaging and a single Lyapunov template; shows how acceleration relates to averaging weights.
- Sound continuous-time analysis: existence/uniqueness, Lyapunov decrease, and an alternative energy functional. The primal reformulation and examples (orthant, simplex) are instructive; the accelerated replicator dynamics are interesting in their own right.
- Adaptive heuristic: simple and principled (drives down the Lyapunov derivative), preserves the nominal rate, and compares favorably to gradient/speed restart in reported experiments.
- Relation to prior work is discussed and the framework subsumes earlier ODEs by appropriate choices of a(t), r(t).

Weaknesses
- Notational/typo issues materially affect clarity: e.g., in Corollary 1 and examples, η should be η(t) = r(t) w(t)/W(t) = r(t) a(t), but the text sometimes writes η = w r W; also a(t) is written as β t where the computations require a(t)=β/t. These inconsistencies are confusing and should be fixed.
- The adaptive rule is under-specified in continuous time (“a(t) ≥ η(t)r(t) otherwise”)—how large should a be? The discrete heuristic sets ak to βk/√s upon lack of progress, but the mapping between the continuous-time condition and the discrete trigger is heuristic. The promised discrete Lyapunov proof is in the supplement, not in the main text.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: low-dimensional (R^3) simplex-constrained problems; no large-scale or stochastic settings; no comparison to other modern adaptive accelerated schemes beyond restarts; limited discussion of sensitivity to hyperparameters (β, step size, ψ, R).
- Theoretical guarantees stop at preserving O(1/r(t)) in continuous time; there are no demonstrated faster rates with adaptivity (e.g., linear for strongly convex) nor robustness guarantees in discretization beyond the supplement.

Quality
- The continuous-time theory appears technically sound, with clear derivations and reasonable assumptions (Lipschitz ∇f and mirror map). The Lyapunov analysis is straightforward but useful. The Er derivation is correct under stated smoothness/monotonicity assumptions.
- The main technical caveats are clarity-impacting typos in key formulas and the lack of precise, constructive rules for a(t) in the adaptive scheme (beyond inequality constraints).
- The discretization and its guarantees are deferred to the supplement; having at least a theorem statement and brief proof sketch in the main text would strengthen perceived completeness.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, with a logical progression (definition → Lyapunov → examples → heuristic → experiments).
- However, several notational inconsistencies/typos (noted above) will confuse readers. The paper would benefit from a careful pass to ensure consistency for a(t), w/W, η, and the units in Theorem 2/Corollary 1/examples.
- The adaptive heuristic section should be more explicit about how to choose a(t) when the inner product is negative (e.g., a minimal increase policy), and the discrete algorithm should be explained with clearer rationale for each step.

Originality
- Moderate. The generalized averaging parametrization and Lyapunov-based condition unify and slightly extend prior ODE formulations; the accelerated replicator example is a nice application. The adaptive-averaging idea guided by Lyapunov decrease is simple and, to my knowledge, novel in this context.
- The contributions build on established ODE perspectives (Su et al., Krichene et al., Wibisono et al.) rather than fundamentally new rates or dynamics.

Significance
- Conceptually valuable for the theory of accelerated methods, offering a clean way to think about weights and adaptivity in continuous time, and potentially inspiring new discretizations.
- Practical impact is currently limited by modest empirical scope and lack of stronger discrete-time guarantees. With improved experiments and clearer algorithms, this could influence how practitioners implement adaptivity in accelerated mirror descent.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all formula inconsistencies: explicitly state η(t)=r(t) w(t)/W(t)=r(t) a(t); correct a(t)=β/t in the examples; ensure Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 match.
- Specify a constructive rule for increasing a(t) in the adaptive heuristic (e.g., minimal a making the second term negative by a fixed margin), and provide a convergence guarantee for the discrete algorithm in the main text.
- Expand experiments: higher dimensions, real datasets, stochastic gradients; compare to other adaptive/accelerated baselines; include sensitivity analyses.
- Clarify the role and choice of ψ and R in the discretization; discuss computational cost per iteration.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean unifying framework and Lyapunov analysis with instructive examples.
- Simple, principled adaptive heuristic that preserves the rate and improves practice over restarts in tested cases.
- Relevance to NeurIPS optimization community; connects to influential recent work.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity issues in key equations; adaptive scheme under-specified; discrete guarantees deferred to supplement.
- Limited experimental validation and scope; no stronger theoretical rates from adaptivity.

Overall assessment
This is a solid theoretical paper with a neat unifying view and a promising adaptive idea, but it needs clearer presentation and stronger discrete-time results/experiments. I lean toward a weak accept if the clarity issues are fixed, as the conceptual contribution is of interest to the community; otherwise, it is borderline.
