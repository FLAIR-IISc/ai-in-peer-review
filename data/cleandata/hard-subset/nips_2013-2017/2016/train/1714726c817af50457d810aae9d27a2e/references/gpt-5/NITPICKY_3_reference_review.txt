Summary
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for convex optimization with Lipschitz gradients over a convex set X, focusing on accelerated mirror descent (AMD) seen through an averaging lens. The authors introduce a generalized two-parameter family AMDw,η, where the state Z accumulates gradients at rate η(t), and the primal state X is an average (with weights w(t)) of the mirror-map trajectory ∇ψ∗(Z(·)). They analyze a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f∗) + Dψ∗(Z, z∗) for any increasing r(t), and provide sufficient conditions on w, η ensuring Lr is non-increasing and that f(X(t)) − f∗ = O(1/r(t)). They also derive a “kinetic-plus-potential” energy Er(t) that decreases under the same conditions. A primal-only representation is given, with examples for the positive orthant and the simplex (leading to an accelerated replicator ODE). Finally, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses the weights to further decrease Lr and present a discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) with experiments comparing adaptive averaging to restarting heuristics.

Relation to prior work
This work is closely related to:
- Nesterov’s acceleration and its ODE interpretations (Su-Boyd-Candès, 2016; Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett, 2015), and the variational perspective of Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan (2016), which also provides general families of ODEs with target rates via time-varying scalings.
- Mirror descent ODEs (Nemirovski & Yudin; MD dynamics) and their constrained variants via mirror maps.
- Restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès, Su-Boyd-Candès).
The generalized averaging viewpoint is a unifying lens; the adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves a Lyapunov decrease attempts to give guarantees absent in common restart schemes.

Strengths
- Clear continuous-time derivation of a generalized AMD via two weight functions (w, η), with a Lyapunov argument yielding O(1/r(t)) rates.
- Existence/uniqueness result under mild conditions (initialization at t0>0).
- Useful reparametrization a(t) = w(t)/W(t) and constructive recipe for weights achieving a desired rate r(t).
- A second energy Er that decreases under the same conditions and has a kinetic/potential interpretation; neat derivation using monotonicity of ∇ψ∗.
- Primal representations and concrete examples (positive orthant, simplex), including an “accelerated” replicator ODE, will be of interest to communities beyond optimization.
- The idea of adaptively choosing a(t) to further decrease Lr is natural and potentially useful in practice.

Weaknesses and detailed issues (many are small but numerous)
Quality/Correctness
- Example 1 and 2 contain a critical sign/dimension error. Given r(t)=t^2, the corollary requires a(t) ≥ r′(t)/r(t) = 2/t. The text sets a(t)=β t (β≥2), but all subsequent computations (w(t)=(t/t0)β−1, η(t)=β t) are consistent with a(t)=β/t, not β t. The same mistake appears for the general r(t)=tp (should be a(t)=β/t, β≥p). This must be corrected.
- Section 4: To make the term ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w) vanish, one needs η = (w/W) r = a r, equivalently a = η/r. The paper incorrectly states “we set a(t)=η(t)r(t)” and later writes the bracket as r − η a (instead of r − η/a). This inversion recurs multiple times (also see the proof of Theorem 3 where the correct 1/a factor appears). This is a serious notational/algebraic inconsistency that obscures the core condition.
- Algorithm 1 suffers from multiple issues preventing reproducibility:
  - Undefined symbols: γ (line 4), βmax (line 6).
  - Likely typos in λk: “λk = √s ak / (+ √s ak)” is malformed; presumably λk = √(s ak) / (1 + √(s ak)).
  - The update rules do not specify how x(k) is formed before computing ž(k+1), x̃(k+1). The roles of Dψ and R need precise definitions and norms.
  - The mapping from continuous-time (a(t), η(t), r(t)) to discrete (ak, step sizes, weights) is not fully specified. The guarantee that the discrete Lyapunov decreases is deferred to the supplement.
- Claims of “quadratic convergence rate” are misleading; the paper means O(1/t^2), not quadratic (which in optimization usually means Q-quadratic local convergence).
- Experiments: setup is underspecified (choices of β, s, γ, R, stopping, line-searches, Lipschitz constants, how constraints are enforced numerically). Without the supplement and figures, the empirical claims are not verifiable.

Clarity
- Numerous typographical and formatting issues:
  - “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)”: missing ordinal.
  - Equation/condition references sometimes inconsistent (e.g., writing r − η a instead of r − η W/w).
  - Switching between t0 and 0 initialization across sections; maintain consistency.
  - Footnote markers and superscripts not formatted properly; stray “2” in “first-order accelerated method2”.
  - In the simplex example, spacing/notation in the Hessian is hard to parse (ezi/∑k ezk etc.).
  - Several references incomplete (missing years, venues, pages) and inconsistent styles.
- Several key arguments depend on the supplement (discretization, rate proofs, extra experiments). The main text should be self-contained enough to reproduce central claims.

Originality
- The AMD family with generalized averaging and the Lyapunov-based sufficient conditions are interesting but close in spirit to the variational framework in Wibisono et al. (mapping α, β to a, r) and Krichene et al. The main conceptual novelty is the adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the Lyapunov decrease; however, in continuous time this is rather direct (choose a(t) to make the bracket small/zero). The discrete algorithmic contribution would be the more impactful novelty, but it is currently underspecified and contains errors.

Significance
- If corrected and made precise, the adaptive averaging idea could be a practical alternative to restarts, and the accelerated replicator dynamics may be of cross-disciplinary interest. However, as presented, the discrete method is not yet ready for practitioners, and the empirical section is too thin and opaque to establish practical significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all a vs 1/a inconsistencies. Explicitly restate condition (2) as ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩(r − η/a) ≤ 0 with a = w/W, and ensure examples and the adaptive rule use a = η/r when equality is desired.
- Correct Examples 1–2: a(t)=β/t, β≥p, yielding w(t) ∝ (t/t0)β−1 and η(t)=β t^{p−1}.
- Present a clean discrete algorithm with all symbols defined, correct λk, specified mirror map, regularizer, step sizes, and a discrete Lyapunov whose decrease is proven in the main text (or at least clearly summarized).
- Expand experimental details: parameter choices, problem instances beyond R^3, sensitivity, comparisons with fixed schedules and restarts on higher-dimensional benchmarks; include ablations showing the effect of adaptive a_k.
- Clean up citations and formatting; ensure consistent initialization and notation; avoid ambiguous phrasing (“quadratic convergence”).
- Discuss limitations: dependence on checking trajectory-dependent conditions, robustness under noise/stochastic gradients, and how to select r(t) beyond t^2.

Overall recommendation
Despite an interesting unifying viewpoint and a promising heuristic, the paper contains multiple algebraic inconsistencies, serious typos in key examples, and an unreproducible/disordered discrete algorithm. The empirical section is under-specified. Given the number of issues, I recommend rejection in its current form. With careful corrections, a rigorous discrete-time development, and stronger experiments, this could become a solid contribution.
