

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies a family of continuous-time accelerated descent dynamics with generalized averaging, extending previous work on continuous-time interpretations of accelerated methods. The authors introduce a broad class of ODEs (AMDw,η) parameterized by weight functions w and η, and provide a unified Lyapunov analysis that establishes sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate. The key contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence guarantees while improving empirical performance. The paper builds on the work of Krichene et al. [11] and Su et al. [20], extending their framework to include generalized averaging schemes.

## Strengths

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a rigorous Lyapunov analysis (Theorem 2) that provides clear sufficient conditions for convergence. The mathematical development is sound, with well-justified proofs and a clear exposition of the relationship between the continuous-time dynamics and convergence rates. The connection to replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained problems (Section 3) is particularly insightful, demonstrating the generality of the framework and providing an interesting application to evolutionary game theory.

The adaptive averaging heuristic (Section 4) is theoretically grounded and well-motivated, as it preserves the Lyapunov function and thus the convergence rate while potentially improving performance. The numerical experiments (Section 5) convincingly demonstrate that adaptive averaging outperforms existing restart heuristics in several test cases, particularly for strongly convex problems. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient mathematical detail for an expert reader to understand and reproduce the results.

## Weaknesses

The primary limitation is the focus on continuous-time dynamics, which may limit the paper's relevance to practitioners who typically implement discrete algorithms. While the paper provides a discrete version (Algorithm 1), it lacks thorough analysis of how well this discretization preserves the continuous-time convergence guarantees. The authors should provide a more detailed comparison between the continuous and discrete settings.

The numerical experiments are limited to simple R³ simplex problems, which may not be representative of more complex machine learning applications. Expanding to higher-dimensional problems or standard machine learning datasets would strengthen the empirical claims. The paper also does not compare the adaptive averaging heuristic to state-of-the-art accelerated methods beyond restart heuristics, such as Catalyst [O'Donoghue & Candès, 2015] or other recent acceleration frameworks.

The connection to the broader literature on accelerated optimization could be strengthened. While the paper cites relevant work, it doesn't explicitly position itself against more recent advances in the field, such as those addressing non-convex optimization or distributed settings.

## Recommendation

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of accelerated descent dynamics. The mathematical analysis is sound, the adaptive heuristic is well-motivated, and the empirical results are promising. While the continuous-time focus limits immediate practical impact, the theoretical insights could inform future work on discrete-time algorithms.

**Pro arguments for acceptance:** Strong theoretical contribution with rigorous analysis; insightful connection to replicator dynamics; adaptive heuristic preserves convergence guarantees while improving performance; well-written and well-organized.

**Con arguments for rejection:** Limited practical relevance due to continuous-time focus; numerical experiments too limited in scope; lacks comparison to recent accelerated methods beyond restart heuristics.

Given the quality of the theoretical contribution and the promising empirical results, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of accelerated optimization methods, which is a core topic at NIPS. The authors should address the concerns about the discrete-time analysis and expand the numerical experiments in a revised version, but these are not critical issues that would prevent acceptance.

The paper would benefit from a clearer statement about how the continuous-time insights might inform discrete-time algorithm design, and more comprehensive empirical validation on standard machine learning problems. Nevertheless, the theoretical contribution stands on its own merits as a significant advance in the understanding of accelerated descent dynamics.
