Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for minimizing a smooth convex function over a closed convex set. It introduces a family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) ODEs with generalized averaging, parameterized by two positive functions: w(t) governing averaging weights, and η(t) governing gradient accumulation in the dual variable. The authors define a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), where r(t) is a target rate function and Dψ* is a Bregman divergence. They provide sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) ensuring Lr is nonincreasing and thus f(X(t)) − f* = O(1/r(t)). This recovers the t^2 rate of classical acceleration and extends it to a broader family of rates via the choice of r. They also present an “energy” function Er(t) that decreases under the same conditions.

The paper gives a primal representation of the dynamics, shows examples for positive orthant and simplex constraints, and derives an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics. It then proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that selects the weight schedule online to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative whenever possible, while preserving the guaranteed rate. A discrete-time instantiation for r(t)=t^2 is given (Algorithm 1), and numerical experiments on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems compare the proposed heuristic with gradient and speed restarting heuristics, showing favorable performance.

This work builds on and unifies several lines of research: continuous-time interpretations of accelerated methods (e.g., Su et al. [20]), accelerated mirror descent in continuous/discrete time [11], and the variational/Bregman Lagrangian perspective [23]. It also connects to adaptive methods (AdaGrad/Adam) and to inertial gradient flows with damping [Attouch et al.], as well as replicator dynamics in game theory.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The continuous-time analysis appears mathematically sound. Existence/uniqueness is established under Lipschitz assumptions and t0>0. The Lyapunov analysis is clear, with a transparent decomposition of d/dt Lr leading to two sufficient conditions. The constructive corollary (η = w r/W and w/W ≥ r′/r) is straightforward and recovers known schedules as special cases.
- The energy function Er decreasing under the same conditions is a useful complement.
- The adaptive averaging rule is well-motivated: adjust a(t) = w/W to make the second term in the Lyapunov derivative nonpositive and preferably negative. However, it remains a heuristic in continuous time (no prescribed strategy for “how much larger” to take a(t) beyond the inequality).
- The discrete algorithm for r(t)=t^2 is described, and the paper claims a convergence bound in the supplement based on a discrete Lyapunov function. In the main text, details on the precise assumptions (smoothness, strong convexity of R, choice of step size s, parameter β) and the proof sketch are minimal. The connection between the continuous-time condition involving ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ and the discrete surrogate test f(x̃(k+1)) ≤ f(x̃(k)) could be better justified.
- Experiments are consistent with the theory qualitatively (monotonicity of Lr and Er for the adaptive method), but they are small-scale.

Clarity
- The presentation of the ODE family, the Lyapunov argument, and the construction of weights via a(t)=w/W is clear and well-organized. The equivalence to a primal representation and the two illustrative constrained examples are helpful.
- Some conditions (e.g., the trajectory-dependent inequality in Theorem 2) are not checkable a priori; the paper addresses this by providing a constructive corollary, but it would help to signal this earlier.
- Minor issues: several typographical inconsistencies, incomplete bibliographic entries, and occasional dense notation (e.g., switching between dual and primal variables) could be streamlined. Algorithm 1 references a regularizer R with properties not fully specified in the main text.

Originality
- The generalization to arbitrary w(t), η(t) with a tunable rate r(t) via a single Lyapunov template provides a unifying perspective that encapsulates earlier accelerated mirror descent and the variational formulation of [23]. The explicit averaging-based construction and the energy/Lyapunov viewpoint are close in spirit to prior work but offer a different, arguably simpler, sufficient-condition pathway.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the continuous-time Lyapunov decrease, and its discrete counterpart tailored to first-order accelerated methods, appear to be the main novel contributions. Accelerating replicator dynamics via averaging is also an interesting byproduct, though replicator dynamics and mirror descent connections are known.

Significance
- The continuous-time framework with generalized averaging and the constructive conditions can inform algorithm design. The adaptive averaging heuristic is potentially useful in practice as an alternative to restarts and is easy to implement.
- The current empirical evaluation is limited to low-dimensional simplex problems; broader experiments (high-dimensional machine learning benchmarks, diverse constraint sets and mirror maps, stochastic gradients) would better support impact claims.
- The discrete-time theory in the main text is brief; stronger, self-contained guarantees would increase practical significance.

Pros
- Clear, unified Lyapunov framework yielding O(1/r(t)) rates and recovering known accelerated schedules.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with a principled goal (monotone Lyapunov), preserving rates.
- Primal formulations and illustrative examples, including an accelerated replicator dynamics.
- Existence/uniqueness and an auxiliary energy function provide a complete dynamical picture.

Cons
- The key sufficient condition in Theorem 2 is trajectory-dependent; the constructive corollary may be conservative.
- The adaptive rule is underspecified in continuous time; discrete implementation departs from the exact Lyapunov condition and relies on heuristics (e.g., function decrease on x̃).
- Limited experiments; lack of large-scale evaluations and broader baselines.
- Discrete-time guarantees and parameter selection guidance (β, s, R) are not fully developed in the main text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a more detailed, self-contained discrete-time analysis in the main paper: precise assumptions, statement of a convergence theorem with constants, and the relationship between the discrete tests and the continuous Lyapunov decrease.
- Clarify how to choose β, step size s, and the regularizer R; discuss sensitivity.
- Extend experiments to higher dimensions, different mirror maps (e.g., Euclidean, Mahalanobis), and include additional baselines (e.g., FISTA with restarts, heavy-ball, Bregman Lagrangian discretizations).
- Analyze strongly convex objectives: can adaptive averaging yield linear rates with appropriate r(t)?
- Improve presentation and references (complete bibliographic details; fix typos).

Overall assessment
The paper offers a coherent continuous-time framework for accelerated mirror descent with generalized averaging and a simple, principled adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves Lyapunov decrease and target rates. The theoretical continuous-time results are solid; the main limitations are the modest novelty relative to recent variational/ODE analyses and the limited discrete-time theory and experiments. With strengthened discrete guarantees and broader empirical validation, the contribution would be more compelling. Arguments for acceptance hinge on the unifying Lyapunov perspective and the adaptive heuristic; arguments against focus on the limited scope of experiments and discrete-time rigor.
