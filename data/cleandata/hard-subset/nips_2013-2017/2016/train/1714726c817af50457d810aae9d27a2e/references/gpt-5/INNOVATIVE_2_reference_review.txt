Summary
This paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) through the lens of generalized averaging. The authors introduce a two-weight ODE family AMDw,η in which the mirror-dual variable Z(t) integrates gradients at a rate η(t) while the primal iterate X(t) is a weighted average of the mirror map ∇ψ∗(Z(·)) with weights w(t). They propose a simple Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f∗) + Dψ∗(Z, z∗) and give sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) to ensure that Lr is non-increasing, yielding a convergence guarantee f(X(t)) − f∗ ≤ O(1/r(t)). They also provide a constructive recipe to design w and η for any desired rate r, recovering classical t2 rates and extending to tp, p ≥ 1.

The paper gives a primal-only representation of the dynamics, recovers known inertial/variational ODEs as special cases (e.g., Wibisono et al.), and derives accelerated versions of replicator dynamics for the simplex and Lotka-Volterra-type dynamics on the positive orthant. A second “energy” function Er = f(X) + (1/r)Dψ∗(Z, ∇ψ(X)) is shown to be non-increasing under the same conditions.

A main practical proposal is an adaptive averaging heuristic: adapt a(t) := w(t)/W(t) in time to make the derivative of Lr as negative as possible while preserving its decrease, which retains the original rate guarantee. A discrete version is provided for the t2 rate, compared empirically with gradient and speed restart heuristics on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems. The authors report that adaptive averaging is consistently competitive and often better, and preserves monotonicity of the Lyapunov surrogate.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on and generalizes prior continuous-time views of acceleration, notably Su–Boyd–Candès (Nesterov ODE), Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett (accelerated mirror descent via averaging), and Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan (variational characterization). The present contribution extends AMD by allowing general averaging schedules and providing a unifying Lyapunov template tied to a target rate r(t). The adaptive averaging heuristic appears new relative to restart heuristics and provides a continuous-time rationale for rate-preserving adaptivity. The accelerated replicator dynamics connection is also interesting and, to my knowledge, not previously stated in this form.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean, unified Lyapunov-based framework that decouples the target rate r from the averaging/accumulation schedules (w, η), with constructive recipes to meet the desired rate.
- Bridging views: Clear connections among mirror descent, variational ODEs, and replicator/Lotka–Volterra dynamics; the primal representation is helpful for intuition.
- New adaptive idea: Adaptive averaging that provably preserves the Lyapunov decrease (and hence the rate) in continuous time; simple, interpretable discrete instantiation.
- Technical soundness: Existence/uniqueness addressed; Lyapunov and energy functions carefully derived; conditions are transparent and recover known results as special cases.
- Potential impact: A general design template for accelerated dynamics and a promising practical heuristic that could influence algorithm design beyond the toy settings shown.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation: Experiments are small-scale (R3, simplex) and deterministic. There is no evaluation on higher dimensions, real datasets, or stochastic gradients; comparisons to popular adaptive methods (Adagrad, Adam) are not provided despite the discussion.
- Discretization gap: While the continuous-time guarantees are clear, theoretical guarantees for the discrete adaptive algorithm are deferred to the supplement and summarized only briefly; it would strengthen the paper to present a main-text theorem with assumptions and a precise rate.
- Practical guidance: The heuristic requires choices (β, step size s, regularizers ψ, R). Guidance on tuning and sensitivity is sparse; ablations would help.
- Assumptions and scope: Smoothness/Lipschitz assumptions on ∇f and mirror maps are stated, but the extension to non-smooth composite objectives (proximal settings) is only alluded to via prior work; making the scope explicit would improve clarity.
- Notational density: Some notational overload (e.g., a(t) introduced mid-proof; X̌, Ž, etc.) and minor typos make parts harder to parse; a brief notation table would help.

Questions for the authors
- Can you include in the main text a discrete-time convergence statement for Algorithm 1 (assumptions, rate, and whether the Lyapunov analogue is monotone)?
- How does adaptive averaging behave under noise (stochastic gradients)? Can the Lyapunov argument be adapted to expected decrease or high-probability bounds?
- For strongly convex f, can you establish faster rates (linear in discrete time or O(e−βt) in continuous time) under adaptive averaging without restarts?
- How sensitive is performance to β and a1? Any principled selection rules?
- Can your framework accommodate composite (non-smooth) objectives with proximal operators while preserving guarantees?

Minor comments and suggestions
- Clarify early that a(t) = w/W is used repeatedly; keep notation consistent.
- The choice t0 > 0 is reasonable; briefly quantify how it affects practical discretizations.
- In Section 3, explicitly state smoothness assumptions on ψ for Theorem 3 and the primal forms.
- Provide more detail on R and Dψ in Algorithm 1, and how one instantiates them on the simplex (e.g., entropy and KL/Bregman divergences).
- Fix minor typos and formatting (e.g., missing slashes in w(t)/W(t) in Theorem 1 proof; equation numbering).

Broader impact and significance
This work offers a versatile and conceptually appealing way to design and analyze accelerated dynamics via adaptive averaging with rate-preserving guarantees. The accelerated replicator connection is likely to interest communities beyond optimization (evolutionary dynamics, game theory). If the discrete adaptive scheme is further validated and extended to stochastic/composite settings, it could become a practical alternative to restart heuristics.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Novel and bold conceptual contributions with solid continuous-time analysis and a promising adaptive idea. The main limitations are modest empirical scope and limited discrete-time theory in the main text, but given the potential to open a useful design avenue for accelerated methods, I lean positive.

Confidence: High (familiar with continuous-time analyses and accelerated methods).
