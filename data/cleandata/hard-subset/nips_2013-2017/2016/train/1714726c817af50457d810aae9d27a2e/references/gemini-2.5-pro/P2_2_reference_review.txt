**Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"**

**Summary**

This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated convex optimization. It generalizes the authors' previous work on Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) by introducing a broad family of dynamics, `AMDw,η`, parameterized by two user-defined positive weight functions: `w(t)` for the averaging scheme and `η(t)` for the rate of gradient accumulation. The core theoretical contribution is a unified Lyapunov analysis for this family. The authors derive sufficient conditions on `w`, `η`, and a desired convergence rate function `r(t)` that guarantee the objective function value converges at a rate of `1/r(t)`. This analysis provides a constructive method for designing dynamics with a prescribed convergence rate (e.g., `O(1/t^p)`).

Building on this theoretical framework, the paper's main practical contribution is a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The insight is that the sufficient conditions for convergence can be actively exploited: when the trajectory is making good progress, the averaging weights `w(t)` can be increased to accelerate faster, while still provably maintaining the Lyapunov function and thus the convergence guarantee. The paper presents a discrete version of this heuristic and provides numerical experiments comparing it to popular restarting heuristics on several simplex-constrained optimization problems. As a notable example, the framework is shown to produce an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics, an important ODE in evolutionary game theory.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The continuous-time analysis is rigorous and elegant. The use of a generalized Lyapunov function `Lr(X,Z,t)` to provide a unified analysis for the entire `AMDw,η` family is a strong theoretical contribution. The derivation of the sufficient conditions in Theorem 2 is clear and the result is powerful, as it connects the choice of weight functions directly to a guaranteed convergence rate. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis.

A minor weakness lies in the transition from the continuous-time adaptive heuristic (Equation 8) to its discrete implementation (Algorithm 1). The continuous heuristic depends on the sign of `〈∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)〉`, which measures the alignment of velocity with the negative gradient direction. The discrete algorithm, however, adapts its parameter `ak` based on the sign of `f(x̃(k+1)) - f(x̃(k))`, a change in function value at an auxiliary sequence `x̃`. While this is an intuitive and reasonable proxy, the paper could benefit from a more explicit justification for this specific choice and a discussion of the relationship between these two conditions. The details are deferred to the supplement, but a brief justification in the main text would strengthen the link between theory and practice.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very well-written and organized. It follows a logical progression from the general continuous-time formulation, to the Lyapunov analysis, to the specific primal-space examples, and finally to the proposed adaptive heuristic and experiments. The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are communicated effectively. The use of an "energy function" `Er` in Theorem 3 provides a nice physical intuition for the dynamics, interpreting the Bregman divergence term as a form of kinetic energy. The figures in the experimental section are excellent, providing a multi-faceted view of the dynamics (objective value, trajectory, Lyapunov function, and energy function) that clearly illustrates the behavior of the different methods.

**Originality:**
The work builds upon a now-established line of research connecting accelerated optimization methods to second-order ODEs (e.g., Su et al. [20], Wibisono et al. [23], and the authors' previous work [11]). However, it makes several novel contributions:
1.  **Generalization:** The `AMDw,η` family is a significant and novel generalization of previously studied dynamics, which typically use a fixed weighting scheme (e.g., `w(t) ∝ t^(β-1)`).
2.  **Theoretically-Grounded Heuristic:** The key original contribution is the adaptive averaging heuristic. While other heuristics like restarting exist, they are often ad-hoc and can lack convergence guarantees for general convex problems (as demonstrated by the increasing Lyapunov function in the experiments). The proposed heuristic is derived directly from the Lyapunov analysis and, by construction, preserves the `O(1/r(t))` convergence rate. This is a significant advance over purely empirical heuristics.
3.  **Accelerated Replicator Dynamics:** The derivation of an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics is a novel and interesting application of the framework, connecting accelerated optimization to a well-known model from another field.

**Significance:**
The paper's results are significant for both the theory and practice of optimization.
Theoretically, the unified Lyapunov framework provides a deeper understanding of the role of averaging in acceleration and offers a principled way to design and analyze new accelerated methods.

Practically, the adaptive averaging heuristic is a promising contribution. Accelerated methods are known to be sensitive to parameters and can suffer from oscillations. Existing restarting heuristics are effective but can be aggressive and lack theoretical guarantees. Adaptive averaging offers a "gentler" way to control oscillations by modulating the averaging weights rather than resetting the dynamics entirely. The fact that it comes with a preserved convergence rate guarantee is a major advantage.

The primary limitation on significance is the scope of the numerical experiments. While they effectively illustrate the method's properties and advantages in low-dimensional settings, they do not demonstrate its performance on larger-scale machine learning problems where methods like Adam or Adagrad are prevalent. The paper dismisses these as not being designed for accelerated methods, which is true, but they are the dominant adaptive methods in practice. A comparison against them, even with caveats, on a benchmark problem (e.g., logistic regression on a standard dataset) would substantially strengthen the paper's claims of practical impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel and elegant generalization of the continuous-time dynamics for accelerated mirror descent.
2.  The core contribution, an adaptive averaging heuristic, is directly derived from a rigorous Lyapunov analysis and comes with a formal convergence rate guarantee, a significant advantage over many existing heuristics.
3.  The experimental results, though limited in scale, clearly demonstrate the promised theoretical properties and show favorable performance compared to standard restarting schemes.
4.  The connection to and acceleration of the replicator dynamics is a nice, self-contained contribution that bridges disciplines.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The experimental validation is conducted on small, illustrative problems. The practical utility for large-scale NIPS-style problems remains undemonstrated.
2.  The connection between the continuous-time heuristic and its discrete implementation could be made more rigorous in the main text.

**Conclusion:**
Overall, this is an excellent paper. It presents a strong theoretical framework that leads to a novel, well-motivated, and practically promising adaptive heuristic for accelerated methods. The strengths, particularly the originality of the theoretically-grounded heuristic, far outweigh the weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
