

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper proposes a generalization of accelerated mirror descent dynamics through a family of ODEs with generalized averaging, parameterized by weight functions w and η. The authors build upon prior work by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) and Su et al. (NIPS 2014), extending their continuous-time interpretation of accelerated methods to a broader class of averaging schemes. They introduce a Lyapunov function analysis to prove convergence rates and propose an "adaptive averaging heuristic" that dynamically adjusts the weights based on the solution trajectory.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is Theorem 2, which provides sufficient conditions for a Lyapunov function to guarantee convergence at rate 1/r(t). The authors derive a special case (Corollary 1) where these conditions are satisfied, and they show how their approach recovers existing accelerated methods as special cases (e.g., Example 1 recovers the scheme in Krichene et al. 2015). The paper also provides a primal representation of the dynamics and demonstrates an application to replicator dynamics on the simplex.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical analysis using Lyapunov functions is technically sound and well-motivated.
- The connection to replicator dynamics is interesting and provides a concrete application.
- The paper builds logically on established work in continuous-time optimization.
- The numerical experiments compare the proposed method with relevant baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main contribution appears to be a formal generalization of existing approaches rather than a substantial methodological advance. The authors acknowledge this when stating "we provide a positive answer" to the question of whether different averaging schemes can achieve accelerated rates. However, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate how this generalization leads to meaningful improvements over established methods.

The adaptive averaging heuristic (Algorithm 1) is presented as a novel contribution, but it bears significant resemblance to existing restarting heuristics (e.g., O'Donoghue and Candès, 2015). The claim that it "preserves the convergence rate" is problematic because Theorem 2 applies to the continuous-time ODE, while the discrete-time algorithm lacks a rigorous convergence analysis. The paper states "we give a bound on the convergence rate of Algorithm 1 in the supplementary material," but this is insufficient for a NIPS submission, as the main paper should contain the core theoretical results.

The numerical experiments are limited in scope and scale. The authors test on simple examples in R^3 with only four objective functions (strongly convex quadratic, weakly convex quadratic, linear, and KL divergence). These experiments do not demonstrate the method's utility on large-scale problems or complex real-world applications where accelerated methods are typically applied. The claimed "significant improvement" in the strongly convex case is not quantified with sufficient detail to support such a strong claim.

The paper also fails to adequately compare with the most recent and relevant work. For example, it doesn't compare with the adaptive restart method proposed by O'Donoghue and Candès (2015), which is the state-of-the-art for restarting accelerated methods. The paper's claim that "adaptive averaging compares favorably in all examples" is not compelling given the limited experimental scope.

Most critically, the paper does not convincingly establish how this work advances the state of the art beyond existing accelerated methods. The connection to replicator dynamics is interesting but doesn't provide substantial new insights into optimization theory. The adaptive heuristic appears to be a minor variation on existing approaches without sufficient justification for its novelty or superiority.

**Overall assessment:**
While the paper presents technically sound analysis of a generalization of existing continuous-time optimization dynamics, it fails to demonstrate significant methodological advances or practical benefits over established methods. The theoretical contribution is incremental, and the experimental validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims of "significant improvement." The discrete-time algorithm lacks rigorous convergence guarantees, and the numerical experiments are limited in scope and scale.

As a skeptical reviewer who prefers established methods and rigorous validation, I find the paper's claims overreaching relative to its actual contribution. The work represents a minor extension of existing ideas rather than a substantial advancement in the field. The paper would benefit from more extensive numerical validation on challenging optimization problems, a clearer articulation of how this approach differs from and improves upon existing methods, and a more rigorous analysis of the discrete-time algorithm.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's contribution is too incremental to warrant acceptance at NIPS, and the lack of strong theoretical guarantees for the discrete-time algorithm and insufficient experimental validation undermine the main claims.
