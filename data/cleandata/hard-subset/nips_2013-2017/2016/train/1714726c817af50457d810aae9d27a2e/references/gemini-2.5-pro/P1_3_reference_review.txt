This paper presents a generalization of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent dynamics, building upon recent work connecting Nesterov's methods to second-order ODEs. The authors introduce a family of dynamics, `AMDw,η`, parameterized by two functions: `w(t)` which controls a generalized averaging scheme, and `η(t)` which scales the gradient. The core of the paper is a unified analysis of this family using a Lyapunov function `Lr`, which yields sufficient conditions on `w` and `η` to guarantee a convergence rate of O(1/r(t)). This framework is shown to be general enough to recover previous results and to derive new dynamics, such as an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics for optimization over the simplex. Based on this analysis, the authors propose a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. This heuristic dynamically adjusts the averaging weights to accelerate convergence while, crucially, preserving the theoretical rate guarantee by ensuring the Lyapunov function remains non-increasing. The paper concludes with numerical experiments comparing this new heuristic to standard restarting schemes, showing favorable performance.

This work fits well within the growing literature on continuous-time analysis of optimization algorithms (e.g., Su et al. [20], Krichene et al. [11], Wibisono et al. [23]). It provides a significant generalization of the averaging scheme presented in [11] and uses this generalization to motivate a new, theoretically-grounded adaptive method.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Unified Theoretical Framework (Quality, Significance):** The main contribution is the generalization of the accelerated mirror descent ODE to the `AMDw,η` family and its analysis via the Lyapunov function `Lr`. This provides a clean, unified perspective on how different averaging and gradient scaling schemes can achieve acceleration. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 offer simple and interpretable conditions for guaranteeing a desired convergence rate, which is a valuable theoretical tool.

2.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Adaptive Heuristic (Originality, Significance):** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a key strength. Unlike many popular restarting heuristics ([17], [20]), which can be highly effective but often lack guarantees for general convex functions, this method is derived directly from the condition to decrease the Lyapunov function. The guarantee that the `O(1/r(t))` rate is preserved is a strong theoretical advantage, which is also confirmed empirically in the experiments where `Lr` is shown to be non-increasing.

3.  **Interesting Connections and Examples (Clarity, Originality):** The paper does an excellent job of grounding the abstract framework in concrete examples. The derivation of an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics in Section 3 is a particularly nice and insightful result, connecting the abstract optimization dynamics to a well-known equation from evolutionary game theory.

4.  **Clear Presentation and Strong Empirical Support (Clarity, Quality):** The paper is well-written and the theoretical derivations are easy to follow. The numerical experiments in Section 5 are well-designed and compelling. The visualizations are particularly effective, clearly showing the trajectories and the evolution of the objective, Lyapunov, and energy functions. The results demonstrate that the adaptive averaging heuristic is not just theoretically sound but also practically effective, often outperforming established restarting methods.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity on the Discretization of the Heuristic (Clarity):** There appears to be a disconnect or a lack of detailed explanation between the continuous-time adaptive heuristic in equation (8) and its discrete implementation in Algorithm 1. Equation (8) suggests choosing `a(t) ≥ η(t)/r(t)` when progress stalls (i.e., when the term multiplying `⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩` in (3) needs to be positive). This implies a more aggressive averaging scheme. However, Algorithm 1's logic (lines 6-8) is to keep `ak` small when progress is made (`f(x̃(k+1)) ≤ f(x̃(k))`) and reset it to a larger default value `β/k√s` otherwise. The interaction between `ak = min(ak-1, ...)` and the reset condition is not immediately obvious and its connection to the continuous-time logic could be elaborated upon for clarity. A more detailed derivation of Algorithm 1 from the principles of Section 4 would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Analysis of Improved Rates (Quality):** The paper proves that the adaptive heuristic *preserves* the baseline `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate. However, a major motivation for restarting heuristics is their ability to achieve faster (e.g., linear) convergence rates on specific function classes like strongly convex problems. The current analysis does not show that adaptive averaging can achieve such an improved rate. While this is noted as a direction for future work, it remains a limitation compared to the analysis available for some restarting schemes in specific settings.

3.  **Incremental Aspects (Originality):** While the generalization is significant, the core analytical tools (continuous-time ODEs for acceleration, Lyapunov functions based on Bregman divergence) build very directly on previous work, particularly [11] and [20]. The contribution is more of a skillful and insightful generalization and application rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new paradigm.

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. Its primary strengths are the elegant theoretical framework for generalized averaging and the novel, well-motivated adaptive heuristic that comes with stronger guarantees than typical restarting schemes. The connection to accelerated replicator dynamics is a nice bonus. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to clarity in the derivation of the discrete algorithm and the scope of the theoretical analysis for the adaptive method. These do not detract significantly from the paper's value. The work is technically sound, original, clearly presented, and its results are likely to be significant for researchers working on the theory and design of optimization algorithms.

I recommend **acceptance**.
