

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper introduces a novel framework for analyzing accelerated descent dynamics through a generalized averaging scheme. The authors extend previous work on continuous-time interpretations of accelerated optimization methods (Su et al., 2014; Krichene et al., 2015) by proposing a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η. The key contribution is the adaptive averaging heuristic, which dynamically adjusts the weight functions along solution trajectories to preserve the convergence rate while improving empirical performance.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a unified Lyapunov function approach to analyze the convergence rate of AMD dynamics, establishing sufficient conditions for a given rate 1/r(t). Second, it demonstrates how this framework can accelerate well-known dynamics like the replicator equation in evolutionary game theory. Third, and most importantly, it proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes the weight functions w(t) to ensure the Lyapunov function remains decreasing, thereby preserving the convergence rate while potentially improving performance. This adaptive approach differs fundamentally from existing restarting heuristics (O'Donoghue & Candès, 2015; Wibisono et al., 2016) by continuously adjusting the averaging scheme rather than periodically resetting the algorithm.

The theoretical foundation is strong, with Theorems 2 and 3 establishing rigorous conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing. The connection to replicator dynamics (Section 3) is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates how the proposed framework can accelerate a well-known ODE from evolutionary game theory. The numerical experiments (Section 5) convincingly show that adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics across multiple problem types, including strongly convex, weakly convex, and linear objectives on the simplex.

The paper's novelty is its central contribution: the adaptive averaging heuristic. While previous work has explored restarting strategies for accelerated methods, this paper presents a fundamentally different approach that dynamically adjusts the averaging scheme without restarting. This represents a significant conceptual advance in understanding and improving accelerated optimization methods. The connection between the continuous-time framework and discrete algorithms (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated, and the theoretical guarantees extend to the discrete setting.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical detail to allow reproduction of the results. The theoretical analysis is sound, with appropriate references to related work. The experimental results are thorough and provide strong empirical support for the proposed approach.

Minor weaknesses include some technical details that could be clarified (such as the relationship between the continuous and discrete formulations) and the fact that the paper doesn't fully explain why adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics theoretically. However, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's strong conceptual contribution.

In terms of significance, this work represents a meaningful advance in the understanding of accelerated optimization. The adaptive averaging heuristic could potentially be applied to many accelerated first-order methods beyond those discussed in the paper. The connection to replicator dynamics also opens up interesting possibilities for applications in evolutionary game theory and related fields.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual contributions, I find this paper highly compelling. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a genuinely new idea that addresses a fundamental aspect of accelerated methods in a novel way. While the paper could benefit from more detailed theoretical analysis of why adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics, this is a minor concern given the paper's strong theoretical foundation and empirical results.

The paper clearly advances the state of the art in continuous-time optimization dynamics and provides a new direction for improving accelerated methods. It is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on continuous-time optimization (Su et al., 2014; Krichene et al., 2015) by introducing a fundamentally new approach to adaptive acceleration.

I recommend accepting this paper. The novel adaptive averaging heuristic represents a significant conceptual contribution to the field, with strong theoretical guarantees and compelling empirical results. The paper makes a clear and important step toward understanding how to improve accelerated optimization methods, and the adaptive approach has the potential to be widely adopted in future work.
