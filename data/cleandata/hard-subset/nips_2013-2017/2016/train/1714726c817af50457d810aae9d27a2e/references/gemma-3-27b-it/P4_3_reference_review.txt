This paper investigates continuous-time dynamics for accelerated optimization, specifically focusing on a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions for averaging and gradient accumulation. The authors build upon previous work connecting Nesterov’s acceleration to ODEs (Su et al. 2016, Krichene et al. 2016) and aim to identify conditions under which different averaging schemes preserve or improve convergence rates. They derive sufficient conditions for Lyapunov stability and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts weights based on progress, offering guarantees on convergence rate preservation. The paper includes theoretical analysis, a primal representation of the dynamics, example dynamics for specific constraint sets (positive orthant, simplex), and numerical experiments comparing the adaptive heuristic to existing restarting methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of understanding acceleration in continuous-time optimization dynamics and the motivation for exploring different averaging schemes. The connection to existing literature on Nesterov acceleration and continuous ODE interpretations is well established.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The theoretical analysis, based on Lyapunov functions, provides a solid foundation for understanding the convergence behavior of the proposed AMD dynamics. Theorems 2 and 3, and associated corollaries, offer concrete sufficient conditions for convergence and rate preservation.
* **Generality:** The generalization of AMD dynamics with arbitrary weighting functions *w* and *η* provides a flexible framework for analysis.
* **Adaptive Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution. The idea of adjusting weights based on progress, guided by the Lyapunov function's derivative, is intuitively appealing and theoretically justified.
* **Illustrative Examples:** The derivation of accelerated replicator dynamics for the simplex constraint set is a nice example demonstrating the applicability of the framework to a problem with connections to evolutionary game theory.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The numerical experiments are well-designed, comparing the adaptive averaging heuristic with established restarting methods across multiple objective functions and constraint sets. The inclusion of Lyapunov and energy function plots in the experimental results offers insightful information beyond just the objective function value.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Density:** The paper is quite dense with notation, particularly in the mathematical derivations. While rigorous, this can make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the specific mathematical tools used.
* **Limited Novelty of the Core Idea:** While the adaptive heuristic is a logical and well-supported extension, the fundamental idea of adjusting step sizes or weights based on progress is not entirely novel in optimization (e.g., adaptive gradient methods like Adam). The key contribution lies in adapting this idea to the context of *accelerated* methods while preserving the convergence rate guarantee.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper does not extensively discuss the practical challenges of implementing the adaptive heuristic, such as tuning parameters or dealing with noisy gradient estimates.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares to gradient and speed restarting heuristics, it doesn’t compare against more recent and potentially relevant adaptive optimization algorithms (beyond mentioning Adagrad and Adam briefly as being different in spirit).
* **Discretization Details:** The discussion of the discretization of the adaptive averaging heuristic is somewhat brief. More details on the discrete algorithm and its theoretical properties would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound analysis of continuous-time optimization dynamics with a generalization of accelerated mirror descent.
* The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution that addresses a natural question arising from the theoretical framework.
* The paper provides theoretical guarantees on convergence rate preservation and supports these guarantees with comprehensive numerical experiments.
* The work builds upon and contributes to a growing body of research connecting optimization algorithms to continuous-time dynamical systems.
* The derived accelerated replicator dynamics provide an interesting application of the framework.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The core idea of adaptive averaging, while well-executed, isn't groundbreaking in the broader optimization landscape.
* The notational density could hinder accessibility for a wider audience.
* The lack of comparison to more recent state-of-the-art algorithms could limit the perceived impact of the work.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization dynamics. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the adaptive heuristic is well-motivated and supported, and the numerical experiments are thorough. While the core idea isn't entirely novel, the application to accelerated methods and the convergence rate guarantees distinguish this work.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and potentially include a discussion of practical implementation challenges and comparisons to more recent algorithms.
