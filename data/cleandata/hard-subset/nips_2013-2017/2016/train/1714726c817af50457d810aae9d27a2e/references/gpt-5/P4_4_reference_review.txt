Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for minimizing a smooth convex function over a closed convex set, focusing on accelerated mirror descent (AMD). The main contribution is a two-parameter family of accelerated dynamics AMDw,η in which (i) gradients are accumulated in a dual variable Z with a time-varying rate η(t), and (ii) the primal iterate X is a weighted average of the mirror map applied to Z, with averaging weights governed by w(t). A unified Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ∗(Z,z*) is proposed; under two conditions relating w, η, and a desired rate function r(t), Lr is non-increasing along trajectories, implying f(X(t))−f* ≤ const./r(t). An equivalent primal form is derived, and examples on the positive orthant and the simplex are given, including an “accelerated” replicator dynamics. The paper further proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that selects the averaging schedule online to decrease the Lyapunov function whenever possible while preserving the nominal 1/r(t) rate. A discrete-time algorithm is provided for the quadratic rate r(t)=t^2, and empirical comparisons on low-dimensional constrained problems suggest advantages over restart-based heuristics.

The work is positioned within the body of continuous-time perspectives on Nesterov-type acceleration (e.g., Su–Boyd–Candès [20], Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan [23]) and prior AMD analysis [11]. It generalizes the averaging viewpoint of [11] by allowing broader weight schedules, connects to the Bregman-Lagrangian family [23] via a specific parameterization, and relates to acceleration-through-averaging observations (e.g., Flammarion–Bach). The adaptive heuristic contrasts with restart strategies (O’Donoghue–Candès [17], Su–Boyd–Candès [20]) by providing Lyapunov-based guarantees under convexity.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Continuous-time analysis: The existence/uniqueness result (Theorem 1) follows from standard Lipschitz arguments (Cauchy–Lipschitz), with the technical choice of t0>0 to avoid singular w/W. The Lyapunov analysis is straightforward and transparent: the derivative bound (Eq. 3) yields sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) ensuring monotonicity of Lr and the rate f(X(t))−f* ≤ Lr(t0)/r(t). Corollary 1 offers a practical construction via a(t)=w/W and η=w r/W satisfying w/W ≥ r′/r, with concrete examples r(t)=tp. The secondary “energy” function Er (Theorem 3) is also shown to decrease under the same key condition, using monotonicity of ∇ψ∗.
- Adaptive heuristic: The continuous-time rule that sets a(t)=η(t)r(t) when ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩>0 and a(t)≥η(t)r(t) otherwise makes the mixed term in Eq. 3 nonpositive, preserving Lyapunov decrease and hence the nominal rate provided η≥r′. This is mathematically consistent with Theorem 2.
- Discretization: A discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is proposed for r(t)=t^2 with claims (in the supplement) of a convergence-rate bound via a discrete Lyapunov argument. In the main text, details (step-size conditions, dependence on L, choice and role of R) are omitted, so the discrete-time guarantees are not fully assessable here.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-organized, with derivations explained and key assumptions stated (smoothness, Lipschitzness of ∇f, ∇ψ∗; convexity; initialization at t0). The use of dual and primal forms (Z, Ž) is helpful but notationally heavy. Some algorithmic details (e.g., line 6 with “βmax”) and parameter choices could be clarified. Several proofs and definitions (mirror maps, discrete analysis) are deferred to the supplement; brief sketches or explicit conditions in the main text would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- Generalized averaging with explicit Lyapunov-based conditions that map desired rates r(t) to admissible weight schedules appears novel beyond [11]; the paper recovers known rates and connects to [23] while allowing broader weight choices.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the continuous-time Lyapunov decrease and rate is a new angle relative to restart heuristics, with a principled criterion tied to the sign of ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩.
- The explicit accelerated replicator dynamics derived from the AMD framework is a neat and instructive example.

Significance
The continuous-time framework can guide algorithm design, offering interpretable conditions for achieving target rates and a mechanism to adapt averaging while retaining guarantees. This contributes to understanding acceleration as averaging. The empirical section is small-scale (mostly R^3), so practical impact on large-scale ML problems is not demonstrated; however, the ideas are potentially transferable to broader settings.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, general Lyapunov framework that unifies and extends accelerated mirror descent with flexible weights and target rates.
- Conceptually simple adaptive averaging heuristic with continuous-time guarantees that preserve rates.
- Connections to established ODE views ([20], [23]) and a concrete accelerated replicator example.
- Empirical indications that adaptive averaging can outperform popular restarts in the tested cases.

Arguments against acceptance
- Discrete-time theory for the adaptive scheme is not presented in the main text; practical usefulness depends on discrete implementations and step-size choices.
- Experiments are limited in scale and scope; no high-dimensional benchmarks or stochastic settings.
- Some conditions are trajectory-dependent (Theorem 2, condition 2), and while the corollary addresses this, guidance on tuning η and a beyond r(t)=t^2 is limited.
- Notation and some algorithmic lines could be clearer; several key proofs deferred to the supplement.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide in the main paper a concrete discrete-time convergence statement for Algorithm 1, including assumptions on step sizes, smoothness, and strong convexity (if any).
- Compare more explicitly with the Bregman-Lagrangian parameterization [23], clarifying when AMDw,η recovers or generalizes those dynamics.
- Clarify Algorithm 1 line 6 (“βmax_k√s” appears to be a typo; likely β k √s) and the role/requirements on R (strong convexity parameter, choice).
- Include higher-dimensional experiments and sensitivity to step size; report computational costs per iteration versus restart baselines.
- Discuss robustness to noise and potential stochastic variants.

Overall, the paper provides a technically sound and conceptually useful continuous-time perspective with a novel adaptive averaging idea; the main limitation is the limited discrete-time theory and experiments.
