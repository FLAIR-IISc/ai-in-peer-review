### **Summary**

This paper presents a generalized framework for accelerated mirror descent dynamics in continuous time. The authors introduce a broad family of dynamics, denoted AMDw,η, parameterized by two time-varying weight functions: w(t) for averaging the trajectory and η(t) for scaling the gradient accumulation. The core theoretical contribution is a Lyapunov-based analysis that provides simple, sufficient conditions on these weight functions to guarantee a desired convergence rate of O(1/r(t)). This framework elegantly unifies and extends previous work, such as the authors' own in [11] which used a fixed `t^p` weighting scheme.

Building on this theoretical foundation, the paper's main practical contribution is a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. This heuristic dynamically adjusts the averaging weights w(t) to maximally decrease the Lyapunov function at each step, thereby aiming for faster convergence while provably preserving the baseline O(1/r(t)) rate. This is contrasted with popular restarting heuristics, which often lack such guarantees for non-strongly convex problems. The framework's utility is demonstrated by deriving an accelerated version of the well-known replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained optimization. Finally, numerical experiments show that the proposed adaptive heuristic compares favorably to standard restarting schemes across several convex optimization problems.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and General Theoretical Framework:** The paper's main strength lies in its clear and powerful generalization of continuous-time accelerated dynamics. The formulation of AMDw,η and the subsequent Lyapunov analysis (leading to Theorem 2) provide a clean, unified perspective on how averaging and gradient accumulation contribute to acceleration. It makes explicit the conditions required to achieve a certain rate, moving beyond specific constructions (like `w(t) = t^(β-1)`) to a general principle. The inclusion of a second energy function (Er) in Theorem 3 further deepens the physical intuition of the dynamics.

2.  **Principled Heuristic with Guarantees:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a standout contribution. It is not an ad-hoc modification but a direct, clever application of the theoretical findings. The key insight—to choose weights that exploit the "slack" in the Lyapunov function's derivative bound (inequality 3)—is both simple and effective. Crucially, this heuristic comes with the guarantee that the Lyapunov function remains non-increasing, ensuring the `1/r(t)` convergence rate is preserved. This provides a significant advantage over restarting schemes whose effect on the convergence rate in the general convex case is often not well understood.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Insight:** The numerical experiments are well-designed and compelling.
    *   They test on a diverse set of objective functions (strongly/weakly convex, linear, KL), providing a comprehensive comparison.
    *   Crucially, the plots show not only the objective function value but also the evolution of the Lyapunov function `Lr` and the energy `Er`. This provides direct empirical validation of the paper's theoretical claims, clearly illustrating that `Lr` is non-increasing for the adaptive method but can oscillate for restarting methods. This is an excellent example of experiments being used to build intuition beyond just showing performance improvement.
    *   The results demonstrate that adaptive averaging is consistently competitive and, in some cases, significantly better than popular restarting heuristics.

4.  **Novel Connections and High Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics is a novel and interesting result, connecting the abstract optimization framework to a well-known model in evolutionary game theory. This serves as a powerful example of the framework's generative capabilities. The authors also do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to related literature, such as Su et al. [20], Wibisono et al. [23], and various restarting/adaptive methods.

### **Weaknesses and Questions**

1.  **Details of Discretization:** The transition from the continuous-time adaptive strategy (Equation 8) to the specific update rules in Algorithm 1 is quite brief in the main text. While the algorithm is plausible, the specific choices (e.g., `ak = min(ak-1, β_max / (k√s))` and the condition `f(x̃(k+1)) - f(x̃(k)) > 0`) seem somewhat heuristic. A little more intuition in the main paper about why this specific discretization is a faithful and effective implementation of the continuous-time idea would be beneficial. For example, why is the objective function at `x̃(k)` the right quantity to check for "making progress"?

2.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The discrete algorithm introduces new parameters, such as `β` and `β_max`. The paper does not discuss how these were chosen for the experiments or how sensitive the algorithm's performance is to them. While this is a common issue with many algorithms, a brief comment would strengthen the experimental section.

3.  **Comparison to Other Adaptive Methods:** The introduction briefly mentions Adagrad and Adam, correctly noting they are not designed for acceleration in the same way. However, it might be interesting to include one of them in the experiments, particularly for the unconstrained or positive-orthant case. While the theoretical motivations are different, practitioners often reach for these methods, and a direct comparison could be illuminating.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions to both the theory and practice of accelerated optimization methods. The generalized continuous-time framework is elegant and insightful, and the resulting adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, principled, and empirically effective. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and well-situated within the relevant literature. It advances our understanding of acceleration and provides a practical algorithm that appears to outperform existing heuristics while offering stronger theoretical guarantees. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance and is likely to inspire further research into adaptive and continuous-time optimization methods.
