Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated first-order dynamics for minimizing a smooth convex function over a convex set. Building on the accelerated mirror descent (AMD) viewpoint of Krichene et al. (NIPS’15), the authors introduce a two-parameter family of ODEs AMDw,η in which (i) a dual variable Z accumulates gradients at an adaptive rate η(t), and (ii) the primal iterate X(t) is a weighted average (with weight function w) of a mirror-map transform of the dual trajectory. The main theoretical contribution is a unified Lyapunov analysis: for a target rate r(t), they propose Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) and show simple sufficient conditions on (w,η,r) under which Lr is nonincreasing and f(X(t))−f* ≤ const/r(t). A trajectory-dependent inequality is reduced to a practical condition via Corollary 1, yielding a broad set of schedules—recovering, e.g., the t^2 rate with polynomial averaging weights.

They also present an “energy” function Er(t) = f(X(t)) + (1/r(t))Dψ*(Z,∇ψ(X)), which decreases under the same condition and provides physical intuition (potential + kinetic). A primal representation is derived and exemplified for orthant and simplex constraints, yielding an accelerated variant of the replicator dynamics.

Motivated by the Lyapunov derivative bound, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that modulates the weight schedule online to further decrease Lr when possible while preserving the target rate. A discrete algorithm (for r(t)=t^2) is given, with experiments on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems comparing adaptive averaging to gradient and speed restarts. Empirically, adaptive averaging is competitive or better and consistently yields decreasing Lyapunov value.

The work relates to: Nesterov’s acceleration; Su–Boyd–Candès’ ODE; Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett’s AMD (NIPS’15); Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan’s variational perspective and Bregman Lagrangian; and restart heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès). It also connects to replicator/Lotka–Volterra dynamics (Sigmund; Weibull) and adaptive first-order methods (Adagrad, Adam), clarifying distinctions.

Quality:
- Theory: The Lyapunov framework is sound and unifies numerous weight schedules. The existence/uniqueness argument is standard and careful (initializing at t0>0; W(t0)>0). The reduction of the trajectory-dependent condition to Corollary 1 is elegant and recovers known accelerated schedules while generalizing them.
- Novelty vs prior ODEs: Compared to [23], the averaging-centric parameterization (two degrees of freedom w,η) and the adaptive averaging principle guided by Lyapunov decrease appear original, as is the explicit accelerated replicator ODE. The energy function is a nice complement.
- Discrete algorithm: The adaptive heuristic is reasonable and the mapping t≈k√s is standard. However, guarantees for the discrete scheme are deferred to the supplement; in the main text one only learns that a discrete counterpart of Lr is used. Practical guidance for step sizes and hyperparameters is limited.
- Empirics: Experiments are clean and illustrative, but small scale (R^3 simplex). The reported behaviors (Lyapunov/energy monotonicity, oscillation reduction, outperforming restarts on strongly convex) are plausible but not comprehensive.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized, with helpful derivations and intuition. The primal forms and examples are instructive. There are a few notational slips that should be fixed:
- In Examples 1–2, a(t) should be β/t (not β t) to satisfy a(t)≥r′(t)/r(t) and to match the subsequent expressions w(t)∝(t/t0)^{β−1} and η(t)=a(t)r(t)=β t. As written, “a(t)=β t” is dimensionally inconsistent and contradicts the derived w(t).
- The role of constants (β, γ, s) and the regularizer R in Algorithm 1 needs clearer specification. Define γ, state assumptions on R (strong convexity parameter, smoothness), and discuss how to choose s for stability.

Originality:
The generalized averaging viewpoint and adaptive averaging heuristic provide a novel and simple lever to design accelerated dynamics and preserve Lyapunov decrease. The explicit accelerated replicator dynamics are of independent interest. Relative to Wibisono et al., the contribution is complementary: rather than a Lagrangian generating family of ODEs and discretizations with provable rates, this work offers a unifying Lyapunov template parameterized by (w,η,r) and a practical adaptive rule.

Significance:
Conceptually, the paper clarifies the link between acceleration and averaging and offers a principled way to tune weights to maintain Lyapunov decrease. This is likely to influence both theoretical analyses and heuristic design of accelerated methods, especially in constrained/mirror settings. The adaptive averaging idea is simple and potentially impactful. The empirical section would benefit from larger-scale tests, but the methodological contribution is solid.

Pros:
- Clean, unified Lyapunov analysis covering a broad family of accelerated mirror ODEs with generalized averaging.
- Simple sufficient conditions that recover known rates and enable new schedules.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantee (in continuous time) to preserve the rate and decrease Lyapunov.
- Clear primal representations and an accelerated replicator dynamics example.
- Existence/uniqueness and an interpretable energy function.

Cons:
- Main sufficient condition (Theorem 2 condition (2)) is trajectory-dependent; the constructive corollary addresses it but narrows choices.
- Discrete algorithmic guarantees are light in the main paper; hyperparameter guidance is minimal.
- Experiments are small-scale and deterministic; no large-scale or stochastic settings, and no comparisons to modern adaptive restart schemes beyond two baselines.
- Notational/typo issues in Examples 1–2; some symbols (γ, R) insufficiently specified.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix the a(t)=β/t typos and ensure consistency across examples.
- Clarify Algorithm 1: define γ and R rigorously; state the discrete Lyapunov and convergence bound in the main text (even briefly), with dependence on L, strong convexity of R, and step size constraints.
- Provide guidelines or adaptive rules for choosing β and s; include sensitivity analyses.
- Add experiments on higher-dimensional problems (e.g., logistic regression with simplex or orthant constraints), wall-clock comparisons, and robustness to noisy/stochastic gradients. Compare also to FISTA with adaptive restarts and to Bregman Lagrangian discretizations where applicable.
- Discuss whether adaptive averaging can yield faster than 1/t^2 rates under strong convexity (e.g., linear rate) and what continuous-time conditions would imply that.

Overall recommendation:
This is a solid and reasonably novel contribution that extends the continuous-time acceleration literature with a unifying averaging-based perspective and a practical adaptive heuristic. Despite modest experiments and some presentation issues, the theoretical development is clear and potentially useful. I lean toward acceptance after minor revisions focusing on clarity and an improved discrete-time discussion.
