## NIPS 2016 Review: Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of accelerated gradient methods. It generalizes a family of continuous-time dynamics, provides sufficient conditions for convergence, and proposes a novel adaptive averaging heuristic with guarantees on convergence rate preservation. While the theoretical development is strong, the numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the experimental validation and clarify some points in the presentation.

**Summary of Ideas:**

The paper builds upon the established connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs). Previous work (Su et al., Krichene et al.) demonstrated that Nesterov’s acceleration could be viewed through the lens of a specific ODE with an averaging scheme. This paper generalizes that averaging scheme, studying a broader family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two weight functions, *w(t)* and *η(t)*.  The authors establish a Lyapunov function and provide conditions under which the dynamics converge at a rate determined by *r(t)*. Crucially, they then propose an adaptive heuristic to dynamically adjust the averaging weight *w(t)* based on the decrease of the Lyapunov function, aiming to preserve the theoretical convergence rate. This work extends prior heuristic approaches (e.g., gradient/speed restarts) by offering a guaranteed convergence rate.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper’s mathematical analysis is a significant strength. The derivation of the Lyapunov function and the sufficient conditions for convergence are well-presented and convincing.  The extension of previous work to a more general family of AMD dynamics is a genuine theoretical advance.
* **Novel Adaptive Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a key contribution. The fact that it preserves the theoretical convergence rate is a substantial advantage over existing heuristics, which often lack such guarantees.
* **Connection to Replicator Dynamics:**  Demonstrating the connection to the replicator dynamics in the simplex-constrained case is insightful and highlights the broader applicability of the framework. This bridges optimization with areas like game theory and evolutionary dynamics.
* **Clear Motivation & Context:** The introduction effectively motivates the work by connecting it to the resurgence of first-order methods and the importance of continuous-time dynamics for algorithm design.
* **Well-Written & Organized:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easier to follow the theoretical developments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The numerical experiments are somewhat superficial.  While they demonstrate that the adaptive averaging performs comparably to or better than existing heuristics, the experiments are limited to a few examples and relatively small dimensions (R3). A more comprehensive evaluation, including a wider range of problem instances (e.g., larger scales, different conditioning numbers) and comparisons to state-of-the-art optimization solvers, would significantly strengthen the paper.  The claim of "significant improvement in some cases" feels unsubstantiated without more detailed results.
* **Complexity of Primal Representation:** The primal representation derived in Section 3, while theoretically interesting, feels somewhat less practical. The introduction of the Hessian term (∇2ψ∗ ◦ ∇ψ) increases the computational complexity and might hinder direct implementation. The discussion of its advantages feels somewhat cursory.
* **Lack of Discussion on Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t thoroughly discuss the sensitivity of the method to the choice of the initial weight function *a(t)* or the parameters β and *s* in the discrete implementation (Algorithm 1).  Understanding this sensitivity is crucial for practical application.
* **Minor Clarification Needed:** The notation can be slightly dense at times.  For instance, the meaning of λk in Algorithm 1 could be clarified slightly more explicitly.



**Originality:**

The generalization of the AMD dynamics and the development of the adaptive averaging heuristic are original contributions. While the idea of adaptive step sizes/weights is not new (e.g., Adagrad, Adam), applying it in the context of *accelerated* methods with theoretical guarantees is a novel aspect. The connection to the replicator dynamics offers a fresh perspective on established problems.

**Significance:**

The work has the potential to be significant. A guaranteed convergence rate preservation with adaptive averaging is a valuable contribution.  The theoretical framework developed here could inform the design of more robust and efficient accelerated optimization algorithms. The connection to replicator dynamics could lead to applications in diverse fields. However, the impact will depend on further validation through more extensive experimental results.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and insightful analysis.
* Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with convergence rate guarantee.
* Connection to relevant concepts in game theory and evolutionary dynamics.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited experimental validation.
* Primal representation is less practical.
* Lack of discussion on sensitivity analysis.



**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance contingent on the following revisions:

1. **Expanded Experimental Evaluation:**  Conduct more comprehensive experiments with a wider range of problem instances, larger dimensions, and comparisons to state-of-the-art optimization solvers.  Provide detailed results to support the claim of "significant improvement in some cases."
2. **Sensitivity Analysis:** Discuss the sensitivity of the method to the choice of parameters (e.g., β, *s*, initial weight function *a(t)*).
3. **Clarify Notation:** Provide slightly more explicit explanations of certain notation (e.g., λk in Algorithm 1).
4. **Minor Improvements:** Proofread for minor typographical errors and enhance the clarity of some sentences.



I believe that addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
