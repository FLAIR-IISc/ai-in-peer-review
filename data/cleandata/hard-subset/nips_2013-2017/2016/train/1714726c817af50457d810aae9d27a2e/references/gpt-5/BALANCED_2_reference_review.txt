Summary
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for minimizing a convex, smooth objective over a convex set. Building on the mirror-descent viewpoint and the interpretation of Nesterov’s acceleration as a second-order ODE, the authors introduce a broad family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics with generalized averaging. The dynamics are parameterized by two positive weight functions: w(t) governs averaging of a transformed dual trajectory, and η(t) scales gradient accumulation in the dual. A Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) is proposed; under simple inequalities coupling r, w, η, Lr is nonincreasing, yielding a convergence guarantee f(X(t))−f* ≤ const/r(t). A second “energy” function Er that blends potential and kinetic terms is also shown to decrease under the same conditions. The paper gives a primal-only formulation, illustrates examples (positive orthant and simplex), and shows that the replicator dynamics on the simplex admit an “accelerated” variant via the proposed averaging. Finally, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that locally picks w(t) to decrease the Lyapunov function and present a discrete-time algorithm whose rate matches O(1/k^2) while being competitive with restart heuristics (gradient and speed restarts) on low-dimensional experiments.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to (i) Nesterov’s acceleration and its ODE interpretations (Su-Boyd-Candès 2016), (ii) the proximal/mirror version of accelerated ODEs (Krichene et al. NIPS 2015), (iii) variational/Bregman Lagrangian perspectives (Wibisono et al. 2016), and (iv) inertial gradient flows with Lyapunov analyses (Attouch et al.). Compared to these, the novelty is the generalized averaging family AMDw,η, conditions guaranteeing a chosen rate r(t), and an adaptive averaging scheme that preserves the continuous-time Lyapunov decrease (and its discrete analogue), with a new accelerated replicator instance.

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis is clean. The existence/uniqueness argument (Theorem 1) is standard and careful about the t0>0 issue. The Lyapunov computation (Theorem 2), the constructive conditions via a(t)=w/W, and the recovery of classic t^2 rates are sound.
- The “energy” functional is insightful and connects to physical intuition (potential/kinetic).
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is motivated by directly decreasing d/dt Lr and guarantees preserved rate in continuous time; the discrete-time algorithm is reasonable and empirically competitive with restarts.

Concerns:
- The key condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on the unknown trajectory through ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ and r−ηW/w, hence is not verifiable a priori. The useful corollary (η = w r/W, with w/W ≥ r′/r) removes this dependence but yields no strict decrease of Lr from the second term. It would help to formalize the gap between provable conditions and the practically adaptive rule.
- The adaptive rule in continuous time specifies “a(t) ≥ η(t)r(t)” when the directional term is non-positive, but gives no concrete choice. The discrete instantiation gives a specific schedule (ak nonincreasing, resetting when f(x̃) increases), but theoretical guarantees for the discrete algorithm appear limited to preserving the O(1/k^2) rate (details deferred to the supplement). Sharper discrete-time analysis (e.g., stability, robustness to step size, parameter selection) is missing.
- Experiments are small-scale (mostly 3D simplex) and limited in scope. The baselines are two restart heuristics; comparisons to strong modern adaptive methods (e.g., different mirror maps, other accelerated proximal schemes) or to the Bregman Lagrangian discretizations would strengthen the empirical case. Strongly convex settings report notable gains, but no theoretical linear-rate result is provided.
- Some notation/typos can confuse. Example 1 states a(t)=β t but subsequent algebra and conclusion correspond to a(t)=β/t. This should be corrected. The dependence/regularity assumptions on ψ, ψ* (e.g., differentiability) are scattered between main text and supplement.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with a clear chain from ODE definition to Lyapunov analysis and examples. The primal representations help intuition. However:
- Consolidate assumptions (smoothness, strong convexity of ψ, Lipschitz constants) and domains in one place.
- Fix the a(t) typo and streamline the derivation around equations (3)–(4).
- Better explain the discrete algorithm’s parameters (β, s, γ, choice of R) and sensitivity.

Originality
Moderately original. The generalized averaging parameterization and the explicit Lyapunov-based construction of η, w for any r(t) are nice. The adaptive averaging heuristic that ensures monotone Lyapunov decrease is a new angle distinct from restarts. The accelerated replicator instance is an elegant byproduct. The work sits close to Wibisono et al.’s variational framework but offers a different constructive lens via averaging weights.

Significance
Conceptually valuable for understanding and designing accelerated methods via averaging. Practically, the discrete heuristic is plausible and may be useful, but broader evidence and stronger discrete-time guarantees would increase impact. The accelerated replicator dynamics could interest game theory and population dynamics audiences.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concrete, implementable rule for choosing a(t) when ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ ≤ 0, and analyze its effect on w(t) (e.g., boundedness, continuity).
- Strengthen discrete-time theory: precise statement and proof of the convergence rate for Algorithm 1 (in the main text), stability regions for step size s, role of β and γ, and guarantees under strong convexity (linear rate?).
- Expand experiments: higher-dimensional problems, different constraint sets and mirror maps (e.g., Euclidean ball, box), non-smooth composite objectives (proximal setting), sensitivity ablations, and wall-clock comparisons.
- Connect more explicitly to Wibisono et al. by positioning AMDw,η within the Bregman Lagrangian family and clarifying similarities/differences in rates and discretizations.
- Clean up notation and references (several bib entries lack full details).

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, correct continuous-time analysis with a unifying Lyapunov framework and constructive design of weights to achieve target rates.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic preserving Lyapunov decrease and competitive empirical behavior versus standard restarts.
- Insightful examples, including an accelerated replicator dynamics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited discrete-time theoretical guarantees and small-scale experiments; practical significance not fully demonstrated.
- The main sufficient conditions either depend on the trajectory or reduce to a conservative equality case; limited insight into the benefits of adaptivity beyond heuristics.
- Some clarity/typo issues in key derivations.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a useful conceptual advance and a plausible heuristic with promising initial evidence. With strengthened discrete analysis and broader experiments, it could have substantial impact.
