

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper proposes a unified framework for accelerated mirror descent dynamics with generalized averaging, establishing sufficient conditions for convergence rates using Lyapunov functions. The authors extend previous work on continuous-time accelerated methods, particularly Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11], by introducing a family of ODEs parameterized by weight functions w and η. The paper's main contribution is the adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate while empirically improving performance over restart heuristics.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical foundation for accelerated mirror descent dynamics with generalized averaging.
- The connection to the replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained problems is interesting and well-motivated.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is theoretically grounded and shows promising empirical results compared to existing restart heuristics.
- The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on continuous-time interpretations of accelerated methods.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies and notational errors that undermine its theoretical rigor and clarity. These issues are substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite the paper's theoretical interest.

1. **Critical notational confusion:** The paper uses "r" both as a fixed parameter (r ≥ 2) in the initial formulation and as a function r(t) in the Lyapunov function. This is particularly problematic in Theorem 2 where the condition "r(t)−η(t)W(t)w(t)" should be "r(t)−η(t)W(t)/w(t)" (see equation 3). The paper consistently miswrites the condition as "r(t)−η(t)W(t)w(t)" instead of the correct "r(t)−η(t)W(t)/w(t)".

2. **Inconsistent and confusing notation:** The paper uses X for both the feasible set and the primal variable, Z for the dual variable, and W for both the weight function and the integral of w(τ). This leads to serious ambiguity throughout the paper, particularly in Theorem 1 and the proof of Theorem 2.

3. **Mathematical errors:** 
   - In Corollary 1, the condition "w(t)W(t) ≥ r'(t)r(t)" should be "w(t)/W(t) ≥ r'(t)/r(t)".
   - The paper states "W(t) = ∫t 0 w(τ)dτ" without specifying the lower limit (t0 or 0), creating confusion about the initialization.
   - In the adaptive averaging heuristic (8), the condition "a(t) = η(t)r(t)" is missing the division by w(t) that would make the condition meaningful.

4. **Formatting and typographical errors:** 
   - "f(X(t))− f? ≤ C/t2" should be "f(X(t)) - f* ≤ C/t^2".
   - "r ≥ 2 is a fixed parameter" but later "r(t)" is used as a function.
   - "x?" should be "x*" consistently throughout.
   - "∇ψ*(Z(τ))" should be "∇ψ*(Z(τ))" (the star should be on ψ, not the gradient).

5. **Theoretical inconsistencies:** 
   - The proof of Theorem 1 claims "w(t)/W(t) ≤ w(t)/W(t0)" but this is only true if W(t) ≥ W(t0), which is not necessarily the case for t < t0.
   - The paper states "the solution trajectories of this ODE exhibit a quadratic convergence rate" for general r, but the quadratic rate (1/t^2) only holds for r(t)=t^2, not for general r(t).

6. **Citation issues:** The paper cites Nesterov's original work as having "convergence rate o(1/k^2)" when it actually has O(1/k^2), which is a significant technical inaccuracy.

7. **Clarity issues:** The paper's claim that "the continuous-time ODE can be interpreted as coupled dynamics of a dual variable Z(t) which evolves in the dual space E∗, and a primal variable X(t) which is obtained as the weighted average" is not clearly derived or explained, making the connection opaque to the reader.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper addresses an interesting topic and presents a theoretically grounded framework for accelerated mirror descent, the numerous technical inconsistencies, notational errors, and mathematical inaccuracies significantly undermine the paper's credibility and clarity. The notational confusion between r as a parameter and r(t) as a function is particularly problematic and appears throughout the paper. The mathematical errors in the key conditions (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1) are substantial and would likely lead to incorrect conclusions in applications.

These issues are not merely minor formatting inconsistencies but fundamental problems that affect the paper's technical soundness. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to resolve these notational and mathematical issues before it can be considered for publication. Given the stringent quality standards of NIPS, I recommend rejection.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is theoretically grounded and shows empirical promise.
- The connection to replicator dynamics is novel and interesting.
- The paper provides a unified framework for continuous-time accelerated methods.

*Con:*
- Critical mathematical errors in the main theorems (Theorem 2, Corollary 1).
- Consistent notational confusion between r and r(t).
- Multiple typos and formatting errors that undermine technical credibility.
- The paper's key conditions are incorrectly stated, which would mislead readers.
- The paper fails to clearly explain the connection between the continuous-time and discrete-time methods.
