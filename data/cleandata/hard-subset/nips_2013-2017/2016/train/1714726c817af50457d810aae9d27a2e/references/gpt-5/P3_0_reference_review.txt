Summary
The paper studies a broad family of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two positive weight functions: w(t) for averaging and η(t) for gradient accumulation. Using a Lyapunov framework Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors provide simple sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) under which Lr is nonincreasing, yielding a convergence guarantee f(X(t))−f* = O(1/r(t)). This unifies and generalizes prior ODE views of acceleration (e.g., Su-Boyd-Candès; Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan) and earlier work by the authors on AMD. The paper also introduces a second “energy” function Er that decreases under the same conditions, gives a primal representation of the dynamics, and derives concrete instances for the positive orthant and the simplex, yielding an “accelerated replicator” ODE. Finally, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic: choose the weighting schedule a(t) := w(t)/W(t) adaptively along trajectories to maximize decrease of the Lyapunov function while preserving the O(1/r(t)) rate; a discrete variant (Algorithm 1) is tested against gradient and speed restarts on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems, showing favorable empirical behavior.

Quality
- Theory: The Lyapunov analysis is clean and leverages the AMD structure effectively. Theorem 1 (existence/uniqueness) is standard given Lipschitzness and the W(t) regularization. Theorem 2 gives transparent sufficient conditions linking r, w, η; Corollary 1 turns the trajectory-dependent condition into a constructive design via a(t):=w/W. The constructive examples for r(t)=t^p are consistent with known accelerated schedules and recover past results.
- Technical caveats: There are several notational/typographical inconsistencies that obscure the key relationships:
  - In Corollary 1 and the subsequent discussion, the equality ensuring condition (2) is η(t) = a(t) r(t), i.e., a(t) = η(t)/r(t). In Sec. 4, the heuristic states “set a(t)=η(t) r(t) if <∇f, Ẋ> > 0,” which contradicts the earlier condition and is dimensionally inconsistent. This should be corrected to a(t)=η(t)/r(t) (the choice that makes r(t)−η(t)/a(t)=0).
  - Example 1: For r(t)=t^2, one needs a(t) ≥ 2/t. The text appears to write a(t)=β t rather than a(t)=β/t, and also uses w(t)=a(t)a(t0) e∫ a(τ)dτ instead of w(t)=a(t)/a(t0) e∫ a(τ)dτ per Eq. (4). The intended result w(t)=(t/t0)^{β−1} and η(t)=β t matches prior work, but the derivation as printed has mistakes.
  - The derivation of Theorem 3 uses a:=w/W. The link to Theorem 2’s condition (2) is correct when η=ar, but this should be stated carefully.
These issues are likely fixable but affect clarity and trust in the adaptive section.
- Discretization: The discrete Algorithm 1 and its proof are deferred to the supplement. Given that the empirical section relies on it, a succinct statement of the guarantee (assumptions, rate, and any step-size/β ranges) should be included in the main text. It would also help to discuss stability/robustness to misspecification of β and s, and how ak interacts with the composite structure (ψ, R).

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with helpful background and intuition. The primal forms and the concrete examples (positive orthant and simplex/replicator) are valuable for intuition. However, the noted typos around a(t), w(t), and the heuristic are significant and should be corrected. A summary “design recipe” that maps a desired rate r(t) to valid (a,w,η), together with 2–3 canonical choices, would improve readability. Clarify assumptions on ψ ensuring ∇ψ* is Lipschitz and, in Sec. 3, when ψ is smooth so that ∇ψ=(∇ψ*)^{-1} holds.

Originality
The work builds on and unifies several strands:
- AMD and ODE interpretations of Nesterov acceleration (Su-Boyd-Candès; Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett).
- Variational/energy viewpoints (Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan).
The main novel contributions are: (i) a general two-weight AMD family with a simple Lyapunov-based rate certificate for arbitrary rates r(t); (ii) an adaptive averaging heuristic that provably preserves the Lyapunov decrease and rate in continuous time; and (iii) an accelerated replicator ODE derived from the primal form. While the ODE and Lyapunov methodologies are established, the adaptive averaging angle and its coupling to averaging weights appear new and interesting.

Significance
Conceptually, the paper clarifies the role of averaging in acceleration and offers a principled way to tune the averaging online so that a Lyapunov function decreases. If the discrete method can be shown to be stable and effective more broadly, this could influence the design of practical accelerated methods beyond standard restarts. The current empirical evidence is limited to low-dimensional simplex problems and a few objectives. To increase impact, it would help to:
- Evaluate on higher-dimensional problems and compare to tuned restart baselines and modern adaptive methods (e.g., adaptive restart variants, adaptive momentum).
- Include quantitative metrics (final suboptimality vs iterations/time), sensitivity to step-sizes, and ablations on the adaptive rule.

Specific comments and questions
- Please fix the a(t) relation: the condition that zeroes the second term in (3) is η(t)=a(t) r(t). The text in Sec. 4 appears to use a(t)=η(t) r(t), which is incorrect.
- Example 1/2: correct a(t)=β/t and the formula for w(t) per Eq. (4).
- Theorem 1: explicitly state the assumptions on ψ (strong convexity/smoothness) that ensure ∇ψ* is Lipschitz and that X(t) remains in X for the examples with indicator functions (simplex).
- Theorem 3: clarify the smoothness needed (ψ twice differentiable) and whether the result extends to non-smooth ψ via monotone operator theory.
- Adaptive heuristic: in continuous time you require η(t) ≥ r′(t) to preserve the rate; how is this enforced in the discrete algorithm, and what is the discrete analogue of r′?
- Discretization: provide a short theorem in the main text stating the convergence guarantee for Algorithm 1 (assumptions, constants), and how ak’s non-increasing updates affect complexity.
- Experiments: describe how step sizes and β were chosen/tuned; whether restarts were tuned for fairness; add higher-dimensional results and quantitative tables.
- Relation to Wibisono et al.: you remark that AMDp,w,η generalizes their family; could you more directly map parameters to show your adaptive choice corresponds to a valid time-varying “metric/friction” in their Lagrangian framework?

Pros
- Clear, flexible Lyapunov framework yielding O(1/r(t)) rates and unifying prior ODE views.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with a principled guarantee in continuous time.
- Insightful primal forms and accelerated replicator dynamics.

Cons
- Several distracting typos/notation errors in core formulas (a,w,η).
- Discrete algorithm’s theory is deferred; empirical validation is limited and small-scale.
- Trajectory-dependent condition (2) is not checkable a priori; practical guidance relies on the corollary and heuristic.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The theoretical framework and adaptive idea are interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper needs careful correction of equations and stronger discrete-time evidence. If the authors fix the noted issues and add a concise discrete guarantee plus more compelling experiments, this would be a solid NIPS contribution.
