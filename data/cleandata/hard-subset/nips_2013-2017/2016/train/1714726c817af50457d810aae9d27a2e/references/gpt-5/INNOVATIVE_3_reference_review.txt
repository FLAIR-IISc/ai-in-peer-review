Summary
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) through a generalized “averaging” lens. It introduces a family of first-order ODEs parameterized by two positive weight functions w(t) (averaging) and η(t) (gradient accumulation), and analyzes them via a Lyapunov function L_r(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + D_{ψ*}(Z,z*), where r(t) encodes a target rate. The main result gives simple sufficient conditions (η ≥ r′ and a trajectory-dependent inner-product condition) guaranteeing that L_r is nonincreasing and that f(X(t))−f* ≤ C/r(t). A constructive corollary shows how to choose w and η to meet a desired rate (e.g., r(t)=t^p), using the ratio a(t)=w/W. The paper also provides a second “energy” function E_r that decreases under the same conditions, a primal-only representation connecting to known inertial/variational formulations, and concrete instances for positive-orthant and simplex constraints. A particularly nice application is an “accelerated replicator” dynamics on the simplex.

Building on this, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) on the fly based on the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ so as to further decrease L_r without sacrificing the 1/r(t) rate. A discrete-time scheme is given for r(t)=t^2 (with a Lyapunov-style analysis deferred to the supplement). Experiments on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems compare favorably against gradient and speed restarts.

Relation to prior work
- Connects to Su–Boyd–Candès’ ODE for Nesterov acceleration and to Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett’s AMD interpretation via averaging. The generalized weighting/averaging and Lyapunov construction unify and extend these.
- The primal reformulation relates to the Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan variational/Bregman Lagrangian perspective; the authors make this connection explicit (e.g., a(t)=e^α, r(t)=e^β).
- Restart heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès; Su–Boyd–Candès) are contrasted both conceptually and empirically.
- Links to mirror descent, replicator dynamics, and entropy geometry are well-situated.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual contribution: a clean, general averaging framework with explicit rate-preserving conditions and a constructive recipe to realize prescribed rates.
- Elegant Lyapunov analysis that is simple yet flexible; the second energy function E_r adds interpretability (potential + kinetic) and diagnostic value.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic is intuitive, principled (it directly reduces the Lyapunov derivative), and addresses a practical gap versus ad-hoc restarts; it preserves the target rate in continuous time.
- The acceleration of replicator dynamics on the simplex is an appealing and potentially impactful bridge between optimization and evolutionary dynamics.
- Clear existence/uniqueness discussion; good linkage between dual and primal forms and geometry via ψ/ψ*.

Weaknesses
- The crucial condition (2) in Theorem 2 is trajectory-dependent and not checkable a priori; the main usable case becomes the corollary (η = w r/W, w/W ≥ r′/r). While sufficient, this slightly narrows the generality in practice.
- The discrete-time algorithmic analysis for adaptive averaging is relegated to the supplement. The main paper would benefit from at least a self-contained theorem with assumptions and a proof sketch, especially since the contribution’s novelty is largely algorithmic/practical.
- Experiments are limited to 3D simplex problems; conclusions about practical benefits would be stronger with higher-dimensional tasks (e.g., logistic regression, sparse softmax losses), different geometries (Euclidean ψ, box/simplex/ℓ1-balls), and comparisons to strong baselines (FISTA with/without adaptive restart, heavy-ball with restart).
- Parameterization details (choice/sensitivity of β, step size s, γ, regularizer R) and how to set them without tuning are not fully addressed. Any backtracking or line-search variant would be useful for robustness.
- Strongly convex case: the framework hints at faster-than-1/t^2 rates via r(t)=e^{μ t} or tp with restarts, but concrete discrete-time results and guarantees are not provided.

Questions/clarifications for authors
- Can you provide an explicit discrete-time theorem (in the main text) for Algorithm 1: rate, assumptions (L, strong convexity optional), and constants?
- How sensitive is performance to β and s? Could one design an adaptive rule for η(t) as well?
- For strongly convex f, can adaptive averaging achieve linear rates in discrete time without higher-order steps? Any connection to Nesterov’s constant-step AGD with adaptive restart?
- In the simplex experiments, how does accelerated replicator with adaptive averaging compare to Euclidean AGD with projection (with/without restart) for the same step budgets?
- Can the adaptive scheme be extended to stochastic gradients, and would the Lyapunov decrease accommodate noise via averaging/variance control?

Minor comments
- A brief table mapping special choices of ψ to induced geometry and the operator ∇^2ψ*∘∇ψ would aid readability.
- Clarify the role of R in Algorithm 1 and its relation to ψ (e.g., when R=ψ).
- A schematic showing how a(t) segments translate to w(t) growth (exponential vs polynomial) is helpful; you mention this but a figure would solidify intuition.

Evaluation
- Quality: Good theoretical development; algorithmic analysis partly deferred; experiments limited.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized; some results (discrete-time guarantees) deserve prominence.
- Originality: High; the adaptive averaging and unified Lyapunov-based rate control via generalized weights feel genuinely new.
- Significance: Promising; likely to influence both theoretical views on acceleration and practical heuristics beyond restarts, especially in mirror/geometric settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually new and elegant framework; principled adaptive heuristic with guarantees in continuous time.
- Bridges accelerated optimization and replicator dynamics meaningfully.
- Clear potential for broader impact and follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation and missing main-text discrete-time guarantees.
- Practical deployability (parameter choices, robustness) needs more evidence.
