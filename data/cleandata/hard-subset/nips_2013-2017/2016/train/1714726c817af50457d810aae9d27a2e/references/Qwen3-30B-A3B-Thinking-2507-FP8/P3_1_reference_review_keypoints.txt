- Proposes a family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) continuous-time dynamics parameterized by averaging weight w and accumulation rate η.  
- Provides a unified Lyapunov-based analysis giving sufficient conditions for specified convergence rates.  
- Introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes weights along trajectories while preserving convergence rates.  
- Applies the framework to accelerate replicator dynamics on the simplex.  
- Theoretical analysis and proofs are rigorous and well-structured.  
- Clear definition and use of a Lyapunov function, with explicit sufficient conditions (Theorem 2, Corollary 1).  
- Well-motivated examples for constraint sets like the positive orthant and simplex.  
- Empirical validation shows the adaptive heuristic outperforms restarting methods in tested examples.  
- Continuous-time analysis is not convincingly justified as necessary for the discrete algorithm implementation.  
- The connection between the continuous-time framework and the discrete Algorithm 1 is insufficiently explicit.  
- Insufficient comparison to existing adaptive methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) in theory and experiments.  
- Numerical experiments are limited to low-dimensional (R^3) problems and do not demonstrate scalability.  
- Not clearly differentiated from prior work linking acceleration and averaging (Krichene et al., Su et al.).  
- The adaptive heuristic resembles existing adaptive schemes, and distinctions/improvements are not fully clarified.  
- Relation to discretization-focused work (Wibisono et al.) needs clearer positioning.  
- Significance is limited: results are incremental and practical impact on large-scale ML is not demonstrated.  
- Interesting connection to replicator dynamics, but practical applications and impact are not articulated.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound and publishable but incremental; reviewer recommends weak acceptance.  
- Suggested improvements: stronger justification for continuous-time approach, larger-scale experiments, detailed comparison with adaptive optimizers, and clearer positioning vs. prior work.
