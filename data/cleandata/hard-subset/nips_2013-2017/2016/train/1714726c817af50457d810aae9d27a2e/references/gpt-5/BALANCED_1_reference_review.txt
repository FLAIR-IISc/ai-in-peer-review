Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated first-order dynamics for convex optimization over a closed convex set X. Building on the mirror-descent viewpoint, it introduces a two-parameter family of accelerated mirror descent ODEs AMDw,η with generalized averaging weights w(t) (used in forming the averaged primal variable X(t)) and an accumulation rate η(t) for the dual variable Z(t). The main analytical device is a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), where r(t) is a user-chosen target rate function. Theorem 2 provides sufficient conditions on w, η, r under which Lr is non-increasing along trajectories, yielding the continuous-time convergence bound f(X(t))−f* ≤ Lr(X(t0),Z(t0),t0)/r(t). A constructive corollary shows how to choose w and η to achieve any power rate r(t)=t^p (p≥1). The paper also presents a “kinetic+potential” energy function Er that is monotone under the same conditions, gives a primal formulation of the dynamics, and instantiates examples (positive orthant; simplex), yielding an “accelerated replicator” ODE.

Motivated by improving empirical performance while preserving guarantees, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t)=w(t)/W(t) online to decrease d/dt Lr whenever possible. A discretization for the p=2 case leads to Algorithm 1 (an accelerated mirror-descent scheme with an adaptive schedule ak), and numerical experiments on simplex-constrained problems in R^3 compare the heuristic favorably to restart schemes (gradient and speed restarts).

The work relates to: continuous-time views of Nesterov acceleration (Su, Boyd, Candès; Attouch et al.), the Bregman Lagrangian/variational perspective (Wibisono, Wilson, Jordan), mirror descent ODEs (Nemirovski & Yudin), acceleration-as-averaging (Flammarion & Bach), and restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès). The primal form reveals a clear connection to the family in [23]; the simplex case connects to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory.

Strengths
- Unifying viewpoint: AMDw,η provides a clean, flexible family of accelerated continuous-time dynamics where the desired rate r(t) enters the Lyapunov directly. The sufficient conditions are simple and constructive.
- Conceptual clarity: The Lyapunov/energy analysis is concise, and the primal representation links the operator ∇^2ψ*∘∇ψ to feasibility-preserving gradient transforms with interpretable examples (orthant, simplex/replicator).
- Adaptive idea: The adaptive averaging heuristic is intuitive (tune a to decrease the Lyapunov derivative), preserves the continuous-time rate by construction, and contrasts meaningfully with restart heuristics.
- Empirical illustration: Experiments corroborate monotonic decrease of Lr under the heuristic and show reduced oscillations versus fixed averaging and restarts.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to variational ODEs: The primal formulation shows AMDw,η essentially recovers the ODE family of [23] by setting a(t)=e^{α(t)}, r(t)=e^{β(t)}, η(t)=a(t)r(t). The generalization via weights and the Lyapunov proof are neat but may be seen as incremental conceptually. The main distinctive contribution is the adaptive averaging heuristic rather than the continuous-time family itself.
- Trajectory-dependent condition: The key condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on ⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩ and is not checkable a priori. The constructive corollary removes this dependence but restricts the choice of w,η; the adaptive heuristic reintroduces trajectory dependence and requires access to a sign condition or proxies.
- Discretization guarantees: The claim that the discrete algorithm preserves the 1/k^2 rate under adaptivity is deferred to the supplement and appears limited to the r(t)=t^2 case and a specific mirror/prox setup. There is no non-asymptotic complexity bound with explicit constants or robustness to step-size choices. Extensions to p>2 or strongly convex (linear-rate) settings are not provided.
- Empirical scope: Experiments are 2D-simplex (R^3) visualizations with entropy geometry and deterministic gradients. There is no large-scale evaluation, stochastic/noisy gradients, diverse geometries, or wall-clock/runtime analysis. Hyperparameter sensitivity (β, s, γ, choice of R) is not explored.
- Clarity/minor issues: Some derivations are deferred; Algorithm 1 leaves γ, R unspecified beyond convexity/smoothness. There are minor notational slips (see below).

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis appears sound and transparent; existence/uniqueness is handled carefully (start at t0>0). However, the discrete-time theory for adaptivity is not developed in the main text and is limited in scope.
Clarity
- Generally well written with helpful examples and intuition (energy interpretation, replicator). Some implementation details (parameter choices; computing the sign proxy for adaptivity) could be clearer. Minor typos noted below.
Originality
- Moderate. The adaptive averaging heuristic and its link to preserving a Lyapunov function in accelerated mirror descent is the most original aspect. The ODE family and rate construction largely overlap with the variational perspective.
Significance
- Moderate. The framework may inform algorithm design, and the adaptive heuristic is practically interesting. The current empirical and discrete-theory scope is limited, which tempers impact.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Clarify relation to [23]: Precisely state the mapping between α,β in [23] and your w,η,r, and delineate what is new (e.g., the Lyapunov template with generalized averaging and the adaptive policy).
- Discrete guarantees: Move at least a concise theorem and proof sketch from the supplement to the main paper for Algorithm 1 (assumptions on ψ, R, step sizes, and the claimed O(1/k^2) rate under adaptivity). Discuss stability, parameter ranges, and whether the monotone Lyapunov property holds in discrete time under your test.
- Strongly convex case: Provide theory (or at least empirical) evidence on whether adaptive averaging yields faster than O(1/k^2), e.g., O((1−c/k)^k) in continuous time or linear rates in discrete time, and how it compares to restart schedules with provable linear rates.
- Practical diagnostics: In the heuristic, the continuous-time condition uses ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩. In Algorithm 1 you trigger adaptivity based on f(x̃(k+1)) > f(x̃(k)). Justify this proxy theoretically or provide ablations comparing alternative diagnostics (e.g., gradient inner product, velocity norm).
- Experiments: Add higher-dimensional benchmarks (e.g., entropy geometry on larger simplices, Euclidean geometry with box constraints), wall-clock comparisons, sensitivity to β and s, and stochastic/noisy gradients.
- Minor/typos:
  - Example 1: r′(t)/r(t)=2/t, so a(t) should be β/t (β≥2), not “β t”. The subsequent derivation of w(t)=(t/t0)^{β−1} is consistent with a(t)=β/t.
  - In Section 2, “w(t) aẊ” likely means with a(t)=w(t)/W(t).
  - Some references lack full bibliographic details; ensure consistency with NeurIPS formatting.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, unified Lyapunov analysis; constructive rate design.
- Elegant connection to replicator dynamics; energy interpretation.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and empirically promising; preserves Lyapunov decrease.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental over variational ODE framework; main novelty is heuristic.
- Limited discrete-time theory and empirical scope; small-scale experiments only.
- Trajectory-dependent conditions and implementation proxies not fully justified.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper offers a neat and potentially useful perspective and a promising heuristic, but the discrete-time guarantees are limited, the experimental evidence is narrow, and the contribution over existing continuous-time frameworks seems modest. Strengthening the discrete analysis, broadening experiments, and clarifying the relation to [23] would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
