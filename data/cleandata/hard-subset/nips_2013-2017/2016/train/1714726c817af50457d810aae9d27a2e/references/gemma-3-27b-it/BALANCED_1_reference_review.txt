This paper investigates accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics, proposing a generalized framework and a novel adaptive averaging heuristic. The authors build upon their previous work connecting Nesterov's acceleration to continuous-time ODEs, seeking to understand if different averaging schemes can yield comparable or improved convergence rates. They establish sufficient conditions for Lyapunov stability and demonstrate the preservation of a desired convergence rate, and introduce an adaptive weighting scheme guided by the rate of change of the Lyapunov function. Numerical experiments validate the performance of the proposed heuristic.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of AMD dynamics, extending previous work and offering a unified framework. The Lyapunov function analysis is rigorous and provides clear sufficient conditions for convergence.
* **Novelty of Approach:** The generalized averaging scheme and the adaptive weighting heuristic are novel contributions. The idea of adaptively adjusting weights based on the Lyapunov function's derivative is insightful and potentially impactful.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper effectively connects to a rich body of literature on accelerated methods, continuous-time dynamics, and related fields like evolutionary game theory.  The authors carefully place their work within this context.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The numerical experiments are well-designed, comparing the adaptive averaging heuristic to established restarting methods across different objective functions and constraint sets. The inclusion of visualizations of trajectories and Lyapunov/energy function values enhances understanding.
* **Primal Representation:**  Deriving the primal representation of the AMD dynamics provides useful insight and could be valuable for algorithm design and implementation.  The example regarding the replicator equation is particularly compelling.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the mathematical derivations and understand the key ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Practical Impact of Theory:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the conditions for the convergence rate preservation (Corollary 1) might be restrictive and difficult to verify in practice. The practical benefit of guaranteeing the same convergence rate when other heuristics might empirically perform better isn’t fully highlighted.
* **Heuristic Justification:** The rationale behind the adaptive averaging heuristic, while intuitively plausible, could benefit from further justification. Why specifically decreasing the Lyapunov derivative? Is there a theoretical argument suggesting this is optimal or at least a good strategy?
* **Experiment Scope:**  The numerical experiments primarily focus on the simplex constraint. Expanding the experiments to include other common constraint sets (e.g., L1 ball, box constraints) would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons to existing restarting heuristics are provided, a comparison to more recent and sophisticated adaptive methods like Adam or variants of accelerated gradient descent with adaptive learning rates would be valuable.
* **Lack of Computational Complexity Analysis:**  The paper doesn’t discuss the computational cost of implementing the adaptive averaging heuristic compared to other methods. This is a crucial consideration for practical adoption.
* **Potential for Tighter Bounds:** The authors mention that for faster rates r(t) = t^p, p > 2,  discretization preserving the rate requires higher-order methods.  Exploring potential discretization schemes that *approximate* the rate while remaining first-order might be a valuable direction.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel contribution to the understanding of accelerated mirror descent dynamics.
* The adaptive averaging heuristic shows promising performance in the numerical experiments.
* The connections to related work are well-established, and the paper builds upon a strong foundation of existing research.
* The clarity of the presentation and the comprehensiveness of the experiments are commendable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical impact of the theoretical results may be limited due to restrictive conditions.
* The justification for the adaptive averaging heuristic could be strengthened.
* The scope of the numerical experiments is somewhat limited.
* The lack of comparison to state-of-the-art adaptive methods leaves some questions unanswered.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a solid theoretical foundation and promising results. While the practical impact of the theoretical guarantees may be limited, the novel adaptive averaging heuristic and the insightful analysis of AMD dynamics are valuable contributions to the field.  I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest:

1. **Discussing the practical limitations of the convergence rate conditions:** Acknowledge the potential difficulty in verifying these conditions and exploring how the heuristic performs even when they are not strictly met.
2. **Providing a more rigorous justification for the adaptive averaging heuristic:**  Explore connections to optimization principles or control theory that support the chosen adaptation strategy.
3. **Expanding the numerical experiments:** Include experiments with a wider range of constraint sets and compare the performance to more recent adaptive methods.
4. **Adding a brief discussion on the computational complexity:**  Analyze the overhead of implementing the adaptive weighting scheme.
