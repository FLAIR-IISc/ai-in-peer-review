## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics

**Summary:**

This paper investigates continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order optimization methods, building upon recent work connecting Nesterov acceleration to ODEs. The authors generalize previous formulations to a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions `w` and `η`, analyzing their convergence properties using a Lyapunov function. A key contribution is demonstrating that different averaging schemes can achieve quadratic convergence, and proposing a novel adaptive heuristic for computing `w(t)` that provably preserves the convergence rate.  The paper also provides primal representations of the dynamics and applies them to specific constraint sets like the positive orthant and the simplex, deriving an accelerated replicator dynamic in the latter case. Numerical experiments validate the performance of adaptive averaging against existing restarting heuristics.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly on Su et al. (2016) [20] and Krichene et al. (2016) [11], extending their ODE interpretations of accelerated methods to a more general framework. It's situated within a growing body of literature exploring continuous-time analysis of optimization algorithms (e.g., Attouch et al., 2015 [2], Brown & Bartholomew-Biggs, 1982 [5]), motivated by its analytical tractability and insights into discrete methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is positioned against existing heuristics like gradient and speed restarting (e.g., O’Donoghue & Candès, 2015 [17]; Su et al., 2016 [20]), and connects to adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam ([7, 10]), though the authors rightly emphasize the distinction in their focus on acceleration. The application to the replicator dynamics draws connections to evolutionary game theory and viability theory ([22, 4]).

**Strengths:**

* **Generalization and Rigor:** The generalization of AMD dynamics and the subsequent Lyapunov analysis offer a significant theoretical contribution. The conditions for convergence are clearly stated and reasonably accessible.
* **Adaptive Averaging Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a major strength. The provable convergence rate preservation is a valuable guarantee lacking in many existing heuristics. The intuition behind adapting weights based on Lyapunov function decrease is compelling.
* **Primal Representation and Applications:**  The derivation of primal representations is helpful for understanding the dynamics and facilitates application to specific problems. The accelerated replicator dynamic on the simplex is a particularly nice result.
* **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments provide evidence supporting the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging heuristic, demonstrating its competitive performance compared to established techniques. The visualization of trajectories and Lyapunov/Energy functions is insightful.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the numerical experiments are well-executed, they are limited to relatively low-dimensional problems (R3).  Scaling up to higher dimensions would provide more compelling evidence of practical utility.
* **Theoretical Complexity:** The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, can be dense and potentially inaccessible to readers not deeply familiar with convex analysis and differential equations. A more intuitive explanation of the Lyapunov function and its role could be helpful.
* **Choice of r(t):** The discussion regarding the choice of the convergence rate function `r(t)` could be expanded. While the examples with `r(t) = t^2` are illustrative, a more thorough exploration of different rate functions and their implications would strengthen the analysis.
* **Comparison to Existing Adaptive Methods:** While the paper acknowledges Adagrad and Adam, a more detailed comparison of their mechanisms and potential applicability to accelerated methods would be beneficial.  Could the adaptive averaging heuristic be combined with adaptive learning rate schemes?
* **Discretization Details:** The description of the discretization in section 4 feels somewhat abrupt. A more detailed discussion of the chosen discretization scheme and its impact on convergence guarantees would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding continuous-time dynamics for accelerated optimization.
* The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a novel and provably effective approach to improving convergence.
* The results are well-supported by theoretical analysis and numerical experiments.
* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in the optimization community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited scope of the numerical experiments raises concerns about the practical scalability of the proposed method.
* The theoretical complexity may hinder accessibility for some readers.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The generalization of AMD dynamics and the development of the adaptive averaging heuristic are particularly noteworthy. While the limited scope of the experiments and theoretical complexity are minor weaknesses, they do not outweigh the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions to potentially address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly expanding the experimental section and providing a more accessible explanation of the theoretical concepts).

**Confidence:** 8/10
