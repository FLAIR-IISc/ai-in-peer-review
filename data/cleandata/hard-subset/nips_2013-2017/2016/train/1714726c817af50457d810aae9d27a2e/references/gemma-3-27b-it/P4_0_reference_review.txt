This paper investigates accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics with a broad family of averaging schemes. The authors provide sufficient conditions for convergence, demonstrate an equivalent primal representation, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic with provable convergence guarantees. The work builds upon prior research connecting Nesterov’s accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs, notably [20, 11], and expands the understanding of acceleration and averaging in optimization.

The paper is well-structured and logically presented. The authors clearly define the problem, provide necessary background, and build upon previous work in a coherent manner. The derivation of the AMD dynamics and the Lyapunov function analysis are technically sound. The presentation of the primal representation in Section 3, with examples for specific constraint sets like the simplex, is insightful and demonstrates the applicability of the framework. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic in Section 4 appears promising and the provided guarantees are valuable. The numerical experiments in Section 5 offer some empirical support for the proposed approach, comparing it with established restarting heuristics.

A key strength is the generality of the framework. The authors effectively broaden the scope of AMD by considering a wide class of averaging weight functions, providing a more unified analysis and identifying conditions for achieving desired convergence rates.  The connection to replicator dynamics via the simplex constraint example is a notable contribution, linking optimization techniques to concepts in evolutionary game theory. The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 2 and Corollary 1, offer concrete guidance for designing effective averaging schemes.  The analysis of the Lyapunov and Energy functions provides useful insights into the dynamics of the optimization process. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The numerical experiments, while demonstrating the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging, are somewhat limited in scope. They focus primarily on the simplex constraint and relatively simple objective functions.  More comprehensive experiments with diverse problem settings (e.g., different dimensions, different constraint sets, strongly convex vs. non-convex objectives) would strengthen the empirical validation. The discussion of related work, while present, could be more thorough. For example, connections to online learning algorithms like Adagrad and Adam [7, 10] are briefly mentioned but could be elaborated upon, particularly regarding the differences in their design goals and theoretical properties compared to the proposed adaptive averaging.  The discussion regarding discretization schemes for achieving faster rates (referencing [23]) lacks detail.  While acknowledging the existence of such schemes, it doesn't delve into the challenges or trade-offs associated with them, leaving the reader with an incomplete picture. The notation could be slightly improved for readability, particularly in the primal representation (Section 3).  For instance, consistently denoting the primal variable as ‘x’ instead of switching between ‘X’ and ‘x’ might enhance clarity. Finally, while the convergence rate is preserved, the paper doesn’t explore if the adaptive averaging can lead to *faster* convergence than fixed-weight schemes under certain conditions, beyond what's implied by potentially decreasing the Lyapunov derivative more rapidly.

Arguments for acceptance:

*   **Novelty:** The generalized AMD framework and the adaptive averaging heuristic represent a novel contribution to the field of accelerated optimization.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported, with clear proofs and derivations.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental question in optimization – how to effectively leverage averaging for acceleration – and provides a practical approach with convergence guarantees.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear presentation of ideas.

Arguments against acceptance:

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope and could benefit from more extensive testing.
*   **Incomplete Discussion of Related Work:** The connections to existing adaptive methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) could be explored more thoroughly.
*   **Lack of Exploration of Faster Convergence:** The paper focuses on preserving the convergence rate, but doesn't investigate the potential for achieving faster rates.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the theory and practice of accelerated optimization. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the technical soundness, novelty, and potential significance of the work justify acceptance.  I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS.
