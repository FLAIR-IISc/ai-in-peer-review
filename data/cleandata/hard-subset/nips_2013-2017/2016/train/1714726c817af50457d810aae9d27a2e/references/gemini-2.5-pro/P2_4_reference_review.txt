### **Summary**

This paper presents a generalized framework for continuous-time accelerated mirror descent dynamics, termed `AMDw,η`. This framework parameterizes the dynamics by two functions: `w(t)`, which controls a generalized averaging scheme for the primal variable, and `η(t)`, which scales the gradient accumulation in a dual variable. The authors use a Lyapunov function analysis to derive a simple and elegant sufficient condition on `w` and `η` to guarantee a convergence rate of `f(X(t)) - f* ≤ O(1/r(t))` for a desired rate function `r(t)`. This generalizes their previous work [11] and provides a unified perspective on acceleration as a form of averaging.

Building on this framework, the paper's main practical contribution is a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The heuristic adaptively chooses the averaging weights to maximally decrease the Lyapunov function at each step, while crucially preserving the theoretical `O(1/r(t))` convergence guarantee. The authors provide a discrete version of this heuristic (Algorithm 1) and present numerical experiments on low-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems. The results show that their adaptive method compares favorably to standard restarting heuristics [17, 20], often converging faster and avoiding the non-monotonic behavior of the Lyapunov function observed with restarts.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is technically sound. The Lyapunov analysis in Section 2 is clean, easy to follow, and leads to the main theoretical result (Theorem 2) in a very direct manner. The derivation is elegant and provides clear insight into the conditions required for acceleration. The connection drawn to primal-space dynamics in Section 3, including the derivation of an accelerated replicator equation, is a nice demonstration of the framework's power. The experiments, though limited in scope, clearly support the central claims about the adaptive heuristic's performance and its guarantee of a decreasing Lyapunov function.
*   **Weaknesses:** The theoretical analysis for the *adaptive* heuristic, while guaranteeing it preserves the `O(1/t^2)` rate, does not provide any stronger guarantees. Restarting heuristics are often used with the hope of achieving a linear rate on strongly convex problems. It is unclear if adaptive averaging can achieve this, and this question is not addressed. The proof for the convergence rate of the discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is deferred to the supplementary material, which makes the main paper slightly less self-contained.

#### **Clarity**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear motivation, introduces the general `AMDw,η` framework, provides the key theoretical analysis, discusses concrete examples, and then presents the adaptive heuristic and experiments. The flow is excellent. The mathematical notation is standard and the derivations are presented with sufficient detail. Figure 1 provides a helpful visual schematic of the proposed dynamics.
*   **Weaknesses:** The paper assumes a fair amount of familiarity with concepts from convex analysis like mirror maps, Bregman divergences, and convex conjugates. While this is reasonable for a NIPS audience, a bit more intuition in the main text could have broadened its accessibility. For instance, the discussion of the energy function `Er` and its interpretation as potential + kinetic energy is insightful but could be developed further.

#### **Originality**

*   **Strengths:** The paper makes several original contributions.
    1.  The `AMDw,η` framework is a novel and useful generalization of previous continuous-time models of acceleration [11, 20]. It provides a unified lens through which to view different acceleration schemes.
    2.  The adaptive averaging heuristic is a new idea. Unlike existing restarting heuristics which can violate the conditions for guaranteed convergence rates (as shown in the experiments), this method is designed *from* the Lyapunov analysis to preserve the rate guarantee. This is a significant conceptual advantage.
    3.  The specific application to derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics is, to my knowledge, novel and interesting. It connects the abstract optimization framework to a well-known model from evolutionary game theory.

*   **Weaknesses:** The work is an incremental, albeit significant, step building upon a recent line of research connecting ODEs and accelerated optimization [20, 11, 23]. It uses similar analytical tools (Lyapunov functions) as prior work.

#### **Significance**

*   **Strengths:** The paper's findings are significant for both theory and practice. Theoretically, it deepens our understanding of the role of averaging in acceleration and provides a flexible framework for designing and analyzing new accelerated methods. The continuous-time perspective continues to provide valuable insights. Practically, the adaptive averaging heuristic is a simple, effective, and theoretically-grounded alternative to popular restarting schemes. Given that restarting is widely used to improve the practical performance of accelerated methods, a new competitor with stronger guarantees is an important contribution that researchers and practitioners are likely to use and build upon. The method appears simple to implement.

*   **Weaknesses:** The practical significance is currently limited by the scope of the experiments. The tests are performed on 3D problems. It remains to be seen how the heuristic performs on high-dimensional, large-scale machine learning problems (e.g., logistic regression, SVMs) where methods like Adam and Adagrad (which the paper briefly mentions) are dominant. The comparison to these other adaptive methods is only superficial.

### **Detailed Comments**

*   The condition for adapting `a(t)` in the discrete Algorithm 1 is `f(x̃(k+1)) - f(x̃(k)) > 0`. This seems to be a discrete proxy for the continuous-time condition `⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩ > 0`. It would be helpful to explicitly state this connection and justify this choice of proxy.
*   The experiments convincingly show that `Lr` is non-increasing for adaptive averaging and can increase for restarting. This is a very strong visual argument for the proposed method's stability and is a highlight of the experimental section.

### **Recommendation**

I recommend **Accept**.

The paper presents a clear, elegant, and technically sound generalization of continuous-time accelerated dynamics. The main contribution, an adaptive averaging heuristic derived directly from the theoretical analysis, is novel and significant. It offers a compelling alternative to existing restarting heuristics by preserving theoretical convergence guarantees, a property that is empirically verified in the experiments. While the experimental validation could be more extensive, the paper's theoretical insights and the potential practical utility of the proposed heuristic make it a strong contribution to the optimization and machine learning community.
