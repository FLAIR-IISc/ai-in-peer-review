

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper introduces a novel framework for accelerated optimization methods through the lens of continuous-time dynamics, with a focus on adaptive averaging. The authors generalize the accelerated mirror descent dynamics by introducing a family of ODEs parameterized by weight functions w and η, and provide a unified Lyapunov analysis that establishes sufficient conditions for convergence at a specified rate. The key innovation is their "adaptive averaging heuristic," which dynamically adjusts the weight functions along solution trajectories to preserve convergence guarantees while potentially improving performance. The paper demonstrates how this framework can accelerate the replicator dynamics, a significant connection to evolutionary game theory.

The paper builds on the established line of work connecting Nesterov's acceleration to continuous-time ODEs (Su et al., 2014; Krichene et al., 2015), but significantly extends it by introducing a flexible family of accelerated dynamics with adaptive weights. Previous work on acceleration has primarily focused on fixed-rate schemes or restarting heuristics (O'Donoghue & Candès, 2015; Wibisono et al., 2016), while this paper proposes a fundamentally different approach that adaptively computes the weights to maximize progress.

The theoretical contributions are strong and well-motivated. The authors provide rigorous proofs for the Lyapunov conditions, establish a clear connection between the weight functions and convergence rates, and derive a primal representation that reveals connections to existing dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic is particularly compelling as it preserves the convergence rate guarantees while potentially improving performance - a significant advancement over existing restarting heuristics that often sacrifice theoretical guarantees for empirical gains.

The numerical experiments, while limited to R^3 for visualization purposes, provide convincing evidence that the adaptive averaging heuristic outperforms existing methods (including gradient and speed restarting heuristics) across multiple objective functions. The paper correctly notes that the Lyapunov function remains decreasing under adaptive averaging, unlike restarting heuristics, which is a theoretically important distinction.

The paper's most significant strength is its conceptual novelty - the adaptive averaging approach represents a fresh perspective on acceleration that could influence both continuous-time and discrete-time optimization. While the numerical experiments are somewhat limited, the theoretical foundation is robust enough to justify the claim that this approach provides a new direction for accelerated optimization.

I note that the paper could benefit from more discussion about the computational complexity of implementing the adaptive heuristic in practice, and a more thorough analysis of the discrete-time version. However, given my preference for novel concepts over methodological completeness, these are minor concerns.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a bold new direction that could lead to improved practical algorithms while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The connection to replicator dynamics is particularly elegant and demonstrates the broad applicability of the framework.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a clear distinction from previous work. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the numerical results support the theoretical claims. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of optimization dynamics that should be of interest to the NeurIPS community.

While the paper focuses on continuous-time dynamics, the discrete-time adaptation (Algorithm 1) suggests practical implications that could be valuable for practitioners. The approach could potentially be extended to other accelerated methods beyond mirror descent, opening up new research directions.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for accelerated optimization that extends previous work in a meaningful way. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant conceptual advance that could have both theoretical and practical impact. I believe this paper makes an important contribution to the field and deserves acceptance at NeurIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**
