This paper investigates accelerated mirror descent dynamics with a focus on generalized averaging schemes. The authors aim to provide a unified analysis and demonstrate that various averaging strategies can achieve, and potentially improve upon, existing convergence rates. They introduce a Lyapunov function and derive sufficient conditions for convergence, culminating in an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate. While the topic is relevant to NIPS, and builds upon established work in continuous-time optimization and acceleration, the presentation suffers from a multitude of minor flaws that collectively detract from its overall impact.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Topic:** The study of continuous-time dynamics and accelerated methods is a vital area within optimization, particularly concerning large-scale machine learning problems.
*   **Unified Framework:** The authors attempt to provide a unified analysis of accelerated mirror descent using a generalized averaging scheme, which is a valuable goal.
*   **Lyapunov Analysis:** The use of a Lyapunov function to establish convergence guarantees is a standard and rigorous approach.
*   **Adaptive Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic, which aims to preserve the convergence rate, shows promise and is a worthwhile contribution.
*   **Connection to Replicator Dynamics:** Deriving an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics is a nice application of the framework.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Novelty in Core Ideas:** The fundamental connection between averaging and acceleration isn’t entirely novel (acknowledged by the authors referencing [8]), and the proposed framework, while general, doesn’t present a groundbreaking theoretical leap.
*   **Overabundance of Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:** Throughout the paper, there's a disturbing number of small errors. For example, the initialization condition in the AMD dynamics (1) includes 't0' which isn’t consistently used or explained relative to the standard continuous-time setup that usually starts at t=0.  The notation is often inconsistent (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface for variables).
*   **Insufficient Explanation:**  Crucial aspects are glossed over.  The justification for initializing the ODE at t0 > 0 is briefly mentioned but lacks a detailed explanation impacting readability. The discussion of the Bregman divergence lacks sufficient detail for a reader unfamiliar with the concept.
*   **Unclear Motivation for Specific Choices:** The choice of the Lyapunov function (2) is presented without a clear rationale beyond being non-negative. A deeper explanation of *why* this particular function is suitable would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Weak Empirical Evaluation:** While the numerical experiments provide some evidence for the adaptive averaging heuristic, they are not rigorous enough. The comparison to restarting heuristics is limited in scope, and the results aren't presented with sufficient statistical rigor. Simply *showing* the plots isn’t enough; quantitative comparisons are needed. The claim of "significant improvement" lacks concrete supporting data.  The experiments are also limited to R3 which isn't sufficient to establish generalizability.
*   **Overly Dense Writing Style:** The paper is overly dense and difficult to read. Sentences are often long and complex, and the overall flow of ideas is disrupted by unnecessary technical details.
*   **Incomplete References:** Some references are cited but not fully detailed in the bibliography, a significant oversight. (e.g., [6, 9, 5]).
*   **Discrepancy in Notation:** The shift between AMDw,η and AMD′w,η is confusing and not well motivated. Using primed notation suggests a derivative or a modified version, but the authors don’t clearly explain the relationship.

**Specific Examples of Issues:**

*   In the introduction, the equation for X(t) contains a missing integral bound, creating ambiguity.
*   The explanation of the mirror map's connection to convex analysis feels cursory and lacks sufficient detail.
*   The discretization of the heuristic in Section 4 is presented without enough justification for the specific choices made, and it lacks a clear discussion of the approximation error introduced by the discretization.
*   The discussion of related work feels superficial; the authors don't thoroughly engage with the existing literature on adaptive optimization methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in optimization.
*   The Lyapunov analysis provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic has the potential to improve the performance of accelerated methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks significant novelty beyond extending existing ideas.
*   The numerous minor errors and inconsistencies detract from the overall quality of the work.
*   The empirical evaluation is weak and lacks statistical rigor.
*   The writing style is dense and difficult to follow.
*   The overall presentation is sloppy, suggesting a lack of meticulousness.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the ideas presented, the numerous flaws, particularly the pervasive lack of attention to detail, lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the core contributions aren't entirely without merit, the paper's deficiencies significantly outweigh its strengths. A substantial revision, addressing all the issues outlined above (and likely uncovering others during a thorough rewrite), would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors should prioritize clarity, rigor, and a more comprehensive evaluation of their method. The level of polish expected for NIPS is currently not met.
