### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order optimization of convex functions. Building on prior work that connects Nesterov's accelerated method to a second-order ODE with a specific averaging scheme (e.g., Su et al., 2015; Krichene et al., 2015), this work generalizes this connection significantly. The authors introduce a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by two general, positive weight functions: `w(t)` for the averaging scheme and `η(t)` for the gradient accumulation rate.

The core theoretical contribution is a unified analysis of this family of ODEs via a Lyapunov function `L_r`. The authors derive simple and elegant sufficient conditions on `w`, `η`, and a desired rate function `r(t)` that guarantee the dynamics converge with a rate of `1/r(t)`. This provides a principled framework for designing and analyzing accelerated dynamics.

Based on this theoretical insight, the paper proposes a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The goal is to choose the averaging weights `w(t)` adaptively to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, thereby potentially improving empirical performance. Crucially, and in contrast to popular restarting heuristics, this adaptive scheme is designed to provably preserve the theoretical `1/r(t)` convergence rate for general convex problems. The paper illustrates the framework by deriving an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained optimization and provides a discrete-time implementation of the adaptive heuristic. Finally, numerical experiments on low-dimensional problems compare the proposed method against standard accelerated mirror descent and two restarting heuristics, showing favorable performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant Generalization and Unification:** The paper provides a significant and elegant generalization of the continuous-time view of accelerated optimization. Moving from a specific averaging weight (`t^(r-1)`) to a general family of dynamics parameterized by `w(t)` and `η(t)` is a strong conceptual contribution. The unified Lyapunov analysis (Theorem 2) that yields simple sufficient conditions for a desired `1/r(t)` convergence rate is a powerful and insightful result.

2.  **Principled and Novel Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a key strength. It is directly and cleverly motivated by the continuous-time analysis, aiming to make the term `⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩(r(t) - η(t)W(t)/w(t))` as negative as possible. This provides a principled way to improve performance, unlike more ad-hoc restarting schemes. The fact that this heuristic comes with a formal guarantee that the Lyapunov function remains non-increasing (and thus the convergence rate is preserved) is a major advantage over restarting methods, whose guarantees often apply only to specific function classes (e.g., quadratics or strongly convex).

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. It smoothly transitions from the general continuous-time theory (Section 2), to concrete examples and primal representations (Section 3), and finally to the practical adaptive heuristic and its experimental validation (Sections 4 and 5). The mathematical derivations are clear and easy to follow.

4.  **Instructive Examples and Experiments:** The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics is an excellent example that grounds the abstract framework in a well-known dynamical system from other fields. The experiments, while simple, are effective. Visualizing not only the objective function but also the Lyapunov function `L_r` and the energy `E_r` provides strong empirical validation of the theory and gives clear insight into the behavior of the different methods.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Continuous-to-Discrete Gap:** The primary theoretical results are for continuous-time ODEs, while the proposed algorithm is discrete. The link between the condition `⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩ > 0` that motivates the continuous-time adaptation and the condition `f(x̃(k+1)) - f(x̃(k)) > 0` used in Algorithm 1 feels heuristic. A more formal justification for this choice and a more detailed discussion of the discrete-time analysis would strengthen the paper. While the supplementary material is mentioned, bringing some of that analysis into the main text, especially a discrete version of the Lyapunov argument, would make the paper more self-contained and convincing.

2.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are confined to 3D simplex-constrained problems. While this is excellent for visualization, it doesn't demonstrate the method's effectiveness on larger-scale problems commonly found in machine learning. Including experiments on standard ML tasks (e.g., logistic regression or SVMs on benchmark datasets) would substantially increase the paper's perceived impact and significance to the NIPS community.

3.  **Discussion on Strong Convexity:** Restarting heuristics are particularly effective for strongly convex problems, where they can help achieve a linear convergence rate. The paper's experiments show a significant gain for the adaptive method on a strongly convex quadratic, which is very promising. However, the theoretical discussion is limited to the `O(1/t^2)` rate for general convex functions. A discussion of whether adaptive averaging could also provably or empirically achieve faster rates for strongly convex functions would be a valuable addition. This is a key point of comparison with restarting, and the current paper does not fully address it.

4.  **Comparison with Other Adaptive Methods:** The paper briefly mentions and dismisses methods like Adagrad and Adam. While their mechanism is different (adapting a per-coordinate learning rate), they are nonetheless the most widely used "adaptive" methods in practice. A more nuanced discussion of the relationship, or even an empirical comparison, would be helpful. For instance, are there problem types where adaptive averaging is superior to Adam, and vice-versa? Clarifying this would better position the work within the broader optimization landscape.

### Decision

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong and elegant theoretical contribution. The generalization of accelerated dynamics and the development of a principled adaptive heuristic with convergence guarantees are significant achievements. The weaknesses identified above are primarily related to the scope of the experiments and the depth of the discussion on certain topics, rather than fundamental flaws in the core ideas. The paper clearly meets the high standards of NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper presents novel, sound, and significant research that advances our understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The suggestions above are intended to help the authors further strengthen an already excellent paper for the final version.
