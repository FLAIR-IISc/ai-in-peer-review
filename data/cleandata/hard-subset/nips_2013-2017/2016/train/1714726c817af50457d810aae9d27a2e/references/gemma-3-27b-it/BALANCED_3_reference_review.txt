This paper investigates continuous-time dynamics for accelerated optimization, specifically focusing on Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) with generalized averaging schemes. The authors build upon their previous work connecting Nesterov’s acceleration to continuous-time ODEs and demonstrate sufficient conditions for a broad family of AMD dynamics to achieve a specific convergence rate, formalized through a Lyapunov function. They further propose an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to preserve this convergence rate and show empirical improvements over existing restarting heuristics.

The core idea of exploring different averaging schemes within the AMD framework is novel and a natural extension of prior work [11, 20]. The Lyapunov function-based analysis provides a solid theoretical foundation, offering sufficient conditions for convergence and demonstrating the connection between the weighting function and the convergence rate. The derivation of primal representations of the dynamics and the connection to the replicator equation for simplex-constrained problems are valuable contributions, showcasing the versatility of the framework. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a compelling extension, and the empirical results offer promising evidence of its effectiveness. 

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds on established theoretical frameworks (mirror descent, Lyapunov stability) and provides rigorous analysis of the proposed dynamics.
* **Generality:** The authors explore a broad family of averaging schemes, providing a generalized perspective on AMD.
* **Novelty:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution, offering a method to dynamically adjust averaging weights based on solution trajectory behavior.
* **Connections to Other Fields:** The derivation of the replicator equation for simplex constraints highlights the applicability of the framework to evolutionary game theory and related areas.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the potential advantages of the adaptive averaging heuristic.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical concepts and derivations, even complex ones.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical experiments, while promising, are somewhat limited. More extensive testing on a broader range of problem types and dimensions would strengthen the empirical claims. The comparison against stronger baselines (e.g., more sophisticated adaptive methods like Adam) is lacking.
* **Practical Considerations of the Heuristic:** The adaptive averaging heuristic, while theoretically sound, introduces additional computational overhead due to the need to evaluate the Lyapunov function derivative. A discussion of this practical cost and its potential impact on performance is missing.
* **Discussion of the Constant C in Convergence Rate:** The paper states the convergence rate is  f(X(t))− f? ≤ C/t2. The dependence of 'C' on initial conditions and other parameters isn’t fully explored. A deeper understanding of this constant is crucial for practical applications.
* **Assumptions on Differentiability:**  The analysis relies on assumptions of differentiability (specifically twice differentiability for the primal representation). Discussing the robustness of the results to weaker assumptions or alternative scenarios would enhance the paper.
* **Related Work Gaps:** While the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, it could benefit from acknowledging more recent advances in adaptive optimization methods beyond Adam and Adagrad, particularly those inspired by momentum and variance reduction techniques.


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of accelerated optimization.
* The Lyapunov-based analysis provides strong guarantees on convergence rates.
* The adaptive averaging heuristic is a promising direction for improving the performance of AMD.
* The connections to other fields (evolutionary game theory) broaden the impact of the work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The empirical evaluation is limited, and the practical impact of the adaptive averaging heuristic is not fully demonstrated.
* The additional computational cost associated with the heuristic is not addressed.
* The paper doesn’t fully address potential limitations related to the assumptions on differentiability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution and has the potential to significantly impact the field of accelerated optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the ideas are novel, and the empirical results are encouraging. However, the limitations in the empirical evaluation and the lack of discussion regarding practical considerations raise some concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:

1. **Expand the numerical experiments:**  Include more diverse problem types, higher dimensions, and comparisons against stronger baselines (e.g., Adam, other adaptive methods).
2. **Discuss the computational cost:** Provide an analysis of the additional computational overhead introduced by the adaptive averaging heuristic.
3. **Clarify the dependence of the constant C:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of the constant 'C' in the convergence rate and its relationship to problem parameters.
4. **Address differentiability assumptions:** Discuss the robustness of the results under weaker assumptions.
5. **Strengthen Related Work:**  Expand the related work section to include more recent advancements in adaptive optimization.
