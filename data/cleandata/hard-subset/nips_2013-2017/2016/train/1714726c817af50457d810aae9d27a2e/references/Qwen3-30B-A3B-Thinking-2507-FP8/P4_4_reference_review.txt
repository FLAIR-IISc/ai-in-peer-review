

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated optimization, specifically generalizing the Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) framework. The authors propose a broad family of AMD dynamics parameterized by two weight functions w and η, which govern the averaging and gradient accumulation rates, respectively. They provide a unified Lyapunov analysis to establish convergence rates and introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate while improving empirical performance. The paper builds on previous work by Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11], extending their continuous-time ODE framework to a more general class of dynamics.

The paper's main contribution is a sufficient condition for a Lyapunov function to guarantee a desired convergence rate, which generalizes previous results. The authors demonstrate that their framework can accelerate the replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory, providing a new perspective on this established class of dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic, which adaptively computes weight functions to maintain the Lyapunov function's decreasing property, is shown to outperform existing restarting heuristics in numerical experiments.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-constructed proofs for the main theorems (Theorems 2 and 3). The authors carefully establish conditions under which the Lyapunov function decreases, and they provide clear examples (positive-orthant and simplex-constrained dynamics) that illustrate the theory. The experimental section (Section 5) provides empirical validation of the adaptive averaging heuristic against existing restarting methods, with clear visualizations of objective function values, trajectories, and energy functions. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for continuous-time analysis to inform discrete-time algorithms, and discusses the relationship between the Lyapunov function and energy function.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the key concepts (e.g., Lyapunov function, mirror maps, Bregman divergence) are properly defined. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, including the discrete-time version of the adaptive averaging heuristic (Algorithm 1). The examples in Section 3 effectively illustrate the theoretical framework, and the figures (Figure 1 and Figure 2) enhance understanding of the dynamics. The connection to previous work is well-explained, with appropriate citations.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by generalizing the AMD framework to include a broad family of averaging schemes. While the connection between acceleration and averaging has been observed in prior work (e.g., [8]), this paper provides a rigorous continuous-time framework that unifies previous results and extends them to a broader class of dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel compared to existing restarting heuristics [17, 20], as it preserves the convergence rate while adaptively choosing weight functions. The extension to replicator dynamics (Section 3.2) is also a meaningful application that demonstrates the framework's utility beyond standard optimization problems.

**Significance:** The continuous-time framework for accelerated optimization is significant as it provides deeper theoretical insights into the behavior of accelerated methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic addresses a practical challenge in accelerated optimization, offering a method that preserves convergence guarantees while improving empirical performance. The connection to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory (a well-established area) broadens the applicability of the framework. The experimental results demonstrate that adaptive averaging outperforms existing heuristics in multiple test cases, particularly for strongly convex problems, suggesting practical relevance for optimization practitioners.

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-proven theorems
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates
- Connection to replicator dynamics provides new theoretical insights
- Comprehensive experimental validation with clear comparisons
- Clear exposition of the continuous-time framework and its relationship to previous work

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of how continuous-time analysis directly informs discrete-time algorithms beyond the specific examples provided
- The discrete-time version of the adaptive averaging heuristic (Algorithm 1) lacks thorough analysis of discretization error
- Experiments are limited to simplex-constrained problems in R^3; more diverse experiments would strengthen the claims
- The paper does not explicitly compare adaptive averaging to other adaptive methods (e.g., Adam, Adagrad) in the context of accelerated methods
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations for large-scale machine learning problems

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by generalizing the AMD framework and providing a unified Lyapunov analysis.
2. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing methods.
3. The connection to replicator dynamics is theoretically interesting and well-motivated.
4. The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and comprehensive experiments.
5. The work builds meaningfully on previous NIPS papers [11, 20] while extending the framework in a non-trivial way.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1. The paper's primary focus on continuous-time dynamics may limit its immediate practical impact for discrete-time optimization practitioners.
2. The experimental section lacks diversity in problem types and dimensions.
3. The connection to broader machine learning applications is not sufficiently developed.
4. The discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented without thorough analysis of its convergence properties.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of accelerated optimization methods through its continuous-time framework. The adaptive averaging heuristic is both theoretically grounded and empirically effective, representing a meaningful advancement over existing approaches. While the paper's focus on continuous-time dynamics may limit its immediate practical impact for discrete-time optimization, the theoretical insights and empirical results are compelling. The paper meets all the criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance, with the minor weaknesses being outweighed by the substantial theoretical and empirical contributions. The work is well-positioned to influence future research on accelerated optimization methods.
