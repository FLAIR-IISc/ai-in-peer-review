

# Review: "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary
This paper studies a family of continuous-time accelerated descent dynamics with generalized averaging schemes, extending the authors' previous work on accelerated mirror descent [11]. The authors introduce a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two weight functions w and η, and provide a unified analysis using Lyapunov functions to establish convergence rates. They prove sufficient conditions under which these dynamics achieve a given convergence rate 1/r(t), and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes the weights while preserving the convergence rate. The paper demonstrates applications to simplex-constrained problems, including an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics, and provides numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging with existing restart heuristics.

The work builds upon the continuous-time interpretation of accelerated methods pioneered by Su et al. [20] and the authors' own previous work [11], extending the theoretical understanding of the relationship between averaging and acceleration in optimization.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical proofs. The authors establish clear sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to decrease, which guarantees the desired convergence rate (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1). The proof techniques are appropriate for the problem and the analysis is well-executed. The numerical experiments in Section 5 provide convincing empirical evidence that the adaptive averaging heuristic outperforms existing restart heuristics across multiple problem types (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, and KL divergence objectives).

The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the fact that the second condition in Theorem 2 depends on the solution trajectory and may be hard to check a priori. The authors also appropriately discuss the relationship between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms (Section 4).

One minor limitation is that the paper doesn't provide a formal analysis of the discrete version of the adaptive averaging heuristic (Algorithm 1), though the authors mention they provide a bound in the supplementary material. It would be helpful to have a brief sketch of the discrete Lyapunov analysis in the main paper.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear definitions of mathematical concepts and consistent notation. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and motivation, and the paper flows logically from theoretical development to examples to experiments.

The connection between continuous-time analysis and discrete algorithms could be clearer. While the authors mention in Section 4 that they provide a discrete version of their heuristic, the transition from continuous to discrete is not explicitly explained. A brief discussion of how the continuous-time analysis informs the discrete algorithm design would strengthen the paper.

The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why adaptive averaging works. While the mathematical conditions are well-established, a more intuitive explanation of why the adaptive choice in Equation (8) preserves the convergence rate would help readers grasp the core insight.

## Originality

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on continuous-time interpretations of accelerated methods. While the general approach of studying continuous-time dynamics for optimization is not new (citing [6, 9, 5, 20, 2, 1, 3, 11, 23]), the authors' generalization of the averaging scheme and their unified analysis of conditions for different convergence rates is novel.

The key original contribution is the adaptive averaging heuristic (Section 4), which dynamically adjusts the weights while preserving the convergence rate. This is a significant improvement over existing restart heuristics, which provide empirical speedups but lack theoretical guarantees for general convex problems. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work like Adagrad [7] and Adam [10], which are not designed for accelerated methods.

The application to the replicator dynamics (Section 3) is also an interesting contribution that connects optimization to evolutionary game theory, providing a concrete example of how the general framework can be applied to a specific constrained problem.

## Significance

The results have significant theoretical and practical implications for optimization. The theoretical contribution provides a deeper understanding of the relationship between averaging and acceleration, which could inspire further research in this area. The adaptive averaging heuristic has practical significance as it provides a theoretically grounded method for improving the performance of accelerated methods without sacrificing convergence guarantees.

The numerical experiments demonstrate that adaptive averaging outperforms existing restart heuristics across multiple problem types, with particularly significant improvements in the strongly convex case. This suggests the heuristic could be valuable for practitioners implementing accelerated methods in machine learning applications.

The connection to the replicator dynamics is particularly significant, as it provides a new perspective on a well-known ODE from evolutionary game theory, potentially opening new research directions at the intersection of optimization and game theory.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides both theoretical insights and practical improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear sufficient conditions for convergence
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates while improving performance
- Interesting application to replicator dynamics with connections to game theory
- Comprehensive numerical experiments demonstrating superiority over existing methods
- Clear connection to previous work on continuous-time interpretations of accelerated methods

**Weaknesses:**
- The connection between continuous-time analysis and discrete algorithm design could be clearer
- The paper could provide more intuitive explanation of why the adaptive heuristic works
- The discrete analysis of Algorithm 1 is not sufficiently detailed in the main paper

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization, providing both theoretical insights and a practical improvement over existing methods. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in the field. The adaptive averaging heuristic is particularly compelling as it provides a theoretically grounded approach to improving accelerated methods that outperforms existing restart heuristics.

The paper advances the state of the art in understanding and implementing accelerated optimization methods, and the results are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on optimization algorithms for machine learning.
