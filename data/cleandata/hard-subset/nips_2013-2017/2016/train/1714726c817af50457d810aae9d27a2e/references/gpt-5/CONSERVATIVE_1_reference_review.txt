Summary
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for convex optimization with a focus on accelerated mirror descent (AMD). It introduces a family of ODEs parameterized by two positive weight functions w(t) (for averaging) and η(t) (for gradient accumulation), and analyzes them via a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), where r(t) prescribes the desired convergence rate. Theorem 2 gives sufficient conditions for Lr to be nonincreasing, implying f(X(t))−f* = O(1/r(t)); a simple corollary ties η and w via η = w r / W with W = ∫ w, yielding the standard O(1/t^2) rate as a special case. A second “energy” function Er is shown to decrease under the same conditions. The paper also derives a primal representation, instantiates the dynamics on the positive orthant and simplex (yielding an “accelerated replicator” ODE), and proposes an “adaptive averaging” heuristic: adapt a(t)=w/W online to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative while preserving the rate. A discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented and numerically compared to gradient-restart and speed-restart heuristics on simple 3D simplex-constrained problems.

Relation to prior work
This paper builds on a large literature connecting accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs (e.g., Su-Boyd-Candès, Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan) and on prior AMD analyses (Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett). The generalized AMD family and rate conditions are closely related to the Bregman Lagrangian perspective (choice of α, β schedules) and to the averaging/weighting viewpoint (e.g., Flammarion-Bach). The replicator dynamics connection is classical; the “accelerated replicator” variant follows from the mirror structure.

Strengths
- Provides a unified Lyapunov-based analysis for a broad class of weighted averaging AMD ODEs with simple sufficient conditions ensuring O(1/r(t)) rates in continuous time.
- Clean primal formulations, including concrete examples on the positive orthant and simplex that clarify the geometry (replicator dynamics).
- The energy function Er offers an intuitive “potential + kinetic” viewpoint.
- The idea of adapting the averaging weights to ensure a monotone Lyapunov decrease is natural and potentially useful. Empirical comparisons to restart heuristics are provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Algebraic/notation inconsistencies in Section 4 undermine confidence. In particular:
  • To make the term ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w) vanish, with a = w/W this requires η = a r (equivalently a = η/r), not a = η r as written. 
  • For r(t)=t^2 and η(t)=β t, one has η/r = β/t and η·r = β t^3; the text states “η(t)r(t) = β t,” which is incorrect.
  • In Examples 1–2, a(t) must be β/t (consistent with the subsequent integral), but the text states a(t)=β t in places. These errors affect the statement of the adaptive rule and the conditions ensuring Lyapunov decrease.
- The key sufficient condition in Theorem 2 is trajectory-dependent (sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩). The corollary circumvents this via η = a r, but the adaptive heuristic then departs from that equality without a rigorous continuous-time convergence analysis beyond “preserves the rate.” Clarifying, correcting, and tightening the adaptive rule and its guarantees is essential.
- Discrete-time theory is largely deferred to the supplement. The main paper provides Algorithm 1 but no formal theorem in the main text showing rate, stability, or monotonicity under the adaptive choice. Given that practical impact is in discrete time, stronger, clearly stated discrete guarantees are needed.
- Experiments are small-scale (R^3 simplex) and limited to toy objectives. There is no large-scale or stochastic setting, no comparison with widely used accelerated methods (e.g., FISTA with adaptive restart), and no ablations/sensitivity to β, step size s, or the choice of ψ*, R. Claims of consistent improvement should be substantiated more broadly.
- Novelty is incremental relative to the Bregman Lagrangian and previous AMD formulations: the generalized weighting appears to be a reparameterization of known schedules, and the adaptive averaging heuristic is conceptually similar to restart—in effect adjusting the “time-scaling.” The contribution seems more of a unifying presentation plus a heuristic than a substantial theoretical advance.
- Clarity issues: the heavy notation (w, W, a, η, r) and multiple reparameterizations plus the above inconsistencies make the paper harder to follow. Some assumptions (e.g., global Lipschitz continuity of ∇ψ*) deserve more discussion; for entropy/softmax, Lipschitz constants depend on norms and domain.
- References are incomplete (many entries lacking details), which is below NeurIPS standards.

Questions and requests for the authors
1. Please correct the inconsistencies in Section 4 and throughout regarding a, η, r (e.g., whether η = a r or a = η r, and the identity “ηr = β t”). Provide a clean, unambiguous statement of the adaptive rule and re-derive the consequences.
2. Provide a rigorous discrete-time theorem (in the main paper) for Algorithm 1 under the adaptive rule: conditions, rate, and monotonicity of the discrete Lyapunov/energy. If the discrete rule only preserves O(1/k^2), is there any provable advantage over fixed schedules or restart?
3. Clarify the relation to the Bregman Lagrangian schedules (α, β): are your conditions equivalent to choosing α, β to ensure an invariant? What is genuinely new beyond that framework?
4. Expand experiments: higher-dimensional problems, standard convex benchmarks, FISTA/Nesterov with adaptive restart, sensitivity analyses for β and step size, and report implementation details for reproducibility (ψ*, R, tuning).
5. Compare more directly with Flammarion-Bach (averaging/step-size connection) and discuss whether your adaptive averaging yields different behavior than adaptive step-size/momentum schemes.
6. Clean up and complete the references.

Pros
- Unifying Lyapunov framework for generalized averaging in AMD.
- Concrete primal formulations and intuitive energy interpretation.
- The adaptive averaging idea is simple and potentially useful.
- Some empirical evidence versus common restarts.

Cons
- Multiple algebraic/notation errors in key sections; trajectory-dependent conditions.
- Weak discrete-time theory in the main text; practical guarantees unclear.
- Limited, small-scale experiments; sparse baselines.
- Incremental novelty relative to established continuous-time acceleration frameworks.
- Clarity and reference quality need significant improvement.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The direction is interesting and potentially valuable, but the current version has critical inconsistencies, insufficient discrete-time guarantees, and limited empirical validation. With corrected derivations, stronger discrete theory, and more thorough experiments, the paper could be much stronger. Confidence: medium.
