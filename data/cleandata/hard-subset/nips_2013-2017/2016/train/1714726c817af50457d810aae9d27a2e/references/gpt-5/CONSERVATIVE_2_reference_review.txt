Summary
The paper studies a family of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics with generalized averaging. The dynamics are parameterized by two positive weight functions w(t) (for averaging) and η(t) (for gradient accumulation). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), the authors provide sufficient conditions under which Lr is nonincreasing and derive a convergence guarantee f(X(t)) − f* ≤ const/r(t). They show how to choose w and η to attain target rates r(t) = t^p (recovering t^2 as a special case). They also define a second “energy” function Er that is decreasing under similar conditions. A primal representation is developed, with examples for the orthant and simplex; in the simplex case, the dynamics reduce to an “accelerated replicator” ODE. Motivated by the Lyapunov analysis, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts w via a(t)=w/W based on the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩, and gives a discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1). Small-scale experiments (mostly on R^3 simplex problems) compare the heuristic against gradient and speed restart schemes, showing empirical improvements and a decreasing Lyapunov surrogate.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on continuous-time interpretations of Nesterov acceleration (Su et al., Wibisono et al.) and on Krichene et al. (accelerated mirror descent via averaging). The general AMD family and the Lyapunov-based rate certification are closely related to the Bregman-Lagrangian framework (Wibisono et al.) and to prior inertial dynamics analyses (Attouch et al.). The “accelerated replicator” connection is a nice conceptual bridge to evolutionary dynamics.

Strengths
- Clear, unified Lyapunov analysis for a broad AMD family with generalized averaging, recovering known rates and schedules as special cases.
- The Er energy decomposition provides additional intuition and is consistent with prior dynamical systems perspectives.
- Primal representations for important constraints (orthant, simplex) are well worked out; the accelerated replicator dynamics are interesting and potentially useful.
- The adaptive averaging idea—choosing weights to reduce the time derivative of the Lyapunov function—is conceptually appealing and connects the analysis to a practical heuristic.
- Empirical comparisons include standard restarting heuristics.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited novelty at the continuous-time level. The general ODE, rate certification via Lyapunov/Bregman energy, and the ability to target r(t) = t^p are well covered by Wibisono et al. and related inertial dynamics work. The main new element here is the adaptive averaging heuristic, which is not theoretically established in discrete time.
- Discrete-time guarantees are insufficient. The paper asserts rate preservation because the continuous-time Lyapunov derivative is nonpositive, but the proposed discrete rule departs from the exact condition that enforces d/dt Lr ≤ 0. The main text contains no theorem establishing an O(1/k^2) (or better) rate for Algorithm 1 under reasonable assumptions. The description states that a bound is in the supplement, but no formal statement/proof appears in the body, and the algorithm contains several ambiguities (see below).
- Algorithmic clarity issues. The discrete scheme’s update of ak has inconsistent scaling and typos:
  - a(t) is defined as w/W and equals β/t for quadratic rate; in Algorithm 1, ak is updated via “ak = βk√s” in some places and “ak = min(ak−1, βmax k √s)” elsewhere. Dimensions/monotonicity claims (“results in a non-increasing sequence ak”) contradict the update that resets ak to βk√s when f increases. It is difficult to reproduce or verify guarantees without a precise rule.
  - Several constants (β, γ, step size s, the regularizer R) are not specified or tuned; the dependence of performance on these choices is unknown.
- Empirical validation is too limited. Most experiments are on R^3 simplex to visualize trajectories. There are no standard convex benchmarks (e.g., constrained logistic regression, portfolio simplex problems, L1-constrained least squares, entropy-regularized objectives) and no higher-dimensional evaluations beyond a brief mention. There is no wall-clock comparison, sensitivity analysis of β/s, or robustness checks. Comparisons exclude widely used discrete methods (FISTA with adaptive restarts, tuned Nesterov variants) beyond the two restart heuristics cited.
- Practicality of the continuous-time adaptive rule is unclear. The proposed choice depends on ⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩, but in discrete time this is replaced by a surrogate condition on f(x̃(k+1)) − f(x̃(k)). There is no analysis that this surrogate enforces a discrete Lyapunov decrease, nor a principled rule for “a(t) ≥ η(t) r(t)” beyond setting a large value.
- Some connections to related work could be deepened (e.g., explicit positioning w.r.t. the Bregman Lagrangian family; how the proposed a(t) scheduling relates to time-dilation in Wibisono et al.; relation to Attouch-Peypouquet vanishing damping schedules).

Quality
Technically sound in the continuous-time part; the proofs given/outlined appear standard and correct. However, for NeurIPS, the main potential impact lies in the discrete algorithm; here the analysis is incomplete and the algorithm specification is not clean enough for reproduction.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized, with helpful intuition. However, Algorithm 1 has notational inconsistencies, and the adaptive rule needs a precise, unambiguous statement. Important discrete-time results relegated to the supplement should be summarized as formal theorems in the main text.

Originality
Moderate. The generalization of AMD with arbitrary averaging weights and the Lyapunov analysis are incremental relative to prior continuous-time acceleration frameworks. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel but currently lacks rigorous discrete-time support.

Significance
Currently limited by the absence of solid discrete-time guarantees and by narrow empirical validation. With stronger theory and broader experiments, the idea could be influential.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, general continuous-time analysis with connections to mirror descent, replicator dynamics, and energy functions.
- Conceptually elegant adaptive averaging idea guided by Lyapunov decrease.
- Potentially useful perspective for designing accelerations under constraints.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient discrete-time theory for the proposed adaptive algorithm; unclear whether the rate is preserved in practice.
- Algorithm specification has inconsistencies making reproduction difficult.
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale and not compelling for NeurIPS standards.
- Incremental novelty relative to existing continuous-time acceleration literature.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise, correct statement and proof of a discrete Lyapunov inequality and a convergence rate for Algorithm 1, including all constants and assumptions (smoothness, strong convexity variants).
- Fix and clarify the adaptive rule for ak (exact formulae, monotonicity, stopping criteria) and provide sensitivity analysis.
- Add comprehensive experiments on standard convex problems with constraints, higher dimensions, and comparisons to FISTA/AGD with adaptive restarts and tuned baselines; include runtime and robustness.
- Discuss how your framework relates technically to the Bregman Lagrangian time-dilation view and whether your adaptive a(t) can be interpreted as adaptive time change.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The conceptual contribution is interesting, but the paper needs stronger discrete-time guarantees and significantly expanded experiments to meet NeurIPS standards.

Confidence: High.
