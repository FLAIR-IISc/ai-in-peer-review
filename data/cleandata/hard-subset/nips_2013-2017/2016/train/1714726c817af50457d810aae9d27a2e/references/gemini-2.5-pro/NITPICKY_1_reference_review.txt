### NIPS Review

**Summary**

This paper investigates a general family of continuous-time dynamics for accelerated mirror descent, termed AMDw,η, parameterized by two continuous weight functions, w(t) and η(t), which control the averaging scheme and the gradient accumulation rate, respectively. The primary theoretical contribution is a unified Lyapunov analysis for this family of ODEs. The authors derive sufficient conditions on the weight functions w and η to guarantee a convergence rate of O(1/r(t)) for a given rate function r(t). This framework is shown to recover previous results for r(t)=t^2 and enables the construction of dynamics with other rates. The authors derive a primal-space representation for these dynamics and demonstrate that for simplex-constrained problems, this leads to an accelerated version of the well-known replicator dynamics. Building on their analysis, the paper proposes a novel adaptive averaging heuristic, designed to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function while provably preserving the theoretical convergence rate. Finally, numerical experiments on low-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems compare this new heuristic to standard accelerated mirror descent and popular restarting heuristics.

**Strengths**

1.  **General and Unified Framework:** The AMDw,η formulation provides an elegant and general framework for studying continuous-time accelerated methods. The use of a flexible Lyapunov function Lr to derive sufficient conditions for a desired convergence rate 1/r(t) is a powerful analytical tool that unifies and extends prior work (e.g., [11], [20]).

2.  **Novel Adaptive Heuristic with Guarantees:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution. Unlike many popular restarting heuristics (e.g., [17], [20]) which lack guarantees for general convex problems, this method is constructed explicitly to preserve the Lyapunov function's non-increasing property, thereby maintaining the O(1/r(t)) convergence rate. This is a theoretically appealing property.

3.  **Interesting Connections:** The paper draws a clear and interesting connection between the general mirror descent framework and specific, well-known dynamics. The derivation of an "accelerated replicator dynamics" is a nice example that provides a new perspective on accelerating dynamics relevant to fields like evolutionary game theory.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents several strong ideas, it is marred by a substantial number of flaws ranging from minor formatting issues to significant clarity problems that affect the reproducibility and technical soundness of the work.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from a general lack of meticulousness.
    *   **Formatting:** A minor but noticeable issue is the author's affiliation footnote appearing before the conference name in the header. Important equations in the introduction that define the dynamics from [11] are presented without equation numbers, making them difficult to reference.
    *   **Algorithm 1:** The presentation of the main algorithmic contribution, the discrete adaptive averaging method, is severely flawed.
        *   Line 5 contains an obvious typo: `λk = √sak + √sak`. This renders the algorithm nonsensical and irreproducible. The text provides no clarification.
        *   Line 4 introduces a regularizer `R(x̃, x(k))` without any prior definition or motivation in the continuous-time section. Its relationship to the Bregman divergence `Dψ` used in line 3 is unclear.
        *   The flow of variables is confusing. `x(k)` is used to compute `ž(k+1)` and `x̃(k+1)`, which are then combined to form `x(k+1)`. The algorithm box does not clearly show how the sequence `x(k)` is updated or initialized, though one might infer it from context.
    *   **Theoretical Exposition:** The justification for the discretization is extremely brief, stating only that it uses the "correspondance t = k√s". This is insufficient to bridge the gap between the continuous-time analysis and the discrete-time algorithm. Furthermore, the condition for adaptation in the algorithm (`f(x̃(k+1)) > f(x̃(k))`) is different from the one derived in the continuous analysis (`〈∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)〉 > 0`). The reason for and implications of this change are not discussed.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Gaps:**
    *   The assumption that `(∇ψ*)⁻¹ = ∇ψ` in Section 2, used to prove Theorem 3, is a strong one that is mentioned only in passing. While standard for some choices of ψ, its implications should be discussed more carefully.
    *   The claim that the presented framework is a generalization of the ODE studied in Wibisono et al. [23] is stated without proof or derivation. The connection between the two formulations is not obvious, and without a more detailed argument, this claim appears unsubstantiated.
    *   The proof for the convergence rate of Algorithm 1 is deferred to the supplementary material. While this is acceptable, the opaque and flawed presentation of the algorithm in the main text undermines confidence in any such proof.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evidence provided is insufficient.
    *   The experiments are confined to 3-dimensional problems on the probability simplex. While useful for visualization, this is a toy setting. The introduction motivates the work by the need to handle large-scale, high-dimensional datasets, but the experiments do not demonstrate the method's effectiveness in such regimes.
    *   The claim that the method offers a "significant improvement in some cases" is based on a very limited set of low-dimensional examples. A more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of problems, including higher dimensions and different constraint sets, is needed to substantiate these claims.

**Recommendation: Reject**

I recommend rejection for this paper. Although the core conceptual contributions—the general ODE framework and the rate-preserving adaptive heuristic—are interesting and potentially significant, the execution is poor. The paper is plagued by a lack of care in its presentation, culminating in an irreproducible main algorithm (Algorithm 1). The connection between the continuous-time theory and the discrete-time implementation is tenuous, and the experimental validation is too limited to be convincing. The sheer number of minor and major flaws prevents me from considering this a "complete piece of work" suitable for publication at NIPS. The authors have a promising direction, but the paper requires a major revision to address the clarity, reproducibility, and evaluation deficiencies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically grounded adaptive heuristic for accelerated methods that provably preserves the convergence rate.
*   The generalized continuous-time framework and its Lyapunov analysis are elegant and provide a useful tool for understanding and designing accelerated algorithms.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The main proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented with a critical typo and undefined terms, making it irreproducible.
*   The justification for the discretization from the continuous-time ODE is weak and lacks rigor.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited to low-dimensional toy problems and is insufficient to validate the claims for the large-scale settings that motivate the work.
*   The paper contains numerous minor errors, notational inconsistencies, and unsubstantiated claims (e.g., the generalization of [23]) that reflect a lack of thoroughness.
