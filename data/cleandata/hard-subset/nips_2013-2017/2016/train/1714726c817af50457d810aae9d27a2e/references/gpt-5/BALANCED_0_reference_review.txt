Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time dynamics for convex optimization with constraints and smooth objectives, focusing on accelerated mirror descent (AMD). It introduces a generalized family of ODEs parameterized by two positive weight functions: (i) an accumulation rate η(t) for the dual variable Z(t), and (ii) an averaging weight w(t) that defines the primal variable X(t) as a weighted average of a mirror map applied to Z(·). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), the authors give sufficient conditions on (w, η) that guarantee Lr is nonincreasing and hence f(X(t)) − f* ≤ const/r(t). This recovers the classical O(1/t^2) rate and more generally any prescribed r(t), provided the weights satisfy certain relations. They also derive a complementary “energy” function Er combining potential and kinetic terms that decreases under the same conditions.

The paper provides a primal formulation of the dynamics and illustrative examples, including positive-orthant and simplex constraints; notably, the simplex case yields an “accelerated replicator” ODE. Building on the Lyapunov analysis, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that selects the instantaneous averaging rate a(t) = w(t)/W(t) to decrease Lr (or at least not increase it), thus preserving the continuous-time convergence rate. They provide a discrete-time algorithm (mirror-prox-like) corresponding to r(t) = t^2 and empirically compare it against gradient and speed restarts (O’Donoghue & Candès; Su, Boyd & Candès) on small simplex-constrained problems. The adaptive averaging heuristic is reported to be competitive or superior and to maintain a decreasing Lyapunov function along the discrete trajectory.

This work builds on and connects to: Nesterov acceleration; the ODE interpretation of Nesterov’s method (Su et al.); accelerated mirror descent and averaging (Krichene et al.); variational/Bregman Lagrangian perspectives (Wibisono et al.); and inertial dynamics (Attouch et al.). It also highlights relations to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A clean, general AMD ODE with a Lyapunov r(t)-template that recovers known accelerated flows and clarifies the role of averaging in acceleration.
- Clear sufficient conditions: Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 give simple, interpretable conditions on w and η to ensure a target rate 1/r(t).
- Complementary energy function: Theorem 3 provides a physically intuitive decreasing energy that helps interpret the dynamics.
- Primal formulations and examples: The derivations for the orthant and simplex are instructive; the accelerated replicator dynamics is a nice connection likely of independent interest.
- Adaptive idea: The adaptive averaging heuristic is simple, grounded in the Lyapunov decrease, and compatible with preserving the continuous-time rate. The discrete algorithm is practical and easy to implement.
- Empirical illustrations: Experiments show improved behavior vs. restarts in several toy settings, and the monotonic Lyapunov trend is convincing.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strength of theory for discretization: The main theoretical guarantees are in continuous time. The discrete algorithm and its guarantees (including the claimed preservation of r(t) rate) are deferred to the supplement; in the main text, only a brief statement appears. It is unclear under what step-size conditions a discrete Lyapunov function decreases, and what exact rate is guaranteed. Given that restarts are widely used in discrete-time methods, a stronger discrete analysis in the main paper would significantly increase impact.
- Condition (2) in Theorem 2: The sufficient condition depends on trajectory quantities, ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w) ≤ 0, which is not directly checkable a priori. Corollary 1 imposes η = wr/W and w/W ≥ r′/r; while simple, this is somewhat restrictive and ties η and w tightly. The adaptive rule is designed to enforce negativity, but in discrete time the proposed surrogate (based on decrease in f at x̃) departs from the continuous-time condition; the precise connection needs clarification.
- Limited empirical scope: Experiments are 2–3D simplex problems. There are no medium/large-scale tests, no stochastic/noisy gradients, and no comparisons on standard ML benchmarks. It is hard to assess practical significance from these small-scale demonstrations.
- Relation to existing frameworks: The connection to Wibisono et al.’s Bregman Lagrangians (and to Attouch–Peypouquet inertial systems) is noted but could be sharpened: when does the proposed family strictly generalize those, and when is it equivalent (e.g., via η = ar)? How does adaptive averaging relate to adaptive restarts in those frameworks?
- Clarity/presentation issues: Several derivations depend on extra smoothness (twice differentiable ψ*), which is stated but could be emphasized with clear assumption blocks. Notation around a(t) = w/W appears in multiple places; consolidating and checking dimensional consistency (e.g., the “1/a” in Theorem 3’s proof) would help. References are incomplete/bibliographic details are missing for many entries.
- Claims about faster-than-1/t^2: The text hints at r(t) = tp with p > 2 and mentions higher-order discretizations (Nesterov cubic). However, the paper does not provide discrete algorithms achieving those rates; this should be framed cautiously.

Quality
- Technical content is sound for the continuous-time analysis, with careful Lyapunov arguments and existence/uniqueness. The discrete-time part needs stronger, more visible guarantees and empirical breadth.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but could be improved by tightening assumptions, consolidating notation, and moving essential discrete-time theory from the supplement. Figures are helpful.

Originality
- The generalized averaging perspective and adaptive averaging heuristic appear novel relative to prior AMD/ODE work. The accelerated replicator instantiation is a nice addition.

Significance
- Potentially significant if the adaptive idea translates to robust gains in practical algorithms; current experiments are too limited to support strong claims. Strengthening discrete-time theory and empirical evaluation would improve impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clean, general Lyapunov framework unifying accelerated dynamics via averaging.
- Conceptually simple adaptive mechanism with principled grounding.
- New connections (accelerated replicator) likely of interest to multiple communities.

Arguments against acceptance
- Discrete-time guarantees and empirical evaluation are limited; practical significance remains unclear.
- The key sufficient condition relies on trajectory-dependent inequalities; enforceability is not fully addressed in discrete time.
- Presentation and references need polishing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide main-text theorems for the discrete algorithm: explicit Lyapunov function, step-size conditions, and convergence rate guarantees.
- Expand experiments to higher dimensions and standard ML tasks (e.g., entropy-regularized ERM on the simplex), include stochastic gradients, and ablate β, step size, and choices of ψ, R. Compare to Nesterov with adaptive restarts, heavy-ball, and, where applicable, AdaGrad/Adam baselines.
- Clarify the relationship to Bregman Lagrangian dynamics and inertial systems; specify when your family subsumes prior flows.
- Consolidate assumptions on f and ψ/ψ*; streamline notation for a(t), w, W, η; double-check derivations (units and constants).
- Complete and standardize references.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The continuous-time analysis is elegant and the adaptive idea is promising, but the discrete-time theory and empirical validation are not yet strong enough for NeurIPS. Strengthening the discrete guarantees and providing broader experiments could make this a solid contribution.
