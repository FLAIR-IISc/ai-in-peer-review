This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated convex optimization, specifically focusing on a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions for averaging and gradient accumulation. The authors build upon previous work connecting Nesterov's accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs (e.g., Su et al. [20], Krichene et al. [11]) and aim to understand whether different averaging schemes can achieve or surpass existing convergence rates. They provide sufficient conditions for convergence and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate. The paper includes a primal representation of the dynamics, examples for specific constraint sets (positive orthant and simplex), and numerical experiments demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed heuristic.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper effectively situates its contribution within the growing body of work linking discrete accelerated methods to continuous-time dynamics. It accurately references relevant papers (e.g., Nesterov’s work [14, 15], Su et al. [20], Krichene et al. [11]).
*   **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for their analysis, including the use of a Lyapunov function and a sufficient condition for convergence. The theorems and corollaries are clearly stated and well-motivated.
*   **Novelty of Generalized Averaging:**  The generalization of the AMD dynamics to arbitrary averaging schemes is a novel contribution.  Exploring the impact of different weight functions (w and η) is a valuable direction.
*   **Practical Heuristic:**  The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant practical contribution. The guarantee of preserving the convergence rate, unlike some existing heuristics, is a strong point.
*   **Illustrative Examples:** The examples with the positive orthant and simplex constraints (particularly the connection to the replicator equation) are insightful and demonstrate the applicability of the framework to different problem settings.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging to other heuristics are well-designed and provide evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** While the experiments are well-executed, they are limited to a relatively small set of objective functions and dimensions. Expanding the experiments to higher-dimensional problems and more diverse objective functions would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Complexity of Primal Representation:** While the primal representation is valuable, its derivation and presentation are somewhat dense. It could benefit from further simplification and explanation.
*   **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The adaptive averaging heuristic introduces additional computation to determine the weights *w(t)*. The paper doesn't thoroughly address the computational overhead of this adaptation compared to fixed-weight schemes.
*   **Reliance on Smoothness Assumptions:** The analysis relies heavily on the assumption of smooth distance generating functions (ψ*).  Discussing the implications of relaxing this assumption would be beneficial.
*   **Notation:** The notation can be dense in some places, especially when introducing the primal form of the equations.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound generalization of accelerated mirror descent dynamics.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant practical contribution with a guaranteed convergence rate.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and builds upon a strong foundation of existing work.
*   The numerical experiments provide compelling evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The numerical experiments lack breadth and depth, limiting the generalizability of the conclusions.
*   The complexity of the primal representation and the lack of discussion regarding computational cost are minor drawbacks.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theory and practice of accelerated optimization. The generalization of AMD dynamics and the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic are novel and promising. While the numerical experiments could be more extensive, the theoretical rigor and practical relevance of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

Accept.
