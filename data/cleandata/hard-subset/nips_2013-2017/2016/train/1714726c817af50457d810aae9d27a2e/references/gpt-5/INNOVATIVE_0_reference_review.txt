Summary
The paper studies a broad family of continuous-time first-order dynamics for convex optimization that generalizes accelerated mirror descent via two time-varying weight functions: an averaging weight w(t) and an accumulation rate η(t). The authors introduce a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) and derive simple sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) under which Lr is non-increasing, yielding convergence f(X(t))−f* ≤ C/r(t). This unifies and extends previous ODE views of acceleration (e.g., Su–Boyd–Candès; Krichene et al.) by showing many averaging schedules achieve the same (or prescribed) rates. They also present a second “energy” function Er that always decreases under the same conditions, offering a physically intuitive metric.

The paper gives a primal-only formulation, illustrates the operator ∇²ψ*∘∇ψ for two canonical domains (positive orthant and simplex), and derives an accelerated variant of replicator dynamics. The key algorithmic contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t)=w(t)/W(t) along the trajectory to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative while preserving the target rate r(t). A discrete-time instantiation (for r(t)=t²) is proposed and compared empirically to gradient and speed restarts on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems, showing consistent improvements and monotonic Lyapunov/energy behavior.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on the ODE view of Nesterov’s acceleration (Su–Boyd–Candès) and proximal/MD variants (Krichene et al. 2015).
- Closely related to Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan’s variational perspective, but the present work emphasizes averaging weights and Lyapunov-based sufficient conditions, providing a constructive “schedule design” recipe and an adaptive rule that preserves rates.
- Connects to multiplicative-weights/replicator dynamics and shows how to accelerate them via averaging.
- Empirically compares to adaptive restart heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès; Su–Boyd–Candès).

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis appears sound. The Lyapunov inequality is clean and the sufficient conditions are transparent. The construction of w via a(t) and the examples (r(t)=t^p) are clear.
- The alternative energy function Er and its monotonicity proof are neat and insightful.
- The adaptive averaging rule is well-motivated by the Lyapunov derivative; the discrete method is reasonable. However, the discrete-time theoretical guarantees are deferred to the supplement and are only for r(t)=t²; for p>2 the paper points to higher-order discretizations (Wibisono et al.).
- Experiments are illustrative but small-scale (R^3 simplex). They qualitatively support the claims, including monotonic Er and improved behavior over restarts.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The role of each assumption (Lipschitz ∇f, properties of ψ, ∇ψ*) is stated. The dependence on t0>0 and existence/uniqueness are appropriately handled.
- The trajectory-dependent condition ⟨∇f(X),Ẋ⟩(r−ηW/w)≤0 is explained, and a constructive corollary (η = w r W; w/W ≥ r′/r) is provided, followed by the adaptive rule.
- Some derivations are pushed to the supplement (e.g., discrete algorithm analysis), but the main ideas are accessible.

Originality
- The generalized averaging framework with explicit sufficient conditions and the adaptive averaging heuristic are novel and conceptually elegant. The accelerated replicator dynamics example is a nice and original connection.
- The second energy function and its interpretation add value.
- While related to Wibisono et al.’s unifying Lagrangian view and to averaging–acceleration connections (Flammarion–Bach), the emphasis on adaptive averaging guided by a Lyapunov derivative is a fresh and promising angle.

Significance
- Provides a simple recipe to design weight schedules achieving desired rates in continuous time, and an adaptive mechanism that is rate-preserving and empirically competitive with restarts.
- The replicator dynamics acceleration could interest communities in game theory and evolutionary dynamics.
- The potential to extend adaptive averaging to stochastic, composite, or large-scale settings is significant, though currently untested.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical framework with a unifying Lyapunov analysis and constructive conditions.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic grounded in energy decrease; preserves O(1/t²) rate in the presented setting.
- Elegant primal representation and concrete examples (orthant and simplex), including accelerated replicator dynamics.
- Empirical comparisons to strong baselines (restarts) show consistent improvements and better monotonicity properties.

Weaknesses / Questions
- Discrete-time theory is limited: the rate guarantee for the adaptive scheme is only stated for the r(t)=t² case in the supplement; no guarantees for stochastic or composite objectives.
- Experiments are low-dimensional and synthetic; no large-scale benchmarks, wall-clock/iteration complexity, or sensitivity analyses (β, step size, choice of R, ψ).
- The adaptive rule relies on conditions like sign of ⟨∇f(X),Ẋ⟩; in discrete-time the proxy using f(x̃(k+1))−f(x̃(k))>0 is plausible but the correspondence could be clarified and analyzed more thoroughly.
- Assumptions such as Lipschitz ∇ψ* (and smooth ψ) may be strong in some settings; discuss scope and constants for typical mirror maps (e.g., entropy on the simplex).
- Relationship and distinctions with Wibisono et al.’s Bregman Lagrangian and with Flammarion–Bach’s averaging view could be elaborated to better situate the contribution.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a more detailed discrete-time analysis of the adaptive rule (beyond r=t²), or clarify limitations.
- Add higher-dimensional experiments and standard benchmarks; include sensitivity ablations and hyperparameter tuning protocols; report computational overhead of adaptivity.
- Discuss extensions: composite objectives (prox), stochastic gradients, and strong convexity (can adaptive averaging achieve faster rates in practice/theory?).
- Clarify the practical evaluation of Lyapunov functions (they require x* and z*), and emphasize diagnostics that do not assume access to the minimizer.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper proposes a conceptually clean and novel perspective—adaptive averaging in accelerated mirror-descent-type dynamics—with principled Lyapunov-based conditions, a decreasing energy function, a concrete adaptive heuristic, and promising empirical behavior. Despite limited discrete-time theory and small-scale experiments, the ideas are bold and likely to influence future algorithm design and analysis.
