## Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates continuous-time dynamics for accelerated mirror descent (AMD), building on recent work connecting Nesterov-style acceleration to ODEs. The authors generalize a previously studied family of AMD dynamics by introducing arbitrary averaging weight functions. They derive sufficient conditions on these weights, alongside a rate function *r(t)*, to guarantee a convergence rate of 1/r(t). Importantly, they propose a novel adaptive averaging heuristic designed to maintain this convergence rate by dynamically adjusting the weights based on the Lyapunov function's derivative.  The paper concludes with numerical experiments demonstrating the performance of this heuristic compared to existing restarting methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds upon a solid mathematical framework, rigorously analyzing the AMD dynamics using Lyapunov function arguments. The generalization to arbitrary averaging weights is a natural and valuable extension.
* **Novel Adaptive Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution.  The guarantee of maintaining the convergence rate (unlike many existing heuristics) is a compelling advantage. The intuitive idea of adapting weights to minimize the Lyapunov function’s decrease is sound.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature on accelerated methods, continuous-time optimization, and replicator dynamics.  It correctly identifies the relationship to Nesterov acceleration and previous work on restarting heuristics.
* **Illustrative Examples:**  The examples of the dynamics for positive-orthant and simplex-constrained problems (specifically the connection to the replicator equation) are insightful and demonstrate the applicability of the framework.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments provide initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging heuristic. The comparison to existing methods is useful and highlights the potential benefits.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are helpful, they are relatively limited. The comparison is primarily focused on a specific setting (simplex-constrained problems, entropy function).  More diverse problem instances (e.g., strongly convex and non-convex functions, different constraint sets) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Lack of Detailed Analysis of the Heuristic’s Complexity:** The paper lacks a discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the adaptive averaging heuristic.  How does the cost of computing and applying the adaptive weights compare to the cost of the original accelerated method or restarting heuristics?
* **Theoretical Results Primarily in Continuous Time:** The focus is heavily on continuous-time dynamics. While valuable, translating these results to practical discrete-time algorithms (and proving convergence guarantees for those algorithms) is crucial. The algorithm presented in Section 4 is a good start, but a more thorough analysis of its convergence properties is needed.
* **Some Notation is Dense:** The mathematical notation, while generally correct, can be quite dense, making it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the field to fully grasp the details.

**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are generally well-supported by theoretical analysis. The Lyapunov function approach is rigorous. However, the lack of detailed analysis of the heuristic's computational cost slightly reduces the overall quality score.
* **Clarity:** The writing is generally clear, though the mathematical notation is occasionally dense.  The organization is logical, and the paper effectively motivates the research.
* **Originality:** The generalization of AMD dynamics and the adaptive averaging heuristic are novel contributions. The connection to replicator dynamics is also a unique and interesting perspective. The related work section is comprehensive.
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant.  The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a principled approach to improving the performance of accelerated methods, with a guaranteed convergence rate.  If the empirical results hold in broader settings, this could be a valuable tool for practitioners.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous analysis.
* Novel and promising adaptive averaging heuristic with a convergence guarantee.
* Clear connection to existing research and a well-defined research problem.
* Illustrative examples and initial empirical validation.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of numerical experiments.
* Lack of detailed analysis of the heuristic’s computational complexity.
* Primarily focused on continuous-time dynamics; more work is needed to translate results to discrete algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The theoretical results are rigorous, the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and promising, and the paper is well-written and clearly motivated. While the limited scope of experiments and the lack of detailed complexity analysis are weaknesses, they are not severe enough to warrant rejection. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the following:

1. **Expand the numerical experiments:**  Include results on a broader range of problem instances (e.g., strongly convex and non-convex functions, different constraint sets) to demonstrate the robustness of the adaptive averaging heuristic.
2. **Discuss the computational complexity:**  Provide an analysis of the computational overhead introduced by the adaptive averaging heuristic compared to existing methods.
3. **Further develop the discrete-time algorithm:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the convergence properties of the discrete-time algorithm presented in Section 4, perhaps drawing on recent work on discretizing accelerated methods.
