

**Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"**

This paper investigates a family of continuous-time dynamics for convex optimization, generalizing the accelerated mirror descent framework from Krichene et al. [11]. The authors propose a broader class of dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, and provide conditions under which a Lyapunov function guarantees convergence at a specified rate. They introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates while potentially improving practical performance, with connections to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical framework unifies various accelerated methods under a continuous-time perspective.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and appears to outperform existing restart heuristics in numerical experiments.
- The connection to replicator dynamics provides a novel application for accelerated methods.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Technical gaps in proofs:** The proof of Theorem 2 contains a critical gap in inequality (3). The paper claims "≤ (f(X(t))− f?)(r′(t)− η(t)) + 〈 ∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t) 〉( r(t)− η(t)W(t)/w(t) )" but fails to show the intermediate steps justifying this inequality. The application of convexity of f is not clearly demonstrated, making the proof incomplete.

2. **Notational inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous inconsistent notations throughout. For example, in equation (1), "X(t) = X(t0)W (t0)+ ∫ t t0 w(τ)∇ψ∗(Z(τ))dτ W (t)" has inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators. The notation "Lr" in the text is inconsistent with the definition "Lr" in equation (2), and "W(t)" is sometimes written as "W (t)".

3. **Lack of novelty clarification:** The paper claims to generalize [11] but doesn't clearly state how their framework differs from their previous work. The connection to the work in [8] (which observed the connection between acceleration and averaging) is mentioned but not fully contextualized in relation to the current contribution.

4. **Incomplete experimental validation:** The numerical experiments in Section 5 lack statistical significance testing and multiple runs to confirm the results. The paper mentions "additional experiments in higher dimension" in the supplementary material, but the main paper doesn't include these results, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the claims.

5. **Missing figures:** The paper references "Figure 1" but no figure is included in the provided text, making it impossible to evaluate the claims about the dynamics' behavior.

6. **Ambiguous connection to existing work:** The paper claims adaptive averaging is "not designed for accelerated methods" like Adagrad and Adam, but doesn't explain why existing adaptive methods don't work for acceleration. This claim requires stronger justification.

**Overall assessment:**

While the paper presents theoretically interesting ideas, the numerous technical gaps, formatting inconsistencies, and lack of clear novelty significantly undermine its contribution. The proof of Theorem 2 is incomplete, which is a fundamental issue for a theoretical paper. The notational inconsistencies and missing figures make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims. The experimental results are promising but insufficiently validated.

The paper's potential significance is overshadowed by these technical and presentation flaws. The authors should address the following before resubmission: (1) provide a complete proof of Theorem 2 with all intermediate steps, (2) ensure consistent notation throughout, (3) clearly articulate the novelty relative to their previous work and other recent NIPS papers, (4) include the referenced figures, and (5) provide more thorough experimental validation with statistical significance tests.

Given the stringent standards of NeurIPS, I recommend rejection due to the substantial technical and presentation flaws that prevent a clear assessment of the paper's contribution. While the theoretical framework is interesting, the current presentation does not meet the quality standards expected for publication at NeurIPS.
