This paper explores a generalization of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics, introducing a family of ODEs parameterized by weight functions *w* and *η*. The authors aim to understand if different averaging schemes can achieve, or even improve upon, the quadratic convergence rate established for the original AMD formulation. They derive sufficient conditions for Lyapunov stability and demonstrate how to construct weight functions to guarantee this rate. A key contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to preserve the convergence rate by dynamically adjusting the weights based on the Lyapunov function’s derivative. The paper also provides a primal representation of the dynamics and illustrates its application to specific constraint sets, like the simplex, deriving an accelerated replicator equation.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to formalize and generalize the connection between acceleration and averaging in optimization. The Lyapunov analysis, while standard, is presented clearly and the derivation of sufficient conditions for convergence is a reasonable step. The primal representation is a valuable addition, allowing for concrete examples like the accelerated replicator dynamics.

However, the paper suffers from a significant lack of novelty and thorough experimental validation. The core idea of adapting averaging schemes isn’t fundamentally new – adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam, though not explicitly designed for accelerated methods, achieve similar goals via gradient-based adaptation.  The authors acknowledge this but dismiss these methods as unsuitable, which feels somewhat superficial.  More importantly, the theoretical results, while correct, are largely expected extensions of existing work (e.g., [11, 20]). The connection to replicator dynamics is interesting, but replicator dynamics itself is a well-studied area, and simply accelerating it doesn’t necessarily represent a major breakthrough.

The numerical experiments are the most significant weakness. While the comparison to restarting heuristics is a good idea, the visualization is limited to R3 and doesn't provide a rigorous quantitative comparison. The claim of “significant improvement in some cases” lacks concrete data. The experiments do not convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the adaptive averaging heuristic *over* simpler, well-established methods.  Furthermore, the lack of comparison to standard accelerated methods (e.g., Nesterov's method) makes it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach. The authors mention higher-order methods ([23]) but don’t actually compare against them. The discussion of discretization and maintaining convergence rates for faster rates *r(t) = t<sup>p</sup>* is alluded to, but not fully explored or tested.  The argument that the energy function always decreases with the restarting heuristic doesn't equate to good performance; decreasing energy is a necessary, but not sufficient, condition for effective optimization. 

The writing is generally clear, but some sections could be more concise. The presentation of the mathematical derivations is standard for this field, but the lack of detailed explanation of the intuition behind certain choices (e.g., the form of the Lyapunov function) makes it harder to follow.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a mathematically sound generalization of AMD dynamics.
*   The Lyapunov analysis provides theoretical guarantees on convergence rates.
*   The connection to replicator dynamics is a potentially interesting application.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea lacks significant originality.
*   The experimental validation is weak and does not convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods.
*   The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed approach.
*   The connection to related adaptive methods (Adagrad, Adam) is superficial.
*   The contribution feels incremental rather than transformative.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper is borderline. While technically sound, it lacks the originality and compelling experimental evidence required for acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical results are a reasonable extension of existing work, but the lack of a strong empirical demonstration of benefit – especially compared to well-established methods – significantly weakens the contribution. The numerical experiments are insufficient to justify the claims made. I lean towards **reject** but would be open to reconsideration if the authors significantly expand the experimental evaluation and provide a more compelling demonstration of the practical advantages of their adaptive averaging heuristic.  I recommend that the authors thoroughly compare their method against Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent, Adam, and other relevant benchmarks, and present a more detailed quantitative analysis of the results.
