- Introduces generalized Accelerated Mirror Descent dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t) with a unified Lyapunov analysis providing sufficient conditions for convergence rates.  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes w(t) to preserve theoretical convergence rates while potentially improving empirical performance.  
- Connects the framework to known dynamics (e.g., replicator equation) and unifies prior ODE-based accelerated methods (Su et al., Krichene et al.).  
- Provides rigorous proofs (Theorem 2 and corollary) showing the Lyapunov function decreases under precise conditions.  
- Numerical experiments across problem types show adaptive averaging outperforms established restarting heuristics, especially for strongly convex problems.  
- Supplementary material supplies the discrete-time counterpart, extra theoretical analysis, and algorithmic details.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear notation, logical progression, and illustrative examples (positive orthant, simplex).  
- Recommends adding a brief main-text overview of the continuous-to-discrete connection for practical readers.  
- Notes a limitation: one condition in Theorem 2 depends on the solution trajectory and is difficult to verify a priori.  
- Suggests clarifying the need to initialize at t0 > 0 in the existence/uniqueness proof.  
- Requests more experimental detail on computational complexity and how adaptive averaging scales with problem dimension.  
- Suggests a brief comparison to adaptive optimizers (Adagrad, Adam) to highlight differences and applicability.  
- Recommends discussing scenarios where adaptive averaging may not yield significant benefits over standard accelerated methods.  
- Arguments for acceptance: unified theoretical framework, rate-preserving adaptive heuristic, strong empirical gains, novel replicator connection, and clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: adaptive heuristic could be viewed as a minor extension, experiments cover limited problem types, and comparisons to other adaptive methods could be stronger.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — technically sound, original, significant, and well-presented.
