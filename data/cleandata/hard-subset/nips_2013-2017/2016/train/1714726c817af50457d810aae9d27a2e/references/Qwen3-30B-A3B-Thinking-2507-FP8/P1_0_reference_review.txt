

# Peer Review for "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary

This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for convex optimization, focusing on accelerated descent methods. The authors propose a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, which allows for a flexible averaging scheme. They provide a Lyapunov-based analysis to establish convergence rates under certain conditions on these weight functions. The key contribution is a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate while potentially improving performance. The paper also connects these dynamics to the replicator equation for simplex-constrained problems, which has applications in evolutionary game theory.

This work builds on prior research on continuous-time dynamics for optimization, particularly the connections between Nesterov's accelerated methods and ODEs established by Su et al. [20] and the accelerated mirror descent framework by Krichene et al. [11]. The paper extends these works by introducing a generalized averaging scheme and a novel adaptive heuristic.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound analysis of accelerated descent dynamics. The theoretical results (Theorems 2, 3, and Corollary 1) are well-supported with rigorous proofs. The authors carefully establish sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing, which guarantees the desired convergence rates. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, as it preserves the conditions needed for the Lyapunov function to decrease.

The numerical experiments are well-designed and clearly demonstrate the advantages of the adaptive averaging approach over existing restarting heuristics across multiple problem types (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, and KL divergence). The results are convincing and provide good evidence for the practical benefits of the proposed method.

The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need for specific conditions to guarantee the Lyapunov function is decreasing, and they correctly note that the adaptive heuristic preserves the convergence rate but doesn't necessarily improve it.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The connection between the continuous-time dynamics and discrete optimization algorithms is well-explained, which is important for readers interested in practical applications.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the adaptive averaging heuristic translates to discrete time. Algorithm 1, while presented, is somewhat cryptic without the supplementary material. A clearer description of the discrete implementation would improve the paper's accessibility.

The examples (positive-orthant and simplex-constrained dynamics) are well-chosen and help illustrate the theoretical framework. The connection to replicator dynamics for the simplex case is particularly interesting and well-explained.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a generalized averaging scheme for accelerated descent dynamics. While prior work [11, 20] established connections between acceleration and averaging, this paper extends these results to a broader family of averaging schemes and provides conditions for convergence rates.

The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and provides a principled way to adapt the weights along the solution trajectories while preserving the convergence rate. This is a significant improvement over existing restarting heuristics, which do not guarantee the same convergence rate.

The connection to replicator dynamics for simplex constraints is an interesting application that demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical framework. While replicator dynamics have been studied before, this paper provides a clear acceleration mechanism through the proposed averaging scheme.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work in the introduction and related work sections, citing relevant prior contributions and explaining how the current work extends them.

## Significance

This work advances our understanding of accelerated optimization methods by providing a unified framework for analyzing a broad class of accelerated dynamics. The theoretical results have direct implications for the design of new optimization algorithms.

The adaptive averaging heuristic has practical significance for practitioners using accelerated methods, as it provides a simple yet effective way to improve performance without sacrificing convergence guarantees. The numerical experiments demonstrate clear advantages over existing heuristics, particularly in the strongly convex case.

The connection to replicator dynamics opens up interesting possibilities for applications in evolutionary game theory and related fields. This interdisciplinary connection adds to the significance of the work.

The paper advances the state of the art in understanding the relationship between continuous-time dynamics and discrete optimization algorithms, which is a fundamental question in optimization theory.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Elegant theoretical framework for analyzing accelerated descent dynamics
- Rigorous Lyapunov-based convergence analysis
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rate
- Clear numerical experiments demonstrating practical advantages
- Interesting connection to replicator dynamics for simplex constraints
- Careful discussion of limitations and theoretical conditions

**Weaknesses:**
- Discrete implementation details (Algorithm 1) could be clearer
- Limited discussion of computational complexity of the adaptive heuristic
- The connection to specific discrete-time accelerated methods (e.g., Nesterov's method) could be more explicit
- Analysis limited to convex optimization; extension to non-convex settings would be interesting
- The paper doesn't discuss potential drawbacks or scenarios where adaptive averaging might not be beneficial

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The theoretical results are sound, the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and well-motivated, and the numerical experiments convincingly demonstrate practical advantages.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for those interested in the continuous-time perspective on accelerated methods.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. Consider expanding the explanation of Algorithm 1 to make the discrete implementation more accessible. A step-by-step description of how the adaptive averaging is computed in practice would be helpful.

2. It would be useful to more explicitly connect your continuous-time dynamics to specific discrete-time accelerated methods (e.g., Nesterov's accelerated gradient method). How does your adaptive averaging heuristic relate to the discrete-time variants?

3. Discuss the computational complexity of implementing the adaptive averaging heuristic. How does it compare to existing heuristics in terms of additional computational cost?

4. The paper could be strengthened by including a brief discussion of potential limitations or scenarios where adaptive averaging might not be beneficial compared to other heuristics.

5. The supplementary material mentions a bound on the convergence rate for Algorithm 1. It would be helpful to mention this bound in the main text, as it's an important theoretical contribution.

6. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the adaptive averaging heuristic might extend to non-convex optimization problems, even if only as a direction for future work.

Overall, this is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The adaptive averaging heuristic is particularly promising for practitioners looking to improve the performance of accelerated optimization methods.
