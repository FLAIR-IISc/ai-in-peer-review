Summary
The paper studies a broad family of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two weight functions w(t) (averaging) and η(t) (gradient accumulation). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), the authors give simple sufficient conditions on w and η to ensure Lr is non-increasing, which implies a convergence rate f(X(t)) − f* ≤ const/r(t). This unifies earlier analyses of accelerated dynamics and shows that acceleration can be seen as averaging with appropriately chosen time-varying weights. They also introduce a decreasing “energy” E_r(t) = f(X(t)) + (1/r(t)) Dψ*(Z,X̌(t)) that resembles potential plus kinetic energy.

They derive a primal formulation of the dynamics, give examples on the positive orthant and the simplex, and obtain an “accelerated replicator” ODE. Motivated by the Lyapunov analysis, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) = w/W online to decrease the Lyapunov function whenever possible, while preserving the continuous-time rate. A discretization for the r(t) = t^2 case is proposed (Algorithm 1) and empirically compared to popular restart heuristics (gradient restart and speed restart) on small simplex-constrained problems. The experiments show favorable behavior for adaptive averaging.

Relation to prior work
- The formulation and Lyapunov analysis connect closely to the Bregman Lagrangian viewpoint of Wibisono et al. (2016) and to inertial gradient ODEs (Su et al., Attouch et al.), where families of time-scaled ODEs yield rates 1/r(t). The condition η = (w r)/W and w/W ≥ r′/r recovers the family that attains a prescribed rate in [23]; the primal representation also subsumes that family.
- The averaging interpretation links to Flammarion & Bach (“From averaging to acceleration”) and to earlier continuous-time interpretations of Nesterov’s method.
- The main novel element is the adaptive averaging heuristic designed to monotonically decrease the Lyapunov function in continuous time, and its discretization contrasted with popular restart heuristics. The explicit accelerated replicator ODE is a nice byproduct.

Strengths
- Clear, unified Lyapunov framework that transparently recovers known accelerated rates and highlights the role of averaging weights.
- Simple sufficient conditions (Theorem 2, Corollary 1) that are easy to instantiate for r(t) = t^p.
- Primal reformulation and worked examples (orthant, simplex) that make the geometry concrete; the accelerated replicator dynamics are of independent interest.
- Conceptually appealing adaptive averaging rule: it targets the Lyapunov’s time derivative directly and thus provably preserves the continuous-time rate.
- Experiments, albeit small scale, nicely visualize trajectories, energy, and Lyapunov behavior, and show consistent improvements over restarts.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty relative to the Bregman Lagrangian framework [23] is limited on the theoretical side. The condition η = (w r)/W with w/W ≥ r′/r and the resulting 1/r(t) rate essentially recapitulate that framework in different notation. The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning: what is strictly new beyond the energy/Lyapunov presentation and the adaptive heuristic?
- The key adaptive averaging contribution is only guaranteed in continuous time. The discrete algorithm’s rate guarantee is deferred to the supplement; in the main text, it is unclear under what step size and regularity conditions the monotonic Lyapunov decrease and the O(1/k^2) rate survive discretization. A concise theorem and proof sketch in the main text would substantially strengthen the paper.
- Condition (2) in Theorem 2, ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − η W/w) ≤ 0, depends on the trajectory. While Corollary 1 removes the dependence, it collapses to the known parametrization. For the adaptive rule, feasibility of enforcing η ≥ r′ and a(t) ≥ η r is not always discussed beyond the r(t)=t^2 choice; more guidance on choosing η for general r would help.
- Smoothness and geometry assumptions are not fully specified. The paper assumes ∇f and ∇ψ* are Lipschitz but does not specify norms or compatibility with the Bregman geometry. For entropy on the simplex, Lipschitz constants are norm-dependent. Please clarify the norm pair used for L-smoothness and strong convexity, and when ∇ψ* is globally Lipschitz on E* with domain constraints (indicator δX).
- The discrete algorithm uses both ψ (for the ž-update) and a separate regularizer R for the x̃-update without clear motivation. Why is R needed if ψ already encodes the geometry? How are γ, β, s chosen, and how sensitive is performance?
- The adaptive discrete rule replaces the continuous-time criterion sign⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩ with a simple decrease test f(x̃(k+1)) ≤ f(x̃(k)). This proxy may be conservative/fragile; more discussion on this choice, its theoretical status, and alternatives (e.g., using ⟨∇f(x(k)), x(k+1) − x(k)⟩) would be helpful. Also, there are typos in the definition of ak (lines 6–8).
- Experiments are small (R^3 simplices), deterministic, and do not include modern baselines such as monotone FISTA, line-search variants, or stochastic settings where adaptivity could shine. Claims of superiority over restarts should be tempered or supported with broader tests and timing/robustness analyses.
- Strongly convex case: the paper conjectures faster rates are possible with adaptive averaging but provides no theory. Either add a partial result (e.g., linear convergence under strong convexity and appropriate η,w) or tone down the claim.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a succinct theorem (in the main text) establishing that the discrete adaptive method achieves O(1/k^2) under standard assumptions, with a clear Lyapunov argument and explicit constants and step size restrictions.
- Clarify the relationship and differences with [23]: what cannot be obtained directly from the Bregman Lagrangian? Emphasize what the adaptive rule achieves that restart-based schemes do not, ideally with a theoretical statement.
- Specify norms and smoothness/strong convexity assumptions consistently throughout; discuss when ∇ψ* is Lipschitz for common choices (entropy, Euclidean).
- Clean up Algorithm 1 notation (λk formula, ak updates), define all parameters, and explain the role of R versus ψ.
- Expand experiments: higher dimensions, different geometries (orthant), noisy gradients, and compare against monotone FISTA/line-search FISTA. Report wall-clock and sensitivity to parameters.

Pros
- Elegant and unified continuous-time analysis; clear energy interpretation.
- Simple, general adaptive idea grounded in Lyapunov decrease; promising empirical behavior.
- Useful explicit accelerated replicator dynamics.

Cons
- Theoretical novelty over existing ODE frameworks is modest.
- Discrete-time guarantees are not established in the main text; experiments are limited.
- Some assumptions/definitions lack precision; algorithmic details need clarification.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The adaptive averaging idea is interesting and broadly applicable, and the paper is well written and insightful in continuous time. With stronger discrete-time guarantees and more extensive experiments, it would be a clear accept.

Confidence
High on the relation to prior continuous-time acceleration literature; medium on the discrete algorithm’s guarantees given they are in the supplement.
