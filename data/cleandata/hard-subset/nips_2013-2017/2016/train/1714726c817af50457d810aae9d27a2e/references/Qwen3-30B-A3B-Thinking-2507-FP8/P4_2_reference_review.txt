

**Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"**

The paper studies continuous-time accelerated descent dynamics for convex optimization, extending the work of Su et al. [20] and the authors' prior NIPS paper [11]. It introduces a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, and establishes a unified analysis using a Lyapunov function to guarantee convergence rates of 1/r(t) under specific conditions. The authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the theoretical convergence rate while improving empirical performance, and demonstrate its application to the replicator dynamics in simplex-constrained optimization.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical analysis. The authors prove existence and uniqueness of solutions using the Cauchy-Lipschitz theorem, establish sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to decrease (Theorem 2), and derive a discrete algorithm with convergence guarantees. The numerical experiments in Section 5 provide empirical support for the theoretical claims, demonstrating that adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics in several examples. The paper carefully distinguishes between continuous-time and discrete-time settings, though the connection between the two could be strengthened.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical results to applications. Mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the examples (positive-orthant-constrained dynamics, simplex-constrained dynamics) effectively illustrate the theory. The supplementary material (referenced but not included here) likely provides additional detail for reproducibility. The Lyapunov function framework is effectively used to unify the analysis, making the theoretical contributions accessible.

**Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution by generalizing the AMD dynamics to a broader family with generalized averaging. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel in its approach to preserving convergence rates while adaptively computing weights. While building on previous work (Su et al. [20], Krichene et al. [11]), the paper extends these by introducing a unified framework for weight functions. The connection to the replicator dynamics in Section 3 is a significant new application of acceleration techniques. The paper properly cites relevant NIPS work and distinguishes its contributions from prior art.

**Significance**: The results have meaningful implications for optimization algorithm design, particularly for constrained convex problems. The adaptive averaging heuristic could benefit practitioners seeking to improve accelerated methods' performance. The paper advances the understanding of continuous-time dynamics in optimization, which has been a topic of interest at NIPS (e.g., [11, 20]). The numerical experiments demonstrate that adaptive averaging outperforms existing heuristics in some cases, suggesting practical utility.

**Strengths**: 
1. Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-justified sufficient conditions for convergence.
2. Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance.
3. Clear connection to the replicator dynamics, providing a meaningful application.
4. Well-structured presentation with effective examples.

**Weaknesses**:
1. The discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) appears somewhat ad hoc; a more thorough analysis of discrete-time convergence would strengthen the practical implications.
2. Numerical experiments are limited to low-dimensional (R^3) examples; more extensive experiments on larger-scale problems would better demonstrate practical utility.
3. The paper does not demonstrate a faster convergence rate than the theoretical bound, only a heuristic that preserves the rate while improving performance in practice.

**Arguments for acceptance**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the analysis of accelerated descent dynamics. The generalization of AMD dynamics and adaptive averaging heuristic represent a meaningful extension of previous work. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis with supporting experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance**: The connection to practical discrete-time algorithms is somewhat tenuous, and the numerical experiments lack scalability. The paper does not demonstrate a faster convergence rate than existing methods, only a heuristic that preserves the rate.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of continuous-time optimization dynamics. The generalization of AMD dynamics and adaptive averaging heuristic represent a meaningful advancement beyond prior work at NIPS. While the practical implications could be strengthened with more extensive experiments, the theoretical contribution is strong and relevant to the NIPS community. The paper meets all four evaluation criteria: it is technically sound, clearly written, original in its generalization and heuristic, and significant for advancing the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The paper's contribution aligns well with NIPS topics on optimization algorithms and continuous-time dynamics.
