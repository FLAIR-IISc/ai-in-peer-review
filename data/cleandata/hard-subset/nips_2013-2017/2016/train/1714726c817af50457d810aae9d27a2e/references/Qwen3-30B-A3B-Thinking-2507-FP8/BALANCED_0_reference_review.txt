

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies a family of continuous-time accelerated descent dynamics for convex optimization, generalizing prior work on Nesterov's accelerated methods [14, 15, 20] and the authors' own prior work on accelerated mirror descent [11]. The authors introduce a unified framework using Lyapunov functions to analyze convergence rates for a broad class of "Accelerated Mirror Descent" (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η. The main contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate while potentially improving empirical performance.

The paper makes several strong theoretical contributions. Theorem 2 provides sufficient conditions for a Lyapunov function to be decreasing, guaranteeing convergence at a specified rate. Corollary 1 gives explicit constructions of weight functions that satisfy these conditions, and the adaptive heuristic (Section 4) is grounded in the theoretical framework by preserving the Lyapunov function. The primal representation (Section 3) nicely connects to known dynamics, particularly showing how the replicator dynamics can be accelerated. The numerical experiments (Section 5) convincingly demonstrate that adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics across various convex optimization problems.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical framework is well-constructed and general, providing a unified perspective on accelerated methods through continuous-time dynamics.
2. The adaptive averaging heuristic is theoretically grounded and practically useful, preserving the convergence rate while improving empirical performance.
3. The connection to replicator dynamics in the simplex-constrained case is particularly elegant and demonstrates the framework's applicability to well-studied problems in evolutionary game theory.
4. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper does not sufficiently explain why the adaptive heuristic is necessary when the optimal predetermined weights (which also guarantee the convergence rate) could be used. The authors claim the adaptive heuristic "gives a significant improvement," but don't provide a clear theoretical justification for why the adaptive choice would outperform the optimal predetermined choice in general.
2. The relationship to other adaptive methods like AdaGrad [7] and Adam [10] is superficial. The authors note that these methods are "not designed for accelerated methods," but don't elaborate on why their approach is fundamentally different or more appropriate for acceleration.
3. The paper lacks a discussion of the computational cost of the adaptive heuristic. In discrete time, evaluating the adaptive weights might require additional gradient evaluations, which could offset any potential speedup.
4. The theoretical results are somewhat abstract, and the paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations of why the adaptive heuristic works well, beyond just the theoretical guarantee.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. The authors should clarify the theoretical advantage of the adaptive heuristic over using the optimal predetermined weights. Do they provide better convergence rates for non-strongly convex functions, or are they simply more robust to unknown problem parameters?
2. The paper should more thoroughly compare the adaptive heuristic to other adaptive methods, particularly in the context of acceleration. This would strengthen the claim that their approach is uniquely suited for accelerated methods.
3. The authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of their adaptive heuristic and analyze its trade-offs with the potential speedup.
4. The paper could include a more intuitive explanation of the adaptive heuristic, perhaps by visualizing how the weights change along the trajectory for different problem instances.

**Assessment:**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-justified theoretical results. The numerical experiments provide good empirical validation, though the theoretical explanation for the adaptive heuristic's superiority is somewhat lacking.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, though some sections (particularly the primal representation) could be made more accessible to readers less familiar with mirror descent.

**Originality:** The paper generalizes prior work on accelerated methods through a unified framework with adaptive averaging. The connection to replicator dynamics is particularly novel and shows the framework's applicability to established problems.

**Significance:** The work advances the understanding of accelerated methods through continuous-time dynamics and provides a theoretically grounded heuristic that improves empirical performance. The connection to replicator dynamics demonstrates practical applicability, and the adaptive heuristic could have broad implications for optimization algorithms.

**Recommendation:** Accept. While the paper has some weaknesses in explaining the necessity of the adaptive heuristic, its theoretical contributions, clear organization, and empirical validation make it a strong contribution to the field. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a meaningful advancement in understanding and improving accelerated optimization methods, and the paper provides sufficient theoretical grounding and empirical evidence to support its claims.

The paper advances the state of the art in understanding accelerated methods through continuous-time dynamics, and the adaptive heuristic it introduces provides a practical improvement with theoretical guarantees. The connection to replicator dynamics is particularly compelling, demonstrating the framework's applicability to well-studied problems. With minor revisions to clarify the theoretical advantages of the adaptive heuristic and its relationship to other adaptive methods, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
