Summary
The paper studies continuous-time first-order dynamics for convex optimization and generalizes accelerated mirror descent (AMD) by introducing a family of ODEs parameterized by two positive weight functions: w(t) controlling averaging and η(t) controlling gradient accumulation. The authors define a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*) and provide sufficient conditions under which Lr is nonincreasing, yielding a convergence guarantee f(X(t)) − f* ≤ const/r(t). This unifies and extends prior acceleration-ODE connections by allowing broad averaging schemes; the standard O(1/t^2) rate is recovered and generalized to r(t) = t^p. They derive a primal form of the dynamics, give examples (positive orthant; simplex), and obtain an “accelerated replicator” ODE. They further introduce a second energy function Er that decreases under the same conditions. Building on the Lyapunov analysis, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts the weights to further decrease Lr while preserving the rate, and they present a discrete algorithm for the t^2 case. Experiments on simplex-constrained problems in R^3 compare adaptive averaging to gradient- and speed-based restarting heuristics, showing consistent improvements and monotonic decrease of the Lyapunov/energy functions.

Relation to prior work
The work relates to continuous-time interpretations of Nesterov acceleration and Bregman Lagrangians (Su et al.; Wibisono et al.), and to mirror descent and proximal acceleration (Nesterov; Nemirovski–Yudin; Krichene et al.). It is well-positioned with respect to restart heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès; Su–Boyd–Candès) and adaptive first-order methods (AdaGrad/Adam), clarifying that those adapt step sizes rather than averaging. The paper correctly shows how its ODE covers earlier AMD as a special case and connects to the variational family in Wibisono et al. via appropriate choices of a(t)=w/W and η(t).

Quality
- Theory: The continuous-time analysis is technically sound. The existence/uniqueness result uses standard Lipschitz arguments. The main Lyapunov derivative calculation is clean; Theorem 2’s conditions are transparent and Corollary 1 gives a constructive recipe for w,η given a target r. The energy function Er and its monotonicity via monotonicity of ∇ψ* are plausible and useful for interpretation.
- Discretization: For r(t)=t^2, the adaptive heuristic is discretized into Algorithm 1. The paper states rate guarantees are in the supplement; in the main text, formal discrete-time proofs are not shown, and the adaptive rule relies on checking f(x̃) decreases, which is only indirectly tied to the continuous-time inner-product condition. This is the main theoretical gap: a clear, fully detailed discrete-time guarantee under adaptive averaging is not presented in the body.
- Experiments: Empirically sound but limited in scope (R^3 simplex). They demonstrate decreased Lyapunov and energy values and improved convergence over restarts, including in a strongly convex setting.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The ODE derivations, Lyapunov analysis, and the role of a(t)=w/W are clearly explained. The primal forms for common mirror maps (entropy on orthant/simplex) are helpful and illuminate the accelerated replicator dynamics. Some notation could be streamlined (e.g., repeated use of X for both average and primal variable; explicit link between the sign test in continuous time and the discrete monotonic f test). More implementation details (step size, β, γ choices) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The generalized AMD with arbitrary averaging weights and the accompanying Lyapunov analysis appear novel and provide a unifying perspective that subsumes earlier ODEs.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the Lyapunov rate is an original and appealing idea; linking adaptivity to the sign of the Lyapunov derivative term is insightful.
- The accelerated replicator dynamics is a nice conceptual contribution.

Significance
The work strengthens the theoretical understanding of acceleration via averaging and offers a principled way to adaptively improve practical performance while preserving guarantees. If the discrete-time guarantees for adaptive averaging are fully established, the approach could influence the design of accelerated algorithms beyond restart heuristics. As is, significance is moderate: strong theory in continuous time, promising but limited experiments, and partial discrete-time theory.

Pros
- Clear unifying ODE framework for accelerated mirror descent with generalized averaging.
- Simple sufficient conditions for prescribed rates via a Lyapunov function; constructive recipe for w,η.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic tied to Lyapunov decrease; preserves rate in continuous time.
- Secondary energy function offering physical intuition; connection to accelerated replicator dynamics.
- Empirical improvements over restart heuristics and monotone Lyapunov behavior.

Cons
- Discrete-time theory for the adaptive rule is deferred to the supplement; main text lacks a complete convergence proof and clear conditions under which O(1/k^2) is preserved.
- Experimental evaluation is small-scale (R^3 simplex), with limited tasks and no large-scale or stochastic tests; no sensitivity/ablation studies.
- The connection between the continuous-time sign condition and the discrete f(x̃) test is heuristic; a more faithful discrete analogue would strengthen claims.
- Some related algorithmic baselines (e.g., FISTA with backtracking/monotone variants, OGM) and comparisons to adaptive acceleration schemes are not discussed.

Suggestions
- Bring key parts of the discrete-time analysis for Algorithm 1 into the main paper, including explicit assumptions under which O(1/k^2) holds with adaptive averaging.
- Clarify the discrete test used to set ak and its relation to the continuous-time condition; consider a discrete Lyapunov derivative surrogate in the algorithm.
- Expand experiments to higher dimensions and other constraints; include comparisons to FISTA with monotone/backtracking and to tuned restart schedules; add sensitivity to β,s and ablations.
- Provide implementation details and code. Minor notation cleanup for readability.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical framework and adaptive averaging idea are solid and novel enough for NeurIPS, and the paper is clearly written. The main weakness is the limited empirical evaluation and the lack of fully developed discrete-time guarantees in the main text. Strengthening these would elevate the contribution.
