This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, framing the process as a coupling of dual and primal variables through averaging. The authors provide sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate using a Lyapunov argument, demonstrate acceleration of replicator dynamics via averaging, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantees and empirical validation.

The paper builds upon the recent connection between Nesterov’s accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs (e.g., [20, 11, 23]). Specifically, it generalizes the framework presented in [11], offering a more flexible analysis of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) through the introduction of weight functions *w* and *η*. The work addresses a natural question—whether different averaging schemes can impact convergence rates—and offers a positive response. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a noteworthy contribution, differentiating itself from existing methods like adaptive restarting [17, 20] by providing convergence guarantees, which are often lacking in prior work. The connection to replicator dynamics, a concept from evolutionary game theory, provides an interesting application of the framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The use of a Lyapunov function to establish convergence conditions is rigorous and well-motivated. The sufficient conditions provided in Theorem 2 are clearly stated and offer a useful tool for analyzing AMD dynamics.
*   **Generality:** The framework of AMD with generalized averaging (*w*, *η*) is quite broad, allowing for analysis of various accelerated descent algorithms.
*   **Adaptive Averaging Heuristic:** The proposed heuristic is a significant contribution. It adaptively computes weights to speed up convergence, and importantly, provides guarantees of preserving the quadratic convergence rate.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper effectively links to and builds upon prior research in accelerated methods, continuous-time dynamics, and evolutionary game theory. The replicator dynamics example provides a concrete illustration of the framework's applicability.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments demonstrate that adaptive averaging performs competitively with, and sometimes outperforms, existing heuristics.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly outlines the motivation and contributions, and the mathematical notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty in Core Framework:** While the generalization of AMD with *w* and *η* is useful, the core idea of analyzing convergence via Lyapunov functions is not entirely novel (see, e.g., [6, 9]). The primary innovation lies in the adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   **Complexity of Conditions:** While the sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) are clear, they may be difficult to verify in practice. The requirement of knowing or estimating the gradient of the Lyapunov function may be challenging.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments are relatively limited.  While the results are encouraging, further experimentation with a broader range of objective functions, constraint sets, and problem dimensions would strengthen the conclusions. The comparison to other *adaptive* methods beyond restarting (e.g., Adagrad, Adam mentioned in the introduction) is missing.
*   **Discrete Algorithm Details:** The description of the discrete adaptive averaging algorithm (Algorithm 1) is somewhat terse. More details about the implementation, such as the choice of step size *s* and the regularizer R, would be helpful.
*   **Reference to Attouch et al. (2018):** The reference to Attouch et al. regarding the rate of convergence of Nesterov's method appears late in the paper and isn't fully integrated into the discussion. This paper seems to offer a more precise characterization of the convergence rate.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound and well-motivated extension of existing work on accelerated descent dynamics.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution, offering a practical algorithm with convergence guarantees.
*   The numerical experiments provide empirical support for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The paper is well-written and clear, making it accessible to researchers in the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core framework, while generalized, is not radically novel.
*   The practical applicability of the theoretical conditions may be limited.
*   The scope of the numerical experiments is relatively narrow.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. While the core framework isn't groundbreaking, the adaptive averaging heuristic is a valuable contribution that addresses a practical problem in optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are encouraging. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically:

*   Expand the discussion of the limitations of the theoretical conditions and suggest practical strategies for verifying them.
*   Consider adding more extensive numerical experiments, including comparisons to other adaptive methods.
*   Provide more details about the implementation of Algorithm 1, including the choice of step size and regularizer.
*   Integrate the reference to Attouch et al. (2018) more fully into the discussion of convergence rates.
