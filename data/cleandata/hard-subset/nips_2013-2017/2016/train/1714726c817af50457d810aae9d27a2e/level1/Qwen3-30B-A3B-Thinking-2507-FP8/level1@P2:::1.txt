

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through the lens of continuous-time dynamics. The authors propose a generalized framework for accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by two weight functions w(t) and η(t), and provide sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate via a Lyapunov argument. They then introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes the weights w(t) to accelerate convergence while preserving the convergence rate guarantee. The paper includes theoretical guarantees for the continuous-time case and a discrete version of the algorithm, along with numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging against existing heuristics like adaptive restarting.

The paper builds upon recent NIPS work by Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11], which established connections between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs. The authors generalize this perspective by introducing a more flexible averaging scheme (AMDw,η) and showing how it unifies previous approaches. The connection to replicator dynamics (an important ODE in evolutionary game theory) is particularly noteworthy, as it demonstrates a practical application of their framework.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide rigorous proofs for their main theorems, including Theorem 2 which establishes sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing, guaranteeing convergence at the desired rate. The adaptive averaging heuristic is carefully designed to maintain these theoretical guarantees while improving empirical performance. The numerical experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method against relevant baselines (gradient restart, speed restart) across multiple objective functions (strongly convex quadratic, weakly convex quadratic, linear, and KL divergence). The results consistently show that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as existing heuristics, with significant improvements in some cases, particularly for strongly convex problems. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, noting that the Lyapunov function may not converge to zero when multiple minimizers exist.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulations are clear, with the authors taking care to define all necessary concepts (e.g., Bregman divergence, mirror map). The transition from continuous-time to discrete-time is well-explained, and Algorithm 1 provides a concrete implementation of the adaptive averaging heuristic. The examples in Section 3 (positive-orthant-constrained dynamics and simplex-constrained dynamics) are particularly helpful for illustrating the general framework. The numerical experiments are presented clearly with well-labeled figures, making it easy to understand the comparative performance of different methods.

**Originality:** While the continuous-time perspective on accelerated methods has been explored in recent NIPS papers (Su et al. [20], Krichene et al. [11]), the authors make a significant contribution by generalizing the averaging scheme and introducing the adaptive averaging heuristic. The key novelty lies in the adaptive computation of weights that preserves the convergence rate guarantee while improving empirical performance. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by providing a unified framework for various averaging schemes and proving that adaptive averaging preserves the convergence rate. The connection to replicator dynamics (Section 3) is an original application of their framework that demonstrates the broader relevance of their approach.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for both theoretical and practical reasons. Theoretically, the paper provides a unified framework for understanding accelerated methods through continuous-time dynamics, which has been a fruitful area of research at NIPS. Practically, the adaptive averaging heuristic offers a simple yet effective way to improve the convergence of accelerated methods without sacrificing convergence guarantees. The empirical results showing improved performance over existing heuristics, particularly in the strongly convex case, suggest that this approach could be valuable for practitioners. The connection to replicator dynamics also opens up new possibilities for applications in game theory and other fields.

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous proofs
- Clear connection to previous NIPS work (Su et al. [20], Krichene et al. [11])
- Practical adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence guarantees
- Well-designed numerical experiments with multiple test cases
- Novel application to replicator dynamics
- Excellent clarity and organization

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity between adaptive averaging and restarting heuristics
- The theoretical analysis focuses on the continuous-time case, with only a sketch of the discrete-time analysis in the supplementary material
- While the experiments show improved performance, the paper does not provide a comprehensive analysis of when adaptive averaging is particularly advantageous compared to other methods

**Conclusion:**
This paper makes a strong contribution to the understanding and application of accelerated optimization methods. The theoretical results are sound, the adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and empirically validated, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. The connection to replicator dynamics provides a compelling application example, and the results have clear significance for both the theory and practice of optimization. The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria at a high level, with particularly strong quality and clarity. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a meaningful advancement in the field of accelerated methods that could benefit both researchers and practitioners.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's theoretical contributions are significant, the experimental results are compelling, and the writing is clear and well-organized. The work builds meaningfully on recent NIPS publications while offering novel insights and a practical heuristic that improves upon existing approaches.
