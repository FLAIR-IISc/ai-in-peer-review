- Studies accelerated mirror-descent via generalized averaging: coupled ODE Ż = −η(t)∇f(X) with X a weighted mirror-average, and Lyapunov Lr to get f(X)−f* ≤ O(1/r(t)).
- Derives simple sufficient conditions on η and w for Lyapunov decrease and unifies/extensions of AMD and ODE acceleration literature.
- Provides an equivalent primal representation and concrete examples (positive orthant, simplex), yielding an “accelerated” replicator dynamics.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that decreases the Lyapunov derivative while preserving target rate, with a discrete-time first-order algorithm claiming O(1/k^2)-type rate.
- Continuous-time analysis is sound: existence/uniqueness handled, transparent Lyapunov derivative bound, and a second energy function Er adds intuition.
- Framework is general and subsumes prior AMD, linking parameter choices a(t), r(t), η(t); examples clarify the operator interpretations.
- Discrete-time convergence guarantees are mainly in the supplement; main text lacks assumptions (on ψ, R, step-sizes) and a proof sketch for verifiability.
- Heuristic–theory gap: continuous trigger uses sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩, while discrete heuristic uses f(x̃(k+1)) ≤ f(x̃(k)); need a clear discrete Lyapunov and bridge between triggers.
- Paper is well written and organized; reparameterization and Lyapunov analysis are crisp and examples helpful.
- Some technical assumptions (smoothness/strong convexity of ψ, ψ∗; Lipschitzness of ∇ψ∗; properties of R) should be summarized explicitly in the main text.
- Discretization details and the discrete-rate proof should be summarized as a theorem with a main-text proof sketch.
- Experimental setup lacks specifics (step sizes, β, γ, s, stopping criteria) and quantitative metrics.
- Novelty: generalized AMD with explicit weight/learning-rate design via Lyapunov is a neat unification; adaptive averaging heuristic appears novel and distinct from restarts and adaptive step-size methods.
- Acceleration of replicator dynamics is a notable conceptual contribution with potential interest in game dynamics and learning-in-games.
- Significance is moderate: theory offers a useful design toolkit, but empirical evaluation is limited to low-dimensional problems and needs broader benchmarks and timing comparisons.
- Pros: clean continuous-time framework, adaptive averaging with theoretical guarantees and promising empirical behavior, strong links to prior ODE-based acceleration, and nice simplex application.
- Cons: discrete-time guarantees deferred to supplement, heuristic differs from continuous condition, small-scale qualitative experiments, no strong-convexity linear-rate results, and some mirror-map assumptions unstated.
- Key questions for authors: include a discrete-time theorem with assumptions/proof sketch; can adaptive averaging yield linear rates under μ-strong convexity without restart; sensitivity/adaptive rules for β,γ,s; and higher-dimensional wall-clock comparisons to FISTA/restarted methods.
- Suggested revisions: move a concise discrete-time theorem/Lyapunov argument to main text, add higher-dimensional constrained ML experiments with time-to-ε plots, explore stochastic/noise robustness, and discuss computational overhead of adaptive averaging vs restarts.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — solid theoretical contribution and novel adaptive idea, but empirical validation should be expanded.
