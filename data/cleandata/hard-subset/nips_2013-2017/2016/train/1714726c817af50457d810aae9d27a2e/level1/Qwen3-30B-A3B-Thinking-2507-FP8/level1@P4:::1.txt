

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through the lens of continuous-time ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The authors generalize the Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics introduced in prior NIPS work (Krichene et al., 2015), parameterized by two weight functions w and η. Using a Lyapunov argument, they provide sufficient conditions on these weight functions to achieve a desired convergence rate. The paper demonstrates how the replicator dynamics (mirror descent on the simplex) can be accelerated through a simple averaging scheme, and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function. The authors prove this heuristic preserves the quadratic convergence rate and provide numerical experiments comparing it with adaptive restarting heuristics.

The paper builds upon the NIPS 2015 work by Krichene et al. ("Accelerated Mirror Descent in Continuous and Discrete Time") and the NIPS 2014 work by Su et al. ("A Differential Equation for Modeling Nesterov's Accelerated Gradient Method"), extending their continuous-time framework to a more general averaging scheme. It also connects to the replicator dynamics literature in evolutionary game theory, which has been studied in the context of optimization (e.g., Hofbauer and Sigmund, 1998).

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees using Lyapunov functions, with clear proofs of sufficient conditions for convergence. Theorems 2 and 3 establish precise conditions under which the Lyapunov function decreases, guaranteeing the convergence rate. The adaptive averaging heuristic is theoretically grounded and proven to preserve the convergence rate.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to examples and experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is clearly articulated, with Algorithm 1 providing a concrete implementation of the adaptive averaging heuristic.

3. **Originality:** The paper generalizes prior work on accelerated mirror descent by introducing a broad family of AMD dynamics parameterized by two weight functions. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, distinct from existing restarting heuristics, and provides a new perspective on accelerating first-order methods. The application to replicator dynamics on the simplex is particularly insightful.

4. **Significance:** The adaptive averaging heuristic addresses a practical problem in optimization with clear empirical benefits over existing methods. The numerical experiments demonstrate significant improvements in some cases, particularly for strongly convex problems. The work has potential applications beyond optimization, including evolutionary game theory through the replicator dynamics connection.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees for Adaptive Heuristic:** While the paper proves the adaptive averaging heuristic preserves the convergence rate, it does not provide guarantees on the rate of decrease of the Lyapunov function under adaptation. The authors show it preserves the quadratic convergence rate but don't establish whether it achieves a faster rate than predetermined schedules.

2. **Narrow Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments are limited to simplex-constrained problems in R3. While the supplementary material mentions additional experiments in higher dimensions, the main paper doesn't provide comprehensive comparisons across diverse problem types and dimensions.

3. **Connection to Practical Implementation:** The paper focuses on continuous-time dynamics and provides a discrete analog via Algorithm 1, but doesn't thoroughly discuss computational complexity or practical implementation challenges for large-scale problems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a theoretically sound generalization of accelerated mirror descent dynamics
- Introduces a novel adaptive averaging heuristic with provable convergence guarantees
- Demonstrates clear empirical improvements over existing methods
- Connects optimization theory to replicator dynamics with potential applications in game theory
- Builds upon and extends previous NIPS work in a meaningful way

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited scope of numerical experiments (only simplex-constrained problems)
- Does not establish whether adaptive averaging achieves faster convergence rates than predetermined schedules
- The discrete implementation (Algorithm 1) could benefit from more detailed computational complexity analysis

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of optimization, extending the continuous-time framework for accelerated methods with a novel adaptive averaging heuristic. The work is technically sound, well-written, and builds upon relevant previous NIPS work. While the numerical experiments could be expanded, the theoretical guarantees and clear empirical improvements over existing methods make this a valuable contribution to the field. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant advancement in understanding how to accelerate first-order methods while preserving convergence guarantees.

The paper satisfies all four review criteria: it is of high technical quality with rigorous proofs, it is clearly written with well-organized presentation, it demonstrates novelty through the generalization of accelerated mirror descent dynamics and the adaptive averaging heuristic, and it addresses a significant problem in optimization with potential impact on both theory and practice.

The paper is within scope for NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning optimization with a novel theoretical approach. The work is a meaningful contribution to the field of optimization algorithms, which is a core topic for NIPS.
