Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated mirror-descent dynamics for constrained smooth convex optimization through the lens of generalized averaging. The authors consider a coupled continuous-time system in dual and primal variables: Ż(t) = −η(t)∇f(X(t)), while the primal X(t) is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f∗) + Dψ∗(Z, z∗), they derive simple sufficient conditions on η and w that guarantee Lr is decreasing and thus f(X(t)) − f∗ ≤ O(1/r(t)). This unifies and extends the averaged formulation of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) in Krichene et al. [11] and connects to ODE perspectives on acceleration (e.g., Su et al. [20], Wibisono et al. [23]). The work also provides an equivalent primal representation, with examples for the positive orthant and the simplex. In particular, the replicator dynamics (mirror descent on simplex) obtain an “accelerated” variant via the proposed averaging scheme.

The second main contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that modifies the weight function to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative, while preserving the target rate. The authors analyze this in continuous time and propose a discrete-time algorithm (a first-order accelerated method) that preserves the O(1/k^2)-type rate. Experiments on low-dimensional constrained problems compare favorably to adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient restart [17], speed restart [20]).

Quality
- Theory: The continuous-time analysis is sound and clean. Existence/uniqueness (Theorem 1) is handled via Cauchy–Lipschitz using a reformulation where w(t)/W(t) is bounded away from t0>0. The Lyapunov derivative bound (Eq. 3) yields transparent sufficient conditions (Theorem 2), including a useful trajectory-independent corollary by tying η, w, r (Corollary 1). The second “energy” function Er is a nice addition that matches physical intuition and provides additional structure.
- Generality: The framework subsumes prior AMD and connects to [23] by identifying parameter choices linking a(t), r(t), and η(t). The examples (orthant, simplex) help instantiate the operator ∇^2ψ∗∘∇ψ and make the interpretation concrete (accelerated replicator).
- Discrete time: The discrete adaptive averaging algorithm is natural; however, the key convergence guarantee is deferred to the supplement. For a NeurIPS paper, more details in the main text (assumptions on ψ, R, step-size regimes, proof sketch) would improve verifiability.
- Heuristic–theory gap: The continuous-time condition uses the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩, but the discrete heuristic triggers on f(x̃(k+1)) ≤ f(x̃(k)). The paper claims preservation of the quadratic rate; it would be helpful to clearly articulate the discrete Lyapunov function and show how the update decisions ensure its decrease.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The Lyapunov analysis is crisp, and the reparameterization w/W = a(t) clarifies design freedom. The primal forms and concrete examples are helpful. That said:
- Some technical assumptions (smoothness/strong convexity of ψ, ψ∗; properties of R; Lipschitzness of ∇ψ∗) could be summarized more explicitly in the main text, with a minimal set needed for each theorem.
- The discretization details and rate proof could be summarized in a short theorem with a proof sketch in the main text.
- Experimental setup should include more specifics (step sizes, β, γ, s, stopping criteria) and quantitative metrics.

Originality
- The generalized AMD with explicit weight/learning-rate conditions via Lyapunov analysis is a neat unification and extension beyond [11]. The explicit design recipe via a(t) and the corollary are conceptually simple yet useful.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic appears novel for accelerated mirror descent and distinct from adaptive restart and from adaptive step-size methods (Adagrad [7], Adam [10]).
- The acceleration of replicator dynamics via averaging is a nice conceptual contribution that may interest communities in game dynamics and learning-in-games.

Significance
- Theory: The sufficient conditions provide a simple design toolkit for accelerated dynamics with target rates, and the adaptive mechanism preserves rates while often improving practical performance. This is a useful addition to the ODE view of acceleration.
- Practice: Current experiments are low-dimensional (mostly R^3 simplex) and limited to a few convex objectives. To claim broader impact, more realistic, higher-dimensional tests (e.g., constrained logistic regression, portfolio, sparse models) and wall-clock/time-to-accuracy comparisons against FISTA with adaptive restart, projected accelerated methods, and modern mirror-prox variants would strengthen the case.
- Overall, significance is moderate: the conceptual unification and the adaptive averaging idea are interesting and potentially influential, but the empirical evaluation is not yet commensurate.

Pros
- Clean, general continuous-time framework with explicit, easily verifiable conditions for guaranteed rates.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantees (monotone Lyapunov/energy, preserved rate) and promising empirical behavior vs. adaptive restarting.
- Clear linkages to and generalization of prior ODE-based acceleration (Su et al. [20], Wibisono et al. [23]) and to averaging–acceleration connections (Flammarion & Bach [8]).
- Nice application to replicator dynamics on the simplex.

Cons
- Discrete-time guarantees are largely in the supplement; the heuristic trigger differs from the continuous-time condition and deserves a clearer theoretical bridge.
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale and mostly qualitative; lacks higher-dimensional, standardized benchmarks and timing.
- No results under strong convexity beyond heuristics; could the adaptive scheme provably achieve linear rates without restart?
- Some assumptions (smoothness of ψ∗, feasibility mapping) could be stated more explicitly with discussion of practical mirror maps.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide in the main text a theorem (with assumptions) for the discrete algorithm’s rate and a brief proof sketch?
2. Under μ-strong convexity, can adaptive averaging achieve O(exp(−√(μ/L) k)) or linear rates without restarting? Any partial results?
3. How sensitive is performance to choices of β, γ, s? Any adaptive rules for these?
4. Can you report higher-dimensional experiments (e.g., d≥1000), with wall-clock comparisons to FISTA with adaptive restart and projected AGD?

Suggestions
- Move a concise discrete-time theorem and Lyapunov argument to the main paper.
- Add experiments on standard constrained ML problems (e.g., simplex-constrained logistic regression), with quantitative tables and time-to-ε plots.
- Explore stochastic gradients and noise robustness; relate to Adagrad/Adam-style adaptivity.
- Discuss computational overhead of adaptive averaging vs. restart triggers.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper provides a clear and general analysis of accelerated descent with generalized averaging and introduces a simple, theoretically grounded adaptive averaging heuristic. The theoretical contribution is solid and of interest to the NeurIPS optimization community; empirical validation should be expanded, but the methodological clarity and connections to prior work merit acceptance.
