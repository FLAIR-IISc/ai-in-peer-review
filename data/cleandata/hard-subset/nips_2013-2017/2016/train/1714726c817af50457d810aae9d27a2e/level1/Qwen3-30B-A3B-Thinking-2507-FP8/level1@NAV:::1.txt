

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, introducing a generalized averaging scheme for accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics. The authors provide a unified Lyapunov-based analysis of AMD dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), establishing sufficient conditions for desired convergence rates. They demonstrate how the replicator dynamics (mirror descent on the simplex) can be accelerated through this averaging framework, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes weights to speed up convergence while preserving the quadratic convergence rate. The paper builds on prior NIPS work by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) and Su et al. (NIPS 2014), extending their continuous-time analysis to a broader family of dynamics.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical framework is technically sound with well-constructed Lyapunov arguments that clearly connect continuous-time dynamics to discrete-time acceleration. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, theoretically justified, and empirically outperforms existing restarting heuristics in the experiments. The clarity of the mathematical exposition is excellent, with well-organized sections and clear definitions. The significance of the work is demonstrated through both theoretical contributions and empirical results showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art heuristics.

Potential weaknesses include limited experimental scope (only R^3 simplex examples), which leaves open questions about performance on more complex constrained problems. While the paper references related work, the connection to Flammarion & Bach's "From averaging to acceleration" (COLT 2015) could be more explicitly discussed to better position the novelty. Additionally, the practical implications for large-scale machine learning applications could be strengthened with more concrete examples.

Pro arguments: Strong theoretical foundation, novel adaptive heuristic with proven convergence guarantees, clear exposition, and compelling empirical results showing consistent improvements over existing methods.

Con arguments: Limited experimental validation across diverse problem types, and insufficient discussion of how this work relates to recent related contributions.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the theoretical understanding of accelerated optimization dynamics while providing a practical heuristic that improves upon existing methods. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
