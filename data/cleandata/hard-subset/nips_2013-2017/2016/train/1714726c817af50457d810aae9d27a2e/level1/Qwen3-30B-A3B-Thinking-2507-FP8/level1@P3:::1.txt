

# Peer Review: "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a unified continuous-time framework. The authors generalize the accelerated mirror descent dynamics by introducing a flexible averaging scheme parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), and provide sufficient conditions via Lyapunov analysis for achieving desired convergence rates. The work builds upon and extends the continuous-time perspective of accelerated methods pioneered by Su et al. (NIPS 2014) and Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015), while offering novel theoretical insights and practical heuristics.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) a rigorous Lyapunov analysis for the generalized accelerated mirror descent dynamics; (2) an elegant connection to replicator dynamics (accelerating evolutionary game theory dynamics); (3) a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance; and (4) numerical experiments demonstrating advantages over existing restarting heuristics.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation:** The Lyapunov analysis is well-executed and provides clear, verifiable conditions for convergence. The paper successfully unifies various accelerated methods under a single framework, showing how different weight functions correspond to known methods (e.g., the standard Nesterov acceleration scheme). The derivation of sufficient conditions in Theorem 2 is particularly strong.

2. **Novel Application:** The connection to replicator dynamics represents a significant contribution to both optimization and evolutionary game theory. The paper demonstrates how to accelerate this well-studied dynamics with a simple averaging scheme, which could have meaningful implications for applications in game theory and evolutionary biology.

3. **Practical Heuristic:** The adaptive averaging heuristic (Algorithm 1) is theoretically grounded and preserves the convergence rate guarantees, which is a substantial improvement over existing heuristics that often sacrifice theoretical guarantees for empirical speedups. The paper convincingly demonstrates that adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics in numerical experiments.

4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical framework is developed systematically, with clear transitions between sections. The examples (positive orthant and simplex dynamics) are well-chosen to illustrate the theory's generality and utility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Discrete-Time Connection:** The primary weakness is the insufficient development of the discrete-time counterpart. While the paper mentions a discrete version in the supplementary material, the theoretical guarantees for the discrete algorithm are not fully established. The connection between the continuous-time Lyapunov analysis and the discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is not rigorously justified, which is a critical gap given that the main application would be in discrete optimization algorithms.

2. **Limited Experiments:** The numerical experiments are limited to simple problems on the simplex in R^3. While this is sufficient to demonstrate the core idea, the paper would be stronger with experiments on more complex problems (e.g., higher-dimensional problems or non-simplex constraints) and comparisons with a broader set of adaptive methods.

3. **Practical Implementation Details:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is described in continuous time, but the discrete implementation (Algorithm 1) lacks details on how to compute the necessary quantities efficiently. The paper doesn't address computational complexity or practical considerations for large-scale problems.

4. **Theoretical Limits:** The paper claims "significant improvements" in some cases, but the experiments don't quantify these improvements or demonstrate a consistent advantage across different problem types. The analysis is limited to convex problems, while the paper briefly mentions non-convex applications without theoretical justification.

**Pro/Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro (for acceptance):* This is a theoretically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of accelerated methods through a unified continuous-time framework. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, preserves convergence guarantees, and shows empirical promise. The connection to replicator dynamics is insightful and well-executed. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, with strong theoretical grounding.

*Con (against acceptance):* The lack of rigorous discrete-time convergence guarantees undermines the practical significance of the proposed heuristic. The experiments are too limited to fully support the claim of "significant improvements" across diverse problem types. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with other adaptive methods beyond restarting heuristics.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of accelerated methods through a unified continuous-time perspective. The paper makes a compelling case for adaptive averaging as a superior heuristic that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance. While the discrete-time connection is not fully developed, the continuous-time framework is elegant and well-justified.

The paper is clearly within NIPS scope, as it addresses fundamental questions in optimization theory with significant implications for machine learning. The work builds meaningfully on prior NIPS publications (Su et al. 2014, Krichene et al. 2015) while providing novel theoretical insights and practical heuristics.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the discrete-time connection and expand the experimental evaluation. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, and the adaptive averaging heuristic has the potential to influence future work on accelerated methods.
