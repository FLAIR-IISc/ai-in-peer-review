- Studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent with generalized averaging and introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves accelerated rates.  
- Formulates a broad ODE family parameterized by gradient accumulation rate η(t) and averaging weights w(t).  
- Analyzes the system via Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t)=r(t)(f(X)−f*)+Dψ*(Z,z*) and derives sufficient conditions on (η,w,r) to guarantee rate 1/r(t).  
- Recovers known accelerated schedules as special cases and gives a primal representation linking to mirror descent and replicator dynamics on the simplex.  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging rule that directly targets decreasing the Lyapunov derivative and preserves accelerated rates.  
- Presents a discrete-time scheme with guarantees for the quadratic (t^2) rate.  
- Empirical comparisons show favorable performance versus adaptive restart heuristics, with strong gains on some strongly convex instances.  
- Theoretical contributions are sound: existence/uniqueness, Lyapunov decrease under verifiable conditions, and an energy function that decreases in the same regime.  
- Discrete-time analysis for the adaptive rule appears correct, though several proofs are deferred to the supplement.  
- Empirical evaluation supports claims but is limited in scale, diversity, and lacks stochastic/noisy-gradient experiments.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized; dual/primal formulations are helpful.  
- Suggested clarity improvements: move key discrete-proof parts from supplement, clarify role and sensitivity of initialization t0, provide guidance for choosing β and step sizes, and expand algorithm pseudocode and computational-cost discussion.  
- Originality: novel generalized averaging view with trajectory-dependent conditions and an adaptive averaging rule targeting Lyapunov decrease; accelerating replicator dynamics is a neat example.  
- Significance: framework could influence design of accelerated methods beyond fixed schedules and restarts; practical impact would be strengthened by larger-scale and stochastic tests.  
- Pros: unified Lyapunov analysis giving design rules, formal guarantees for adaptive averaging, clear replicator/mirror-map connections, and empirical gains over restarts.  
- Cons: trajectory-dependent condition in Theorem 2 limits a priori design and practical tuning, experiments are small-scale and deterministic, no guarantees beyond quadratic rate for strongly convex objectives, and some proofs/discretization details are relegated to the supplement.
