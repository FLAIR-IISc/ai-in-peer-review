

# Peer Review: "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending the continuous-time framework introduced by Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11]. The authors propose a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), which can be interpreted as a coupling of a dual variable accumulating gradients at rate η(t) and a primal variable obtained as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory. The authors provide a unified Lyapunov-based analysis that gives sufficient conditions on w and η to achieve desired convergence rates. They demonstrate how this framework can accelerate the replicator dynamics (a mirror descent method on the simplex) and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes the weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function. The work builds directly on Krichene et al. [11], which established the connection between accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs, and extends it by providing a more general framework and adaptive mechanism.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Quality**: The paper provides a technically sound theoretical analysis using Lyapunov arguments, establishing sufficient conditions for convergence rates. Theorems 2 and 3 are well-motivated and rigorously proven, with the proofs carefully checking the necessary conditions.

2. **Novel Contribution**: The adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution that preserves the convergence rate while potentially improving empirical performance. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work by providing a unified framework for various averaging schemes, rather than just studying specific cases.

3. **Practical Significance**: The numerical experiments demonstrate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as existing heuristics (like adaptive restarting) and provides significant improvements in some cases, particularly for strongly convex problems. The connection to replicator dynamics is especially interesting, as it opens new applications in evolutionary game theory.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical notation. The examples (positive orthant and simplex dynamics) effectively illustrate the general framework. The adaptive averaging heuristic is clearly motivated and described.

5. **Comprehensive Comparison**: The experiments compare adaptive averaging against multiple existing heuristics (gradient restarting, speed restarting) across different objective functions, providing a thorough empirical evaluation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Analysis**: The theoretical guarantees are limited to continuous-time dynamics, and while the authors provide a discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1), the convergence rate bound for the discrete version is only given in the supplementary material. A more detailed analysis of the discrete-time convergence would strengthen the paper.

2. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper lacks discussion of the computational cost of the adaptive averaging heuristic compared to simpler restart heuristics. For instance, how much additional computation is required per iteration to compute the adaptive weights?

3. **Limited Experimental Scope**: While the experiments in R^3 are well-designed, the paper only considers a few specific objective functions. It would be valuable to see results on larger-scale optimization problems or more complex constrained settings that are common in machine learning.

4. **Comparison with Other Adaptive Methods**: The paper briefly mentions Adagrad and Adam but doesn't thoroughly compare adaptive averaging with these methods, which are widely used in practice for first-order optimization.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The theoretical contribution is significant and fills an important gap in the understanding of accelerated methods.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, provides theoretical guarantees, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages.
- The connection to replicator dynamics provides a new perspective on evolutionary game theory applications.
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents both theoretical and experimental results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The discrete-time analysis is not sufficiently developed in the main paper.
- The practical implications for large-scale machine learning applications could be better articulated.
- The experimental scope is limited to small-scale problems, which may not fully reflect the method's utility in real-world scenarios.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing heuristics. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes an important contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning.

The main weaknesses are the limited analysis of the discrete-time version and the relatively small scale of the experiments. However, these do not detract significantly from the paper's main contributions. The theoretical framework is general enough to be applicable to a wide range of constrained optimization problems, and the empirical results suggest practical utility.

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the NIPS community and would be of interest to researchers working on optimization algorithms, continuous-time dynamics, and their applications in machine learning. The paper advances the state of the art in understanding and improving accelerated first-order methods.

**Recommendation: Accept**
