## Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, framing it as a coupling of dual and primal variables through averaging. The authors provide sufficient conditions for convergence based on a Lyapunov argument and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to speed up the decrease of this Lyapunov function. They demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that this adaptive approach preserves the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods and performs competitively with existing heuristics like adaptive restarting.

The work builds on a growing body of literature connecting Nesterov’s accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs (e.g., [20, 11, 23]). Notably, it generalizes the approach in [11] by studying a broader family of Accelerated Mirror Descent dynamics parameterized by weight functions. The paper clearly articulates the connection between averaging and acceleration, formalizing observations made in [8].  The proposal of an adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution, particularly the theoretical guarantee that it preserves the original convergence rate – a claim not made for other heuristics like adaptive restarting [17, 20]. The numerical experiments, while limited, provide initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in the strongly convex case.

A key strength lies in the rigorous theoretical analysis utilizing a Lyapunov function, offering a principled approach to understanding the convergence properties of the proposed dynamics. The paper is well-structured and the derivation of primal representations for the ODEs is clearly presented. The inclusion of the replicator dynamics example helps to illustrate the applicability of the framework. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses.  While the theoretical guarantees are valuable, the conditions for their applicability – particularly ensuring η(t) ≥ r’(t) – might be restrictive in practice. The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope; expanding to a broader range of problem classes and dimensions would strengthen the empirical validation. Furthermore, the discussion of related work, while present, could be more comprehensive. For example, the connection to online learning methods like Adam [10] could be explored in greater detail. The notation is occasionally dense and could benefit from some simplification. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical framework for analyzing accelerated descent dynamics.
*   Adaptive averaging heuristic with proven convergence guarantees.
*   Clear connection to existing literature on accelerated methods and ODEs.
*   Initial empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Restrictive theoretical conditions for guaranteeing convergence.
*   Limited scope of numerical experiments.
*   Could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work. 



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While not groundbreaking, the theoretical rigor, the novel adaptive averaging heuristic, and the promising experimental results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
