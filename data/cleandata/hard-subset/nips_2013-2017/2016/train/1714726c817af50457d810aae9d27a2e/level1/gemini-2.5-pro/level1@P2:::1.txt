### Summary

This paper presents a unified framework for studying a general family of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics for constrained convex optimization. The dynamics are parameterized by two time-varying functions: `η(t)`, which controls the rate of gradient accumulation for a dual variable, and `w(t)`, which defines the weights for averaging the mirrored dual trajectory to produce the primal variable. The authors' main analytical tool is a Lyapunov function that combines the objective function value with a Bregman divergence term. By analyzing the time-derivative of this function, they derive simple, sufficient conditions on `w(t)` and `η(t)` to guarantee a convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))`, where `r(t)` is a desired rate function (e.g., `r(t)=t^2`).

This framework generalizes the authors' previous work [11] and connects to other continuous-time perspectives on acceleration [20, 23]. As a concrete example, the paper shows how this framework can be used to derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics for optimization on the probability simplex.

The paper's core contribution is a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. Motivated by the desire to make the Lyapunov function decrease as quickly as possible, this heuristic adaptively adjusts the averaging weights `w(t)` based on the progress of the optimization trajectory. A discrete-time version of this algorithm is proposed, and it is shown to preserve the `O(1/k^2)` convergence rate of the non-adaptive accelerated method. This is a notable advantage over many other heuristics, such as adaptive restarting, which often lack formal guarantees for non-strongly convex problems. Finally, numerical experiments on low-dimensional constrained problems demonstrate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as, and sometimes significantly better than, standard gradient-based and speed-based restarting heuristics.

### Review

This is a high-quality, well-written paper that makes several valuable contributions to the literature on accelerated optimization methods. It provides a clear and elegant generalization of continuous-time accelerated dynamics and introduces a novel, principled adaptive heuristic with theoretical guarantees.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The use of a Lyapunov function to analyze the `AMDw,η` family of dynamics is clean and powerful. The derivation in equation (3) is straightforward and effectively motivates the sufficient conditions presented in Theorem 2. This provides a clear recipe for designing accelerated dynamics with a desired convergence rate. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a direct and clever consequence of this analysis, aiming to make the upper bound on the Lyapunov derivative as negative as possible. Crucially, the authors provide a guarantee that their heuristic preserves the `O(1/r(t))` convergence rate, a property not always present in other adaptive schemes. The experimental results, while limited in scale, appear to be carefully executed and support the paper's claims.

One minor weakness is that the theoretical justification for the *improvement* offered by the adaptive scheme is not fully developed; the theory guarantees it doesn't hurt the worst-case rate, but the argument for why it helps is largely intuitive and empirically demonstrated. Furthermore, the analysis of the discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) is deferred to the supplementary material, which makes the main paper slightly less self-contained.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work within the context of continuous-time analysis of optimization algorithms. The flow from the general ODE family to the Lyapunov analysis and finally to the adaptive heuristic is logical and easy to follow. The notation, while dense, is used consistently. The examples provided in Section 3, particularly the derivation of standard and accelerated replicator dynamics, are insightful and serve as excellent illustrations of the framework's utility. The figures are informative, clearly showing the trajectories and the behavior of the objective, Lyapunov, and energy functions for different methods.

**Originality:** The work builds upon a strong foundation of recent research connecting ODEs and accelerated optimization [11, 20, 23]. While the general framework is an elegant generalization rather than a completely new paradigm, the main contribution—the adaptive averaging heuristic—is novel. To my knowledge, adapting the averaging weights in this manner, guided by a Lyapunov function, has not been proposed before. It provides a fresh alternative to the more common adaptive restarting or adaptive learning rate schemes. The application of this framework to accelerate the replicator dynamics is also a novel and interesting contribution.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are significant for both researchers and practitioners. For researchers, it provides a unified and flexible framework for analyzing and designing accelerated methods, potentially inspiring new dynamics and algorithms. For practitioners, it offers a new heuristic that is simple to implement, comes with a formal convergence guarantee, and demonstrates strong empirical performance against established alternatives on the tested problems. The guarantee of preserving the worst-case convergence rate is particularly important, as it provides a level of safety that is often missing from other heuristics. If these promising results hold on larger-scale machine learning problems, adaptive averaging could become a valuable tool in the optimization toolkit.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Heuristic with Guarantees:** The paper introduces adaptive averaging, a novel and well-motivated heuristic for accelerated methods that is guaranteed to preserve the `O(1/k^2)` worst-case convergence rate.
2.  **Unified Theoretical Framework:** It presents a clean, general framework for continuous-time accelerated mirror descent that unifies and extends previous work.
3.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is technically sound, exceptionally well-written, and easy to follow.
4.  **Promising Empirical Results:** Experiments show that the proposed method performs competitively with or better than popular restarting heuristics.
5.  **Interesting Concrete Application:** The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics is a novel and insightful example of the framework's power.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Improvement:** The theory explains why the heuristic is safe (preserves the rate) but doesn't formally characterize the conditions under which it provides significant speedups over non-adaptive or restarting schemes.
2.  **Small-Scale Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on low-dimensional problems, leaving its scalability and performance on large, high-dimensional machine learning tasks as an open question.

In summary, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a solid, elegant, and insightful contribution that advances our understanding of accelerated optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
