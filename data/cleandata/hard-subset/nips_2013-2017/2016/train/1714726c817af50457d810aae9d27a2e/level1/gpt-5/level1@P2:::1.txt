Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated first-order dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a generalized “accelerated mirror descent” (AMD) system. The dynamics couple a dual accumulation Ż(t) = −η(t)∇f(X(t)) with a primal variable X(t) that is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory via weights w(t). The main technical device is a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ∗(Z,z*), with sufficient conditions on η and w ensuring non-increase of Lr and hence objective decay at rate O(1/r(t)). This unifies and extends previous accelerated ODE analyses (e.g., Su-Boyd-Candès 2016; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan 2016; Attouch–Peypouquet–Redont line of inertial systems) and earlier accelerated mirror descent (Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett, NIPS 2015). The paper also gives primal forms, including an accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex, and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts weights to decrease d/dt Lr whenever possible. A discrete-time instantiation is provided (supplement) and empirically compared against adaptive restarting heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès 2015; Su et al. 2016).

Quality (technical soundness and support)
- The Lyapunov argument is clean and general: inequality (3) reduces the analysis to two verifiable conditions (η ≥ r′ and a sign condition involving r − ηW/w). Corollary 1 gives a simple sufficient choice η = (w/W)r and w/W ≥ r′/r. The reparameterization a(t) = w(t)/W(t) is convenient and leads to explicit constructions.
- The primal representations are correct and insightful; the examples for the positive orthant and simplex recover known MD and replicator dynamics and articulate how acceleration enters via averaging and time-varying gain.
- The auxiliary “energy” Er, proven decreasing under the same conditions, offers an interpretable diagnostic that aligns with empirical plots.

However, there are nontrivial clarity/consistency issues in the formulas that should be corrected:
- In Example 1 and Example 2, for r(t) = t^2 (and, more generally, r(t) = t^p), the text states a(t) = β t; given the requirement a(t) ≥ r′(t)/r(t) = p/t, this should read a(t) = β/t (β ≥ p). The subsequent expressions for w(t) and η(t) are consistent with a(t) = β/t, not β t.
- In the adaptive heuristic (Eq. (8)), to make the second term in (3) vanish when ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ > 0, one needs η = a r, i.e., a(t) = η(t)/r(t), not a(t) = η(t) r(t). The dimension/units and the discussion following (8) (e.g., the remark “η(t)r(t) = β t” under r(t) = t^2, η = β t) are inconsistent. These look like typographical inversions but matter for correctness and readability.
- The discrete-time guarantee that adaptive averaging “preserves the quadratic convergence rate” is delegated to the supplement. The main text gives helpful intuition but would benefit from a short theorem statement summarizing the discrete guarantee and its assumptions (smoothness, strong convexity of R, step-size regime).

Empirical evaluation is careful in illustrating dynamics (objective, Lyapunov, energy, trajectories) and benchmarking against gradient and speed restarts. That said, experiments are small-scale (R^3 visualization; some higher-dimensional results in the supplement) and limited to deterministic settings; stronger evidence on higher dimensions and wall-clock sensitivity would bolster claims about practical gains over restarting.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear derivation from the dual/primal formulations to conditions for acceleration. The replicator example is illustrative. However, the noted sign/scale typos (a(t) vs 1/t, a = η r vs η/r) are confusing and should be corrected throughout. A schematic summarizing how to pick r, then a = w/W, then w and η would help practitioners. Clarify initialization at t0 > 0 and how this maps to discrete initialization.

Originality
- Generalized averaging weights with a unified Lyapunov analysis that recovers and extends known accelerated dynamics are original and useful.
- The accelerated replicator dynamics and its derivation via averaging are novel in this form and likely of interest to the game-theory/optimization interface.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is a simple yet principled alternative to restarts, with the distinctive property of preserving the Lyapunov decay rate by design.

Significance
The work advances our understanding of accelerated dynamics by isolating the role of averaging weights and providing constructive conditions for target rates. The adaptive averaging heuristic is promising and compares favorably to popular restart rules. While the discrete-time results do not exceed O(1/k^2), they provide a new control knob that can be combined with existing methods. Impact would increase with broader empirical validation and sharper discrete-time theory (e.g., for strongly convex f, stochastic gradients, or variance reduction).

Pros
- Clear, general Lyapunov framework yielding verifiable sufficient conditions for desired rates.
- Conceptual insight: acceleration via adaptive averaging; clean primal/dual interpretations.
- Novel accelerated replicator dynamics.
- Simple heuristic with guarantees of rate preservation; empirical gains over restarts in some cases.
- Connections to and unification of several strands of recent work.

Cons
- Important typographical/sign inconsistencies around a(t), η, and r that hinder understanding.
- Discrete-time theory is largely in the supplement; main text lacks a concise theorem/assumptions.
- Experiments are modest in scale and scope; no stochastic or large-scale tests.
- No theoretical improvement over O(1/k^2) in the non-strongly convex case; adaptive averaging’s advantages are mostly empirical.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the a(t) and heuristic expressions; explicitly state: choose a(t) ≥ r′(t)/r(t), w(t) via (4), and η(t) = a(t) r(t).
- Provide a short theorem in the main text summarizing the discrete-time guarantee (assumptions, rate).
- Expand experiments with higher-dimensional problems, different mirror maps, and comparisons on runtime and robustness; consider stochastic settings.
- Discuss relation and differences to the Bregman Lagrangian (Wibisono et al.) more explicitly.

Overall recommendation
A solid and insightful theoretical contribution with a promising heuristic and nice connections to replicator dynamics. With the formula corrections and a crisper discrete-time summary, I would lean accept; as is, it is borderline but still positive due to clarity of the main idea and potential impact.
