1. Studies a family of continuous-time accelerated first-order constrained convex optimization dynamics, generalizing AMD via functions w(t) and η(t).  
2. Presents a unified Lyapunov-based analysis that gives sufficient conditions on w(t), η(t) to guarantee O(1/r(t)) convergence.  
3. Uses the framework to construct an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics on the probability simplex.  
4. Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that greedily selects averaging weights to decrease the Lyapunov function while preserving O(1/t^2) rate.  
5. Provides a discrete-time algorithm implementing the heuristic and compares it experimentally to adaptive restarting schemes.  
6. Strength — technically sound Lyapunov analysis with clear derivations and a provable preservation of accelerated rates for the heuristic.  
7. Strength — original contributions: generalizing AMD to a w,η family, introducing adaptive averaging distinct from adaptive learning rates/restarting, and accelerating replicator dynamics.  
8. Strength — well-written and logically structured paper with helpful figures and clear progression from theory to experiments.  
9. Strength — potential significance: flexible framework for designing accelerated methods and a practical heuristic with theoretical guarantees; initial experiments are supportive.  
10. Weakness — experimental validation is limited to low-dimensional (R^3) simplex problems, lacking large-scale and real-world tests.  
11. Weakness — discrete-time convergence proof is deferred to the supplement and the continuous-to-discrete heuristic correspondence needs clearer justification in the main text.  
12. Weakness — partly incremental over the authors' prior NIPS paper [11], as the core acceleration–averaging connection was previously established.  
13. Arguments for acceptance — novel, practical adaptive averaging with theoretical guarantees, unified Lyapunov framework, and competitive initial empirical results.  
14. Arguments against acceptance — insufficient large-scale empirical evidence and perceived incremental novelty.  
15. Overall conclusion — strengths and theoretical/practical contributions outweigh weaknesses, but broader experiments and clearer discrete-time justification are needed.
