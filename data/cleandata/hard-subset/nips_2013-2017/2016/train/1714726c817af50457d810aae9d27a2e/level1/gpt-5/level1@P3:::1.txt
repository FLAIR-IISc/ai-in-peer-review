Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies a broad family of accelerated mirror-descent (AMD) dynamics in continuous time, parameterized by two weight functions: η(t), the accumulation rate of gradients in a dual variable Z, and w(t), the weights used to average the mirrored dual trajectory to form the primal variable X. Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors derive sufficient conditions linking η, w, and a desired rate function r to ensure monotone decrease of Lr and thus a convergence rate O(1/r(t)). This unifies and extends continuous-time analyses that connect Nesterov acceleration to ODEs, e.g., Su-Boyd-Candès (2016), Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett (NIPS 2015), and the Bregman Lagrangian perspective of Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan (2016). The paper also gives a primal representation, recovers the replicator dynamics on the simplex as mirror descent, and proposes an accelerated replicator dynamics via generalized averaging.

The main algorithmic novelty is an adaptive averaging heuristic: adapt a(t) := w/W online to make the term ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w) nonpositive, thereby decreasing Lr (and a related “energy” Er) while preserving the nominal rate r(t) in continuous time. A discrete-time scheme for the quadratic rate r(t)=t^2 is provided (Algorithm 1), claimed to preserve O(1/k^2) via a discrete Lyapunov analysis in the supplement, and empirically compared to adaptive restart heuristics (O’Donoghue-Candès, Su-Boyd-Candès). Experiments on simplex-constrained problems indicate that adaptive averaging is competitive and often better than restarts.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: The weight-based formulation and Lyapunov analysis cleanly encompass standard accelerated mirror descent and related ODEs, with simple sufficient conditions that are easy to interpret (η ≥ r′, and a coupling between r and w via a(t)=w/W).
- Clear link to prior ODE-based acceleration: The mapping to Wibisono et al.’s parameters (a(t)=e^α, r(t)=e^β) and recovery of known schedules provides good context.
- New energy function Er with monotonicity guarantees under the same conditions, offering additional insight.
- Adaptive averaging is principled: the rule is derived to directly minimize the Lyapunov derivative, hence preserves the intended rate r(t) by construction in continuous time.
- Replicator dynamics acceleration on the simplex is a neat, interpretable example of accelerated mirror descent in a familiar domain (evolutionary game theory).
- Empirical comparison to adaptive restarting is relevant and shows clear, sometimes substantial benefits.

Weaknesses and questions
- The key sufficient condition (2) of Theorem 2 depends on the unknown trajectory through ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩. The continuous-time heuristic (choose a(t) to force the product ≤ 0) is elegant, but the discrete implementation departs from this test and instead uses monotonicity of f at x̃(k), which is only a proxy. The theoretical link between the discrete rule (Lines 6–8) and the sign of ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩ is not justified in the main text; the discrete proof is relegated to the supplement. It would help to state a concrete theorem (with assumptions) in the main paper for the discrete rate and to clarify how the proxy ensures the discrete analog of condition (2).
- Scope of guarantees: The work preserves the O(1/r(t)) rate but does not show improvements over the baseline rate (e.g., O(1/k^2)) under additional structure (strong convexity). Since the experiments show large gains for strongly convex problems, a theoretical result (e.g., linear rate with an appropriate adaptive rule) would strengthen the contribution, especially in light of results by Attouch–Peypouquet–Redont on inertial systems with damping and vanishing viscosity, and known linear-rate restarts for strongly convex problems.
- Practicality of constructing w from a discontinuous a(t): Eq. (4) implies that changes in a(t) induce exponential changes in w(t). The paper remarks on this qualitatively, but a discussion on the stability/robustness of the induced weights (and their discrete counterparts) would be useful, especially under noisy gradients.
- Experimental scope is limited: Most experiments are in R^3 on the simplex with entropy geometry. Larger-scale tests (e.g., high-dimensional constrained logistic regression or portfolio problems), other mirror maps (e.g., Euclidean, Mahalanobis), and comparisons beyond restarts (e.g., Catalyst, adaptive momentum) would better establish practical significance.
- Assumptions and smoothness: The analysis assumes ∇ψ* is Lipschitz and sometimes ψ* twice differentiable. For entropy on the simplex, ∇ψ* (softmax) is smooth and globally Lipschitz, but clarifying norms and global constants would improve rigor. Similarly, clarify conditions under which Theorem 1 applies with indicator terms in ψ handled via conjugacy.

Quality
Technically sound at the continuous-time level; the Lyapunov calculus and sufficient conditions are correct and connect well to prior ODE analyses. The energy function Er is a nice addition. The discrete-time guarantee is plausible but underspecified in the main text; moving key statements from the supplement would improve trust. Empirical evidence is consistent but small-scale.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The dual/primal formulations and examples are helpful. Some notational overload (X̌, Ž) and the jump from the continuous-time heuristic to Algorithm 1 could be clearer. It would help to provide a brief, self-contained statement of the discrete convergence theorem and explicit assumptions on R and ψ in the main body.

Originality
Moderate to good. The generalized averaging viewpoint and conditions unify and extend existing ODE analyses, and the adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel, principled alternative to restarts, with guarantees to preserve rates in continuous time (and claimed discrete-time preservation). The accelerated replicator example is an interesting contribution.

Significance
Potentially useful. If the adaptive averaging heuristic consistently matches or improves on restarting without harming worst-case rates, it could influence practice in accelerated first-order methods, especially in constrained/mirror settings. Demonstrating benefits at scale and providing stronger theory in the strongly convex case would increase impact.

Suggestions
- State a main discrete-time theorem with the exact conditions under which Algorithm 1 achieves O(1/k^2), and clarify how the f-monotonicity proxy enforces a discrete analog of ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩(…) ≤ 0.
- Analyze the strongly convex case; can adaptive averaging yield provable linear rates or improved constants versus restarts?
- Expand experiments to higher dimensions and other geometries; compare to Catalyst and other adaptive acceleration variants.
- Discuss robustness under stochastic gradients and potential integration with variance reduction.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and unifying continuous-time analysis with clear sufficient conditions.
- Principled, easy-to-implement adaptive heuristic with guarantees to preserve rates.
- Interesting connection and application to replicator dynamics.
- Empirical improvements over widely used restart heuristics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Discrete-time guarantees are not prominently or fully justified in the main text.
- Limited experimental scope; unclear performance in high dimensions and other settings.
- No theoretical speedup beyond preserving baseline rates, especially under strong convexity.

Overall, this is a thoughtful contribution to the theory and practice of accelerated mirror descent with a promising adaptive mechanism. With stronger discrete-time exposition and broader experiments, it would be a clear accept; as is, I lean toward weak accept.
