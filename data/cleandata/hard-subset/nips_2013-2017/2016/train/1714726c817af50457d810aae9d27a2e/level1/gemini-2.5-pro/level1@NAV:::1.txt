This paper presents a generalized continuous-time framework for accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics. Building on prior work that connects Nesterov's accelerated methods to second-order ODEs (e.g., Su et al., NIPS 2014; Krichene et al., NIPS 2015), the authors introduce a broader family of dynamics parameterized by a gradient accumulation rate η(t) and an averaging weight function w(t). The core theoretical contribution is a Lyapunov-based analysis that provides simple, sufficient conditions on η and w to guarantee a desired convergence rate. This framework is then used to motivate a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic, which dynamically adjusts the averaging weights to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function. This method is presented as an alternative to popular adaptive restarting schemes.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Pros:**
1.  **Quality and Originality:** The unified theoretical framework for AMD dynamics is elegant and technically sound. Using a Lyapunov function to derive general conditions on the weighting schemes is a clean and powerful approach. The resulting adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and directly motivated by the theory.
2.  **Significance:** The key advantage of the proposed heuristic is that, by construction, it preserves the O(1/t^2) convergence rate guarantee of the underlying accelerated method. This provides a formal assurance of stability that is often missing from other heuristics like adaptive restarting, which can be effective in practice but lack similar guarantees for non-strongly convex problems.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and structured. It clearly progresses from the general continuous-time theory to a concrete discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) and provides insightful examples, such as the derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics.

Despite these strengths, there are some minor weaknesses.
**Cons:**
1.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** The numerical experiments, while illustrative and well-visualized, are confined to low-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems. The paper's impact would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating the heuristic's performance on larger-scale machine learning tasks where accelerated methods are widely used.
2.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper briefly mentions other adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam but could benefit from a more detailed discussion comparing the mechanisms. While Adagrad adapts the learning rate and this work adapts the averaging weights, both respond to the optimization geometry, and a deeper comparison would be valuable.

Overall, the paper makes a strong contribution by introducing a principled and theoretically-grounded heuristic for improving the practical performance of accelerated methods. The guarantee of preserving the convergence rate is a significant advantage over existing approaches. The weaknesses are minor and relate primarily to the scope of the evaluation. I recommend acceptance.
