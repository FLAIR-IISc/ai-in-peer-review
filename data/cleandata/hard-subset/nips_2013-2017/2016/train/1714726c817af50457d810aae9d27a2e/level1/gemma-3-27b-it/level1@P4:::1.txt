This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, framing the process as coupled dynamics of a dual and primal variable, driven by averaging. The authors provide sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate using a Lyapunov argument, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to speed up convergence. They demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that this heuristic preserves the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods and often outperforms adaptive restarting schemes.

The paper builds upon prior work connecting Nesterov’s accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs (Su et al., 2016; Krichene et al., 2016), generalizing the approach to a broader family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics. The connection to replicator dynamics (Weibull, 1995) is a nice example, and the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a natural extension of existing ideas like adaptive restarting (O’Donoghue & Candès, 2015; Su et al., 2016). The theoretical guarantees on the adaptive averaging, preserving the quadratic convergence rate, are a significant contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The Lyapunov analysis is well-executed and the conditions for convergence are clearly stated. The connection between the continuous-time dynamics and discrete-time algorithms is well-motivated. The derivation of the primal representation is helpful for understanding the dynamics.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a good overview of the motivation and contributions.  Equations are clearly presented, and the flow of the argument is easy to follow.
* **Originality:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution, building upon existing acceleration techniques. The analysis providing guarantees on the convergence rate is also original. The generalization of the AMD framework is a solid step forward.
* **Significance:** The results are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on optimization algorithms.  Preserving the convergence rate while potentially accelerating convergence is a valuable property. The comparison to existing heuristics provides a useful benchmark.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments demonstrate the efficacy of the adaptive averaging, they are limited to relatively simple examples (quadratic, linear, KL divergence).  More extensive testing on a wider range of problems, including large-scale machine learning datasets, would strengthen the claims.
* **Complexity of Notation:** The numerous indices and symbols (e.g., ψ, ψ*, r, η, w) can make the mathematical expressions challenging to parse. While necessary for rigor, a more streamlined notation could improve readability.
* **Connection to Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, a more detailed discussion of how the adaptive averaging heuristic relates to other adaptive methods (e.g., AdaGrad, Adam – Duchi et al., 2011; Kingma & Ba, 2014) beyond the brief mention in the introduction would be beneficial. Specifically, highlighting the differences in design goals and applicability.
* **Discretization Details:** The description of the discretization in Algorithm 1 is somewhat brief. More details on the choice of step size *s* and the justification for the specific update rule for *a<sub>k</sub>* would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated descent.
* The theoretical guarantees on convergence rate are a significant contribution.
* The experiments, although limited, support the claim that the heuristic performs well in practice.
* The paper builds upon and extends a growing body of work connecting optimization algorithms to continuous-time dynamics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The notation can be somewhat complex.
* The connection to other adaptive optimization methods is not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The technical analysis is rigorous, the results are promising, and the paper is generally well-written. While the experimental evaluation could be more extensive and the notation somewhat simplified, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend acceptance.
