Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) for constrained smooth convex optimization. It introduces a two-parameter family of dynamics AMDw,η, where a dual variable Z accumulates gradients at rate η(t), and the primal iterate X is the weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), the authors derive sufficient conditions on (η,w) to certify a target rate 1/r(t). A corollary gives a constructive recipe via a(t) = w/W to realize polynomial rates r(t) = t^p. They also define an “energy” function Er(t) = f(X(t)) + (1/r(t))Dψ*(Z(t), ∇ψ(X(t))) that decreases under the same conditions.

The paper gives a primal representation of the dynamics, highlights the operator ∇^2ψ*∘∇ψ, and instantiates the ODE for the positive orthant and for the simplex. In the simplex, this yields an accelerated variant of the replicator dynamics: the standard replicator on Ž with gradient evaluated at the X-averages.

Building on the Lyapunov analysis, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) to make the Lyapunov derivative as negative as possible subject to preserving the guarantee. They provide a discrete-time method (Algorithm 1) intended to preserve the quadratic 1/k^2 rate for r(t)=t^2, and report experiments comparing adaptive averaging to gradient and speed restarting heuristics. Results on small-scale constrained problems suggest adaptive averaging is at least competitive and sometimes substantially better.

The work connects closely to: continuous-time interpretations of Nesterov’s methods (Su, Boyd, Candès 2014; Wibisono, Wilson, Jordan 2016), accelerated mirror descent (Krichene, Bayen, Bartlett 2015), and heuristic restarts (O’Donoghue, Candès 2015; Su et al. 2014). It generalizes prior AMD analyses by allowing general w,η, produces an accelerated replicator example, and proposes a new adaptive mechanism guided by a Lyapunov argument.

Strengths
- Clear Lyapunov framework with verifiable sufficient conditions for achieving a prescribed rate; recovers known accelerated schedules as special cases.
- Constructive recipe for weight design and new perspective tying acceleration to averaging, with explicit examples (orthant, simplex) and a clean primal form.
- Introduction of an energy function that decreases under the same conditions, offering additional insight.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic is principled (directly tied to decreasing the Lyapunov) and comes with continuous-time guarantees; the discrete variant claims to preserve the quadratic rate.
- Empirical comparisons to restart heuristics indicate consistent or better performance, with notable gains in some strongly convex cases.
- Fits well within ongoing work on ODE perspectives for acceleration and contributes a distinct control knob (weights) beyond restart.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- A likely typo in Examples 1–2: since r′(t)/r(t)=p/t, a(t) should be chosen as β/t (β≥p), not β t; the subsequent derivations for w(t) match a(t)=β/t. This should be corrected.
- The adaptive rule in continuous time depends on the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩, but the discrete algorithm triggers changes in a_k based on f(x̃(k+1))−f(x̃(k))>0. The theoretical bridge from the inner-product condition to a function-value test is not explicit in the main text; guarantees for Algorithm 1 are deferred to the supplement. Including a concise statement of the discrete Lyapunov inequality and precise assumptions (smoothness, strong convexity of R, properties of ψ) in the main paper would strengthen the work.
- Algorithm 1 contains an apparent typographical error in the definition of λ_k; the current expression is ambiguous. Precise update formulas and parameter schedules are needed for reproducibility.
- Experimental scope is limited (mostly R^3 for visualization). While additional higher-dimensional experiments are said to be in the supplement, the main claims would be more convincing with moderate- to large-scale results, sensitivity to β and step size, and performance on standard benchmarks.
- The paper claims preservation of the 1/k^2 rate under adaptive averaging; however, there is no demonstrated theoretical acceleration beyond 1/k^2 for general convex functions, and the strongly convex improvements are empirical only. Clarifying whether stronger rates (e.g., linear) can be obtained under strong convexity with adaptive averaging would increase significance.
- Assumptions such as Lipschitzness of ∇ψ* and twice-differentiability of ψ* are used at various points; a consolidated assumptions section would improve readability. Discussion of how restrictive these are for common constraints beyond simplex/orthant would help.

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis appears technically sound, with standard ODE existence/uniqueness arguments and careful Lyapunov-based derivations. The conditions are sufficient and recover known accelerated schedules. The energy function proof uses monotonicity of ∇ψ* appropriately.
- Discrete-rate guarantees are deferred; bringing key lemmas/results into the main text would improve completeness.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The primal formulations and examples are instructive.
- Fix typographical errors (a(t) choice; λ_k formula) and make the adaptive rule/guarantees in discrete time more explicit.
- Minor notation issues: make consistent use of t0 and initialization; ensure all variables (e.g., γ in Algorithm 1) are defined.

Originality
- The generalized averaging view with explicit conditions, the accelerated replicator instantiation, and the Lyapunov-guided adaptive averaging heuristic are novel contributions beyond prior ODE-based acceleration work.

Significance
- Provides a new design dimension (adaptive averaging) for accelerated methods with theoretical guarantees in continuous time and claimed preservation of discrete rates. The accelerated replicator dynamics could be of independent interest in constrained optimization and game dynamics. Broader impact would be strengthened by more extensive empirical validation and clearer discrete-time theory.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid continuous-time theory with constructive conditions; unifies and extends prior AMD analyses.
- Principled adaptive heuristic tied to Lyapunov decrease; promising empirical results versus popular restarts.
- Clear examples and potential applicability to constrained settings (simplex/orthant).

Cons for acceptance
- Discrete-time guarantees and algorithmic details are not fully presented in the main text; some update formulas appear incorrect/ambiguous.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scale and scope.
- No theoretical improvement beyond quadratic rate for general convex; strong convexity benefits are empirical.

Suggestions
- Correct the a(t) and λ_k typos; include the discrete Lyapunov inequality and a theorem with conditions under which Algorithm 1 achieves O(1/k^2).
- Expand experiments to higher dimensions and real datasets; include sensitivity analyses and ablations on the adaptive rule.
- Clarify assumptions and discuss applicability beyond the showcased constraints; consider comparisons to other adaptive schemes where appropriate.

Questions for the authors
- Can the adaptive averaging heuristic be shown to achieve faster rates under strong convexity (e.g., linear) without restarts?
- What precise condition on the discrete iterates ensures the discrete analogue of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w) ≤ 0?
- How sensitive is performance to β and the step size schedule? Are there parameter-free variants?
