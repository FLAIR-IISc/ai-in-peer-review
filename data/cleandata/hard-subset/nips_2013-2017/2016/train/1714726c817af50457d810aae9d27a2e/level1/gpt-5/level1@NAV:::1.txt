This paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent with generalized averaging and introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves accelerated rates. The authors formulate a broad ODE family parameterized by a gradient accumulation rate η(t) and averaging weights w(t), and analyze it via a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*). They derive sufficient conditions on (η, w, r) that guarantee a target rate 1/r(t), recover known schedules as special cases, and provide a primal representation connecting to mirror descent and replicator dynamics on the simplex. Building on the ODE viewpoint of Nesterov acceleration (Su, Boyd, Candès; Wibisono et al.) and prior work on accelerated mirror descent (Krichene et al.), they propose adaptive averaging that decreases the time derivative of Lr, preserving rates while adapting weights. A discrete-time scheme is presented with guarantees for the quadratic rate, and experiments compare favorably to adaptive restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès), with especially strong gains on some strongly convex instances.

Quality: The theoretical contributions are sound and carefully derived: existence/uniqueness, Lyapunov decrease under verifiable conditions, and an energy function that decreases under the same regime. The discrete-time analysis for the adaptive rule (for r(t)=t^2) appears correct, though several proofs are deferred to the supplement. Empirical evaluation supports the claims but is limited in scale and diversity.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and generally well-organized. The dual/primal formulations are helpful; suggestions: (i) move key parts of the discrete proof from the supplement; (ii) clarify the role of initialization at t0 and sensitivity to its choice; (iii) provide guidance for choosing β and step sizes; (iv) expand the algorithmic pseudocode and computational cost discussion.

Originality: The generalized averaging view with trajectory-dependent conditions and the adaptive averaging rule that directly targets Lyapunov decrease are novel. The acceleration of replicator dynamics on the simplex is a neat and instructive example. The work is well-situated with respect to NIPS and related literature (Su et al.; Wibisono et al.; O’Donoghue & Candès; Adagrad/Adam).

Significance: The framework could influence the design of accelerated methods beyond fixed schedules and restarts, and the replicator example connects optimization and game dynamics. Practical impact would be strengthened by larger-scale tests and stochastic settings.

Pros:
- Unified Lyapunov analysis yielding design rules for w and η.
- Adaptive averaging with formal guarantees that preserves accelerated rates.
- Clear connection to replicator dynamics and mirror maps.
- Empirical gains over restart in several cases.

Cons:
- Trajectory-dependent condition in Theorem 2 limits a priori design; practical tuning remains.
- Experiments are small-scale and deterministic; no stochastic/noisy gradients.
- No stronger-than-quadratic guarantees for strongly convex objectives.
- Some proofs and discretization details relegated to the supplement.
