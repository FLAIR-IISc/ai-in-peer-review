This paper presents a compelling study of accelerated descent dynamics, focusing on the interplay between averaging and convergence rates. The core idea – framing accelerated methods as coupled dual/primal dynamics governed by weighting functions – is well-motivated and provides a useful lens for analysis and algorithm design. The theoretical results, particularly the Lyapunov-based convergence guarantees for a broad class of averaging schemes (AMDw,η), are strong and represent a significant contribution. The adaptive averaging heuristic proposed is intuitive, well-justified by the theory, and empirically shown to be competitive with existing methods like adaptive restarting.

The paper effectively builds upon prior work in continuous-time optimization, specifically referencing and generalizing results from Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11].  The connection to replicator dynamics and evolutionary game theory is a nice touch, demonstrating the broader applicability of the framework. The authors clearly articulate the problem and the motivation for their approach, and the writing is generally clear and well-organized.  The inclusion of a primal representation (AMDpw,η) and examples for specific constraint sets (positive orthant, simplex) adds to the paper’s depth.

However, the paper isn't without its weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is thorough, it relies on fairly strong assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz gradients, differentiable ψ). Exploring the robustness of the results to relaxations of these assumptions would strengthen the work.  The discussion of the energy function (Er) feels somewhat disconnected from the main contribution. While interesting, its relevance isn’t fully elucidated, and the link to restarting heuristics could be expanded. The numerical experiments, while encouraging, are limited in scope.  Comparing adaptive averaging to more sophisticated adaptive methods (e.g., Adam [10], though the authors acknowledge its different design goals) could provide a more nuanced evaluation.  The discussion of existing restarting heuristics sometimes feels cursory; a more detailed comparison of their strengths and weaknesses, beyond empirical performance, would be valuable. The connection to Wibisono et al. [23] is mentioned briefly, but the implications of their higher-order discretization for the proposed adaptive averaging method are not explored.  Finally, the statement about Nesterov’s accelerated method having a convergence rate *faster* than 1/k^2 in the reference to Attouch et al. is inaccurate and should be corrected.

Specifically, concerning clarity, equation (3) could benefit from more explicit justification of each step; the leap from the derivatives of Lr to the final inequality might be unclear to some readers.  Similarly, the intuition behind the condition a(t) = η(t)r(t) is not fully explained. Providing a more intuitive explanation would strengthen the motivation.

In terms of originality, the paper doesn't present a completely novel algorithm, but rather a principled approach to *designing* averaging schemes within a well-established framework. The theoretical contributions, however – the Lyapunov analysis for AMDw,η and the convergence guarantees for the adaptive heuristic – *are* original and significant. The application of this framework to the replicator dynamics is also a novel contribution. 

Regarding significance, the work is important because it provides a more solid theoretical foundation for adaptive averaging schemes in accelerated optimization. The adaptive averaging heuristic itself is a practical contribution that could be readily adopted by practitioners. The insights gained from studying the continuous-time dynamics could inform the design of new algorithms and provide a deeper understanding of the behavior of existing ones. The potential to extend this work to stronger convexity assumptions and explore connections to higher-order methods further enhances its significance.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and Lyapunov-based convergence guarantees.
*   Intuitive and well-motivated adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   Competitive empirical performance compared to existing methods.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
*   Novel application to replicator dynamics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relatively limited scope of numerical experiments.
*   Strong assumptions in the theoretical analysis.
*   Somewhat disconnected discussion of the energy function.
*   Potential for more detailed comparison with related work.
*   Inaccurate statement regarding Nesterov’s convergence rate.



**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The theoretical results are strong, the adaptive averaging heuristic is promising, and the paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  Addressing the minor points regarding clarity and the convergence rate claim would further strengthen the paper.
