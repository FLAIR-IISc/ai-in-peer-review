- Paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with two weight functions w and η, deriving sufficient conditions for convergence via Lyapunov analysis.
- Generalizes prior AMD and Su et al. continuous-time frameworks to a broader averaging scheme and connects to replicator dynamics on the simplex.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights to speed Lyapunov decrease and proves it preserves the quadratic convergence rate.
- Theorems 2 and 3 give rigorous Lyapunov-based sufficient conditions guaranteeing the desired convergence rates.
- Paper is well-organized, notation is consistent, and the link between continuous dynamics and a discrete Algorithm 1 is clearly presented.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and distinct from restarting heuristics, offering a new perspective on accelerating first-order methods.
- Empirical results show clear improvements over existing methods in some cases, especially for strongly convex problems.
- The work has potential broader impact, including applications in evolutionary game theory via replicator dynamics.
- Does not provide guarantees on the rate of Lyapunov decrease under the adaptive heuristic beyond preserving the overall convergence rate.
- Numerical experiments are narrowly scoped (main paper limited to simplex-constrained problems in R^3), limiting empirical evidence across diverse problems and dimensions.
- Practical implementation and computational complexity for large-scale discrete implementations (Algorithm 1) are not thoroughly analyzed.
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically sound generalization of AMD, novel adaptive heuristic with provable guarantees, empirical improvements, and meaningful connection to replicator dynamics.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experimental scope, no proof that adaptation yields faster rates than fixed schedules, and insufficient discrete implementation complexity analysis.
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution, well-written and novel; numerical evaluation could be expanded but the paper is a valuable addition to optimization literature.
