- Studies accelerated constrained convex optimization as coupled dual-primal dynamics via averaging.
- Provides Lyapunov-based sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) for achieving target convergence rates.
- Generalizes accelerated mirror descent by introducing weight functions w and η for flexible averaging.
- Shows averaging can accelerate replicator dynamics, linking to evolutionary game theory.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes weights and preserves quadratic convergence with theoretical guarantees.
- Empirically validates the adaptive heuristic, showing competitive or superior performance to some existing heuristics.
- Builds on continuous-time ODE interpretations of Nesterov’s methods and extends prior frameworks (e.g., [11]).
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated using a clear Lyapunov argument.
- Framework is broad and general, enabling analysis of various accelerated descent algorithms.
- Strong connections to existing literature and clear, consistent presentation.
- Core framework’s novelty is limited since Lyapunov-based analyses exist in prior work.
- Theorem 2’s sufficient conditions may be hard to verify in practice and require gradient/Lyapunov estimates.
- Numerical experiments are limited in scope and lack comparisons to other adaptive optimizers (e.g., Adagrad, Adam).
- Discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) lacks implementation details (step size s, regularizer R).
- Reference to Attouch et al. (2018) is belated and not fully integrated into the convergence-rate discussion.
- Arguments for acceptance: rigorous extension, valuable adaptive heuristic with guarantees, supportive experiments, clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: not radically novel, practical applicability of conditions unclear, experiments are narrow.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept with minor revisions.
- Suggested revisions: discuss limitations/practical verification of conditions, expand experiments (include other adaptive methods), detail Algorithm 1 implementation choices, and better integrate Attouch et al. (2018).
