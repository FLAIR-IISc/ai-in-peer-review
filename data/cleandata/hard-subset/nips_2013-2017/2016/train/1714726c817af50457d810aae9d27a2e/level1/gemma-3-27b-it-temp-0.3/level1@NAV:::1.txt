## Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, framing it as a coupled dual-primal system governed by weighting functions. The authors provide sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate using a Lyapunov argument, demonstrating acceleration of replicator dynamics as a specific example. They then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to dynamically compute these weights, proving convergence guarantees in continuous and discrete time and validating its performance through numerical experiments.

The work builds upon a growing body of research connecting accelerated methods (like Nesterov’s) to continuous-time ODEs [20, 2, 11, 23]. The authors effectively formalize the connection between acceleration and averaging, extending previous observations [8] into a more rigorous framework. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution, distinguishing itself from existing adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam [7, 10] by specifically targeting accelerated dynamics. The numerical results, comparing adaptive averaging to adaptive restarting [20], are encouraging, showing comparable or improved performance.

A key strength of the paper lies in its theoretical grounding. The Lyapunov analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the convergence properties of the proposed dynamics. The authors are careful to provide sufficient conditions and demonstrate their applicability through examples. The inclusion of both continuous and discrete-time analysis is valuable, bridging the gap between theory and practical implementation. The clarity of the writing is also commendable, with a logical flow and well-defined notation. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed heuristic. While the experiments show promising results, the analysis focuses on relatively simple examples. Exploring the performance on more complex, high-dimensional datasets would strengthen the claims. Additionally, the connection to existing restarting heuristics could be further elaborated – a more detailed comparison of the underlying mechanisms driving their performance would be insightful. The discussion of related work, while present, could be expanded to include more recent advancements in adaptive optimization techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with Lyapunov analysis.
*   Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with convergence guarantees.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Positive empirical results compared to existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of numerical experiments.
*   Lack of detailed comparison with other adaptive methods beyond restarting.
*   Could benefit from a more extensive discussion of limitations.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed heuristic is well-motivated, and the experimental results are promising. While some areas could be strengthened, the paper represents a valuable addition to the NIPS program and I recommend acceptance.
