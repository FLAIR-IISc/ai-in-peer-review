- Generalizes prior work to a broad Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) ODE family parameterized by averaging weight w(t) and accumulation rate η(t).
- Uses a Lyapunov function Lr to derive simple sufficient conditions on w(t), η(t) that guarantee Lr is non-increasing and yield O(1/r(t)) convergence.
- Framework unifies averaging-based acceleration and connects to Su et al. and Wibisono et al. ODE-based analyses.
- Demonstrates the framework by deriving an accelerated replicator dynamics for optimization over the probability simplex.
- Proposes a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that greedily adjusts w(t) to maximize instantaneous Lyapunov decrease while maintaining convergence conditions.
- The adaptive heuristic provably preserves the baseline O(1/t^2) accelerated rate, distinguishing it from common restarting heuristics.
- Presents a discrete-time version (Algorithm 1); the discrete-time convergence proof is deferred to the supplement.
- Empirical results on low-dimensional problems show adaptive averaging is competitive with or outperforms standard AMD and restarting schemes.
- The paper is technically strong: continuous-time analysis is elegant, rigorous, and claims are well-supported.
- Writing and presentation are clear and accessible, with helpful figures and contextualization in related work.
- Original contributions include the AMDw,η framework, the adaptive averaging heuristic, and the accelerated replicator dynamics application.
- Theoretical significance: provides a unified lens on averaging in acceleration and a Lyapunov-based design recipe for new dynamics.
- Practical significance: offers a principled alternative to restarting heuristics that could be useful if scalable to large problems.
- Main experimental limitation: evaluations are limited to low-dimensional synthetic tasks, so scalability and overhead on real ML problems are unclear.
- Main theoretical limitation: no proof that the adaptive scheme achieves faster-than-baseline rates (only rate preservation).
- Presentation completeness issue: full discrete-time analysis is in the supplement, making main-paper algorithmic guarantees less self-contained.
- Overall verdict: strengths outweigh weaknesses; reviewer recommends acceptance.
