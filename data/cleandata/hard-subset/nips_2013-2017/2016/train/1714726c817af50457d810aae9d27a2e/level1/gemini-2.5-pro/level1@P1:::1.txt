### **Summary**

This paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization from the perspective of continuous-time dynamics. The authors generalize their previous work [11] by proposing a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by two functions: an averaging weight function `w(t)` and a gradient accumulation rate `η(t)`. The dynamics are described as a coupled system where a dual variable `Z(t)` integrates gradients, and the primal variable `X(t)` is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory.

The core of the theoretical analysis relies on a Lyapunov function, `Lr`. The authors derive simple, sufficient conditions on `w(t)` and `η(t)` that guarantee `Lr` is non-increasing, which in turn establishes a convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))`, where `r(t)` is a user-chosen rate function (e.g., `r(t)=t^2` for the standard accelerated rate). This framework is a nice generalization and provides a unified view of averaging-based acceleration, connecting to prior work by Su et al. [20] and Wibisono et al. [23]. As a compelling example, the paper shows how this framework can be used to derive an accelerated version of the well-known replicator dynamics for optimization over the probability simplex.

The main practical contribution is a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The key insight is that the sufficient conditions for convergence can sometimes be met with slack. The heuristic adaptively modifies the averaging weights `w(t)` to make the Lyapunov function decrease as steeply as possible at each moment, while still satisfying the conditions required for the convergence guarantee. This provides a principled way to speed up convergence that, unlike many popular restarting heuristics [17, 20], provably preserves the `O(1/t^2)` rate for general convex problems. The authors present a discrete-time version of this heuristic (Algorithm 1) and provide numerical experiments on low-dimensional problems, comparing it favorably against standard accelerated mirror descent and two common restarting schemes.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The continuous-time analysis is elegant and sound. The use of a Lyapunov function to derive sufficient conditions on the averaging and gradient accumulation schedules is a powerful and clear analytical tool. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical derivations in Section 2. The paper is a complete piece of work, progressing logically from a general continuous-time theory to a specific, practical heuristic and its experimental validation. The authors are honest about the limitations of existing heuristics (e.g., lack of guarantees for non-strongly convex problems), which motivates their work well. One minor weakness is that the convergence proof for the discrete-time algorithm is deferred to the supplementary material. While understandable for space reasons, this makes the main paper's claims about the algorithm's guarantees slightly less self-contained.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, and the mathematical development is logical. The formulation of the AMD dynamics in equation (1) and the subsequent Lyapunov analysis are presented with precision. The intuition behind the adaptive averaging heuristic—to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function—is simple and powerful. Figure 1 provides a helpful schematic of the dynamics, and the experimental results in Figure 2 are clearly presented and interpreted. The paper does an excellent job of placing its contributions within the context of related work, making it accessible to readers familiar with the area.

**Originality:** The work contains several original contributions.
1.  The general `AMDw,η` framework is a novel and useful generalization of the ODEs studied in [11, 20, 23]. It unifies different averaging schemes under a single analytical lens.
2.  The main contribution, the adaptive averaging heuristic, is a new idea for improving the practical performance of accelerated methods. It is conceptually different from adaptive learning rate methods (like Adagrad) and from the popular restarting heuristics. The fact that it comes with a formal guarantee of preserving the convergence rate is a significant point of novelty.
3.  The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics is a novel and interesting application of the framework, connecting the field of accelerated optimization to concepts from evolutionary game theory.

While the paper builds upon the recent and active line of research connecting ODEs to accelerated optimization, its specific contributions are distinct and advance the field.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are significant for both theoretical and practical reasons.
1.  **Theoretical Significance:** The unified framework provides deeper insight into the role of averaging in acceleration. The Lyapunov analysis offers a clear recipe for designing and analyzing new accelerated dynamics.
2.  **Practical Significance:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is a promising practical tool. It provides a principled alternative to restarting schemes that often require tuning and may lack formal guarantees. The experiments, although on small-scale problems, show that it is competitive and can offer substantial improvements. If these results hold up on larger-scale machine learning problems, this heuristic could be widely adopted. The work is likely to be of high interest to researchers working on optimization theory, algorithm design, and large-scale machine learning. It opens up clear avenues for future work, such as proving faster rates for the adaptive scheme on strongly convex problems or applying it to a wider range of algorithms.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Insight:** The paper introduces a novel and principled adaptive heuristic for accelerated methods, which is a significant departure from existing approaches like restarting. The underlying continuous-time framework is elegant and provides new insights into the mechanics of acceleration.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The heuristic is backed by a solid Lyapunov-based analysis in continuous-time, guaranteeing that the baseline `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate is preserved. This is a key advantage over many other heuristics.
*   **Promising Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as, and sometimes significantly better than, widely-used restarting heuristics.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a technically sophisticated topic accessible and its contributions clear.
*   **Broad Interest:** The topic of accelerating first-order methods is of central importance to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are conducted on low-dimensional (R^3) synthetic problems. It is unclear how the method's performance and overhead will scale to high-dimensional, real-world machine learning tasks.
*   **Lack of Proof for Faster Rates:** While the heuristic is motivated by the idea of speeding up convergence, the theory only guarantees preservation of the original rate. A theoretical result showing improved rates (e.g., linear convergence for strongly convex functions) would have made the contribution much stronger.
*   **Discrete-Time Analysis in Supplement:** The full analysis of the proposed discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is not in the main paper, which slightly weakens the completeness of the presented work.

**Conclusion:** The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically-grounded heuristic for improving accelerated optimization methods. It provides valuable insights and a promising practical algorithm that is likely to inspire follow-up work. I recommend acceptance.
