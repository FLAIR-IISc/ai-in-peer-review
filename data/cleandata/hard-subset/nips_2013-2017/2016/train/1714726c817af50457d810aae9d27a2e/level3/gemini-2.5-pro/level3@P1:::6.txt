This paper studies continuous-time accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization problems. The dynamics are formulated as a coupling between a dual variable, which accumulates gradients, and a primal variable, which is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory. The authors introduce a general family of dynamics, AMDw,η, parameterized by an averaging weight function `w(t)` and a gradient rate function `η(t)`. The central technical tool is a Lyapunov function, which is used to derive sufficient conditions on `w` and `η` to achieve a desired `1/r(t)` convergence rate. As an example, the framework is shown to provide an accelerated version of the well-known replicator dynamics.

The main contribution of the paper is a novel heuristic called "adaptive averaging." Instead of using a fixed schedule for the averaging weights, this heuristic adaptively adjusts the weights `w(t)` to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function at each step. A key theoretical result is that this adaptive scheme is guaranteed to preserve the original `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate for non-strongly convex objectives, a guarantee that is often missing for other heuristics like adaptive restarting. The authors present a discrete-time version of the algorithm and provide numerical experiments comparing its performance against standard accelerated mirror descent and two restarting heuristics on several low-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Novelty and Theoretical Guarantees:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel and interesting idea. The main strength of the paper is that this heuristic is not just an ad-hoc modification but is derived from the paper's theoretical framework and, crucially, comes with a proof that it preserves the `O(1/k^2)` worst-case convergence rate. This provides a significant advantage over restarting heuristics, for which such guarantees on general convex problems are often not available.

2.  **Unifying Framework:** The paper presents an elegant and general continuous-time framework (AMDw,η) for studying accelerated mirror descent methods. The use of a Lyapunov function to analyze a broad class of averaging and gradient accumulation schemes is a clean and powerful approach that could inspire future work in algorithm design and analysis.

3.  **Interesting Connections:** The paper makes a valuable connection by showing how the replicator dynamics, an important ODE in game theory, can be seen as a form of mirror descent and can be accelerated within the proposed framework. This illustrates the generality of the approach.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is quite dense and difficult to follow for a reader not already an expert in this specific area of continuous-time optimization dynamics and mirror descent. It assumes significant familiarity with concepts like mirror maps, Bregman divergences, and convex duality without providing sufficient background or intuition. For instance:
    *   The derivation leading to the bound in inequality (3) is technically involved. While the steps are present, the paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of what each term represents and why the specific choices are made.
    *   Similarly, the motivation and interpretation of the second energy function `Er` in Theorem 3 could be expanded upon. The connection to "kinetic energy" is mentioned briefly but could be made more concrete.
    *   A short primer on mirror descent and the dual-primal setup, perhaps in an appendix, would greatly improve the paper's accessibility to the broader NIPS audience.

2.  **Insufficient Discussion of Related Work:** The paper mentions adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam but dismisses them in a single sentence as "not designed for accelerated methods." This is insufficient. While the mechanisms are different (adapting learning rates vs. adapting averaging weights), these are the most widely used adaptive methods in the field. A more detailed discussion is needed to contrast the goals and approaches, explain why one might be preferred over the other in certain settings, and discuss potential synergies.

3.  **Limited Empirical Validation and Significance:** The practical significance of the proposed method is not well-established due to the limited experimental evaluation. All experiments are performed in R^3. While this is useful for visualization, it is a toy setting that provides no evidence of the method's scalability or effectiveness on high-dimensional problems commonly encountered in machine learning. The claim that adaptive averaging "performs at least as well as adaptive restarting, with significant improvements in some cases" is a strong one, but it is not convincingly supported without tests on larger, more realistic benchmark problems. This is a major weakness for a paper submitted to NIPS.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   To improve clarity, I strongly recommend the authors add more explanatory text around the main theoretical derivations. For example, when taking the derivative of `Lr(X(t), Z(t), t)`, explain what each of the three resulting terms represents before combining them.
*   The experimental section must be strengthened. To demonstrate the significance of this work, please include experiments on higher-dimensional, standard machine learning problems (e.g., logistic regression, LASSO, or SVMs with relevant constraints) and compare against the same baselines. This would provide crucial evidence about the scalability and practical utility of adaptive averaging.
*   The reference to the "solid line" in the caption of Figure 1 is ambiguous, as multiple lines are solid. Please clarify by using color, different line styles (e.g., dashed/dotted), or more specific labels.
*   Please expand the related work section to include a more nuanced comparison with adaptive learning rate methods like Adagrad, RMSProp, and Adam, as discussed above.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces a theoretically sound and novel adaptive heuristic for accelerated optimization. The guarantee that it preserves the convergence rate is a strong and important contribution. However, the paper is currently hampered by a lack of clarity that makes it inaccessible to a general audience, and more importantly, by a very limited experimental evaluation that fails to demonstrate the practical significance and scalability of the proposed method. While the theoretical ideas are promising, the work in its current form feels incomplete. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide compelling experimental results on higher-dimensional, more realistic problems.
