- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights along solution trajectories to accelerate convergence, extending replicator dynamics to an accelerated form.  
- Solid theoretical foundation using Lyapunov arguments, providing sufficient conditions for convergence and guarantees for the heuristic.  
- Thorough numerical experiments on varied objective types showing adaptive averaging outperforms existing heuristics like adaptive restarting.  
- Well-written and organized presentation with clear continuous- and discrete-time formulations enabling reproducibility.  
- Experiments are limited to low-dimensional problems (e.g., R^3), raising questions about scalability and robustness.  
- Insufficient comparison to other adaptive techniques (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) regarding implementation and computational cost.  
- Heavy emphasis on continuous-time theory with limited discussion of practical implications for discrete-time algorithms in real-world ML.  
- Pros: introduces a novel, theoretically sound acceleration method that can inspire further research.  
- Pros: adaptive averaging is a meaningful methodological contribution with validating numerical evidence.  
- Cons: experimental scope is narrow and lacks challenging, high-dimensional benchmarks.  
- Cons: practical impact on discrete-time methods and applications is not fully explored.  
- Recommendation: accept—significant theoretical and methodological contribution despite experimental and practical gaps.
