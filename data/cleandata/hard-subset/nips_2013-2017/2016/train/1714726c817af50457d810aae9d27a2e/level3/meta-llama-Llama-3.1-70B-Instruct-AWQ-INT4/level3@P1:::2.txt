This paper presents a novel extension of the accelerated Mirror Descent algorithm, which is a popular optimization technique in machine learning. The authors introduce a generalized averaging scheme, allowing for more flexibility in the choice of weights, and propose an adaptive averaging strategy for a discretization of the algorithm. The adaptive averaging idea is novel and comes with a clear intuition, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed algorithm, including a Lyapunov function and a primal reformulation. The reduction to replicator dynamics is also an interesting contribution, as it shows that the proposed algorithm can be applied to a broader range of problems.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The extension from simple to generalized averaging is relatively straightforward, and the authors only consider the quadratic-rate case. Achieving faster rates would require higher-order methods, which are not always applicable. Additionally, the analysis of the energy function and the primal reformulation, while interesting, offer limited advancement for machine learning.

The adaptive averaging strategy is not fully theoretically grounded, as it relies on discretization approximations, and the authors do not provide an explicit convergence proof. This makes the adaptive averaging strategy effectively a heuristic. Furthermore, the paper lacks a comparison to other existing averaging schemes for Nesterov-style algorithms, which makes it unclear whether the proposed strategy has a practical advantage.

The experiments presented in the paper are limited to a toy quadratic/linear objective and are not convincing for general or strongly convex cases. However, the authors have committed to adding high-dimensional/non-quadratic experiments, a convergence proof for adaptive averaging, implementation on Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method, and comparisons to other adaptive averaging schemes in their rebuttal.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a novel and interesting extension of the accelerated Mirror Descent algorithm, it has some limitations, and the authors need to address these limitations to make the paper more convincing.

Strengths:

* Novel extension of the accelerated Mirror Descent algorithm
* Adaptive averaging strategy with a clear intuition
* Thorough analysis of the proposed algorithm
* Reduction to replicator dynamics

Weaknesses:

* Limited to quadratic-rate case
* Analysis of energy function and primal reformulation offers limited advancement for machine learning
* Adaptive averaging strategy is not fully theoretically grounded
* Lack of comparison to other existing averaging schemes
* Limited experiments

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and interesting extension of the accelerated Mirror Descent algorithm.
* The adaptive averaging strategy has a clear intuition and is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed algorithm.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper has some limitations, including the limited extension to generalized averaging and the lack of a convergence proof for the adaptive averaging strategy.
* The experiments are limited and not convincing for general or strongly convex cases.
* The paper lacks a comparison to other existing averaging schemes.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with the condition that the authors address the limitations mentioned above in their final version.
