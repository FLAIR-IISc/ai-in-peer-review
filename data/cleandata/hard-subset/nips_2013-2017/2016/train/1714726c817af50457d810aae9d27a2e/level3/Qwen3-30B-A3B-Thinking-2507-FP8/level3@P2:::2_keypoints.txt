- Summary: Generalizes continuous-time accelerated mirror descent with flexible averaging w(t) and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic, showing preserved quadratic continuous-time rate and some empirical gains.

- Relation to prior work: Builds directly on Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016); core framework and Lyapunov approach largely follow prior paper.

- Strength 1: Adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and intuitively increases weight on trajectory segments that make progress.

- Strength 2: Presents a clear unified continuous-time framework for accelerated mirror descent using Lyapunov analysis.

- Strength 3: Nicely explains and connects to replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained problems.

- Strength 4: Continuous-time analysis is technically sound with proper Lyapunov construction.

- Weakness 1: Limited originality — generalized averaging is a trivial extension and core framework mirrors prior work.

- Weakness 2: Theoretical gap — no discrete-time convergence proof for the adaptive averaging heuristic, so it remains essentially a heuristic.

- Weakness 3: Limited experiments — evaluations restricted to simple low-dimensional quadratic/linear problems (R^3).

- Weakness 4: No comparison to other adaptive averaging schemes for Nesterov-style algorithms, leaving practical advantage unclear.

- Weakness 5: Narrow focus on quadratic convergence (r(t)=t^2); faster/higher-order rates not considered, limiting applicability.

- Quality: Continuous-time theory is strong but discrete algorithmic grounding is insufficient and experiments are weak.

- Clarity: Well-structured and clearly written with precise math and good explanation of continuous-to-discrete connections.

- Originality: Main idea has merit but insufficient novel contribution beyond the authors' prior NIPS 2016 work.

- Significance: Limited practical impact without discrete-time guarantees and broader empirical support; replicator connection interesting but not transformative.

- Arguments for acceptance: Intuitive heuristic with potential, unified theoretical framing, and a neat replicator dynamics application.

- Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient novelty, lack of discrete-time convergence proof, weak experimental validation, and missing comparisons to alternatives.

- Recommendation: Borderline reject for NIPS as submitted; acceptable for a workshop unless authors add proofs, stronger experiments, and comparisons.
