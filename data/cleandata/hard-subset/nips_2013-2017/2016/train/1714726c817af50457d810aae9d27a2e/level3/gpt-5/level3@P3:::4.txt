Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies a broad class of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics that couple a dual variable Z accumulating gradients at rate η(t) with a primal variable X obtained as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). The central conceptual move is to generalize the usual polynomial averaging schedule behind accelerated dynamics into an explicit, generalized averaging scheme and to analyze it via a Lyapunov function. The authors derive sufficient conditions on η and w that guarantee a target rate r(t) through a clean Lyapunov argument and show how to construct schedules meeting these conditions. They then propose a principled adaptive averaging heuristic that modulates w(t) along the trajectory to further decrease the Lyapunov function, and give guarantees for the continuous-time evolution and a discrete-time method that preserves the O(1/k^2) rate. Empirically, their adaptive averaging compares favorably to popular restart heuristics.

The work builds on and unifies several lines: the ODE viewpoint on acceleration (Su et al., Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan), accelerated mirror descent (Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett), and restart heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès). It contributes (i) a generalized AMD family parameterized by (η, w), (ii) a Lyapunov design recipe that recovers and extends known schedules, (iii) a primal representation illustrating constrained dynamics (positive orthant and the simplex), including an “accelerated replicator” ODE, and (iv) a new adaptive averaging mechanism with theoretical guarantees and numerical comparisons to restarting.

Quality
- Theory: The Lyapunov construction is simple and transparent. Theorem 2 provides verifiable sufficient conditions; Corollary 1 gives an easily usable design rule. The construction of w via the auxiliary a(t) is elegant and recovers classical t^p rates. The second energy function Er offers an additional decreasing quantity that helps interpret the dynamics. The primal reformulation is useful for constrained sets; the replicator example is particularly illuminating. The discrete-time algorithm with adaptive averaging is stated and is claimed to preserve the quadratic rate; the proof sketch is deferred to the supplement.
- Empirics: Experiments on simplex-constrained problems (quadratic, linear, KL) show that adaptive averaging is at least as good as gradient and speed restarting and can be significantly better in the strongly convex case. The plots of Lr and Er support the theoretical monotonicity under the adaptive rule.
- Assumptions and scope: The analysis assumes differentiable convex f with Lipschitz gradient and smooth mirror maps; existence/uniqueness requires t0>0 for general w. These are standard in the ODE literature, but clarity on the practical implications for step-size choices and discretization stability is important.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well organized. The Lyapunov derivation (ineq. (3) and Theorem 2) is easy to follow. The construction of weight schedules via a(t) is crisp. The primal forms for specific feasible sets are helpful. A few suggestions:
- Centralize the intuition behind condition (2) in Theorem 2 (the role of sign(⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩)) and tie it earlier to the adaptive rule.
- Summarize the discrete-time algorithm’s guarantee and parameter recommendations (e.g., β, s) in the main text, not only the supplement.
- Briefly discuss how to choose r(t) in practice and what rates are feasible under discretization beyond p=2.

Originality
The generalized averaging formulation for AMD and the Lyapunov design rule that simultaneously prescribe η and w for a target r(t) are novel and unify prior special cases. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a fresh alternative to restarts: it adjusts averaging weights along the trajectory to lower the Lyapunov function rather than resetting momentum. The acceleration of replicator dynamics via averaging is an attractive example that strengthens the paper’s conceptual reach.

Significance
This work advances the ODE perspective on acceleration in a direction that is both principled and practical. The ability to design η and w for a desired rate and to adapt weights online offers a potentially general tool for accelerated methods, especially under constraints via mirror maps. The empirical finding that adaptively averaging weights along solution trajectories can yield speedups comparable to, and sometimes exceeding, restart-based accelerations is a valuable insight likely to influence practice. The framework also connects naturally to variational formulations (Wibisono et al.) and could spur further developments for faster rates in strongly convex settings and for stochastic/composite objectives.

Weaknesses and questions
- Discretization details: The main text defers the discrete-time analysis and conditions under which the quadratic rate is preserved. A concise theorem in the main body with assumptions and constants would strengthen the impact.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: The heuristic depends on β and step size s; guidance on robustness and tuning would help practitioners. How sensitive are results across problems and dimensions?
- Generality of guarantees: The adaptive rule is analyzed primarily for r(t)=t^2. Can the approach extend to p>2 with first-order schemes, or does it require higher-order discretizations? What about composite (nonsmooth) terms via proximal operators?
- Stochastic settings: The inner-product test in the adaptive rule may be noisy with stochastic gradients; is a smoothed/robust variant possible?
- Empirical breadth: Experiments are mainly on low-dimensional simplex problems; additional results on higher-dimensional and different constraint sets (e.g., boxes, spectrahedra) would broaden the case.

Pros
- Clear, general Lyapunov framework that recovers known accelerated schedules and yields new ones.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantees that preserve the target rate.
- Insightful primal representations and the accelerated replicator example.
- Empirical improvements over established restarting heuristics; monotone Lyapunov decrease observed.
- Well grounded in and connected to recent ODE/variational acceleration literature.

Cons
- Discrete-time guarantees and parameter guidance are mostly in the supplement; limited practical tuning advice.
- Experimental scope is modest and focused on simplex constraints and relatively small dimensions.
- No treatment of stochastic gradients or composite objectives; unclear robustness to noise.

Overall assessment
This is a technically sound and conceptually elegant paper that contributes a unified analysis and a practically relevant adaptive mechanism for accelerated mirror descent dynamics. The new method is very interesting, and the paper is likely to be useful to researchers designing and analyzing accelerated algorithms, especially under constraints. With a bit more emphasis on discrete-time guarantees and broader experiments, it would be even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.
