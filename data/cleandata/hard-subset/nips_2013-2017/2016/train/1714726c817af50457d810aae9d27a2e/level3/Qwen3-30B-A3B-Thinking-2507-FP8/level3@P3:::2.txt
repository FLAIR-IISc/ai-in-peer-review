

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper extends ODE-based accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) with a generalized averaging scheme and proposes an adaptive averaging strategy for discretization. The authors build upon their prior NIPS 2015 paper ("Accelerated Mirror Descent in Continuous and Discrete Time") by generalizing the averaging mechanism and introducing an adaptive heuristic for computing weights in the averaging scheme. The core idea is to adaptively adjust the weights in the weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function, which guarantees the convergence rate.

The paper makes several contributions: (1) a unified study of AMD dynamics using a Lyapunov function approach; (2) a generalized averaging scheme with sufficient conditions for achieving desired convergence rates; (3) an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate while empirically outperforming existing restart-based heuristics; and (4) numerical experiments on simplex-constrained problems showing adaptive averaging's effectiveness.

**Strengths:**
- The adaptive averaging idea is novel and intuitively well-motivated, as it directly targets the Lyapunov function's rate of decrease.
- The continuous-time analysis is technically sound, with clear sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing.
- The paper provides valuable connections to replicator dynamics, showing how accelerated mirror descent can be applied to evolutionary game theory.
- The energy function interpretation (Theorem 3) offers a useful physical analogy that improves understanding of the dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The main theoretical contribution (generalized averaging) is largely trivial, as the extension from the simple case to the generalized case follows directly from the authors' prior work. The paper primarily considers only the quadratic-rate case (r(t)=t²), which has been extensively studied in prior work.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic lacks a complete theoretical foundation. While the continuous-time version has some guarantees, the discrete-time implementation lacks a formal convergence proof, making it effectively a heuristic without rigorous justification.
- The experimental validation is limited to toy quadratic/linear objectives on the simplex in R³. The paper does not demonstrate effectiveness on more complex, high-dimensional, or non-quadratic problems, which limits its practical significance for machine learning applications.
- The paper fails to compare against other existing averaging schemes for Nesterov-style algorithms, making it unclear whether adaptive averaging provides a meaningful advantage beyond restart heuristics.
- Several analyses (energy function interpretation, primal reformulation, reduction to replicator dynamics) are interesting but offer limited advancement for machine learning practice.

**Originality:** The paper's main contribution is incremental. The core ODE formulation, Lyapunov function approach, and discretization strategy all follow directly from the authors' NIPS 2015 paper. While the adaptive averaging idea is novel, its implementation and theoretical grounding are insufficiently developed. The extension to generalized averaging is trivial and does not represent a substantial advance.

**Significance:** The significance is limited. The paper does not achieve faster convergence rates (which would require higher-order methods), and the adaptive averaging heuristic lacks strong theoretical guarantees. The experiments are not convincing for general or strongly convex cases, and the paper does not demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods in practical machine learning scenarios.

**Quality:** The technical content is sound in continuous time, but the discrete-time analysis is incomplete. The paper is well-written and organized, but the experimental section is weak, and the theoretical claims about adaptive averaging are not fully supported.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written with good organization. The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is well-explained, and the supplementary material provides useful additional details.

**Conclusion:** The paper presents an interesting heuristic (adaptive averaging) with intuitive motivation, but the theoretical foundation is incomplete, and the experimental validation is limited. The incremental nature of the contribution, combined with the lack of rigorous discrete-time guarantees and convincing experiments, significantly reduces the paper's impact. The post-rebuttal commitments (high-dimensional experiments, convergence proof, comparisons to other schemes) are necessary to justify acceptance.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and intuitively appealing, with promising initial experimental results. The continuous-time analysis is sound and provides useful insights into accelerated methods.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** The theoretical contribution is largely incremental, the discrete-time analysis lacks rigor, the experiments are limited to toy examples, and the practical significance for machine learning is unclear.

I recommend rejection, as the paper's contribution does not represent a significant advancement beyond the authors' prior work, and the lack of rigorous theoretical guarantees and convincing empirical validation limits its scientific value. The authors should address the theoretical gaps and expand their experiments before resubmission.
