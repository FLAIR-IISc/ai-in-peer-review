This paper presents a generalized continuous-time framework for accelerated mirror descent and introduces a novel adaptive heuristic for setting the averaging weights. The work builds directly upon the recent line of research connecting Nesterov's accelerated methods to second-order ordinary differential equations (ODEs), particularly the interpretation of accelerated mirror descent as a weighted averaging scheme of a dual trajectory, as developed by Su et al. (2014) and Krichene et al. (2015).

The authors first generalize the dynamics from Krichene et al. (2015) to a broader family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by two time-varying functions: `η(t)`, which controls the rate of gradient accumulation in the dual, and `w(t)`, which defines the weights for averaging the mirrored dual trajectory to produce the primal variable. The core theoretical contribution is a Lyapunov-based analysis (Theorem 2) that provides simple, sufficient conditions on `w(t)` and `η(t)` to guarantee a convergence rate of O(1/r(t)) for a desired rate function `r(t)`. This analysis elegantly unifies different averaging schemes under a single theoretical umbrella.

Based on this analysis, the paper's main practical contribution is the "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The key insight is that the derivative of the Lyapunov function has a term that can be made negative by appropriately choosing the averaging weights, thereby accelerating the decrease of the Lyapunov function beyond the guaranteed rate. The proposed heuristic adaptively adjusts the weights based on the sign of `⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩`, effectively increasing the weight on parts of the trajectory that make progress. The authors provide a discrete-time version of this heuristic (Algorithm 1) and present numerical experiments comparing it against the standard (fixed-schedule) accelerated method and common restarting heuristics on several constrained convex optimization problems.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality and Soundness:** The paper is technically sound. The Lyapunov argument is simple, elegant, and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. A significant strength is that the adaptive averaging heuristic, by construction, is guaranteed to preserve the O(1/t^2) convergence rate of the underlying accelerated method. This provides a formal guarantee of robustness that is often missing from other heuristics like adaptive restarting, which can, in principle, degrade performance.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow, especially for a reader familiar with the ODE interpretation of accelerated methods. The motivation is clearly laid out, and the progression from the generalized continuous-time dynamics to the adaptive heuristic is logical. The primal-space formulation in Section 3, which connects the proposed dynamics to the well-known replicator dynamics, is a nice addition that provides further intuition.

*   **Significance and Potential Impact:** The potential impact of this work is high. Accelerated first-order methods are ubiquitous in machine learning, and heuristics to improve their practical performance are of great interest. This paper proposes a heuristic that is both simple to implement and effective. The observation that a small, adaptive modification to the averaging scheme can lead to substantial empirical improvements over both fixed schedules and popular restarting schemes is an important finding. The fact that this speed-up comes with theoretical guarantees makes the method particularly appealing for practitioners.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Originality:** The work is a natural and direct extension of Krichene et al. (2015). While the core framework is not a paradigm shift, the idea of using the derivative of the Lyapunov function to guide an adaptive averaging scheme is novel and insightful. The contribution is thus an important and clever refinement of an existing powerful technique, rather than a completely new approach.

*   **Analysis of the Discretized Algorithm:** The paper's theoretical analysis is performed entirely in continuous time. However, the algorithm tested in the experiments is a discrete-time version. The authors mention in the main text and supplement that the convergence rate of the discretized algorithm can be bounded using a discrete counterpart of the Lyapunov function. It would significantly strengthen the paper to include a formal theorem and at least a sketch of this proof in the main body. This would bridge the gap between the continuous-time theory and the practical algorithm, giving the reader full confidence that the theoretical guarantees translate to the discrete setting.

*   **Connection to Other Methods:** For strongly convex quadratic optimization, Nesterov's accelerated gradient method is known to be closely related to the Conjugate Gradient (CG) method. It would be interesting to discuss if the proposed adaptive averaging scheme has any connections to the line-search or coefficient choices made by CG. Does the heuristic, in this specific case, learn coefficients that approximate those of CG? A brief discussion could provide deeper insight and better position the work within the broader optimization literature.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a simple, well-motivated, and empirically effective heuristic for improving accelerated first-order methods. The method is grounded in a solid and elegant continuous-time Lyapunov analysis and, crucially, comes with theoretical guarantees that it cannot perform worse than the standard non-adaptive version. The empirical results show favorable performance against existing heuristics.
*   **Con:** The work is an incremental, though very useful, extension of prior work. The theoretical analysis of the practical, discrete-time algorithm is relegated to the supplement, which weakens the link between theory and practice in the main paper.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on accelerated optimization. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is both practical and theoretically sound. Despite the incremental nature of the theoretical framework, the novelty of the adaptive scheme and its strong empirical performance make this a significant contribution that is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
