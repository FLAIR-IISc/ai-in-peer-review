- Studies a general continuous-time family called Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) coupling a dual gradient-accumulator and a primal averaged mirror trajectory.
- Formulates AMD via two parameter functions: gradient accumulation rate η(t) and averaging weight w(t), generalizing continuous-time views of Nesterov methods.
- Core analysis uses a Lyapunov function to derive simple, general sufficient conditions on η(t) and w(t) guaranteeing O(1/r(t)) convergence (Theorem 2).
- Introduces a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that greedily modifies weights to decrease the Lyapunov function.
- Proves the adaptive averaging scheme preserves the worst-case O(1/t^2) convergence rate.
- Provides a discrete-time version of the algorithm and applies the framework to derive an accelerated replicator dynamics example.
- Numerical experiments compare adaptive averaging to standard accelerated mirror descent and adaptive restarting on simplex-constrained problems, illustrating empirical improvements.
- Strength: technically sound, clean unified continuous-time framework, and clear exposition.
- Strength: originality and significance of adaptive averaging with theoretical guarantee superior to many restarting heuristics.
- Weakness: experiments are limited to low-dimensional (R^3) problems and lack large-scale real-world evaluations.
- Weakness: theory only guarantees preservation of rate; no proof of faster rates (e.g., linear under strong convexity) despite empirical gains.
- Weakness: derivation/justification of the discrete-time heuristic (Algorithm 1) from continuous conditions could be clearer in the main text.
- Recommendation: overall positive — novelty and theoretical foundations outweigh weaknesses; reviewer recommends acceptance.
