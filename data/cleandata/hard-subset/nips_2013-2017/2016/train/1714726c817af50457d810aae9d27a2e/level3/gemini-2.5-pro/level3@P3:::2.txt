This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated mirror descent (AMD), building upon recent work connecting Nesterov's accelerated methods to second-order ODEs. The authors generalize the ODE framework from their prior work (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015) by introducing a family of dynamics parameterized by a gradient accumulation rate function, η(t), and an averaging weight function, w(t). The core analysis uses a Lyapunov function, similar to their previous paper, to derive sufficient conditions on η(t) and w(t) that guarantee a specific convergence rate of O(1/r(t)).

The main contribution of the paper is a novel adaptive averaging heuristic. Instead of using a fixed schedule for the averaging weights, this strategy adaptively chooses the weights to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function at each step. The authors provide a continuous-time guarantee for this heuristic, showing that it preserves the theoretical convergence rate. The paper presents several other analyses, including an energy function interpretation of the dynamics and a primal-space formulation, which reveals a connection to an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics. Experimental results on low-dimensional problems suggest that a discrete-time version of the adaptive averaging heuristic can outperform standard restarting heuristics.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Heuristic with Clear Intuition:** The core idea of adaptive averaging is novel and elegant. Grounding the heuristic in the continuous-time Lyapunov analysis provides a clear and compelling intuition: adaptively tune the algorithm's parameters to make the "energy" of the system dissipate as quickly as possible. This approach guarantees that the theoretical convergence rate is at least preserved, which is a desirable property not always guaranteed by other heuristics like restarting.

2.  **Improved Theoretical Understanding:** The paper contributes several valuable theoretical insights. The formulation of an "energy function" (Equation 5), composed of potential and kinetic terms, offers a nice physical interpretation of the dynamics. Furthermore, the primal reformulation and its application to simplex-constrained problems, yielding an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics, is an interesting and insightful connection.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the mathematical derivations are presented clearly. The structure is logical, guiding the reader from the general continuous-time framework to the specific adaptive heuristic and its discretization.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality and Significance Concerns:** The paper's main framework is a direct and somewhat incremental extension of the authors' previous NIPS paper [11]. The Lyapunov function, the structure of the ODE, and the discretization strategy are largely inherited from that work. The generalization from a specific `t^(r-1)` averaging scheme to a generic `w(t)` feels like a trivial extension, and its utility is limited as the analysis and experiments ultimately focus on the quadratic `r(t) = t^2` rate, which was already the focus of prior work. The claim that faster rates could be achieved is not substantiated, as this would require higher-order methods which are outside the scope of the proposed framework.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees for the Main Contribution:** While the continuous-time intuition for adaptive averaging is strong, the discrete-time implementation (Algorithm 1) is effectively a heuristic. The paper does not provide a formal convergence proof for this algorithm, and the connection to the continuous-time analysis relies on discretization approximations. This leaves the primary contribution without solid theoretical footing, which is a significant weakness.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experiments are not convincing enough to demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed method for the machine learning community. They are limited to low-dimensional (R^3) toy problems with simple quadratic, linear, or KL-divergence objectives. It is unclear if the observed benefits would carry over to high-dimensional, non-quadratic, or strongly convex problems typically encountered in machine learning. The authors promise to add such experiments, which would be essential for a stronger paper.

4.  **Missing Comparisons:** The experimental comparison is limited to restarting heuristics. However, there are other adaptive schemes for Nesterov-style methods, including other forms of adaptive averaging. Without comparing to a broader range of relevant baselines, the practical advantage of the proposed method remains unclear.

**Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel and intuitive heuristic (adaptive averaging) for accelerated methods.
*   The heuristic is grounded in the continuous-time Lyapunov analysis, which guarantees that the underlying convergence rate is preserved.
*   Provides new theoretical insights, including an energy function interpretation and an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Major originality concerns: The core ODE framework and Lyapunov analysis are a minor extension of the authors' prior NIPS paper [11].
*   The main contribution (adaptive averaging) is not fully supported by theory; the discrete version is a heuristic without a convergence proof.
*   Experimental validation is weak and unconvincing, limited to low-dimensional toy problems, which fails to demonstrate practical significance for the ML community.
*   Important baselines are missing, specifically, comparisons to other adaptive averaging schemes.

**Recommendation:**

While the core idea of adaptive averaging is interesting and intuitive, the paper in its current form feels incomplete. The limited originality, lack of a convergence proof for the proposed algorithm, and weak empirical evidence are significant concerns. The authors acknowledge several of these weaknesses and have committed to addressing them (e.g., adding a convergence proof, more comprehensive experiments, and comparisons). If these commitments are met, the paper could become a solid contribution. However, as it stands, the weaknesses outweigh the strengths.
