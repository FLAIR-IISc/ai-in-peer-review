This paper studies a family of continuous-time dynamics for accelerated constrained convex optimization, referred to as Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD). The dynamics are elegantly formulated as a coupling between a dual variable that accumulates gradients at a rate η(t) and a primal variable obtained by a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). The main theoretical contribution is a Lyapunov-based analysis that provides sufficient conditions on the functions w(t) and η(t) to guarantee a desired convergence rate of 1/r(t). This framework recovers and generalizes previous results on accelerated methods, for instance, showing how the replicator dynamics can be accelerated.

Building on this analysis, the paper proposes a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The core idea is to adaptively choose the averaging weights w(t) to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, thereby speeding up convergence. A key strength of this approach is the theoretical guarantee that it preserves the O(1/t^2) convergence rate for non-strongly convex functions, a guarantee that is missing for some popular restarting heuristics. The paper presents a discrete-time version of the algorithm and provides numerical experiments on low-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems, comparing the proposed method against standard accelerated mirror descent and two restarting heuristics.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The concept of adaptive averaging is a novel and interesting alternative to the widely studied restarting heuristics for speeding up accelerated methods. The continuous-time framework provides an elegant and unified way to analyze a broad class of accelerated dynamics.
2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The use of a Lyapunov function to derive sufficient conditions for a given convergence rate is a powerful technique. The primary theoretical result—that the adaptive heuristic preserves the convergence rate—is a significant advantage over other heuristics that lack such guarantees for general convex functions.
3.  **Clarity of Motivation:** The motivation for the work is clear, and the connection between acceleration and averaging is well-established by relating to prior work. The derivation showing that the replicator dynamics can be accelerated is a nice, concrete example of the framework's utility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is quite dense and may be difficult to follow for readers who are not experts in continuous-time optimization dynamics and mirror descent. More intuition and background could significantly improve accessibility.
    *   Specifically, the introduction of the Lyapunov function `Lr` in Equation (2) and the subsequent derivation leading to the inequality in (3) would benefit from more explanation. Providing intuition for why `Lr` is a suitable choice (e.g., how it balances the objective function value with a notion of "energy" in the dual space) would be very helpful.
    *   Similarly, a more intuitive explanation of the terms in the inequality (3) would help the reader understand the conditions derived in Theorem 2.

2.  **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evidence for the method's effectiveness is limited. The experiments are conducted on toy problems in R^3. While these are excellent for visualization and illustrating the method's behavior, they do not demonstrate its practical significance or scalability to the high-dimensional problems common in machine learning. The claims of outperforming restarting heuristics would be much stronger if supported by at least one experiment on a larger-scale, more realistic problem.

3.  **Discussion of Related Work:** The paper briefly mentions adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam but dismisses them as "not designed for accelerated methods." While true, a more detailed discussion would strengthen the paper. It would be valuable to contrast the philosophy of adapting the averaging weights (as done here) with that of adapting per-coordinate learning rates or momentum terms. This would better position the contribution within the broader landscape of adaptive optimization methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically-grounded adaptive heuristic for accelerated optimization.
*   The guarantee that the method preserves the O(1/t^2) convergence rate is a strong and important result.
*   The continuous-time analysis provides a valuable, unified perspective on a family of accelerated dynamics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical significance of the proposed method is not well-established due to the limited scope of the experiments (only low-dimensional problems).
*   The paper's clarity could be improved, potentially limiting its impact on a broader NIPS audience.

**Detailed Comments:**
*   I strongly recommend adding experiments on a higher-dimensional problem to demonstrate the scalability and practical utility of adaptive averaging.
*   Please consider expanding the discussion in Section 1 and 2 to provide more intuition behind the Lyapunov function and the core derivations. This would make the paper's elegant ideas accessible to a wider audience.
*   In Section 1, a more nuanced discussion of the relationship and differences between adaptive averaging and other adaptive methods (Adagrad, Adam, etc.) would be beneficial.
*   Minor point: In the figures showing trajectories (e.g., Figure 2), it can be difficult to distinguish between the different methods. Please consider using different line styles (dotted, dashed) in addition to or instead of colors to improve clarity, especially for black-and-white printing.
