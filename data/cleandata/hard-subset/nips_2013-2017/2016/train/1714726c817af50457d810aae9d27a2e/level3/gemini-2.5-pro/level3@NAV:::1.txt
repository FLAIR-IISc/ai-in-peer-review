This paper presents a novel adaptive heuristic for Nesterov's accelerated method, building upon the continuous-time ODE interpretation established by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The core idea is to generalize the averaging scheme within the accelerated descent dynamics and introduce an intuitive rule to adaptively reweigh iterates. This reweighing is designed to greedily decrease a Lyapunov function, thereby accelerating convergence while provably maintaining the underlying convergence rate. This work relates to previous efforts on improving accelerated methods, such as adaptive restarting, but distinguishes itself by providing stronger theoretical guarantees.

The paper has several significant strengths. The primary contribution is a simple, elegant, and practical heuristic with strong theoretical grounding. Unlike many prior heuristics, the proposed adaptive averaging method is guaranteed to dominate the standard fixed-schedule averaging, ensuring that the O(1/t^2) convergence rate is preserved. The mathematical argument, rooted in a Lyapunov analysis, is clear and easy to follow for readers familiar with the continuous-time optimization literature. The originality lies in demonstrating how a small, adaptive modification to the averaging weights—a previously fixed component—can yield dramatic performance improvements. The empirical evaluation is convincing, showing promising speed-ups over both the standard Nesterov scheme and adaptive restarting heuristics across a broad class of convex functions. Given the widespread use of Nesterov's methods, this work has a large potential for practical impact.

The main weakness is that the technical development is a close extension of prior work, primarily building on the framework of Krichene et al. While the continuous-time analysis is sound, the paper would be substantially strengthened by including a more detailed analysis of the discretized process in the main text. Providing a formal proof sketch that the O(1/k^2) rate is preserved for the discrete algorithm would make the contribution more complete and self-contained.

For future work, the authors might investigate the links between their adaptive averaging scheme and the Conjugate Gradient method on strongly convex quadratics. Exploring this connection could provide deeper insights into the nature of the acceleration achieved. Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a valuable and impactful contribution to the optimization community.
