The paper "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics" proposes a novel adaptive averaging heuristic for constrained convex optimization. The authors claim that their method preserves the original convergence rate for non-strongly convex objectives, which is a significant advantage over existing restarting heuristics. The paper provides both theoretical and experimental evidence to support this claim.

The reviewer found the paper hard to follow and recommends adding background material to make the content more accessible to a broader audience. Additionally, the reviewer suggests clarifying abbreviations and terms to avoid confusion. For instance, the term "AMD" is used throughout the paper, but its meaning is not explicitly stated until later sections.

The reviewer also requests more intuitive explanations for certain equations, such as those between lines 33-34 and 46-47. These sections appear to be crucial to the development of the adaptive averaging heuristic, but the reviewer found them difficult to understand.

Furthermore, the reviewer asks for a discussion of the relation to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad and Adam. These methods are widely used in deep learning and other applications, and a comparison with the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic would be valuable.

The reviewer also questions the practical significance of the results, as the experiments are conducted in R^3, which is a relatively low-dimensional space. The reviewer requests evidence of scalability to higher dimensions, as this would be more representative of real-world applications.

Finally, the reviewer suggests minor improvements, such as using a dotted line or colors to show the primal trajectory in Figure 1, as the current reference to the solid line is ambiguous.

In conclusion, the paper presents an interesting and potentially significant contribution to the field of constrained convex optimization. However, the reviewer's comments highlight the need for clarification, additional background material, and more intuitive explanations to make the content more accessible to a broader audience.
