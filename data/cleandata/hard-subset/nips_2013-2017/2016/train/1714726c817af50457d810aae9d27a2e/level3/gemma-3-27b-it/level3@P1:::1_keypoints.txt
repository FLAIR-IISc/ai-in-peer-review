- Investigates adaptive averaging schemes within accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization.  
- Builds on continuous-time ODE connections to Nesterov’s methods and proposes a heuristic to adaptively compute weights in dual-trajectory averaging.  
- Dynamically adjusts weights based on the Lyapunov function decrease, preserving the quadratic convergence rate with theoretical guarantees.  
- Unifies various accelerated mirror descent dynamics via a single framework parameterized by weight functions w and η.  
- Derives accelerated replicator dynamics as a concrete example, showing applicability to evolutionary game theory.  
- Provides a rigorous mathematical formulation and a Lyapunov argument giving sufficient conditions for desired convergence.  
- Presents a well-defined discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) that is implementable.  
- Adaptive averaging heuristic is principled and maintains theoretical guarantees while aiming to speed convergence.  
- Numerical experiments show encouraging performance gains, especially in the strongly convex case, with useful trajectory and energy visualizations.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope, focusing mainly on the simplex constraint.  
- Analysis of the discretized process and formal convergence proof for the discrete algorithm are missing or only briefly mentioned.  
- Comparison to adaptive learning-rate methods (Adagrad, Adam) is brief and should be expanded.  
- The connection to Conjugate Gradient methods is noted but underdeveloped.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and notationally consistent; some primal-representation sections lack intuitive explanation.  
- The adaptive averaging heuristic and its theoretical justification represent a novel contribution.  
- Significance: addresses fundamental problem of accelerating convergence and is likely useful to practitioners and researchers.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel heuristic with guarantees, rigorous analysis, empirical improvements, and strong theoretical grounding.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experiments, missing discrete-process analysis, and insufficient comparisons to existing adaptive optimizers.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with promising contributions; recommended for acceptance subject to further analysis and broader experiments.
