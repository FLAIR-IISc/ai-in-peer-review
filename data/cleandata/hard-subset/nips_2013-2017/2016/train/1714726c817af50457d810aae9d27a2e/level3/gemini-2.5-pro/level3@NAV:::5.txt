This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order methods in constrained convex optimization. Building on prior work connecting Nesterov's methods to second-order ODEs (e.g., Su et al., 2020), the authors introduce a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent dynamics. The core theoretical tool is a Lyapunov function, which is used to derive sufficient conditions on the system's weight functions to guarantee a desired convergence rate. The paper's main contribution is a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. This heuristic adaptively computes the averaging weights to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, thereby speeding up convergence while provably preserving the underlying convergence rate guarantees.

**Strengths:**
*   The central idea of adaptive averaging is novel, intuitive, and well-motivated. By design, the heuristic ensures the Lyapunov function remains non-increasing, which elegantly guarantees that the method preserves the O(1/t^2) convergence rate. This provides a significant advantage over prior restarting heuristics, which often lack such guarantees for general convex problems.
*   The theoretical framework provides a unified and insightful perspective on a broad class of accelerated dynamics, clearly linking the choice of averaging and gradient accumulation schedules to the convergence rate via the Lyapunov analysis.
*   The empirical results are promising. Across several convex optimization problems, the proposed method performs competitively with or significantly better than established adaptive restarting techniques.

**Weaknesses:**
*   As a reviewer with limited expertise in the analysis of continuous-time dynamics, the connection between the continuous-time theory and the discrete-time implementation (Algorithm 1) could be more thoroughly elaborated in the main text for clarity.
*   The experiments focus on comparisons to restarting schemes. While this is the most direct comparison, including other adaptive first-order methods (e.g., Adam) could provide a broader context for the method's practical performance, even if those methods are not based on the same acceleration principles.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality contribution to the optimization literature. The work is technically sound, and the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is an original and significant idea that advances the state-of-the-art in adaptive acceleration. Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale convex optimization are likely to find these ideas useful. The paper is clearly written and provides a solid foundation for future work.
