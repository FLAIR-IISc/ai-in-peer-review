

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization using a Lyapunov function approach. It generalizes previous work by Krichene et al. [11] on accelerated mirror descent dynamics by introducing a family of ODEs parameterized by weight functions w and η. The authors provide sufficient conditions on these weights to achieve desired convergence rates and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes weights to speed up the Lyapunov function decrease. The method is empirically compared to adaptive restarting techniques, showing favorable results in numerical experiments.

The paper's main strengths include the clear theoretical development using Lyapunov arguments, the novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance, and the interesting application to replicator dynamics on the simplex. The work builds naturally on previous NIPS contributions by Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11], extending the connection between continuous-time dynamics and acceleration.

Regarding novelty, while the adaptive averaging idea is promising, the paper could better distinguish itself from related adaptive methods. The comparison to adaptive restarting is valuable, but the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this approach differs from other adaptive methods like Adagrad [7] or Adam [10] in the context of accelerated methods.

The quality of the theoretical analysis appears sound, with clear sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing. The empirical results are well-presented and support the theoretical claims. The paper is generally well-written, though the distinction between the proposed method and previous adaptive approaches could be clearer.

In terms of significance, the method offers a theoretically grounded approach to accelerating constrained optimization, which could be valuable for practitioners. However, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of when this approach would be preferable to existing methods.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that builds nicely on previous work. The adaptive averaging heuristic appears promising and merits further investigation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better clarify the novelty and practical impact of the approach.
