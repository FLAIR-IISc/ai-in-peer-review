This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization via a primal–dual ODE framework that extends Krichene et al.’s Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD). The key idea is to generate the primal trajectory by a generalized averaging of the mirror of the dual variable, with time-varying weight functions w(t) and η(t). Using a Lyapunov construction, Theorem 2 gives clean sufficient conditions on these weights to guarantee a target rate 1/r(t); by selecting r(t) = t^p, the authors obtain continuous-time rates faster than the usual 1/t^2. They also propose an adaptive scheme that selects averaging weights to steepen the Lyapunov decrease, with provable guarantees in continuous time, and compare this heuristic empirically to popular restarting techniques. The paper connects to prior ODE views of acceleration (Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan) and to mirror/replicator dynamics on constrained domains.

Quality: The continuous-time analysis is rigorous and well executed. The Lyapunov argument and the conditions linking w, η, and r are transparent and unifying; the primal reformulation and examples (e.g., accelerated replicator dynamics) are instructive. The adaptive weighting rule is simple and its Lyapunov monotonicity is clear. However, the discrete-time theory is not fully developed: while the authors state quadratic-rate preservation for a discretization and provide supplemental bounds, it remains open whether the improved continuous rates (p > 2) can be realized by practical first-order discretizations without moving to higher-order methods, and whether the adaptive scheme retains its guarantees beyond the quadratic regime.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized; assumptions and constructions are mostly clear. It would help to consolidate conditions on ψ, ∇ψ*, and initialization at t0, and to explicitly state which guarantees hold in main text vs. supplement.

Originality: The generalization from fixed polynomial weights to a broad class of averaging schedules and an adaptive Lyapunov-driven weighting is novel within the AMD line. The link to accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex is appealing.

Significance: The framework is potentially impactful for designing and analyzing accelerated methods under constraints. The adaptive averaging heuristic, if shown to transfer discrete-time gains beyond 1/k^2, could be widely useful.

Related work: The paper should more thoroughly cite and contrast with Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors on inertial gradient flows with vanishing viscosity and faster-than-1/t^2 rates, and clearly position its Lyapunov design relative to those dynamics.

Pros:
- Rigorous, unifying continuous-time analysis; clear sufficient conditions.
- Generalized and adaptive averaging with provable Lyapunov decrease.
- Insightful examples (replicator) and empirical comparisons to restarting.

Cons:
- Discrete-time theory limited; no proof that faster continuous rates persist after discretization for first-order schemes.
- Experiments are modest and largely low-dimensional; limited sensitivity analysis.
- Incomplete coverage of closely related inertial ODE literature (Attouch–Peypouquet et al.).

Suggestions:
- Clarify discrete-time guarantees and, if possible, establish conditions under which p > 2 continuous rates discretize to practical algorithms.
- Expand experiments (higher dimensions, additional constraints), include sensitivity to β/step sizes, and ablations of adaptive weighting vs. restarting.
- Add and discuss the Attouch–Peypouquet line of work and other inertial Lyapunov designs.
