- Studies accelerated first-order methods via continuous-time dynamics and introduces a broader family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) parameterized by two weight functions.  
- Main theoretical contribution: a Lyapunov-function analysis giving sufficient conditions on weights to achieve desired convergence rates.  
- Shows preservation of the O(1/t^2) rate for non-strongly convex objectives.  
- Proposes a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic that adaptively selects averaging weights to speed convergence and provides a formal guarantee.  
- Strength: original, theoretically grounded heuristic with elegant continuous-time Lyapunov analysis.  
- Potential significance: adaptively tuning averaging (rather than momentum/step size) is an underexplored and potentially practical direction if scalable.  
- Clarity concern: manuscript is hard to follow for readers not expert in continuous-time mirror descent; needs more background on mirror maps and Bregman divergence.  
- Technical exposition gaps: key derivations (inequality (3) and the energy-function analysis) lack intuition and step-by-step explanation.  
- Experimental weakness: validation limited to low-dimensional R^3 toy problems; lacks high-dimensional, ML-relevant scalability tests.  
- Relation to prior work: insufficient discussion of connections to adaptive methods like Adagrad/Adam and adaptive learning-rate literature.  
- Minor: Figure 1’s legend is ambiguous (multiple solid lines); use distinct line styles or colors for clarity.
