- Paper revisits accelerated mirror descent (AMD) via ODEs and proposes an adaptive averaging mechanism that modifies time-varying primal weights.
- Uses a Lyapunov argument to analyze generalized AMD ODEs with weight w(t) and accumulation rate η(t), giving sufficient conditions for 1/r(t) convergence.
- Introduces a heuristic that adaptively adjusts w(t) to monotonically decrease the Lyapunov energy and dominate fixed schedules.
- Presents constrained examples including an accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex and a discretization claimed to preserve O(1/k^2) rate.
- Continuous-time analysis is sound, clear, and accessible to readers familiar with prior ODE-based AMD work.
- The monotonic Lyapunov decrease makes the dominance guarantee transparent and convincing in continuous time.
- Discrete-time section is promising but lacks a clear, self-contained convergence proof in the main text (assumptions, step-size stability, precise rate statement).
- Need a discussion of sensitivity to hyperparameters (β) and step size for the discrete algorithm.
- Empirical results show consistent speed-ups over adaptive restarts but are limited in scale and mainly on low-dimensional/simplex problems.
- Paper is well written and organized; including the discrete Lyapunov proof would improve readability.
- Should compare to related “adaptive averaging” work (e.g., Flammarion–Bach) and more sharply position against adaptive restart methods.
- Technical development closely extends prior ODE analyses, but the adaptive weighting heuristic and dominance guarantee are novel.
- The accelerated replicator example is a neat, compelling application demonstrating broader applicability.
- Significance: a simple adaptive modification with theoretical safety and empirical gains is potentially impactful given wide use of Nesterov-style methods.
- Missing: stronger-convexity results, links to Conjugate Gradient on quadratics, and exploration of robustness in stochastic/noisy or higher-dimensional settings.
- Pros: clean Lyapunov framework, provably dominating adaptive averaging, broad applicability (mirror maps/constraints), and empirical gains versus restarts.
- Cons: discrete-time analysis not centralized/sharpened, limited experimental breadth, unassessed parameter sensitivity/robustness, and unexplored CG connections.
- Suggestions: provide a full discrete-time convergence analysis (stability and complexity constants), add higher-dimensional and stochastic experiments, and investigate links between adaptive weights and CG directions on quadratics.
