Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies a broad family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics for constrained convex optimization, parameterized by two time-varying functions: η(t), controlling the accumulation of gradients in a dual variable Z(t), and w(t), defining a generalized averaging that forms the primal iterate X(t) as a weighted average of the mirror map ∇ψ∗(Z(τ)). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f∗) + Dψ∗(Z, z∗), the authors derive simple sufficient conditions on η and w under which Lr is nonincreasing and f(X(t)) − f∗ = O(1/r(t)). Choosing r(t) = t2 recovers the canonical O(1/t2) accelerated rate. They also give an equivalent primal ODE, connect their framework to past continuous-time analyses of acceleration, and show that replicator dynamics on the simplex can be “accelerated” via averaging.

Building on this, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that, at each time, chooses the weight ratio a(t) = w(t)/W(t) to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative whenever possible, while preserving the same convergence rate guarantees. A discrete-time algorithm is presented for the quadratic-rate case (r(t) = t2), and the authors prove that the quadratic rate is preserved. Empirically, on simplex-constrained toy problems (quadratic, linear, KL), adaptive averaging performs as well as or better than adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient restart of O’Donoghue & Candès and speed restart of Su, Boyd & Candès), with sizable gains in some instances and more stable Lyapunov decrease.

This work extends and unifies several lines of research at NIPS and beyond: continuous-time interpretations of acceleration (Su-Boyd-Candès; Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan), accelerated mirror descent including constrained/proximal settings (Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett), and practical heuristics such as adaptive restarting (O’Donoghue-Candès). It also relates to the observation that acceleration often arises from appropriate averaging (e.g., Flammarion-Bach), and highlights an interesting link to evolutionary game-theoretic replicator dynamics.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: A clean Lyapunov framework that provides sufficient conditions on η and w to achieve a target rate r(t), with illustrative examples (r(t) = tp).
- Generality: Works for constrained convex problems via mirror maps; includes a primal representation; maintains feasibility.
- Novel heuristic: Adaptive averaging is simple, principled (drives the Lyapunov derivative down), and comes with continuous-time guarantees plus a discrete-time quadratic-rate result.
- Practical relevance: Adaptive averaging compares favorably to adaptive restarting in experiments and yields more monotone Lyapunov/energy behavior.
- Interesting connection: Acceleration of replicator dynamics via averaging is elegant and could have broader implications.

Weaknesses
- Empirical scope: Experiments are small-scale (R3 simplex) and mostly illustrative. It is unclear how the gains translate to higher dimensions, other constraint sets, composite objectives, or real ML tasks.
- Discrete-time analysis: The main text sketches the algorithm and defers key proofs to the supplement. Stability and rate preservation beyond p = 2 (and beyond smooth f) are not addressed.
- Heuristic details: Some algorithmic steps/parameters (e.g., line 6 “βmax”, exact choices of R and Dψ, step-size policy) are terse or slightly ambiguous in the main text, which may hinder reproducibility.
- Comparative baselines: Only restarting heuristics are considered. Comparisons to line-search-based accelerations, monotone variants, or adaptive steps (Adagrad/Adam in constrained settings) are not included.
- Strongly convex case: Although the heuristic empirically excels there, theoretical guarantees for faster (e.g., linear) rates under strong convexity are left as future work.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The continuous-time analysis appears careful and correct, with transparent sufficient conditions (η ≥ r′ and the coupling condition on a(t)). The Lyapunov and energy functions are well-motivated. The discrete-time quadratic-rate claim is plausible but relegated to the supplement; more detail in the main paper would strengthen the contribution.
- Empirical support: Qualitative and quantitative plots support the claims, particularly monotone decrease of the Lyapunov function with adaptive averaging. However, the experiments are limited in scale and variety.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and well-organized, with helpful derivations (AMD’, primal forms) and concrete examples (orthant, simplex/replicator).
- Notational load is heavy (e.g., X̌, Ž, a(t)), and some algorithmic details in Algorithm 1 need clarification (exact definitions of R and Dψ, parameter β, line 6 expression). A short “how to set η and w” recipe and a clearer mapping between continuous and discrete parameters would help.

Originality
- The adaptive averaging heuristic that directly targets the Lyapunov derivative, along with general conditions linking η, w, and target rate r(t), appears novel. The acceleration of replicator dynamics via averaging is also original and interesting. The work builds thoughtfully on established continuous-time acceleration frameworks.

Significance
- The framework can influence the design of accelerated methods for constrained problems, offering an alternative to restarting with provable rate preservation and potentially improved stability. Impact will depend on stronger discrete-time results and broader empirical validation, but the ideas are promising.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand discrete-time analysis in the main text (assumptions, stability, rate proofs), and discuss extensions to strong convexity and composite objectives.
- Provide guidance for setting η(t), w(t), and β in practice; include sensitivity analyses.
- Scale experiments to higher dimensions and diverse constraints (e.g., ℓ1 ball, spectrahedron), and include comparisons to alternative monotone/line-search accelerations.
- Clarify Algorithm 1 (remove typos, define all symbols and choices of R and Dψ; detail restart baselines and their tuning for fairness).

Reviewer expertise
My expertise on continuous-time ODE analyses is limited; I am more familiar with first-order methods and practical heuristics. I nevertheless find the adaptive-averaging idea interesting and promising.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, general Lyapunov framework for accelerated dynamics with constrained domains.
- Simple adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantees and encouraging empirical behavior.
- Novel perspective that may influence practice beyond restarting heuristics.
- Interesting application to replicator dynamics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental scope; unclear scalability and generality in discrete time.
- Key discrete-time guarantees not fully presented in the main paper.
- Some clarity issues in algorithm specification and parameterization.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The conceptual contribution and principled heuristic are valuable, and with minor clarifications and expanded empirical validation, the paper could have meaningful impact on accelerated optimization for constrained problems.
