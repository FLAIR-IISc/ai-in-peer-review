### Summary of the paper

This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated convex optimization. It generalizes the framework presented in prior work (e.g., Krichene et al., 2015) by introducing a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics. These dynamics are parameterized by two functions: `η(t)`, which controls the rate at which a dual variable accumulates gradients, and `w(t)`, which defines a generalized weighted averaging scheme to produce the primal variable.

The core theoretical contribution is a unified analysis of this family of ODEs using a Lyapunov function. The authors derive simple and elegant sufficient conditions on `η(t)` and `w(t)` that guarantee a desired convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))`. This analysis provides a clear and insightful connection between the choice of averaging schedule and the resulting convergence behavior. As a concrete example, the paper shows how the well-known replicator dynamics from evolutionary game theory can be accelerated using this averaging framework.

Building on this theoretical foundation, the paper's main practical contribution is a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. Instead of using a fixed, pre-determined schedule for the weights `w(t)`, this heuristic adaptively chooses them to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function along the solution trajectory. A key advantage of this approach, unlike many existing heuristics like adaptive restarting, is that it provably preserves the theoretical `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate by construction. The authors present a discrete-time version of this heuristic and provide numerical experiments comparing it to standard accelerated mirror descent and popular restarting schemes. The results demonstrate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as restarting, with significant improvements in some cases.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is sound, clear, and elegant. The use of the Lyapunov function `Lr` to unify the analysis of the `AMDw,η` family of dynamics is a strong contribution. The derivation of the sufficient conditions in Theorem 2 is straightforward and convincing. The work is complete, connecting the continuous-time theory to a concrete discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) and validating its performance empirically. The authors are careful to state their assumptions and the claims are well-supported by the analysis. The claim that the discrete-time algorithm preserves the quadratic convergence rate is a crucial part of the contribution, and its proof is appropriately provided (in the supplementary material).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The narrative flows logically from the generalized continuous-time framework to the specific examples and finally to the proposed adaptive heuristic. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, relating the work to the existing literature on continuous-time optimization dynamics and heuristics for acceleration. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow. The connection between the continuous-time conditions in Theorem 2 and the discrete-time heuristic in Algorithm 1 is made explicit and intuitive.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While building on the recent trend of analyzing optimization algorithms via ODEs, it introduces several novel ideas.
1.  The generalization of accelerated dynamics to a family parameterized by arbitrary averaging weights `w(t)` and rate functions `η(t)` is a new and powerful perspective.
2.  The main contribution, the adaptive averaging heuristic, is a genuinely new idea. It provides a principled alternative to the more ad-hoc restarting schemes. The concept of using the Lyapunov function not just for analysis but also for *control* (i.e., guiding the algorithm's parameters) is very clever and insightful.
3.  The paper effectively distinguishes its contribution from prior work. It clearly explains how its framework generalizes previous ODE models and how adaptive averaging differs from both restarting schemes and adaptive learning rate methods like Adagrad.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be influential.
1.  The theoretical framework provides a deeper understanding of the role of averaging in accelerated methods. This perspective could inspire the design of new, more sophisticated optimization algorithms.
2.  The adaptive averaging heuristic is practical, easy to implement, and comes with stronger theoretical guarantees than popular alternatives. Practitioners looking to speed up accelerated methods now have a compelling new tool that is guaranteed not to harm the worst-case convergence.
3.  The empirical results, while on relatively low-dimensional problems, are promising and clearly demonstrate the potential of the method. The fact that it outperforms or matches adaptive restarting, a widely used and effective heuristic, suggests it could have a real-world impact.

The main weakness is minor: the theoretical guarantee for the discrete-time version of the algorithm, which is central to its practical appeal, is deferred to the supplementary material. While this is standard practice, it means the main paper's claims partially rest on analysis not present in the body of the work.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Insight:** Introduces a novel and insightful generalization of accelerated dynamics and proposes a genuinely new "adaptive averaging" heuristic based on this framework.
*   **Strong Theory:** The theoretical analysis is elegant, technically sound, and provides a clear justification for the proposed method.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposed heuristic is simple, practical, and addresses the important problem of improving the empirical performance of accelerated methods.
*   **Superior Guarantees:** Unlike existing restart heuristics, adaptive averaging provably preserves the method's worst-case convergence rate, which is a significant advantage.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are promising and effectively demonstrate the competitiveness and potential benefits of the proposed approach compared to established baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical guarantees for the discrete-time algorithm, a key part of the practical contribution, are in the supplementary material.
*   The empirical evaluation is conducted on low-dimensional problems. While effective for visualization and demonstrating the core behavior, evaluation on larger-scale machine learning problems would further strengthen the paper's impact.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a beautiful and complete piece of work that advances both the theory and practice of accelerated optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
