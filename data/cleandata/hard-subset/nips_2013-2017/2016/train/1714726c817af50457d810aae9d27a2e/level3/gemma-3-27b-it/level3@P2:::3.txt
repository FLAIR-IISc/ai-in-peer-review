This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, building upon the recent primal-dual ODE framework introduced by the authors and others. The core contribution lies in a generalization of averaging schemes used to construct the primal trajectory, providing sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) for achieving desirable convergence rates. The authors demonstrate the applicability of their framework by deriving an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic with provable convergence guarantees, comparing its performance favorably to existing restarting techniques through numerical experiments.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the continuous-time analysis appears rigorous. It naturally extends the authors’ previous work and contributes to the growing body of research connecting accelerated methods to continuous dynamical systems. The exploration of different averaging schemes and the derivation of convergence rates under relatively general conditions are valuable additions. The empirical evaluation provides further support for the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic. The connection to replicator dynamics is also a nice illustration of the theory.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the authors establish convergence guarantees for the adaptive averaging heuristic in continuous time, the analysis of the discrete version (Algorithm 1) is largely relegated to the supplementary material and lacks the same level of detail. A more thorough analysis of the discrete case, including a tighter bound on the convergence rate, would significantly strengthen the paper. The paper acknowledges this gap, but it remains a limitation.

Furthermore, the related work section, while reasonably comprehensive, omits several relevant contributions. Notably, the recent work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and collaborators on accelerated methods and their connection to vanishing viscosity solutions (e.g., Attouch et al., 2017; Attouch, Peypouquet, & Redont, 2015) is missing. These papers offer a complementary perspective on acceleration and dynamical systems and should be discussed. Also, the earlier work on cubic regularization of Newton's method by Nesterov (2008) could be relevant to the discussion on higher-order accelerated methods.

A key open question, also alluded to by the authors, concerns the preservation of the improved continuous-time convergence rates after discretization. While the authors mention results by Wibisono et al. (2016) on preserving rates for faster r(t) = t^p, it isn't clear if the adaptive averaging scheme itself leads to rates exceeding the standard 1/k^2 in discrete time. Investigating this aspect would be an interesting direction for future work.

**Strengths:**

*   Rigorous analysis of continuous-time dynamics.
*   Clear and well-motivated problem formulation.
*   Generalization of averaging schemes and convergence guarantees.
*   Empirically validated adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   Connection to replicator dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited analysis of the discrete-time algorithm.
*   Omission of relevant related work (e.g., Attouch, Peypouquet).
*   Unclear whether improved continuous rates are preserved after discretization.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and mathematically sound approach to analyzing and improving accelerated first-order methods.
*   The theoretical results are significant and contribute to our understanding of the interplay between continuous and discrete optimization dynamics.
*   The empirical validation provides confidence in the effectiveness of the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of a thorough analysis of the discrete-time algorithm is a significant weakness.
*   The omission of relevant related work weakens the positioning of the paper within the broader literature.
*   The open question regarding the preservation of convergence rates after discretization leaves some uncertainty about the practical impact of the theoretical results.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the weaknesses mentioned above are noteworthy, the strengths of the paper – particularly the rigorous analysis, novel approach, and empirical validation – outweigh them. The paper is of high quality and should be accepted to NIPS 2016, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the discrete-time analysis and including the missing related work in a revised version.
