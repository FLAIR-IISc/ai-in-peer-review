This paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with generalized averaging and proposes an adaptive averaging strategy for its discrete-time counterpart. The dynamics couple a dual trajectory Z(t) accumulating gradients at rate η(t) and a primal trajectory X(t) formed as a weighted average of the mirrored dual path with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors give sufficient conditions on η and w ensuring a convergence rate O(1/r(t)); for the standard choice r(t)=t^2, they recover the familiar O(1/t^2) rate. They also provide a primal formulation, identify an “energy” function Er that decreases under the same conditions, and specialize the ODE to the simplex to obtain an accelerated variant of replicator dynamics. Building on the insight that the Lyapunov decrease can be strengthened by choosing a(t)=w(t)/W(t) to make the cross term negative, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that selects a(t) online to decrease Lr whenever possible. A discrete scheme (Algorithm 1) is presented and compared empirically to adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient and speed restarts).

Relation to prior work: The paper fits within the line of work connecting Nesterov’s acceleration to ODEs (Su et al., 2014; Wibisono et al., 2016) and extends the authors’ earlier AMD analysis (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015) by allowing a general weighting function and designing a Lyapunov-based adaptive scheme. The energy function and primal formulations echo known constructions in mirror descent and variational accelerations. Empirical baselines follow O’Donoghue & Candès’ restarts.

Quality:
- Theory: The Lyapunov analysis in continuous time is sound and recovers known rates under mild conditions (η ≥ r′ and a trajectory-dependent orthogonality/negativity condition). The corollary providing a constructive recipe for w via a(t) ≥ r′/r and η = a r is clean but essentially reproduces standard polynomial weights. The proposed adaptive rule that decreases d/dt Lr is well-motivated in continuous time; however, the discrete-time algorithm is largely heuristic. The paper states preservation of quadratic rates, but the main text does not provide a complete, rigorous convergence proof for the adaptive averaging discretization, and the conditions ensuring that the discrete surrogate Lyapunov decreases are not clearly spelled out. This makes the main algorithmic contribution effectively heuristic at submission time.
- Analysis extras: The energy function and primal representation are technically correct and help interpret the dynamics. The replicator specialization is interesting, though expected from mirror descent on the simplex; the “accelerated replicator” obtained by averaging the mirrored dual path is a modest twist rather than a fundamentally new dynamic.
- Experiments: The empirical validation is limited (low-dimensional, deterministic, simple losses). It does not convincingly demonstrate advantages on practically relevant ML problems or robust gains in strongly convex regimes.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written, with a clear statement of the Lyapunov conditions and illustrative examples. However, it could better delineate what is new relative to [Krichene et al. 2015] and to the variational framework of Wibisono et al. The discrete algorithm’s assumptions, parameter choices (β, step size), and practical guidance need to be made explicit. The dependence of condition (2) on the unknown trajectory should be discussed in terms of verifiable sufficient conditions beyond the corollary.

Originality:
- Generalizing AMD to arbitrary w(t) and η(t) under a Lyapunov template feels incremental; the key sufficient conditions essentially recover known schedules. The adaptive averaging idea—choosing a(t) to reduce the Lyapunov derivative—is a nice conceptual insight, but its discrete instantiation is not fully novel relative to the broader literature on adaptive restarts/adaptivity unless backed by guarantees. The accelerated replicator is a neat example but not of broad conceptual novelty.

Significance:
- If accompanied by a rigorous discrete-time analysis and stronger experiments, adaptive averaging could be a useful alternative to restarting for first-order accelerated methods. As it stands, the contribution is mainly conceptual and explanatory, with limited evidence of practical impact.

Empirical evaluation:
- Benchmarks are toy (R^3 simplex with quadratic/linear/KL objectives). There is no evaluation on high-dimensional, structured losses (e.g., logistic regression, sparse regularization), nor on strongly convex settings with condition-number variation, nor on stochastic gradients. Comparisons exclude other forms of averaging schedules and modern adaptive schemes tuned for acceleration.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clean, unified Lyapunov treatment of generalized averaging in AMD; easy-to-check constructive recipes.
- Intuitive adaptive averaging idea grounded in decreasing the Lyapunov/energy.
- Clear primal formulation and an informative energy interpretation.
- Accelerated replicator example connects optimization and evolutionary dynamics.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incremental theory; main novelty (adaptive averaging) lacks a rigorous discrete-time convergence proof in the main text.
- The generalized averaging extension is straightforward and yields limited new insight beyond prior AMD/variational ODE work.
- Experiments are small-scale and do not establish practical superiority; missing comparisons to alternative averaging schemes.
- Focused largely on the quadratic-rate case; faster rates require higher-order schemes not central to the contribution.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a complete discrete-time convergence analysis of adaptive averaging with explicit conditions on step sizes, β, and mirror maps; state theorems in the main paper.
- Compare against additional averaging schedules (e.g., fixed polynomial, exponential, line-search-based) and adaptive schemes (beyond restarts), including sensitivity studies.
- Add high-dimensional experiments on convex ML benchmarks (logistic regression, Lasso/simplex-constrained problems), both weakly and strongly convex, and include stochastic/noisy gradients.
- Clarify what is novel vs. [Krichene et al. 2015] and [Wibisono et al. 2016], and discuss limitations when condition (2) cannot be enforced.
- Explore extension to higher-order accelerated methods as promised, with empirical and theoretical support.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The paper offers a neat perspective and a promising heuristic, but the theoretical and empirical evidence for the discrete algorithm is not yet strong enough for NeurIPS. Strengthening the discrete-time guarantees and expanding experiments could make this a solid contribution.
