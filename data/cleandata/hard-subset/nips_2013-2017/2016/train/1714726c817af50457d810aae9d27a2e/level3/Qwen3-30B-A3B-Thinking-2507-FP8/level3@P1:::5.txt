

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending recent work on continuous-time interpretations of Nesterov's accelerated methods. The authors introduce a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, providing sufficient conditions on these functions to achieve desired convergence rates using a Lyapunov argument. The paper builds upon Su et al.'s connection between Nesterov's method and continuous-time ODEs, and Krichene et al.'s accelerated mirror descent dynamics, while introducing a more flexible framework.

The key theoretical contribution is a unified Lyapunov analysis that establishes conditions under which the solution trajectories exhibit quadratic convergence rates. The authors prove that for specific choices of w and η (satisfying certain differential conditions), the Lyapunov function Lr is non-increasing, guaranteeing f(X(t)) - f* ≤ C/r(t). They demonstrate how this framework generalizes previous results, including the replicator dynamics on the simplex, which they accelerate through their averaging scheme.

The main practical contribution is the adaptive averaging heuristic, which adaptively computes the weights w(t) to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function while preserving the convergence rate. This differs from existing restart heuristics (like gradient or speed restarting) that lack theoretical guarantees for general convex functions. The authors provide a discrete-time implementation (Algorithm 1) and prove convergence rate bounds for the adaptive method.

The experimental section compares adaptive averaging against standard accelerated mirror descent, gradient restarting, and speed restarting on simplex-constrained problems with various objective functions. Results show adaptive averaging performs at least as well as restart methods, with significant improvements in strongly convex cases. The experiments confirm that the Lyapunov function decreases under adaptive averaging (unlike restart methods), while the energy function Er remains non-increasing across all methods.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical contribution is solid, with clear Lyapunov analysis establishing sufficient conditions for convergence rates.
2. The adaptive averaging heuristic is theoretically grounded and provides a principled alternative to ad-hoc restart methods.
3. The connection to replicator dynamics on the simplex is a nice application with potential in evolutionary game theory.
4. Experiments are well-designed and effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't fully address how the adaptive averaging heuristic scales to large-scale problems, which is a common concern for optimization methods.
2. The connection to existing adaptive first-order methods (like Adagrad or Adam) could be more thoroughly discussed, as these also adapt weights but aren't designed for acceleration.
3. While the numerical results are promising, the paper doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational cost between adaptive averaging and restart methods.
4. The theoretical analysis focuses on continuous time, and while the discrete-time version is provided, the connection between continuous and discrete convergence rates could be strengthened.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The Lyapunov argument is well-constructed and the sufficient conditions are clearly established. The experimental results support the theoretical claims.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good mathematical exposition. The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is made explicit.

Originality: The paper makes a clear contribution by generalizing accelerated mirror descent with a flexible averaging scheme. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and distinct from existing restart approaches. The connection to replicator dynamics provides a concrete application that demonstrates the method's utility.

Significance: The work advances the theoretical understanding of accelerated methods by providing a unified framework for accelerated dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic has practical potential for improving convergence in optimization, particularly for constrained problems. While the problem is well-studied, the proposed approach offers a theoretically grounded alternative to existing heuristics.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a unified theoretical framework for accelerated descent dynamics
- Introduces a novel adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantees
- Demonstrates practical benefits through thorough experiments
- Connects to important applications (replicator dynamics in game theory)
- Builds upon and extends recent NIPS work (Krichene et al. 2016)

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited discussion of computational cost compared to existing methods
- Some connection to related adaptive first-order methods could be strengthened
- The paper doesn't address potential limitations in high-dimensional settings
- The improvement over existing methods, while significant in some cases, might be marginal in others

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the theory and practice of accelerated optimization methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a principled approach to improving convergence rates while preserving theoretical guarantees, and the experimental results support its effectiveness. While the paper could benefit from deeper discussion of computational considerations and connections to related work, it represents a solid advance in the field. The work is clearly written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in optimization with potential practical applications. I recommend acceptance.
