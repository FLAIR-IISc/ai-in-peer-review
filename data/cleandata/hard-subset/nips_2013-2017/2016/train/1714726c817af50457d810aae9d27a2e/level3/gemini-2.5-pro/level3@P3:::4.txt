This paper studies a general class of continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order optimization on constrained convex sets. The dynamics, termed Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD), are formulated as a coupled system of ODEs for a dual variable `Z(t)` and a primal variable `X(t)`. The key generalization is that the dynamics are parameterized by two functions: `η(t)`, which controls the rate of gradient accumulation in the dual, and `w(t)`, which defines the weights for averaging the mirrored dual trajectory to produce the primal variable. This work directly extends the continuous-time analysis of Nesterov's methods presented by Su et al. (2014) and Krichene et al. (2015), which can be seen as specific instances of this more general framework.

The main theoretical contribution is a Lyapunov-based analysis that provides simple and sufficient conditions on `w(t)` and `η(t)` to guarantee a desired convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))`. Building on this analysis, the authors propose a novel and intuitive heuristic called "adaptive averaging." The core idea is to adaptively adjust the averaging weights `w(t)` to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function's value along the solution trajectory. This provides an alternative to popular restarting heuristics (O'Donoghue & Candès, 2013), with the key advantage that it is guaranteed by construction to preserve the method's worst-case `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate. The paper provides a discrete-time version of the algorithm and presents numerical experiments comparing it favorably against standard accelerated descent and restarting schemes on several constrained optimization problems.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the derivation of sufficient conditions for convergence via the Lyapunov function (Theorem 2) is elegant and insightful. The claims are well-supported by both the continuous-time theory and the numerical experiments. The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method against relevant baselines across a variety of objective functions (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear), and the visualizations effectively illustrate the different qualitative behaviors of the algorithms.

**Originality and Significance:** The paper makes several original and significant contributions.
1.  The generalized ODE framework (AMDw,η) provides a unified perspective on a broad family of accelerated dynamics, clarifying the distinct roles of gradient accumulation (`η`) and trajectory averaging (`w`).
2.  The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel and principled approach to speeding up accelerated methods. Unlike many heuristics that are based on geometric or speed-based intuition, this method is derived directly from the theoretical tool used to prove convergence (the Lyapunov function). This is a very clean and appealing idea.
3.  The central insight—that adaptively re-weighting the trajectory's history can provide empirical acceleration similar to restarting momentum—is a significant conceptual contribution. It offers a new lens through which to view and design accelerated algorithms.
4.  The guarantee that adaptive averaging preserves the worst-case convergence rate is a notable advantage over restarting schemes, whose guarantees are often limited to specific problem classes (e.g., quadratic or strongly convex). This makes the proposed heuristic more reliable.
5.  The application to derive an "accelerated replicator dynamics" is a nice, concrete example that demonstrates the framework's utility and potential impact in other fields like game theory.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-established in the introduction, relating the work to key developments in the field. The progression from the general continuous-time model to the specific adaptive heuristic and its discretization is logical and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the primal representation in Section 3 helps build intuition for the dynamics.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

The paper is very strong, and the weaknesses are minor.
1.  The transition from the continuous-time adaptive heuristic in equation (8) to the discrete-time implementation in Algorithm 1 could be explained in slightly more detail. The paper states the correspondence is `t = k√s`, but the specific logic for the update rule `ak = min(ak-1, ...)` and the condition `f(x̃(k+1)) - f(x̃(k)) > 0` feels like a particular design choice. A brief justification for this specific discretization would strengthen the link between theory and practice.
2.  While the experiments convincingly show that adaptive averaging is effective, the theoretical analysis only guarantees that it *preserves* the `O(1/t^2)` rate. An interesting direction for future work, which could be mentioned, is whether one can prove *faster* rates for the adaptive scheme under additional assumptions (e.g., for strongly convex functions), which is where restarting is known to excel.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant generalization of continuous-time models for accelerated optimization.
*   Introduces a principled and original adaptive heuristic (adaptive averaging) derived directly from the theoretical analysis.
*   The proposed heuristic has stronger theoretical guarantees (preservation of the convergence rate) than some competing methods.
*   Empirical results are strong and demonstrate that the method is competitive with or superior to established restarting heuristics.
*   The work is well-written, technically sound, and provides significant new insights into the nature of acceleration.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper could provide a slightly more detailed justification for the specific discretization choices made in Algorithm 1.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that I enjoyed reading. It offers a novel theoretical framework, a clever and practical algorithm, and compelling experimental validation. The new method is very interesting and provides a valuable new perspective on how to speed up accelerated optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
