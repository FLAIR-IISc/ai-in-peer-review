

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, framing it as a coupling of dual and primal variables with generalized averaging. The authors present a unified continuous-time framework (AMDw,η) parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), and provide sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate using a Lyapunov argument. They then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes weights to accelerate convergence while preserving the quadratic convergence rate, and validate it experimentally against existing restarting heuristics.

The work builds on recent NIPS contributions by Su et al. (NIPS 2014) who connected Nesterov's accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs, and by the authors themselves (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015) who generalized this to constrained problems. The paper also relates to the replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory (Weibull, 1995), providing an accelerated version of this important ODE.

**Quality**: The theoretical foundation is sound, with rigorous proofs establishing sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to decrease. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and preserves the convergence guarantees of the non-adaptive method. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach (e.g., performance guarantees for non-convex problems, which are increasingly important in machine learning).

**Clarity**: The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. Key equations (particularly those around lines 33-34 and 46-47) lack intuitive explanations, making it difficult to follow the authors' reasoning. The connection between the continuous-time dynamics and the discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is not sufficiently explained, and the paper would benefit from more background on mirror descent and accelerated methods for readers unfamiliar with the field. Figure 1's reference to the "solid line" is ambiguous and should be clarified with distinct line styles or colors.

**Originality**: The paper makes a novel contribution by generalizing the averaging scheme in accelerated mirror descent and proposing a principled adaptive heuristic. The connection between adaptive averaging and preserving convergence rates is not trivial, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work on restarting heuristics. However, the paper does not adequately discuss the relationship to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad, Adam), which would strengthen the context and highlight the novelty more clearly.

**Significance**: While the theoretical contribution is significant for the continuous-time analysis of accelerated methods, the practical significance is questionable. The experiments are conducted in R^3 (simplex in 3D), which is a toy setup that doesn't demonstrate scalability to real-world machine learning problems. The paper lacks evidence of performance on higher-dimensional problems or more complex objective functions, making it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed heuristic for actual applications.

**Strengths**:
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with clear sufficient conditions for convergence
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence guarantees
- Comprehensive comparison with existing restarting heuristics
- Clear connection to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory

**Weaknesses**:
- Poorly explained key equations that hinder understanding
- Limited experimental validation in low-dimensional settings
- Insufficient discussion of relationship to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization
- Ambiguous figure presentation
- Lack of discussion regarding practical implementation challenges

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- The theoretical framework provides a unified perspective on accelerated methods
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and preserves convergence guarantees
- The paper makes a clear contribution to the understanding of acceleration in continuous-time dynamics

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- The paper's lack of clarity and insufficient experimental evidence limit its practical significance
- The limited scope of experiments (R^3) fails to demonstrate scalability to real-world problems
- The connection to broader adaptive optimization methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) is underdeveloped

Overall, this paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of accelerated descent dynamics. However, the lack of clarity in key sections, the limited experimental validation, and the absence of discussion regarding practical implementation challenges significantly weaken its impact. To improve the paper, the authors should provide more intuitive explanations for the key equations, expand the experimental validation to higher dimensions, clarify the relationship to other adaptive methods, and improve the figure presentation.

I recommend a borderline decision. The theoretical contribution is strong, but the paper's current form lacks sufficient evidence of practical significance to justify acceptance at NIPS. With substantial revisions addressing the clarity issues and expanding the experimental validation, this could become a strong contribution to the field.
