Summary
This paper studies accelerated mirror-descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through the lens of generalized averaging. The authors consider a dual trajectory Z(t) that accumulates gradients at time-varying rate η(t), and a primal trajectory X(t) formed as a weighted average of the mirrored dual path with weights w(t). A Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) combining suboptimality and a Bregman divergence is used to give sufficient conditions on η and w that guarantee a target convergence rate 1/r(t). This unifies and extends previous continuous-time acceleration analyses (e.g., Nesterov-inspired ODEs [20], accelerated mirror descent [11], and the variational framework [23]). The paper also provides a primal-only view, gives examples for the positive orthant and simplex (leading to an “accelerated replicator” dynamics), and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses weights to decrease the time derivative of Lr. The authors provide continuous-time guarantees, a discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) that preserves the O(1/k^2) rate, and experiments comparing adaptive averaging against adaptive restarting heuristics [17, 20], showing favorable performance, especially on strongly convex examples.

Quality
- Theory: The continuous-time analysis is sound and clearly extends existing Lyapunov approaches. The sufficient conditions in Theorem 2 are transparent; the constructive Corollary 1 and examples (r(t)=tp) make the design of w,η tangible. The additional energy function Er is a nice complement. However, the key guarantee for the discrete-time method is deferred to the supplement; a concise theorem and proof sketch in the main text would strengthen the contribution. Also, condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on the trajectory; the proposed adaptive rule sets a(t) to enforce nonpositivity, but a brief discussion of existence/measurability and robustness to discretization noise would help.
- Algorithmics: Algorithm 1 is plausible and easy to implement for mirror maps where the prox/duality operations are standard. Practical guidance on selecting β, γ, and stepsize s is limited; sensitivity studies would be useful.
- Experiments: The R^3 experiments are illustrative and allow trajectory visualization, but they are insufficient to establish practical significance. Scalability to higher dimensions and standard ML tasks (e.g., logistic regression with l2 or l1 constraints, sparse softmax) is not demonstrated.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well written but can be hard to follow due to dense notation and many moving parts (w, W, η, r, a, X, Z, X̌, Ž). Suggestions:
- Provide more background early: a compact primer on mirror maps and Bregman divergence; a diagram/table listing symbols; and a brief roadmap of how continuous-time results translate to Algorithm 1.
- Clarify abbreviations (AMD, AMD′, AMDp) on first use and keep a consistent notation for the “checked” variables X̌,Ž.
- The step converting the averaging relation into AMD′ (Ẋ(t)W(t)+X(t)w(t)=w(t)∇ψ∗(Z(t))) and the inequality leading to Eq. (3) would benefit from more intuition: e.g., “X is a moving average of mirrored dual points; differentiating produces an alignment term Ẋ scaled by the cumulative weight W, which couples with the gradient-accumulation in Z to form a descent inequality. The convexity step replacing ⟨∇f(X),X−x?⟩ with f(X)−f? is the crux that yields the Lyapunov decrease.”
- Figure 1: the “solid line” reference is ambiguous; use dotted vs solid or distinct colors to distinguish primal/dual/mirrored trajectories.

Originality
- The generalized averaging view (parametrized by w,η) and the explicit Lyapunov-based design rule for these weights appear novel, while subsuming prior ODE-based accelerations [11, 20, 23].
- The identification and acceleration of replicator dynamics under constraints are interesting and likely of independent appeal.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is a simple but original idea tailored to accelerated methods, distinct from adaptive step-size methods like Adagrad/Adam [7,10].

Significance
- Conceptually, the work deepens our understanding of acceleration-as-averaging and offers a principled way to tune weights to maintain a target rate while potentially improving practical convergence.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is promising and competitive with popular restarting rules; preserving the O(1/k^2) rate while adapting is valuable.
- To maximize impact, larger-scale experiments and comparisons on standard convex tasks are needed, as well as clearer guidance for implementation.

Specific comments and questions
- Relation to adaptive methods (Adagrad, Adam) and non-convex optimization: The paper contrasts the goals (adaptive step sizes vs adaptive averaging for acceleration). Please expand on how adaptive averaging might behave in non-convex settings: could Er remain decreasing, and would the heuristic avoid spurious oscillations? Clarify whether your conditions extend when f is non-convex (likely not for Lr) and whether Er provides any practical safeguard.
- Discrete-time theory: Move a concise theorem (assumptions, statement, proof sketch) from the supplement to the main text establishing that Algorithm 1 preserves O(1/k^2). State explicit conditions on s, β, γ, strong convexity of R vs ψ, and Lipschitz constants required.
- Implementability/overheads: Discuss the computational cost per iteration relative to FISTA/accelerated prox, especially when ψ and R differ. Is one of the steps redundant under common choices (e.g., R=ψ)? How sensitive is performance to β and the rule in lines 6–8 (e.g., smoothing a_k updates)?
- Initialization and numerical stability: Since W(0)=0 motivates t0>0, discuss how this maps to discrete-time initialization and whether near-initial transients cause instabilities for large a or β.
- Scope of constraints: Beyond simplex and orthant, can you cover l2/l∞ balls or general proximable sets? A brief example (e.g., Euclidean ball with ψ(x)=½||x||2) would help.
- Experiments: Please add higher-dimensional experiments (e.g., d≥1000), including:
  - Strongly convex logistic regression with l2 constraint.
  - Simplex-constrained softmax regression or KL objectives.
  - Compare against gradient and speed restarts, FISTA with adaptive restart, and a fixed accelerated baseline. Report wall-clock and iteration counts.
- Wibisono et al. [23] connection: You note AMDp,w generalizes their family; make the mapping explicit in a short proposition, including the identification a(t)=eα(t), r(t)=eβ(t) and η=a r, and clarify when your conditions imply their rates and vice versa.
- Minor: Ensure consistent terminology (“quadratic convergence rate” here means O(1/k^2), not Q-quadratic). Complete bibliographic information in references.

Pros
- Clear, unifying Lyapunov framework for generalized averaging in accelerated mirror descent.
- Simple adaptive averaging heuristic with continuous-time guarantees and discrete-time rate preservation.
- Novel acceleration of replicator dynamics; insightful primal/dual perspectives.
- Empirically competitive with adaptive restarting; promising qualitative behavior.

Cons
- Main experiments are toy (R^3); limited evidence of scalability.
- Some crucial discrete-time guarantees are relegated to the supplement.
- Notation density and a few key derivations need more intuition.
- Limited guidance on hyperparameters and computational overheads.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution with a promising heuristic. With clearer exposition and stronger empirical validation at scale, it would be a strong fit for NIPS.
