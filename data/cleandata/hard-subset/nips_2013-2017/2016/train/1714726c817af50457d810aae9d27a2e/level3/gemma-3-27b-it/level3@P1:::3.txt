This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, building upon the authors’ previous work establishing a connection between Nesterov’s accelerated methods and a family of continuous-time ODEs. The central contribution lies in a generalized framework for analyzing accelerated mirror descent dynamics, parameterized by averaging and gradient accumulation weights. The authors provide sufficient conditions, formalized in Theorem 2, under which specific choices of these weights guarantee a desired convergence rate. Notably, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic with provable convergence guarantees – preserving the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods in the discrete-time setting – and demonstrate its empirical performance favorably compared to existing heuristics like adaptive restarting.

The work is well-motivated, situating itself within the broader resurgence of interest in first-order methods driven by the scale of modern machine learning datasets. The connection to continuous-time dynamics is clearly articulated, emphasizing its value for both analysis and algorithm design. The authors thoroughly relate their work to previous investigations, referencing key papers on accelerated methods [14, 15, 20, 11] and continuous-time optimization [6, 9, 5, 2, 1, 3, 23].  The derivation of the accelerated replicator dynamics as a special case, when the feasible set is the probability simplex, is a particularly insightful application and highlights the versatility of the proposed framework.  The careful discussion of existence and uniqueness of solutions (Theorem 1) and the exploration of energy functions (Theorem 3) demonstrate a rigorous approach to the continuous-time analysis.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The technical presentation is clear and well-organized. The Lyapunov argument used to establish convergence rates is sound and thoroughly explained. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution, providing a principled way to dynamically adjust averaging weights while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The empirical evaluation, while limited in scope, provides encouraging results, suggesting that the adaptive method performs at least as well as, and often better than, existing heuristics.  The inclusion of the primal representation of the dynamics (Section 3) offers valuable intuition and facilitates analysis.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the authors mention the importance of discretization, the analysis of convergence rates after discretization is not pursued in depth. The crucial question of whether the improved continuous-time rates translate to the discrete setting – a key advantage of continuous-time analysis – remains open. While the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of adaptive averaging, they primarily focus on the simplex-constrained case. Expanding the empirical evaluation to a broader range of problems and constraints would strengthen the claims. Furthermore, the related work section, while comprehensive, omits several relevant contributions. Specifically, the recent work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and co-authors [25, 26] on fast convergence of inertial dynamics, and its connections to vanishing viscosity methods, deserves mention, as it directly relates to the analysis of accelerated methods. The theoretical analysis of the discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is delegated to the supplementary material, which while acceptable, may obscure its significance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The generalized framework for analyzing accelerated mirror descent with adaptive averaging is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The Lyapunov argument and convergence guarantees are rigorously established.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a promising approach to improving the performance of accelerated methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Discretization Analysis:** The lack of a thorough analysis of convergence rates after discretization is a significant omission.
*   **Restricted Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are limited in scope and could benefit from broader coverage.
*   **Minor Omission in Related Work:** The exclusion of recent relevant work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and colleagues.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed method is well-motivated and practical, and the empirical results are encouraging. While the lack of extensive discretization analysis and the limited scope of the experiments are minor drawbacks, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors address the discretization analysis in future work and include the omitted related work in the final version.
