This paper studies accelerated mirror-descent dynamics for constrained smooth convex optimization and generalizes prior continuous-time formulations by introducing two time-varying weights: η(t) controlling dual gradient accumulation and w(t) controlling averaging in the primal. Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*)+Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors derive sufficient conditions under which trajectories achieve the target rate 1/r(t); standard O(1/t^2) rates arise as special cases. They also provide a primal-only representation, illustrating an “accelerated replicator” for simplex constraints. Building on this, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses the averaging schedule to monotonically decrease the Lyapunov (and a related “energy”) function, and present a discrete-time algorithm that preserves the accelerated O(1/k^2) rate. Empirically, on simplex-constrained problems, adaptive averaging matches or outperforms adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient and speed restarts).

Relation to prior work: The analysis connects to Su–Boyd–Candès’ ODE for Nesterov acceleration, Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett’s accelerated mirror descent, and Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan’s variational/Bregman Lagrangian view. The empirical baseline of adaptive restart (O’Donoghue–Candès) is appropriate. The paper’s novelty is to frame acceleration via generalized averaging weights and to adapt these weights directly by decreasing the Lyapunov derivative, rather than restarting.

Strengths:
- Clear continuous-time Lyapunov argument that unifies several accelerated dynamics and yields simple, checkable sufficient conditions.
- Conceptually neat adaptive averaging heuristic with guarantees that preserve the accelerated rate and a clean discrete-time instantiation.
- Illustrative connection to accelerated replicator dynamics under constraints.
- Empirical comparisons show consistent gains over popular restarts.

Weaknesses:
- Condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on the trajectory; while Corollary 1 provides a sufficient construction, more discussion on when the adaptive rule reliably satisfies the conditions in practice would help.
- Experiments are small-scale (mostly low-dimensional simplex); broader benchmarks (higher-d, different constraint sets, composite/prox settings) would strengthen significance.
- Clarify terminology: “quadratic convergence rate” might be read as superlinear; here it means O(1/t^2) or O(1/k^2).
- Limited discussion of robustness (stochastic gradients, noise) and sensitivity to hyperparameters (β, step sizes, choice of ψ and R).

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and sound within the smooth convex, deterministic setting; discrete-time guarantees are plausible but could be expanded. Clarity: Generally well organized; some notation could be streamlined and algorithmic details (tuning, complexity) expanded. Originality: The adaptive averaging mechanism and its Lyapunov-based design appear novel relative to restart-based heuristics. Significance: Promising direction for constrained accelerated methods; impact will depend on broader empirical validation and extensions.

Arguments for acceptance:
- New, principled adaptive mechanism with guarantees.
- Unifying, elegant analysis and practical gains over strong baselines.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited experimental scope and missing robustness studies.
- Some conditions are trajectory-dependent and may be hard to verify in practice.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
