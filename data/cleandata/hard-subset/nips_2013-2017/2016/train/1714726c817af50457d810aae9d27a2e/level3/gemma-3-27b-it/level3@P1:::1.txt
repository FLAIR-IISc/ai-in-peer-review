This paper investigates adaptive averaging schemes within the framework of accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization. Building on the connection between Nesterov’s accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs (as established in prior work like Su et al., NIPS 2016, and Krichene et al., NIPS 2016), the authors propose a novel heuristic for adaptively computing weights in a weighted average of the dual trajectory, aiming to speed up convergence. The core idea is to dynamically adjust weights based on the decrease of a Lyapunov function, guaranteeing preservation of the quadratic convergence rate. This is a significant contribution, as existing heuristics like adaptive restarting (O’Donoghue & Candès, 2015) often lack such theoretical guarantees.

The paper begins with a thorough introduction to the problem and relevant background. It clearly establishes the motivation for studying continuous-time dynamics and their relationship to discrete optimization algorithms. The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the authors provide a Lyapunov argument demonstrating sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate. A key strength lies in the unification of different accelerated mirror descent dynamics under a single framework parameterized by weight functions *w* and *η*. The derivation of accelerated replicator dynamics as a specific example highlights the practical applicability of their approach to areas like evolutionary game theory (Weibull, 1995). 

The adaptive averaging heuristic presented is a natural extension of the theoretical framework. By adaptively adjusting weights based on the Lyapunov function's derivative, the authors provide a principled way to accelerate convergence without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. Their proposed discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-defined and lends itself to implementation. The numerical experiments, while limited in scope, offer encouraging evidence of performance gains compared to existing heuristics, particularly in the strongly convex case.  The inclusion of visualizations of trajectories and energy function behavior adds to the clarity of the results.

However, several weaknesses warrant consideration. While the paper builds on prior work by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016), it could benefit from a more extensive discussion of the related literature on adaptive learning rates, such as Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011) and Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014). Though the authors acknowledge these methods, a more detailed comparison would solidify the novelty of their approach.  The analysis of the discretized process, as suggested in the key points, is crucial for establishing the practical convergence rate and is only briefly mentioned. A formal proof, perhaps in the supplementary material, would significantly strengthen the contribution. The experiments primarily focus on the simplex constraint; expanding the evaluation to other convex sets and objective function classes would increase confidence in the generalizability of the results.  Furthermore, the connection to Conjugate Gradient methods, also mentioned in the key points, deserves more elaboration. 

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined mathematical framework and a clear Lyapunov argument. The claims are supported by the theoretical analysis and empirical results, though further analysis of the discrete process would enhance the rigor. It is a complete piece of work, providing a novel heuristic with convergence guarantees.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are reasonably clear. However, some sections, particularly those delving into the primal representation, could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
*   **Originality:** The adaptive averaging heuristic and its theoretical justification represent a novel contribution. While building upon existing work on accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics, the authors present a unique approach with demonstrably better performance in certain scenarios. The work is adequately referenced, and the authors demonstrate awareness of related research.
*   **Significance:** The results are important because they address a fundamental problem in optimization: accelerating convergence. The proposed heuristic is likely to be useful for practitioners and researchers working with Nesterov’s accelerated methods. The preservation of the convergence rate is a particularly valuable contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   Well-motivated and rigorously analyzed.
*   Empirically demonstrates improvements over existing methods.
*   Builds on a strong foundation of prior work in continuous-time optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of numerical experiments.
*   Missing analysis of the discretized process and its convergence rate.
*   Could benefit from a more detailed comparison to existing adaptive learning rate methods (Adagrad, Adam).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a promising contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. While some areas could be strengthened through further analysis and experimentation, the originality, quality, and significance of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS 2016.
