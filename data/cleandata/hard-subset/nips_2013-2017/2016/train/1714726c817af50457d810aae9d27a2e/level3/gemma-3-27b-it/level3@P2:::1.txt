This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, building on the connection between Nesterov’s accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs established in prior work, particularly Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016, referenced as [11] in the paper). The authors present a generalized framework for analyzing these dynamics, parameterized by weight functions for averaging and gradient accumulation, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to improve convergence. The core idea is to dynamically adjust these weights based on the progress of the optimization process, aiming to maintain or even accelerate the quadratic convergence rate characteristic of accelerated first-order methods.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining the resurgence of interest in first-order methods and the benefits of analyzing optimization algorithms through a continuous-time lens. The connection to Nesterov’s acceleration and the prior work of Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11] is clearly articulated, positioning this work as a logical extension of established research. The theoretical analysis, centering around a Lyapunov argument, is sound and provides sufficient conditions for convergence. The presentation of the primal representation and the example dynamics for both positive-orthant constrained and simplex-constrained problems are insightful and demonstrate the versatility of the proposed framework. Specifically, the derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamic is a compelling illustration.

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical rigor, clear exposition, and potential for practical impact. The adaptive averaging heuristic is intuitively appealing, and the theoretical guarantee of preserving the convergence rate is a significant advantage over existing heuristics like adaptive restarting [17, 20]. The experiments, while limited in scope, provide encouraging evidence of the heuristic’s effectiveness, showing at least comparable performance and, in some cases, significant improvements over existing methods. The authors have clearly identified the key contributions and thoughtfully addressed potential limitations.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the experiments demonstrate promising results, they are relatively small-scale and focus primarily on the simplex-constrained case. A more extensive empirical evaluation, including a wider range of problem classes and dataset sizes, would strengthen the claims of improved performance. The discussion of related work, although adequate, could be expanded to include more recent developments in adaptive optimization methods, such as those leveraging second-order information or momentum techniques. Further, the comparison to Adagrad [7] and Adam [10] feels somewhat cursory – a more in-depth discussion of how adaptive averaging differs from and potentially complements these methods would be valuable.

As noted in the key points, a theoretical analysis of the *discretized* process, explicitly demonstrating the preservation of the convergence rate in discrete time, would be a strong addition. While the authors hint at this in the supplemental material, a more detailed treatment in the main body would be beneficial. Finally, exploring the potential connection between adaptive averaging and Conjugate Gradient methods for strongly convex quadratics, as suggested, could provide further theoretical insights and potentially lead to new algorithmic developments.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of accelerated mirror descent dynamics and a well-justified adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic represents a novel approach to improving the convergence of accelerated first-order methods.
*   **Potential Impact:**  The technique has the potential to significantly improve the performance of widely used optimization algorithms like Nesterov’s method.
*   **Clear Exposition:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with the intricacies of continuous-time optimization dynamics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are somewhat limited in scope and do not cover a sufficiently broad range of problem classes.
*   **Missing Discrete Analysis:** A detailed analysis of the discretized process and convergence rate preservation is absent from the main text.
*   **Limited Comparison to Existing Adaptive Methods:** The comparison to established adaptive optimization methods (e.g., Adam) could be more thorough.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical framework is solid, the proposed heuristic is insightful, and the potential for practical impact is significant. While the empirical evaluation could be more extensive and the analysis of the discretized process more detailed, these weaknesses do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the aforementioned points. Specifically, I would encourage the authors to expand the experimental section and provide a more detailed analysis of the discretized algorithm.
