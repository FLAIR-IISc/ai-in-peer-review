Summary
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) through the lens of generalized averaging. It introduces a two-parameter family of ODEs AMDw,η that couple a dual variable accumulating gradients at rate η(t) with a primal variable that is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory using weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors give sufficient conditions on w and η that ensure Lr is decreasing, yielding convergence rate O(1/r(t)). This unifies and extends earlier analyses linking acceleration and averaging (e.g., Su et al. (NIPS’14), Wibisono et al. (2016), and Krichene et al. (NIPS’15)). The paper also provides a primal reformulation, interprets an additional “energy” function Er that decreases under similar conditions, and instantiates the framework for the positive orthant and the simplex, yielding an “accelerated replicator” dynamics.

Building on this, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses a(t) = w(t)/W(t) to further decrease the time derivative of the Lyapunov function whenever possible. A discrete-time version (Algorithm 1) is given for the classical quadratic-rate case r(t)=t^2, and empirical comparisons are provided against adaptive restart heuristics (gradient and speed restarts) on small-scale simplex-constrained problems. The experiments suggest adaptive averaging is competitive with, and sometimes better than, restarting.

Strengths
- Clear and unifying Lyapunov-based analysis that isolates the role of the averaging weights, with simple sufficient conditions (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1).
- Conceptually appealing link between acceleration and averaging; continuous-time perspective is insightful and well aligned with prior NIPS work.
- Primal reformulation and examples (orthant, simplex/replicator) help intuition and may be of independent interest.
- Adaptive averaging idea is simple, intuitive, and practically relevant; empirical results indicate it can outperform restart heuristics in some cases.
- The energy interpretation complements the Lyapunov analysis and may be useful beyond this paper.

Weaknesses
- The theoretical contribution beyond prior ODE-based analyses appears modest. Much of the machinery (Lyapunov potential, rate selection via r(t), connection to Wibisono et al.’s variational view) is very close to earlier work, and the “generalized averaging” extension largely rephrases existing accelerated ODEs with different weight schedules.
- The second Lyapunov condition depends on the trajectory, and the proposed way to exploit it (adaptive averaging) is only partially justified. The discrete-time method is presented as a heuristic; the guarantees stated in the abstract are not clearly and rigorously established in the main text. It remains unclear under which assumptions the quadratic rate is provably preserved in discrete time.
- Limited scope: the discrete development focuses on the t^2 rate; faster rates are acknowledged to require higher-order methods (e.g., Nesterov’s cubic), but there is no concrete demonstration that adaptive averaging integrates well with such methods.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to low-dimensional, largely toy objectives on the simplex. There are no high-dimensional or truly challenging ML tasks, no noisy or stochastic gradients, and no robust comparisons to other averaging/acceleration variants (e.g., Flammarion & Bach’s averaging view, Catalyst-style acceleration, or alternative averaging schedules).
- Practical advantage is not firmly established without comparisons to other reasonable weight-adaptation rules and stronger baselines (proximal variants, momentum tuning, linesearch-based restarts).

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound in continuous time; the Lyapunov derivation is correct and well presented. However, the discrete-time adaptive averaging remains heuristic in the current form, and the empirical section is too narrow to support broad claims.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The roles of r, w, and η are clearly separated. Derivations are readable, and the examples help. The paper would benefit from making the discrete-time guarantees (if any) explicit in the main text, clarifying assumptions, and tightening notation around the adaptive rule.
- Originality: Moderate. The generalized averaging perspective and the adaptive averaging heuristic are novel angles, but they build closely on prior NIPS work and related continuous-time analyses. The accelerated replicator instantiation is a nice but incremental byproduct.
- Significance: Currently limited. The framework incrementally refines our understanding of accelerated dynamics, but the empirical and theoretical evidence is insufficient to influence practice. Stronger discrete guarantees and broader experiments could raise the impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, unified Lyapunov framework; solid continuous-time insights.
- Simple adaptive averaging idea that could be adopted by practitioners.
- Nice connection to replicator dynamics that may interest game-theory-oriented NIPS readers.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental theory relative to prior NIPS papers; generalized averaging feels largely cosmetic.
- Discrete-time adaptive averaging lacks a clear, rigorous convergence proof in the main text.
- Experimental validation is too limited to establish practical relevance.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Clarify precisely what is proved for Algorithm 1: under what assumptions is the quadratic rate preserved? State the theorem in the main paper, sketch the proof, and move technical details to the supplement.
- Compare against additional baselines: (i) fixed but different averaging schedules, (ii) alternative weight-adaptation rules (e.g., monotone vs nonmonotone), (iii) Catalyst-like acceleration for composite problems, and (iv) heavy-ball with restarts.
- Provide higher-dimensional experiments (n ≥ 1000), composite objectives with proximal steps, and instances with weak/strong convexity and ill-conditioning. Include wall-clock and iteration counts.
- Discuss robustness to noise/inexactness: can adaptive averaging coexist with stochastic gradients or inexact proximal oracles?
- For the accelerated replicator, illustrate benefits on game-theoretic benchmarks or large-scale simplex problems (e.g., entropy-regularized LPs).
- Tighten the discussion on rates: can the Lyapunov design yield sharper constants or improved rates under strong convexity (e.g., linear with appropriate choices of r, w, η) without invoking higher-order methods?

Post-rebuttal note
The promised additions—strong discrete-time convergence proof for adaptive averaging, expanded high-dimensional non-quadratic experiments, comparisons to other averaging schemes, and an implementation within cubic-regularized Newton—would materially strengthen the paper. If the proof is sound and the experiments show consistent gains beyond simple restarts on realistic problems, my recommendation would improve.

Recommendation
Borderline reject (leaning reject) in current form, due to limited novelty beyond prior ODE-based analyses and insufficient discrete-time theory/experiments. The paper could become competitive with stronger discrete guarantees and a broader empirical evaluation.
