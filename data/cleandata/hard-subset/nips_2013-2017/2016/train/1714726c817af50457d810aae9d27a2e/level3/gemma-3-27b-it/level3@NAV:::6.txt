## Review of “Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics”

This paper investigates adaptive averaging heuristics for accelerated descent dynamics in constrained convex optimization. The core idea is to adaptively adjust averaging weights to improve convergence, building on the connection between accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs. The authors demonstrate that their proposed heuristic preserves the original convergence rate for non-strongly convex objectives and present numerical evidence suggesting it performs competitively with existing restarting heuristics.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of prior research in accelerated methods, referencing key papers by Nesterov, Su et al., and others. The connection made between accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics is well-established, and this paper attempts to leverage that connection for improved algorithm design. The proposal of an adaptive averaging scheme is a natural extension of the work on restarting heuristics, aiming to provide theoretical guarantees absent in previous approaches.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The initial sections lack sufficient background information for readers unfamiliar with the nuances of accelerated methods and mirror descent. Several abbreviations and terms (e.g., 'r', 'w', 'η', 'ψ') are introduced without clear definitions or motivations. The equations between lines 33-34 and 46-47, while central to the theoretical development, are presented without sufficient intuitive explanation, making it difficult to grasp the underlying concepts. 

A crucial omission is a discussion of how this adaptive averaging heuristic relates to other adaptive methods commonly used in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad and Adam. While the authors acknowledge these methods, a comparative analysis of their underlying principles and differences would significantly strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the practical significance of the proposed method is questionable, as the experiments are limited to R^3, a rather toy setup. The authors need to provide evidence of scalability to higher dimensions to demonstrate the real-world applicability of their approach.  Finally, Figure 1's use of a solid line to represent the primal trajectory is ambiguous; a dotted line or distinct coloring would greatly enhance its clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretically sound approach to adaptive averaging with convergence guarantees.
*   Potentially valuable contribution to the understanding of accelerated methods.
*   Promising experimental results, although limited in scope.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Poor clarity and lack of background information.
*   Insufficient intuitive explanation of key equations.
*   Missing connection to related work in adaptive optimization.
*   Limited experimental evaluation in low dimensions. 



**Overall:** While the core idea of the paper is interesting and the theoretical results are potentially valuable, the current presentation significantly hinders its understanding and impact. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to revise and address the clarity and scope issues outlined above. A more detailed explanation of the method’s relation to existing adaptive optimization techniques and comprehensive experiments in higher dimensions are essential for acceptance.
