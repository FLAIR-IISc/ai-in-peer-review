## Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated descent dynamics in constrained convex optimization. The core idea is to dynamically adjust averaging weights to speed up convergence while preserving the original quadratic convergence rate for non-strongly convex objectives. The authors establish theoretical guarantees for their approach and demonstrate its performance through numerical experiments, claiming advantages over existing restarting heuristics.

The paper builds upon the connection between accelerated methods (like Nesterov’s) and continuous-time ODEs, a well-established line of research in optimization (e.g., [20, 11]). The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a natural extension of this framework, aiming to improve upon existing strategies like adaptive restarting [20]. The theoretical analysis, leveraging a Lyapunov argument, is a strength, providing a rigorous foundation for the claimed convergence properties. 

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues. The initial sections lack sufficient background material for readers unfamiliar with the continuous-time dynamics perspective of optimization. Several abbreviations and terms (e.g., “AMD,” “ψ,” “r”) are introduced without adequate explanation, hindering comprehension. The equations between lines 33-34 and 46-47, while central to the analysis, are not intuitively explained – a more detailed discussion of their meaning and role would be beneficial. 

Furthermore, the connection to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad and Adam, is not discussed, despite being relevant. While the authors acknowledge these methods, a deeper comparison of their approaches and motivations would strengthen the paper. The practical significance is also questionable. The experiments are limited to R^3, a toy setup, and do not provide convincing evidence of scalability to higher dimensions, which is crucial for real-world applications. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is sound and provides guarantees on convergence.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution within the continuous-time optimization framework.
*   The experimental results, while limited, suggest potential benefits over existing heuristics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is difficult to follow due to a lack of background and unclear explanations.
*   The experiments lack scalability and do not demonstrate practical relevance.
*   The connection to related work in adaptive optimization is superficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but its lack of clarity and limited experimental validation significantly detract from its impact. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should prioritize improving the paper’s readability, providing more context, and conducting experiments in higher dimensions to demonstrate the practical viability of their approach. Addressing the connection to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization would also be valuable. Finally, clarifying Figure 1 with a dotted line or color coding to distinguish the primal trajectory is a minor but necessary improvement.
