This paper studies a broad family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics for constrained convex optimization, parameterized by two functions: η(t), the rate at which a dual variable Z accumulates gradients, and w(t), the weights used to average a mirrored dual trajectory to produce a primal X. The authors develop a Lyapunov framework with an explicit target rate r(t), derive sufficient conditions on (η, w) that make the Lyapunov function Lr decrease, and consequently obtain a convergence guarantee f(X(t)) − f* = O(1/r(t)). They further introduce an “adaptive averaging” heuristic that chooses the effective averaging rate a(t) = w(t)/W(t) along the trajectory to (i) maintain the Lyapunov decrease (hence preserve the target rate) and (ii) make Lr drop faster when possible. The paper also shows a primal-only representation of the dynamics, illustrates special cases (positive orthant, simplex), and derives an accelerated replicator ODE. A discrete-time algorithm with adaptive averaging is proposed (for r(t) = t^2), and experiments on simplex-constrained problems compare against popular adaptive restarting heuristics. Empirically, adaptive averaging is on par with or better than restarting, sometimes substantially so.

Relation to prior work. This work builds on the continuous-time perspective on acceleration (e.g., Su, Boyd, Candès 2014) and on the AMD framework for constrained problems (Krichene, Bayen, Bartlett 2015). It is closely related to the Bregman Lagrangian/variational view (Wibisono, Wilson, Jordan 2016), which characterizes families of ODEs achieving O(1/r(t)) rates. The proposed adaptive averaging is conceptually distinct from adaptive restart methods (O’Donoghue and Candès 2015; Su et al. 2014), in that it modifies the averaging weights (and thereby the coupling between Z and X) rather than resetting the dynamics. The replicator acceleration connects to evolutionary dynamics (Weibull; Sigmund) and the observation that averaging can underlie acceleration (Flammarion and Bach).

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a unifying Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) with transparent sufficient conditions that expose the role of w, η, and r.
- Generality: accommodates general mirror maps on convex sets; covers and extends known AMD dynamics; provides primal and dual formulations and a natural energy function Er that also decreases under the same conditions.
- Novel adaptive averaging idea: a simple rule that locally decreases d/dt Lr whenever possible; theoretically preserves the target rate and is easy to discretize.
- Applications and examples: accelerated replicator dynamics are interesting and potentially impactful in game theory and other domains.
- Empirical evidence: on several simplex-constrained problems, adaptive averaging matches or outperforms adaptive restart, with clear reductions in oscillations and monotone Lyapunov decrease.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Verifiability of the Lyapunov condition: The second condition in Theorem 2 depends on ⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩ along the unknown trajectory. While the paper gives a convenient sufficient choice (Corollary 1) and then adapts a(t) online, it would help to discuss how sensitive the method is to estimation noise of this inner product in discrete time and, if gradients are stochastic, whether the condition can be enforced in expectation.
- Discrete-time guarantees: The main discrete result for adaptive averaging (preserving the O(1/k^2) rate) is deferred to the supplement. It would strengthen the paper to include a succinct main-text theorem and proof sketch clarifying the assumptions (smoothness of ψ*, regularity of R, step sizes, choice of β) and any constants.
- Experimental scope: Experiments are small-scale (R^3 simplex) and limited to entropy mirror map. Adding higher-dimensional problems, other feasible sets (e.g., ℓ1 ball, spectrahedron) and mirror maps, and stronger baselines (e.g., FISTA with restart for Euclidean settings) would better establish robustness. A sensitivity analysis for β, step size s, and the “max” cap in ak would be informative, as would ablations isolating the effect of adaptivity versus fixed schedules.
- Strong convexity: The heuristic yields notable gains in strongly convex cases; it would be valuable to provide theory (even partial) showing linear or faster-than-quadratic rates in continuous and discrete time under strong convexity, or clarify limits.
- Clarity and presentation: Some notation (e.g., a(t) = w/W) is introduced mid-derivation; collecting key definitions early and adding a high-level algorithm box for the continuous-time heuristic (analogous to Algorithm 1) would improve readability. The role and selection of z* in Lr could be discussed (dependence of constants, multi-solution cases). A brief practical recipe for choosing r, w, η from desired rates would help practitioners.

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis appears technically sound: existence/uniqueness (Theorem 1) is handled carefully via AMD′ and t0 > 0; the Lyapunov derivation and sufficient conditions are clear and recover known schedules as special cases; the energy function Er adds insight.
- The main limitation is the trajectory-dependent condition in Theorem 2; the adaptive rule is a reasonable remedy, but further robustness analysis (noise, discretization) would elevate the result.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, with helpful derivations and examples. Minor suggestions above could make the paper more accessible to non-experts.

Originality
- The generalized averaging/Lyapunov framework and, especially, the adaptive averaging mechanism that preserves the target rate constitute novel contributions beyond restart-based heuristics and prior continuous-time analyses.

Significance
- The framework may influence how accelerated methods are designed and tuned, particularly for constrained/mirror settings where restarts can be less principled. Accelerated replicator dynamics are of independent interest. Stronger and broader empirical validation would increase impact.

Questions for the authors
- Can adaptive averaging be extended to stochastic gradients while preserving a rate in expectation?
- How should β, s, and the ak cap be chosen in practice? Are there data-driven rules?
- For strongly convex f, can you obtain a provable linear rate with adaptive averaging, either in continuous or discrete time?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of ψ (mirror map)?

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled, and general theoretical framework that unifies and extends accelerated dynamics.
- Simple, novel adaptive heuristic with guarantees to preserve rates and promising empirical behavior.
- Interesting application to replicator dynamics and useful energy/Lyapunov insights.

Arguments against acceptance
- Discrete-time guarantees and experiments are limited in scope; more extensive evaluation and a stronger main-text discrete analysis would strengthen confidence.
- Some conditions are trajectory-dependent and may be delicate under noise/discretization; robustness is not fully addressed.

Overall, this is a solid and promising contribution to accelerated first-order methods in constrained/mirror settings, with a neat adaptive idea and clean theory. I lean positive, contingent on clarifying discrete guarantees and expanding experiments.
