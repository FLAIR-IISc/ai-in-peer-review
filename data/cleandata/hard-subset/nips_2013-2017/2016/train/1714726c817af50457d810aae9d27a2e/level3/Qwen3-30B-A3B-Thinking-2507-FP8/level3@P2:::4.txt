

**Review for "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"**

This paper studies continuous-time accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, focusing on a generalized averaging scheme that extends previous work on accelerated mirror descent. The authors formulate a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), and provide a unified Lyapunov analysis to establish sufficient conditions for achieving a target convergence rate. Their key contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function, with theoretical guarantees and empirical comparisons to existing methods.

The paper builds upon recent work by Su et al. (NIPS 2014) and Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) that connected Nesterov's accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs. The authors generalize these results by introducing a more flexible averaging scheme that can be applied to a broader class of constrained optimization problems. They provide a Lyapunov function analysis to establish sufficient conditions for achieving convergence rates of the form 1/r(t), and demonstrate how their framework can accelerate replicator dynamics on the simplex. The adaptive averaging heuristic they propose is novel in its systematic approach to improving convergence while preserving theoretical guarantees.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The Lyapunov function approach is well-motivated, and the sufficient conditions derived (Theorems 2 and 3) are clear and verifiable. The authors provide a detailed proof of the main results and demonstrate their adaptive averaging heuristic with comprehensive numerical experiments. The experiments compare the proposed method with existing heuristics (gradient restarting and speed restarting) on multiple objective functions (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, and KL divergence), confirming that adaptive averaging preserves convergence rates while outperforming existing methods in many cases. The paper acknowledges limitations, noting that the Lyapunov function may not converge to zero with multiple minimizers.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the authors provide helpful examples (positive orthant and simplex dynamics) to illustrate their framework. The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete optimization algorithms is clearly explained. The only minor improvement would be to make the relationship between continuous and discrete versions of the adaptive heuristic more prominent in the main text rather than relegating it to the supplementary material.

**Originality**: This work makes a clear contribution to the growing literature on continuous-time interpretations of accelerated methods. While the connection between acceleration and averaging was noted in prior work (e.g., [8]), the authors provide a systematic framework for analyzing and generalizing this connection. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by providing a unified Lyapunov analysis covering a broader class of averaging schemes. The application to accelerating replicator dynamics on the simplex is particularly novel and interesting.

**Significance**: The paper addresses an important problem in optimization: how to improve convergence of accelerated methods while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a principled approach that outperforms existing heuristics in many cases, with potential applications in machine learning and other fields requiring constrained optimization. The connection between continuous-time analysis and discrete algorithms is valuable for both theoretical understanding and practical algorithm design. The results advance the state of the art in optimization by providing a new perspective on acceleration that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance.

**Strengths**: The paper provides a unified theoretical framework for accelerated descent dynamics with generalized averaging, develops a novel adaptive heuristic with theoretical guarantees, includes comprehensive numerical experiments, and clearly connects continuous-time analysis to discrete algorithms. The application to replicator dynamics on the simplex is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses**: The discrete-time adaptation of the adaptive heuristic (Algorithm 1) could be better explained in the main text rather than in supplementary material. The connection to other adaptive methods (like AdaGrad and Adam) is mentioned but not deeply explored. The paper focuses primarily on the quadratic rate r(t) = t², though this is a common and important case.

**Recommendation**: This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to optimization with clear practical implications. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. The work is a good fit for NIPS as it provides novel insights into optimization methods that will be valuable to both theoretical and applied researchers. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance.
