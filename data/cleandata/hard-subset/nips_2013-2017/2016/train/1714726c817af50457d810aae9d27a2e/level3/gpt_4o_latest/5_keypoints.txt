- Introduces a novel adaptive averaging heuristic for constrained convex optimization to enhance convergence rates.
- Uses Lyapunov functions to derive sufficient conditions for weights and to prove convergence guarantees.
- Proposes a generalized accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics framework linking continuous-time dynamics to discrete algorithms.
- Shows adaptive averaging preserves the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods in discrete time.
- Empirical results indicate adaptive averaging matches or outperforms restarting heuristics, especially on strongly convex problems.
- Applies AMD to replicator dynamics, illustrating connections between optimization and evolutionary game theory.
- Experiments are limited to low-dimensional settings (e.g., R^3) and a small set of objectives, lacking real-world large-scale tests.
- Does not benchmark against popular adaptive optimizers like Adam or Adagrad.
- The heuristic lacks theoretical guarantees for faster-than-quadratic rates, limiting its theoretical impact in some settings.
- Introduces extra computational overhead from adaptive weight computations without a thorough cost–benefit analysis.
- Pro: Novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated approach that advances accelerated methods for constrained problems.
- Con: Empirical scope and theoretical guarantees are limited, and implementation complexity is not fully addressed.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to expand empirical validation and analyze computational overhead and theoretical limits.
