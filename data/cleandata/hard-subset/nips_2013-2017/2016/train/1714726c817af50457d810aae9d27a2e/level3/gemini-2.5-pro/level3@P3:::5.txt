This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order convex optimization. Building on prior work that connects Nesterov's accelerated method to a second-order ODE (Su et al., 2014) and its generalization to constrained optimization via mirror descent (Krichene et al., 2015), the authors propose and analyze a more general family of accelerated mirror descent dynamics. These dynamics are parameterized by two functions: an averaging weight function `w(t)` and a gradient accumulation rate `η(t)`. The main theoretical contribution is a unified analysis using a Lyapunov function, which provides simple sufficient conditions on `w` and `η` to achieve a desired convergence rate.

Based on this analysis, the authors introduce the paper's core practical contribution: an "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The insight is that the rate of decrease of the Lyapunov function depends on the choice of `w(t)`. The proposed heuristic adaptively adjusts this weight function to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function at each step. This provides a novel mechanism for empirically speeding up convergence, contrasting with the popular "restarting" heuristics (O'Donoghue & Candès, 2013). The authors present a discrete-time version of their algorithm and provide compelling numerical experiments on simplex-constrained problems, comparing adaptive averaging against standard accelerated mirror descent and two common restarting schemes.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Promising Heuristic:** The central idea of adaptive averaging is both interesting and intuitive. Instead of completely resetting the dynamics when progress stalls, as restarting schemes do, this method modulates the averaging process to emphasize parts of the trajectory that are more productive. This is a conceptually new and elegant approach to improving the practical performance of accelerated methods.

2.  **Solid Theoretical Grounding:** The use of a single Lyapunov function to analyze a broad family of dynamics is a strong and unifying theoretical tool. The derivation of sufficient conditions for a desired convergence rate in Theorem 2 is clear and provides the foundation for the adaptive heuristic. A key advantage of the proposed heuristic is that it is guaranteed by construction to preserve the O(1/t^2) convergence rate, which is a stronger guarantee than is typically available for restarting heuristics on non-strongly convex problems.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The numerical experiments, while on low-dimensional problems, convincingly demonstrate the promise of adaptive averaging. The method consistently performs as well as or better than adaptive restarting heuristics across several convex objectives (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, etc.). The improvement in the strongly convex case is particularly significant. The plots of the Lyapunov and energy functions provide excellent insight into the behavior of the different methods.

4.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It effectively builds from a general continuous-time framework to a specific, practical discrete-time algorithm. The connection to the replicator dynamics is a nice, concrete example that helps ground the more abstract parts of the theory.

### Weaknesses

As a reviewer with limited expertise in the analysis of continuous-time optimization dynamics, my comments are focused on the broader contributions and potential areas for improvement.

1.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are confined to 3-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems. While this is excellent for visualization and for demonstrating the core concept, it leaves open the question of how well the method performs on higher-dimensional problems or problems with different geometries that are common in machine learning.

2.  **No Proof of Faster Convergence:** While the experiments show that adaptive averaging can significantly outperform the baseline, the theoretical analysis only guarantees that the original O(1/t^2) rate is preserved. The authors rightly note this as a direction for future work, but it remains a gap between the empirical results and the theory. Can one prove, for example, a linear rate for strongly convex functions under this heuristic?

### Detailed Comments

- The intuition behind why adaptive averaging is superior to restarting could be elaborated on. Does it avoid "throwing away" useful momentum information that restarting discards? A deeper discussion of this in the conclusion or analysis section would be valuable.

- The discrete implementation in Algorithm 1 has several parameters (β, s, etc.). A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm to these parameters, or guidance on how to set them, would strengthen the practical contribution of the paper.

- The observation that the energy function `Er` appears to be decreasing for restarting heuristics as well is very interesting. This suggests `Er` might be a useful tool for analyzing restarting schemes and could be a fruitful avenue for future research.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel and promising adaptive heuristic for accelerated optimization. The idea is well-motivated by an elegant continuous-time analysis and backed by strong empirical results. The work is a significant contribution to the literature on understanding and improving accelerated first-order methods.
