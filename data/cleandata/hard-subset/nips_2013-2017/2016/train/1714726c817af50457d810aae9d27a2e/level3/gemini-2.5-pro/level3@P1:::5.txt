### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates continuous-time accelerated first-order dynamics for constrained convex optimization. The authors introduce a general family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by two time-varying functions: `η(t)`, which controls the rate of gradient accumulation for a dual variable, and `w(t)`, which defines the weights for averaging the mirrored dual trajectory to produce the primal variable. This formulation generalizes previous work that connected specific ODEs to Nesterov's accelerated methods (e.g., Su et al. 2014, Krichene et al. 2015).

The core of the theoretical analysis relies on a Lyapunov function that combines the optimization objective with a Bregman divergence term. By analyzing the time derivative of this function, the authors derive simple and elegant sufficient conditions on the weight functions `w(t)` and `η(t)` that guarantee a desired convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))`. As an example of this framework's utility, the paper shows how the well-known replicator dynamics for optimization on the simplex can be accelerated.

The main practical contribution is a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. Instead of using a fixed schedule for the weights `w(t)`, this heuristic adaptively adjusts them to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function whenever possible. This provides a principled way to speed up convergence while provably preserving the `O(1/r(t))` rate. The authors present a discrete-time version of this heuristic and show that it maintains the `O(1/k^2)` rate of standard accelerated methods. Finally, numerical experiments compare adaptive averaging with adaptive restarting schemes on several simplex-constrained problems, demonstrating that the proposed method performs competitively and, in some cases, significantly better.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The use of a Lyapunov function to analyze a general class of dynamics is a rigorous and powerful approach. The derivations appear sound, and the claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis. The work is self-contained, presenting a theoretical framework, a novel heuristic derived from it, a corresponding discrete-time algorithm, and experimental validation. The authors are careful to position their work and honestly discuss the limitations of prior heuristics (e.g., the lack of guarantees for non-strongly convex problems).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation and context. The main theoretical result (Theorem 2) and its derivation are presented clearly and are easy to follow. The central idea of adaptive averaging is also explained intuitively.

A few areas could be improved for clarity. Section 3, which derives the primal representation, is quite dense. While correct, a bit more intuition on the role of the `∇²ψ* ◦ ∇ψ` operator would be helpful for a broader audience. Similarly, the transition from the continuous-time heuristic in equation (8) to the discrete implementation in Algorithm 1 could be elaborated on more in the main text, as the reader currently has to refer to the supplementary material for a full understanding.

**Originality:** This work makes several original contributions. While the study of optimization dynamics is an active field, the formulation of a general family of accelerated dynamics parameterized by `w` and `η` is a novel and valuable conceptual step. The main novelty is the adaptive averaging heuristic itself. It is a fundamentally different approach to speeding up accelerated methods compared to the more common restarting schemes. Its direct motivation from the Lyapunov analysis gives it a strong theoretical grounding that many other heuristics lack.

**Significance:** The paper has the potential for significant impact.
1.  **Theoretically:** The unified framework provides new insights into the role of averaging in acceleration and could be a useful tool for designing and analyzing future optimization methods.
2.  **Practically:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is a concrete and promising contribution. It offers a compelling alternative to adaptive restarting, with the key advantage of a provable convergence rate guarantee. The experimental results, though on small-scale problems, suggest it can offer substantial performance improvements. Researchers and practitioners working with accelerated first-order methods will likely find these ideas useful and may be motivated to build upon them.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, principled, and elegant adaptive heuristic for accelerated methods that is directly derived from a solid theoretical framework.
*   **Pro:** The proposed heuristic comes with theoretical guarantees on its convergence rate, which is a significant advantage over some existing ad-hoc restart schemes.
*   **Pro:** The general continuous-time framework for analyzing accelerated mirror descent is a strong conceptual contribution in its own right.
*   **Pro:** The empirical evaluation, while limited in scale, shows that the method is highly competitive with and often superior to standard restarting heuristics.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
*   **Con (minor):** The experimental validation is limited to low-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems. While sufficient for a proof of concept, demonstrating the heuristic's effectiveness on larger-scale machine learning problems would strengthen the paper's practical claims.
*   **Con (minor):** Some key details, particularly regarding the discretization and the proof of convergence for the discrete algorithm, are deferred to the supplementary material. Including a bit more of this analysis or intuition in the main paper would make it more self-contained.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a thoughtful and rigorous study with both theoretical depth and practical relevance. I recommend acceptance.
