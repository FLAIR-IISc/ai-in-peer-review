## Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, building upon the primal-dual ODE framework introduced by Krichene et al. The core idea is to analyze the impact of different averaging schemes applied to the mirrored dual trajectory on the convergence rate of the dynamics. The authors provide theoretical convergence rates in the continuous setting, demonstrating improvements over the standard 1/t² rate, and propose an adaptive weighting scheme with provable guarantees. They empirically validate this scheme, comparing it to established restarting techniques.

The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and technically sound. The continuous-time analysis is rigorous, and the derivation of convergence rates is convincing. The connection between acceleration and averaging is nicely formalized, and the proposed adaptive weighting scheme is a logical extension of previous work. The empirical results provide support for the theoretical findings, showing competitive performance with existing heuristics. The paper successfully extends the framework of Krichene et al. by providing a more nuanced understanding of the role of averaging in accelerated methods.

However, a significant weakness is the omission of a theoretical analysis for the discrete version of the algorithm. While the authors provide a discretization and empirical results, a theoretical understanding of the convergence rate in the discrete setting is crucial for establishing the practical utility of the proposed method. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work. Specifically, the authors should cite foundational work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and co-authors on accelerated methods and their connection to ODEs (e.g., their work on fast convergence of inertial dynamics and Hamiltonian systems). These works provide important context and establish a clear lineage for the current research. 

An open question remains regarding whether the improved continuous-time convergence rates persist after discretization. While the empirical results are encouraging, a theoretical analysis is needed to confirm this. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and insightful analysis of averaging schemes in accelerated methods.
*   Rigorous theoretical results for the continuous-time dynamics.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Empirically validated adaptive weighting scheme.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of theoretical analysis for the discrete algorithm.
*   Missing citations to relevant work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and co-authors.
*   Unclear whether improved continuous rates translate to the discrete case.

**Overall:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The theoretical contributions are valuable, and the adaptive averaging scheme is a promising direction for future research. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the missing citations and acknowledging the need for a theoretical analysis of the discrete algorithm in future work.
