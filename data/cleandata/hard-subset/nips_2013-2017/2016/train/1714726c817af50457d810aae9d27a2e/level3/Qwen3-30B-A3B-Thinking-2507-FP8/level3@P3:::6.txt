

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, proposing a generalized averaging framework that preserves convergence rates for non-strongly convex objectives. The authors formulate the problem as a continuous-time ODE, demonstrating that appropriate choice of weight functions for averaging yields desired convergence rates. They then introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights to accelerate convergence while preserving the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods.

The work builds upon recent NIPS contributions on continuous-time optimization dynamics, particularly the connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and ODEs established by Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11]. The authors extend this framework to a broader family of averaging schemes, providing a unified Lyapunov-based analysis. This represents a meaningful extension to a growing line of research at NIPS that interprets optimization algorithms as continuous-time processes.

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with well-justified conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing. Theorem 2 provides a clear sufficient condition for the convergence rate, and the adaptive heuristic is rigorously shown to preserve this rate. The numerical experiments, while limited, do demonstrate that adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics in several cases.

2. **Originality:** The paper presents a novel generalization of the averaging scheme in accelerated mirror descent, extending prior work by Krichene et al. [11]. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a fresh perspective on accelerating optimization methods, distinct from existing restarting techniques.

3. **Significance:** The continuous-time dynamics framework has proven valuable for understanding and improving optimization algorithms, making this work relevant to the NIPS community. The theoretical guarantee of preserving convergence rates while potentially improving empirical performance is a significant contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Clarity:** The paper is difficult to follow due to insufficient background material and ambiguous notation. Key concepts like mirror maps and Bregman divergence are not adequately explained for readers unfamiliar with optimization theory. Equations (3) and (4) (lines 33-34 and 46-47) require more intuitive explanation - the authors should provide verbal descriptions of what these equations mean in terms of the optimization process, not just mathematical derivation.

2. **Practical Significance:** The numerical experiments are conducted exclusively in R³, a toy setup that does not demonstrate scalability to higher dimensions. The authors should provide evidence of the method's performance on larger-scale problems to establish practical relevance. The paper currently lacks a discussion of computational complexity or runtime comparisons.

3. **Discussion of Related Work:** The paper does not sufficiently discuss the relationship between adaptive averaging and adaptive methods in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad, Adam). While mentioned briefly in the introduction, the authors do not explain why their approach is specifically tailored for accelerated convex optimization rather than being a general adaptive method. This is a missed opportunity to better situate their work within the broader optimization literature.

4. **Figure 1:** The reference to the "solid line" is ambiguous. The authors should use different line styles or colors to clearly distinguish the primal trajectory from other components.

**Specific Recommendations:**

- Add a brief background section explaining mirror maps and Bregman divergence at the beginning of the paper.
- Provide intuitive explanations for the key equations (3) and (4), connecting them to the optimization process.
- Expand the experimental section to include higher-dimensional problems and runtime comparisons.
- Add a paragraph discussing how adaptive averaging relates to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization, explaining why their approach is specifically designed for accelerated convex optimization.
- Revise Figure 1 to clearly distinguish the primal trajectory using a different line style or color.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theory of accelerated optimization methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, well-motivated, and theoretically well-founded. However, the paper's current presentation is too dense for the general NIPS audience, and the limited experimental evidence weakens the claim of practical significance.

The strengths (solid theoretical foundation, novel heuristic) outweigh the weaknesses (clarity issues, limited experiments), but the authors must address the clarity concerns and provide more substantial experimental evidence to justify the significance of their method. With these improvements, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept (after major revisions)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The theoretical framework extends prior work on continuous-time optimization dynamics
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and preserves convergence rates
- The paper provides a unified analysis of accelerated mirror descent dynamics

**Arguments for Rejection (if not revised):**
- The paper assumes too much background knowledge from the reader
- Experiments are limited to low-dimensional toy examples
- The relationship to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization is inadequately discussed
- The practical significance of the method is not sufficiently demonstrated
