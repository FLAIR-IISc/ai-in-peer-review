This paper studies accelerated mirror descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending prior work by the authors (Krichene et al., NIPS 2016) on a Lyapunov-based analysis of these dynamics. The core contribution lies in a generalized averaging scheme parameterized by weight functions and a novel adaptive averaging heuristic designed to speed up convergence. The authors provide sufficient conditions for convergence, demonstrate acceleration of the replicator dynamics as an example, and present numerical experiments suggesting comparable or superior performance to adaptive restarting heuristics.

The paper is well-motivated, building on the connection between Nesterov’s accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs, a line of research prominently featured in recent NIPS conferences (Su et al., NIPS 2016; Wibisono et al., NIPS 2016). The authors clearly summarize relevant background and relate their work to existing literature, including references to Attouch et al. and Nesterov's foundational work. The mathematical treatment is rigorous, and the Lyapunov argument is presented clearly. The extension to the replicator dynamics offers a concrete illustration of the proposed framework.

However, despite the technical soundness, the paper's overall impact feels incremental. The generalization from simple averaging to the parameterized scheme `AMDw,η` feels somewhat trivial; much of the initial analysis feels like a direct consequence of applying standard techniques to this broader class. The focus on the quadratic convergence rate in the discretization, while justifiable, limits the broader applicability of the results. While the adaptive averaging heuristic is intuitively appealing, it remains largely a heuristic due to the discretization approximations and the lack of a complete convergence proof – as the authors themselves acknowledge. The energy function analysis, primal representation, and application to the replicator dynamics, while insightful, do not represent substantial advancements for the machine learning community.

A key weakness is the limited empirical evaluation. The experiments are restricted to a relatively simple setting – a toy quadratic/linear objective in R3. While the results suggest that adaptive averaging performs well, they are not convincing evidence of a general advantage, especially for strongly convex problems where other adaptive methods (e.g., Adam, Duchi et al., 2011) are well-established. The absence of comparisons to other existing averaging schemes specifically designed for Nesterov-style algorithms (Flammarion & Bach, 2016) further weakens the empirical claim. Furthermore, the visualization of trajectories, while informative, doesn't provide a quantitative assessment of performance gains.

**Strengths:**

*   Technically sound analysis based on Lyapunov arguments.
*   Clear presentation and connection to related work.
*   Intuitive and novel adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   Illustrative example with replicator dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental contribution – generalization feels trivial.
*   Lack of a complete theoretical convergence proof for the heuristic.
*   Limited and somewhat unconvincing empirical evaluation.
*   No comparison to other existing averaging schemes.
*   Focus on quadratic rate limits broader applicability.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a logically sound extension of existing work on accelerated methods.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic is a reasonable and potentially useful contribution.
*   The analysis provides a deeper understanding of the interplay between averaging and acceleration.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The contribution feels incremental and lacks substantial novelty.
*   The heuristic remains largely unproven and relies on discretization approximations.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to support strong claims of practical advantage.
*   The paper does not significantly advance the state-of-the-art in machine learning optimization.

**Overall Assessment:**

I currently lean towards **reject**. While the paper is technically competent and presents some interesting ideas, the incremental nature of the contribution, the lack of a complete theoretical foundation, and the limited empirical evaluation do not justify acceptance to NIPS. However, the authors’ commitment to address the reviewers’ concerns – including adding high-dimensional/non-quadratic experiments, providing a convergence proof, implementing the algorithm on Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method, and comparing to other adaptive averaging schemes – is encouraging. If these commitments are fulfilled with convincing results, a revision could potentially change my assessment. As it stands, the paper requires significant additional work to reach the standards for publication at NIPS.
