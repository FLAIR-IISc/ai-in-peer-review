This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization from the perspective of continuous-time dynamics. Building on recent work that connects Nesterov's accelerated methods to second-order ordinary differential equations (ODEs), particularly the averaging interpretation from Krichene et al. (2015), the authors propose a generalized family of "Accelerated Mirror Descent" (AMD) dynamics. These dynamics are parameterized by two functions: a rate `η(t)` for accumulating gradients in a dual variable, and a weight `w(t)` for averaging the mirrored dual trajectory to produce the primal variable.

The core of the paper's theoretical contribution is a Lyapunov-based analysis of these generalized dynamics. The authors derive simple, sufficient conditions on `η(t)` and `w(t)` that guarantee a desired convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))`. This framework is then used to introduce the main contribution: a novel and intuitive "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The heuristic adaptively chooses the averaging weights `w(t)` to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function at each step, thereby seeking to accelerate convergence beyond the guaranteed rate. A key theoretical advantage of this approach is that, by construction, it is guaranteed to preserve the baseline convergence rate of the non-adaptive method, a property not established for other popular heuristics like adaptive restarting. The paper demonstrates the framework by showing how to accelerate the replicator dynamics on the probability simplex and provides a discrete-time version of the adaptive algorithm. Numerical experiments on several objective functions show that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as, and often significantly better than, standard accelerated methods and adaptive restarting schemes.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The mathematical arguments, centered on a Lyapunov function analysis, are sound, elegant, and provide a clear path from the generalized ODE to the adaptive heuristic. The claim that the adaptive method preserves the convergence rate is well-supported by the continuous-time analysis. The authors are careful to state their assumptions and the conditions under which their results hold. The experimental evaluation is well-designed, comparing the proposed method against relevant baselines across a sensible range of convex optimization problems, and the results convincingly support the paper's claims about the heuristic's practical benefits.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The progression from the general ODE formulation in Section 2 to the adaptive heuristic in Section 4 is logical and easy to follow. The authors provide good intuition for their method, for instance, by connecting the Bregman divergence term in their energy function to kinetic energy. The visualizations of the trajectories on the simplex in the experiments are particularly effective at illustrating the qualitative differences between the compared methods.

**Originality:** The work is situated in a growing line of research on the continuous-time interpretation of accelerated optimization. While the core ODE framework is a direct generalization of prior work (Krichene et al., 2015), the main contribution—the idea of an adaptive averaging scheme based on greedily decreasing a Lyapunov function—is novel and insightful. This provides a new, principled approach for designing heuristics for accelerated methods that go beyond simple restarting. The application to accelerating the replicator dynamics is also a nice, original contribution that connects this optimization perspective to other fields like evolutionary game theory.

**Significance:** The paper's potential impact is high. Nesterov's accelerated methods are ubiquitous in machine learning and large-scale optimization. The paper proposes a simple, easy-to-implement modification that appears to offer dramatic empirical speed-ups over both the standard fixed-schedule schemes and existing adaptive heuristics. The fact that this performance boost comes with a formal guarantee of not being worse than the original method makes it a very appealing practical tool. Researchers and practitioners working on first-order optimization are likely to use and build upon these ideas.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**
This is an excellent paper that combines elegant theory with promising practical results. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and provides a new perspective on improving accelerated methods. I have two minor suggestions for improvement:

1.  While the paper's main theoretical development is in continuous time, the proposed heuristic is implemented as a discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1). The authors state that a proof preserving the quadratic convergence rate for this algorithm is provided in the supplementary material. It would strengthen the paper if a brief sketch or the key intuition of this discrete-time analysis could be included in the main text. This is an important link, as the guarantees for the practical algorithm are what matter most to users.

2.  For the specific case of unconstrained, strongly convex quadratic objectives, the conjugate gradient (CG) method provides the optimal polynomial-time algorithm. Given the impressive performance of adaptive averaging in the strongly convex case shown in Figure 2, it would be fascinating to see a discussion of the potential connections between this heuristic and CG. For example, does adaptive averaging implicitly learn something akin to the conjugate directions that CG explicitly constructs? Exploring this link could provide deeper theoretical insights into why the heuristic works so well.

These are minor points, and the paper is a strong contribution as it stands.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses (Pros and Cons):**

**Pros:**
*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and principled adaptive heuristic for accelerated first-order methods.
*   The heuristic is guaranteed to preserve the baseline `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate, unlike some popular restarting heuristics.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance, with significant speed-ups over standard acceleration and adaptive restarting across various problem types.
*   High potential for practical impact, given the widespread use of Nesterov's methods.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and theoretically sound.

**Cons:**
*   The theoretical framework is a close extension of prior work by the same authors.
*   The analysis of the discrete-time algorithm, which is crucial for the practical implementation, is relegated entirely to the supplementary material.
