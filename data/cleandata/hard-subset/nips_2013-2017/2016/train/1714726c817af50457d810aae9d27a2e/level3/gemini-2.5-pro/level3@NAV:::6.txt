This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization from the perspective of continuous-time dynamics. Building on prior work that connects Nesterov's acceleration to a second-order ODE [20] and its generalization to mirror descent [11], the authors propose a broader family of Accelerated Mirror Descent dynamics parameterized by two weight functions. The core theoretical contribution is a Lyapunov function-based analysis that provides sufficient conditions on these weights to achieve a desired convergence rate, notably preserving the O(1/t^2) rate for non-strongly convex objectives. Based on this analysis, the authors introduce a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic that adaptively chooses the averaging weights to speed up convergence. This is positioned as an alternative to popular restarting heuristics [17, 20], with the key advantage of providing a formal guarantee on the convergence rate.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Quality:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded heuristic. The main strength is providing a formal guarantee that the proposed adaptive scheme preserves the O(1/t^2) convergence rate for non-strongly convex problems, a guarantee often missing for restarting heuristics. The continuous-time analysis via a Lyapunov function is elegant and provides clear insights.
*   **Significance:** The idea of adaptively tuning the averaging schedule, rather than the momentum or step size, is an interesting and underexplored direction. If scalable, this could offer a valuable new tool for practitioners, complementing existing acceleration techniques.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity:** The paper is challenging to follow for readers not already deeply familiar with the continuous-time analysis of mirror descent. The manuscript would be substantially improved by adding more background material on mirror maps and Bregman divergence. Furthermore, the key theoretical steps, such as the derivation leading to inequality (3) between lines 33–34 and the energy function analysis around lines 46–47, lack sufficient intuition and would benefit from more detailed, step-by-step explanations.
*   **Significance and Experimental Validation:** The practical significance of the proposed method is not convincingly demonstrated. The experiments are confined to a low-dimensional R^3 space, which amounts to a toy problem. For a NIPS paper, it is crucial to show that the method is effective and scalable on higher-dimensional problems relevant to the machine learning community.
*   **Relation to Prior Work:** While Adagrad and Adam are mentioned, the paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion on the relationship between adaptive averaging and the family of adaptive learning rate methods used in non-convex optimization.

**Minor Issue:**
*   In Figure 1, the reference to the primal trajectory as a "solid line" is ambiguous as multiple lines are solid. Using a dotted line or distinct colors would enhance clarity.
