- Studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent dynamics parameterized by averaging weight w(t) and gradient accumulation rate η(t), with a unified Lyapunov analysis giving sufficient conditions for convergence.

- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that greedily decreases the Lyapunov function, offering an alternative to restarting and preserving the O(1/t^2) rate.

- Presents a discrete-time algorithm and numerical experiments on simplex-constrained problems showing strong empirical gains, especially in the strongly convex case.

- Theoretical contribution: single Lyapunov function unifies a broad family of dynamics; Theorem 2 provides clear sufficient conditions for desired rates.

- Novel and intuitive heuristic that modulates averaging instead of fully resetting momentum as in restarting schemes.

- Guarantee by construction to preserve the O(1/t^2) convergence rate, stronger than typical restarting guarantees on non-strongly convex problems.

- Strong empirical performance on tested problems, with informative plots of Lyapunov and energy functions.

- Clear exposition and useful connection to replicator dynamics that grounds the theory.

- Limited experiments (3D simplex problems) leave scalability and performance on higher-dimensional or different-geometry problems unclear.

- No theoretical proof that adaptive averaging yields faster-than-O(1/t^2) convergence (e.g., linear rates for strongly convex functions) — gap between empirical and theoretical results.

- Suggest elaborating on why adaptive averaging outperforms restarting (e.g., not discarding useful momentum).

- Recommend discussing sensitivity and tuning guidance for discrete algorithm parameters (β, s, etc.).

- Note that the energy function Er decreasing under restarting heuristics is an interesting observation and a promising direction for analyzing restarting schemes.
