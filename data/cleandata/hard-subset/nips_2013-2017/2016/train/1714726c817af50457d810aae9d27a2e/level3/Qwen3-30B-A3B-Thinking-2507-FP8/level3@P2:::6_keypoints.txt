- Studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization via a unified continuous-time AMDw,η framework with weight functions and Lyapunov-based sufficient conditions for convergence.  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes weights, preserves quadratic convergence, and is empirically compared to restarting heuristics.  
- Theoretical foundation is rigorous with clear proofs ensuring the Lyapunov function decreases and guarantees are preserved for the adaptive method.  
- Paper lacks discussion of limitations, notably performance guarantees or applicability to non-convex problems.  
- Significant clarity issues: key equations (lines 33–34, 46–47) lack intuitive explanation, and the link between continuous dynamics and discrete Algorithm 1 is under-explained.  
- Needs more background on mirror descent and accelerated methods for non-expert readers.  
- Novelty: generalizes averaging in accelerated mirror descent, provides a principled adaptive heuristic, and connects to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory.  
- Fails to adequately relate the approach to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad, Adam).  
- Practical significance is questionable: experiments are limited to a toy R^3 simplex setup and do not demonstrate scalability or performance on higher-dimensional/complex problems.  
- Strengths: rigorous theory, novel adaptive averaging that preserves guarantees, thorough comparison to restarting heuristics, and connection to replicator dynamics.  
- Weaknesses: poorly explained key equations, limited low-dimensional experiments, insufficient discussion of adaptive non-convex methods, ambiguous figure presentation, and missing practical implementation discussion.  
- Arguments for acceptance: unified continuous-time perspective and a well-motivated adaptive heuristic that maintains convergence guarantees.  
- Arguments against acceptance: clarity problems, insufficient experimental validation/scalability, and underdeveloped ties to broader adaptive optimization literature.  
- Recommendation: borderline—accept only after substantial revisions (clarify equations and intuition, expand experiments to higher dimensions, relate to other adaptive methods, and improve figures and implementation discussion).
