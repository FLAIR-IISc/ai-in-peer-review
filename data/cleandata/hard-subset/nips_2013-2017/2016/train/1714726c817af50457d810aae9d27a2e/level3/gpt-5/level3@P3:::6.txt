Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies accelerated mirror descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through the lens of generalized averaging. The core idea is to decouple a dual variable Z that accumulates gradients at rate η(t) and a primal variable X that is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors derive sufficient conditions on η and w that guarantee a target rate 1/r(t). This unifies and extends prior continuous-time analyses of accelerated methods (Su-Boyd-Candès; Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan) and earlier work showing that Nesterov acceleration can be interpreted via averaging. The paper further derives a primal-only form and illustrates that replicator dynamics on the simplex can be “accelerated” by this averaging scheme. The main algorithmic contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic: choose the weight schedule a(t)=w(t)/W(t) to decrease the time derivative of the Lyapunov function whenever possible, thereby preserving the original rate even without strong convexity. The discrete-time realization (Algorithm 1) is compared to adaptive restart heuristics (gradient- and speed-based), showing consistent and sometimes significant gains.

Strengths:
- Conceptual unification: clean sufficient conditions on η and w that recover known rates, including O(1/t^2) as a special case.
- Novel heuristic: adapt averaging weights to enforce Lyapunov decrease; this provides monotonicity guarantees that typical restart heuristics lack for general convex objectives.
- Clear links to mirror descent and replicator dynamics; the primal representation is useful and broadly applicable.
- Theoretical support in continuous time and a discrete scheme with preserved quadratic rate (details in the supplement).

Weaknesses:
- Clarity: the paper is hard to follow in places; some key steps and abbreviations are not introduced with enough intuition.
- Experiments: mostly R^3 toy problems; limited evidence of scalability to higher dimensions and practical constrained ML tasks.
- Practical guidance: how to choose β, step sizes, and regularizers R; stability under noisy/stochastic gradients is not addressed.

Quality:
The continuous-time analysis appears technically sound. The Lyapunov inequality and sufficient conditions (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1) are standard but well adapted to the averaging view. The energy function Er adds insight. However, the discrete-time analysis is relegated to the supplement; at least a theorem statement and proof sketch should appear in the main text. The empirical section supports the claims qualitatively but is limited in scale and scope. The heuristic’s proxy test in Algorithm 1 (using f(x̃(k+1)) ≤ f(x̃(k))) for the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ deserves justification.

Clarity:
The exposition would benefit from more background and a notation table. Please define AMD, AMD′, AMDp, MD, MDp, r(t), w(t), η(t), mirror map, and Bregman divergence at first use. A short “cheat sheet” of identities used repeatedly (e.g., ∇ψ*(Z)−X = (W/w) Ẋ) would help. Figures: clarify Figure 1 by using dotted or colored lines to distinguish trajectories; the “solid line” reference is ambiguous.

Requested intuitive explanations:
- Lyapunov derivative bound (the inequality leading to condition (2) of Theorem 2). Intuition: start from Lr = r(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*). Differentiating yields r′(f(X)−f*) + r⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩ + ⟨Ż, ∇ψ*(Z)−∇ψ*(z*)⟩. Using Ż = −η∇f(X) and the averaging identity ẊW + Xw = w∇ψ*(Z), one gets ∇ψ*(Z)−X = (W/w) Ẋ. Hence the last term is −η⟨∇f, X + (W/w)Ẋ − x*⟩ ≤ −η(f(X)−f*) − η(W/w)⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩ by convexity. Grouping terms yields the displayed inequality: d/dt Lr ≤ (f(X)−f*)(r′−η) + ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩(r − η W/w).
- Energy derivative (Theorem 3). Intuition: Er combines “potential” f(X) and “kinetic” Dψ*(Z,∇ψ(X))/r. Differentiating Dψ*(Z, X̌) gives ⟨∇ψ*(Z)−X, Ż⟩ − ⟨Ẋ, Z−X̌⟩. With ∇ψ*(Z)−X = (W/w)Ẋ and Ż = −η∇f(X), plus monotonicity of ∇ψ*, the second term is nonnegative, leading to d/dt Dψ*(Z, X̌) ≤ −(η W/w)⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩. Substituting into d/dt Er shows non-increase when r − η W/w ≤ 0.

Originality:
Adapting averaging weights to preserve a Lyapunov decrease for accelerated mirror descent is, to my knowledge, new and distinct from adaptive restart schemes. The connection to replicator dynamics is a nice byproduct. The work is well positioned relative to Nesterov, Su-Boyd-Candès, and Wibisono et al.

Significance:
If the heuristic scales and remains robust in practice, it could become a standard alternative to restarts for accelerated methods in constrained settings. As presented, the significance is promising but not yet demonstrated beyond small-scale examples.

Relation to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization:
Please expand the discussion contrasting adaptive averaging with Adagrad and Adam. Key differences:
- Object of adaptation: your method adapts averaging weights (coupling between X and Z) to control a Lyapunov decrease; Adagrad/Adam adapt per-coordinate learning rates/preconditioning of gradients.
- Setting and guarantees: your guarantees target convex, accelerated dynamics with explicit Lyapunov monotonicity; Adagrad/Adam are usually analyzed for convex/online or stochastic nonconvex without acceleration.
- Potential synergies: could a(t) adaptation be combined with per-coordinate preconditioners (e.g., diagonal ∇2ψ* or Adagrad accumulators) or with stochastic gradients? What breaks in the Lyapunov proof under noise?

Questions for authors:
- Practical test: How reliable is the discrete proxy (lines 7–8 in Algorithm 1) for the sign of ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩? Any sensitivity analysis or backtracking variant?
- Parameter choices: Guidance for β, step size s, and regularizer R across problems?
- Robustness: Behavior under stochastic gradients or approximate proximal maps?
- Complexity: Per-iteration cost vs restart baselines; wall-clock comparisons.

Requests to strengthen the paper:
- Add a concise background/notation section and a table of symbols.
- Include the discrete-time theorem and a proof sketch in the main paper.
- Provide higher-dimensional experiments (e.g., n ≥ 1000) on representative constrained tasks: simplex-constrained logistic regression, portfolio optimization, l1-ball or box constraints; report objective vs time and iteration; compare to adaptive restarts and a tuned baseline without heuristics.
- Ablations: effect of turning off the adaptive rule; sensitivity to β; alternative tests for the Lyapunov decrease.
- Clarify Figure 1 with dotted/colored lines for the primal trajectory.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear conceptual contribution and unifying analysis.
- New heuristic with theoretical guarantees beyond standard restarts.
- Appealing connections to replicator dynamics and mirror descent.

Cons for acceptance:
- Clarity issues impede accessibility; key steps need more intuition.
- Experimental evidence is narrow; scalability and practical relevance not yet demonstrated.
- Discrete-time guarantees are not presented in the main text.

Overall assessment:
A solid and original contribution at the intersection of continuous-time analysis and accelerated first-order methods. With improved exposition and stronger empirical validation at scale, this would be a strong NIPS paper. I lean toward borderline accept, contingent on clarity revisions and additional higher-dimensional experiments.
