This paper studies accelerated mirror descent (AMD) through a generalized continuous-time ODE with two degrees of freedom: a gradient accumulation rate η(t) in the dual variable and a weighting schedule w(t) used to average the mirrored dual trajectory into the primal iterate. A Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) yields simple sufficient conditions on (η,w) to attain any target rate 1/r(t); standard O(1/t^2) rates are recovered as a special case. The formulation yields a primal representation, with examples for orthant and simplex constraints; in particular, it exhibits an “accelerated replicator” dynamics by averaging the trajectory of a scaled replicator flow. Motivated by the Lyapunov differential inequality, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts the instantaneous weighting a(t) = w(t)/W(t) to make the Lyapunov derivative as negative as possible while preserving the rate guarantee. A discrete-time instantiation for r(t)=t^2 (matching first-order accelerated methods) is given; the authors show the quadratic rate is retained and present experiments on simplex-constrained problems comparing to gradient and speed restarting. Empirically, adaptive averaging is at least as good as restarting and sometimes substantially better.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on Krichene, Bayen, Bartlett (NIPS 2015) who introduced AMD and its continuous/discrete analyses, on Su, Boyd, Candès (NIPS 2014) linking Nesterov acceleration to second-order ODEs, and on the variational ODE view of Wibisono, Wilson, and Jordan (2016). It contrasts with adaptive restarting heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès; Su et al.) by adapting averaging weights inside the accelerated dynamics, with Lyapunov-based guarantees even without strong convexity. The work also connects to averaging/acceleration insights (Flammarion & Bach) and to adaptive first-order methods (Adagrad, Adam) while serving a different purpose. The replicator example situates the contribution in the literature on dynamics over simplexes.

Quality:
- Theory: The ODE well-posedness (Theorem 1) is standard and correct under the stated Lipschitz assumptions. The Lyapunov calculation (Theorem 2) is clean; the trajectory-dependent sign condition is addressed by (i) a constructive schedule (Corollary 1) and (ii) the adaptive rule that enforces nonpositivity of the cross-term. The energy function Er that decreases under the same condition is a nice addition and provides physical intuition. The explicit construction of w via a(t)=w/W and the examples for r(t)=tp are helpful.
- Discretization: The paper gives a discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) for the t^2 rate and claims a preserved O(1/k^2) rate via a discrete Lyapunov argument in the supplement. Given the centrality of discrete-time performance, more details in the main text would strengthen the contribution, particularly the conditions on ψ, R (smoothness/strong convexity), stepsize selection s, and robustness to inexact solves in the proximal subproblems.
- Experiments: The qualitative 2D-simplex visualizations are instructive and the comparisons to gradient/speed restarting are fair as a first cut. However, the empirical section is small-scale and focuses on R^3; quantitative results on higher-dimensional problems (e.g., l1 or simplex-constrained logistic regression, portfolio optimization, or large KL problems) and wall-clock metrics would provide stronger evidence.

Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. The derivation of AMD’ and the Lyapunov argument are easy to follow. The primal forms and worked examples (orthant, simplex/replicator) improve readability. Suggestions: clarify the intuition behind a(t) as the instantaneous averaging “rate” and how constant vs. ramping a(t) shape the effective weights w; bring parts of the discrete analysis from the supplement into the main paper; specify all hyperparameters (β, s) and tuning protocol in the experiments.

Originality: The generalized averaging parametrization and the adaptive rule guided by a Lyapunov derivative are simple but novel within the AMD/ODE framework. The connection to the replicator dynamics is a nice conceptual contribution. That said, the work is a close extension of Krichene et al.’s AMD analysis, and the novelty is primarily in the adaptive weighting heuristic rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic class.

Significance: Given the ubiquity of accelerated first-order methods, a robust, rate-preserving adaptive heuristic that is competitive with restarting is practically valuable. If the discrete-time guarantees and empirical gains extend to higher dimensions and common ML objectives, the impact could be substantial. Stronger results for strongly convex objectives (e.g., linear convergence with explicit constants) and stochastic settings would further increase significance.

Pros:
- Clear, unified Lyapunov framework yielding simple sufficient conditions for arbitrary rates r(t).
- Insightful primal forms and an accelerated replicator dynamics example.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic that provably preserves the target rate and empirically improves over fixed schedules and restarts.
- Simple, modular idea that can be incorporated into existing accelerated methods.

Cons:
- Discrete-time theory for the adaptive scheme is mostly deferred to the supplement; stronger main-text guarantees would help.
- Experiments are limited to low-dimensional simplex problems; no large-scale or composite objectives.
- Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity (β, s) and computational overhead versus restarts.
- For strongly convex problems, no theory showing improved rates over standard acceleration; the CG link is only suggested.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Move key parts of the discrete Lyapunov proof and rate guarantee for Algorithm 1 into the main paper, including assumptions on ψ and R and stepsize conditions.
- Provide higher-dimensional experiments on standard benchmarks (e.g., sparse logistic with l1 constraints, portfolio simplex problems), with wall-clock and iteration counts, and ablations of the adaptive rule (e.g., thresholds, smoothing).
- Analyze the strongly convex case: can adaptive averaging yield provable linear rates with better constants or robustness than restarting?
- Explore the hinted connection to Conjugate Gradient on quadratic objectives; even partial theory or diagnostic plots (e.g., weight schedules vs. polynomial filters) would be valuable.
- Discuss extensions to composite objectives and stochastic gradients; is the Lyapunov-based adaptivity stable under noise?

Overall evaluation: A solid and well-presented extension of AMD with a practically appealing adaptive averaging rule and clean continuous-time guarantees. Despite modest novelty, the idea is useful and the paper will interest the NIPS community working on optimization dynamics and acceleration. I lean toward acceptance contingent on strengthening the discrete-time analysis and experiments.
