This paper studies a family of continuous-time dynamics for accelerated mirror descent (AMD). It generalizes the framework of Krichene et al. [NIPS 2015], which modeled accelerated descent as a coupling of a dual variable accumulating gradients and a primal variable formed by a specific weighted average of the dual trajectory. This work extends that model to a broader class of dynamics parameterized by a gradient accumulation rate function η(t) and an averaging weight function w(t). Using a Lyapunov function analysis similar to prior work, the authors derive sufficient conditions on η(t) and w(t) to guarantee a convergence rate of 1/r(t). As an example, this framework is used to derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics for optimization over the probability simplex.

The paper's main contribution is a novel heuristic called "adaptive averaging." Instead of using a fixed schedule for the averaging weights, this method adaptively chooses the weights to greedily decrease the value of the Lyapunov function at each step. The authors propose a discrete-time implementation of this heuristic for the O(1/k^2) convergence rate case and provide numerical experiments comparing its performance against standard accelerated mirror descent and two common restarting heuristics (gradient-based and speed-based) on low-dimensional constrained optimization problems.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of the Heuristic:** The central idea of adaptive averaging is original and well-motivated. Tying the adaptation of the averaging weights directly to the descent of a Lyapunov function provides a principled foundation for the heuristic, which is an elegant alternative to more ad-hoc restarting criteria.
*   **Theoretical Insight:** The paper offers a unified view of a general class of accelerated dynamics, clarifying the role of averaging. The analyses, including the introduction of a secondary energy function (Er) and the primal-space reformulation, enhance the theoretical understanding of these continuous-time systems. The connection to and acceleration of the replicator dynamics is an interesting theoretical contribution.
*   **Guaranteed Rate Preservation:** A key advantage of the proposed heuristic (in its continuous-time formulation) is that it is designed to preserve the O(1/r(t)) convergence rate by construction, as it ensures the Lyapunov function remains non-increasing. This is a stronger guarantee than is typically available for restarting heuristics, which can sometimes fail to converge at the accelerated rate.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Theoretical Framework:** The generalization from the specific averaging scheme in [11] to the generalized one presented here feels incremental. The analytical tools, particularly the Lyapunov function Lr, are directly adopted from this prior work, making the core theoretical extension straightforward. The practical utility of analyzing rates beyond O(1/t^2) is also limited, as the paper notes that achieving them typically requires higher-order methods, which have different computational trade-offs.
*   **Lack of a Discrete-Time Proof:** While the adaptive averaging idea is derived from a continuous-time analysis, the paper presents a discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) without a corresponding convergence proof. The guarantees on the Lyapunov function hold for the ODE, but it is not shown that they transfer to the discretization. This makes the proposed algorithm a heuristic, albeit a well-motivated one. A formal analysis showing that Algorithm 1 achieves an accelerated rate would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments are performed on low-dimensional (R^3) problems with simple quadratic, linear, or KL-divergence objectives. While these serve as a proof of concept, they are not sufficient to demonstrate the practical advantages of adaptive averaging for machine learning applications, which often involve high-dimensional, non-quadratic, and/or non-strongly convex objectives. The claim of "significant improvement" over restarting relies on a single strongly convex example, which may not generalize.
*   **Missing Comparisons:** The paper compares adaptive averaging to restarting heuristics but omits comparisons to other adaptive or non-standard averaging schemes for Nesterov-style acceleration. For example, it would be valuable to understand how this method relates to the work of Flammarion and Bach [8] or other approaches that modify the momentum/averaging parameters.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

1.  The analysis of the energy function Er in Theorem 3 is interesting. It is observed that Er appears to decrease for restarting methods as well. Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical relationship between restarting and this energy function? Does restarting correspond to a specific choice of w(t) and η(t)?
2.  Regarding Algorithm 1, what is the intuition behind the update `ak = min(ak-1, βmax/k√s)`? It seems to enforce a non-increasing `ak` but the `βmax` term is not fully explained.
3.  The authors mention that achieving rates faster than O(1/t^2) requires higher-order methods like Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method. Have the authors considered whether the adaptive averaging idea could be applied in that context to potentially yield practical improvements?

In summary, the paper introduces a novel and intuitive adaptive heuristic for accelerated methods, which is its primary strength. However, the surrounding theoretical framework is an incremental extension of existing work, and the practical utility of the proposed algorithm is not yet established due to the lack of a discrete-time convergence proof and limited experimental evidence.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea of adaptive averaging is novel, elegant, and provides a new perspective on designing heuristics for accelerated methods.
*   The paper provides useful theoretical insights into the connection between averaging and acceleration in continuous-time dynamics.
*   Initial experimental results suggest the heuristic is promising and can outperform established restarting schemes in some settings.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The theoretical contribution beyond the heuristic itself is incremental.
*   The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) is not supported by a convergence proof, leaving its theoretical properties unclear.
*   The experiments are too preliminary (low-dimensional toy problems) to demonstrate a significant impact on the field.

I am currently leaning towards acceptance, conditional on the authors addressing the theoretical gap for the discrete algorithm and providing more compelling experimental results in the final version.
