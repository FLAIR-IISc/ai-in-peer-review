## Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper investigates adaptive averaging schemes within the framework of accelerated descent dynamics, building upon the ODE interpretation of Nesterov’s acceleration established by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The core idea is to design a heuristic that adaptively reweights iterates based on the rate of decrease of a Lyapunov function, aiming to accelerate convergence beyond standard fixed-schedule averaging. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for their approach, demonstrating it dominates fixed-schedule averaging and preserves the quadratic convergence rate in discrete-time. Empirical results across various functions suggest promising speed-ups.

The paper’s strength lies in its elegant extension of existing theory. The connection to the ODE perspective of accelerated methods is well-motivated, and the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic feels natural and intuitive. The theoretical analysis, while relying on prior work, is clearly presented and provides a solid foundation for the proposed method. The inclusion of both continuous and discrete-time analysis is commendable, and the empirical validation across a range of functions strengthens the claims. The authors also appropriately position their work relative to other adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam, highlighting the specific focus on accelerated schemes. 

However, the paper isn't without limitations. While the theoretical guarantees are valuable, the conditions for their applicability (specifically, the need for a smooth distance-generating function) might limit the method’s broad applicability. The analysis of the discretized process, while mentioned as future work, is crucial for fully understanding the practical convergence behavior and should ideally be included. Furthermore, the link to Conjugate Gradient for strongly convex quadratics, also suggested as future work, seems potentially significant and a brief exploration, even if preliminary, would add value. The numerical experiments, while encouraging, could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art optimizers beyond the listed heuristics.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and guarantees.
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating speed-ups.
*   Clear and concise writing, building upon established work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical assumptions.
*   Lack of analysis of the discretized process to confirm convergence rate preservation.
*   Potential for more comprehensive empirical evaluation.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a promising extension of existing techniques, and the theoretical and empirical results support its potential for practical impact. Addressing the suggested technical improvement (discretization analysis) would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
