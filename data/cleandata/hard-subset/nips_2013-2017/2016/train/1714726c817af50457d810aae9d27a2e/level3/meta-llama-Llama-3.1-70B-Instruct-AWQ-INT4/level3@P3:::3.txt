This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization in both continuous and discrete settings, building upon the work of Krichene et al. on primal-dual ODE approaches. The authors focus on the primal trajectory obtained by averaging the mirror of the dual variable and generalize the averaging schemes. They provide a unified study of the accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics using a Lyapunov argument, which yields sufficient conditions on the averaging coefficients to achieve a desired convergence rate.

The paper's main contributions include a new Lyapunov function that provides convergence rates faster than the usual 1/t^2 in the continuous setting, and an adaptive choice of averaging weights with provable convergence guarantees. The authors also propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that empirically compares favorably to existing restarting techniques known to improve accelerated methods.

The paper is well-written, and the continuous analysis is rigorous. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and the proposed approach, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with the topic. The paper also includes a thorough discussion of related work, although the reviewer notes that prior related work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors should be cited.

The reviewer finds the paper interesting, particularly the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic, which seems to perform at least as well as other heuristics for first-order accelerated methods and in some cases considerably better. The numerical experiments in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, and the authors provide a clear and detailed description of the algorithm and its implementation.

However, the reviewer notes that the theoretical analysis of the discrete version of the algorithm is omitted, which is a limitation of the paper. Additionally, the reviewer raises the open question of whether the improved continuous rates persist after discretization.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work. The paper is also well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with the topic.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a new approach to accelerated first-order methods, which is a novel combination of familiar techniques. The authors clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions and provide a thorough discussion of related work.

In terms of significance, the results in the paper are important, and other researchers are likely to use these ideas or build upon them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The paper provides a unique theoretical and pragmatic approach to accelerated first-order methods.
