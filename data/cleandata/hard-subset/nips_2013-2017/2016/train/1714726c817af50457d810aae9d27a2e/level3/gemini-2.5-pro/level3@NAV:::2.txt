This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated mirror descent (AMD), extending the ODE framework of Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The authors introduce a generalized averaging scheme for the primal variable and use a Lyapunov analysis to establish sufficient conditions for a desired convergence rate. The main contribution is a novel adaptive averaging heuristic for a discretized version of the dynamics, which aims to improve performance over existing heuristics like adaptive restarting.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its proposal of a novel adaptive averaging heuristic. The intuition behind adaptively adjusting the averaging weights to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function is clear and elegant. Preliminary experiments suggest it can outperform standard restarting techniques in certain settings. Additionally, the paper provides several reformulations (e.g., primal representation, connection to replicator dynamics) that contribute to a deeper theoretical understanding of these dynamics.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that temper its contribution. A major concern is the paper's limited originality and significance beyond the prior work of Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The core theoretical tools, including the Lyapunov function and the discretization strategy, are directly adopted from this earlier paper. The extension to a generalized averaging scheme feels incremental, as the analysis only meaningfully considers the established quadratic-rate case.

Crucially, the proposed adaptive averaging strategy, while intuitive, is not rigorously justified for the discrete-time algorithm. The authors acknowledge that approximations are made during discretization, and the paper lacks a formal convergence proof for this central contribution, rendering it an interesting but unsubstantiated heuristic. The empirical evaluation is also not convincing. Experiments are confined to a low-dimensional, toy problem with quadratic and linear objectives, which provides little evidence of the method's efficacy on complex, high-dimensional, or general convex problems relevant to machine learning. Furthermore, the paper fails to compare its adaptive scheme to other existing averaging schemes for Nesterov-style methods, making its practical advantage unclear.

In summary, while the paper introduces a novel heuristic, its theoretical contributions are incremental, and its central proposal lacks rigorous proof. The authors have committed to addressing these shortcomings post-rebuttal, but based on the submitted version, the limited empirical evidence and theoretical gaps make the work feel incomplete and its significance difficult to assess.
