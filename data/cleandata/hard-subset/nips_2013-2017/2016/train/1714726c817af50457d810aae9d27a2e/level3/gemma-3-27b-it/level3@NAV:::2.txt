## Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, focusing on a generalized averaging scheme within the Mirror Descent framework. The core contribution is a novel, adaptive averaging heuristic designed to speed up convergence by dynamically adjusting averaging weights based on the decrease of a Lyapunov function. The authors provide a continuous-time analysis, demonstrate quadratic convergence in discrete time, and present preliminary numerical experiments.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work, clearly situating itself within the lineage of research connecting Nesterov's accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs, notably referencing the seminal work of Su et al. [20] and their own earlier NIPS paper [11]. The extension of the averaging scheme from simple to generalized is acknowledged as straightforward, and the connections to replicator dynamics offer an interesting application. The authors appropriately acknowledge and build upon existing Lyapunov function-based analyses.

The strength of this work lies in the intuitive appeal of the adaptive averaging heuristic and the theoretical insights gained through its analysis. The energy function interpretation and primal reformulation contribute to a deeper understanding of the dynamics, even if their direct impact on machine learning applications is limited as the authors themselves concede. The identified connection to replicator dynamics is a nice theoretical result.

However, significant concerns regarding originality and practical impact temper this assessment. A substantial portion of the theoretical framework – the Lyapunov function approach and discretization schemes – directly mirrors the authors' prior NIPS publication [11]. The extension to generalized averaging appears incremental, and is largely focused on the quadratic rate case. While the adaptive averaging idea is novel, its theoretical grounding remains weak. The lack of a rigorous convergence proof beyond the continuous-time setting and the reliance on discretization approximations leaves it effectively as a heuristic. Furthermore, a critical missing component is a comparative analysis against other established averaging schemes used in conjunction with Nesterov-style algorithms. The experiments, limited to a toy quadratic/linear objective, are insufficient to demonstrate generalizability or practical advantage, particularly in strongly convex scenarios.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The adaptive averaging idea is novel and intuitively appealing. The analysis offers additional understanding of accelerated descent dynamics. 

**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of a strong theoretical guarantee (convergence proof), high reliance on prior work (particularly [11]), limited practical evaluation, and missing comparison to related work. The incremental nature of the contribution, given the strong dependence on prior publications, raises concerns about significance.

**Overall:** While the paper presents a worthwhile exploration of averaging schemes in accelerated descent, the limited novelty, weak theoretical guarantees, and lack of compelling experimental validation currently outweigh its strengths. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the post-rebuttal commitments, specifically focusing on expanding the experimental evaluation and establishing a robust convergence proof. A strong comparison to existing averaging schemes is also crucial.
