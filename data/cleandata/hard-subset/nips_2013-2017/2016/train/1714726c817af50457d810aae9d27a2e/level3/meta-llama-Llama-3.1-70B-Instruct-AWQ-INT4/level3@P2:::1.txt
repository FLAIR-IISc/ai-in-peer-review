The paper "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics" studies the problem of minimizing a convex function over a closed convex subset of Euclidean space. The authors use a continuous-time dynamics approach to analyze the convergence rate of accelerated descent methods. They provide a unified study of a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by two positive, continuous weight functions. The authors give sufficient conditions on these weight functions to achieve a desired convergence rate and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate.

The paper is well-written, and the mathematical argument is simple and easy to follow, given familiarity with the prior work of Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The technical development is a close extension of prior work but has large potential practical impact due to Nesterov's widespread use. The authors demonstrate that a small adaptive modification can yield dramatic improvements over fixed-weight Nesterov schemes.

The paper's main contribution is the design of an adaptive reweighting of iterates that is guaranteed to dominate the standard fixed-schedule averaging. The proposed heuristic is natural and simple to implement, making it a promising approach for accelerating convergence in a broad class of functions. The empirical evaluation shows promising speed-ups across a range of functions, including strongly convex quadratics, weakly convex quadratics, linear functions, and the Kullback-Leibler divergence.

The paper's strengths include its clear and concise writing style, the simplicity and elegance of the mathematical argument, and the potential practical impact of the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the continuous-time dynamics and provide sufficient conditions for the convergence rate.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of analysis of the discretized process. The authors should consider including an analysis of the discretized process to show that the convergence rate is preserved. Additionally, the authors may want to investigate links between the adaptive averaging and Conjugate Gradient for strongly convex quadratics, as suggested in the paper.

In terms of originality, the paper builds upon prior work but provides a significant extension and improvement over existing methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution that has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.

The paper's significance is high, as it addresses a fundamental problem in convex optimization and provides a new and improved approach for accelerating convergence. The results have the potential to be widely applicable and to make a significant impact in the field.

Overall, I would rate the paper as follows:

* Quality: 9/10
* Clarity: 9/10
* Originality: 8/10
* Significance: 9/10

I would recommend accepting the paper for publication in NIPS, pending minor revisions to address the potential weaknesses mentioned above.
