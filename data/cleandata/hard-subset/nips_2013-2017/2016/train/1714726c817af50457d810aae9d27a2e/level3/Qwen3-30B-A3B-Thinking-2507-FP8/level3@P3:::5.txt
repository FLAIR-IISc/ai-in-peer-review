

# Review: Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization using a Lyapunov function approach. The authors generalize mirror descent dynamics with a broad family of averaging schemes parameterized by weight functions w and η, where w is used in the averaging and η determines the rate at which the dual variable accumulates gradients. The key insight is that by properly choosing these weight functions, one can achieve a desired convergence rate. The paper builds on the authors' previous work (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015) which established a connection between acceleration and averaging in mirror descent, and relates to Su et al.'s (NIPS 2014) work linking Nesterov's accelerated method to continuous-time ODEs.

The main contribution is a unified Lyapunov function analysis that provides sufficient conditions on w and η to achieve a convergence rate of 1/r(t). The authors show that their framework recovers previous results (e.g., the quadratic rate for r(t)=t²) as special cases, and demonstrate how it can be applied to accelerate the replicator dynamics on the simplex. The most novel contribution is the adaptive averaging heuristic, which adaptively computes the weights w(t) to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function. This heuristic preserves the convergence rate guarantees while providing empirical improvements over existing methods.

The paper's theoretical contribution is solid. Theorem 2 provides clear sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing, and Corollary 1 gives a constructive method for choosing w and η to achieve a desired convergence rate. The adaptive averaging heuristic (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated and has clear theoretical justification. The empirical evaluation in Section 5 compares adaptive averaging to two restarting heuristics (gradient restart and speed restart) across several test problems on the simplex. The results consistently show that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as, and often better than, the restarting heuristics, with significant improvements in strongly convex cases.

Strengths:
- Theoretical foundation is strong with clear sufficient conditions for convergence rates
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and well-justified
- Empirical results are comprehensive and demonstrate clear advantages
- The paper builds logically from previous work while making meaningful contributions
- The connection to replicator dynamics on the simplex is insightful

Weaknesses:
- The paper could provide more intuition about why adaptive averaging works better than restarting
- The theoretical guarantees for the discrete-time algorithm are somewhat limited (only a convergence rate bound is provided)
- The experimental section could include more detailed comparisons with other adaptive methods

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs are clear and the empirical results are well-designed. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent and the examples help illustrate the general theory. The figures (particularly Figure 2) effectively communicate the empirical findings.

Originality: The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on accelerated optimization methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel approach that differs significantly from existing restarting heuristics. The connection between adaptive averaging and the Lyapunov function decrease provides a fresh perspective on acceleration.

Significance: The results have potential impact for practitioners using accelerated methods in constrained optimization. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a practical way to improve convergence without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. The paper's approach could be extended to other accelerated methods beyond mirror descent.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for constrained convex problems. The adaptive averaging heuristic is promising and appears to be a significant improvement over existing heuristics. While the paper has some minor weaknesses in the experimental design and theoretical scope, the overall contribution is strong and well-supported.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical contribution with well-justified sufficient conditions
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with strong empirical results
- Good connection to previous work on continuous-time dynamics
- Solid experimental evaluation across multiple problem types
- Preserves theoretical convergence guarantees while improving empirical performance

Arguments against acceptance:
- The theoretical analysis for the discrete-time algorithm is somewhat limited
- The adaptive averaging heuristic could benefit from more detailed theoretical analysis
- The scope is somewhat narrow (focused on mirror descent)

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on accelerated optimization methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is promising and appears to be a meaningful improvement over existing approaches. I recommend acceptance.
