This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization by extending the primal-dual ordinary differential equation (ODE) approach from Krichene et al. The central idea is to generalize the averaging scheme used to generate the primal trajectory. The authors provide a unified analysis for a broad family of dynamics, deriving sufficient conditions on the averaging weights and gradient accumulation rates to achieve a desired convergence rate of 1/r(t). Notably, this framework allows for the derivation of continuous-time convergence rates faster than the standard O(1/t^2). Building on this analysis, the paper introduces a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts weights to speed up convergence while provably maintaining the theoretical rate guarantees.

The paper's strengths are numerous. The continuous-time analysis is rigorous, elegant, and provides valuable insights into the connection between averaging and acceleration. The generalization of the averaging scheme is a significant conceptual contribution, unifying various dynamics within a single framework. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a compelling and original alternative to the popular restarting techniques, and its provable convergence guarantees are a key advantage. The empirical evaluation is well-executed, clearly demonstrating that the adaptive method performs competitively and often surpasses restarting heuristics, particularly on strongly convex problems. The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

Despite these strengths, there are some weaknesses. The manuscript would be improved by citing and discussing the extensive related work on inertial dynamics by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors, which offers a complementary perspective on continuous-time accelerated methods. The most significant limitation is the lack of a detailed theoretical analysis for the discrete-time version of the algorithm in the main body of the paper. While the continuous-time results are impressive, their ultimate significance hinges on whether the improved rates (faster than O(1/t^2)) can be preserved after discretization into a practical algorithm. This remains an important open question that the paper raises but does not fully address.

In summary, this is a high-quality, interesting, and technically sound paper that advances our understanding of accelerated optimization. The continuous-time analysis is a clear highlight, though the work would be strengthened by a more thorough treatment of its discrete-time implications and a broader contextualization with related literature.
