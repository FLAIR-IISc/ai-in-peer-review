This paper studies a generalized class of accelerated mirror descent dynamics, framed as a system of continuous-time Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs). Building upon prior work connecting Nesterov's methods to second-order ODEs (Su et al., 2014; Krichene et al., 2015), the authors formulate these dynamics using a generalized averaging scheme, parameterized by an averaging weight function `w(t)` and a learning rate `η(t)`. The central theoretical result is a Lyapunov-based analysis that derives sufficient conditions on these functions to guarantee a target convergence rate `1/r(t)`. Based on this framework, the paper proposes a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic that dynamically adjusts the weights to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, aiming to improve empirical performance while preserving the theoretical convergence rate.

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength is its elegant and insightful theoretical contribution. The unified analysis of a broad class of accelerated dynamics via a single Lyapunov function is a powerful and welcome generalization. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant and novel idea. Unlike popular restarting schemes (O'Donoghue & Candès, 2013), which often lack formal guarantees outside of specific problem classes, this adaptive method is guaranteed by construction to preserve the `1/r(t)` convergence rate. This provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for an empirical speed-up technique. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and presents a complete piece of work with both a solid theoretical foundation and promising numerical experiments that compare favorably against established restarting methods.

**Weaknesses:**
The main weakness lies in the scope of the experimental validation. While the experiments on the simplex in R^3 are illustrative and provide valuable visualization, they are limited to low-dimensional, synthetic problems. The claims about the heuristic's practical advantages would be significantly more compelling if supported by results on larger-scale problems more typical of modern machine learning applications. Furthermore, while the continuous-time analysis is rigorous, the connection to the discrete-time algorithm could be strengthened with a more detailed derivation or a discrete version of the Lyapunov analysis to make the guarantees for the proposed algorithm more explicit.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, theoretically-grounded perspective on accelerating first-order optimization. The concept of adaptive averaging with rate-preservation guarantees is an important contribution to the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant theoretical framework for a broad class of accelerated ODEs.
*   Introduces an adaptive heuristic with stronger theoretical guarantees than popular restarting schemes.
*   The work is technically sound, clearly written, and connects well with existing literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Empirical validation is limited to low-dimensional toy problems, which may not reflect performance on larger-scale tasks.
