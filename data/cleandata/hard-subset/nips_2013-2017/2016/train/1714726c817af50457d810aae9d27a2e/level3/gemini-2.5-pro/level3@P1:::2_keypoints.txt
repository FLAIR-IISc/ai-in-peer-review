- Paper studies a family of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent ODEs parameterized by η(t) and w(t), using a Lyapunov analysis to get O(1/r(t)) rates.  
- Main contribution is a discrete-time adaptive averaging heuristic that greedily decreases the Lyapunov function to (empirically) accelerate convergence.  
- Continuous-time analysis and derivation of sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) are technically sound and well-presented.  
- Addition of a second energy function (Theorem 3) and primal-space representations (e.g., accelerated replicator dynamics) improve theoretical insight.  
- The discrete Algorithm 1 is presented as a heuristic inspired by the ODE, but lacks a formal convergence proof in discrete time.  
- The adaptive rule uses the proxy condition f(x̃(k+1)) - f(x̃(k)) > 0 for ⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩ > 0, and the impact of this approximation is not analyzed.  
- Some algorithmic choices (e.g., a_k = min(a_{k-1}, ...)) appear ad-hoc and are not directly motivated by the continuous theory.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, with useful illustrations (Figure 1), but the cont.-to-discrete connection needs more explanation.  
- Originality is mixed: adaptive averaging is novel and clever, but the analytical framework is largely incremental from the authors' prior work.  
- The focus on recovering O(1/t^2) rates limits novelty since faster rates would require different (higher-order) methods.  
- Empirical validation is weak: experiments are limited to toy 3D problems, so practical benefits on large-scale/high-dimensional tasks are unclear.  
- The paper omits comparisons to relevant averaging-based acceleration methods (other than restarting heuristics).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel adaptive averaging idea, elegant continuous-time analysis, and preliminary promising experiments.  
- Arguments for rejection: high incrementality, lack of discrete-time theoretical guarantees for the main algorithm, weak experiments, and missing relevant baselines.
