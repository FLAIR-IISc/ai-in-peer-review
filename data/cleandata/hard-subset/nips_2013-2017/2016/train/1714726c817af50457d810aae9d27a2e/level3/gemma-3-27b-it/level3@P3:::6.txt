This paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated descent dynamics in constrained convex optimization. The core idea is to analyze the continuous-time dynamics as a coupling of dual and primal variables, and to demonstrate that carefully chosen averaging schemes can preserve the original quadratic convergence rate for non-strongly convex objectives. The authors provide a Lyapunov argument for sufficient conditions and experimentally validate their approach against existing restarting heuristics. While the core idea is interesting and potentially valuable, the presentation suffers from clarity issues and a limited scope of experimental validation.

The work builds upon recent research connecting Nesterov’s accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs [20, 11, 23]. The authors aim to extend this connection by exploring different averaging schemes and proposing an adaptive method that doesn’t sacrifice the established convergence rate – a weakness of existing restarting heuristics [17, 20]. This connection to prior work is adequately acknowledged, though a more detailed discussion of the theoretical limitations of existing heuristics beyond simply stating the lack of convergence guarantees would strengthen the motivation.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The Lyapunov argument presented appears sound, and the established sufficient conditions for preserving the quadratic convergence rate are a significant contribution. The connection between the averaging scheme and the convergence rate is clearly articulated mathematically.
* **Novelty:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel approach to accelerating descent dynamics, offering a potential advantage over existing methods by maintaining the theoretical convergence rate.
* **Connection to Replicator Dynamics:** The application and acceleration of the replicator dynamics is a nice example demonstrating the broader applicability of the proposed framework.
* **Potential Significance:** If scalable, this method could improve the efficiency of first-order optimization methods, which are crucial for large-scale machine learning problems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity:** The paper is difficult to follow. The introduction jumps quickly into technical details without adequately motivating the problem or providing sufficient background on accelerated methods and mirror descent. Many abbreviations (e.g., AMD, ODE) are used without initial definition, hindering understanding.
* **Intuition:** The equations between lines 33-34 and 46-47 lack intuitive explanation. What do these equations *mean* in terms of the optimization process? What is the geometric interpretation of the mirror map and its gradient? Explaining these concepts more clearly would greatly improve accessibility.
* **Relation to Adaptive Methods:** The discussion of relation to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization (Adagrad, Adam) is too brief. While the authors mention these methods, a more in-depth comparison – highlighting the differences in their objectives and applicability – would be beneficial. Are there potential synergies between adaptive averaging and methods like Adam?
* **Scalability:** The experiments are conducted in R^3, a toy setup. This severely limits the practical significance of the results. The authors need to provide evidence of scalability to higher dimensions. While computational cost is a valid concern, even experiments in R^10 or R^20 would offer more confidence in the method’s viability.
* **Figure 1:** The reference to the solid line in Figure 1 is ambiguous. Using a dotted line or distinct colors to represent the primal trajectory would improve clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The theoretical contribution regarding Lyapunov stability and convergence rate preservation is valuable.
* The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel approach with potential benefits.
* The connection to the replicator dynamics demonstrates a broader applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of clarity and intuitive explanation makes the paper difficult to understand.
* The limited experimental validation casts doubt on the practical significance of the method.
* The superficial discussion of related work in adaptive optimization limits the broader impact of the contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but its impact is limited by clarity issues and a lack of compelling experimental evidence. The work, in its current state, feels incomplete. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the aforementioned weaknesses. Specifically, the authors should prioritize: (1) significantly improving the clarity and accessibility of the paper, (2) providing more intuitive explanations of the key equations and concepts, (3) expanding the experimental validation to higher dimensions, and (4) elaborating on the relationship to existing adaptive optimization methods. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its potential for positive impact.
