- Introduces a generalized Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) continuous-time framework parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t) with convergence conditions via a Lyapunov argument.  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively chooses weights to accelerate Lyapunov decrease and provably dominates fixed-schedule averaging.  
- Shows the adaptive averaging preserves the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods.  
- Provides a unified theoretical framework linking continuous-time dynamics and accelerated optimization methods, extending Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015).  
- Mathematical development and Lyapunov analysis are rigorous and clearly presented.  
- Properly situates the work relative to prior continuous-time dynamics (e.g., Su et al. 2014) and adaptive restarting heuristics (O'Donoghue & Candès 2015).  
- Demonstrates acceleration of replicator dynamics as a compelling application example.  
- Empirical results show adaptive averaging performs as well as or better than existing heuristics across several convex problems (strong/weak quadratics, linear, KL divergence).  
- Experiments are well-designed with clear visualizations and comparisons to relevant baselines.  
- Main theoretical weakness: limited analysis of the discrete-time counterpart; discrete guarantees largely relegated to supplementary material.  
- Main empirical weakness: experimental scope is relatively narrow, focusing mainly on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems (R3).  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, original in its adaptive averaging guarantee, practically relevant, and suitable for NIPS.
