

This paper extends the ODE-based accelerated mirror descent framework introduced in Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016) by introducing a generalized averaging scheme parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t). The authors provide sufficient conditions for convergence using a Lyapunov argument and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to dynamically compute weights to accelerate convergence. The approach is validated on the replicator dynamics (accelerated simplex-constrained optimization) and compared to existing restarting heuristics.

The paper's main strengths lie in its clear connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete optimization algorithms, the intuitive adaptive averaging heuristic (which increases weights when the trajectory makes progress), and the interesting application to replicator dynamics. The energy function interpretation (Theorem 3) provides valuable physical intuition about the system. The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate that adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics in some cases, particularly for strongly convex problems.

However, the paper has significant limitations that affect its contribution. The core technical contribution—generalized averaging—is trivially extendable from the authors' prior work (Krichene et al., NIPS 2016). The analysis only considers the quadratic convergence rate (r(t)=t²), while faster rates require higher-order methods that are not discussed. The adaptive averaging heuristic lacks theoretical grounding: there is no explicit convergence proof for the discrete implementation, and the paper doesn't establish how the adaptive heuristic compares to other existing adaptive methods for accelerated optimization (e.g., Adagrad or Adam). The experiments are limited to simple quadratic/linear objectives in R³, with no comparison to other adaptive averaging schemes, making it difficult to assess the practical advantage.

The paper's originality is questionable given the close relationship to prior NIPS work. While the adaptive averaging idea is novel, the paper doesn't clearly establish how it differs from or improves upon existing approaches. The extension from simple to generalized averaging is straightforward and of limited theoretical or practical significance. The significance is further diminished by the narrow scope of experiments and lack of broader applicability demonstration.

The quality of the theoretical analysis is sound within the restricted scope, but the discretization details (Algorithm 1) lack rigorous convergence guarantees. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the adaptive heuristic differs from existing restarting strategies. The clarity is generally good, with well-structured arguments, though the connection to prior work could be more explicit.

The significance of the work is limited by the narrow scope of the analysis and experiments. While the adaptive averaging heuristic shows promise in specific cases, the paper doesn't establish it as a general improvement over existing methods. The claim that it "performs at least as well as adaptive restarting" is not convincingly supported by the limited experiments.

Pro: Clear connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms; Intuitive adaptive averaging heuristic; Interesting replicator dynamics application.

Con: Trivial extension of prior work; Only quadratic rate considered; No convergence proof for adaptive heuristic; Limited experiments (low-dimensional, simple objectives); No comparison to other adaptive methods.

The paper's main weakness is its lack of theoretical grounding for the adaptive averaging heuristic. While the continuous-time analysis is sound, the discrete implementation lacks rigorous guarantees, making it effectively a heuristic without theoretical justification. The experiments don't convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods in more challenging settings.

The authors' post-rebuttal commitments (additional high-dimensional experiments, convergence proof, implementation on cubic-regularized Newton method) would substantially strengthen the paper, but these are not included in the current submission. Without these, the paper's contribution is marginal compared to the prior NIPS work it builds upon.

In conclusion, while the adaptive averaging heuristic has intuitive appeal and shows promise in limited experiments, the paper fails to establish a significant theoretical contribution or broad practical advantage over existing methods. The technical extension is trivial, the analysis is restricted to a single convergence rate, and the experimental validation is insufficient. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive theoretical analysis and broader experimental validation to establish its significance.
