This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, proposing an adaptive averaging heuristic to improve convergence rates. The authors establish sufficient conditions for convergence and demonstrate, through both theoretical analysis and numerical experiments, that their method preserves the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods while performing favorably compared to adaptive restarting heuristics. The work builds on the connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs, expanding on prior work by Krichene et al. (2016) and Su et al. (2016).

The core idea is to formulate accelerated mirror descent as a coupling of dual and primal variables, controlled by weight functions η(t) and w(t). The authors derive conditions on these weights to achieve a desired convergence rate, and then introduce a heuristic for adaptively computing w(t) based on the Lyapunov function’s decrease. This adaptive averaging aims to improve convergence by dynamically adjusting the averaging scheme. A significant contribution is demonstrating that this adaptation preserves the original convergence rate, a guarantee often lacking in existing heuristics like adaptive restarting (O’Donoghue & Candès, 2016; Su et al., 2016).

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, utilizing a Lyapunov argument to establish convergence guarantees. The derived conditions on η and w are clear and mathematically well-defined.
* **Novelty:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution, offering a different approach to accelerating descent dynamics. The preservation of the original convergence rate is a particularly valuable theoretical result.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively builds upon existing research connecting accelerated methods to continuous-time dynamics, providing a logical extension of previous findings.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging heuristic, showing at least comparable performance to, and often improvements over, existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Accessibility:** The paper is dense and challenging to follow. The introduction of numerous variables and equations without sufficient contextualization hinders understanding, particularly for readers not deeply familiar with the field. Expanding on background material related to mirror descent and Lyapunov functions would be beneficial. A glossary of abbreviations and a more intuitive explanation of terms would also improve readability.
* **Equation Explanation:** Equations between lines 33-34 and 46-47 lack intuitive explanation. While mathematically precise, they could benefit from a more descriptive breakdown of each term and its role in the overall dynamics.
* **Limited Scalability Evidence:** The numerical experiments are limited to R^3, a relatively low-dimensional setting. This raises concerns about the scalability of the proposed method to higher-dimensional problems, which are common in machine learning applications. Demonstrating performance on larger datasets or higher-dimensional problems is crucial to establish practical significance.
* **Related Work Discussion:** While related work is cited, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of connections to adaptive methods commonly used in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011) and Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014). A clear delineation of how this work differs from and extends these methods would strengthen the contribution.
* **Figure Clarity:** Figure 1 is somewhat ambiguous. The reference to the "solid line" is unclear, and it would be helpful to use a dotted line or different colors to clearly distinguish the primal trajectory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to accelerating descent dynamics.
* The preservation of the convergence rate is a significant theoretical result.
* The empirical results support the effectiveness of the proposed heuristic.
* The work builds upon a strong foundation of existing research in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper’s clarity and accessibility are limited, potentially hindering widespread adoption.
* The lack of scalability evidence raises concerns about practical significance.
* The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The theoretical results are strong, and the adaptive averaging heuristic shows promise. However, the limited clarity and scalability evidence are significant weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should focus on improving the paper’s readability by providing more background material, clarifying explanations of equations, and discussing connections to related work in non-convex optimization. Additionally, demonstrating the method's performance on higher-dimensional problems would substantially strengthen the paper’s impact. Addressing these points would greatly enhance the paper’s accessibility and solidify its contribution to the NIPS community.
