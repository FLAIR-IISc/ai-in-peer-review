

# Review for "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending the authors' prior work on accelerated mirror descent dynamics (Krichene et al., NIPS 2016). The authors propose a generalized averaging scheme for continuous-time accelerated mirror descent dynamics, characterized by two weight functions η(t) and w(t), and introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights to accelerate convergence. The paper demonstrates that adaptive averaging preserves the quadratic convergence rate and shows empirical improvements over existing restarting heuristics in simple examples.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:** The paper builds directly on the authors' NIPS 2016 work (Krichene et al.) which established a connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and a family of continuous-time ODEs. The current paper generalizes this framework by introducing a more flexible averaging scheme (parameterized by w(t)) and proposes an adaptive heuristic for computing these weights. While the continuous-time analysis is well-motivated, the paper's main contribution appears to be the adaptation of this framework to a discrete-time setting with the proposed heuristic.

**Strengths:**
1. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and intuitively appealing, as it increases weights on trajectory portions that make progress.
2. The paper provides a clear unified framework for accelerated mirror descent dynamics using Lyapunov analysis.
3. The connection to replicator dynamics (for simplex-constrained problems) is interesting and well-explained.
4. The continuous-time analysis is technically sound with proper Lyapunov function construction.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Originality:** The core framework (including the Lyapunov function) directly follows the authors' prior NIPS 2016 paper. The extension to generalized averaging is trivial (simply replacing the fixed weight with a function), and the main novelty (adaptive averaging) is not sufficiently distinct from the framework already established.
   
2. **Theoretical Gaps:** The discrete-time analysis lacks a convergence proof for adaptive averaging. The paper only provides continuous-time guarantees and relies on the discrete-time heuristic without establishing a formal convergence rate for the proposed algorithm. This makes the adaptive averaging effectively a heuristic rather than a theoretically grounded method.

3. **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments are restricted to simple toy problems (R^3) with quadratic/linear objectives. The paper does not demonstrate performance on more challenging, higher-dimensional problems or non-quadratic objectives. The claim of "significant improvements in some cases" is not convincingly supported by the limited experiments.

4. **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The paper does not compare adaptive averaging to other existing adaptive averaging schemes for Nesterov-style algorithms, making it difficult to assess the practical advantage over alternatives.

5. **Narrow Scope:** The paper focuses exclusively on the quadratic convergence rate (r(t)=t²), while faster rates require higher-order methods (not considered here). This limits the applicability of the results to more general optimization settings.

**Quality:** The continuous-time analysis is technically sound, but the discrete-time adaptation lacks sufficient theoretical grounding. The experimental validation is too limited to support the paper's claims about empirical advantages.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with precise mathematical formulations. The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is well-explained.

**Originality:** While the adaptive averaging idea has merit, the paper's core framework builds directly on the authors' prior NIPS 2016 work. The extension to generalized averaging is trivial, and the main contribution is not sufficiently novel compared to existing literature.

**Significance:** The results offer limited advancement for machine learning practice. The adaptive averaging heuristic has some empirical benefits, but without theoretical guarantees and limited experimental validation, its significance is questionable. The connection to replicator dynamics is interesting but does not represent a substantial advancement for machine learning applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is intuitively appealing and has potential practical value.
- The paper provides a unified framework that clarifies connections between accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics.
- The extension to replicator dynamics is a nice application example.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The main contribution is not sufficiently novel compared to the authors' prior work.
- The discrete-time analysis lacks a convergence proof, making the method theoretically incomplete.
- The experimental validation is too limited to support the paper's claims.
- The paper does not compare to other adaptive averaging schemes, making the practical advantage unclear.

**Overall Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting heuristic with some empirical benefits, but the lack of theoretical grounding for the discrete-time method, limited experimental validation, and lack of sufficient novelty compared to prior work make it a borderline submission. The authors' post-rebuttal commitments to add more experiments, provide a convergence proof, and compare to other adaptive schemes would address many of the weaknesses. However, based on the current submission, the paper does not make a sufficiently strong contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS. It would be more appropriate for a workshop or a venue with lower theoretical bar.
