

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending the primal-dual ODE framework introduced by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) and Su et al. (NIPS 2014). The authors propose a generalized averaging scheme for Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, characterized by two weight functions η(t) and w(t), and provide sufficient conditions for achieving desired convergence rates using a Lyapunov argument. The paper makes several contributions: (1) it generalizes the averaging scheme beyond previous work; (2) it derives a continuous-time adaptive heuristic for computing weights w(t) that preserves convergence rates; (3) it shows how the replicator dynamics (a mirror descent variant on the simplex) can be accelerated; and (4) it empirically demonstrates that adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics.

The paper's main strength lies in its rigorous continuous-time analysis. Theorem 2 provides clear sufficient conditions for achieving convergence rates of 1/r(t), and the Lyapunov function approach is well-motivated and technically sound. The connection between the continuous-time dynamics and the replicator equation in evolutionary game theory is particularly interesting and well-explained. The paper is clearly written, with well-structured arguments and helpful examples that illustrate the theoretical results. The numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging to restarting heuristics are thorough and demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple problem types, including strongly convex quadratics, weakly convex quadratics, linear functions, and KL divergence minimization.

However, the paper has two significant weaknesses. First, the discrete-time analysis of the proposed adaptive averaging algorithm is omitted, despite the paper's focus on optimization algorithms that are typically implemented discretely. The theoretical guarantees for the continuous-time dynamics do not automatically carry over to discrete implementations, which is a critical gap for an optimization paper. The authors mention a discrete version in Algorithm 1 but provide no convergence guarantees for it, making it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed heuristic.

Second, the paper fails to cite relevant prior work on continuous-time accelerated methods. The work of Attouch, Peypouquet, and Redont (e.g., "Fast convergence of an inertial gradient-like system with vanishing viscosity," 2015; "Fast convex optimization via inertial dynamics with Hessian driven damping," 2016) establishes similar continuous-time interpretations of accelerated methods and should be acknowledged. While the paper mentions Su et al.'s work, it does not situate its contributions within this broader literature, which weakens the novelty claim.

The significance of the paper is moderate. The continuous-time analysis provides valuable theoretical insights into accelerated methods, and the adaptive averaging heuristic shows empirical promise. However, the lack of discrete-time guarantees limits its practical impact, as most optimization practitioners work with discrete algorithms. The paper does not establish whether the improved continuous-time rates persist after discretization, which is a critical open question.

Strengths:
- Rigorous continuous-time analysis with clear sufficient conditions
- Well-written and well-organized presentation
- Interesting connection to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory
- Comprehensive empirical comparison showing adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics
- Theoretical guarantees for the continuous-time adaptive heuristic

Weaknesses:
- Missing discrete-time analysis for the proposed algorithm
- Failure to cite relevant prior work on continuous-time accelerated methods
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations
- No theoretical analysis of the discrete version of the algorithm

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a theoretically grounded contribution to understanding accelerated optimization methods. While the continuous-time analysis is strong, the omission of discrete-time guarantees represents a significant gap. The authors should address these concerns before publication.

Pro-acceptance arguments: The continuous-time analysis is rigorous and the adaptive heuristic is well-motivated. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of accelerated methods.

Con-acceptance arguments: The lack of discrete-time analysis is a critical weakness for an optimization paper. The failure to cite relevant prior work undermines the novelty claim. Without discrete-time guarantees, the practical impact of the proposed method remains unclear.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that would benefit from addressing the discrete-time analysis and properly situating the work within the existing literature on continuous-time accelerated methods. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should add a discrete-time convergence analysis and properly cite the relevant prior work.
