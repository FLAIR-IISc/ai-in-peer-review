

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending Krichene et al.'s primal-dual ODE framework. The authors introduce a generalized averaging scheme for the primal trajectory obtained by weighting the mirror of the dual variable, providing sufficient conditions for convergence rates in continuous time. Theorem 2 establishes convergence rates faster than the usual 1/t² under appropriate conditions on the averaging coefficients. The authors propose an adaptive weighting heuristic that provably preserves the convergence rate and empirically demonstrate its superiority over existing restarting techniques.

The paper makes several significant contributions. The continuous-time analysis is rigorous and well-executed, with clear connections to prior work on accelerated methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a novel approach to improving convergence rates without sacrificing theoretical guarantees, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness across multiple optimization problems. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides valuable insights into the relationship between continuous-time dynamics and discrete optimization algorithms.

However, the paper has notable limitations. The theoretical analysis of the discrete version of the algorithm is omitted, which is a significant gap given that the ultimate application of these methods is in discrete optimization. The authors should explicitly cite prior related work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors, as noted in the key points. The open question regarding whether the improved continuous-time rates persist after discretization remains unaddressed, which is a critical limitation for practical applications.

The paper's significance is substantial for optimization researchers, as it provides a principled approach to adaptive acceleration that could improve practical implementations of accelerated methods. While the continuous-time analysis is strong, the absence of discrete-time guarantees limits the immediate practical impact. The authors should address these limitations in future work.

Overall, this is a well-written, interesting paper with strong continuous-time analysis. The main weakness is the lack of discrete-time theoretical guarantees, which prevents it from fully realizing its potential impact. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing this gap and citing the relevant prior work. Given the importance of acceleration techniques in modern optimization, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical gaps.
