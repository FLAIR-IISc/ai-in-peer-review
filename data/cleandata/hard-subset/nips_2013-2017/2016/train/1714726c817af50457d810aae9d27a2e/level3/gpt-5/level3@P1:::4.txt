This paper studies a broad class of accelerated mirror-descent (AMD) dynamics with generalized averaging, unifying and extending recent ODE views of acceleration. The authors model acceleration as the coupling of a dual state Z accumulating gradients at rate η(t) and a primal state X obtained by averaging a mirrored trajectory with weights w(t). They introduce a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) with a target rate schedule r(t), and derive simple sufficient conditions on (η,w) ensuring Lr is nonincreasing, yielding convergence f(X(t))−f* ≤ O(1/r(t)). This recovers the classical O(1/t^2) rate for r(t)=t^2 and includes faster schedules r(t)=t^p. They also present a primal form of the dynamics and show, on the simplex, that the replicator equation admits an “accelerated” version under their framework. Motivated by the Lyapunov bound, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses a(t)=w/W on the fly to make the Lyapunov derivative as negative as possible while preserving the rate. They prove guarantees in continuous time and argue that a discrete version maintains the quadratic rate of first-order accelerated methods. Experiments (mostly low dimensional, simplex-constrained) compare adaptive averaging to adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient and speed restart) and indicate consistent improvements, sometimes significantly so.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on the ODE perspectives connecting Nesterov’s methods to continuous-time dynamics (Su-Boyd-Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan) and prior AMD work (Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett), while addressing empirical heuristics such as adaptive restarting (O’Donoghue–Candès). The generalized averaging viewpoint unifies several schedules and offers a direct Lyapunov route to guarantees that restarting lacks outside special cases. The accelerated replicator instance connects to classical mirror descent with entropy and evolutionary dynamics.

Quality: The continuous-time analysis is technically sound. Existence/uniqueness (Theorem 1) is standard under the Lipschitz assumptions, with the subtle but correct choice of t0>0 to avoid w/W blow-up. The Lyapunov derivative calculation (inequality (3)) is clear and the two key conditions in Theorem 2 are transparent: η≥r′ and a sign condition involving ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w). Corollary 1 shows a constructive, scale-invariant recipe via a(t)=w/W and η=ar. The examples r(t)=t^p are consistent. The additional “energy” function Er that decreases under the same conditions is insightful and aligns with mechanical analogies. The primal forms are correctly derived and make the accelerated replicator dynamics explicit. The adaptive heuristic is simple: increase a(t) beyond η(t)r(t) when the inner product allows further decrease of Lr. In discrete time, the proposed algorithm and its rate claim are delegated to the supplement; while plausible (given the discrete Lyapunov), the main text would benefit from a brief statement of the discrete theorem and assumptions. Empirical methodology is reasonable, though limited in scale.

Clarity: The exposition is generally clear and well organized. The introduction gives appropriate context. A notational suggestion: define a(t)=w/W early and consistently use it to reduce clutter; emphasize that “quadratic rate” refers to r(t)=t^2 (continuous-time O(1/t^2), translating to O(1/k^2) under the provided discretization). Clarify the role of β in η(t)=βt and provide guidance on choosing β in practice. The dependence of condition (2) in Theorem 2 on the trajectory is acknowledged; giving an intuitive geometric interpretation (directional progress vs. averaging gain) would help readers. A small schematic explaining how constant a(t) segments imply exponential growth in w(t) would make Section 4 more digestible.

Originality: The paper’s main novelty is (i) the generalized averaging family with a clean Lyapunov-based prescription for (η,w) to realize a desired rate and (ii) the adaptive averaging heuristic that provably preserves the target rate while aiming to decrease the Lyapunov function faster. The accelerated replicator instance and the additional decreasing energy are also nice additions. While closely related to the variational ODE view, the averaging lens is distinct and provides new, actionable design degrees of freedom.

Significance: The framework could influence how we design and tune accelerated methods, especially under constraints and mirror maps. Having an adaptive mechanism with guarantees that competes with (and sometimes improves on) restarting is valuable. The accelerated replicator dynamics may find use in game-theoretic and simplex-constrained applications. That said, current empirical evidence is small-scale; broader benchmarks (high-dimensional ML tasks, composite objectives, stochastic/noisy gradients) would strengthen the practical impact. Also, the paper does not yet deliver improved asymptotic rates in strongly convex settings; demonstrating or disproving that potential is an interesting direction.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, unified continuous-time analysis yielding simple sufficient conditions for targeted rates.
- Elegant and novel adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantees (rate preserved) and empirical gains over popular restarting methods.
- Useful primal representations and an appealing accelerated replicator example on the simplex.
- Additional decreasing energy function that aids understanding and diagnostics.

Cons for acceptance:
- The key trajectory-dependent condition in Theorem 2 is not verifiable a priori; guarantees rely on adaptive enforcement rather than global properties.
- Discrete-time theory for the adaptive scheme is mostly in the supplement; the main text lacks a concise theorem and assumptions.
- Experiments are limited in scale and scope (mostly 3D simplex); no tests on large-scale or stochastic problems, nor composite/nonsmooth settings.
- Practical guidance on hyperparameters (β, step sizes) and sensitivity analyses are minimal.

Suggestions for improvement:
- State a main-text discrete-time theorem (assumptions, rate) for Algorithm 1 and briefly sketch the Lyapunov argument.
- Provide more extensive experiments: higher dimensions, different constraint sets and mirror maps, composite objectives, and stochastic gradients.
- Explore strongly convex settings: can adaptive averaging achieve linear rates with clear conditions?
- Offer heuristics or adaptive rules for choosing β and step sizes; report sensitivity.
- Release code to facilitate adoption.

Overall assessment: A solid and well-executed theoretical contribution with a simple, principled heuristic that competes favorably with widely used restarts. While the empirical section is limited, the ideas are clean and likely to be influential. I recommend acceptance (leaning to weak accept given the scope of experiments).
