This paper presents a novel adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated descent dynamics, building on the ODE interpretation of Nesterov’s acceleration established in prior work (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015). The core idea is to adaptively reweight iterates based on a Lyapunov function’s decrease, offering a theoretical guarantee of preserving the quadratic convergence rate. The authors demonstrate promising speed-ups in numerical experiments across various functions.

The paper's strength lies in its elegant theoretical foundation. The Lyapunov argument provides a solid justification for the proposed heuristic, distinguishing it from previous adaptive averaging approaches that lacked such guarantees. The connection to the continuous-time ODE framework offers valuable insights into the behavior of accelerated methods and allows for a more principled design of adaptive schemes. The mathematical presentation is generally clear and accessible, particularly for readers familiar with Krichene et al. (2015). Extending the prior work to adaptive weights while maintaining the convergence rate is a significant accomplishment. The empirical results, while not overwhelmingly dramatic, consistently show performance at least comparable to adaptive restarting, with improvements observed in some cases. The inclusion of the replicator dynamics example illustrates the applicability of the framework beyond standard optimization.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is strong, it remains relatively confined to the established continuous-time framework. The authors themselves suggest analyzing the discretized process to demonstrate rate preservation, a crucial step to solidify the practical relevance of the method. This analysis is currently only mentioned in the supplementary material. The numerical experiments, while informative, are somewhat limited in scope. Expanding the experiments to include larger datasets, more complex functions, and comparisons with state-of-the-art optimization algorithms (e.g., Adam) would strengthen the empirical validation.  The connection to Conjugate Gradient, also suggested by the authors, could be further explored. A more detailed discussion of why the proposed method performs better (or worse) than other adaptive methods in specific scenarios would be beneficial. Finally, the presentation could be improved by adding a figure illustrating the adaptive weighting scheme in action – visualizing how the weights change during optimization would enhance intuition.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel adaptive averaging heuristic grounded in a sound theoretical framework. While building on prior work, the adaptation of weights based on the Lyapunov function’s derivative is a new contribution.  The authors clearly differentiate their approach from existing adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam, highlighting its specific focus on accelerating first-order methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the Lyapunov analysis and the connection to established results on accelerated gradient methods. The numerical experiments, though limited, provide evidence supporting the efficacy of the proposed heuristic. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work and suggest directions for future research. The paper feels complete, presenting a cohesive argument and supporting evidence.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors provide sufficient background information and definitions. The notation is consistent.  However, some sections, particularly those dealing with the mathematical derivations, could benefit from additional explanations and intuitive interpretations.

**Significance:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic has the potential to significantly impact the practical application of accelerated descent methods. The theoretical guarantee of preserving the convergence rate is a key advantage. The simplicity of the heuristic makes it easy to implement and integrate into existing optimization pipelines. The method addresses a difficult problem – how to dynamically adjust parameters in accelerated methods – in a principled and effective manner. It advances the state-of-the-art by providing a robust and theoretically sound adaptive scheme.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation based on Lyapunov analysis.
*   Preservation of convergence rate guarantee.
*   Novel adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   Promising empirical results.
*   Clear and generally accessible presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of numerical experiments.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of the discretized process in the main text.
*   Potential for further exploration of connections to Conjugate Gradient.
*   The practical improvements over existing heuristics, while present, are not always dramatic.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical insights and the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic are significant and warrant acceptance. Addressing the suggested improvements (particularly a more comprehensive analysis of the discrete process and expanded experiments) would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
