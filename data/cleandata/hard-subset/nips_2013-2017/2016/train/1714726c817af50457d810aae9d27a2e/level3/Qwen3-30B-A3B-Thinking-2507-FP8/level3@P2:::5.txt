

# Review for "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

The paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a continuous-time framework. It builds upon recent NIPS work by Su et al. (NIPS 2014) and Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) that established connections between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs. The authors introduce a generalized Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), where the primal variable is obtained as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory. Using a Lyapunov argument, they provide sufficient conditions on η and w to achieve desired convergence rates, and demonstrate how this framework can accelerate the replicator dynamics for optimization on the simplex.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it establishes a unified theoretical framework for accelerated mirror descent dynamics using a Lyapunov function, extending previous work by providing conditions under which convergence rates of the form 1/r(t) can be guaranteed. Second, it proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes the weights w(t) to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function. The authors prove that this heuristic preserves the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods in discrete time. Third, they provide numerical experiments showing that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as, and sometimes significantly better than, existing heuristics like adaptive restarting.

The paper excels in Quality. The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear theorems and proofs establishing the conditions under which the Lyapunov function decreases. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and the connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is clearly articulated. The experimental section provides comprehensive comparisons with existing methods across multiple problem types (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, and KL divergence), with well-chosen visualizations of the trajectory behavior.

The paper is Clear in its presentation. The mathematical derivations are well-organized, with careful definitions of key concepts (e.g., the Bregman divergence, the mirror map). The connection between the continuous-time dynamics and the discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-explained, and the supplementary material provides helpful additional details. The paper effectively demonstrates how the adaptive averaging heuristic can be implemented in practice.

The work demonstrates Significant Originality. While previous work has studied accelerated descent dynamics (Su et al., NIPS 2014; Krichene et al., NIPS 2015), this paper generalizes the framework by introducing adaptive averaging, which is a novel approach to accelerating these dynamics. The application to accelerating the replicator dynamics on the simplex is particularly interesting, as this ODE plays an important role in evolutionary game theory and viability theory. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from related work such as Adagrad and Adam, noting that their method is specifically designed for accelerated methods.

The Significance of the work is substantial. Accelerated first-order methods are widely used in machine learning, and the proposed heuristic could improve their practical performance. The theoretical guarantees provided (preserving convergence rates) address a key limitation of existing heuristics like adaptive restarting, which often lack such guarantees. The empirical results show tangible improvements, particularly in the strongly convex case, which suggests this approach could be valuable for practitioners.

The main weakness of the paper is the limited discussion of the practical computational cost of the adaptive averaging heuristic. While the algorithm is well-described, it would be helpful to provide some discussion of how the adaptive computation of weights affects the per-iteration complexity compared to existing methods.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical framework for accelerated descent dynamics
- Well-motivated adaptive averaging heuristic with strong theoretical guarantees
- Comprehensive experimental validation showing consistent improvements over existing methods
- Clear connection to previous NIPS work and broader optimization literature
- Potential practical impact on accelerated optimization methods

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited discussion of computational complexity of the adaptive heuristic
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is primarily validated on simplex-constrained problems, with limited exploration of other constraint sets
- The paper could better elaborate on how the adaptive heuristic relates to other adaptive methods in the literature

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding and implementation of accelerated optimization methods. The theoretical framework is sound, the adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, and the empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over existing approaches. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. I believe this work would be of interest to the NIPS community and would be a valuable addition to the conference proceedings.
