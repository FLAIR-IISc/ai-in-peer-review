This paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with generalized averaging weights and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the baseline accelerated rate. The dynamics couple a dual variable accumulating gradients at rate η(t) with a primal variable defined as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory using weights w(t). Via a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors give sufficient conditions on (η,w) guaranteeing a convergence rate O(1/r(t)). They show how earlier ODEs for accelerated methods (e.g., Su-Boyd-Candès; Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan) arise as special cases, and derive a primal representation that clarifies feasibility preservation under constraints (orthant, simplex). A notable example is an accelerated version of replicator dynamics on the simplex. The main algorithmic contribution is an adaptive choice of averaging weight a(t)=w(t)/W(t) that greedily decreases d/dt Lr while maintaining rate guarantees; a discrete-time instantiation preserves the quadratic rate (O(1/k^2)) and is compared empirically to adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient and speed restarts). Experiments in R^3 indicate that adaptive averaging matches or outperforms restarts and yields a more consistently decreasing Lyapunov surrogate.

Relation to prior work: The paper extends the continuous-time interpretations of acceleration (Nesterov; Su et al., NIPS’16; Wibisono et al., variational perspective) by decoupling the gradient accumulation rate and the averaging weights, and by using a Lyapunov construction that yields simple, verifiable sufficient conditions. It contrasts with empirical restart rules (O’Donoghue & Candès) by providing guarantees even without strong convexity. It also connects to mirror descent and replicator dynamics (Nemirovsky & Yudin; Krichene et al., NIPS’15). The discussion of AdaGrad/Adam is helpful but could be expanded (see suggestions).

Quality:
- Theory: The derivation of sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) and the corollary mapping desired rates r(t) to feasible (w,η) are sound and clearly imply O(1/r(t)) convergence. The alternative energy function Er that is monotone under the same conditions adds insight. The discrete-time adaptive averaging preserves the quadratic rate; it would help to state step-size conditions and any needed smoothness/strong convexity of R explicitly in the main text (currently deferred to the supplement).
- Scope of guarantees: The adaptive heuristic provably preserves 1/r(t) in continuous time and has a discrete counterpart with rate guarantees for r(t)=t^2. It would be valuable to clarify whether similar guarantees extend to p>2 rates with higher-order discretizations (beyond citing [23]).
- Experiments: The reported results are consistent with the theory, but are limited to low-dimensional (R^3) toy problems and one geometry (simplex).

Clarity:
- The manuscript is often hard to follow due to notation density and forward references. Please add a concise background section on mirror maps, Bregman divergences, Lyapunov functions, and the AMD family (define AMDw,η and AMD′w,η once and maintain consistent notation).
- The key inequalities around the Lyapunov derivative (the steps leading to inequality (3); equations between lines 33–34 and 46–47 in the submission) deserve more intuition: explain how Ẋ and Ż interact through the averaging, why setting η(t)=a(t)r(t) cancels the problematic term, and give a geometric interpretation (e.g., balancing potential vs kinetic energy).
- Clarify the t0>0 initialization and its practical impact on discretization; explain the mapping t=k√s and how ak approximates a(t).
- Figure 1: the “solid line” reference is ambiguous; use color/dashed/dotted styles to distinguish primal vs dual vs averaged trajectories.

Originality:
- The generalized averaging formulation and the adaptive averaging heuristic are, to my knowledge, novel within the accelerated ODE framework, and the acceleration of replicator dynamics is a nice, original illustration. The work is clearly differentiated from restarting and from step-size adaptation in AdaGrad/Adam.

Significance:
- The conceptual contribution—control of acceleration via averaging weights with Lyapunov-based guarantees—is promising and could influence the design of robust heuristics in accelerated methods. However, the practical significance would be stronger with evidence of scalability and applicability beyond the simplex and R^3.

Specific suggestions/questions:
1. Relation to adaptive methods beyond convexity: The paper briefly mentions AdaGrad/Adam as step-size adaptation for (often) non-convex problems. Please discuss whether adaptive averaging could extend to non-convex settings (e.g., convergence to stationary points with Er monotonicity), and how it compares conceptually and practically to moment-based adaptivity. Is there a composite/non-smooth extension (prox-regularizers) and what changes in the Lyapunov argument?
2. Checkable conditions: Theorem 2’s condition (2) depends on ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩. Besides Corollary 1, can you provide other simple sufficient, trajectory-independent recipes for (w,η), possibly using bounds on L or curvature?
3. Discrete-time details: State the exact conditions on ψ*, R, and s for preserving O(1/k^2). Provide a proof sketch in the main text. Discuss the computational overhead of function-value checks used by adaptive averaging vs restarting triggers.
4. Scalability: Add higher-dimensional experiments (e.g., d≥1000) with other geometries (Euclidean ball, ℓ1 ball) and standard convex ML tasks (logistic regression, constrained softmax, entropic OT). Report both iterations and wall-clock time, and sensitivity to β and s.
5. Strongly convex case: Since experiments show notable gains there, can you provide theory showing linear-rate phases or faster-than-1/k^2 transient under adaptive averaging?
6. Energy function: You observe Er is non-increasing empirically even under restart. Can you formalize conditions under which restart ensures Er decreases (e.g., velocity reset implies kinetic term drops)?

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid and general continuous-time analysis with a clear Lyapunov framework.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with guarantees; elegant connection to replicator dynamics.
- Discrete algorithm retains O(1/k^2) rate and empirically improves over popular restarts.
- Broadens understanding of acceleration via averaging rather than momentum alone.

Cons against acceptance:
- Clarity issues and heavy notation impede accessibility; some key intuitions are missing.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to low-dimensional toy setups; scalability not demonstrated.
- Discrete-time guarantees for settings beyond p=2 and broader constraints are not fully developed in the main text.

Minor comments:
- Define all abbreviations on first use (AMDw,η, Bregman divergence, mirror map).
- Ensure consistent use of bold vs standard fonts for vectors/scalars; avoid overloading X for multiple roles.
- Fix Figure 1 styling as noted; label axes and trajectories clearly.
- Provide a short practical guide to choosing β, a1, and s.

Overall, this is a theoretically strong and original paper with promising heuristics. With improved exposition and more substantive experiments demonstrating scalability, it would make a valuable contribution.
