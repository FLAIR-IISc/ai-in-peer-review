

This paper introduces a novel framework for accelerated descent dynamics through generalized averaging, extending the connection between acceleration and averaging observed in prior work. The authors study a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), providing sufficient conditions via Lyapunov analysis to achieve desired convergence rates. The paper makes several significant contributions that build upon and extend NIPS 2014 work by Su et al. and NIPS 2015 work by Krichene et al., which established continuous-time interpretations of Nesterov's accelerated methods.

The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 2, which provides sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function Lr to be non-increasing, guaranteeing convergence at rate 1/r(t). The authors then derive Corollary 1, which gives explicit constructions for w(t) and η(t) that satisfy these conditions, and demonstrate how this framework generalizes previous results (e.g., for r(t)=t²). The paper's most novel contribution is the adaptive averaging heuristic (Section 4), which adaptively computes weights w(t) to maximize the decrease of the Lyapunov function, preserving the convergence rate while empirically improving performance. This approach differs fundamentally from existing restarting heuristics (e.g., gradient restart in [17] or speed restart in [20]), which lack theoretical guarantees for non-strongly convex problems.

The paper excels in quality: The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful attention to existence and uniqueness of solutions (Theorem 1). The Lyapunov argument is elegantly constructed and provides a unified framework for understanding acceleration. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated by the Lyapunov analysis and provides concrete theoretical guarantees. The numerical experiments (Section 5) are comprehensive, comparing adaptive averaging against multiple restart heuristics across various constrained optimization problems (simplex, positive orthant) and objective functions (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, KL divergence). The results consistently show adaptive averaging outperforming or matching restart heuristics, with significant improvements in strongly convex cases.

The paper is exceptionally clear: The mathematical presentation is well-organized, with careful definitions and step-by-step derivations. The examples (Section 3) for positive-orthant and simplex constraints make the abstract framework concrete, particularly the connection to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory. The primal representation of the dynamics (equation 6) provides valuable intuition about how the acceleration mechanism operates.

The originality is strong: While previous work established connections between acceleration and averaging (e.g., [8, 11]), this paper generalizes the averaging scheme to a broad class of dynamics and introduces the adaptive heuristic that leverages this generalization. The adaptive averaging approach represents a novel perspective on acceleration that differs from both traditional restarting heuristics and adaptive learning rate methods like Adam. The paper carefully situates its contribution relative to recent NIPS work, making it clear how it extends beyond previous approaches.

The significance is substantial: The work provides a theoretically grounded method for improving convergence of accelerated first-order methods that could have practical impact across optimization problems in machine learning. The adaptive averaging heuristic is particularly promising as it preserves convergence rates while empirically improving performance. The connection to replicator dynamics (a well-studied ODE in game theory) demonstrates the framework's applicability beyond standard optimization problems.

One minor weakness is that the paper doesn't explore the computational cost of adaptive averaging versus restart heuristics in detail. While the experiments show performance improvements, the authors could better quantify the additional computational overhead of the adaptive mechanism. However, this is a small concern given the strong theoretical and empirical results.

The paper's main strength is its elegant theoretical framework that unifies and extends previous work on accelerated methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is particularly compelling as it provides a simple yet powerful way to improve convergence without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. The numerical experiments robustly support the theoretical claims, showing consistent performance gains across diverse problem instances.

Pro arguments for acceptance: 
- Theoretical framework is rigorous and general
- Adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and well-motivated
- Strong empirical results across multiple problem types
- Clear connections to prior work and new applications (replicator dynamics)
- Preserves theoretical convergence guarantees while improving empirical performance

Con arguments for acceptance:
- Minor computational overhead of adaptive mechanism not fully quantified
- Limited to convex optimization (though this is consistent with the problem scope)

This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the theory of accelerated optimization methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a compelling new perspective on acceleration that could influence future algorithm design. The combination of rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical validation makes this a strong candidate for NIPS. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing both theoretical insights and practical algorithmic improvements.
