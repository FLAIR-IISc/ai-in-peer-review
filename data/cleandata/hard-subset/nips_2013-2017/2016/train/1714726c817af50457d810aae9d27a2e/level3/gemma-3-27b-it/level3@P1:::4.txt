This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, framing it as a coupling between a dual variable accumulating gradients and a primal variable obtained through a weighted average of the dual trajectory. The authors provide sufficient conditions, based on a Lyapunov argument, for achieving a desired convergence rate, and specifically demonstrate how the replicator dynamics can be accelerated using their framework. A novel adaptive averaging heuristic is proposed to dynamically compute weights to expedite convergence, with accompanying guarantees in continuous and discrete time and validation via numerical experiments.

The work builds upon a growing body of research connecting Nesterov’s accelerated methods with continuous-time ODEs (e.g., [20, 2, 11]).  The paper’s strength lies in generalizing this connection to a broader family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions for both the dual variable’s rate and the averaging scheme. This generalization allows for a more unified analysis, demonstrating how different averaging schemes can achieve, and potentially surpass, existing convergence rates. The application to the replicator dynamics is a nice concrete example, connecting optimization with evolutionary game theory [22].  The authors appropriately reference relevant literature, including work by Attouch et al. [ ], which also explores convergence rates in related settings.

The paper’s strengths are considerable. The theoretical analysis, leveraging a Lyapunov function, is well-motivated and clearly presented. The introduction of the adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution. The provided convergence guarantees, especially the preservation of the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods in discrete-time, are impressive. The numerical experiments, while perhaps not exhaustive, provide encouraging evidence that adaptive averaging performs competitively with, and sometimes surpasses, established heuristics like adaptive restarting [17, 20]. The connection to energy functions (Theorem 3) offers additional insight into the dynamics.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. The notation, while generally clear, can be dense, potentially hindering accessibility for readers unfamiliar with the existing literature on accelerated methods. While the theoretical results are strong, the conditions required for their application (e.g., differentiability of the weight functions) might be restrictive in practice.  The numerical experiments, while positive, are relatively limited in scope; expanding them to a wider range of problem instances and dimensions would strengthen the empirical validation.  The discussion of related adaptive methods (Adagrad, Adam [7, 10]) feels somewhat brief.  While the authors acknowledge that these methods are geared towards sparse gradients, a more nuanced comparison might be valuable, particularly regarding the interplay between adaptation strategies and acceleration.  Furthermore, the connection to Wibisono et al. [23] is mentioned but could be elaborated, especially regarding potential synergies between adaptive averaging and higher-order acceleration techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The generalized AMD framework and the adaptive averaging heuristic represent a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The Lyapunov-based analysis provides rigorous guarantees on convergence rates.
*   **Potential Impact:** The adaptive averaging heuristic could be a valuable tool for practitioners seeking to accelerate first-order optimization algorithms.
*   **Connections to other fields:** The application of the framework to replicator dynamics demonstrates a breadth of applicability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Notation/Accessibility:** The density of the notation may pose a barrier for some readers.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical experiments could benefit from wider scope and more detailed analysis.
*   **Restrictive Conditions:**  The theoretical requirements on weight function differentiability may limit practical applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to NIPS. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the potential practical impact outweigh the minor weaknesses.  The paper advances the state-of-the-art in accelerated optimization and provides valuable insights into the interplay between averaging, acceleration, and adaptation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically suggesting the authors consider expanding the numerical experiments and clarifying the discussion of related adaptive methods.
