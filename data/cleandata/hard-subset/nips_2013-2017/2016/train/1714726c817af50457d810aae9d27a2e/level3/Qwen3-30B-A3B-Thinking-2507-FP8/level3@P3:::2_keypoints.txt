- Extends accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with a generalized averaging scheme and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic for discretization.
- Contributions: unified Lyapunov-based AMD analysis; sufficient conditions for generalized averaging; adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves rates and empirically beats restart heuristics; experiments on simplex-constrained problems.
- Novel adaptive averaging idea that directly targets the Lyapunov function's rate of decrease.
- Continuous-time analysis is technically sound with clear sufficient conditions for Lyapunov decrease.
- Provides useful connections to replicator dynamics and an energy-function interpretation that improve intuition.
- Generalized averaging is largely a trivial extension of prior work and the paper focuses mainly on the well-studied quadratic-rate case (r(t)=t^2).
- Adaptive averaging lacks a rigorous discrete-time convergence proof, leaving the discretized method effectively heuristic.
- Experiments are limited to toy quadratic/linear objectives on the simplex in R^3 and do not test high-dimensional or non-quadratic problems.
- Fails to compare against other averaging schemes for Nesterov-style algorithms, so practical advantage over existing methods is unclear.
- Several interesting analyses (energy interpretation, primal reformulation, replicator reduction) offer limited practical impact for ML.
- Overall originality is incremental: core ODE formulation and Lyapunov approach follow the authors' NIPS 2015 work; adaptive idea is new but underdeveloped.
- Significance is limited: no improved convergence rates, weak theoretical guarantees for discrete time, and unconvincing experiments for ML applications.
- Quality: continuous-time theory is solid, discrete-time analysis incomplete; paper is well-written but experimental section is weak.
- Clarity: paper is clearly written, well-organized, and supplemented with useful additional material.
- Conclusion/recommendation: recommend rejection unless authors provide discrete-time convergence proofs, high-dimensional experiments, and comparisons to other schemes before resubmission.
- Pro-accept: adaptive averaging is novel and promising; continuous-time insights are valuable.
- Con-accept: contribution is incremental, discrete-time theory is lacking, and empirical validation is insufficient.
