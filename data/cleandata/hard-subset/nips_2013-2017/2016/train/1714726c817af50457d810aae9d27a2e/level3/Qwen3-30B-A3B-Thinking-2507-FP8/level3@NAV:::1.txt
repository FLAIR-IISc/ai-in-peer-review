

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through the lens of ordinary differential equations (ODEs), building on the ODE interpretation of Nesterov's acceleration introduced by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The authors extend this framework to develop an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts the weights in the weighted average of iterates, which they prove preserves the quadratic convergence rate while outperforming fixed-schedule averaging and prior adaptive heuristics like restarting. The key insight is that the adaptive weights can be computed to ensure the Lyapunov function decreases at each step, guaranteeing the convergence rate is maintained.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The mathematical argument is elegant and follows naturally from prior work on accelerated methods, making it accessible to readers familiar with Krichene et al. The empirical evaluation across multiple problem classes demonstrates consistent improvements over existing methods, with particularly significant gains in strongly convex cases. The practical impact is clear, as Nesterov's method is widely used, and a simple adaptive modification yields dramatic improvements.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the continuous-time analysis is rigorous, the authors do not provide a thorough analysis of the discretized process to confirm the convergence rate is preserved in discrete time, as suggested in the key points. Additionally, the connection between adaptive averaging and Conjugate Gradient for strongly convex quadratics, which could provide deeper theoretical insights, is not explored.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims and complete experimental evaluation. The clarity is excellent, with a logical flow and well-organized presentation. The originality lies in the simple yet effective adaptive heuristic, which extends prior work in a meaningful way. The significance is high, as the method offers practical improvements to a widely used optimization technique with broad applicability.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's of high quality, clear, original in its heuristic approach, and significant due to the practical impact on a fundamental optimization method. The minor technical gaps noted don't undermine the strong contribution. I recommend acceptance.
