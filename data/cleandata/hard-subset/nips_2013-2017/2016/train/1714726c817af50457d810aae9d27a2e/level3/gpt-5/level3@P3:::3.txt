Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization through a continuous-time lens, extending the primal–dual accelerated mirror descent (AMD) ODE framework of Krichene, Bayen, and Bartlett (NIPS 2015). The key novelty is to decouple the accumulation rate of the dual variable η(t) from the averaging weights w(t) used to form the primal trajectory as a weighted average of the mirror of the dual path. Within this generalized family AMDw,η, the authors introduce a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) and provide sufficient conditions on η and w (Theorem 2) ensuring that Lr is nonincreasing, which yields a convergence rate O(1/r(t)). This unifies and substantially generalizes earlier averaging schedules. By appropriate choices of w and η (e.g., r(t)=tp with p>2), the continuous dynamics achieve rates faster than the standard O(1/t2).

The paper offers a primal reformulation, illustrating the structure on constrained sets, including the positive orthant and the simplex, where the method yields an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics. It also proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses the instantaneous weight ratio a(t)=w(t)/W(t) to reduce the Lyapunov derivative whenever possible, thereby preserving the O(1/r(t)) rate in continuous time. Empirically, a discrete-time instantiation (Algorithm 1) is compared to adaptive restarting techniques (gradient restart and speed restart). Adaptive averaging performs at least as well, and sometimes significantly better, on low-dimensional constrained problems.

The paper is well-aligned with a substantial body of work on inertial/accelerated dynamics in optimization (Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan) and should also be situated relative to the inertial ODE literature of Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors, who obtained faster-than-1/k^2 discrete rates and continuous-time analyses for forward–backward and Hessian-damped systems.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous continuous-time analysis via a flexible Lyapunov construction that recovers and extends prior AMD results, including existence/uniqueness (Theorem 1), rate conditions (Theorem 2), and a complementary energy function (Theorem 3).
- Conceptual advance: disentangling η(t) and w(t) and characterizing when averaging schemes achieve any prescribed rate 1/r(t); explicit examples achieve rates faster than 1/t^2 in continuous time.
- Insightful primal representations and concrete constrained examples, including an accelerated replicator ODE.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with provable continuous-time guarantees; simple principle (decrease Lyapunov derivative) and compelling practical alternative to restarts.
- Empirical comparisons to widely used restarting heuristics indicate competitive or improved performance.

Weaknesses
- Discrete-time theory is largely omitted from the main text. While the abstract claims quadratic-rate preservation in discrete time and the supplement sketches a Lyapunov-style analysis, the paper does not provide a full, carefully stated theorem with assumptions and proof for Algorithm 1. This is the main gap given the paper’s practical ambitions.
- The faster-than-1/t^2 rates are established in continuous time; it remains unclear when such rates persist under first-order discretizations without moving to higher-order schemes. The connection to Bregman Lagrangian discretizations that realize faster rates (e.g., cubic-regularized methods) is mentioned but not developed.
- Experiments are small-scale (R3) and limited in scope; no tests on higher-dimensional machine learning problems, stochastic gradients, or noisy settings where adaptive methods are most impactful.
- Related work discussion could more fully acknowledge and contrast with Attouch–Peypouquet–Redont’s inertial dynamics and the “faster than 1/k^2” results for forward–backward schemes, clarifying similarities/differences in Lyapunov structures and damping/weight schedules.

Quality
The continuous-time contributions are technically sound and well-supported. The Lyapunov analysis is carefully executed, and the conditions are transparent. The adaptive rule’s guarantee (preservation of the continuous-time rate) follows cleanly from the Lyapunov derivative. However, the discrete-time guarantees for the proposed algorithm are underdeveloped in the main paper; claims should be either proved in the main text or clearly qualified.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with helpful derivations and examples. Suggestions:
- Move the discrete-time result (statement, assumptions, proof sketch) into the main text, even if condensed.
- Clarify the exact condition in Corollary 1 (η(t)=a(t)r(t) with a(t)=w(t)/W(t)) to avoid confusion.
- More explicitly discuss when r(t)=tp with p>2 is feasible to discretize with first-order updates, and the trade-offs relative to higher-order methods.

Originality
The generalized averaging framework and adaptive weighting for accelerated dynamics are novel and nontrivial extensions of prior AMD work. The accelerated replicator dynamics example is also appealing. The paper sits at a fruitful intersection of inertial ODEs, mirror descent, and averaging.

Significance
Conceptually significant for understanding acceleration via averaging and for designing adaptive mechanisms with guarantees. Practical impact hinges on discrete-time theory and scalability; with stronger discrete guarantees and larger experiments, this could influence the design of robust accelerations in constrained settings.

Specific prior work to cite and discuss more fully
- H. Attouch and J. Peypouquet, “The rate of convergence of Nesterov’s accelerated forward-backward method is actually faster than 1/k^2,” SIOPT 2016.
- H. Attouch, J. Peypouquet, and P. Redont: inertial gradient-like systems with vanishing viscosity and Hessian-driven damping.
- Connections to the Bregman Lagrangian view (Wibisono et al.) when targeting r(t)=tp, p>2.

Open questions for authors
- Under what conditions on f, ψ, and step sizes can the adaptive averaging scheme maintain faster-than-1/k^2 rates after discretization with first-order updates? Can you provide a negative result if not possible?
- How sensitive is the method to noise and stochastic gradients? Can the Lyapunov framework accommodate expected decreases?
- Can you provide higher-dimensional experiments (e.g., simplex-constrained logistic regression, portfolio optimization) and wall-clock comparisons?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, general continuous-time theory; unifying and extendable framework.
- Simple, principled adaptive mechanism with guarantees; interesting empirical behavior.
- Clear exposition and insightful constrained examples.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing discrete-time theoretical development in the main text; unclear persistence of improved rates after discretization.
- Limited empirical scope and scale.
- Related work on inertial dynamics could be better integrated.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution and proposes a promising adaptive mechanism. Strengthening the discrete-time analysis in the main text and expanding empirical validation would make it a clear accept.
