Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies accelerated mirror descent (AMD) through a continuous-time lens and generalizes the averaging view initiated by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) and connected to the variational perspective of Wibisono et al. (2016) and the ODE of Su, Boyd, Candès (2016). The authors introduce a two-parameter family of accelerated ODEs AMDw,η in which (i) a dual trajectory Z(t) accumulates gradients at rate η(t), and (ii) the primal variable X(t) is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), they give simple sufficient conditions on w and η that guarantee a target convergence rate 1/r(t); this recovers classical t−2 rates and enables more general schedules. They also introduce an “energy” function Er that is nonincreasing under the same conditions.

Beyond this unification, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts the instantaneous weighting schedule a(t) = w(t)/W(t) on the fly to further decrease the Lyapunov (and energy) functions while preserving the nominal convergence rate. They show how this yields an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics on the simplex and provide a discrete algorithm that preserves the quadratic rate for r(t)=t^2. Empirically, they compare adaptive averaging to gradient- and speed-restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès, 2015; Su et al., 2016) and observe competitive or better performance across several convex objectives, with notable gains on strongly convex problems.

Quality
- Theory: The continuous-time analysis is clean and self-contained, building on well-known Lyapunov techniques. The sufficient conditions (η ≥ r′ and the coupling condition involving w,η,r) are transparent and encompass standard accelerated schedules as special cases. The “dominance” of adaptive averaging is precise in the Lyapunov/energy sense: by construction, the adaptive choice makes d/dt Lr ≤ 0 and can decrease it faster than the fixed schedule. The primal reformulation clarifies how mirror structure keeps trajectories feasible, and the replicator example is nicely worked out.
- Discretization: The discrete algorithm and the claim that the quadratic rate is preserved appear mostly in the supplement. Given that practitioners use discrete methods, it would strengthen the paper to bring at least one main theorem and proof sketch into the body, including the precise conditions under which Lr,k is nonincreasing and the rate bound for Algorithm 1.
- Scope and assumptions: The results assume smooth convex f with Lipschitz gradient and smooth mirror map; this is standard for accelerated mirror descent. It would be helpful to discuss robustness to gradient noise or inexact proximal/mirror steps, and whether the adaptive mechanism remains well behaved for stochastic gradients.
- Experiments: The comparisons are fair and include both restart baselines. The qualitative diagnostics (objective, trajectory, Lr, Er) support the claims. However, most figures focus on R^3 simplex problems; additional larger-scale experiments (e.g., Euclidean setting with logistic regression/LASSO, constrained linear programs, higher-dimensional simplices) and wall-clock or oracle complexity would increase the empirical significance.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, and the main ideas are easy to follow if one is familiar with prior ODE analyses of acceleration. Figures and the replicator example aid intuition. Suggestions:
- Move the discrete-time theorem and a succinct version of Algorithm 1 (with all hyperparameters and choices of ψ, R spelled out) into the main text.
- Clarify the relationship between a(t), w(t), and η(t) earlier, and restate the canonical choices that recover t−2.
- When introducing t0 > 0, add a brief note on how it maps to discrete initialization and practical steps to avoid division by W(t).
- Unify notation around x, x̃, ž and keep a quick reference table for ψ, ψ*, Dψ, Dψ*.

Originality
The work is a close but nontrivial extension of Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015): it generalizes the averaging viewpoint to a broad family parameterized by w,η and provides a simple adaptive mechanism with provable Lyapunov/energy decrease and preserved rate. The accelerated replicator dynamics and the new energy function are nice additions. While the ingredients (Lyapunov analysis, mirror maps, ODE view) are known, the particular adaptive averaging proposal and its guarantees are, to my knowledge, new and practically relevant.

Significance
Acceleration is ubiquitous; a simple, general, and provably safe adaptive heuristic is valuable. The proposed method is easy to implement, orthogonal to existing restarts, and shows promising gains. If the discrete-time analysis is strengthened and broader experiments are added, this could have meaningful impact on practical accelerated methods. The link to replicator dynamics may also spur cross-pollination with game dynamics.

Pros
- Clear and unified Lyapunov analysis yielding general sufficient conditions for accelerated rates.
- Simple adaptive averaging rule that provably preserves the base rate and decreases Lr/Er.
- Accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex and intuitive primal forms.
- Empirical improvements over restart heuristics in several settings; reductions in oscillations.

Cons
- Discrete-time guarantees (rate preservation, monotonicity of the discrete Lyapunov) are mostly deferred to the supplement; bring a main theorem to the body.
- Experiments are relatively small-scale and focused on low-dimensional simplices; broader tasks and metrics would strengthen significance.
- No discussion of stochastic/noisy gradients or inexact oracles.
- Relation to conjugate gradient and heavy-ball methods on quadratics is intriguing but unexplored.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide a main-text theorem (with assumptions) for Algorithm 1 that states the convergence rate and Lyapunov monotonicity in discrete time?
- How sensitive is the heuristic to the choice of β and step size s? Any principled tuning guidance?
- Can adaptive averaging be combined with adaptive restarts to yield further gains, or does one subsume the other?
- For strongly convex quadratics, is there a formal connection to Conjugate Gradient (e.g., via Krylov subspace interpretations of the induced averaging weights)?
- How does the method perform with stochastic gradients or coordinate updates? Does the Lyapunov argument extend with additional noise terms?

Minor comments
- Consider adding a concise table connecting choices of r(t) ↔ a(t) ↔ w(t),η(t).
- Note that the “dominance” guarantee is in the Lyapunov/energy sense; be explicit about when it implies monotone decrease in f.
- A few references could be tightened (years/venues missing in the list).
