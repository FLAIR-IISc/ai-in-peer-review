- Studies continuous-time Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics for constrained convex optimization parameterized by η(t) (gradient accumulation) and w(t) (trajectory averaging).

- Generalizes prior continuous-time analyses (Su et al. 2014; Krichene et al. 2015), which appear as special cases of this framework.

- Main theoretical contribution: Lyapunov-based analysis giving simple, sufficient conditions on η(t) and w(t) to guarantee O(1/r(t)) convergence.

- Introduces "adaptive averaging," a heuristic that greedily adjusts averaging weights w(t) to decrease the Lyapunov function along the trajectory.

- Adaptive averaging is guaranteed by construction to preserve the worst-case O(1/t^2) convergence rate, unlike many restarting heuristics.

- Provides a discrete-time implementation (Algorithm 1) derived from the continuous dynamics and compares it empirically to baselines.

- Empirical results show adaptive averaging performs favorably versus standard accelerated descent and restarting schemes across various constrained problems and objective types.

- High technical quality: rigorous, elegant analysis (Theorem 2) with well-supported theoretical and empirical claims.

- Originality/significance: unified AMD framework clarifies distinct roles of η and w and offers a principled, theory-derived adaptive heuristic.

- Key insight: adaptively re-weighting trajectory history can yield empirical acceleration similar to restarting momentum.

- Theoretical advantage: adaptive averaging’s guarantees are broader than restarting schemes, which often require stronger problem assumptions.

- Application example: derivation of an "accelerated replicator dynamics" demonstrates broader utility (e.g., game theory).

- Clarity: paper is well-written, well-organized, with consistent notation and helpful primal representation for intuition.

- Weakness: the discretization from continuous adaptive heuristic to Algorithm 1 needs more justification (mapping t = k√s, the ak update rule, and the f(x̃(k+1))−f(x̃(k)) condition).

- Suggestion/future work: although adaptive averaging preserves O(1/t^2), it would be valuable to investigate whether faster rates can be proven under additional assumptions (e.g., strong convexity).

- Arguments for acceptance: novel generalization, principled adaptive heuristic, stronger theoretical guarantees, solid empirical validation, and high-quality exposition.

- Argument against acceptance: minor need for more detailed justification of specific discretization choices in the algorithm.
