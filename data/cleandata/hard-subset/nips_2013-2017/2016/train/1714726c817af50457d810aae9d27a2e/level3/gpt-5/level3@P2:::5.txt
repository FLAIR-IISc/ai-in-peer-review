Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization from a continuous-time perspective. It introduces a broad family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) ODEs parameterized by two positive functions: η(t), which controls how a dual variable Z(t) accumulates gradients, and w(t), which determines a generalized averaging of the mirrored dual trajectory to produce the primal iterate X(t). The authors construct a Lyapunov function L_r(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + D_{ψ*}(Z,z*) and provide sufficient conditions on η and w under which L_r is non-increasing, implying a convergence guarantee of order 1/r(t). They recover the classical O(1/t^2) rate by suitable choices of w and η and generalize to r(t)=t^p.

They give an equivalent primal representation of the dynamics, recover mirror descent as a limiting case, and present explicit forms for positive-orthant and simplex constraints. Notably, they derive an “accelerated replicator” dynamics for optimization over the simplex. They also define a second energy function E_r that is guaranteed to decrease under the same conditions.

Motivated by practical heuristics such as adaptive restarting (Su–Boyd–Candès, O’Donoghue–Candès), the paper proposes “adaptive averaging”: adapt a(t)=w(t)/W(t) along the trajectory to further decrease d/dt L_r without sacrificing the nominal rate. A discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) is derived for the quadratic rate case, with a proof sketch in the supplement that the O(1/k^2) rate is preserved. Experiments on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems indicate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as gradient/speed restarting and sometimes better, while maintaining monotonic decrease of the Lyapunov function.

This work builds on and connects several lines: continuous-time views of acceleration (Nesterov; Su–Boyd–Candès), mirror descent and Bregman geometry (Nemirovski–Yudin), and variational characterizations of accelerated methods (Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan). It is closest to Krichene et al. (accelerated mirror descent) and Wibisono et al. (variational ODE family), extending them by introducing generalized averaging and an adaptive scheme with guarantees.

Quality
- Theory: The Lyapunov framework is clean, and the sufficient conditions (η(t) ≥ r′(t) and an alignment condition involving ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ and w,η) are clearly derived. The corollary that ties η and w via a(t)=w/W simplifies checking the conditions and recovers known rates for r(t)=t^p. The decreasing energy E_r is a nice addition. A limitation is that the key alignment condition depends on the trajectory and is generally hard to verify or enforce a priori; the paper addresses this by proposing adaptive averaging that enforces non-increase of L_r, but the continuous-time heuristic is turned into a discrete algorithm with proofs relegated to the supplement. The guarantees for strongly convex objectives are not strengthened beyond O(1/k^2).
- Algorithms: The discrete adaptive method is reasonable and appears implementable. The presentation would benefit from moving part of its analysis into the main paper (e.g., a theorem with assumptions and a self-contained proof sketch).
- Experiments: The empirical evaluation is clear but limited: small dimension (mostly R^3) and a few objective classes. Comparisons are to adaptive restarting heuristics; it would be informative to include baselines such as FISTA with restarts, and to test on higher-dimensional constrained problems and different mirror maps.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The continuous-time development, Lyapunov analysis, and primal/dual viewpoints are presented clearly. Some suggestions:
- Provide more intuition for the alignment condition in Theorem 2 and how adaptive averaging enforces it.
- Move a concise statement of the discrete-time convergence guarantee of Algorithm 1 to the main text.
- Offer practical guidance for choosing β, step size s, and regularizers R, ψ*, and discuss sensitivity.
- Clarify assumptions on ψ,ψ* (smoothness/strong convexity) in one place and note what breaks when ψ is not twice differentiable.

Originality
The generalized averaging perspective that decouples η and w and the adaptive averaging heuristic that provably preserves the Lyapunov decrease appear novel. The acceleration of replicator dynamics via averaging is an interesting and fresh application. While the work leverages known ODE-based analyses of acceleration, the contributions are a nontrivial extension.

Significance
The results consolidate and generalize continuous-time analyses of accelerated methods and propose a practical heuristic with monotonic Lyapunov decrease guarantees. If validated more broadly, adaptive averaging could become a useful alternative to restarting strategies for accelerated mirror-descent-type methods, particularly under constraints and non-Euclidean geometries. The current experiments are suggestive but limited; broader evidence and stronger rates under additional structure (e.g., strong convexity) would increase impact.

Pros
- Clear and unifying Lyapunov framework for generalized averaging with explicit conditions for desired rates.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence guarantees and works well empirically.
- Insightful primal representation and explicit examples, including accelerated replicator dynamics.
- Additional decreasing energy function provides useful diagnostic/intuition.

Cons
- The key alignment condition is trajectory-dependent; practical enforceability and interpretation could be expanded.
- Discrete-time analysis is partly in the supplement; no improved rates in the strongly convex case.
- Experimental evaluation is small-scale; limited diversity of constraints, geometries, and higher dimensions.
- Some implementation details (parameter selection, computational overhead) are not fully discussed.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide conditions under which adaptive averaging yields improved rates in the strongly convex setting (e.g., O(1/k^{2+ε}) or linear after some phase)?
- How sensitive is Algorithm 1 to β and step size s? Any adaptive strategies for these?
- What is the computational overhead of maintaining a(t) and the two proximal-like subproblems per iteration versus standard accelerated methods with restart?
- Can adaptive averaging be combined with stochastic gradients, and what guarantees would remain?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a main-text theorem for the discrete algorithm with assumptions and a short proof.
- Add higher-dimensional experiments (e.g., d ≥ 1000), additional geometries (e.g., simplex, l1-ball, spectrahedron), and baselines (FISTA with restart, mirror-prox variants).
- Provide ablation studies on the adaptive rule and visualization of a(t), w(t) evolution to support the intended intuition.
- Discuss extensions to stochastic or coordinate variants and to the variational ODE family of Wibisono et al.

Overall recommendation
The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution with a novel adaptive averaging heuristic and promising initial experiments. I find the idea interesting and potentially impactful. With some strengthening of the discrete-time analysis in the main text and broader experiments, this would be a strong submission.
