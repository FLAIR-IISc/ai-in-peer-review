This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization from the perspective of continuous-time dynamics. The authors propose a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) ordinary differential equations (ODEs), which they characterize as a coupling between a dual variable that accumulates gradients at a rate η(t) and a primal variable that is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory, with weights w(t).

The main theoretical contribution is a unified analysis of this family of ODEs using a Lyapunov function. By constructing a Lyapunov function Lr that incorporates a desired convergence rate 1/r(t), the authors derive simple sufficient conditions on the weight functions η(t) and w(t) that guarantee the convergence rate f(X(t)) - f* = O(1/r(t)). This framework elegantly generalizes previous continuous-time models of acceleration (e.g., Su et al., 2014; Krichene et al., 2015) and provides a constructive way to design dynamics with a specific convergence rate. As an interesting example, the authors show how the well-known replicator dynamics from evolutionary game theory can be accelerated using this averaging scheme.

Building on this analysis, the paper introduces a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The core idea is to adaptively choose the averaging weights w(t) to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, thereby speeding up convergence empirically. This heuristic is translated into a practical discrete-time algorithm which is proven to preserve the O(1/k^2) convergence rate of the underlying accelerated method. Numerical experiments on several constrained optimization problems show that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as, and sometimes significantly better than, popular adaptive restart heuristics.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The Lyapunov-based analysis is rigorous, and the derived conditions for convergence are clean and insightful. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical proofs and empirical results. The work is presented as a complete contribution, connecting the continuous-time theory to a concrete discrete-time algorithm and validating its performance. The authors are careful to evaluate their method against strong baselines and honestly present the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation and high-level ideas are introduced clearly in Section 1. The main theoretical development in Section 2 flows logically from the definition of the dynamics to the derivation of the Lyapunov function's derivative and the resulting convergence conditions. The examples in Section 3, particularly the connection to an accelerated replicator dynamics, provide excellent intuition. The figures in the experimental section are very informative, showing not only the objective value but also the trajectory, the Lyapunov function, and the proposed energy function, which helps in understanding the different behaviors of the compared heuristics.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the study of accelerated methods via ODEs has been an active area, this paper's contribution of a generalized averaging framework is a novel and powerful perspective. The key insight that acceleration can be manipulated and improved by adaptively tuning the averaging weights is new. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a principled alternative to existing restarting schemes. Unlike many heuristics, it is derived directly from the theoretical analysis (i.e., decreasing the Lyapunov function) and comes with a formal guarantee that it preserves the worst-case convergence rate, which is a significant advantage. The work is well-situated within the literature and clearly distinguishes its contributions from prior art.

**Significance:** The paper has high potential for impact.
1.  **Conceptual Contribution:** The unified framework provides a deeper understanding of acceleration, reframing it in terms of averaging. This perspective could inspire the design of new optimization algorithms.
2.  **Practical Contribution:** The adaptive averaging algorithm is a practical and effective tool. Given its strong empirical performance and theoretical grounding, it could be readily adopted by practitioners looking to speed up accelerated methods. It provides a valuable alternative to adaptive restarting, which can sometimes be overly aggressive or lack guarantees for non-strongly convex problems.
3.  **Future Work:** The ideas presented here are likely to stimulate further research, for instance, in proving faster convergence rates for the adaptive scheme under stronger assumptions (e.g., strong convexity) or extending the framework to other classes of algorithms.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel and elegant theoretical framework that unifies and generalizes the continuous-time analysis of accelerated methods.
*   **Pro:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is principled, directly motivated by the Lyapunov analysis, and highly effective in practice.
*   **Pro:** A key strength is that the heuristic is proven to preserve the O(1/k^2) convergence rate, a guarantee not always available for other restart heuristics.
*   **Pro:** The empirical comparison to state-of-the-art restart heuristics is thorough and demonstrates that the proposed method is competitive and often superior.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very clearly written, technically sound, and presents a complete piece of work from theory to practice.

### Arguments against Acceptance
*   **Con (Minor):** The experiments are conducted in low dimensions to facilitate visualization. While the supplement may contain higher-dimensional results, including one such experiment in the main paper would have further strengthened the empirical claims.
*   **Con (Minor):** The theoretical analysis guarantees that adaptive averaging *preserves* the convergence rate. A proof that it *improves* the rate under certain conditions (e.g., for strongly convex functions) would make the contribution even stronger, though the authors rightly point to this as future work.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The combination of elegant theory, a novel and practical algorithm, and strong empirical results makes it a clear accept.
