- Central contribution: generalizes continuous-time primal-dual ODEs to a family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by averaging weight w(t) and gradient accumulation rate η(t).

- Theoretical core: unified Lyapunov-based analysis (Theorem 2) giving sufficient conditions on w(t), η(t) to guarantee a 1/r(t) convergence rate.

- Recoveries and extensions: framework recovers prior results and yields new continuous-time dynamics with convergence faster than O(1/t^2).

- Practical method: proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses weights to maximally decrease the Lyapunov function while preserving the original convergence rate.

- Empirical validation: numerical experiments show the adaptive averaging performs competitively or better than popular adaptive restarting techniques.

- Strength (clarity/quality): paper is well-written, technically sound, and uses a single elegant Lyapunov function to unify many dynamics and examples (e.g., accelerated replicator dynamics).

- Strength (originality): generalization of averaging and derivation of faster-than-O(1/t^2) continuous rates are significant and novel theoretical contributions.

- Strength (practical relevance): adaptive heuristic has provable guarantees for general convex functions and shows promising empirical performance versus restarts.

- Weakness (related work): omits substantial literature on inertial dynamics (e.g., Attouch, Peypouquet et al.) that addresses very similar ODEs and convergence rates.

- Weakness (discrete analysis): main paper lacks a discrete-time theoretical analysis of the proposed adaptive algorithm; the proof is relegated to the supplement.

- Weakness (continuous-to-discrete gap): paper does not address whether faster-than-O(1/t^2) continuous rates can be preserved by practical first-order discretizations; this gap limits assessment of practical impact.

- Suggested fixes: include discussion/citations to Attouch et al., provide a discrete-time proof sketch or more detail, and discuss the continuous-to-discrete limitations and future work.

- Overall verdict: interesting, elegant, and significant work with important practical contributions; recommended for acceptance subject to addressing related-work and discretization concerns.
