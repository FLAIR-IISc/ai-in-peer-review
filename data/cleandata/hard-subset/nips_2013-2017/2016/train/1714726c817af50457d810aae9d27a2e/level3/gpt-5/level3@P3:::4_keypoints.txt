- Proposes a generalized continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) family parameterized by gradient accumulation η(t) and averaging weights w(t).
- Uses a clean Lyapunov-based design to derive sufficient conditions on η and w that guarantee a target convergence rate r(t).
- Provides a constructive recipe (via auxiliary a(t)) to generate weight schedules that recover classical t^p acceleration rates.
- Introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that modulates w(t) online to further decrease the Lyapunov function and gives continuous- and discrete-time guarantees preserving O(1/k^2).
- Presents a primal reformulation for constrained sets, including an “accelerated replicator” ODE as an illustrative example.
- The Lyapunov construction and Theorem 2 are simple, transparent, and provide verifiable sufficient conditions; Corollary 1 gives a practical design rule.
- Empirical results on simplex-constrained problems show adaptive averaging matches or outperforms gradient/speed restarting, especially in strongly convex cases.
- Plots of Lyapunov energies Lr and Er empirically support the theoretical monotonicity under the adaptive rule.
- Analysis assumes differentiable convex f with Lipschitz gradient and smooth mirror maps; existence/uniqueness requires t0>0 for general w.
- Clarity is high overall, but the paper should centralize intuition behind condition (2) in Theorem 2 and tie it earlier to the adaptive rule.
- Suggests summarizing discrete-time guarantees and parameter recommendations (β, s) in the main text rather than the supplement.
- Recommends a brief discussion on choosing r(t) in practice and feasible rates under discretization beyond p=2.
- The generalized averaging formulation and Lyapunov design rule are novel and unify prior ODE-based acceleration work.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is a fresh, momentum-free alternative to restarts and broadens conceptual reach with the replicator example.
- The work is significant for designing η and w for desired rates and for offering a practical tool for constrained accelerated methods.
- Main weaknesses: discrete-time analysis and constants deferred to supplement, limited hyperparameter tuning guidance, modest experimental scope (low-dimensional simplex problems).
- Open questions: extension to p>2 with first-order schemes, handling composite/nonsmooth objectives (proximal), robustness to stochastic gradients and noisy inner-product tests.
- Pros: general Lyapunov framework, new adaptive averaging with theoretical guarantees, insightful primal examples, empirical improvements over restarts, strong connections to variational literature.
- Cons: limited discrete-time/parameter guidance in main text, narrow experimental breadth, no treatment of stochastic or composite settings.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, conceptually elegant, practically relevant; leans toward acceptance with a recommendation to strengthen discrete-time guarantees and broaden experiments.
