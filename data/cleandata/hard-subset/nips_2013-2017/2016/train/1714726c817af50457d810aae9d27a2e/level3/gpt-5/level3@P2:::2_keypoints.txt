- Paper introduces a two-parameter continuous-time AMD family (AMDw,η) coupling dual gradient accumulation η(t) with primal weighted averaging w(t).  
- Uses Lyapunov Lr(X,Z,t)=r(t)(f(X)−f*)+Dψ*(Z,z*) to give sufficient conditions on w and η yielding O(1/r(t)) convergence, unifying prior ODE analyses.  
- Provides a primal reformulation, an energy function Er that decreases under similar conditions, and instantiates dynamics for the positive orthant and simplex (accelerated replicator).  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic choosing a(t)=w(t)/W(t) to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative and gives a discrete Algorithm 1 for r(t)=t^2.  
- Empirical comparisons (small-scale simplex problems) suggest adaptive averaging can match or outperform common restart heuristics.  

- Strength: Clear, unified Lyapunov-based analysis isolating the role of averaging weights with simple sufficient conditions.  
- Strength: Conceptually appealing continuous-time link between acceleration and averaging aligned with prior literature.  
- Strength: Primal reformulation and replicator example aid intuition and may be independently useful.  
- Strength: Adaptive averaging is simple, intuitive, and shows promising empirical behavior in some cases.  
- Strength: Energy interpretation complements Lyapunov analysis and may have broader applicability.  

- Weakness: The theoretical advance beyond earlier ODE-based work is modest; generalized averaging largely reframes existing dynamics.  
- Weakness: Second Lyapunov condition is trajectory-dependent and adaptive averaging is only partially justified; discrete-time results remain heuristic.  
- Weakness: No clear discrete-time guarantee that quadratic rate is preserved; assumptions and proofs are missing/unclear.  
- Weakness: Discrete development limited to t^2 rate; no concrete integration with higher-order (faster) methods.  
- Weakness: Experiments are limited to low-dimensional toy simplex problems with no high-dim, stochastic, or challenging ML tasks.  
- Weakness: Lacks comparisons to other averaging/acceleration methods and stronger practical baselines, so practical advantage is unestablished.  

- Quality: Continuous-time theory is sound; discrete-time adaptive averaging is heuristic and poorly supported by experiments.  
- Clarity: Well written and organized, but needs explicit discrete-time guarantees and tighter notation for the adaptive rule.  
- Originality: Moderate—adaptive averaging and averaging perspective are novel but closely build on prior NIPS work.  
- Significance: Limited in current form due to weak discrete guarantees and narrow empirical validation.  

- Pros for acceptance: Clean unified framework, simple adaptive idea of practical interest, and interesting replicator connection.  
- Cons for acceptance: Incremental theory vs prior work, lack of rigorous discrete-time proof, and insufficient experiments.  

- Key suggestions: Explicitly state and sketch proofs for discrete-time guarantees (Algorithm 1), compare to more baselines (fixed schedules, alternative adaptation rules, Catalyst, heavy-ball), and run high-dimensional, composite, and noisy-gradient experiments.  
- Additional suggestions: Test robustness to stochastic/inexact oracles, illustrate accelerated replicator on game-theory benchmarks, and explore sharper rates/constants under strong convexity.  

- Post-rebuttal note: If authors provide a solid discrete-time convergence proof and broader high-dimensional empirical gains (and integrate with higher-order methods), the paper could be significantly strengthened.  
- Recommendation: Borderline reject (leaning reject) due to limited novelty beyond prior ODE analyses and insufficient discrete-time theory/experiments.
