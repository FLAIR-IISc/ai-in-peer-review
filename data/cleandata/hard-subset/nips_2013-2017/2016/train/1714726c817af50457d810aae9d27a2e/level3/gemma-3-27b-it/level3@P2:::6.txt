This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, proposing an adaptive averaging heuristic that aims to improve convergence rates while preserving the theoretical guarantees of existing methods. The core idea is to dynamically adjust the weights used in averaging the dual trajectory to obtain the primal variable, based on the rate of decrease of a Lyapunov function. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for their approach in continuous-time and demonstrate its effectiveness through numerical experiments, claiming performance comparable to or better than adaptive restarting heuristics.

The paper builds upon a significant body of work connecting Nesterov’s accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs, citing foundational papers by Attouch et al., Su et al., and Nesterov himself. It correctly positions itself within the broader context of first-order methods and the resurgence of interest in continuous-time dynamics for optimization. The connection to replicator dynamics, and its application to simplex-constrained problems, is also a noteworthy contribution, drawing on concepts from evolutionary game theory.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The Lyapunov argument provides a solid foundation for the analysis, and the sufficient conditions given for convergence are clearly stated. The guarantee of preserving the quadratic convergence rate for non-strongly convex problems is a significant achievement.
* **Novelty:** The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a novel approach to accelerating descent dynamics, differing from existing restarting methods. The extension to the replicator dynamics is also a valuable contribution.
* **Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments, while limited in dimensionality (R^3), demonstrate promising results, suggesting the potential of the proposed heuristic. The comparison to existing restarting methods strengthens the claim of improved performance.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind the work – improving upon existing acceleration techniques while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is dense and can be challenging to follow, particularly for readers not deeply familiar with the existing literature on accelerated methods and mirror descent. The introduction of several abbreviations (e.g., AMD, ODE) without sufficient initial explanation hinders understanding. More background material on mirror descent and the connection to Nesterov's acceleration would be beneficial.
* **Intuition and Explanation:** The equations presented between lines 33-34 and 46-47 are presented without sufficient intuitive explanation. While mathematically correct, a more accessible explanation of their meaning and role within the broader framework would significantly improve understanding.
* **Limited Scalability Evidence:** The experiments are restricted to R^3, which is a relatively small-scale setup. While informative, this limits the assessment of the method's scalability to higher-dimensional problems commonly encountered in machine learning. The authors need to provide stronger evidence that the method’s benefits translate to more realistic settings.
* **Relation to Adaptive Gradient Methods:** The paper briefly mentions Adagrad and Adam but doesn't fully explore the connection to adaptive learning rate methods used in non-convex optimization. A more thorough discussion of how this work differs from, and potentially complements, these techniques would be valuable.
* **Figure Clarity:** In Figure 1, the reference to the solid line is ambiguous. Using a dotted line or different colors to clearly distinguish the primal trajectory would improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of accelerated optimization.
* The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a promising approach to improving convergence rates.
* The numerical experiments provide initial evidence of the method's effectiveness.
* The paper is well-connected to the relevant literature and builds upon existing work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from a lack of clarity and accessibility, potentially limiting its impact.
* The limited scalability evidence raises concerns about the practical relevance of the method.
* The discussion of related work, particularly concerning adaptive gradient methods, is insufficient.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The theoretical results are solid, and the adaptive averaging heuristic shows promise. However, the paper’s clarity and the limited scope of the experimental evaluation raise concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with major revisions. The authors should focus on improving the paper’s clarity and accessibility by adding more background material, providing more intuitive explanations of the key equations, and expanding the experimental evaluation to higher dimensions. A more comprehensive discussion of the relationship to adaptive gradient methods in non-convex optimization would also strengthen the paper. Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and increase its value to the NIPS community.
