- Studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with generalized averaging and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic for discrete time.  
- Uses Lyapunov function Lr to give sufficient conditions on η and w for O(1/r(t)) convergence and recovers O(1/t^2) for r(t)=t^2.  
- Provides a primal formulation and an “energy” Er that decreases under the same conditions.  
- Specializes the ODE to the simplex to derive an accelerated variant of replicator dynamics.  
- Proposes choosing a(t)=w(t)/W(t) to make a Lyapunov cross term negative and an online adaptive averaging rule to decrease Lr.  
- Presents a discrete scheme (Algorithm 1) but treats the discrete adaptive averaging as largely heuristic.  
- Continuous-time Lyapunov analysis is sound and recovers known rates under mild conditions.  
- Corollary giving constructive w via a(t) ≥ r′/r and η = a r reproduces standard polynomial weights (incremental).  
- The discrete-time algorithm lacks a complete, rigorous convergence proof and clear conditions ensuring Lyapunov decrease.  
- Energy/primal representations are technically correct and helpful for interpretation.  
- Accelerated replicator example is interesting but expected from mirror descent on the simplex.  
- Empirical validation is limited to low-dimensional, deterministic, toy problems and misses realistic ML benchmarks.  
- Experiments do not evaluate high-dimensional, strongly convex, or stochastic settings, nor compare many alternative averaging/adaptive schemes.  
- Clarity is generally good but needs better delineation from prior work (Krichene et al. 2015, Wibisono et al. 2016) and explicit discrete-algorithm assumptions (β, step size).  
- Originality is incremental: generalized weights are straightforward, and adaptive averaging is conceptually nice but not novel without discrete guarantees.  
- Significance depends on adding rigorous discrete analysis and stronger experiments; currently mainly conceptual.  
- Suggestions: provide full discrete-time convergence proofs with explicit parameter conditions, broaden experiments (high-dim, stochastic, strong convexity), compare more averaging/adaptive baselines, and clarify novelty vs prior work.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak reject due to heuristic discrete algorithm and limited empirical evidence; strengthen proofs and experiments for acceptance.
