This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order methods in constrained convex optimization. It builds upon previous work that connects Nesterov's accelerated methods to second-order ordinary differential equations (ODEs), particularly the authors' prior work in [11] which framed these dynamics as a coupling between a primal and a dual variable. The main contribution is to generalize this framework to a broader class of "Accelerated Mirror Descent" (AMD) dynamics. In this generalized model, the primal variable `X(t)` is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory, and the dynamics are parameterized by two functions: a weighting function `w(t)` for the averaging and a rate function `η(t)` for accumulating gradients.

Using a Lyapunov-based analysis, the paper provides a unified framework for establishing convergence rates. The main theoretical result, Theorem 2, gives simple and elegant sufficient conditions on the functions `w(t)` and `η(t)` to guarantee a desired convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))`. This framework is shown to be flexible enough to recover the known `O(1/t^2)` rate and also to establish faster rates in continuous time (e.g., `O(1/t^p)` for `p > 2`). A key insight is that acceleration can be understood through specific averaging schemes. As a compelling example, the paper demonstrates how the well-known replicator dynamics from evolutionary game theory can be accelerated using this averaging approach.

Based on the continuous-time analysis, the authors propose a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The idea is to adaptively choose the averaging weights to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, which empirically helps to dampen oscillations and speed up convergence. A discrete-time version of this heuristic is presented in Algorithm 1. The paper concludes with numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging to popular restarting heuristics, showing that the proposed method performs comparably or, in some cases, significantly better, while also preserving the monotonic decrease of the Lyapunov function.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the analysis in the continuous-time setting is rigorous and insightful. The use of a generalized Lyapunov function to derive sufficient conditions on the averaging and rate functions is an elegant approach that unifies and extends prior results. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated by the theory and, importantly, comes with a guarantee that it preserves the theoretical convergence rate, which is a notable advantage over many ad-hoc restarting schemes.
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution by generalizing the averaging perspective of accelerated methods. This provides a deeper and more flexible understanding of the mechanics of acceleration. The idea of designing adaptive schemes that are guaranteed to make a Lyapunov function decrease is a powerful one and could inspire further work on adaptive optimization algorithms with provable guarantees. The connection drawn to accelerating the replicator dynamics is a novel and interesting application of the framework.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building intuition, starting from their general ODE formulation, moving to the Lyapunov analysis, and then using the insights gained to motivate their adaptive heuristic. The derivations are clear and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Positioning with Respect to Related Work:** The paper cites some key works from Attouch, Peypouquet, and collaborators [2, 3], but it could do a better job of positioning its contributions relative to this extensive line of research on inertial dynamics and Hessian-driven damping. Their work also heavily relies on Lyapunov analysis for second-order ODEs to prove convergence rates. A more detailed comparison of the two perspectives (averaging vs. inertial/damping) would strengthen the paper and clarify the specific novelty of the authors' framework for the reader.
*   **The Gap between Continuous and Discrete Rates:** A central and exciting result is that the continuous-time framework allows for convergence rates faster than `O(1/t^2)`. However, the discretization presented in Algorithm 1 is tailored to the `r(t) = t^2` case, leading to the standard `O(1/k^2)` discrete-time rate. The paper briefly mentions that achieving faster rates via discretization might require higher-order methods, citing [23]. This is a critical point that deserves more discussion. Can the faster continuous rates be achieved by a practical first-order discretization? If not, what is the fundamental barrier? A more thorough treatment of this "discretization gap" would add significant value and manage expectations about the practical implications of the faster continuous-time rates.
*   **Analysis of the Discrete Algorithm:** The convergence proof for the proposed discrete algorithm with adaptive averaging (Algorithm 1) is deferred to the supplementary material. While this is understandable due to space constraints, the main body of the paper would be more self-contained and convincing if it included a formal theorem stating the discrete-time convergence result and perhaps a brief proof sketch. This would provide the reader with immediate confidence in the claims made about the proposed algorithm.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a novel, elegant, and unifying theoretical framework for understanding accelerated methods from an averaging perspective. The continuous-time analysis is rigorous, and the proposed adaptive heuristic is well-motivated, principled, and shows strong empirical performance while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The paper is very clearly written.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The main weaknesses are the limited discussion on the relationship with the Attouch et al. body of work and, more importantly, the unresolved question of whether the demonstrated faster-than-1/t^2 continuous-time rates can be translated into practical discrete-time first-order algorithms.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its novel theoretical insights, rigorous analysis, and a well-founded new heuristic—far outweigh its weaknesses. The paper is a solid contribution to the optimization and machine learning literature. The points raised above are primarily suggestions for improvement that could be addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
