This paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic for constrained convex optimization that preserves the original convergence rate for non-strongly convex objectives. The authors claim an advantage over existing restarting heuristics and support this both theoretically and experimentally.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem and the proposed solution. The main contribution of the paper is the development of a new heuristic for adaptively computing the weights in accelerated mirror descent. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the heuristic and demonstrate its effectiveness through numerical experiments.

The strengths of the paper are:

* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and the proposed solution.
* The theoretical analysis of the heuristic is thorough and well-supported by mathematical derivations.
* The numerical experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the heuristic and provide a comparison with existing restarting heuristics.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

* The paper assumes a high level of background knowledge in convex optimization and dynamical systems. Adding some background material and clarifying abbreviations/terms would make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
* The equations between lines 33-34 and 46-47 need more intuitive explanation. The authors should consider adding more text to explain the physical significance of these equations and how they relate to the proposed heuristic.
* The authors should discuss the relation to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad, Adam). This would provide a more complete picture of the proposed heuristic and its potential applications.
* The practical significance of the results is not entirely clear. The experiments are conducted in R^3, which is a relatively simple setup. The authors should provide evidence of scalability to higher dimensions.
* Figure 1's reference to the solid line is ambiguous. The authors should use a dotted line or colors to show the primal trajectory.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a new and innovative heuristic for constrained convex optimization.
* The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-supported by mathematical derivations.
* The numerical experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the heuristic and provide a comparison with existing restarting heuristics.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper assumes a high level of background knowledge in convex optimization and dynamical systems.
* The practical significance of the results is not entirely clear.
* The experiments are conducted in a relatively simple setup, and the authors should provide evidence of scalability to higher dimensions.

In conclusion, this paper proposes a new and innovative heuristic for constrained convex optimization. While there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement, the paper is well-organized and clearly written. With some revisions to address the weaknesses, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
