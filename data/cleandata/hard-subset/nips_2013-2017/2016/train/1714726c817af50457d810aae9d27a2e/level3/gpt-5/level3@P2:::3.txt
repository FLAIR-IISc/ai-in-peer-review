Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained smooth convex optimization through a continuous-time primal–dual viewpoint. The central object is a family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two positive functions: η(t), which scales the accumulation of gradients in the dual variable Z, and w(t), which defines a weighted averaging of the mirrored dual trajectory to form the primal variable X. The authors introduce a unifying Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), and give simple sufficient conditions on (η,w,r) ensuring that Lr is nonincreasing, which yields f(X(t)) − f* ≤ const./r(t). This recovers the familiar 1/t^2 rate (r(t)=t^2) and, in continuous time, allows faster polynomial rates r(t)=t^p for p>2 under appropriate choices of w,η. A companion “energy” function Er that also decreases under the same conditions provides additional insight.

Beyond analysis, the paper gives a primal-only realization of the dynamics, recovers known special cases (mirror descent, replicator dynamics on the simplex) and derives an “accelerated replicator ODE.” Motivated by the Lyapunov estimate, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts the instantaneous weighting a(t)=w(t)/W(t) to further decrease Lr (and Er) along trajectories. A discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) is proposed; empirically, the adaptive averaging heuristic competes favorably with popular adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient restart, speed restart) across several simplex-constrained problems.

This work builds on and unifies several strands: Nesterov’s acceleration; continuous-time interpretations of accelerated methods (Su-Boyd-Candès), proximal/constraint-aware interpretations via mirror maps and averaging (Krichene et al.), the variational Bregman Lagrangian perspective (Wibisono et al.), and restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès). It is also related to the inertial ODE literature with Lyapunov analysis and time-varying damping (e.g., Attouch, Peypouquet, and collaborators), which demonstrates faster-than-1/t^2 rates in continuous time under suitable damping; a clearer comparison would strengthen the positioning.

Strengths
- A clean and general Lyapunov framework that isolates how η and w control the rate r(t), unifying multiple accelerated dynamics.
- Conceptually appealing “acceleration via averaging” viewpoint; clear connection between energy decrease and adaptive choice of averaging weights.
- Novel accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex and clear primal representations for standard feasible sets.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with provable continuous-time guarantees and a simple discrete algorithm; empirical gains over widely used restarts in several examples.
- Writing is generally clear; proofs for existence/uniqueness and Lyapunov decrease are careful.

Weaknesses
- The trajectory-dependent condition in Theorem 2 (sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ times a factor) is not verifiable a priori; the corollary imposes a specific coupling η = a r W that may limit practical design space.
- Claims for discrete-time are limited: while the abstract says quadratic rate is preserved, the main text defers discrete analysis and bounds to the supplement. It remains unclear whether the “faster than 1/t^2” continuous rates are at all attainable by first-order discretizations (as the authors note, higher-order methods may be required).
- Experiments are small-scale (R^3 simplex) and focus on specific objectives; broader benchmarks and sensitivity to step sizes and β would improve the empirical case.
- Limited discussion of strong convexity: do the guarantees preserve linear rates or improve constants relative to restarts?
- Limited discussion of robustness to noise/stochastic gradients, where adaptive schemes are particularly relevant.

Quality
The continuous-time analysis is technically sound and builds on standard tools (Lyapunov/Bregman divergences, Cauchy–Lipschitz for existence/uniqueness). The adaptive averaging rule is well-motivated by the Lyapunov derivative. However, the discrete-time theory in the main paper is thin; readers would benefit from at least a theorem statement with clear assumptions and a proof sketch in the main text. It would also be valuable to state explicitly that faster-than-1/k^2 rates in discrete time appear to require higher-order discretizations (as suggested by Wibisono et al.) and are not claimed here for first-order updates.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and readable. Suggestions:
- Promote the discrete-time guarantees (currently in the supplement) into the main text with a precise theorem and conditions.
- Explicitly relate the Lyapunov construction and damping schedule to the inertial ODE literature (Attouch–Peypouquet, Attouch–Redont), clarifying similarities/differences.
- Clarify parameter choices (β, step size s) and provide guidance for choosing ψ*, R in Algorithm 1.
- Minor nitpicks: “correspondance” → “correspondence”; unify notation around a(t)=w/W; ensure all assumptions (Lipschitzness of ∇ψ*) are stated where used.

Originality
The generalized averaging formulation and the adaptive averaging heuristic are original and interesting. The accelerated replicator ODE and the articulation of a decreasing energy Er add insight. The unified Lyapunov view that cleanly recovers known ODEs and accelerations is a valuable contribution.

Significance
Conceptually, this work may influence how we design adaptive variants of accelerated methods beyond restarts. Practically, the heuristic is easy to implement and competes with strong baselines. The impact would be strengthened by fuller discrete-time theory and larger-scale experiments. The connection to faster continuous rates is intriguing, though its algorithmic implications hinge on discretization.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you formalize whether first-order discretizations with adaptive averaging can exceed O(1/k^2) worst-case rates under smooth convexity, or is higher-order information necessary (as per Wibisono et al.)?
- Do your adaptive weights preserve linear convergence under strong convexity? A theorem or empirical study would be helpful.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for β and s, and clarify how these were tuned across methods to ensure fair comparisons.
- Discuss extensions to composite/regularized objectives and stochastic gradients.
- Please expand the discussion comparing your Lyapunov construction to the inertial dynamics literature (Attouch–Peypouquet et al.), which also reports faster continuous-time rates with vanishing damping.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, general theory connecting acceleration and averaging with a unifying Lyapunov framework.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with guarantees and empirical benefits over popular restarts.
- Insightful continuous-time perspective; accelerated replicator dynamics are of independent interest.
- Well-written and technically careful in the continuous-time setting.

Cons for acceptance
- Discrete-time theory is relegated to the supplement; unclear whether claimed benefits extend beyond quadratic rate in practice.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scale and scope; missing large-scale and non-simplex benchmarks.
- Some key conditions are trajectory-dependent and hard to verify or design for a priori.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-executed paper on a timely topic; with stronger discrete-time analysis in the main text and broader experiments, it would make a solid contribution to NIPS.
