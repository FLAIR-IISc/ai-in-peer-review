This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, focusing on the interplay between dual and primal variables and the role of averaging in achieving faster convergence rates. The authors build upon prior work connecting Nesterov’s accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs (e.g., [20, 11]) and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to speed up the decrease of a Lyapunov function. A key contribution is providing convergence guarantees for this adaptive averaging scheme, demonstrating its preservation of the quadratic convergence rate in discrete-time. The experimental results suggest that adaptive averaging performs competitively with, and sometimes outperforms, adaptive restarting methods.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretically grounded approach. The use of a Lyapunov function to analyze the convergence of the accelerated dynamics provides a solid foundation. Extending this to an adaptive scheme and proving convergence guarantees is a noteworthy achievement. The connection made between acceleration and averaging is insightful, particularly as demonstrated through the application to the replicator dynamics, which offers a concrete example of the proposed methodology. The formulation of the problem in terms of both primal and dual variables allows for a more comprehensive understanding of the dynamics involved, as seen in the derivation of AMD’w,η and AMD’p,η. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of extensive discussion of related work, particularly regarding adaptive methods. While Adagrad and Adam [7, 10] are briefly mentioned, a more detailed comparison of their objectives and approaches, especially concerning accelerated methods, would strengthen the contribution. The experimental section, while providing initial evidence of the method’s effectiveness, is somewhat limited. The visualization of trajectories in R3 is helpful, but more comprehensive testing on a wider range of problem instances and larger dimensions would increase confidence in the robustness of the adaptive averaging heuristic. The claim of “significant improvements in some cases” warrants more detailed analysis and quantification.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The Lyapunov analysis and the proof of convergence seem rigorous, although my limited expertise prevents a full assessment. The authors are careful to outline assumptions and conditions required for the theorems to hold. The work feels complete within its defined scope, successfully delivering on its stated objectives. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is standard, and the derivations are relatively easy to follow. The introduction provides sufficient context and motivation. However, some sections could benefit from further elaboration, such as the discussion of Bregman divergences and the interpretation of the energy function in Section 3.  More intuitive explanations of the averaging schemes could aid understanding. 

**Originality:** The combination of Lyapunov analysis with an adaptive averaging scheme for accelerated descent appears to be novel. The application of this framework to the replicator dynamics also provides a unique perspective. While the underlying concepts of accelerated methods and Lyapunov stability are well-established, the specific adaptive heuristic and its theoretical guarantees represent a distinct contribution.  The paper adequately references related work, although, as noted earlier, a more exhaustive comparison to other adaptive optimization methods would further highlight its originality.

**Significance:** The results have the potential to be significant.  An adaptive averaging scheme that preserves convergence rates is a valuable addition to the toolbox of optimization techniques. The theoretical guarantees provided are particularly strong. If the experimental results generalize to a wider range of problems, this method could be readily adopted by practitioners and researchers.  The work contributes to a better understanding of the dynamics underlying accelerated methods and opens avenues for further research into adaptive optimization strategies.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation with Lyapunov analysis and convergence guarantees.
*   Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates.
*   Interesting application to the replicator dynamics.
*   Promising experimental results indicating competitive performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of related adaptive optimization methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam).
*   Somewhat limited experimental evaluation; needs broader testing.
*   Some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations.



Overall, I believe this is a promising paper with a solid contribution to the field. While there are some areas for improvement, the theoretical rigor, novel approach, and potential impact outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the suggestions for improvement in a revised version.
