- Extends continuous-time interpretation of Nesterov to propose an adaptive averaging scheme for accelerated mirror descent (AMD).
- Studies a generalized family of AMD dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, giving sufficient conditions for convergence via Lyapunov analysis.
- Main contribution: an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights on-the-fly to accelerate convergence while preserving continuous-time guarantees.
- Theorem 2: provides sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to decrease, unifying prior results.
- Proof techniques are clean, build on Krichene et al. (2015), and are accessible to readers familiar with continuous-time optimization dynamics.
- Empirical validation across strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, and KL-divergence objectives shows adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics.
- Particularly strong empirical improvements in the strongly convex case and for simplex-constrained (replicator) dynamics.
- The adaptive heuristic is simple, requires no extra hyperparameters, and is easy to implement.
- The paper is very clear and well-organized, with a precise algorithm description and good motivation for discrete implementations.
- Supplementary material offers helpful background on mirror maps and convex analysis for less familiar readers.
- Originality is moderate: extends existing continuous-time acceleration frameworks but adds a non-trivial, practically useful adaptive mechanism.
- Significance is high due to practical impact on widely used accelerated methods and potential applications (e.g., evolutionary game theory).
- Strengths: rigorous theoretical guarantees, comprehensive experiments, practical and simple heuristic, clear connection to prior NIPS work.
- Weakness: lacks a detailed analysis of the discretized process to prove convergence-rate preservation in discrete time.
- Additional weaknesses: the Conjugate Gradient connection is only suggested and unexplored; experiments could be more diverse to demonstrate robustness.
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically grounded heuristic with preserved rates, consistent empirical gains, clear math, substantial practical relevance, and strong writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical extension not highly novel, insufficient discretization analysis, and undeveloped Conjugate Gradient link.
