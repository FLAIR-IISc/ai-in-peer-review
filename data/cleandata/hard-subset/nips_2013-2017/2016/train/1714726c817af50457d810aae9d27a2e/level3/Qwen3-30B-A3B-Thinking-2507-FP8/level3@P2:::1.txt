

# Review for "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization using a continuous-time ODE framework. It extends the work of Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015), which established a connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs, by introducing a generalized averaging scheme for accelerated mirror descent dynamics. The authors formulate a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), and provide sufficient conditions on these functions to guarantee a convergence rate of 1/r(t) using a Lyapunov argument. They then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes the weights to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function, which preserves the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a unified theoretical framework for understanding accelerated methods through continuous-time dynamics. Second, it introduces a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that is guaranteed to dominate fixed-schedule averaging (unlike prior adaptive heuristics), which is a significant theoretical contribution. Third, the empirical results demonstrate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as, and often significantly better than, existing heuristics like adaptive restarting across a range of convex optimization problems, including strongly convex quadratics, weakly convex quadratics, linear functions, and KL divergence.

The paper is technically sound with clear mathematical development. The Lyapunov function analysis is rigorous, and the conditions for convergence are well-justified. The connection to prior work (particularly Krichene et al. NIPS 2015) is well-explained and the relationship to other continuous-time dynamics (e.g., Su et al. NIPS 2014) is properly contextualized. The authors demonstrate how the replicator dynamics (a key method in evolutionary game theory) can be accelerated using their framework, which provides a compelling application example.

The paper excels in clarity. The mathematical development is well-organized, with clear definitions and logical progression. The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is well-explained, and the adaptive averaging heuristic is presented in a way that is accessible to readers familiar with accelerated optimization methods. The experimental section is well-designed, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines, and the results are presented clearly with insightful visualizations of the dynamics.

In terms of originality, while the paper builds on the strong foundation of prior work on continuous-time accelerated dynamics, the specific adaptive averaging heuristic represents a meaningful extension. The key novelty lies in the theoretical guarantee that adaptive averaging preserves the convergence rate (unlike prior adaptive methods), which is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper properly cites and differentiates from related work, including the adaptive restarting heuristics of O'Donoghue and Candès (Foundations of Computational Mathematics 2015) and Su et al. (NIPS 2014).

The significance of this work is substantial. Nesterov's accelerated methods are widely used in machine learning and optimization, yet they often require careful tuning of hyperparameters to achieve optimal performance. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a simple modification that significantly improves convergence without requiring additional tuning, which could have broad practical impact. The paper demonstrates this potential through compelling empirical results across multiple problem types.

The main weakness of the paper is the limited analysis of the discrete-time counterpart. While the authors provide a discrete version of the adaptive averaging heuristic and include some convergence rate analysis in the supplementary material, a more thorough analysis of the discrete process would strengthen the theoretical claims. The paper would benefit from explicitly showing that the discrete algorithm preserves the convergence rate guarantees of the continuous-time analysis, rather than relying on supplementary material.

Another minor weakness is the limited scope of the experiments, which focus primarily on simplex-constrained problems in R3. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and mention additional experiments in higher dimensions in the supplementary material, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation would further demonstrate the generality of the approach.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of optimization. The paper successfully extends prior work on continuous-time accelerated dynamics with a theoretically grounded and practically effective adaptive averaging heuristic. The technical development is sound, the presentation is clear, and the empirical results are compelling. The work has significant potential for practical impact given the widespread use of Nesterov's accelerated methods.

Strengths:
- Solid theoretical foundation building on strong prior work
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantees
- Clear mathematical development and well-organized presentation
- Strong empirical results showing significant improvements over existing methods
- Practical relevance with potential for broad impact

Weaknesses:
- Limited discrete-time analysis (though addressed in supplementary material)
- Relatively narrow experimental scope (simplex-constrained problems in low dimensions)

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a clear scientific contribution to the field of optimization with both theoretical and practical significance. The authors have carefully considered the relationship to prior work and made a meaningful extension that advances our understanding of accelerated optimization methods.
