This paper studies accelerated mirror descent dynamics with generalized averaging. The authors parameterize continuous-time dynamics by weight functions w(t) and gradient accumulation rate η(t), derive sufficient Lyapunov conditions that imply an O(1/r(t)) rate, and give a primal formulation that highlights connections to replicator dynamics on the simplex. Building on this, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that locally decreases the Lyapunov derivative and, upon discretization for r(t)=t^2, provably preserves the O(1/k^2) rate for non–strongly convex objectives. Experiments compare adaptive averaging to gradient/speed restarts (O’Donoghue & Candès) and suggest consistent improvements. The work relates to Nesterov’s acceleration, the ODE views of Su–Boyd–Candès and Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan, and to mirror descent and replicator dynamics.

Quality: The continuous-time analysis via inequality (3) and Theorem 2 appears sound, and the construction of a second energy function is insightful. However, some derivations are difficult to parse; the equations between lines 33–34 and 46–47 would benefit from an intuitive explanation (e.g., a geometric picture of the averaged flow and the role of a(t)=w/W). In the discrete section, please avoid calling O(1/k^2) “quadratic convergence,” which commonly denotes superlinear Newton-like rates.

Clarity: The paper is hard to follow. Please add a concise background on mirror maps, Bregman divergences, and notation; define all abbreviations (AMDw,η, MDp, Er, Lr, etc.) on first use; and include a small glossary/table of symbols. Figure 1’s “solid line” reference is ambiguous—use distinct colors or a dotted line for the primal trajectory.

Originality: The idea of adapting the averaging weights to control the Lyapunov decrease is novel relative to restarting heuristics and complements variational/ODE perspectives. The relation to adaptive methods such as Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011) and Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) is only briefly mentioned; a deeper discussion contrasting objectives (convex vs. non-convex), stochasticity, and per-coordinate adaptivity would strengthen positioning.

Significance: If scalable, the heuristic could be impactful for constrained convex optimization. Currently, experiments are limited to R^3 toy problems; please add higher-dimensional benchmarks (e.g., n≥10^3 simplex, box/L1 constraints), report wall-clock and iteration metrics, and compare against strong baselines (FISTA with adaptive restart, mirror-prox variants). Discuss computational overhead of adapting ak and any tuning sensitivity (β, s).

Pros:
- Clear theoretical guarantee preserving the non-strongly convex O(1/k^2) rate.
- Conceptually simple heuristic with a principled Lyapunov rationale.
- Links to replicator dynamics and energy-based interpretations.

Cons:
- Clarity/notation barriers; key steps lack intuition.
- Limited empirical scope; no evidence of scalability.
- Shallow discussion of relations to modern adaptive methods.
