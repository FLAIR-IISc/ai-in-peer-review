This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, framing it as a coupling of dual and primal variables governed by averaging. The authors present a generalized Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) framework and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to optimize the averaging weights, aiming to speed up convergence. They provide sufficient conditions for convergence based on Lyapunov analysis and demonstrate the approach on the replicator dynamics. Experiments suggest competitive performance compared to adaptive restarting heuristics.

The work builds upon prior research in continuous-time optimization dynamics, particularly the connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and ODEs established in [20, 11]. The authors appropriately reference these works and situate their contribution within this lineage. However, a significant portion of the framework – the Lyapunov function, its use in analyzing AMD, and the discretization approach – directly stems from their earlier NIPS 2016 paper [11], reducing the novelty of the core mathematical development. The extension from a fixed averaging scheme to a generalized one appears relatively straightforward, and focusing solely on the quadratic convergence rate limits the broader applicability of the analysis. 

A strength of the paper is the clear intuition behind the adaptive averaging heuristic. By dynamically adjusting weights based on the Lyapunov function's rate of decrease, the method seeks to accelerate convergence where progress is being made. The analysis of the primal representation of AMD and the derivation of an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics are valuable contributions to understanding the underlying dynamics. Furthermore, the energy function analysis offers an alternative perspective on the algorithm's behavior.

However, the theoretical grounding of the adaptive averaging heuristic remains incomplete. While the Lyapunov analysis provides sufficient conditions for convergence, the discretization introduces approximations that aren't rigorously addressed, rendering the method effectively a heuristic. A crucial weakness is the lack of comparison to other existing averaging schemes tailored for Nesterov-style algorithms. Without such a comparison, the practical advantages of adaptive averaging remain uncertain. The experimental evaluation is also limited; the toy quadratic and linear objectives lack the complexity and scale typical of modern machine learning applications. The results do not provide strong evidence of consistent improvement over established methods, particularly in strongly convex scenarios.  The improvement in some cases is not compelling given the theoretical limitations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a novel and intuitive approach to optimizing AMD.
*   The paper provides a unified framework for analyzing AMD dynamics.
*   The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics is a useful contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty: significant portions of the framework rely heavily on prior work [11].
*   Theoretical incompleteness: the discretization lacks a rigorous convergence analysis.
*   Lack of comparison to existing averaging schemes.
*   Limited and unconvincing experimental evaluation.
*   The generalization of AMD is incremental and lacks substantial impact.

**Overall:**

The paper presents a reasonable extension of existing work on accelerated mirror descent, with a potentially useful adaptive averaging heuristic. However, the limited novelty, theoretical gaps, and insufficient experimental validation raise concerns about its impact. The post-rebuttal commitments—specifically, expanded experiments, a convergence proof, application to higher-order methods, and comparisons to other schemes—are crucial for addressing these weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:** 

I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form. The authors should prioritize fulfilling their post-rebuttal commitments to strengthen the theoretical foundation and demonstrate the practical benefits of their approach. If these commitments are successfully addressed, a revised version of the paper may be suitable for reconsideration.
