- Summary: Analyzes accelerated mirror descent (AMD) via ODEs, generalizes averaging in the primal, uses a Lyapunov function to obtain rate conditions and introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic plus a discretization for r(t)=t^2.

- Core technical tool: Lyapunov Lr(X,Z,t)=r(t)(f(X)−f*)+Dψ*(Z,z*) gives simple sufficient conditions on η and w to recover rate 1/r(t), including O(1/t^2).

- Energy and primal views: Introduces an energy combining potential and kinetic terms and provides primal reformulations that recover known mirror and replicator dynamics.

- Main algorithmic novelty: Adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts averaging weights using sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ to further decrease the Lyapunov function.

- Discretization: Uses an existing AMD discretization for r(t)=t^2 to produce a practical first-order scheme and compares it to gradient and speed restarts.

- Strength: Clear, unified Lyapunov analysis with transparent sufficient conditions and helpful energy intuition.

- Strength: Adaptive averaging is original within the AMD/ODE framework with a clear, appealing intuition.

- Strength: Primal formulations and accelerated replicator dynamics connection are insightful and of independent interest.

- Strength: Empirically, adaptive averaging often matches or improves restart heuristics while keeping Lyapunov/energy decreasing in presented examples.

- Weakness: Many technical elements (Lyapunov form, discretization, rate recovery) largely follow prior ODE/AMD work, so novelty is incremental.

- Weakness: Generalized averaging is only discretized and evaluated for the quadratic-rate case (r=t^2); faster rates need higher-order methods, limiting ML applicability.

- Weakness: Adaptive averaging lacks a rigorous discrete-time convergence proof; the discrete scheme is heuristic based on approximations.

- Weakness: Comparisons omit other averaging/monotone schemes (e.g., monotone FISTA, alternative schedules, backtracking), leaving practical advantage unclear.

- Weakness: Experiments are limited to low-dimensional/toy problems; no large-scale, high-dimensional, stochastic, or standard ML benchmarks.

- Quality: Continuous-time analysis is sound; discrete-time claims are weaker and lack precise assumptions and proofs preserving O(1/k^2).

- Clarity: Well written and organized; derivations and intuition are clear but should better separate novel contributions from inherited results and clarify Algorithm 1 choices.

- Originality: Moderate—adaptive averaging is novel, but most theoretical pieces are incremental extensions of prior work.

- Significance: Conceptually useful for AMD and connecting replicator dynamics to acceleration, but practical significance is uncertain without stronger discrete theory and broader experiments.

- For acceptance: Offers a clean Lyapunov framework, a novel adaptive averaging idea with promising empirical behavior, and interesting replicator dynamics connections.

- Against acceptance: Incremental relative to prior ODE-based AMD work, discrete adaptive scheme lacks rigorous proof, and empirical evaluation is insufficiently convincing.

- Suggestions: Provide a discrete-time convergence proof for Algorithm 1, compare to other averaging/monotone/backtracking schemes, add high-dimensional/non-quadratic and stochastic experiments, and clarify step-size/regularizer choices.

- Post-rebuttal conditions: If authors add high-dimensional experiments, a discrete convergence proof, cubic-regularized Newton implementation, and more comparisons, the reviewer would likely move toward acceptance.

- Recommendation: Weak reject in current form due to limited originality, absence of discrete-time proof, and insufficient experiments; promising if commitments are delivered.
