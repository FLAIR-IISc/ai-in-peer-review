- Main contribution: generalizes AMD ODEs by decoupling dual accumulation η(t) and averaging weights w(t), giving family AMDw,η and Lyapunov-based conditions for O(1/r(t)) rates.
- Key theoretical tool: Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t)=r(t)(f(X)−f*)+Dψ*(Z,z*) with sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) ensuring nonincreasing energy.
- Continuous-time novelty: shows by appropriate w,η choices (e.g., r(t)=t^p, p>2) dynamics can achieve rates faster than 1/t^2.
- Primal reformulation: derives concrete constrained examples, including an accelerated replicator ODE on simplex/positive orthant.
- Adaptive averaging: proposes a heuristic that picks a(t)=w(t)/W(t) to reduce Lyapunov derivative and preserves continuous-time O(1/r(t)) rate.
- Empirics: discrete Algorithm 1 compared to gradient/speed restarts; adaptive averaging is competitive or sometimes significantly better on low-dimensional problems.
- Theorem/analysis strengths: clear existence/uniqueness (Theorem 1), rate conditions (Theorem 2), and complementary energy (Theorem 3).
- Conceptual advance: disentangling η and w clarifies when averaging achieves any prescribed rate and unifies prior averaging schedules.
- Practical gap: discrete-time theory is underdeveloped in the main text; no full theorem/assumptions/proof for Algorithm 1 provided.
- Discretization concern: unclear if faster-than-1/t^2 continuous rates persist under first-order discretizations without higher-order schemes.
- Empirical limitation: experiments are small-scale (R^3) and lack high-dimensional, stochastic, or noisy scenarios where adaptivity matters.
- Related work gap: should more fully compare to Attouch–Peypouquet–Redont and faster-than-1/k^2 forward–backward inertial dynamics and Bregman Lagrangian connections.
- Clarity suggestions: move discrete-time result into main text, clarify Corollary 1 condition η(t)=a(t)r(t), and discuss discretizability of r(t)=t^p (p>2).
- Quality judgment: continuous-time analysis is technically sound and transparent; discrete-time claims need qualification or proof.
- Originality: generalized averaging framework and adaptive weighting are novel; accelerated replicator example is appealing.
- Significance: conceptually important for understanding acceleration via averaging; practical impact depends on discrete-time guarantees and scalability.
- Open questions: conditions for preserving faster-than-1/k^2 after first-order discretization, sensitivity to noise/stochastic gradients, and need for higher-dimensional experiments.
- Pros for acceptance: strong continuous-time theory, principled adaptive mechanism with guarantees, clear exposition and constrained examples.
- Cons for acceptance: missing discrete-time theory in main text, limited experiments, and incomplete integration of related inertial-dynamics literature.
- Recommendation: weak accept, contingent on stronger discrete-time analysis and expanded empirical validation.
