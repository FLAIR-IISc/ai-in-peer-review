- Studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization via continuous-time dynamics, extending Krichene et al.'s primal-dual ODE approach.
- Focuses on the primal trajectory obtained by generalized averaging of the mirrored dual variable and gives sufficient conditions on averaging coefficients.
- Main theoretical contribution (Theorem 2) establishes convergence rates and shows continuous-time convergence faster than 1/t^2 under suitable averaging.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes weights to speed Lyapunov decrease and provides provable continuous-time convergence guarantees.
- Empirical results show adaptive averaging performs at least as well as adaptive restarting and yields significant improvements in some cases.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, with a concrete application example (replicator dynamics on the simplex).
- Fails to cite important prior continuous-time work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors, weakening literature positioning.
- Omits discrete-time theoretical analysis and provides no convergence guarantees for the discrete Algorithm 1, a major limitation for practical use.
- Raises but does not address the crucial open question whether improved continuous-time rates persist after discretization.
- Originality: the generalized averaging scheme is novel, but connections to existing literature are not sufficiently established.
- Quality: strong continuous-time analysis but overall quality weakened by missing discrete-time results.
- Significance: offers theoretical insights that could influence algorithm design, but practical significance is limited without discrete-time guarantees.
- Arguments for acceptance: rigorous continuous-time theory, novel adaptive heuristic, and promising empirical evidence.
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of discrete-time theory, missing key citations, and unresolved discretization applicability.
- Recommendation: reject, with the view that the continuous-time contributions are promising and would be stronger with discrete-time analysis and proper citations.
