## Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending the framework of ODE-based accelerated Mirror Descent with a generalized averaging scheme. The core contribution is a novel adaptive averaging strategy designed to improve convergence speed. The authors demonstrate, through a Lyapunov argument, sufficient conditions for convergence and show that the adaptive averaging can, in some cases, outperform existing adaptive methods like restarting. 

The paper builds directly upon prior work, particularly the NIPS 2016 paper by Krichene et al. [11], from which much of the Lyapunov function setup and discretization approach are derived. The extension to generalized averaging feels incremental, with the primary focus remaining on the quadratic convergence case. While the analysis of the primal reformulation and reduction to replicator dynamics provides valuable insights, their direct impact on practical machine learning applications appears limited. 

A significant weakness is the lack of a rigorous convergence proof for the adaptive averaging scheme itself. The authors acknowledge this, framing it as a heuristic due to approximations introduced during discretization. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is restricted to a toy quadratic/linear objective, making it difficult to assess the method’s performance on more complex, real-world problems. The absence of comparisons to other averaging schemes designed for Nesterov-style algorithms further obscures the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Adaptive Averaging:** The adaptive averaging idea is intuitively appealing and offers a potentially effective way to dynamically adjust the averaging weights.
*   **Clear Theoretical Foundation:** The Lyapunov analysis provides a solid theoretical basis for understanding the dynamics and establishing convergence conditions.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The primal reformulation and connection to replicator dynamics deepen the understanding of the underlying optimization process.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Originality:** Heavily relies on prior work (Krichene et al. [11]), with the extension to generalized averaging being relatively trivial.
*   **Lack of Rigorous Proof:** The adaptive averaging scheme is presented as a heuristic due to discretization approximations, lacking a formal convergence guarantee.
*   **Restricted Experimental Evaluation:** Experiments are limited to simple objectives, hindering the assessment of generalizability.
*   **Missing Comparisons:** No comparison to other relevant averaging schemes for Nesterov-style algorithms.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The adaptive averaging idea is novel and potentially valuable.
*   The paper provides a clear and insightful analysis of the dynamics.
*   The authors have committed to addressing the major weaknesses during the rebuttal phase (high-dimensional experiments, convergence proof, implementation on Nesterov’s method, and comparisons to other schemes).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper’s core contribution is incremental and heavily reliant on existing work.
*   The lack of a convergence proof and limited experimental validation raise concerns about the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The current form of the paper does not represent a significant advancement in the field.

**Overall:**

I currently lean towards **rejection**. While the idea is interesting and the analysis is well-executed, the lack of a convergence proof and the limited experimental validation are significant drawbacks. The authors’ post-rebuttal commitments are encouraging, and a strong response addressing these concerns could potentially change my assessment. However, in its current form, the paper does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS.
