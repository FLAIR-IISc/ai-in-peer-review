Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies accelerated mirror descent (AMD) through an ODE lens and generalizes the averaging mechanism used to construct the primal trajectory. The dynamics couple a dual variable Z accumulating gradients at rate η(t) with a primal variable X that is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). The central tool is a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), yielding sufficient conditions on η and w to recover a prescribed rate 1/r(t), including the standard O(1/t^2) rate for r(t) = t^2. The paper also introduces an “energy” function Er combining potential and kinetic terms, gives a primal reformulation that recovers known mirror descent dynamics for specific geometries, and shows an accelerated variant of the replicator dynamics on the simplex.

The main algorithmic proposal is an adaptive averaging heuristic: instead of fixing w(t) (equivalently a(t) = w/W), adapt a(t) using the instantaneous sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ to further decrease Lr beyond the sufficient conditions that render the second term in the Lyapunov derivative zero. The authors discretize the r(t) = t^2 case via a previously proposed discretization of AMD to produce a practical first-order scheme and compare it to popular adaptive restart heuristics (gradient and speed restart).

This work builds on a line of NIPS work connecting acceleration with ODEs and Lyapunov analysis, including Su–Boyd–Candès (NIPS’14), Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett (NIPS’15), and Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan (2016). The generalized averaging view also relates to Flammarion–Bach’s “From averaging to acceleration.”

Strengths
- Clear, unified Lyapunov analysis for AMD with generalized averaging. The sufficient conditions on (η,w,r) are simple and transparent, and the energy function provides helpful intuition.
- The adaptive averaging idea is original within the AMD/ODE framework, with a clear and appealing intuition: increase the weight on trajectory segments that reduce the Lyapunov function faster.
- The primal formulations and the reduction to accelerated replicator dynamics are insightful and likely of independent interest to communities studying evolutionary dynamics and constrained optimization.
- Empirically, adaptive averaging often matches or improves upon adaptive restarting in the presented examples, while maintaining a decreasing Lyapunov (and energy) function.

Weaknesses
- Originality/usefulness: many technical elements (Lyapunov function form, discretization preserving O(1/t^2), rate recovery conditions) follow directly from earlier work, especially Krichene et al. (NIPS’15) and the variational/ODE perspective of Wibisono et al. The generalization from the specific w(t) used previously to “any” w(t) satisfying simple inequalities is relatively straightforward.
- Limited scope of the “generalized averaging” contribution: only the quadratic-rate case (r(t)=t^2) is discretized and studied empirically. Faster rates (p>2) are acknowledged to need higher-order methods (e.g., cubic-regularized Newton), which limits applicability in large-scale ML.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is not fully theoretically grounded in discrete time. The continuous-time argument is clean, but the discrete scheme relies on discretization approximations; a rigorous, verifiable convergence proof for Algorithm 1 is not presented, making it effectively heuristic.
- Comparisons omit other averaging/monotone schemes for Nesterov-style methods (e.g., monotone FISTA, alternative weight schedules, line-search/backtracking variants), so practical advantage remains unclear.
- Experiments are limited: mostly toy 3D simplex problems and quadratic/linear objectives; no large-scale, high-dimensional, or strongly convex regularized ML tasks; no stochastic setting. The replicator example is interesting conceptually but not compelling evidence for broad ML impact.

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis is technically sound and clearly argued. The sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov decrease are correct and easy to verify a posteriori.
- The discrete-time section is weaker: preserving the O(1/k^2) rate under the adaptive scheme is asserted but not proved with clear assumptions on stepsizes, strong convexity of regularizers, and errors due to discretization; the condition analogous to the sign of ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩ is not directly checkable and is replaced by function comparisons that could break the Lyapunov argument.
- Numerical evaluation is too narrow to substantiate robust practical gains.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and organized. The derivations are readable and the intuition for adaptive averaging is clearly conveyed. Suggested improvements: make the distinction between new contributions and those inherited from [11,23] more explicit; clarify the role and selection of R and step sizes in Algorithm 1; provide a more self-contained statement (and proof) of the discrete-time guarantees.

Originality
- Moderate. The adaptive averaging mechanism is a novel variant within the AMD/ODE framework. The rest is largely an incremental generalization and reinterpretation of known ODE-based acceleration analyses.

Significance
- Conceptually useful for understanding AMD and for bridging replicator dynamics and acceleration. Practical significance is uncertain due to the heuristic nature of the discrete algorithm and limited experiments. Stronger evidence on real ML tasks and stronger theory would raise impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clean, unified Lyapunov framework for generalized averaging that will be useful to researchers studying ODE perspectives of optimization.
- Novel adaptive averaging idea with clear intuition and promising empirical behavior relative to common restart heuristics.
- Insightful connections to replicator dynamics and an informative energy function interpretation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental relative to prior NIPS work; the main theoretical pieces largely follow known templates.
- The discrete adaptive scheme lacks a rigorous convergence proof; currently a heuristic.
- Empirical evaluation is insufficiently convincing for ML relevance; no comparisons to other averaging/monotone schemes beyond restarts.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide a complete, discrete-time convergence analysis of Algorithm 1 (assumptions, stepsizes, regularizer conditions), showing preservation of the O(1/k^2) rate under adaptive averaging.
- Compare against other averaging/monotone variants (e.g., monotone FISTA, different weight schedules) and against backtracking/line-search strategies that enforce function decrease.
- Add high-dimensional, non-quadratic benchmarks (logistic regression with l1/l2, simplex-constrained KL problems), including strongly convex cases, and study sensitivity to β and s.
- Explore applicability and empirical behavior under stochastic gradients.
- Clarify when higher-order methods are necessary to surpass O(1/k^2), and whether adaptive averaging helps in those settings.

Post-rebuttal assessment
The commitments to: (i) add high-dimensional/non-quadratic experiments, (ii) supply a discrete-time convergence proof for adaptive averaging, (iii) implement the idea within cubic-regularized Newton, and (iv) compare to additional adaptive averaging schemes would substantially strengthen both significance and quality. If these are delivered convincingly, my recommendation would move toward acceptance.

Recommendation
Weak reject in current form due to limited originality beyond prior ODE-based AMD work, absence of a solid discrete-time convergence proof for the proposed heuristic, and insufficient experiments. Promising idea; with the promised additions, it could be acceptable.
