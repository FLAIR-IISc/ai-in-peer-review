### Summary

This paper studies a family of continuous-time dynamics for accelerated mirror descent (AMD) for constrained convex optimization. Building upon previous work that connects Nesterov's acceleration to a specific ordinary differential equation (ODE) with a particular averaging scheme [11, 20], this work generalizes the dynamics to a broader family parameterized by two functions: `η(t)` which controls the gradient accumulation rate, and `w(t)` which defines a generalized weighted averaging scheme. The authors use a Lyapunov function-based analysis, similar to that in [11], to derive sufficient conditions on `η` and `w` to achieve a desired convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))`.

The paper's main contribution is a novel adaptive averaging heuristic for a discrete-time version of these dynamics. The intuition is to adaptively choose the averaging weights to greedily decrease the value of the Lyapunov function at each step, thereby accelerating convergence while provably maintaining the `O(1/k^2)` rate of the non-adaptive version in continuous time. The authors provide interesting primal-space reformulations of the dynamics, showing for instance that the well-known replicator dynamics can be accelerated using this framework. Numerical experiments on low-dimensional problems suggest that the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic can outperform popular adaptive restarting schemes [17, 20].

### Quality

The paper is technically sound in its continuous-time analysis. The derivation of the sufficient conditions for convergence using the Lyapunov function in Theorem 2 is correct and provides a clear, unified framework for analyzing this family of ODEs. The introduction of a second energy function (Theorem 3) and the primal representation of the dynamics are nice additions that improve the theoretical understanding of the system.

However, the quality of the main practical contribution—the discrete-time adaptive averaging algorithm (Algorithm 1)—is less established. The algorithm is presented as a heuristic inspired by the continuous-time analysis. While the paper claims that the heuristic "preserves the convergence rate," a formal proof for the discrete-time algorithm is missing. The theoretical guarantees are derived for the continuous-time ODE, and the connection to the discretized algorithm is not rigorously established, making the claims about the algorithm's convergence properties essentially conjectures. The adaptive rule itself is based on a condition (`f(x̃(k+1)) - f(x̃(k)) > 0`) which is a proxy for the continuous-time condition `⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩ > 0`, and the impact of this approximation is not analyzed.

### Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the continuous-time analysis is presented logically. The generalization from the specific averaging scheme in [11] to the `(w, η)` parameterized family is a natural and well-explained step. Figure 1 provides a helpful illustration of the dynamics.

However, some parts could be improved. The connection between the continuous-time heuristic (Equation 8) and its discrete implementation (Algorithm 1) could be explained in more detail. The choice of `a_k = min(a_{k-1}, ...)` in the algorithm seems ad-hoc and is not directly motivated by the continuous-time theory. The presentation of the experimental results is clear, but the setup itself is too simplistic to draw strong conclusions (see Significance).

### Originality

The paper's originality is mixed. The core idea of adaptive averaging, driven by the goal of decreasing a Lyapunov function, is novel and clever. It provides a new perspective on adapting accelerated methods, distinct from the popular restarting heuristics.

At the same time, the work is highly incremental. The analytical framework, including the form of the Lyapunov function and the overall proof structure, is taken almost directly from a previous NIPS paper by the same authors [11]. The extension from a specific averaging schedule (`w(t) ∝ t^(β-1)`) to a general `w(t)` is a straightforward generalization that, by itself, is of limited novelty. The paper focuses almost exclusively on recovering the `O(1/t^2)` rate, and the discussion on faster rates simply points out that this would require higher-order methods [23], which limits the practical scope of the generalization. While the connection to replicator dynamics is interesting, it serves more as an illustrative example than a core contribution to the machine learning community.

### Significance

The potential significance of this work lies in the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic, which could offer a principled and effective alternative to restarting schemes. However, the paper's current contribution is limited by several factors:

1.  **Weak Empirical Validation:** The experiments are performed on toy 3D problems with simple objective functions (quadratic, linear, KL-divergence). While illustrative, these experiments are insufficient to demonstrate the method's effectiveness on the large-scale, high-dimensional, and complex optimization problems typically encountered in machine learning. It is unclear if the observed benefits would translate to more practical settings.
2.  **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees for the Algorithm:** As mentioned, the main proposal is a heuristic without a formal convergence proof in its discrete form. This makes it difficult to assess its robustness and theoretical advantages over existing, well-studied heuristics.
3.  **Incomplete Comparisons:** The paper compares only to restarting heuristics. It omits comparisons to other methods that use averaging ideas to achieve acceleration, such as [8], which would be a relevant baseline for an "adaptive averaging" scheme.

In summary, the paper introduces an interesting idea but falls short of being a complete and impactful contribution. The theoretical analysis is a minor extension of prior work, and the main algorithmic proposal is not rigorously analyzed and is validated only on toy problems.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   The adaptive averaging idea is novel, intuitive, and offers a new perspective on improving accelerated methods.
*   The continuous-time analysis is elegant and provides a clean, unified view of a general class of accelerated dynamics.
*   The preliminary experimental results, while limited, suggest the heuristic has promise and can be competitive with or superior to standard restarting schemes.

### Arguments for Rejection:

*   The work is highly incremental, building very closely on the authors' previous NIPS paper [11]. The novel theoretical contributions are minor.
*   The primary practical contribution, the adaptive algorithm, is presented as a heuristic and lacks a rigorous convergence analysis in discrete time.
*   The experimental validation is weak and limited to low-dimensional toy problems, failing to demonstrate the method's significance for practical machine learning applications.
*   The paper misses comparisons to other relevant averaging-based acceleration schemes.
