- Proposes a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that greedily chooses averaging weights to decrease a Lyapunov function.  
- Provides a unified continuous-time framework for accelerated mirror descent parameterized by η(t) and w(t).  
- Introduces a secondary energy function Er and a primal-space reformulation that deepen theoretical understanding.  
- Shows the continuous-time adaptive scheme is designed to preserve the O(1/r(t)) convergence rate by keeping the Lyapunov function non-increasing.  
- Extends acceleration to replicator dynamics on the probability simplex as an illustrative example.  
- The generalization from prior averaging schemes is incremental, and many analytical tools are directly adopted from earlier work.  
- Practical value of rates beyond O(1/t^2) is limited, since faster rates typically require higher-order methods.  
- Presents Algorithm 1 as a discrete-time implementation but lacks a convergence proof transferring ODE guarantees to the discretization.  
- Numerical experiments are limited to low-dimensional (R^3) toy problems with simple objectives, so practical advantages for ML tasks are unproven.  
- Comparison baselines omit other adaptive or non-standard averaging approaches (e.g., Flammarion & Bach) that are relevant.  
- Observes Er decreases for restarting methods and asks whether restarting corresponds to specific η(t), w(t) choices.  
- Requests intuition for Algorithm 1’s update ak = min(ak-1, βmax/(k√s)) and clarification of the βmax term.  
- Asks whether adaptive averaging could be applied to higher-order methods (e.g., cubic-regularized Newton) for rates beyond O(1/t^2).  
- Acceptance arguments: the heuristic is original and principled, the paper offers useful continuous-time insights, and initial experiments are promising.  
- Rejection arguments: theoretical novelty is incremental, the discrete algorithm lacks a proof, and experiments are too preliminary to demonstrate impact.  
- Reviewer leans toward conditional acceptance pending a discrete-time convergence analysis and stronger experimental validation.
