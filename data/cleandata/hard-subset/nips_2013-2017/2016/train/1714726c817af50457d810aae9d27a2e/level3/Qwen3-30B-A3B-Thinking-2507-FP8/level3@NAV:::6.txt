

This paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic for constrained convex optimization, extending the continuous-time accelerated mirror descent framework. The authors frame their approach as a generalized averaging scheme for the primal variable, showing through Lyapunov analysis that their method preserves the quadratic convergence rate for non-strongly convex objectives. The work relates to recent NIPS contributions on continuous-time optimization dynamics (e.g., Su et al. 2016, Krichene et al. 2015) by generalizing the connection between acceleration and averaging.

The paper demonstrates several strengths: The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing sufficient conditions for convergence rates and establishing connections to existing continuous-time dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel in its approach to maintaining convergence guarantees while improving performance, and the experimental results (in R^3) show competitive performance against restarting heuristics. The work makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of accelerated methods through a continuous-time lens.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The mathematical exposition is dense with minimal intuitive explanations for key equations (particularly between lines 33-34 and 46-47), making the paper difficult to follow without prior expertise in continuous-time optimization. The authors should add background material on Lyapunov functions and clarify all abbreviations/terms. The experimental section is particularly problematic: the toy R^3 setup lacks evidence of scalability to higher dimensions, raising questions about practical significance. The authors should address the relationship between their adaptive averaging approach and popular non-convex adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam, which is currently missing. Figure 1's ambiguous reference to the solid line requires clarification with distinct line styles or colors.

While the theoretical contribution is sound (Quality), the presentation fails to adequately communicate the ideas (Clarity), limiting the paper's impact. The significance is potentially high for continuous-time optimization, but the narrow experimental scope and lack of scalability evidence weaken this claim. The paper shows originality in its heuristic but would benefit from broader context.

Overall, this is a technically sound but poorly presented contribution. With substantial revisions to improve clarity, add context, and demonstrate scalability, it could be a strong paper. As submitted, it lacks sufficient clarity and practical significance to warrant acceptance.
