

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending the continuous-time primal-dual ODE framework pioneered by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016). The authors generalize the averaging scheme used in accelerated mirror descent, providing sufficient conditions on weight functions w(t) and η(t) for convergence at a desired rate. The key contribution is Theorem 2, which establishes convergence rates through a Lyapunov function argument, demonstrating that carefully designed averaging can achieve rates faster than the standard 1/t². The paper also introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes weights to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function, with theoretical guarantees that it preserves the quadratic convergence rate in discrete-time.

The paper is well-written and presents a rigorous continuous-time analysis, making an important contribution to the understanding of accelerated methods through the lens of continuous dynamics. The connection to replicator dynamics (a key concept in evolutionary game theory) is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates how the framework can be applied to the simplex constraint, which has significant applications in game theory and biology. The numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging to restarting heuristics (O'Donoghue and Candès, 2015; Su et al., 2014) show promising results, with adaptive averaging performing at least as well as existing methods and providing significant improvements in some cases.

The paper's primary weaknesses lie in three areas. First, the theoretical analysis of the discrete version of the adaptive averaging algorithm is omitted from the main paper (only mentioned in supplementary material), which is a significant gap since the practical application of these methods is in discrete optimization. The authors claim a discrete counterpart is provided in the supplementary material, but this is insufficient for a paper making claims about discrete optimization algorithms. Second, the related work section fails to adequately address prior continuous-time accelerated methods, particularly the work of Attouch, Peypouquet, and Redont on "Fast convex optimization via inertial dynamics with Hessian driven damping" (2016) and "Fast convergence of an inertial gradient-like system with vanishing viscosity" (2016), which are closely related and should be explicitly compared. Third, the paper raises but does not thoroughly address the critical open question of whether the improved continuous rates persist after discretization, despite acknowledging this as an important issue.

Regarding the NIPS review criteria:

Quality: The continuous-time analysis is technically sound and well-executed, but the lack of discrete-time analysis significantly weakens the paper's contribution to the optimization community, which primarily uses discrete algorithms.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the continuous-time analysis.

Originality: The generalization of averaging schemes and adaptive heuristic are novel contributions, though the connection to replicator dynamics was previously explored in Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016).

Significance: The paper advances our understanding of accelerated methods through continuous-time dynamics, which is valuable for algorithm design. However, the practical significance is limited by the incomplete discrete-time analysis, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the adaptive averaging provides a substantial advantage over existing methods in practical applications.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper provides a unified continuous-time framework for accelerated mirror descent with generalized averaging, the adaptive averaging heuristic is theoretically grounded, and the connection to replicator dynamics opens new applications.

Arguments against acceptance: The incomplete discrete-time analysis is a serious limitation, the related work is insufficiently addressed, and the paper doesn't provide a thorough discussion of whether continuous-time improvements persist in discrete settings.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of accelerated methods through continuous dynamics, but its practical impact is undermined by the lack of discrete-time analysis. The authors should address these limitations by providing a complete discrete-time analysis, expanding the related work section to properly contextualize their contributions, and more thoroughly discussing the discretization question. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a solid theoretical paper that advances our understanding of continuous-time optimization dynamics but falls short in terms of practical relevance for the optimization community.
