This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization through a continuous-time primal–dual ODE with generalized averaging. Building on the primal–dual decomposition of accelerated mirror descent (AMD), the authors define trajectories where the dual variable Z integrates gradients at a rate η(t) while the primal variable X is the weighted average of the mirror map of Z using weights w(t). A Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) yields sufficient conditions on η and w that guarantee a target decay f(X(t))−f* ≤ O(1/r(t)). This unifies and extends the averaging viewpoint on acceleration (e.g., Krichene et al. NIPS’15; Flammarion–Bach on averaging vs. acceleration) and connects to the inertial ODE literature (Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan).

The authors derive a primal representation of the dynamics and give explicit examples for the positive orthant and the simplex. On the simplex, the classical replicator dynamics appears as mirror descent, and the paper introduces an “accelerated replicator” via the generalized AMD, illuminating how acceleration can be realized by averaging the mirrored dual trajectory. The paper also introduces a second energy functional Er that decreases under the same conditions, offering an interpretable potential/kinetic decomposition.

A key contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic: instead of fixing w(t), they adapt a(t) = w(t)/W(t) to reduce the time derivative of the Lyapunov function when possible, thereby preserving the continuous-time rate 1/r(t). They provide a discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) that preserves the quadratic O(1/k^2) rate of first-order accelerated schemes, and empirically compare it to gradient and speed restarting heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès; Su et al.). Across several simplex-constrained problems (quadratic, linear, KL), adaptive averaging matches or outperforms restarting, notably in the strongly convex case, while maintaining a decreasing Lyapunov function.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well positioned with respect to continuous-time interpretations of acceleration (Nesterov; Su–Boyd–Candès), variational viewpoints (Wibisono et al.), and restarting (O’Donoghue–Candès). It would benefit from a broader discussion of inertial gradient flows with vanishing/damping terms and associated Lyapunov analyses (Attouch, Peypouquet, Redont), which are closely related in spirit and may inform stronger-rate guarantees under strong convexity.

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis is rigorous. The existence/uniqueness argument is clean; the Lyapunov proof is transparent, and the Er energy is insightful.
- The general conditions of Theorem 2 are trajectory-dependent, but the authors give tractable sufficient conditions (Corollary 1) and constructive recipes (via a(t) and equation (4)). This is a clear strength.
- The continuous-time framework supports rates 1/r(t) beyond 1/t^2 (e.g., r(t)=t^p), and even permits exponential r(t) in principle (r′/r constant) when appropriate convexity holds; this point could be made more explicit.
- The discrete-time analysis in the main paper is limited: while the authors claim preservation of O(1/k^2) and defer proofs to the supplement, the paper would be stronger with a concise in-paper theorem and assumptions (step-size vs. L, parameter ranges, stability).
- Experiments are illustrative but small scale (R^3), focused on simplex constraints. They convincingly show the monotonicity of Lr under adaptive averaging and favorable performance vs. restarting.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and organized. The role of w, η, and r is clearly explained.
- Suggest clarifying the terminology “quadratic rate”: in optimization it often denotes local superlinear convergence; here it means O(1/t^2) or O(1/k^2).
- The recipe for constructing w and η from a desired r is helpful; adding a short “practitioner’s” section on choosing β, step size s, and diagnosing the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ would aid reproducibility.
- The primal examples (orthant, simplex/replicator) are a strong pedagogical addition.

Originality
- Generalizing averaging in AMD to achieve a prescribed rate via Lyapunov design is novel and insightful.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic contrasts with restarting: it continuously modulates weights to keep the Lyapunov derivative nonpositive, with guarantees. This is distinct from standard restart triggers.
- The accelerated replicator interpretation is original and likely to interest communities beyond optimization.

Significance
- Conceptually, the work broadens how we think about acceleration: as a design problem over averaging weights with Lyapunov guidance.
- Practically, the heuristic is simple and competitive with widely used restart techniques; extending to higher dimensions and broader constraint sets could make it impactful in ML applications.
- The potential to target faster continuous-time rates is intriguing; demonstrating which ones carry over to discrete first-order methods would raise the practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear continuous-time theory with constructive sufficient conditions and new energy function.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with guarantees and empirical improvements over restarting.
- Insightful examples, including accelerated replicator dynamics.
- Good writing and cohesion; adds to the growing ODE-based understanding of acceleration.

Cons for acceptance
- Main-text discrete-time analysis is minimal; no finite-time complexity theorem presented in the body.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scale and scope; no large-scale or non-simplex benchmarks.
- Practical guidance on parameter selection and robustness is light.
- Limited discussion of closely related inertial ODE literature (Attouch–Peypouquet et al.) and strong convexity/linear rates within this framework.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the authors formalize linear (exponential) rates in continuous time under strong convexity by choosing r(t)=e^{μt}, and does adaptive averaging preserve this after discretization?
- Provide a succinct in-paper theorem for Algorithm 1: assumptions on L, step-size, and parameters to guarantee O(1/k^2).
- Explore larger-scale experiments and other constraint sets (e.g., ℓ1 ball, spectrahedron).
- Clarify how sensitive performance is to β and s, and whether the heuristic can be made parameter-free.
- Discuss connections and differences with inertial ODEs with vanishing damping and their Lyapunov functions (Attouch, Peypouquet, Redont).

Overall, this is an interesting and well-executed paper with solid theory and a practical heuristic. Strengthening the discrete-time guarantees in the main text and expanding experiments would make it even more compelling. I lean toward acceptance.
