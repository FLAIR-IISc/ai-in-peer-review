This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, presenting a unified framework for analyzing and designing accelerated methods. The authors derive sufficient conditions for convergence rates based on a Lyapunov argument and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to potentially speed up convergence. The key insight is that adaptively adjusting averaging weights along solution trajectories can yield performance comparable to, and sometimes exceeding, existing heuristics like adaptive restarting.

The paper builds upon a growing body of work connecting Nesterov’s accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs [20, 2, 1, 3, 11, 23]. Specifically, it generalizes the framework presented in [11], analyzing a broader family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions for both the dual variable accumulation rate and the primal averaging scheme. This generalization allows for a more systematic study of the interplay between averaging and acceleration. The connection to replicator dynamics [22] and evolutionary game theory is a nice illustration of the broader applicability of the proposed framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The Lyapunov argument provides a solid theoretical foundation for analyzing the convergence properties of the proposed AMD dynamics. The sufficient conditions established in Theorem 2 are clearly stated and reasonably accessible.
*   **Novelty:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution. While the idea of adaptivity is not new (e.g., [7, 10, 17, 20]), the authors provide a principled approach based on decreasing the Lyapunov function and demonstrate its potential through both theoretical analysis and experiments.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are relatively easy to follow. The inclusion of a primal representation (Section 3) and concrete examples (simplex-constrained dynamics) enhances understanding.
*   **Significance:** The results suggest that simple modifications to averaging schemes can significantly impact convergence speed, offering a potentially valuable tool for practitioners and researchers. The preservation of the quadratic convergence rate, demonstrated in discrete time, is a particularly strong result.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments (Section 5) provide preliminary evidence supporting the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging heuristic. Comparisons with established heuristics like adaptive restarting demonstrate competitive performance.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments, while promising, are somewhat limited in scope. The comparisons are primarily conducted on the simplex with a few different objective functions. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of problem types and dimensions would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Discussion of Hyperparameters:** The selection of hyperparameters (e.g., β, s in Algorithm 1) is not discussed in detail. Providing guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice would be helpful.
*   **Connection to Existing Adaptive Methods:** The paper briefly acknowledges adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam [7, 10], but the connection could be explored further. It would be beneficial to discuss how the proposed adaptive averaging differs from, and potentially complements, these existing techniques.
*  **Minor Technical Point**: The link to Attouch et al. [24] regarding the rate of convergence of Nesterov's method appears outdated - the current best known rate is indeed better than 1/k^2.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel contribution to the understanding of accelerated descent dynamics.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic is a promising approach for improving the performance of existing accelerated methods.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a solid empirical validation of the proposed ideas.
*   The connection to replicator dynamics adds breadth and potential applications in other fields.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is limited, and further validation is needed on a wider range of problems.
*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter selection and a more thorough comparison with existing adaptive methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed heuristic is novel and promising, and the experimental results are encouraging. While the experimental evaluation could be expanded, the existing results provide sufficient evidence to support the claims made in the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more comprehensive experimental evaluation and a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter selection. Additionally, updating the reference to Attouch et al. would be appreciated.
