- Studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order optimization on constrained convex problems, generalizing Krichene et al.'s primal-dual ODE framework.  
- Introduces a family of dynamics parameterized by gradient accumulation rate η(t) and averaging weight w(t), generalizing the primal averaging scheme.  
- Main theoretical contribution: unified Lyapunov analysis giving sufficient conditions on η(t) and w(t) to achieve convergence rate 1/r(t).  
- Shows the continuous-time framework can accommodate rates faster than canonical O(1/t^2).  
- Proposes a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that greedily decreases the Lyapunov function while preserving the theoretical rate guarantee.  
- Provides a discrete version of the adaptive algorithm and empirical comparisons on simplex-constrained problems.  
- Strength — rigorous and elegant continuous-time theory using a Lyapunov function that unifies accelerated dynamics and clarifies averaging’s role.  
- Strength — the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and comes with theoretical guarantees (preserves O(1/t^2)), unlike many restarting schemes.  
- Strength — paper is clear, well-organized, and provides insightful examples (e.g., accelerated replicator dynamics).  
- Strength — empirical results (albeit low-dimensional) show the adaptive heuristic performs as well as or better than standard accelerated methods and restarting schemes.  
- Weakness — insufficient discussion of related work on inertial dynamics and Hessian-driven damping (Attouch, Peypouquet, et al.) that offer an alternative continuous-time view.  
- Weakness — theoretical analysis of the discrete adaptive algorithm is deferred to the supplement; main text lacks a discrete Lyapunov sketch or final convergence theorem.  
- Weakness — implications of achieving faster continuous-time rates are unclear after discretization; need discussion on whether such rates are realizable by first-order discrete methods.  
- For acceptance: novel generalization, sound elegant theory, practical adaptive heuristic with guarantees, clear writing, and compelling empirical evidence.  
- Against acceptance: missing discrete-theory justification in main text and omission of relevant inertial-dynamics literature.
