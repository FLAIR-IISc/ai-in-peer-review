- Proposes adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated constrained convex optimization, preserving quadratic convergence of accelerated first-order methods.
- Formulates accelerated mirror descent as coupled dual/primal dynamics controlled by weight functions η(t) and w(t).
- Derives sufficient conditions on η and w for desired convergence rates using a Lyapunov-based analysis.
- Introduces an adaptive rule for w(t) based on the Lyapunov function’s decrease to dynamically adjust averaging.
- Theoretical guarantee that adaptive averaging preserves original convergence rate—uncommon among existing heuristics.
- Builds on continuous-time ODE interpretations of Nesterov acceleration, extending Krichene et al. (2016) and Su et al. (2016).
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and mathematically well-defined, with clear conditions on weights.
- Novel contribution: adaptive averaging heuristic that differs from and can outperform adaptive restarting methods.
- Empirical results show comparable or improved performance versus existing heuristics in the reported experiments.
- Paper is dense and difficult to follow; needs more background on mirror descent and Lyapunov methods for accessibility.
- Key equations (lines 33–34, 46–47) lack intuitive explanations and term-by-term interpretation.
- Numerical experiments are limited to R^3, raising concerns about scalability to higher-dimensional, practical ML problems.
- Related-work discussion should better contrast with adaptive methods in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad, Adam).
- Figure 1 is unclear (ambiguous "solid line"); presentation/legend should better distinguish trajectories.
- Arguments for acceptance: solid theory, novel heuristic, convergence-rate preservation, and supportive empirical evidence.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited clarity, weak scalability evidence, and incomplete related-work connections.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions focused on readability, clearer equation explanations, broader related-work discussion, and higher-dimensional experiments.
