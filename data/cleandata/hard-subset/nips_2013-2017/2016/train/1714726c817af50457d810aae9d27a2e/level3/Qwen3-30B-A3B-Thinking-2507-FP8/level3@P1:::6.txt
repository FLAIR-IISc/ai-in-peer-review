

This paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic for constrained convex optimization, building on the continuous-time accelerated mirror descent framework. The authors establish a unified Lyapunov analysis for a broad family of accelerated descent dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), showing sufficient conditions for achieving desired convergence rates. The key innovation is an adaptive averaging scheme that dynamically computes weights to accelerate convergence while preserving the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated methods in continuous-time. The authors demonstrate that their approach can accelerate the replicator dynamics (mirror descent on the simplex) and provide numerical evidence that adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics in several test cases.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing literature on continuous-time interpretations of accelerated optimization methods, extending the work of Su et al. (NIPS 2014) and Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The Lyapunov analysis framework is well-motivated and provides a theoretical foundation for the adaptive averaging heuristic, which differs from previous work on adaptive methods like Adagrad (Duchi et al., JMLR 2011) and Adam (Kingma & Ba, ICLR 2015) in that it is specifically designed for accelerated methods rather than standard first-order methods.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical foundation is sound: The Lyapunov analysis in Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 provides clear sufficient conditions for convergence rates, and the adaptive averaging heuristic is rigorously shown to preserve the convergence rate.
2. Theoretical novelty: The paper establishes a unified framework for accelerated descent dynamics that generalizes previous results and provides a natural explanation for acceleration through averaging.
3. Experimental validation: The numerical experiments in Section 5 demonstrate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as restarting heuristics, with significant improvements in the strongly convex case.

Weaknesses:
1. Clarity issues: The paper is difficult to follow due to insufficient background on the continuous-time optimization framework. The key equations (33-34 and 46-47) require more intuitive explanation, particularly the connection between the dual variable Z(t), the primal variable X(t), and the averaging scheme. The authors should clarify these relationships before presenting the main theorems.
2. Limited experimental scope: The experiments are conducted in R^3 (simplex in 3 dimensions), which is a toy setup that doesn't demonstrate scalability to practical problems. The authors should provide evidence of performance on higher-dimensional problems or real-world datasets to support the practical significance of their method.
3. Relation to non-convex methods: The paper fails to discuss how their adaptive averaging heuristic relates to adaptive methods used in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad, Adam), which would strengthen the theoretical context and highlight the unique contributions of their approach.
4. Figure 1 ambiguity: The reference to the "solid line" for the primal trajectory is unclear; using different line styles or colors would improve readability.

Regarding the four review criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical results are technically sound and well-supported, though the experimental validation is limited in scope.
- Clarity: The paper would benefit significantly from improved exposition, particularly in explaining the continuous-time dynamics and their relation to discrete optimization algorithms.
- Originality: The paper makes a clear contribution by extending the Lyapunov analysis to a broader family of dynamics and proposing a novel adaptive averaging scheme that preserves convergence rates.
- Significance: The significance is somewhat limited by the narrow experimental validation; while the theoretical contribution is important, the practical impact would be stronger with evidence of performance on larger-scale problems.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of continuous-time accelerated optimization methods with a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates. The unified Lyapunov framework provides valuable insights into acceleration through averaging.

Con arguments for acceptance: The paper's limited experimental scope (R^3 only) and unclear exposition diminish its impact. The connection to non-convex adaptive methods is not discussed, and the practical significance is not sufficiently demonstrated.

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS, but requires substantial revisions to address clarity concerns and broaden the experimental validation. The theoretical contribution is strong, but the paper would benefit from a more accessible presentation of the continuous-time dynamics and evidence of scalability to larger problems. The authors should clarify the relationship between their adaptive averaging heuristic and existing adaptive methods in non-convex optimization, and provide experiments in higher dimensions to demonstrate practical relevance. The Figure 1 ambiguity should also be resolved with clearer visual distinctions between trajectory components.
