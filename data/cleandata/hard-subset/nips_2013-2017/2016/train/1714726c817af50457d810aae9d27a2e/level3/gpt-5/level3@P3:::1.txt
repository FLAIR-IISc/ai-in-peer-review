Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated mirror-descent dynamics through the ODE viewpoint pioneered by Su et al. (NIPS’15) and Krichene et al. (NIPS’15). It introduces a two-parameter family of accelerated dynamics AMDw,η that couples a dual variable Z accumulating gradients at rate η(t) with a primal variable X that is a weighted average of a transformed dual trajectory with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors give simple sufficient conditions on (w, η, r) under which Lr decreases and f(X(t)) − f* = O(1/r(t)). This unifies prior fixed-weight analyses (e.g., r(t)=t^2) and exhibits a broad class of averaging schemes that preserve the accelerated rate.

Building on this, the paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses a(t)=w(t)/W(t) on the fly to further decrease d/dt Lr whenever possible while preserving the target rate. In particular, the adaptive rule guarantees it never underperforms a fixed schedule that merely makes the second term in the Lr derivative vanish. The paper also presents a second “energy” functional Er that is shown to be nonincreasing under the same conditions, offering additional interpretability.

A primal representation is derived and illustrated on the positive orthant and on the simplex, yielding an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics. A discrete-time scheme (Algorithm 1) is obtained for the quadratic rate r(t)=t^2 and is shown to preserve the O(1/k^2) behavior; experiments compare adaptive averaging to adaptive restarting (O’Donoghue & Candès; Su et al.) on several convex objectives, showing consistent speed-ups and monotone Lyapunov decrease for the proposed method.

This work closely extends Krichene et al. (NIPS’15), Su et al., and connects to the variational perspective of Wibisono et al., while offering a new adaptive mechanism with guarantees, contrasting with prior restarting heuristics that lack non-worsening guarantees outside special cases.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean ODE framework: a single Lyapunov function yields simple conditions for accelerated rates with generalized averaging.
- Natural adaptive heuristic with a guarantee to dominate fixed-schedule averaging by minimizing the instantaneous Lyapunov decrease; unlike most restarting heuristics, the target rate is preserved by construction.
- Clear link to widely used accelerated methods; small modification with potentially large practical impact.
- Empirical results show promising and sometimes dramatic speed-ups across a range of convex functions, and the Lyapunov monotonicity is observed in practice.
- The mathematical development is easy to follow for readers familiar with Krichene et al.; the energy functional adds intuition.

Weaknesses and questions
- Technical novelty is incremental: the analysis is a close extension of prior ODE/Lyapunov arguments; the main novelty is the adaptive choice of weights.
- Discrete-time theory: the preservation of the O(1/k^2) rate for the adaptive scheme is mostly delegated to the supplement; it would be valuable to articulate precise conditions (step sizes, β choices, regularizers) in the main text and clarify whether the adaptive rule provably dominates fixed-schedule averaging in discrete time, not only in continuous time.
- Empirical scope is modest (3D simplex, with some higher-dimensional results in the supplement). It would be helpful to include higher-dimensional machine learning benchmarks (e.g., logistic regression, LASSO) and mirror maps beyond entropy.
- Strong convexity: can the adaptive averaging improve linear-rate constants or yield provable faster rates under strong convexity?
- Terminology: “quadratic convergence rate” may be confusing (classically denotes superlinear convergence); consider “O(1/t^2) accelerated rate.”

Quality
- Technically sound within the stated assumptions; the Lyapunov and energy arguments are correct and transparent. The adaptive rule has a clear guarantee in continuous time. The discrete-time preservation claim appears plausible but should be stated and proved more explicitly in the main text (assumptions, parameter choices, stability).

Clarity
- Generally well written and well organized. The ODE, primal form, and examples are helpful. Minor suggestions: define all symbols at first appearance; move core discrete-time assumptions/proofs (for the adaptive scheme) from the supplement; clarify initialization at t0>0 and its discretization counterpart.

Originality
- The adaptive averaging idea is new within the ODE/Lyapunov framework and differs from existing restarts. The technical machinery is a close extension of prior work, but the adaptive domination guarantee is novel and practically relevant.

Significance
- High potential impact due to the ubiquity of Nesterov-type methods. A small, principled modification with monotonic Lyapunov decrease and empirical gains will likely be adopted and extended. The accelerated replicator example may interest communities beyond optimization.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Move the core discrete-time analysis for the adaptive rule into the main paper: state a theorem with precise conditions under which O(1/k^2) is preserved and (if true) a pointwise domination result versus fixed weights.
- Provide an explicit comparison to fixed-schedule AMD on the same problems to substantiate “domination” in discrete time (objective gap vs iterations).
- Analyze discretization error and stability: sensitivity to step size s, choice of β, and mirror map smoothness; characterize when the adaptive schedule remains stable.
- Strongly convex case: investigate whether adaptive averaging improves linear-rate constants or achieves provably faster phases (e.g., matching CG-like behavior on quadratics).
- Explore connections to Conjugate Gradient for strongly convex quadratics: does the adaptive weighting emulate CG’s implicit polynomial acceleration or conjugacy properties?
- Add higher-dimensional experiments on standard ML tasks and mirror maps; include wall-clock comparisons and overhead of adaptive updates; ablations on the acceptance rule (line 7–8 of Algorithm 1).
- Relate more explicitly to Flammarion & Bach (averaging-acceleration link) and to Wibisono et al.’s variational framework.

Reasons to accept
- Simple, well-motivated adaptive mechanism with clear guarantees; dominates fixed schedules in continuous time.
- Empirically competitive or superior to widely used restarting heuristics.
- Clear, teachable analysis and potential broad adoption.

Reasons to reject
- Incremental theoretical novelty relative to prior ODE/Lyapunov analyses.
- Discrete-time guarantees for the adaptive scheme are not fully foregrounded in the main text and could be strengthened.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The contribution is conceptually simple, well-justified, and practically meaningful; with a stronger and more prominent discrete-time analysis, this paper would make a solid addition to the literature on accelerated first-order methods.
