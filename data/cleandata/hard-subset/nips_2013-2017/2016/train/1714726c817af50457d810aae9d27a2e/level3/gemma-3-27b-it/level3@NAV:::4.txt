## Review of “Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics”

This paper presents a compelling investigation into accelerated descent dynamics framed as a coupled dual-primal system, introducing an adaptive averaging heuristic to improve convergence. The authors skillfully build upon recent work connecting accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs, notably extending the framework from [11, 20] to a more general averaging scheme. The core idea – that adaptively weighting solution trajectories can yield empirical speedups comparable to established accelerated techniques – is both novel and intuitively appealing. 

The paper is structured logically, beginning with a theoretical analysis establishing sufficient conditions for convergence via a Lyapunov argument (Theorem 2).  This is a strong foundation.  The subsequent derivation of a primal representation (Section 3) clarifies the dynamics and allows for a concrete example – an accelerated version of the replicator equation – demonstrating the method’s applicability. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic (Section 4) is well-motivated and maintains theoretical guarantees on the convergence rate, a significant strength. Finally, the numerical experiments (Section 5) provide empirical support, showing competitive performance with existing heuristics like adaptive restarting. 

A particular strength lies in the connection drawn between the proposed averaging scheme and existing accelerated methods. The authors convincingly argue that adaptive averaging offers a different perspective on achieving acceleration, potentially providing further insights into the underlying dynamics. The paper clearly references related work, including key contributions in continuous-time optimization [6, 9, 5, 20, 2, 1, 3, 11, 23] and accelerated methods [14, 15].  The inclusion of related work from evolutionary game theory (specifically regarding the replicator dynamics) adds breadth and context.

However, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the computational cost of implementing the adaptive heuristic. While the theoretical guarantees are promising, the practical overhead of adaptively computing weights could be a limiting factor in certain applications. Furthermore, while the numerical experiments are encouraging, a broader range of problem instances and comparisons with more sophisticated adaptive methods (e.g., those utilizing second-order information) would strengthen the findings.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and insightful connection between averaging and acceleration.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with clear convergence guarantees.
*   Well-motivated and practical adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   Competitive empirical performance.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational cost of the adaptive heuristic.
*   Scope of numerical experiments could be expanded.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The novel perspective on accelerated descent, coupled with the theoretical guarantees and empirical validation, makes it worthy of acceptance to NIPS. I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions addressing the points raised regarding computational cost and the expansion of the numerical experiments.
