

This paper extends the continuous-time interpretation of Nesterov's acceleration (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015) to propose an adaptive averaging scheme for accelerated descent dynamics. The authors study a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, and provide sufficient conditions for these dynamics to achieve desired convergence rates using Lyapunov analysis. Their key contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights on-the-fly to accelerate convergence while preserving the theoretical guarantees of the continuous-time dynamics. The paper demonstrates that this approach outperforms existing restarting heuristics across multiple problem classes, including simplex-constrained optimization where it accelerates the replicator dynamics.

The paper excels in quality through its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing (Theorem 2), providing a unified framework that generalizes prior work. The adaptive averaging heuristic (Section 4) is particularly compelling as it guarantees preservation of the convergence rate (unlike prior adaptive methods), which is a significant theoretical contribution. The proof techniques are clean and build naturally on the authors' previous work (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015), making the mathematical development accessible to readers familiar with continuous-time optimization dynamics. The experimental section (Section 5) provides thorough empirical validation across multiple problem types (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, and KL divergence objectives), with clear visualization of the solution trajectories and energy functions. The results consistently show adaptive averaging outperforming restarting heuristics, with particularly notable improvements in the strongly convex case.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors provide a careful exposition of the continuous-time dynamics, with clear definitions and intuitive explanations of the key concepts. The connection between the continuous-time analysis and discrete-time implementation is well-motivated, and the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise. The supplementary material (referenced throughout) provides additional context on mirror maps and convex analysis, which would be helpful for readers less familiar with these concepts. The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical development to empirical validation.

The originality is moderate but significant. The paper doesn't introduce a fundamentally new framework but rather extends existing work on continuous-time interpretations of acceleration with a simple yet powerful adaptive mechanism. However, this extension is non-trivial and addresses a practical limitation of existing methods: prior adaptive approaches (like restarting) often lack convergence rate guarantees, while the proposed method preserves these guarantees. The authors effectively position their work relative to prior NIPS papers (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015; Su et al., NIPS 2014) and other relevant literature, making it clear how this work differs from previous contributions.

The significance is high. Accelerated gradient methods are ubiquitous in machine learning and optimization, yet they often require careful tuning of parameters for optimal performance. The adaptive averaging heuristic proposed here is simple to implement, requires no additional hyperparameters, and provides consistent empirical improvements over existing heuristics. The paper demonstrates that a small adaptive modification can yield dramatic improvements over fixed-weight Nesterov schemes, which has substantial practical implications for practitioners. The connection to replicator dynamics (for simplex constraints) also opens new applications in evolutionary game theory and viability theory.

Strengths:
- Theoretical guarantees are rigorous and well-justified
- Empirical results are comprehensive and compelling
- The adaptive heuristic is simple yet effective
- Clear connection to prior NIPS work
- High practical relevance for widely used optimization methods

Weaknesses:
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the discretized process to show convergence rate preservation (the authors note this as a future direction)
- The connection to Conjugate Gradient for strongly convex quadratics is only suggested as future work without exploration
- The experimental section could include more diverse problem instances to further demonstrate robustness

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for practitioners using accelerated methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a practical solution to a common problem in implementing accelerated methods, with theoretical guarantees that prior adaptive approaches lack. The work is technically sound, well-written, and demonstrates clear significance for the machine learning community. The authors successfully extend prior work while providing a method with immediate practical utility.

Arguments for acceptance:
1. The paper provides a theoretically grounded adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence rate guarantees
2. The empirical results consistently show improvements over existing methods
3. The mathematical development is clear and builds naturally on prior work
4. The practical impact is substantial given the widespread use of accelerated methods
5. The paper is well-written and well-organized

Arguments against acceptance:
1. The theoretical extension is not highly novel (though the adaptive heuristic is)
2. The discretization analysis could be more thorough
3. The connection to Conjugate Gradient is only suggested as future work

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature with strong theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. The adaptive averaging heuristic has the potential to become a standard component in accelerated optimization algorithms. I strongly recommend acceptance.
