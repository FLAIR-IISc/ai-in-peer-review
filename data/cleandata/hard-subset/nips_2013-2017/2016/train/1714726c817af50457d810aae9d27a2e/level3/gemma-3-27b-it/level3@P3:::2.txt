This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics through the lens of ODEs, extending previous work on Mirror Descent with a generalized averaging scheme and introducing a novel adaptive averaging heuristic. The central idea – relating optimization dynamics to coupled dual/primal variables and leveraging Lyapunov arguments – is intuitively appealing and builds upon recent advancements connecting Nesterov-style acceleration to continuous-time ODEs, notably the work of Su et al. (2016) and the authors’ prior NIPS 2015 paper. The paper presents a unified study of these dynamics and provides useful primal representations, including an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Intuition:** The adaptive averaging heuristic, motivated by decreasing the Lyapunov function, is conceptually sound and provides a clear rationale for its design.
* **Unified Framework:** The paper provides a generalized framework for analyzing AMD dynamics, extending existing results and offering a potentially valuable perspective.
* **Novelty (Adaptive Averaging):** While building on existing foundations, the adaptive averaging heuristic itself appears novel and is well-motivated within the established framework.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The Lyapunov function analysis provides some theoretical justification for the proposed method, offering guarantees under certain conditions.
* **Insights from Analyses:** The primal reformulation and reduction to replicator dynamics deepen understanding of the dynamics but are less directly applicable to ML advances.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Originality:** A significant portion of the theoretical framework—the Lyapunov function approach, discretization schemes—directly follows from the authors' previous NIPS 2015 paper and related work. This limits the incremental contribution.
* **Trivial Extension:** The extension from simple to generalized averaging is presented as a major component but appears relatively trivial, and the analysis primarily focuses on the quadratic rate case.
* **Heuristic Nature:** Despite the Lyapunov analysis, the adaptive averaging scheme is ultimately a heuristic. The discretization introduces approximations, and a rigorous convergence proof remains elusive. This reliance on approximations weakens the theoretical foundation.
* **Lack of Comparison:** There is no comparison to other existing averaging schemes for Nesterov-style algorithms. Without this benchmarking, it is difficult to assess the practical advantage of the proposed adaptive averaging.
* **Limited Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is confined to a toy quadratic/linear objective. This severely limits the generalizability of the findings and makes it difficult to draw conclusions about performance in more realistic, strongly convex, or non-convex scenarios. The experiments are not convincing for practical application.
* **Unfulfilled Promises:** The authors committed to substantial additions post-rebuttal (high-dimensional experiments, convergence proof, implementation on Newton's method, comparisons to other schemes). These are crucial for addressing the current weaknesses and bolstering the paper's claims.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its mathematical derivations, but the overall impact is diminished by the reliance on prior work and the lack of compelling empirical evidence. It feels more like a position paper elaborating on existing ideas than a complete, self-contained contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although the mathematical notation can be dense at times. The motivation for the adaptive averaging scheme is clearly explained.

**Originality:** The originality is moderate. While the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, the underlying framework and theoretical tools are largely borrowed from existing literature, specifically the authors' previous work.

**Significance:** The significance is currently limited. While the ideas are interesting, the lack of strong experimental results and the heuristic nature of the method hinder its potential impact. The unfulfilled post-rebuttal commitments are key to elevating the significance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel, intuitively appealing adaptive averaging heuristic.
* Provides a unified framework for analyzing accelerated descent dynamics.
* Builds upon recent advancements in the field, connecting optimization to ODEs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Limited originality due to heavy reliance on prior work.
* The extension of existing frameworks is largely trivial.
* Adaptive averaging remains a heuristic without a rigorous convergence proof.
* Experimental evaluation is inadequate and unconvincing.
* Critical post-rebuttal commitments remain unaddressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper, in its current form, is a borderline submission. While the ideas are interesting and the analysis is technically sound, the lack of substantial novelty, the heuristic nature of the method, and the limited experimental validation raise serious concerns. The unfulfilled post-rebuttal commitments are crucial for addressing these weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to resubmit once the authors have addressed the aforementioned concerns, particularly providing a comprehensive experimental evaluation on more challenging datasets and delivering on the promised convergence proof and comparisons to other averaging schemes. The paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, but it currently falls short of NIPS quality.
