

This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization using continuous-time dynamics, extending Krichene et al.'s primal-dual ODE approach. The authors focus on the primal trajectory obtained through a generalized averaging scheme of the mirrored dual variable, providing sufficient conditions on the averaging coefficients to guarantee specific convergence rates. The work builds on the continuous-time interpretation of accelerated methods, particularly Su et al.'s connection between Nesterov's method and second-order ODEs.

The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 2, which establishes convergence rates under suitable conditions on the averaging coefficients. The authors show that in the continuous setting, they can achieve convergence rates faster than the usual 1/t^2. They propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function, with provable convergence guarantees. Empirical results demonstrate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as adaptive restarting techniques, with significant improvements in some cases.

The paper is well-written, with a clear exposition of the continuous-time analysis. The connection to replicator dynamics for simplex constraints provides a concrete application example. The authors acknowledge the omission of discrete-time theoretical analysis, noting it as an important direction for future work.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. First, it fails to cite important prior work on related continuous-time dynamics by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors (referenced in the paper as "The rate of convergence of Nesterov's accelerated forward-backward method is actually faster than 1/k^2" and "Fast convergence of an inertial gradient-like system with vanishing viscosity"). This omission weakens the positioning of the work within the broader literature on continuous-time optimization dynamics.

Second, the omission of discrete-time theoretical analysis is a significant limitation. While the authors provide a discrete version of the adaptive averaging heuristic (Algorithm 1) and some empirical results, they do not prove convergence guarantees for the discrete algorithm. This is particularly problematic for a paper about optimization algorithms, as their practical implementation requires discrete-time analysis.

Third, the paper raises the crucial open question of whether improved continuous-time rates persist after discretization but does not address it. This question is central to the practical relevance of the continuous-time analysis for discrete optimization algorithms.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of accelerated optimization in continuous time, with a clear and rigorous analysis. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and shows promising empirical results. However, the omission of discrete-time analysis and the failure to properly cite related work are significant weaknesses that affect the paper's quality and significance.

In terms of the four criteria:
- Quality: The continuous-time analysis is strong, but the omission of discrete-time analysis weakens the quality assessment.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written and organized.
- Originality: The generalization of averaging schemes is original, but the connection to prior work is not sufficiently established.
- Significance: The work provides theoretical insights that could inform future algorithm design, but the practical significance is limited by the lack of discrete-time analysis.

Arguments for acceptance: The continuous-time analysis is rigorous and well-executed, the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, and the empirical results are promising. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods.

Arguments against acceptance: The omission of discrete-time theoretical analysis is a major weakness, the related work is not properly cited, and the crucial question of whether continuous-time improvements persist in discrete time remains unanswered.

Overall, the paper presents interesting theoretical insights, but the omission of discrete-time analysis and the failure to properly contextualize the work within the literature are significant drawbacks. While the continuous-time analysis is strong, the paper would be substantially more valuable with discrete-time convergence guarantees and proper citation of related work. I recommend rejection, with the understanding that the continuous-time analysis is promising and could form the basis for a stronger paper with discrete-time analysis added.
