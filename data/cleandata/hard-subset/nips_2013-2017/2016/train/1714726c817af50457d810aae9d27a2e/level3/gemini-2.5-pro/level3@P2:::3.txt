This paper studies continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order optimization on constrained convex problems. Building on the recent framework of Krichene et al. (2015), which models accelerated mirror descent as a coupled primal-dual ODE, this work generalizes the averaging scheme used to construct the primal trajectory. The authors introduce a family of dynamics parameterized by a gradient accumulation rate `η(t)` and an averaging weight function `w(t)`. The main theoretical contribution is a unified analysis via a Lyapunov function, providing sufficient conditions on `η(t)` and `w(t)` to achieve a desired convergence rate of `1/r(t)`. This framework is shown to accommodate rates faster than the canonical `O(1/t^2)` in continuous time.

Leveraging this analysis, the paper proposes a novel adaptive averaging heuristic. Instead of using a fixed schedule for the weights, the heuristic adaptively modifies them to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, thereby accelerating convergence while provably maintaining the theoretical rate guarantee. This is contrasted with popular restarting heuristics which often lack such guarantees. The authors provide a discrete version of the adaptive algorithm and present numerical experiments comparing it favorably against standard accelerated mirror descent and adaptive restarting schemes on several simplex-constrained problems.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Rigorous and Elegant Theory:** The paper's main strength lies in its continuous-time analysis. The use of a Lyapunov function to derive sufficient conditions on the averaging and gradient rate functions is a clean and powerful technique. It provides a unified perspective on a broad class of accelerated dynamics and offers clear insights into the role of averaging in acceleration.

2.  **Novel and Guaranteed Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution. Unlike many heuristics that are motivated by intuition, this method is directly derived from the goal of decreasing the Lyapunov function. The resulting guarantee that the `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate is preserved is a strong theoretical advantage over restarting schemes, for which such guarantees are often absent or limited to specific problem classes.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly motivate the problem, explain their generalization of the ODE dynamics, and logically build towards the adaptive heuristic. The connection between the primal-dual ODE and its purely primal representation is also well-explained, and the examples (e.g., deriving an accelerated replicator dynamics) are insightful.

4.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The numerical experiments, while on low-dimensional problems, convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging heuristic. It performs as well as or significantly better than established restarting techniques across different types of objective functions, validating the practical utility of the proposed idea.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Missing Discussion of Related Work:** The paper's connection to the literature on inertial dynamics and Hessian-driven damping is not sufficiently discussed. The work of Attouch, Peypouquet, and their collaborators provides a rich, alternative continuous-time view of Nesterov's acceleration as a second-order differential equation with vanishing friction. This perspective is highly relevant and should be cited and contrasted with the primal-dual averaging framework presented here (e.g., Attouch & Peypouquet, "Rate of convergence of Nesterov's accelerated forward-backward method", 2016; Attouch et al., "Fast convergence of an inertial gradient-like system with vanishing viscosity", 2016).

2.  **Analysis of the Discrete Algorithm:** The theoretical analysis for the discrete implementation of the adaptive heuristic (Algorithm 1) is deferred to the supplementary material. While space is limited, the absence of even a brief sketch of the discrete Lyapunov argument or the final convergence result in the main paper makes it difficult for a reader to fully assess the claims. Including the main theorem for the discrete case would substantially strengthen the paper.

3.  **Implications of Faster Continuous-Time Rates:** The paper shows that the continuous-time framework can achieve rates faster than `O(1/t^2)`. However, a footnote suggests that discretizing these dynamics leads to higher-order methods (e.g., cubic regularized Newton). This is a crucial point that deserves more prominent discussion. It raises the question of whether the faster rates are achievable by first-order discrete methods. If not, the practical significance of this specific theoretical result is limited. A clearer discussion of the link between the continuous rate `r(t)` and the complexity of the corresponding discrete algorithm would be valuable.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Provides a novel and well-motivated generalization of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent.
    *   The theoretical analysis is sound, elegant, and provides new insights into the role of averaging.
    *   Proposes an adaptive heuristic that is both practical and comes with strong theoretical guarantees, a rare combination.
    *   The paper is well-written and the empirical results are compelling.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The theoretical justification for the implemented discrete algorithm is missing from the main text.
    *   The paper omits discussion of a highly relevant line of work on inertial dynamics for optimization.

Overall, this is an interesting and high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution to the literature on accelerated optimization. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel and promising idea backed by both theory and experiments. The weaknesses, particularly the missing discussion of related work and the deferred analysis of the discrete algorithm, should be addressed but are not fatal flaws. I recommend acceptance.
