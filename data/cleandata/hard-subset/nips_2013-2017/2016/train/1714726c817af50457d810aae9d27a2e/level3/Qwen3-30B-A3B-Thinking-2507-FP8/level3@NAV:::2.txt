

This paper extends ODE-based accelerated Mirror Descent with a generalized averaging scheme and proposes an adaptive averaging strategy for discretization. Building on the authors' prior NIPS 2016 work, they establish a continuous-time framework where the dual variable accumulates gradients at rate η(t), while the primal variable is obtained as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory. The key contribution is an adaptive heuristic that computes weights to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function.

Strengths include clear intuition for the adaptive averaging approach and its preservation of quadratic convergence rates in continuous time. The unified Lyapunov framework provides a useful theoretical perspective, and the replicator dynamics example demonstrates practical applicability to constrained optimization.

However, the paper faces significant limitations. The extension from simple to generalized averaging is trivial, with only the quadratic-rate case (r(t)=t²) considered, which was already covered in the authors' prior work. The adaptive averaging lacks a rigorous convergence proof due to discretization approximations, making it effectively a heuristic rather than a theoretically grounded method. The experiments are limited to toy quadratic/linear objectives on the simplex, failing to demonstrate practical advantage for general or strongly convex cases. Crucially, the paper does not compare with other existing adaptive averaging schemes for Nesterov-style algorithms, obscuring the practical significance of the proposed method.

The originality is questionable as many elements directly follow from the authors' prior NIPS paper. While the energy function interpretation and primal reformulation provide some theoretical insights, they offer limited advancement for machine learning applications. The paper's focus on the quadratic-rate case is particularly limiting, as achieving faster rates would require higher-order methods not addressed here.

The post-rebuttal commitments to add high-dimensional experiments, convergence proof, and comparisons to other schemes are promising but not present in the current submission. Given the limited novelty, lack of theoretical grounding for the key contribution, and insufficient experimental validation, this paper does not meet the quality threshold for NIPS acceptance.
