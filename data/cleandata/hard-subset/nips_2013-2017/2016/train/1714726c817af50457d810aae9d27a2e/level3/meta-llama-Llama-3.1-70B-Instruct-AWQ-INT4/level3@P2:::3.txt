This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization in both continuous and discrete settings, building upon the primal-dual ODE approach of Krichene et al. The authors focus on the primal trajectory obtained by a specific averaging of the mirror of the dual variable and generalize the averaging schemes. The paper provides a unified analysis of the accelerated mirror descent dynamics using a Lyapunov argument, leading to a sufficient condition on the averaging coefficients to achieve a desired convergence rate.

The main contributions of the paper are threefold. Firstly, the authors derive a broad family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by two positive, continuous weight functions w and η, where w is used in the averaging and η determines the rate at which the dual variable accumulates gradients. The authors provide a unified study of this ODE using an appropriate Lyapunov function, which leads to a sufficient condition on η, w, and the desired convergence rate r for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing. This condition is used to establish convergence rates faster than the usual 1/t^2 in the continuous setting.

Secondly, the authors propose an adaptive choice of averaging weights with provable convergence guarantees. The heuristic adaptively computes the weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function, and the authors show that it preserves the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods in discrete time.

Lastly, the authors empirically compare the adaptive weighting scheme to restarting techniques known to improve accelerated methods. The experiments indicate that the adaptive averaging performs at least as well as the restarting heuristics, with significant improvements in some cases.

The paper is well-written, and the continuous analysis is rigorous. However, the theoretical analysis of the discrete version of the algorithm is omitted, which is a significant limitation. Additionally, the reviewer notes that prior related work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors should be cited.

The paper raises an interesting open question: whether the improved continuous rates persist after discretization. This is an important direction for future research, as it has the potential to lead to faster and more efficient optimization algorithms.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and adequately informs the reader. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques, and the adaptive averaging heuristic is a new contribution. The authors clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions and provide adequate references to related work.

**Significance:** The results are important, and other researchers are likely to use these ideas or build upon them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
