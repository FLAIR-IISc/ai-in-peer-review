- Paper analyzes continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with generalized averaging (w(t), η(t)) using a Lyapunov Lr and complementary energy Er.  
- Provides sufficient continuous-time conditions for prescribed rates r(t) and a primal reformulation with examples (including accelerated replicator dynamics).  
- Main novelty is an adaptive averaging heuristic choosing a(t) to reduce d/dt Lr and a discrete scheme intended to preserve 1/t^2 rate.  
- Continuous-time analysis is careful and technically sound with clear sufficient conditions for Lyapunov decrease.  
- Discrete adaptive averaging is under-justified: uses approximations and lacks a convergence proof, so rate preservation claims are heuristic.  
- Experiments are low-dimensional and mostly quadratic/linear, limiting empirical evidence and generality.  
- No comparisons to other averaging schedules used with Nesterov-type methods.  
- Presentation is clear and organized; intuition behind adaptive averaging is well explained.  
- Suggestion: explicitly separate rigorous continuous-time results from heuristic/discrete claims and group assumptions per theorem.  
- Generalized averaging and Lyapunov formulation largely extend prior NeurIPS AMD-ODE work; extension seems straightforward and focused on quadratic rates.  
- Adaptive averaging idea is novel and well-motivated but currently under-theorized.  
- Potential significance if given rigorous theory and high-dimensional robustness, but currently mainly conceptual with limited ML impact.  
- Pros: unified Lyapunov analysis, novel adaptive heuristic, sometimes outperforms restart heuristics while preserving Lyapunov decrease.  
- Cons: incremental over prior work, no discrete-time convergence proof, toy-scale experiments, missing comparisons to other averaging methods.  
- Reviewer leans to rejection unless authors add (i) rigorous convergence proof for adaptive averaging, (ii) high-dimensional/non-quadratic experiments, and (iii) broader comparisons including higher-order methods and other averaging schemes.
