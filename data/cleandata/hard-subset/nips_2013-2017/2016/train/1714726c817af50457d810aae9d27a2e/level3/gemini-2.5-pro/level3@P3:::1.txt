### **Summary**

This paper builds upon the continuous-time ordinary differential equation (ODE) interpretation of Nesterov's accelerated mirror descent, as previously formulated in Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). In that framework, the primal iterate `X(t)` is expressed as a specific weighted average of a mirrored dual trajectory. The key insight of this work is to generalize this averaging scheme by introducing flexible weight functions, `w(t)` and `η(t)`. Using a Lyapunov function analysis, the authors derive sufficient conditions on these weights to guarantee a desired `O(1/r(t))` convergence rate.

The main contribution is a novel and intuitive adaptive heuristic based on this analysis. The heuristic adaptively modifies the averaging weights `w(t)` to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function whenever possible, aiming to accelerate convergence beyond the guaranteed rate. A crucial feature of this adaptive scheme is that, by construction, it is guaranteed to preserve the original convergence rate, ensuring it performs at least as well as the standard fixed-schedule averaging. This provides a "safe" acceleration, a property not formally established for other popular heuristics like adaptive restarting. The authors present a discrete-time version of the algorithm and provide empirical results on several convex optimization problems, showing that their adaptive averaging method compares favorably with, and often significantly outperforms, both the standard accelerated method and adaptive restart schemes.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Potential Impact:** The paper tackles the important and practical problem of speeding up Nesterov's accelerated methods, which are foundational algorithms in machine learning and optimization. The proposed modification is simple, intuitive, and easy to implement, which increases the likelihood that it will be adopted by practitioners and researchers. The demonstration that a small, adaptive change can yield dramatic performance improvements over fixed schemes is a valuable contribution.

2.  **Theoretical Guarantees (Quality):** A major strength is the theoretical underpinning of the adaptive heuristic. Unlike many ad-hoc acceleration heuristics, the proposed method comes with a formal guarantee that it will never perform worse than the baseline accelerated method in terms of the Lyapunov function's decay. This is derived from a clean and simple argument in the continuous-time setting and provides strong justification for the method's robustness.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The mathematical argument, which flows naturally from the time derivative of the Lyapunov function, is elegant and insightful. The generalization from the fixed `w(t) = t^(r-1)` schedule to a broader class of functions is a clean conceptual step that effectively sets the stage for the adaptive scheme.

4.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical experiments provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of adaptive averaging. The comparison against standard acceleration and popular restarting heuristics across a variety of convex objective functions (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear) clearly demonstrates the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The visualizations of the trajectories and the Lyapunov/energy functions are particularly helpful for building intuition.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Originality:** The primary weakness is that the technical framework is a direct and incremental extension of the authors' prior work (Krichene et al., 2015). The core setup, including the ODE formulation and the choice of the Lyapunov function, is inherited from that paper. While the extension is not a major theoretical leap, the novelty and value lie in the idea of *adapting* the averaging scheme within this framework and providing guarantees for it, which is a significant practical and conceptual contribution.

2.  **Analysis of the Discretized Algorithm:** The paper's theoretical analysis is performed entirely in continuous time. While a discrete-time algorithm is presented (Algorithm 1), its analysis is deferred to the supplementary material. The main paper would be substantially stronger if it included at least a sketch of the analysis for the discrete case, showing how the `O(1/k^2)` rate is preserved. In particular, a brief discussion on how the discrete condition `f(x̃(k+1)) > f(x̃(k))` serves as a proxy for the continuous-time condition `⟨∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)⟩ > 0` would be very valuable for bridging the gap between theory and practice.

3.  **Potential for Deeper Connections:** For the special case of strongly convex quadratic minimization, Nesterov's method is known to be equivalent to the Conjugate Gradient (CG) method. It would be interesting to investigate what connection, if any, the proposed adaptive averaging scheme has to CG in this setting. Does the adaptive rule on the weights `a(t)` implicitly recover some aspect of the CG updates? Exploring this could provide deeper insight into why the method performs so well on strongly convex problems, as shown in the experiments.

### **Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that presents a simple, effective, and theoretically-grounded heuristic for improving Nesterov's accelerated methods. The guarantee that the method will not degrade performance is a significant advantage over other common heuristics. While the work is an incremental extension of prior research, its practical importance and potential for wide impact are substantial. The strengths in clarity, theoretical justification, and empirical validation comfortably outweigh the limited originality. I recommend acceptance.
