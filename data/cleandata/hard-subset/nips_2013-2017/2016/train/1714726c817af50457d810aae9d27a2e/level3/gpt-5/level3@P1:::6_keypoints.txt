- Main contribution: studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent with generalized averaging weights and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves baseline accelerated rates.  
- Dynamics: couples a dual gradient-accumulation rate η(t) with a primal weighted average using weights w(t).  
- Lyapunov analysis: introduces Lr(X,Z,t)=r(t)(f(X)−f*)+Dψ*(Z,z*) and gives sufficient conditions on (η,w) ensuring O(1/r(t)) convergence.  
- Special cases: recovers earlier accelerated ODEs (Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan) as instances of the framework.  
- Primal representation: derives a primal form clarifying feasibility preservation under constraints (orthant, simplex) and gives an accelerated replicator dynamics example.  
- Adaptive averaging heuristic: greedily decreases d/dt Lr while maintaining rate guarantees in continuous time.  
- Discrete algorithm: instantiation preserves quadratic rate O(1/k^2) and is empirically compared to gradient/speed restarts.  
- Experiments: in R^3 show adaptive averaging matches or outperforms restarts and yields a more consistently decreasing Lyapunov surrogate.  
- Relation to prior work: decouples gradient accumulation and averaging, extends continuous-time acceleration interpretations, and contrasts with empirical restarting rules by providing guarantees without strong convexity.  
- Connections: relates to mirror descent, replicator dynamics, and briefly discusses AdaGrad/Adam (suggested expansion needed).  
- Theory quality: Theorem 2 and corollary mapping r(t) to feasible (w,η) are sound; alternative monotone energy Er is informative.  
- Missing discrete details: step-size conditions and smoothness/strong-convexity assumptions for discrete results are deferred to supplement and should be stated in the main text.  
- Scope of guarantees: adaptive heuristic proved in continuous time and discrete for r(t)=t^2; unclear if extends to p>2 without higher-order discretizations.  
- Experimental limitations: results are limited to low-dimensional (R^3) toy problems and a single geometry (simplex).  
- Clarity issues: notation is dense with forward references; request to add a concise background on mirror maps, Bregman divergences, Lyapunov functions, and AMD definitions.  
- Key-inequality intuition: request for more intuition on Lyapunov derivative steps, interaction of Ẋ and Ż, and geometric interpretation of η(t)=a(t)r(t) cancellation.  
- Initialization/ discretization: clarify t0>0 initialization, practical impact on discretization, and mapping t=k√s with ak approximating a(t).  
- Figure 1 fix: ambiguous “solid line” reference; recommend clearer styling and labeling of primal/dual/averaged trajectories.  
- Originality: generalized averaging and adaptive heuristic appear novel; accelerated replicator dynamics is an original illustration.  
- Significance: promising conceptual contribution that could influence robust acceleration heuristics but needs scalability evidence beyond simplex and R^3.  
- Specific suggestion 1: discuss extension of adaptive averaging to non-convex settings, stationary-point convergence, and composite/non-smooth extensions.  
- Specific suggestion 2: provide other simple, trajectory-independent sufficient recipes for (w,η) using bounds on L or curvature.  
- Specific suggestion 3: state exact conditions on ψ*, R, and s for discrete O(1/k^2), include a main-text proof sketch, and discuss computational overhead of function-value checks vs restart triggers.  
- Specific suggestion 4: add higher-dimensional experiments (d≥1000), other geometries (Euclidean, ℓ1), and standard convex ML tasks, reporting iterations and wall-clock time and sensitivity to β and s.  
- Specific suggestion 5: provide theory for strongly convex case showing potential linear-rate phases or faster-than-1/k^2 transients under adaptive averaging.  
- Specific suggestion 6: formalize conditions under which restarting ensures Er decreases (e.g., when velocity reset reduces kinetic term).  
- Pros for acceptance: solid continuous-time analysis, novel adaptive averaging with guarantees, elegant replicator dynamics connection, discrete algorithm retains O(1/k^2) and empirical gains over restarts.  
- Cons against acceptance: clarity and notation hinder accessibility, experiments are low-dimensional and not scalable, discrete-time guarantees beyond p=2 not fully developed in main text.  
- Minor comments: define all abbreviations on first use, ensure consistent notation for vectors/scalars and X usage, fix Figure 1 styling/labels, and provide a practical guide for choosing β, a1, and s.
