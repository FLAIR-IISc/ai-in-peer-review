Summary: The paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics with a generalized averaging scheme parameterized by weight and accumulation functions (w(t), η(t)). Using a Lyapunov construction Lr, it provides sufficient conditions for prescribed rates r(t), and introduces a complementary energy function Er. A primal reformulation yields examples for standard domains, including an accelerated variant of replicator dynamics on the simplex. The main novelty is an adaptive averaging heuristic that selects a(t) to decrease the derivative of Lr, and a discrete scheme intended to preserve the quadratic 1/t2 rate; experiments compare against adaptive restarts (O’Donoghue–Candès; Su–Boyd–Candès). The work builds directly on prior NeurIPS papers connecting acceleration and ODEs (Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett; Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan).

Quality: The continuous-time analysis is careful and technically sound, with clear sufficient conditions for Lr to decrease. However, the discrete adaptive averaging is not fully justified: the discretization uses approximations, and the paper lacks an explicit convergence proof for the adaptive scheme. Claims of preserving the quadratic rate thus read as heuristic. Experiments are low-dimensional and mostly quadratic/linear, limiting the empirical evidence, and there is no comparison to other averaging schedules used with Nesterov-type methods.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. The intuition behind adaptive averaging via controlling d/dt Lr is clear. Minor suggestion: separate clearly which results are rigorous for the continuous dynamics versus heuristic/discrete, and group assumptions needed for each theorem.

Originality: The generalized averaging formulation and Lyapunov function largely follow prior NIPS work on AMD; the extension from simple to generalized averaging appears straightforward and is explored only for quadratic rates. The adaptive averaging idea is novel and well-motivated, but currently under-theorized.

Significance: If theoretically grounded and shown robust in higher dimensions, adaptive averaging could be a practical alternative to restarts. As is, the contribution is mostly conceptual; the energy/Lyapunov viewpoints and the replicator connection aid understanding but offer limited immediate ML impact. Faster-than-quadratic rates would require higher-order methods, reducing applicability.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, unified Lyapunov analysis and insightful energy interpretation.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with intuitive rationale.
- Sometimes outperforms restart heuristics; preserves Lyapunov decrease.

Cons against acceptance:
- Incremental over prior AMD-ODE work; generalized averaging is limited in scope.
- No discrete-time convergence proof for the adaptive scheme.
- Experiments are toy-scale; no high-dimensional or non-quadratic results.
- Lacks comparison to other averaging schedules for accelerated methods.

Post-rebuttal: If the authors deliver (i) a rigorous convergence proof for adaptive averaging, (ii) high-dimensional/non-quadratic experiments, and (iii) comparisons including higher-order implementations (e.g., cubic-regularized Newton) and competing averaging schemes, the paper would merit serious reconsideration. Currently, I lean toward rejection due to insufficient theoretical and empirical support for the discrete adaptive method.
