This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, building upon the continuous-time ODE perspective introduced by Krichene et al. The authors generalize a primal trajectory construction based on averaging the mirror of the dual variable, providing convergence rate analysis under suitable conditions on the averaging coefficients. A key contribution is an adaptive weighting scheme with provable convergence guarantees in continuous time and demonstrated comparable performance to restarting techniques in discrete experiments.

The work is well-motivated, positioning itself within the recent trend of connecting accelerated methods to continuous-time dynamics. The authors clearly articulate the connection to Nesterov’s acceleration and prior work [20, 11] framing the problem as a coupled primal-dual system. The Lyapunov function approach is standard and appropriately applied, yielding rigorous convergence results for the continuous setting, demonstrably improving upon the usual 1/t<sup>2</sup> rate under certain conditions. The introduction of an adaptive weighting scheme, and the preservation of the quadratic convergence rate, is a significant result. The empirical validation, comparing adaptive averaging to adaptive restarting, further strengthens the contribution.

The paper’s strengths lie in its theoretical rigor, clarity of presentation, and insightful connection between continuous and discrete optimization. The mathematical derivations are well-structured and easy to follow. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the existing literature and clearly position their work within it. The inclusion of examples, such as the acceleration of the replicator dynamics, helps to illustrate the applicability of the proposed framework.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The most significant limitation is the omission of a theoretical analysis for the discrete version of the algorithm beyond the convergence rate guarantee. While empirical results are provided, a theoretical understanding of the discrete behavior would significantly enhance the paper's impact. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the conditions required for the improved convergence rates in the continuous setting. Are these conditions easily verifiable in practice?  The reliance on Theorem 2, and the difficulties in checking its conditions, potentially limits the practical applicability of the results.

Furthermore, the authors should cite relevant work by Attouch, Peypouquet and coauthors [e.g., 24, 25, 26] which have made significant contributions to the analysis of accelerated gradient methods and their connections to ODEs. These works provide a broader context for the current research and would strengthen the related work section.  The link to work on Hessian-driven damping would also be useful to draw.

Finally, the open question raised by the reviewer – whether the improved continuous rates persist after discretization – is crucial and deserves further emphasis. While the authors provide some evidence via the numerical experiments, a rigorous investigation is needed to address this question fully. The discretization process inherent in translating continuous-time results to practical algorithms can introduce challenges, and understanding the impact of this discretization on the convergence rate is essential.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The generalized averaging scheme and adaptive weighting heuristic represent a novel contribution to the field of accelerated optimization.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The continuous-time analysis is mathematically sound and provides provable convergence guarantees.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and results.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental problem in optimization and has the potential to improve the performance of a wide range of machine learning algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Missing Discrete Analysis:** The lack of a theoretical analysis for the discrete version of the algorithm is a significant weakness.
*   **Practical Verifiability:** The conditions for achieving the improved convergence rates may be difficult to verify in practice.
*   **Limited Scope:** The paper focuses primarily on the theoretical aspects and could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of practical considerations and limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and merits acceptance. The theoretical rigor, clarity, and novelty of the work outweigh the limitations. Addressing the open question regarding discretization and providing a more detailed analysis of the practical implications would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to incorporate the suggested citations and address the reviewer's concerns.
