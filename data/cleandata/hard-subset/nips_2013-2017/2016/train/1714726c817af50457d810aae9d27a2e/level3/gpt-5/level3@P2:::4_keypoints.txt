- Studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) parameterized by dual accumulation η(t) and averaging weights w(t), coupling Ż = −η∇f(X) with X the w-weighted mirror average.
- Introduces Lyapunov Lr = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) and gives sufficient conditions on η,w to ensure Lr nonincreasing and f(X)−f* = O(1/r(t)).
- Provides a primal-only representation, concrete examples (positive orthant, simplex), and an accelerated replicator dynamics instantiation.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t)=w/W on-the-fly to decrease the Lyapunov derivative while preserving continuous-time rates.
- Defines an energy Er = f(X) + (1/r)Dψ*(Z,∇ψ(X)) that is nonincreasing under the same conditions, yielding a potential+kinetic interpretation.
- Derives a discrete algorithm for r(t)=t^2 (Algorithm 1) claiming O(1/k^2) rate; discrete proof is deferred to the supplement.
- Continuous-time analysis is technically clean; key conditions include η(t) ≥ r′(t) and a path-dependent term ⟨∇f,Ẋ⟩(r−ηW/w) ≤ 0.
- A constructive corollary (η = a·r with a ≥ r′/r) guarantees the rate; existence/uniqueness handled via AMD′ with t0>0.
- Adaptive rule ensures nonincreasing Lr in continuous time by toggling a(t) relative to ηr based on sign of ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩.
- Experiments (mainly simplex/R^3) show adaptive averaging is competitive with gradient/speed restarts and exhibits monotonic Lr, but are small-scale.
- Notation is dense in places (e.g., checked variables); a compact notation table and clearer Algorithm 1 (define β,γ,s and prox terms) would help clarity.
- Original contributions: generalized averaging framework with explicit η–w–r conditions, novel adaptive averaging heuristic, accelerated replicator example, and Er viewpoint.
- Significance: solid theoretical unification and a practical heuristic with continuous-time guarantees; impact likely for constrained accelerated methods.
- Main pro: modular Lyapunov analysis yielding verifiable rate conditions and a simple adaptive averaging rule with continuous-time guarantees.
- Other pros: discrete algorithm with claimed O(1/k^2) rate (supplement), insightful primal representations, and empirical comparisons to strong baselines.
- Main cons: discrete-time analysis and parameter guidance (β,s,γ) are missing from the main text.
- Additional cons: key path-dependent condition is hard to verify a priori; heuristic uses potentially noisy quantities in practice.
- Further cons: experiments limited in scale and geometry; sensitivity analysis and broader baseline comparisons are lacking.
- Suggestions: include discrete Lyapunov proof in main paper, give parameter-selection guidance and sensitivity plots, compare more restart variants and larger tasks, and discuss numerical stability when a(t) grows large.
- Overall recommendation: Accept (7/10); reviewer confidence: high.
