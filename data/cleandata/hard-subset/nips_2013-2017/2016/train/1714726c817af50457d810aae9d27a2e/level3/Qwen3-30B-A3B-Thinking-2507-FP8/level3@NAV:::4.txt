

This paper studies a class of ODEs for accelerated descent dynamics with a generalized averaging scheme, building on the continuous-time perspective of accelerated methods pioneered by Su et al. (NIPS 2014) and extended by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The authors formulate a generalized Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) for averaging and η(t) for gradient accumulation rate. Using a Lyapunov argument, they derive sufficient conditions on these functions to guarantee convergence rates, generalizing previous results on Nesterov acceleration. The key innovation is an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights along solution trajectories to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function while preserving convergence guarantees.

The paper excels in technical quality and theoretical rigor. The Lyapunov analysis is sound and provides clear sufficient conditions for convergence, with Corollary 1 offering a constructive method for weight selection. The adaptive averaging heuristic is theoretically grounded and empirically validated through comprehensive numerical experiments on simplex-constrained problems, including the replicator dynamics. The experiments demonstrate significant improvements over existing restart heuristics in several cases, with the adaptive method preserving the quadratic convergence rate while accelerating the Lyapunov function decrease.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient mathematical detail to reproduce results. The connection to previous work is well-established, and the novel insight that adaptive averaging can produce equivalent empirical speedups as accelerated methods represents a meaningful contribution to the literature.

The main limitation is the restricted scope of numerical experiments to simplex-constrained problems, with less discussion of scalability to more complex or high-dimensional settings. Additionally, while the adaptive heuristic is well-motivated theoretically, its relationship to other adaptive methods like Adagrad or Adam could be further explored.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution with significant theoretical and practical implications. It advances the understanding of accelerated dynamics while providing a principled, effective heuristic that preserves convergence guarantees. The work meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (quality), clearly presented (clarity), offers a novel perspective on acceleration (originality), and addresses an important problem with demonstrable improvements (significance). I recommend acceptance.
