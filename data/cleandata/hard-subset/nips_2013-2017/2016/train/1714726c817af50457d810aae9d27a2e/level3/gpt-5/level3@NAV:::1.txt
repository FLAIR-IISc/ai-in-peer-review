This paper revisits accelerated mirror descent (AMD) through its ODE interpretation and proposes an adaptive averaging mechanism that modifies the time-varying weights used to form the primal iterate. Building on Krichene et al. (NIPS’15), Su-Boyd-Candès, and the variational framework of Wibisono et al., the authors analyze a generalized AMD ODE with weight functions w(t) and accumulation rate η(t) via a simple Lyapunov argument. They derive sufficient conditions ensuring 1/r(t) convergence and introduce a heuristic that adaptively adjusts w(t) to further decrease the Lyapunov/energy, thereby dominating fixed schedules. They illustrate the framework on constrained settings, including an accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex, and present a discretization that they claim preserves the O(1/k^2) rate. Experiments against adaptive restarts (O’Donoghue–Candès; Su et al.) show consistent speed-ups.

Quality: The continuous-time analysis is sound and easy to follow for readers familiar with Krichene et al. The monotonic Lyapunov decrease makes the dominance guarantee transparent. The discrete-time section is promising but would benefit from a clearer, self-contained convergence proof in the main text (assumptions, stability conditions on step sizes, and a precise statement about preserved rates), as well as a discussion of sensitivity to β and step size. Empirics are informative but limited in scale and scope (mostly low-dimensional/simplex).

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. The Lyapunov-based proof sketch is concise; still, surfacing the discrete Lyapunov and proof would improve readability. A brief comparison to related “adaptive averaging” (e.g., Flammarion–Bach) and sharper positioning relative to adaptive restart would help.

Originality: The technical development is a close extension of prior ODE-based analyses, but the adaptive weighting heuristic and its dominance guarantee fill a gap left by restart heuristics. The accelerated replicator example is neat.

Significance: Given the ubiquity of Nesterov-style methods, a simple adaptive modification with theoretical safety and empirical gains is impactful. Strong convexity and links to CG on quadratics could further elevate significance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clean Lyapunov framework yielding simple, verifiable conditions.
- Adaptive averaging that provably dominates fixed schedules; preserved rate claimed in discrete time.
- Broad applicability (mirror maps, constraints); compelling accelerated replicator case.
- Empirical gains over well-known restarts.

Cons for acceptance:
- Discrete-time analysis needs to be centralized and sharpened.
- Limited experimental breadth (higher dimensions, stochastic/noisy gradients, nonsmooth objectives).
- Parameter sensitivity and robustness not fully assessed.
- Potential connections to Conjugate Gradient on quadratics left unexplored.

Suggestions: Provide a thorough discrete-time convergence analysis (including stability and complexity constants), add higher-dimensional and stochastic experiments, and investigate the relationship between adaptive weights and CG directions for strongly convex quadratics.
