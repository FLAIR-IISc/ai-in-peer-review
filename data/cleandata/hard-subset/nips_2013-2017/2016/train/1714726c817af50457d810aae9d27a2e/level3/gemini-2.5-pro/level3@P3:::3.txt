This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization by extending the continuous-time primal-dual ordinary differential equation (ODE) framework from Krichene et al. (2016). The central idea is to generalize the averaging scheme used to define the primal trajectory. The authors introduce a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two functions: an averaging weight function `w(t)` and a gradient accumulation rate `η(t)`.

The main theoretical contribution is a unified analysis of this family of ODEs using a Lyapunov function. Theorem 2 provides sufficient conditions on `w(t)` and `η(t)` that guarantee a desired convergence rate of `1/r(t)`. This framework is shown to be general enough to recover previous results and, more importantly, to derive new dynamics with convergence rates faster than the standard `O(1/t^2)` in the continuous setting. Building on this analysis, the paper proposes a novel adaptive averaging heuristic. This heuristic adaptively chooses the averaging weights to maximally decrease the Lyapunov function at each step, thereby guaranteeing that the original convergence rate is preserved. The paper concludes with numerical experiments comparing this adaptive averaging scheme to standard adaptive restarting techniques, showing competitive or improved performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound. The continuous-time analysis is rigorous and elegant. Using a single Lyapunov function to analyze a broad class of dynamics provides a unified and insightful perspective on the role of averaging in acceleration. The connection between the abstract ODE and concrete examples, like the accelerated replicator dynamics, is a nice illustration of the framework's power.

2.  **Originality and Significance:** The generalization of the averaging scheme and the corresponding convergence analysis is a significant and novel contribution. It advances the understanding of continuous-time accelerated methods beyond the specific dynamics studied in prior work. The derivation of convergence rates faster than `O(1/t^2)` is a particularly interesting theoretical result.

3.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a valuable practical contribution. Unlike many restarting heuristics whose guarantees are often limited to specific problem classes (e.g., quadratic or strongly convex), this method comes with a provable guarantee that it preserves the `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate for general convex functions. The empirical results suggest that this theoretically-grounded heuristic performs as well as, and sometimes significantly better than, popular restarting methods.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper overlooks a substantial and highly relevant body of literature on the analysis of accelerated methods via inertial dynamics, particularly the work of Attouch, Peypouquet, and collaborators. For instance, "The rate of convergence of Nesterov’s accelerated forward-backward method is actually faster than 1/k^2" (Attouch & Peypouquet, SIAM J. Opt., 2016) and "Fast convergence of an inertial gradient-like system with vanishing viscosity" (Attouch et al., 2015) deal with very similar second-order ODEs and their convergence properties. Situating the current work within this broader context would significantly strengthen the paper and clarify its specific contributions.

2.  **Lack of Discrete-Time Analysis:** The theoretical analysis is focused almost exclusively on the continuous-time setting. While the proposed adaptive heuristic is presented as a discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1), its theoretical analysis is omitted from the main paper and referenced as being in the supplementary material. For a NIPS paper, where the ultimate goal is often a usable algorithm, this is a significant omission. Including at least a sketch of the discrete-time proof or a more detailed discussion of the challenges in discretizing the dynamics would make the paper more complete.

3.  **The Continuous-to-Discrete Gap:** A key question left unaddressed is whether the intriguing faster-than-`O(1/t^2)` convergence rates achievable in continuous time can be preserved by a practical (i.e., first-order) discretization. It is known that preserving faster continuous rates can sometimes require higher-order methods (as the authors briefly mention in a footnote). A discussion of this gap is crucial for assessing the practical implications of this theoretical finding. Acknowledging this as an important direction for future work would be beneficial.

**Summary of Review:**

This is an interesting and well-executed paper that makes a solid contribution to our understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The generalized ODE framework and the corresponding Lyapunov analysis are elegant and powerful. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel and practical outcome of this theoretical development, backed by both guarantees and promising empirical results.

The main weaknesses are the omission of important related work on inertial dynamics and the lack of theoretical analysis for the discrete-time algorithm in the main paper. Addressing these points, particularly by properly citing and discussing the work of Attouch et al., would substantially improve the paper. Despite these shortcomings, the strengths in originality, technical quality, and potential significance make this a strong paper suitable for acceptance.
