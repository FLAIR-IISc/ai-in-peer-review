

This paper studies a family of continuous-time accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, introducing a generalized averaging scheme that extends previous work on continuous-time representations of accelerated methods. Building on the connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and ODEs established by Su et al. (NIPS 2014) and Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015), the authors provide a unified framework for analyzing accelerated mirror descent dynamics using a Lyapunov argument. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that adaptive averaging of weights along solution trajectories can achieve improved convergence rates while preserving theoretical guarantees.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical rigor. The authors establish sufficient conditions on weight functions η(t) and w(t) to achieve a desired convergence rate using a Lyapunov function, which generalizes previous results. Theorem 2 provides a clear characterization of conditions under which the Lyapunov function decreases, with Corollary 1 offering a practical construction method for weight functions. This theoretical foundation is particularly strong as it addresses a gap in the literature regarding adaptive methods for accelerated optimization, where existing heuristics like restarting often lack theoretical guarantees.

The adaptive averaging heuristic proposed in Section 4 is the paper's most novel contribution. Unlike existing restarting heuristics that lack convergence rate preservation guarantees, the authors' approach adaptively computes weights to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function while maintaining the quadratic convergence rate. The connection to replicator dynamics (Section 3) provides an elegant application to evolutionary game theory, demonstrating the practical relevance of the theoretical framework. The numerical experiments in Section 5 comprehensively compare adaptive averaging with existing restarting heuristics across multiple problem types, showing consistent or superior performance with significant improvements in the strongly convex case.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured exposition that carefully builds from theoretical foundations to practical implementation. The derivation of the primal representation (Section 3) and the clear presentation of the adaptive averaging algorithm (Algorithm 1) make the technical content accessible. The authors effectively use examples to illustrate abstract concepts, particularly with the simplex-constrained dynamics that connect to replicator equations in evolutionary game theory.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the NIPS community. While the connection between acceleration and averaging has been observed previously (e.g., in Flammarion and Bach, COLT 2015), this work provides a rigorous continuous-time framework that generalizes and extends these observations. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a novel approach that bridges theoretical guarantees with empirical performance, distinguishing it from prior work on restarting heuristics.

The significance of this work is substantial. The authors provide both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation for a method that improves upon existing heuristics for accelerating optimization algorithms. The continuous-time perspective offers new insights into accelerated methods, potentially guiding future algorithm design. The connection to replicator dynamics also opens potential applications in evolutionary game theory and other domains where such dynamics are studied.

The paper has few weaknesses. While the theoretical results are strong, the connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete implementations could be more explicitly developed for the adaptive case. Additionally, the paper might benefit from a more detailed discussion of why adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics, beyond the empirical results presented.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical foundation with novel Lyapunov-based analysis
- Adaptive averaging heuristic provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical improvements
- Clear connection to replicator dynamics and evolutionary game theory
- Comprehensive numerical experiments across multiple problem types
- Well-organized and clearly written with effective examples

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some technical details (e.g., Bregman divergence choices) could be more thoroughly explained
- Connection to discrete-time algorithms for the adaptive case could be expanded
- The contribution might be seen as incremental by some reviewers

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization with clear theoretical advances and practical improvements to accelerated methods. The work is well-executed, rigorously proven, and empirically validated, demonstrating significant potential impact on both theoretical understanding and practical implementation of accelerated optimization algorithms. The paper addresses the four key criteria effectively: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), makes a meaningful extension of prior work (Originality), and provides results with clear significance for optimization practitioners (Significance).

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete implementations, and to provide more detailed discussion of the adaptive averaging mechanism. The paper represents a solid contribution to the NIPS community that advances our understanding of accelerated optimization methods.
