This paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic to accelerate constrained convex optimization. A key claim is that the method preserves the baseline convergence rate when the objective is not strongly convex, which addresses a known limitation of many restart-based heuristics. The paper supports this claim with both theoretical analysis and experiments.

I found parts of the paper difficult to follow, as this topic is outside my core area. While I assume the intended audience will recognize the terminology and abbreviations used, I recommend adding brief background and definitions to improve accessibility. In particular, the equations around lines 33–34 and 46–47 would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the underlying ideas and their role in the method.

I also have questions about the relation to adaptive methods commonly used in nonconvex optimization:
- How does your approach relate to adaptive methods such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]? Are there conceptual connections or key differences in how adaptivity is introduced (e.g., in averaging vs. step-size/momentum)?
- Can any insights from your analysis extend to the nonconvex setting, or are there fundamental obstacles?

Regarding practical significance, the experimental evaluation appears limited: results are presented only in R^3, which is a toy setting. How does the method (and comparable baselines) scale to substantially higher dimensions? Please discuss computational overhead, memory requirements, and any implementation details that affect performance in large-scale problems. Additional experiments on higher-dimensional benchmarks would strengthen the empirical case and help quantify gains over standard restarting heuristics and non-adaptive baselines.

Minor comment: The reference to the “solid line” in Figure 1 is ambiguous. Consider using a dotted line or distinct colors to clearly indicate the trajectory of the primal variable.
