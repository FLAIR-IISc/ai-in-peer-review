- Introduces a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) ODEs parameterized by η(t) and w(t), analyzing them via a Lyapunov Lr to obtain O(1/r(t)) convergence.  
- Provides a primal representation with concrete examples (positive orthant, simplex) including a novel “accelerated replicator dynamics.”  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves continuous-time rates and a discretization that maintains O(1/k^2) for first-order methods.  
- Lyapunov-based theoretical analysis is sound, with intuitive sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) and constructive implementable choices (Corollary 1).  
- Introduces an additional “energy” function Er as a helpful complement to the Lyapunov analysis.  
- Discretization aligns with the continuous design and comes with a rate guarantee (proof details in the supplement).  
- Experiments are informative and visual (notably the simplex case), showing adaptive averaging competitive with or superior to adaptive restarts.  
- Experimental scope is limited (mostly R^3); broader baselines and larger-scale tests would strengthen empirical claims.  
- Paper is generally well written and organized, with useful derivations and examples.  
- Notational load is high; a symbol summary table would improve readability.  
- Typo: in Examples 1 and 2 a(t) should be β/t (not β t) to match r(t)=t^p calculations; please fix.  
- Avoid calling O(1/k^2) “quadratic convergence”; prefer “accelerated O(1/k^2) rate.”  
- The generalized averaging formulation and adaptive averaging heuristic appear novel; accelerated replicator dynamics is an original link to evolutionary dynamics.  
- Conceptually unifying and practically relevant; adaptive mirror-averaging could influence adaptive accelerated method design.  
- Continuous-time guarantees plus a discretization that preserves O(1/k^2) make the contribution actionable, though more experiments would increase impact.  
- Strength: clear Lyapunov framework giving simple, constructive sufficient conditions for acceleration with generalized averaging.  
- Strength: insightful primal representation and concrete examples including the simplex/replicator dynamic.  
- Strength: adaptive averaging heuristic has theoretical guarantees and maintains monotone Lyapunov decrease in experiments.  
- Weakness: experiments are narrow; recommend larger-scale tests, more geometries, and additional baselines (FISTA variants, tuned momentum).  
- Weakness: need sensitivity analysis for β and step size s and guidance for parameter selection.  
- Weakness: robustness to noisy/stochastic gradients is untested; unclear extension to stochastic mirror descent.  
- Weakness: Theorem 2’s condition depends on the trajectory and is hard to verify a priori; more discussion on how often the adaptive heuristic meets this in practice is needed.  
- Weakness: clarify role and practical implications of t0 > 0 for existence/uniqueness and discretization.  
- Presentation fixes suggested: give a discrete-proof overview in the main text, define R and Dψ when first used in Algorithm 1, simplify steps 6–8, and fix incomplete references/typos.  
- Questions posed: can exponential r(t) yield linear rates for strongly convex f and what discretization changes are needed; sensitivity and adaptivity of β; formalizing Er non-increase for restarts; extension to composite/proximal/constrained settings.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel adaptive mechanism, unifying Lyapunov analysis, new dynamics, and promising empirical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical evaluation and some clarity/details missing in the main text.  
- Verdict: reviewer leans toward acceptance, recommending stronger experiments and clarity fixes to make the paper more compelling.
