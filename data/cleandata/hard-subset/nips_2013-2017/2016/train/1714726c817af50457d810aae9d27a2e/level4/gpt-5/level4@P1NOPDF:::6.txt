This paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic to accelerate constrained convex optimization. A key strength is the guarantee that, when the objective is not strongly convex, the method preserves the original convergence rate. This appears to offer a clear advantage over existing restarting heuristics, and the paper supports this claim both theoretically and empirically.

That said, I found the paper difficult to follow, as this topic is outside my core area. While I assume the target audience will recognize the abbreviations and terminology, the exposition would benefit from additional background to make the contributions more accessible. In particular, more intuitive explanations around the equations near lines 33–34 and 46–47 would help readers understand the motivation and mechanics of the heuristic, not just the formal steps.

I would also appreciate a discussion of the relationship to adaptive averaging methods commonly used in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]. How does your approach compare to these methods in terms of assumptions (convex vs. non-convex, constrained vs. unconstrained), the nature of the adaptivity, and the resulting guarantees? Are there conceptual connections or important distinctions that situate your heuristic within the broader landscape of adaptive optimization? Even if the goals differ, clarifying similarities and differences would help position the contribution.

Regarding practical significance, the experiments are conducted in R^3, which is a toy setting. It would be useful to demonstrate performance in substantially higher dimensions and to discuss scalability. How does the method (and related baselines) scale computationally and in memory with problem dimension? Are there dependencies on condition numbers, constraint structures, or problem geometry that affect performance? Empirical results on larger-scale problems, along with runtime and iteration counts, would make the case for practicality more compelling.

Minor comment: The reference to the “solid line” in Figure 1 is ambiguous. Consider using a dashed line or distinct colors and add a clear legend to identify the trajectory of the primal variable.
