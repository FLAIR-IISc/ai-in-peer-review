This paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, considering both continuous and discrete settings. The work builds upon the foundation laid by Krichene et al. [7], which demonstrated a connection between Nesterov's method and the discretization of an ordinary differential equation (ODE) system coupling primal and dual trajectories. Specifically, the primal trajectory in [7] is constructed via a specific averaging of the mirror map of the dual variable. This paper generalizes this approach by studying more general averaging schemes. Theorem 2 establishes convergence rates under appropriate conditions on the averaging coefficients. Notably, the authors derive convergence rates in the continuous setting exceeding the typical 1/t<sup>2</sup> rate, a significant improvement. 

An adaptive weight selection strategy with associated convergence guarantees is also presented and evaluated empirically. This adaptive scheme is then compared to established restarting techniques known to enhance the practical convergence of accelerated methods. The theoretical analysis, however, currently remains limited to the continuous case; the discrete version of the algorithm is not analyzed in detail.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-written paper. The analysis of the proposed averaging scheme for accelerated methods is rigorous and clearly presented. While the core ideas behind analyzing continuous dynamics in this context have been developed in a series of recent publications – notably the work of Attouch, Peypouquet, and their collaborators – this paper makes valuable contributions and presents novel results. The central theorem, demonstrating the existence of averaging schemes achieving faster convergence rates than 1/t<sup>2</sup>, is a noteworthy finding. 

The potential for improved rates is exciting, and a key direction for future work would be to investigate whether this enhanced rate is maintained when transitioning to the discrete procedure. Understanding this connection is crucial for practical applicability. It would also be beneficial to briefly discuss the computational cost associated with implementing the proposed adaptive weighting scheme compared to simpler alternatives like restarting, providing a more complete picture of its practical advantages.

**Strengths:**

*   Rigorous analysis of a novel averaging scheme for accelerated methods.
*   Demonstration of convergence rates exceeding 1/t<sup>2</sup> in the continuous setting.
*   Clear presentation and well-written exposition.
*   Relevant extension of prior work by Krichene et al. [7].

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of theoretical analysis for the discrete version of the algorithm.
*   Limited discussion of the computational cost of the adaptive weighting scheme.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the absence of a discrete analysis is a limitation, the theoretical results for the continuous case are significant and the experimental validation provides encouraging evidence. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors acknowledging the limitation of the discrete analysis and potentially outlining a plan for future work addressing this gap.
