This paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, analyzing them through a continuous-time framework and proposing corresponding discrete algorithms. The work continues the line of research initiated by Krichene et al. [7], which interprets Nesterov's method as a discretization of an ordinary differential equation (ODE) system that couples primal and dual trajectories. In that framework, the primal trajectory is obtained by a specific averaging of the mirror of the dual variable. The present paper generalizes this by studying a broader class of averaging schemes.

The main theoretical contribution, Theorem 2, derives convergence rates under suitable conditions on the averaging coefficients. Notably, in the continuous-time setting, the authors show that it is possible to achieve convergence rates faster than the classical 1/t^2 rate. The paper also proposes an adaptive scheme for selecting the averaging weights, provides convergence guarantees, and compares it favorably against standard restarting techniques in the experimental section.

This is an interesting and well-written paper. The analysis of the new averaging scheme within the continuous-time setting is rigorous. While the general approach of analyzing continuous dynamics for acceleration is now well-established through a series of papers, this work provides novel contributions and results. In particular, the main theorem demonstrating the existence of averaging schemes with convergence rates faster than 1/t^2 is a significant finding.

However, there are a few points that weaken the paper. The theoretical analysis for the discrete version of the proposed algorithm is not included. This leads to a critical open question: are the improved convergence rates from the continuous-time analysis preserved when the dynamics are discretized? An analysis of this would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution. Additionally, given the extensive body of work on the connection between accelerated methods and ODEs, the authors should cite the related and foundational work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and their coauthors.
