The paper builds on the ODE interpretation of Nesterov’s acceleration introduced by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) to propose a simple heuristic that adaptively reweights the algorithm’s iterates to speed up convergence. Unlike previous adaptive averaging heuristics, the proposed scheme is guaranteed to dominate standard fixed-schedule averaging (i.e., it is never worse and often better). The empirical evaluation shows consistent speed-ups across a broad class of objective functions.

I found the paper clear and enjoyable to read. The mathematical development is straightforward and easy to follow, particularly for readers familiar with Krichene et al.’s framework. The empirical results are compelling and indicate substantial practical gains. Although the technical contribution is a relatively close extension of prior work, the potential impact is high because Nesterov’s acceleration is widely used in applications. Too often, even in empirical studies, new (and sometimes highly adaptive) methods are compared against Nesterov’s acceleration with a fixed weight schedule; this paper convincingly shows that making Nesterov’s method adaptive requires only a minor modification and can yield significant improvements.

To strengthen the paper technically, I recommend adding an analysis of the discretized algorithm to show that the convergence rate is preserved under the proposed adaptive reweighting. If I am not mistaken, this should be relatively straightforward within the existing framework. I would also be interested in a discussion of connections between the adaptive averaging in Nesterov’s method and the Conjugate Gradient method for strongly convex quadratic problems, as there may be insightful parallels in how momentum and weighting adapt to the geometry of such objectives.
