The paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization. The authors present a framework describing these dynamics as a coupling of a dual variable and a primal variable obtained through weighted averaging of the dual trajectory. Utilizing a Lyapunov argument, they establish sufficient conditions on weighting functions to guarantee a desired convergence rate, and demonstrate this with an accelerated version of replicator dynamics. A novel adaptive averaging heuristic is then proposed to dynamically adjust weights, aiming to expedite the decrease of the Lyapunov function. The authors provide continuous-time guarantees for this adaptive averaging, prove its preservation of quadratic convergence rates in discrete time, and validate its performance against adaptive restarting through numerical experiments, showing comparable or improved results.

This work builds upon a recent resurgence of interest in continuous-time dynamics for optimization [5, 6, 9, 20, 23], which offer analytical advantages and design intuition for discrete algorithms. The connection between Nesterov’s accelerated methods [14, 15] and continuous-time ODEs, as explored in [2, 3, 11, 20], provides a strong foundation for this investigation. Specifically, the paper extends the generalized framework introduced in [11] by exploring different averaging schemes and their impact on convergence. The adaptive averaging heuristic presented is a compelling contribution, addressing the question of whether adaptively chosen weights can outperform fixed schedules or restarting strategies like those proposed in [17, 20].

The strengths of the paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis, utilizing a Lyapunov argument to establish convergence guarantees, and its novel adaptive averaging heuristic. The connection to replicator dynamics is a nice illustrative example, demonstrating the applicability of the proposed framework to a well-studied problem in evolutionary game theory [4, 22]. The numerical experiments provide empirical support for the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging heuristic, showing it to be competitive with, and in some cases superior to, existing methods. The inclusion of both continuous-time and discrete-time analysis strengthens the practical relevance of the work.

However, the paper could benefit from further discussion of the computational cost of the adaptive averaging heuristic. The online computation of weights could potentially introduce overhead compared to simpler restarting schemes. The analysis of the adaptive averaging heuristic is largely limited to the case r(t) = t2; extending the analysis to more general rate functions would be valuable. While the numerical experiments are encouraging, the scope is somewhat limited. Investigating the performance on a wider range of problems and datasets would enhance the robustness of the conclusions. Finally, while the authors acknowledge related work in adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam [7, 10], a more detailed discussion of the differences and potential synergies between these approaches and adaptive averaging would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with clear convergence guarantees.
*   Novel and well-motivated adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating competitive performance.
*   Clear connection to existing work in accelerated optimization and replicator dynamics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational cost of adaptive averaging not fully explored.
*   Analysis primarily focused on r(t) = t2.
*   Limited scope of numerical experiments.
*   Discussion of related adaptive methods could be more detailed.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed heuristic is novel and promising, and the numerical results are encouraging. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above.
