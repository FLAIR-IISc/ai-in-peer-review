This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization problems. The authors present a framework for analyzing these dynamics through a Lyapunov function, establishing sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate. Building on this, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function and demonstrate, through numerical experiments, that this heuristic performs comparably to, and in some cases better than, adaptive restarting techniques.

The core contribution lies in providing a theoretical foundation for understanding the interplay between averaging and acceleration in optimization dynamics. The authors successfully connect Nesterov’s accelerated methods to a continuous-time ODE framework, extending previous work by Su et al. [20] and their own prior research [11]. A notable example is the derivation of an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics when applied to the probability simplex, demonstrating the applicability of their framework to problems in evolutionary game theory [22].  The introduction clearly motivates the study of continuous-time dynamics and highlights its benefits for algorithm design and analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The use of a Lyapunov argument provides a solid theoretical basis for the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic and convergence guarantees.
*   **Generality:** The framework appears quite general, applicable to a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions *w* and *η*.
*   **Novel Heuristic:** The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a promising alternative to existing methods like adaptive restarting, and the numerical results suggest it’s competitive, potentially providing improvements in certain scenarios.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively builds upon and connects to a rich body of work in optimization, dynamical systems, and related fields. References to foundational papers by Nesterov [14, 15], Attouch et al. [1, 2], and others demonstrate a strong awareness of the existing literature.
*   **Clear Presentation of Dynamics:** The derivation of primal representations of the dynamics and the detailed analysis of the replicator dynamics example contribute to the clarity of the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of ideas is interesting, the core concepts of Lyapunov analysis and adaptive step-size selection are not entirely novel in optimization. The main contribution seems to be in the specific application to this AMD framework and the guarantee of preserving the convergence rate.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The numerical experiments, while promising, are somewhat limited in scope. It would be beneficial to see results on a wider range of problem instances and datasets to assess the robustness and scalability of the proposed heuristic. A comparison with more recent adaptive methods like Adam [10] could also be valuable.
*   **Clarity of Conditions:**  The conditions for guaranteeing the convergence rate (Theorems 2 and Corollary 1) are somewhat technical and might be challenging for readers less familiar with convex analysis to fully grasp. A more intuitive explanation of these conditions would be helpful.
*    **Discretization Details:** The description of the Algorithm 1 and its convergence guarantees are relegated to the supplementary material. Integrating a more detailed explanation within the main paper would improve accessibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound investigation of accelerated descent dynamics.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic is a potentially valuable addition to the toolkit of optimization algorithms.
*   The connections to existing work are clearly articulated, demonstrating the paper's place within the broader research landscape.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the work is somewhat incremental.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   The technical details of the theoretical results could benefit from further clarification.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and technically competent paper that addresses an interesting problem in optimization. While the novelty might not be groundbreaking, the contributions are valuable and the work is well-executed. The clear presentation and theoretical rigor make it a worthwhile contribution to the NIPS community.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend the authors consider expanding the experimental evaluation and providing a more intuitive explanation of the key theoretical conditions to enhance the clarity and impact of the work. Additionally, bringing essential details of the discrete algorithm and its guarantees from the supplement into the main text would be beneficial.
