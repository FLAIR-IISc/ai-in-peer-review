This paper presents a study of accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization from a continuous-time perspective. Building on previous work that connects Nesterov's acceleration to a specific ODE system [11], this work generalizes the underlying dynamics. The authors model the accelerated process as a coupling of a dual variable that accumulates gradients and a primal variable obtained through a generalized weighted averaging of the mirrored dual trajectory. This formulation introduces a family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by an averaging weight function `w(t)` and a gradient accumulation rate `η(t)`.

The core theoretical contribution is Theorem 2, which uses a Lyapunov function to establish sufficient conditions on `w(t)` and `η(t)` for achieving a desired convergence rate of 1/r(t). This framework is powerful enough to demonstrate the possibility of constructing continuous-time dynamics with convergence rates faster than the standard O(1/t^2). As a concrete example, the paper shows how the replicator dynamics can be accelerated using this averaging scheme. The authors then propose a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic that dynamically adjusts the weights `w(t)` to maximize the decrease of the Lyapunov function, thereby preserving the theoretical convergence rate guarantees. A discrete-time version of this heuristic is presented and empirically evaluated against common adaptive restarting techniques, showing competitive or superior performance.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the analysis is rigorous. The use of a Lyapunov function to provide a unified study of a broad class of accelerated dynamics is elegant. The claims regarding convergence in the continuous-time setting are well-supported by the provided proofs. The experiments, though on low-dimensional problems, effectively illustrate the behavior of the proposed heuristic and support the claim that it performs favorably against restarting methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. It clearly motivates the generalization from a fixed averaging scheme to a flexible one, and the derivation of the main theorem is easy to follow. The introduction of the adaptive heuristic is a natural consequence of the theoretical analysis. The example illustrating the acceleration of replicator dynamics is particularly insightful.

**Originality:** The work provides several novel contributions. While the analysis of optimization via ODEs is an active area, the generalization of the averaging scheme to achieve variable convergence rates is a new and valuable perspective. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a principled alternative to more ad-hoc restarting schemes, as it is directly derived from the goal of decreasing the Lyapunov function. However, the paper could better position itself with respect to the rich literature on inertial dynamics with Hessian-driven damping. While some relevant papers are cited (e.g., [1, 2, 3]), a more explicit comparison to the work of Attouch, Peypouquet, and their coauthors would help clarify the precise novelty of this contribution within that broader context.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and likely to be influential. The theoretical framework offers a powerful tool for designing and understanding new accelerated methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a practical and promising contribution; because it preserves theoretical convergence guarantees while improving empirical performance, it could be a valuable tool for practitioners. The connection to and acceleration of established dynamics like the replicator equation may also be of interest to researchers in other fields, such as game theory.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

1.  A key question raised by the continuous-time analysis is whether the faster-than-O(1/t^2) rates can be achieved by a corresponding first-order discrete-time algorithm. The paper correctly notes that discretizing these faster ODEs might lead to higher-order methods. A more detailed discussion on the limitations and possibilities of discretizing these dynamics would be a valuable addition.
2.  The full theoretical analysis of the discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) is deferred to the supplementary material. While understandable for space reasons, it would strengthen the paper to include a formal statement of the discrete-time convergence theorem in the main body, as this algorithm is a central practical outcome of the work.
3.  The experiments confirm that the Lyapunov function `Lr` is non-increasing for the adaptive averaging method, which is a strong point. It is interesting to note that the energy function `Er` appears to be non-increasing for the restarting heuristics as well. This empirical observation is intriguing and might warrant a brief comment or hypothesis in the discussion.
