This paper builds upon the continuous-time ODE interpretation of accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) from [7]. The authors extend the corresponding ODE dynamics with a more general averaging scheme and, for its discretization, propose a novel adaptive averaging strategy. The empirical results suggest that, in some cases, this new AMD variant with adaptive averaging can outperform existing AMD algorithms that use restarting.

The primary strength of the paper is the introduction of the adaptive averaging strategy. The idea is novel and is motivated by a clear and appealing intuition derived from the continuous-time dynamics. Additionally, some of the theoretical reformulations, such as the interpretation of the energy function and the connection to replicator dynamics, offer valuable insights that deepen the understanding of accelerated methods.

Despite these positive aspects, the initial submission raised significant concerns regarding its originality and practical contributions.

**Major Weaknesses (in original submission):**

1.  **Limited Originality:** The paper's framework is heavily reliant on a prior NIPS paper [7]. Many core technical elements, including the Lyapunov function and the discretization approach, are directly adopted from this work. The main theoretical extension—from simple to generalized averaging—appears incremental. Furthermore, its utility is not clearly demonstrated, as the authors ultimately focus on the quadratic rate case. Achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which have more limited applicability.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** The central contribution, the adaptive averaging scheme, lacks rigorous theoretical support for the discrete algorithm. While motivated by the convergence conditions of the continuous ODE, the authors do not provide a convergence proof for the discretized version, which relies on an approximation. Consequently, the proposed adaptive method remains a heuristic, as the authors acknowledge. This is a critical theoretical gap.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Alternatives:** The paper fails to position the proposed averaging scheme within the broader context of related work. There is a significant body of literature on adaptive step sizes and averaging strategies for Nesterov's accelerated methods. Without a thorough comparison to these alternatives, it is difficult to assess the practical significance and novelty of the proposed variant.

4.  **Unconvincing Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is insufficient to substantiate the paper's claims. The experiments are confined to a low-dimensional, toy problem with a quadratic/linear objective. This is not convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness, particularly for more general or strongly convex optimization problems commonly found in machine learning.

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment:**

I have read the authors' rebuttal, which directly and constructively addresses the major weaknesses identified above. The authors have committed to making the following significant additions to the final version of the paper:

*   **Expanded Experiments:** They will include experiments on higher-dimensional and non-quadratic problems to provide more compelling evidence of the method's practical advantages.
*   **Theoretical Proof:** They will provide a formal proof of convergence for the discrete algorithm with the adaptive averaging scheme, elevating it from a heuristic to a theoretically grounded method.
*   **Broader Applicability:** They will demonstrate the versatility of their adaptive approach by implementing it on Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method.
*   **Contextualization:** They will add a discussion and comparison with other adaptive schemes for accelerated methods, properly situating their contribution within the existing literature.

These promised additions, if executed well, will transform the paper from a preliminary investigation into a substantial contribution. The lack of theoretical guarantees and convincing experiments were my primary concerns, and the authors have pledged to resolve both. Based on the strength of their rebuttal and their commitment to addressing these critical issues, I have raised my score. My recommendation for acceptance is conditional on the authors successfully incorporating these vital changes into the final manuscript.
