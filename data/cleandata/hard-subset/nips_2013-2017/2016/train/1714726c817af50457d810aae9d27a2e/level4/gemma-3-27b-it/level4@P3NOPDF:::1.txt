This paper presents a compelling and surprisingly simple adaptation of Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent, building upon the ODE interpretation introduced by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The authors leverage this continuous-time perspective to derive a natural heuristic for adaptively reweighting iterates, aiming to accelerate convergence. A key strength of this approach is its theoretical guarantee to outperform standard fixed-schedule averaging schemes – a notable advantage over existing adaptive averaging heuristics. 

The empirical evaluation convincingly demonstrates speed-ups across a variety of functions, further validating the efficacy of the proposed method. I found the paper to be well-written and enjoyable to read; the mathematical arguments are clear and accessible, particularly for those familiar with the prior work of Krichene et al. The connection to the ODE framework is elegantly exploited, resulting in a modification that is conceptually straightforward yet demonstrably effective.

The significance of this work extends beyond the observed empirical gains. The authors rightly point out the common practice of benchmarking novel optimization algorithms *against* Nesterov’s acceleration using a fixed weighting schedule. This paper persuasively argues – and confirms through experimentation – that a modest adaptation can significantly improve Nesterov’s performance, suggesting that many comparative analyses may be overlooking a readily available optimization. This is a crucial observation with potentially broad implications for the field. The pervasive use of Nesterov’s acceleration across diverse applications highlights the potential impact of this work.

While the technical contribution builds closely upon Krichene et al., the adaptation is insightful and addresses a relevant and often-overlooked aspect of practical optimization. To further strengthen the paper, I recommend including an analysis of the discretized process. Demonstrating that the convergence rate is preserved – or, if not, quantifying the impact of discretization – would provide a more complete theoretical foundation. Given the authors’ familiarity with the underlying ODE framework, I anticipate that this analysis should be tractable.

Furthermore, exploring the connections between this adaptive averaging technique and the Conjugate Gradient method for strongly convex quadratic optimization would be a valuable addition. Both methods involve iterative refinements guided by gradient information, and uncovering any formal relationships could provide deeper insights into the behavior and limitations of the proposed approach. This is merely a suggestion for future work, however, and does not detract from the current quality of the paper.

Overall, this is a strong submission with a clear presentation, solid theoretical grounding, and promising empirical results. The insight that adaptive averaging can substantially improve Nesterov's acceleration is both practically relevant and theoretically interesting. I recommend acceptance.
