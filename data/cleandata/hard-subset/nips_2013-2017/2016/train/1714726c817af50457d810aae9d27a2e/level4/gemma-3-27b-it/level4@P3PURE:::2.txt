Building upon prior work establishing a connection between accelerated Mirror Descent and continuous-time Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs), the authors extend the ODE dynamics with a generalized averaging scheme and propose a novel adaptive averaging strategy for its discretization. In certain cases, this new variant of AMD with adaptive averaging outperforms existing AMD algorithms employing restarting. The adaptive averaging strategy itself is conceptually interesting and well-motivated. However, the overall originality and practical usefulness of the contribution remain a concern. Many core elements – including the Lyapunov function and discretization approach – directly follow from a previous NIPS paper [7]. The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging appears incremental, and its practical impact is limited given that the authors focus solely on quadratic convergence rates; achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which are not universally applicable.

While the paper provides insightful results – such as an interpretation of the energy function, a reformulation in primal form, and a reduction to replicator dynamics – these primarily enhance understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent rather than significantly advancing the field of machine learning. Although the adaptive averaging is designed to satisfy conditions sufficient for ODE convergence, its theoretical foundation is incomplete due to approximations introduced during discretization. Critically, the paper lacks an explicit convergence result for the adaptive scheme, rendering it, as acknowledged by the authors, largely heuristic. Furthermore, numerous averaging schemes exist for Nesterov’s accelerated algorithms; without comparison to these alternatives, it is unclear whether the proposed adaptive variant offers a substantial improvement. The experimental evaluation, limited to a toy example with quadratic or linear objectives, is insufficiently compelling to demonstrate the advantages of adaptive averaging, particularly in the context of general strongly convex problems.
