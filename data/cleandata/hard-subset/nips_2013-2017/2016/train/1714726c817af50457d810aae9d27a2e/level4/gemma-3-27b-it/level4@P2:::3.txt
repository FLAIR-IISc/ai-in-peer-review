This paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, building upon the authors’ previous work [7] and related research demonstrating a connection between Nesterov’s method and continuous-time ODEs coupling primal and dual trajectories. The core contribution lies in analyzing more general averaging schemes within this framework, with Theorem 2 providing convergence rate guarantees under specific conditions on the averaging coefficients. The authors demonstrate the possibility of achieving convergence rates faster than 1/t², both in continuous-time and, importantly, preserving the quadratic convergence rate in a discrete-time setting. They further introduce an adaptive weighting scheme with convergence guarantees and validate its performance against existing restarting techniques through numerical experiments. 

This is a well-written and interesting paper that rigorously analyzes novel averaging schemes for accelerated methods. While the analysis of continuous dynamics is now well-established by a growing body of work, this paper offers meaningful contributions and results. Specifically, the result demonstrating the existence of averaging schemes achieving faster than 1/t² convergence is significant. It would be valuable to explore whether this improved rate is maintained when transitioning to a discrete procedure, though the authors acknowledge this is not currently addressed.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis of the generalized averaging scheme is mathematically sound and well-justified.
*   **Novel Contribution:** The demonstration of averaging schemes achieving convergence rates faster than 1/t² is a noteworthy advance.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic offers potential improvements over existing methods, supported by experimental results.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making the core ideas accessible.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively builds upon and contextualizes its contributions within the existing literature on accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Discrete Analysis Limited:** The theoretical analysis primarily focuses on the continuous setting. The absence of a detailed convergence analysis for the discrete version of the adaptive averaging algorithm is a limitation.
*   **Related Work Gap:** The paper could benefit from acknowledging more recent work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and collaborators on related topics in accelerated methods and dynamical systems. These works offer a similar perspective and would enrich the discussion.
*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are encouraging, expanding the range of objective functions and problem sizes could strengthen the empirical validation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods.
*   The demonstration of faster convergence rates with specific averaging schemes is a significant result.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic shows promise and is supported by experimental evidence.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the technical details and contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the discrete algorithm limits the practical impact of the proposed heuristic.
*   The omission of relevant related work weakens the context and completeness of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance. While the paper has some limitations, the core contributions are significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the presentation is clear. The potential benefits of the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic warrant further investigation, and this paper provides a solid foundation for future work. Addressing the points raised regarding the discrete analysis and related work in a follow-up study would further strengthen the contribution.
