Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies accelerated mirror descent (AMD) in continuous time through a generalized averaging formulation. The dynamics couple a dual variable Z that accumulates gradients at rate η(t) with a primal variable X obtained as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), the authors give sufficient conditions on η and w to ensure a prescribed convergence rate 1/r(t) in continuous time. They also present a second “energy” function Er that decreases under the same conditions. The framework recovers known results when r(t) = t^2 and connects acceleration with averaging, extending earlier work that links accelerated methods to ODEs (e.g., Su, Boyd, and Candès, NIPS 2014; Wibisono, Wilson, and Jordan, 2016) and in particular the continuous/discrete AMD analysis of Krichene, Bayen, and Bartlett (NIPS 2015).

The paper provides a primal representation of the dynamics, including examples on the positive orthant and on the simplex, where the accelerated dynamics reduce to an “accelerated” replicator equation. Building on this formulation, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts the weights to decrease the time derivative of the Lyapunov function whenever possible. In continuous time, this preserves the nominal convergence rate dictated by r(t); in discrete time, they propose a scheme that aims to maintain the quadratic rate and compare it empirically to adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient and speed restarting).

Strengths:
- Provides a clean, unified Lyapunov analysis for a broad class of AMD dynamics parameterized by η and w, with clear sufficient conditions for achieving a target rate 1/r(t).
- The energy function Er offers a useful interpretative lens, complementing Lr, and is aligned with the intuition from Hamiltonian/inertial systems literature.
- The primal reformulation clarifies how ∇^2ψ* ∘ ∇ψ shapes the flow and yields concrete examples; the accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex are of independent interest.
- The adaptive averaging idea is intuitive (push more weight where progress is good) and, in continuous time, maintains the Lyapunov decrease; the discrete implementation appears competitive with adaptive restarting in the reported experiments.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Originality is limited. Much of the technical machinery (Lyapunov analysis, continuous–discrete connection) closely follows prior NIPS work on AMD (Krichene et al., 2015) and ODE interpretations of Nesterov’s method (Su et al., 2014; Wibisono et al., 2016). The shift from simple to generalized averaging feels incremental, especially since the paper ultimately focuses on the quadratic rate r(t) = t^2; faster rates would require higher-order methods that are not broadly applicable.
- The pragmatic value of the generalized averaging conditions is unclear. The key condition depends on the trajectory through ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩, which is not checkable a priori, and the constructive prescription largely reduces to the specific r(t) = t^p schedules already known.
- The adaptive averaging method is positioned as a heuristic in discrete time. While the continuous-time Lyapunov analysis is sound, the discretization involves approximations and the paper does not (as submitted) provide a full convergence guarantee or rate proof for the discrete algorithm. This limits its theoretical grounding relative to established accelerated schemes.
- Empirical evaluation is weak: low-dimensional problems with quadratic/linear objectives on the simplex, plus KL. These are not sufficient to demonstrate advantage in more realistic settings (e.g., higher-dimensional, non-quadratic smooth convex, strongly convex cases), nor to assess robustness and sensitivity. Comparisons are limited to restarting heuristics; there is no comparison to alternative averaging strategies used in accelerated methods.
- Practical guidance is limited. There is little discussion on how to choose η(t), w(t), β, and step sizes; no ablations; and no analysis of stability/sensitivity.

Assessment by criteria:
- Quality: Solid continuous-time analysis; discrete-time adaptive averaging lacks a formal convergence guarantee in the submission. Conditions ensuring rate 1/r(t) are clear but may be hard to verify or use constructively beyond standard schedules.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. Some notation is heavy; positioning relative to Krichene et al. (2015), Su et al. (2014), and Wibisono et al. (2016) could be sharpened. Minor typos and phrasing issues can be cleaned up in revision.
- Originality: Moderate. The generalized averaging perspective is a small conceptual extension; the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel in this specific context but close in spirit to existing adaptive/restarting ideas.
- Significance: Potentially useful if backed by stronger discrete-time theory and broader empirical evidence. Current experiments are too limited to establish impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a rigorous convergence analysis (and rate) for the discrete adaptive averaging algorithm under standard assumptions (smooth convex, strongly convex).
- Expand experiments to higher dimensions and non-quadratic objectives; include strongly convex and merely convex cases, and report sensitivity to hyperparameters. Compare against other adaptive/averaging variants and modern baselines.
- Clarify the practical construction/selection of η and w for given r beyond the quadratic case, and discuss limitations when higher-order methods are needed for faster rates.
- Strengthen the discussion of related work to better differentiate the contributions from prior AMD and ODE-based acceleration papers.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, unified continuous-time analysis with interpretable Lyapunov and energy functions.
- Interesting accelerated replicator formulation on the simplex.
- Adaptive averaging is intuitive and shows empirical promise versus restarting.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incremental novelty relative to prior NIPS work on AMD/ODE perspectives.
- Lack of discrete-time convergence guarantees in the submission.
- Limited, low-dimensional experiments; missing comparisons to other averaging strategies.
