- Studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, extending prior ODE-based view of Nesterov to more general averaging schemes.  
- Builds on authors' earlier work mapping Nesterov’s method to an ODE coupling a primal averaged trajectory with a dual variable.  
- Theorem 2 proves convergence rates faster than 1/t^2 in continuous time under specific conditions on averaging coefficients.  
- Proposes an adaptive choice of averaging weights with theoretical guarantees and empirical evaluation.  
- Experimental results show the adaptive averaging heuristic compares favorably to restarting techniques for accelerated methods.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous, well-presented, and grounded in established continuous-time optimization dynamics.  
- Novel contribution: identification of averaging schemes that can potentially outperform standard accelerated rates.  
- Paper is clearly written and technically accessible.  
- Main limitation: analysis focuses on continuous-time dynamics; discrete-time (algorithmic) analysis is missing and deferred to future work.  
- Open question: whether the improved continuous-time rates persist after discretization and in practical implementations.  
- Related work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors should be better acknowledged and discussed.  
- Discussion of broader adaptive optimization literature (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) is lacking and would strengthen context.  
- Practical concern: the conditions required for faster-than-1/t^2 rates may be hard to verify in real problems.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel theory, significant potential for faster convergence, and supportive experiments; rejection arguments: limited discrete analysis and challenging practical conditions.
