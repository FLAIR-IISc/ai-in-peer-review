Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies accelerated first-order dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a general “averaging” view of acceleration. Building on Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015), the authors consider an accelerated mirror descent (AMD) ODE that couples a dual variable accumulating gradients at rate η(t) with a primal variable obtained as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). They derive a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) and give sufficient conditions on (η,w) ensuring Lr is nonincreasing, leading to convergence rate 1/r(t). This framework unifies and extends prior continuous-time analyses that connect Nesterov’s acceleration to ODEs (Su, Boyd, Candès; Wibisono, Wilson, Jordan), and clarifies the role of averaging in acceleration (cf. Flammarion–Bach).

The paper also presents a primal representation of the dynamics, with concrete examples for the positive orthant and the simplex. In particular, it yields an accelerated variant of the replicator dynamics by averaging the mirrored dual trajectory. The authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that locally adjusts the weights to further decrease the Lyapunov function, thereby preserving the target rate 1/r(t). They prove guarantees for the continuous-time heuristic and argue that, with a suitable discretization, the quadratic rate typical of accelerated first-order methods is preserved. Experiments compare adaptive averaging with adaptive restarting heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès; Su–Boyd–Candès), showing competitive or improved performance and monotone decrease of the Lyapunov function along the discrete trajectory.

This work is related to a substantial literature on inertial dynamics and Lyapunov analyses of accelerated methods (e.g., Attouch, Peypouquet, Redont), variational formulations (Wibisono et al.), and adaptive first-order methods (Adagrad, Adam). The paper positions itself by focusing on generalized averaging and adaptive selection of averaging weights.

Quality
- Theory: The continuous-time analysis is rigorous. The Lyapunov construction and the sufficient conditions in Theorem 2 are clear and flexible. The corollary that sets η = w r/W and w/W ≥ r′/r provides a practical recipe, and the alternative “energy” function Er is insightful. The primal formulations and examples (orthant, simplex/replicator) are correct and illuminating.
- Discrete-time: The main discrete-time algorithm and its rate claims are only sketched, with details deferred to the supplement. Given the importance of discretization for NIPS readers, a formal statement (assumptions, theorem, proof sketch) in the main text would substantially strengthen the paper.
- Technical clarity: There appears to be a small typo in Example 1: for r(t)=t^2, one needs a(t) ≥ r′/r = 2/t. Thus a(t) should be β/t (β ≥ 2), not β t. This matches the later derivation w(t) ∝ (t/t0)^{β−1} and η(t) = a(t) r(t) = β t.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The Lyapunov argument, the role of w and η, and the continuous-time guarantees are clearly explained. A few suggestions:
- Clearly separate and summarize the discrete-time result in the main text: list assumptions (smoothness, convexity/strong convexity), algorithmic steps, and a theorem stating the rate and dependence on constants.
- Clarify the choice of t0 > 0, its practical effect on initialization, and how to select it in practice.
- In Algorithm 1, define all quantities (γ, R, Dψ) and clean up notational inconsistencies (e.g., “βmax” vs. β, “correspondance” → “correspondence”).
- Complete the references (years, venues, pages). Include Attouch and Peypouquet’s work more explicitly in the related work and in the introduction to inertial/Lyapunov analyses.

Originality
The generalized averaging perspective and the adaptive averaging heuristic are novel and insightful. The sufficient conditions for Lyapunov decrease unify several continuous-time accelerated dynamics. The acceleration of replicator dynamics via averaging is a nice and accessible example. While continuous-time acceleration has been widely studied, the explicit design and analysis of adaptive averaging policies that provably preserve the target rate appears to be new.

Significance
The work provides a flexible template for designing accelerated methods through averaging and offers a principled alternative to heuristic restarts in both continuous- and discrete-time settings. If the discrete-time results are strengthened, this could influence how practitioners design and tune accelerated methods under constraints and mirror maps. The replicator example connects optimization and game dynamics and may appeal to multiple NIPS communities.

Strengths
- Clear and rigorous continuous-time analysis with a unifying Lyapunov framework.
- General sufficient conditions on (w, η) yielding desired rates; potential for rates faster than 1/t^2 in continuous time (via r(t)=t^p).
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with guarantees that preserve the rate by construction, and practical performance advantages over restarts in some cases.
- Insightful primal representations and instructive examples (orthant, simplex/replicator).
- Monotone energy/Lyapunov decrease observations supported by experiments.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Discrete-time theory is underemphasized in the main text. Provide a formal theorem for Algorithm 1 with assumptions and proof sketch; clarify how the continuous-time conditions translate into step sizes and parameters.
- Scope of experiments is limited (mostly 3D simplex). Add higher-dimensional tests, other feasible sets (e.g., l1-ball, spectrahedron), and comparisons with widely used baselines (e.g., FISTA with restarts, proximal variants).
- Discuss computational overhead of adaptive averaging relative to restarts and standard schedules.
- Clarify the relationship to Wibisono et al.’s variational framework; position your conditions (e.g., η = a r) against their α(t), β(t), and the induced discretizations.
- Explore whether stronger rates (e.g., linear under strong convexity) can be established for the adaptive scheme in discrete time, or state limitations explicitly.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution in continuous time with a unifying view and new guarantees for adaptive averaging.
- Practical heuristic with favorable empirical performance and a clear advantage (monotone Lyapunov decrease) over restarts.
- Broad applicability to constrained/mirror settings and interesting connections to replicator dynamics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient discrete-time theory in the main text; readers may be left uncertain about practical guarantees.
- Experimental evaluation is relatively small-scale and focused on one constraint set.
- Minor clarity/notation issues and incomplete references.

Overall evaluation
This is an interesting and carefully executed paper that advances our understanding of acceleration through generalized averaging and introduces an adaptive strategy with guarantees. Strengthening the discrete-time analysis in the main body and broadening the experiments would make it a stronger NIPS contribution. Even so, the theoretical framework and insights are valuable, and I lean positive, contingent on addressing the clarity and discretization concerns noted above.
