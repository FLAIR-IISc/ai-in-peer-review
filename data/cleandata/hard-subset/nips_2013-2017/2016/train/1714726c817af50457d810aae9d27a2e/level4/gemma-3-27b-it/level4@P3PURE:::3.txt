The paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization in both continuous and discrete settings. The authors continue the work initiated in Krichene et al. [7], which demonstrated that Nesterov’s method can be viewed as a discretization of an ODE system coupling a primal and dual trajectory. Specifically, the primal trajectory is obtained through a specific averaging of the mirror map of the dual variable. In this paper, more general averaging schemes are investigated. Theorem 2 then establishes convergence rates under suitable conditions on the averaging coefficients. In the continuous setting, convergence rates faster than 1/t<sup>2</sup> are derived. An adaptive choice of weights with associated convergence guarantees is also studied and subsequently evaluated in the experimental section. This adaptive scheme is compared with restarting techniques, which are known to empirically improve the convergence of accelerated methods. The theoretical analysis of the discrete version of the algorithm is not included. 

I find this to be an interesting and well-written paper. The analysis of the novel averaging scheme for accelerated methods is presented rigorously in the continuous setting. While the core ideas behind analyzing these continuous dynamics are now well-established in a series of recent papers, this work presents new contributions and results. In this context, the related work by Attouch, Peypouquet and their collaborators should be acknowledged. The main theorem, demonstrating the existence of averaging schemes yielding convergence rates faster than 1/t<sup>2</sup>, is a significant result. However, it would be valuable to understand whether this improved rate is preserved when transitioning to the discrete procedure.
