This paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization from a continuous-time perspective. Building on prior work that connects Nesterov's acceleration to a specific ordinary differential equation (ODE) system [11], the authors generalize this framework to a broader family of accelerated mirror descent dynamics, denoted AMDw,η. These dynamics are parameterized by two functions: `w(t)`, which defines a generalized weighted averaging scheme for the primal trajectory, and `η(t)`, which controls the rate at which a dual variable accumulates gradients.

The core of the theoretical analysis relies on a Lyapunov function. Theorem 2 provides clear, sufficient conditions on the functions `w` and `η` to achieve a desired convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))`. This framework is shown to be general enough to recover existing schemes and also to derive dynamics with convergence rates faster than the standard `O(1/t^2)`. As a compelling example, the paper demonstrates how the well-known replicator dynamics from evolutionary game theory can be accelerated using this averaging approach.

A key practical contribution is the proposal of an "adaptive averaging" heuristic. This method adaptively adjusts the averaging weights `w(t)` to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, thereby speeding up convergence empirically. Crucially, and unlike many existing heuristics, this adaptive scheme is proven to preserve the theoretical `O(1/r(t))` convergence rate. The paper provides a discrete-time version of this heuristic (Algorithm 1) and presents numerical experiments comparing it favorably against standard adaptive restarting techniques on several simplex-constrained problems.

### Strengths

1.  **Rigorous and Unified Framework:** The paper offers an elegant and rigorous analysis of a broad family of accelerated dynamics in continuous time. The use of a single Lyapunov function to derive sufficient conditions for a given convergence rate (Theorem 2) provides a unified and insightful perspective on the role of averaging in acceleration.

2.  **Novel Adaptive Heuristic with Guarantees:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution. While heuristics like adaptive restarting are widely used and effective, they often lack formal guarantees, especially for non-strongly convex problems. The authors provide a simple, intuitive adaptive method that is guaranteed to preserve the method's original convergence rate. This is a valuable theoretical safeguard that many other heuristics lack.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The numerical experiments are well-designed and clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of adaptive averaging. It performs at least as well as, and in some cases significantly better than, established restarting heuristics. The visualizations of the trajectories, objective value, and Lyapunov function provide convincing evidence for the method's behavior and stability.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The theoretical development is logical, and the connection between the continuous-time analysis and the discrete-time heuristic is well-motivated.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Analysis of the Discrete Algorithm:** The theoretical analysis is heavily focused on the continuous-time setting. While this provides excellent intuition, the convergence proof for the discrete-time implementation (Algorithm 1) is deferred to the supplementary material. Given that the discrete algorithm is a central contribution, including at least a sketch of its Lyapunov analysis and the final convergence result in the main paper would make the work more complete and self-contained.

2.  **Practicality of Faster-than-O(1/t^2) Rates:** The paper shows that continuous-time rates faster than `O(1/t^2)` are possible (Example 2). However, a footnote on page 5 suggests that preserving these rates upon discretization requires higher-order methods (e.g., cubic regularization). This is a critical limitation that significantly impacts the practical relevance of this result for *first-order* methods. A more prominent discussion of this point in the main text would be beneficial to properly contextualize the paper's claims and manage reader expectations.

### Detailed Questions and Suggestions

*   **Relation to Other ODEs for Acceleration:** The paper cites the important line of work by Attouch, Peypouquet, et al., which also uses ODEs (e.g., with Hessian-driven damping) to analyze acceleration. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief paragraph explicitly discussing the connections and differences between the averaging-based formulation presented here and those inertial dynamics. This would help readers better position the paper's contributions within the wider literature.

*   **On the Discretization of Faster Rates:** Could the authors elaborate on why discretizing the dynamics for `r(t)=t^p` with `p>2` necessarily leads to higher-order methods? Is this a fundamental barrier, or are there potential avenues for designing first-order algorithms that could capture some of the benefit of these faster continuous-time rates?

### Summary of Contributions and Recommendation

Overall, this is an interesting and high-quality paper that makes several valuable contributions. The generalization of accelerated mirror descent dynamics is elegant, and the resulting insights are useful. The main contribution—a novel adaptive averaging heuristic with formal convergence guarantees and strong empirical performance—is significant and likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the presentation and the scope of the claims regarding faster rates, but they do not undermine the core contributions of the paper. The work is technically sound, original, and advances our understanding of accelerated methods.
