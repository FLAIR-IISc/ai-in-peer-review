This paper builds upon the continuous-time ODE interpretation of accelerated mirror descent, previously developed by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The authors introduce a generalized averaging scheme and derive sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate using a Lyapunov function analysis. Based on this framework, they propose a novel and intuitive heuristic, "adaptive averaging," which dynamically modifies the averaging weights to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function. This approach is guaranteed to preserve the original O(1/t^2) convergence rate, a guarantee often lacking in other adaptive heuristics like restarting. The authors provide empirical results on several constrained convex optimization problems, showing that adaptive averaging performs comparably to or significantly better than adaptive restarting schemes.

I enjoyed reading this paper. The core idea is elegant, the presentation is clear, and the results are promising.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem. Given the widespread use of Nesterov's accelerated methods, a simple, principled, and effective heuristic for improving its practical performance is a valuable contribution. Often, novel adaptive algorithms are compared against a non-adaptive version of Nesterov's method; this work shows that a simple modification can make the baseline much stronger, potentially raising the bar for future comparisons.
*   **Clarity and Elegance:** The mathematical argument is clean and easy to follow, particularly for readers familiar with the prior work. The continuous-time analysis provides excellent intuition for why the adaptive heuristic should work.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** A key strength of the proposed heuristic is that, unlike many restarting schemes, it comes with a proof that the theoretical convergence rate of the underlying accelerated method is preserved. The use of a Lyapunov function to guide the adaptive scheme is a very nice touch.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. Adaptive averaging consistently performs as well as or better than popular restarting heuristics across a range of problems, with particularly notable improvements in the strongly convex case.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
While the technical development is a solid contribution building on prior work, there are a few areas where the paper could be strengthened:

*   **Analysis of the Discrete Algorithm:** The core theoretical contribution is the analysis of the continuous-time dynamics. However, for practical implementation, the discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) is what matters most. The paper states that a convergence rate bound for this algorithm is provided in the supplementary material. For completeness and impact, it would be highly beneficial to include at least a sketch of this proof or the main result of the discrete-time analysis in the main paper. This would more strongly connect the continuous-time intuition with the practical algorithm's guarantees.
*   **Connection to Conjugate Gradient:** For the specific case of unconstrained, strongly convex quadratic optimization, Nesterov's accelerated gradient method is known to be closely related to the Conjugate Gradient (CG) method. It would be insightful to include a discussion on the potential connections between the proposed adaptive averaging scheme and CG. For example, does adaptive averaging make the dynamics behave more like CG in this specific setting? Exploring this connection could provide deeper insights into the nature of the heuristic.
