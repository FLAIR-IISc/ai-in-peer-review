This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, proposing a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics with a generalized averaging scheme. The authors provide sufficient conditions on the weight functions to guarantee a desired convergence rate in continuous time and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate. The paper also includes numerical experiments comparing the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic to existing restarting heuristics.

The main ideas of the paper relate to previous work on accelerated mirror descent and continuous-time ODEs. The authors build upon an early work that connects accelerated Mirror Descent and continuous-time ODE, extending the ODE dynamics with a more general averaging scheme. The proposed adaptive averaging strategy is interestingly new and comes with a good intuition.

The paper's strengths include its ability to provide a unified study of the proposed ODE using an appropriate Lyapunov function, and its ability to give sufficient conditions on the weight functions to guarantee a desired convergence rate. The authors also provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their results, including a primal representation of the ODE and examples of its application to different constraint sets.

However, the paper's weaknesses include its lack of originality, as most elements directly follow from an early NIPS paper. The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging is kind of trivial and not of much use since eventually the authors only consider the case with quadratic rate; faster rate would require higher-order methods which are not always applicable. Additionally, some results, e.g., interpretation of energy function, reformulation into primal form, reduction to replicator dynamics, promote a better understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent algorithm, but are not significantly important to advance machine learning.

The paper's significance is also a concern, as the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is not fully theoretically grounded due to the approximation during discretization. Still, there is no explicit convergent result provided, meaning it is again just a heuristic, as referred by the authors. Also, there exist a number of different averaging schemes of Nester's accelerated algorithms; without comparing to other alternatives, it is unclear whether this adaptive variant makes a big different.

The numerical experiments provided are not convincing enough to demonstrate the advantage with adaptive averaging, especially for the general case with strongly convexity. The experiment on a toy example with only quadratic/linear objective is not interesting or convincing enough to demonstrate the advantage with adaptive averaging.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a clear and well-organized presentation of its results.
* The proposed adaptive averaging strategy is interestingly new and comes with a good intuition.
* The paper's ability to provide a unified study of the proposed ODE using an appropriate Lyapunov function is a strength.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's lack of originality is a concern, as most elements directly follow from an early NIPS paper.
* The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging is kind of trivial and not of much use since eventually the authors only consider the case with quadratic rate.
* The paper's significance is a concern, as the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is not fully theoretically grounded due to the approximation during discretization.
* The numerical experiments provided are not convincing enough to demonstrate the advantage with adaptive averaging.

Overall, I would rate the paper a 6. While it provides some interesting results and a clear presentation, its lack of originality and significance are concerns that need to be addressed.
