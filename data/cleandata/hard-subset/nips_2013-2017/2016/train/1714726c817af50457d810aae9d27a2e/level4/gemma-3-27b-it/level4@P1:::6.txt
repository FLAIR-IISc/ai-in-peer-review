This paper presents an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated constrained convex optimization. The method offers a key advantage over existing restarting heuristics by providing guarantees that it preserves the original convergence rate, even when dealing with non-strongly convex objective functions. Both theoretical analysis and experimental results support this claim. While the paper’s technical depth initially presented a challenge given my background, I recognize that the terminology and abbreviations are likely standard within the target audience. However, incorporating some introductory background information could broaden accessibility. The equations spanning lines 33-34 and 46-47 would benefit from more intuitive explanations to aid understanding. Additionally, exploring the connection between this adaptive averaging approach and methods used in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad [1] or Adam [2], would be valuable. Finally, it would be helpful to discuss the practical significance of this work. The current experiments are conducted in R<sup>3</sup>, a relatively small-scale setup. Understanding how this method (and related work) scales to much higher dimensions, commonly encountered in modern machine learning applications, is crucial. A minor comment regarding Figure 1: the reference to the solid line is somewhat ambiguous. Employing a dotted line or distinct colors to differentiate the trajectory of the primal variable would improve clarity. 

[1] Duchi, John, Elad Hazan, and Yoram Singer. "Adaptive subgradient methods for online learning and stochastic optimization." *Journal of Machine Learning Research* 12.Jul (2011): 2121-2159.
[2] Kingma, Diederik, and Jimmy Ba. "Adam: A method for stochastic optimization." *International Conference on Learning Representations* (ICLR). (2014).

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical framework, including a Lyapunov argument and convergence guarantees.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution that addresses a limitation of existing methods.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed heuristic compared to existing approaches.
*   **Clear Presentation of Equations**: The mathematical notation is clear and allows for a reproduction of the work.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Accessibility:** The paper's technical depth may pose a barrier to readers unfamiliar with the specific subfield.
*   **Limited Scalability Demonstration:** The experiments are limited to a small dimensionality (R<sup>3</sup>), raising questions about scalability.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Non-Convex Methods:** The connection to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization is not fully explored.
*   **Minor Clarity Issue:** The solid line in Figure 1 needs clarification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to accelerating constrained convex optimization.
*   The theoretical guarantees and empirical results provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.
*   The work addresses a relevant problem in optimization and has the potential to impact related fields.
*   The Lyapunov analysis is a significant achievement, and the theoretical guarantees are valuable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited scalability demonstration raises concerns about the practical applicability of the method to large-scale problems.
*   The lack of comparison to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization leaves open the question of potential synergies.
*   The steep learning curve for readers unfamiliar with the area may hinder broader adoption of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. While the limited scalability demonstration and lack of comparison to non-convex methods are minor weaknesses, the theoretical soundness, novelty, and empirical validation outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors consider addressing the scalability issue and exploring the connection to non-convex methods in future work.
