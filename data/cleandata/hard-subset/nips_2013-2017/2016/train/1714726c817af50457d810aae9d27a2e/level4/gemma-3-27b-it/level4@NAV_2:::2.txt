Building upon prior work connecting accelerated Mirror Descent and continuous-time ODEs [11], the authors extend the ODE dynamics with a more general averaging scheme and propose a novel adaptive averaging strategy for its discretization. In some cases, this new variant of AMD with adaptive averaging outperforms existing AMD algorithms employing restarting. The proposed adaptive averaging strategy is intuitively appealing and novel. However, the originality and broader impact remain concerns.

The paper builds heavily upon established results. Many elements – the Lyapunov function, discretization approach, and overall framework – directly follow from a previous NIPS publication [11]. The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging is incremental and lacks significant impact, particularly as the authors primarily consider the quadratic rate case; achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods not always applicable. While results such as the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation into primal form, and reduction to the replicator dynamics enhance understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent, they do not substantially advance the field of machine learning.

The adaptive averaging strategy is introduced to satisfy conditions sufficient for guaranteeing the convergence of the underlying ODE. However, it lacks complete theoretical grounding due to approximations inherent in the discretization process. Crucially, no explicit convergence result is provided, classifying it, as the authors acknowledge, as a heuristic. Furthermore, numerous other averaging schemes exist for Nesterov’s accelerated algorithms; without comparison to these alternatives, the demonstrable benefit of this adaptive variant remains unclear.

The experimental evaluation, focused on relatively simple examples with quadratic or linear objective functions, is not particularly compelling or representative of challenging real-world applications. It lacks the scope needed to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of adaptive averaging, particularly in scenarios involving strong convexity.

These additions address my previous concerns and warrant a reassessment of the paper’s potential impact. I have updated my scores accordingly.
