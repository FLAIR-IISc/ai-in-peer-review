- Paper introduces a family of continuous-time accelerated mirror descent ODEs parameterized by gradient accumulation rate η(t) and averaging weights w(t).
- Analysis uses a Lyapunov function Lr to give sufficient conditions on η and w to achieve a target convergence rate 1/r(t).
- Framework recovers known accelerated rates and yields an accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex as a special case.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that decreases the Lyapunov derivative when possible, preserving nominal continuous-time rates.
- Provides a discrete-time instantiation and experiments (mainly in R^3) comparing adaptive averaging to gradient- and speed-restarting.
- Well-positioned relative to continuous-time acceleration literature (Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan) and restart heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès).
- Clear conceptual unification: AMDw,η family and Lyapunov analysis characterize when averaging preserves desired rates.
- Novel adaptive averaging rule that is simple and preserves Lyapunov decrease in continuous time and quadratic rate in the proposed discretization.
- Useful connections and examples, including primal representations and accelerated replicator dynamics for constrained problems.
- Energy viewpoint via auxiliary energy Er provides interpretability (potential vs. kinetic) and matches experimental monotonicity.
- Discrete-time guarantees depend on a specific scheme; assumptions (smoothness, Lipschitz, strong convexity, properties of ψ/ψ*, step sizes) should be stated explicitly.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to low-dimensional toy problems; scalability to higher-dimensional benchmarks and wall-clock comparisons are needed.
- Report iteration complexity, runtime, and sensitivity to parameters (β, step size s, choice of ψ) in experiments.
- Clarify computational overhead and robustness of adaptive averaging (e.g., monitoring ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩) and behavior under noisy or inexact gradients.
- Paper is dense; add a brief primer on mirror maps, Bregman divergence, and roles of η(t) and w(t), and intuition for the derivative bound and Theorem 2 conditions.
- Unify and define notation (X, Z, Ž, X̌) at first use; explicitly define R, γ, λk, ak in Algorithm 1 and explain their roles.
- Improve figure clarity (distinct line styles/colors) to disambiguate primal and dual trajectories.
- More explicitly contrast the conditions and guarantees with inertial-dynamics and variational perspectives (Attouch–Peypouquet–Redont; Wibisono et al.) to highlight novelty.
- If scalable, method could be a practical, principled alternative to restarts; extensions to composite proximal settings and stochastic gradients would broaden impact.
- Clarify differences from AdaGrad/Adam in design and goals and whether adaptive averaging can combine with coordinate-wise or moment-based adaptations.
- Pros for acceptance: clear Lyapunov-based conditions, simple adaptive heuristic with continuous-time guarantees and discrete discretization preserving quadratic rate, and insightful examples/energy interpretation.
- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical validation and unclear scalability, dense presentation needing more intuition, and discrete-time guarantees/assumptions not stated prominently.
- Key open questions: how to choose β, η(t), and step size in practice and sensitivity to these choices; computational overhead compared to restarting; applicability to composite objectives and stochastic gradients.
- Minor comments: use distinct styles/colors in Figure 1, define all symbols in Algorithm 1 where they appear, and move key background material from the supplement into the main text.
