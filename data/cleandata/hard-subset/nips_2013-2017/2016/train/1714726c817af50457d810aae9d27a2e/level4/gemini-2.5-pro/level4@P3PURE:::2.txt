This paper builds upon prior work connecting accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ODEs. The authors extend the ODE dynamics with a more general averaging scheme and, for one of its discretizations, propose a novel adaptive averaging strategy. The resulting AMD variant is shown in some cases to outperform existing AMD algorithms that use restarting. The proposed adaptive averaging strategy is novel and intuitively appealing. However, despite these positive aspects, the paper's originality and practical utility are significant concerns.

Major Weaknesses:
-   **Limited Originality:** The work's originality is a primary concern. Many core components, such as the Lyapunov function and the discretization approach, are directly adopted from a prior NIPS paper [7]. The main theoretical extension—from simple to generalized averaging—appears incremental. Its utility is also questionable, as the authors ultimately focus on the case with a quadratic rate. Achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which are not always applicable and fall outside the scope of this extension.

-   **Insufficient Theoretical Grounding:** The proposed adaptive averaging scheme lacks a rigorous theoretical foundation. While the strategy is motivated by conditions sufficient for the convergence of the continuous-time ODE, this justification does not fully translate to the discrete algorithm due to approximation errors. Crucially, the paper does not provide an explicit convergence result for the algorithm with adaptive averaging, which, as the authors acknowledge, remains a heuristic. Furthermore, several alternative averaging schemes exist for Nesterov's accelerated algorithms, but the paper provides no comparison, making it difficult to assess whether this adaptive variant offers a significant advantage.

-   **Minor Contributions and Weak Experiments:** The paper's contributions seem minor in impact. While some results—such as the interpretation of the energy function, the reformulation into a primal form, and the reduction to replicator dynamics—enhance the understanding of AMD, their practical importance for advancing machine learning is not clearly established. This is compounded by weak empirical validation. The experiments are limited to a toy example with a quadratic/linear objective, which is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the advantage of the adaptive averaging strategy, especially for the more general case of strongly convex functions.
