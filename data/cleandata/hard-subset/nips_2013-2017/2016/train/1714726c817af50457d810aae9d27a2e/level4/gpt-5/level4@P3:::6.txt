Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a unified continuous-time framework. The central object is an accelerated mirror descent (AMD) ODE parameterized by two weight functions: η(t), controlling the rate at which a dual variable Z(t) accumulates gradients, and w(t), used to form a weighted average X(t) of the mirrored dual trajectory. Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors provide simple sufficient conditions on η and w to guarantee convergence at rate 1/r(t). This ties acceleration to averaging and recovers standard t^2 rates as special cases. The paper also gives a primal representation and demonstrates that the replicator dynamics on the simplex can be “accelerated” by using these averaging weights.

The main algorithmic contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses weights online to further decrease the Lyapunov function whenever possible. The heuristic preserves the baseline convergence rate guarantees in continuous time (and, via a discrete Lyapunov argument, preserves the quadratic rate for the discrete-time scheme when r(t)=t^2). Empirically, the adaptive averaging method performs at least as well as popular adaptive restart heuristics (gradient and speed restarts), with noticeable improvements in some cases. The work builds on and relates to the ODE viewpoints of Nesterov-style acceleration (e.g., Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan), proximal/constraint-aware accelerations via mirror maps, and restart heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès). It also connects to earlier observations linking averaging and acceleration (e.g., Flammarion–Bach).

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: A clean, general Lyapunov framework yields verifiable sufficient conditions for accelerated rates across a family of AMD dynamics. The introduction of a second “energy” functional complements the Lyapunov analysis.
- Generality: The formulation accommodates constraints via mirror maps, covers various rates r(t), and recovers known dynamics as special cases.
- Novel heuristic with guarantees: The adaptive averaging rule is natural (drives the Lyapunov decrease) and, unlike common restart heuristics, comes with preservation-of-rate guarantees even outside the strongly convex setting.
- Illustrative example: The accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex is interesting and may be of independent value in game-theoretic and evolutionary contexts.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Theoretical scope: The sufficient conditions in Theorem 2 include a term that depends on the unknown trajectory. While Corollary 1 offers a usable schedule, the adaptive rule would benefit from more explicit, checkable conditions in discrete time (beyond the supplement) and a discussion of robustness to noise or inexact gradients. Can the approach be extended to stochastic gradients?
- Strongly convex case: The paper emphasizes preservation of the baseline rate; it would be valuable to state whether the adaptive heuristic can provably recover linear rates under standard strong convexity assumptions (as several restart schemes do).
- Discretization details: The mapping t=k√s and the choice of β and step size s critically affect performance. Please provide guidance or adaptive rules for tuning, and quantify the computational overhead of maintaining ak versus using restarts.

Clarity
- Exposition: Parts of the derivation are terse. The transition from AMDw,η to AMD′w,η and the bound leading to Eq. (3) would benefit from intuitive explanation (what each term represents and why the conditions in Theorem 2 are natural). A short boxed recap of mirror maps, Bregman divergence, and their role in maintaining feasibility would help non-specialists.
- Figures: The “solid line” reference in Figure 1 is ambiguous; consider using distinct colors or line styles to disambiguate primal versus dual/mirrored trajectories.
- Notation and assumptions: State clearly when ψ, ψ* are assumed twice differentiable, and what happens when they are not (e.g., use of proximal operators). Clarify the role and choice of z* in Lr, especially in the presence of multiple minimizers (as you briefly note).
- References: Several citations in the reference list appear incomplete; please standardize bibliographic details.

Originality
- The continuous-time perspective on acceleration is well studied; the paper’s original contribution is the generalized averaging view with trajectory-adaptive weights that preserve rates. The accelerated replicator instance and the energy functional are also nice additions. Please further contrast your guarantees with those in Su–Boyd–Candès and Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan (e.g., where your conditions are weaker/stronger, or offer different insights).

Significance and empirical evaluation
- Practical impact: The experiments are in R^3 for visualization. To bolster significance, include higher-dimensional tests relevant to constrained convex problems (e.g., simplex-constrained logistic regression, portfolio optimization on the simplex, sparse learning with simplex/orthant constraints, or box-constrained smooth problems). Report wall-clock time, iterations, and sensitivity to β and s. Compare against (i) accelerated mirror descent with adaptive restart (gradient/speed), (ii) FISTA with restart on comparable proximal constraints, and (iii) mirror descent with AdaGrad-style step-size adaptation (as a non-accelerated baseline).
- Relation to AdaGrad/Adam: While AdaGrad/Adam adapt learning rates and your method adapts averaging weights, it would be useful to clarify complementarity. For instance, can adaptive averaging be combined with coordinate-wise AdaGrad scaling within the mirror map framework? What breaks if gradients are stochastic?

Pros for acceptance
- General and elegant Lyapunov-based analysis unifying several accelerated dynamics.
- Simple, implementable heuristic with provable preservation of rates and improved empirical performance over restarts in your tests.
- Interesting application to replicator dynamics and clear connection to mirror descent with constraints.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical evidence on large-scale/high-dimensional problems and missing comparisons to widely used proximal accelerated methods with restarts.
- Some parts of the exposition are difficult for non-experts; key discrete-time guarantees are deferred to the supplement.
- No demonstrated theoretical advantage over restarts in the strongly convex (linear-rate) regime.

Minor comments
- Clarify the initialization at t0>0 versus t0=0 and the practical implications in discrete time.
- Expand on the intuitive meaning of constant a(t) segments (exponential growth of w(t)) versus a(t)=βt (polynomial growth), and how this manifests in the discrete algorithm.
- Detail computational cost per iteration and memory footprint of Algorithm 1 relative to restart methods.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and well-motivated paper. With stronger experimental validation at scale, clearer exposition of key derivations, and a sharpened comparison to adaptive-rate methods and restart schemes, it would make a solid contribution to the NeurIPS literature on accelerated dynamics.
