The paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization in both continuous and discrete settings. The authors continue the study initiated in Krichene et al. [11], where Nesterov's method is shown to be an appropriate discretization of an ODE system, coupling a primal trajectory with a dual one. In particular, the primal trajectory is obtained by performing a specific averaging of the mirror of the dual variable. In the present paper, more general averaging schemes are studied.

The authors provide a unified study of the ODE using an appropriate Lyapunov function, given by Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f?) + Dψ∗(Z,z?). They give a sufficient condition on η, w, and r for Lr to be a Lyapunov function for the ODE, and show that under these conditions, f(X(t)) converges to f? at the rate 1/r(t). The authors also propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that provides guarantees on the convergence rate.

The paper is well-written, and the analysis of the new averaging scheme for accelerated methods is rigorous. The main theorem, showing that there are averaging schemes with faster convergence rates than 1/t^2, is a relevant one. However, it would be interesting to understand if this improved rate is preserved when passing to the discrete procedure. The authors should be commended for providing a thorough analysis of the continuous dynamics.

The paper also provides a comparison of the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic with restarting techniques, which are known to improve convergence of accelerated methods in practice. The experimental results show that the adaptive averaging heuristic compares favorably to the restarting techniques on all examples, with a significant improvement in the strongly convex case.

Overall, I think this is an interesting and well-written paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The analysis of the new averaging scheme for accelerated methods is rigorous, and the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a valuable addition to the field.
