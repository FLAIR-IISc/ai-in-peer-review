Summary
The paper studies accelerated first-order dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a generalized averaging viewpoint. Building on prior work showing that Nesterov’s method arises as a discretization of a continuous-time system coupling a dual variable and a “mirrored” primal average, the authors introduce a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) ODEs parameterized by two positive weight functions: η(t), which controls gradient accumulation in the dual, and w(t), which specifies the averaging in the primal. Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), they provide sufficient conditions on (η,w,r) under which Lr decreases, yielding convergence rate O(1/r(t)). This recovers the standard O(1/t^2) rate and, in continuous time, allows faster rates by selecting r(t) = t^p with p > 2.

The paper also:
- Gives a primal-only representation of the dynamics and concrete examples for common constraint sets, including an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics on the simplex.
- Introduces a second “energy” function Er that is also shown to be decreasing under the same conditions.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses w(t) (equivalently a(t) = w(t)/W(t)) to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative when possible, preserving the baseline rate guarantees.
- Presents a discrete-time algorithm (for the quadratic rate) and numerical comparisons against adaptive restarting heuristics, showing that adaptive averaging is competitive and sometimes superior.

Relation to previous work
The paper connects and extends several lines of research:
- ODE interpretations of Nesterov’s acceleration (e.g., Su-Boyd-Candès; Krichene-Bayen-Bartlett).
- Variational perspectives on accelerated flows (Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan).
- Restarting heuristics for accelerated methods (O’Donoghue-Candès; Su-Boyd-Candès).
- Inertial gradient-like systems and Lyapunov analyses (Attouch, Peypouquet, Redont and collaborators). Citing this body of work more prominently would help place the contributions within the broader dynamical-systems literature on acceleration and damping.

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis is careful and technically sound. Existence/uniqueness is addressed via a reformulated ODE (AMD′) and appropriate initialization at t0 > 0. The Lyapunov argument is clear and yields a unified condition for O(1/r(t)) rates.
- The energy function Er provides additional insight and appears to align with empirical behavior across heuristics.
- The discrete-time analysis for the adaptive heuristic seems limited in the main paper (with details in the supplement). It remains unclear whether continuous-time rates faster than O(1/t^2) can be preserved by a first-order discretization without resorting to higher-order schemes (consistent with known results).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and well organized. Notation is heavy but consistent. It would help to provide more intuition for condition (2) in Theorem 2, and practical guidance for choosing r, η, and w (or a(t)) beyond illustrative examples.
- Clarifying the practical impact of initializing at t0 > 0, and the computational overhead of adaptive averaging versus restarting, would improve readability.

Originality
- The generalized averaging formulation and the explicit coupling between η(t) and w(t) to control the Lyapunov derivative are original and insightful.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is a simple, principled mechanism that differs from restart schemes.
- The accelerated replicator example is a nice illustration of the framework on constrained domains.

Significance
- The work offers a unifying lens for accelerated dynamics and a flexible recipe to trade off averaging and accumulation to attain desired rates in continuous time.
- The heuristic is practically useful, with experiments suggesting advantages over popular restarting strategies on several problems.
- Establishing whether faster-than-quadratic continuous-time rates can be maintained in discrete time with first-order methods would further raise the impact.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous continuous-time analysis with a unifying Lyapunov framework.
- General, interpretable conditions yielding O(1/r(t)) rates and encompassing known results.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with provable preservation of baseline rates and strong empirical performance.
- Concrete primal formulations and illustrative dynamics (e.g., accelerated replicator).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Discrete-time theory: The main text does not fully establish whether rates faster than O(1/t^2) can be discretized with first-order methods. Clarify the scope of the discrete guarantees (main vs. supplement) and discuss limitations vis-à-vis higher-order discretizations (e.g., cubic-regularized methods).
- Practical guidance: Offer recommendations for selecting r(t), η(t), and a(t) in practice; discuss sensitivity to these choices.
- Comparisons: Expand experiments to include higher-dimensional problems and report computational overhead of adaptive averaging relative to restarts.
- Related work: More explicit connections to Attouch–Peypouquet’s inertial systems and Hessian-driven damping would strengthen the positioning.

Pros
- Solid and general theoretical contribution in continuous time.
- Simple, principled adaptive heuristic with guarantees and good empirical behavior.
- Broad applicability to constrained settings with illustrative examples.

Cons
- Limited discrete-time guarantees in the main paper; unclear feasibility of preserving faster-than-quadratic rates with first-order discretizations.
- Experiments, while informative, are relatively small-scale; overhead and robustness analyses are limited.

Overall assessment
An interesting, well-executed paper that advances our understanding of acceleration via generalized averaging and offers a useful adaptive heuristic. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying the discrete-time scope, adding pointers to related inertial dynamics (Attouch–Peypouquet), and expanding practical guidance.
