

The paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through the lens of continuous-time ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The authors introduce a generalized Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) framework parameterized by weight functions w and η, which allows for flexible averaging schemes. Using a Lyapunov argument, they establish sufficient conditions on these weight functions to achieve desired convergence rates, generalizing previous work by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) that connected Nesterov's accelerated methods to continuous-time dynamics. The authors demonstrate how the replicator dynamics (a mirror descent variant on the simplex) can be accelerated through simple averaging, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function while preserving the quadratic convergence rate.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a unified theoretical framework for analyzing a broad family of accelerated dynamics using a Lyapunov function approach, which generalizes the work of Su et al. (NIPS 2014) on Nesterov's method. Second, it establishes a clear connection between acceleration and averaging, formalizing an observation made in earlier work (Flammarion and Bach, COLT 2015). Third, it proposes a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence guarantees while outperforming existing heuristics like adaptive restarting in numerical experiments.

The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported. The authors carefully establish existence and uniqueness of solutions, provide sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to decrease, and prove convergence rates under these conditions. The proof techniques are sound, with appropriate use of convex analysis and differential equations. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated by the desire to decrease the Lyapunov function more rapidly, and the authors provide a clear discrete-time implementation (Algorithm 1) with theoretical guarantees.

The experimental section is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare adaptive averaging against several existing heuristics (gradient restarting and speed restarting) across multiple problem types: strongly convex quadratic, weakly convex quadratic, linear, and KL divergence objectives on the simplex. The results consistently show that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as existing methods, with significant improvements in the strongly convex case. Crucially, the experiments confirm that the Lyapunov function decreases under adaptive averaging (unlike restarting heuristics), which validates the theoretical guarantees. The authors also provide insightful visualizations of the solution trajectories, objective function values, and energy function behavior.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is clearly explained. The supplementary material provides valuable additional context on mirror maps and the replicator dynamics. The writing is professional and accessible to experts in optimization.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a substantial contribution by generalizing the averaging scheme in accelerated mirror descent and providing a unified Lyapunov analysis. While the connection between acceleration and averaging has been observed previously, the paper provides the first systematic analysis of this relationship using a Lyapunov function, and the adaptive averaging heuristic represents a novel approach that preserves convergence guarantees. The specific application to replicator dynamics on the simplex is particularly interesting and well-motivated.

The significance of the work is evident from both theoretical and practical perspectives. Theoretical significance lies in the unified framework for analyzing accelerated dynamics that preserves convergence guarantees. Practical significance is demonstrated through numerical experiments showing clear improvements over existing methods, particularly for strongly convex problems. This work will be valuable to researchers developing accelerated optimization methods, as it provides a theoretically grounded heuristic for improving convergence rates while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

The paper is technically sound, with no apparent gaps in the theoretical development. The authors carefully address the limitations of existing heuristics (such as adaptive restarting, which does not preserve convergence rates for non-strongly convex problems) and provide a solution that overcomes these limitations. The experimental design is thorough, with multiple objective functions and problem types, and the results are well-presented.

I note that the paper builds upon the authors' previous NIPS work (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015) and connects to other relevant NIPS papers (Su et al., NIPS 2014; O'Donoghue and Candès, FOCM 2015). The related work is appropriately cited and discussed.

Strengths:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear sufficient conditions for convergence
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence guarantees
- Comprehensive numerical experiments across multiple problem types
- Clear connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms
- Well-written and well-organized presentation

Weaknesses:
- The adaptive averaging heuristic could benefit from more detailed analysis of its computational complexity compared to restarting methods
- The paper could more explicitly discuss how the adaptive averaging heuristic relates to other adaptive methods like AdaGrad and Adam

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical framework is sound, the adaptive heuristic is well-motivated and effective, and the experiments provide strong empirical support. The work is well-suited for NIPS, as it advances the state of the art in optimization methods with clear theoretical and practical implications. The paper addresses all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) effectively and would be of interest to a broad audience in machine learning and optimization. I recommend acceptance.
