- Extends the AMD–ODE connection via a generalized averaging scheme and an adaptive averaging heuristic for discretized dynamics.
- Builds on prior continuous-time interpretations of acceleration (Su et al. 2014) and constrained extensions (Krichene et al. 2015).
- Main contribution: unified Lyapunov-based framework with adaptive averaging that dynamically computes weights to preserve convergence rates.
- Provides sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to decrease and derives conditions for specific convergence rates, focusing on quadratic r(t)=t².
- Demonstrates accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex as an application.
- Numerical experiments show adaptive averaging can outperform restarting heuristics on simple constrained problems.
- Theoretical continuous-time analysis is sound and carefully presented.
- Reformulation in primal space and derivation of accelerated replicator dynamics are technically solid and insightful.
- Core framework is incremental and largely builds on Krichene et al. (2015); the generalized averaging extension appears trivial.
- Only the quadratic-rate case is considered; achieving faster rates would require higher-order methods not broadly applicable.
- Adaptive averaging is introduced as a heuristic without rigorous convergence guarantees for the discrete algorithm.
- The claim that the adaptive heuristic “preserves the convergence rate” lacks an explicit discrete-time proof.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to low-dimensional (R³) simplex problems with quadratic/linear objectives, insufficient to show practical relevance.
- No experiments on non-quadratic or high-dimensional problems and limited baseline comparisons beyond restarting heuristics.
- Paper does not convincingly advance the state of the art or justify adoption of the proposed heuristic in practice.
- Recommended improvements: provide discrete convergence guarantees, broader/harder experiments, and comparisons to other adaptive averaging strategies.
- Overall assessment: technically correct but incremental with limited empirical support, reducing its impact and suitability for NIPS.
