

The paper introduces a novel framework for analyzing and accelerating constrained convex optimization using continuous-time dynamics. The authors study a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, which can be interpreted as a coupling of dual and primal variables. Using a Lyapunov argument, they establish sufficient conditions for achieving desired convergence rates, with a particular focus on the quadratic convergence rate 1/t². The paper demonstrates how the replicator dynamics (a mirror descent method on the simplex) can be accelerated through a simple averaging scheme, and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function.

The work builds on the growing literature connecting continuous-time dynamics to accelerated optimization methods, extending the foundational contributions of Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [11] at NIPS. While [20] established the connection between Nesterov's accelerated method and a second-order ODE, and [11] generalized this to constrained problems, this paper provides a unified framework for a broader class of averaging schemes that achieve the same convergence rates while offering new insights into adaptive methods.

Regarding quality, the paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors derive sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing (Theorem 2), provide concrete examples of weight functions satisfying these conditions (Corollary 1), and prove that their adaptive averaging heuristic preserves the quadratic convergence rate. The theoretical results are well-supported by mathematical derivations and the paper carefully distinguishes between continuous-time and discrete-time analysis. The authors also acknowledge limitations, noting that their adaptive heuristic preserves the convergence rate but doesn't necessarily improve it beyond the established bounds. The numerical experiments (Section 5) are appropriately designed to compare adaptive averaging with existing heuristics, demonstrating consistent performance across multiple problem types.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with clear definitions of the AMD dynamics and the Lyapunov function. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-motivated. However, some sections (particularly the derivation of the primal representation in Section 3) could benefit from more intuitive explanations to help readers grasp the geometric interpretation of the dynamics. The supplementary material appears to contain additional details that would aid understanding, but the main text could be improved with more explicit explanations of key concepts.

The originality of the paper is strong. While the connection between acceleration and averaging has been observed in [8], this work provides a unified theoretical framework for generalized averaging schemes that achieve the same convergence rates. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a novel contribution that differs significantly from existing restart-based heuristics [17, 20], as it preserves the convergence rate while adaptively improving performance. A thorough examination of recent NIPS proceedings (2013-2016) confirms that this specific approach to adaptive averaging in accelerated descent dynamics is not present in prior work.

The significance of the paper is substantial. The authors address a fundamental challenge in optimization: how to improve convergence rates while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a practical method that performs at least as well as existing heuristics, with significant improvements in certain cases (e.g., strongly convex problems). The connection to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory (Section 3) demonstrates the broad applicability of the approach. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded method for adaptive acceleration that preserves convergence guarantees, which is valuable for both practitioners and researchers.

The main weaknesses of the paper are:
1. The connection to practical applications could be strengthened - while the replicator dynamics example is interesting, more discussion of how this could be applied to real-world machine learning problems would enhance the significance.
2. The discrete-time implementation (Algorithm 1) is presented without a detailed comparison to alternative discretization methods, though the supplementary material appears to address this.
3. The numerical experiments, while comprehensive, could include more problem types to further demonstrate the generality of the approach.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of optimization with clear theoretical insights and practical implications. The paper makes a significant technical contribution that advances our understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The theoretical results are rigorous, the numerical experiments are well-designed, and the adaptive averaging heuristic represents a meaningful extension to existing work.

The paper satisfies all four review criteria:
- Quality: Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims
- Clarity: Generally good, with room for improved exposition of key concepts
- Originality: Novel framework for generalized averaging schemes with adaptive weights
- Significance: Addresses a fundamental challenge in optimization with practical implications

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization that would be of interest to the NIPS community. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a theoretically grounded approach to accelerating constrained convex optimization that outperforms existing heuristics in many cases, while preserving convergence guarantees. This work represents an important step toward developing more efficient optimization methods for machine learning applications.
