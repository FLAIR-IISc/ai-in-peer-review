Summary
This paper studies accelerated first-order dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a generalized averaging perspective. The authors consider a coupled continuous-time system where a dual variable Z(t) accumulates gradients at rate η(t), and a primal variable X(t) is the weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). They introduce a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), and derive sufficient conditions on η and w to guarantee that Lr decreases, yielding a convergence guarantee f(X(t)) − f* ≤ Lr(X(t0),Z(t0),t0)/r(t) for a user-specified rate function r(t). This unifies and extends prior continuous-time acceleration analyses, including Nesterov-type dynamics and the variational framework.

The paper provides: (i) existence and uniqueness of solutions for the proposed ODE under mild conditions; (ii) a primal representation that clarifies how mirror geometry constrains the dynamics; (iii) concrete examples for the positive orthant and the simplex, including an “accelerated replicator” dynamics; (iv) an additional energy function Er that decreases under the same conditions; and (v) an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts weights online to further decrease Lr. In discrete time, a corresponding algorithm is proposed that preserves the quadratic O(1/k^2) rate of accelerated mirror descent, with experiments (primarily on simplex-constrained problems) showing that adaptive averaging is competitive with and sometimes superior to adaptive restarting strategies (gradient and speed restarts).

Relation to prior work
The work builds on continuous-time analyses of Nesterov acceleration (Su, Boyd, Candès, 2016), variational perspectives (Wibisono, Wilson, Jordan, 2016), and accelerated mirror descent in continuous and discrete time (Krichene, Bayen, Bartlett, 2015). It relates to averaging-based views of acceleration (Flammarion & Bach, 2015), and to restart heuristics (O’Donoghue & Candès, 2015). The adaptive averaging idea is conceptually akin to adaptive step-size methods (Adagrad, Adam), but tailored to accelerated/mirror settings rather than stochastic or coordinate-sparse regimes. The acceleration of replicator dynamics under mirror geometry appears novel and interesting.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: The generalized averaging formulation and Lyapunov analysis provide a clean, modular way to target a desired rate r(t) by designing η and w.
- Theoretical guarantees: Clear conditions under which Lr is a Lyapunov function and Er decreases; existence/uniqueness addressed; connection to known accelerated ODEs is made explicit.
- Practical heuristic with guarantees: The adaptive averaging rule preserves the base convergence rate, unlike some restart heuristics whose guarantees can be limited outside strongly convex settings.
- New example dynamics: Accelerated replicator dynamics for simplex constraints is a compelling illustration with potential relevance in game theory and learning.
- Empirical evidence: On several constrained problems, adaptive averaging is at least competitive and sometimes substantially better than restarts, while maintaining monotonic Lyapunov decrease.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Trajectory-dependent condition: One key condition (sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ multiplied by r − ηW/w) is trajectory-dependent and hard to verify a priori; the corollary offers a constructive route but can be conservative.
- Discrete-time theory is limited in the main text: Guarantees, parameter choices (β, step size s), and sensitivity are mostly deferred to the supplement; clarity on robustness across problems would help.
- Experimental scope: Experiments focus on low-dimensional simplex problems; scalability to higher dimensions and broader constraint sets is not systematically assessed; wall-clock and gradient-call comparisons are absent.
- Comparison set: Restarts are included, but comparisons to other adaptive acceleration schemes (e.g., robust variants of FISTA, alternative restart rules, or parameter-free momentum schedules) are limited.
- Practical guidance: Implementers may need more guidance on selecting r(t), β, and ψ/ψ* in various settings; it would help to clarify when adaptive averaging provides the most benefit.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The Lyapunov-based derivations are standard and carefully executed; the second energy function adds interpretability. Existence/uniqueness are handled. The main limitation is that some discrete-time details and proofs are in the supplement, and the verifiability of the trajectory-dependent condition motivates reliance on the corollary’s construction.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The primal and dual forms are helpful. Suggestions:
- Consolidate assumptions early (smoothness of f, Lipschitzness of ∇ψ*, differentiability of ψ* or ψ where needed).
- Provide more intuition for the condition r = ηW/w and for choosing a(t) = w/W; a brief table of common r(t) choices and resulting (w, η) would aid readers.
- Clarify initialization at t0 > 0 and its practical implications in discretization.
- Streamline notation around Ž, X̌, and the role of mirror maps.

Originality
The adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated mirror descent, with guarantees via Lyapunov decrease, and the accelerated replicator example, are original and interesting. The unifying conditions for general r(t) are a useful addition to the continuous-time literature.

Significance
The work advances our understanding of acceleration in constrained settings and offers a practical heuristic that preserves theoretical rates. If further developed in discrete time and validated at scale, it could influence the design of robust accelerated mirror methods across applications.

Specific suggestions
- Expand discrete-time analysis in the main text: state the key theorem, assumptions, and a short proof sketch; clarify parameter choices and complexity per iteration.
- Report experiments in higher dimensions, include runtime/gradient-call plots, and evaluate sensitivity to β and step size.
- Compare to additional baselines (e.g., FISTA with and without restarts, monotone variants), and ensure fair tuning of restart thresholds.
- Explore strongly convex settings more deeply: can adaptive averaging yield faster than O(1/k^2) rates with appropriate r(t)?
- Discuss stochastic/noisy gradients and composite objectives (proximal terms), and whether adaptive averaging integrates with these.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and general theoretical framework with clear conditions to target desired rates.
- Novel, simple heuristic (adaptive averaging) that preserves guarantees and shows empirical benefits.
- Clear connections to and extensions of influential continuous-time acceleration literature.
- New accelerated replicator dynamics with potential impact beyond optimization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical validation is limited in scope and lacks scalability metrics.
- Discrete-time guarantees and practical guidance are largely deferred to the supplement.
- Some conditions are trajectory-dependent, making practical verification nontrivial without the conservative construction.

Overall recommendation
A well-executed and conceptually valuable paper with a promising heuristic and solid theory. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the discrete-time exposition and broadening the experimental evaluation.
