- Paper studies accelerated mirror descent (AMD) ODEs with two weight functions η(t) and w(t), using a Lyapunov Lr to guarantee 1/r(t) convergence and ties acceleration to averaging.  
- Provides a primal representation and shows accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex as an interesting application.  
- Introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that decreases the Lyapunov function online and preserves baseline convergence rates in continuous time and quadratic discrete-time rates when r(t)=t^2.  
- Empirically, adaptive averaging matches or outperforms popular adaptive restart heuristics (gradient and speed restarts) with noticeable improvements in some cases.  
- Strength: clean, general Lyapunov framework with a complementary second “energy” functional yielding verifiable sufficient conditions for acceleration.  
- Strength: general formulation handles constraints via mirror maps, multiple rates r(t), and recovers known dynamics as special cases.  
- Strength: adaptive averaging is natural, implementable, and comes with preservation-of-rate guarantees even outside strong convexity.  
- Strength: accelerated replicator example is novel and potentially valuable for game-theoretic/evolutionary settings.  
- Theorem 2’s sufficient conditions depend on the unknown trajectory; Corollary 1 gives a usable schedule but discrete-time checkable conditions and robustness to noise/inexact or stochastic gradients are lacking.  
- Unclear whether the adaptive heuristic can provably recover linear rates under standard strong convexity, unlike several restart schemes.  
- Discretization choices (t = k√s, β, step size s) critically affect performance; paper lacks practical guidance or adaptive tuning rules and doesn’t quantify overhead of maintaining ak vs restarts.  
- Exposition is terse in places; transitions (e.g., AMDw,η to AMD′w,η and Eq. (3)) need more intuitive explanation about term meanings and why conditions are natural.  
- Suggest adding a short recap of mirror maps and Bregman divergence for non-specialists.  
- Figures: ambiguous “solid line” reference; recommend distinct colors/line styles to disambiguate primal vs dual trajectories.  
- Notation/assumptions: clarify twice-differentiability of ψ, ψ* (or proximal handling when not), and clarify choice/role of z* when multiple minimizers exist.  
- References: several entries appear incomplete—standardize bibliographic details.  
- Originality: continuous-time acceleration is well studied; main novelty is generalized averaging with trajectory-adaptive weights, plus the energy functional and replicator instance—contrast more directly with Su–Boyd–Candès and Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan.  
- Empirical limitations: experiments only in R^3 for visualization; recommend higher-dimensional tests (e.g., simplex logistic regression, portfolio optimization, sparse learning, box constraints) and reporting wall-clock time, iterations, and sensitivity to β and s.  
- Ask for comparisons to (i) accelerated mirror descent with adaptive restarts, (ii) FISTA with restarts for proximal constraints, and (iii) mirror descent with AdaGrad-style step-size as a baseline.  
- Clarify relation to AdaGrad/Adam and whether adaptive averaging can complement coordinate-wise scaling or handle stochastic gradients.  
- Pros for acceptance: elegant unifying Lyapunov analysis, implementable heuristic with provable rate preservation, and interesting replicator application.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited large-scale empirical evidence, some opaque exposition with discrete-time guarantees in the supplement, and no demonstrated theoretical advantage over restarts in the strongly convex (linear-rate) regime.  
- Minor: clarify initialization at t0>0 vs t0=0 and discrete-time implications.  
- Minor: explain intuitive meaning of constant a(t) vs a(t)=βt and how that affects discrete algorithm behavior.  
- Minor: detail per-iteration computational cost and memory footprint of Algorithm 1 relative to restart methods.
