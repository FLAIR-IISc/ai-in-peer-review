This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization. The authors describe these dynamics as a coupling of a dual variable accumulating gradients at a rate η(t), and a primal variable obtained as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory, with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov argument, they establish sufficient conditions on η and w to achieve a desired convergence rate. As an example, they demonstrate that the replicator dynamics (a mirror descent method on the simplex) can be accelerated using a simple averaging scheme. The paper then proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic which adaptively computes the weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function. They provide guarantees on adaptive averaging in continuous-time, prove that it preserves the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods in discrete-time, and present numerical experiments comparing it with existing heuristics such as adaptive restarting. The experiments indicate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as adaptive restarting, with significant improvements observed in some cases.

I have limited expertise in this specific area. However, the core idea of leveraging adaptive averaging to potentially achieve a faster convergence rate for constrained convex optimization appears interesting and promising. 

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The Lyapunov argument provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method, and the convergence guarantees are a significant strength. The authors carefully consider both continuous and discrete-time settings. However, the theoretical results rely on specific conditions on η and w, and it would be helpful to have a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of satisfying these conditions. The connection to existing accelerated methods and the preservation of convergence rates are well-articulated.

**Novelty:** The approach of studying accelerated descent through the lens of adaptive averaging seems novel. While averaging is a known technique (as acknowledged in relation to [8]), the adaptive scheme and the associated theoretical guarantees represent a contribution. The extension to the replicator dynamics is also a noteworthy application. It's important to note, however, that the paper builds upon a substantial body of work in accelerated methods (e.g., [14, 15, 20, 11]) and the novelty lies in the specific combination and adaptation of existing techniques.

**Impact:** The potential impact of this work is considerable. If the adaptive averaging heuristic proves to be robust and effective in practice, it could provide a valuable tool for solving large-scale convex optimization problems, particularly in machine learning applications. The theoretical guarantees are also important, as they provide confidence in the reliability of the method. The demonstration of its efficacy on the replicator dynamics suggests broader applicability to problems arising in evolutionary game theory and related fields.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections are dense and require careful reading. The notation is standard, but the numerous equations and theoretical details can be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the field. Providing more intuition behind some of the mathematical steps would be beneficial. The discussion of related work is adequate, but could be expanded to include more recent developments in adaptive optimization methods, such as those mentioned in the introduction (e.g., Adagrad, Adam), and discuss their limitations in the context of accelerated methods. The connection to the work of Wibisono et al. [23] could also be elaborated on further. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation based on Lyapunov analysis.
*   Clear convergence guarantees in both continuous and discrete time.
*   Promising numerical results demonstrating competitive performance.
*   Novel application of adaptive averaging to accelerated descent dynamics.
*   Well-defined problem and clear presentation of the proposed solution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical conditions on η and w may be difficult to satisfy in practice.
*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work.
*   The clarity of some sections could be improved with more intuitive explanations.
*   The impact may be limited if the improvement over existing methods is marginal in many practical scenarios.
