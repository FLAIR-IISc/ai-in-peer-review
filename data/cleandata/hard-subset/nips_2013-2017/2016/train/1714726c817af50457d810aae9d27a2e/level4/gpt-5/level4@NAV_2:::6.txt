This paper proposes an adaptive averaging scheme to accelerate constrained convex optimization. The key contribution is a heuristic that adjusts averaging weights along the trajectory while provably preserving the baseline convergence rate even without strong convexity—an advantage over adaptive restarting heuristics. The paper supports this claim via a Lyapunov-based analysis and corroborates it empirically, showing competitive or improved performance against existing restart strategies.

I found parts of the manuscript hard to follow, as this is outside my primary area. I assume the terminology and abbreviations will be familiar to the intended audience, but adding brief background and intuition would help accessibility. In particular, the equations around lines 33–34 and 46–47 would benefit from a more intuitive explanation. I would also appreciate a clearer discussion of how the proposed adaptive averaging relates to adaptive methods used in non-convex optimization such as AdaGrad [1] and Adam [2]. Finally, I am uncertain about the practical impact given that the experiments are conducted in R^3, which is a small-scale setting. How does the approach (and the baselines) perform and scale in substantially higher dimensions? Minor comment: the reference to the solid line in Figure 1 is ambiguous—consider using a dotted line or distinct colors to depict the primal trajectory.

[1] Duchi, John, Elad Hazan, and Yoram Singer. "Adaptive subgradient methods for online learning and stochastic optimization." Journal of Machine Learning Research 12.Jul (2011): 2121–2159. [2] Kingma, Diederik, and Jimmy Ba. "Adam: A method for stochastic optimization." International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR). (2014).
