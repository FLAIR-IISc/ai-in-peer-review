- Paper builds on Krichene et al.'s ODE view of Nesterov to design an adaptive averaging rule that provably dominates fixed-schedule averaging and empirically speeds convergence.
- Clear, well-motivated, and easy-to-follow mathematical development, especially for readers familiar with Krichene et al.
- Simple adaptive reweighting rule with a clean domination guarantee over fixed-schedule averaging.
- Broad and convincing empirical evaluation showing consistent speed-ups across many objective classes.
- High practical relevance given widespread use of Nesterov’s acceleration in applications and baseline comparisons.
- Technical novelty is incremental and closely extends Krichene et al.; the paper should better delineate what is new beyond the domination guarantee and rule.
- Add a discrete-time analysis to show the discretized algorithm’s convergence rate matches continuous-time intuition for a more self-contained theoretical story.
- Discuss potential connections to Conjugate Gradient on strongly convex quadratics and consider empirical comparisons or interpretation in terms of polynomial acceleration/residual orthogonality.
- Clarify precisely in what sense the adaptive method “dominates” fixed averaging (per-iteration, running average, or fixed budget) and whether the guarantee holds uniformly for smooth convex and strongly convex settings.
- Include a brief discussion of computational overhead, implementation details for maintaining adaptive weights efficiently, and sensitivity to hyperparameters to aid practitioners.
- Ensure consistent referencing of Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) and clearly contrast their ODE framework with the new adaptive weighting contribution.
- If space allows, add ablation studies isolating the adaptive reweighting effect from other choices like step-size tuning.
- Overall: well-written, practically important contribution with modest theoretical novelty but strong empirical results; recommend acceptance.
