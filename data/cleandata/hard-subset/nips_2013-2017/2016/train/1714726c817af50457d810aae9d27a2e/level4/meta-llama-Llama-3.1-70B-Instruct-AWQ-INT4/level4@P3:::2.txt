This paper proposes a novel extension of the accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics by incorporating a generalized averaging scheme. The authors also introduce an adaptive averaging strategy for one of its discretizations, which shows promising results in some cases, outperforming existing AMD algorithms with restarting.

The paper's strengths lie in its ability to provide a unified framework for understanding the behavior of AMD dynamics with different averaging schemes. The use of a Lyapunov function to analyze the convergence of the dynamics is well-established in the literature, and the authors' extension to a more general averaging scheme is a natural next step. The primal representation of the dynamics and the reduction to replicator dynamics are also interesting contributions that promote a better understanding of the AMD algorithm.

However, the paper's originality and usefulness are concerns. Most of the elements, such as the Lyapunov function and discretization, directly follow from an early NIPS paper. The extension to generalized averaging is not particularly surprising, and the authors only consider the case with a quadratic rate. Faster rates would require higher-order methods, which are not always applicable.

The adaptive averaging strategy is an interesting contribution, but it is not fully theoretically grounded due to the approximation during discretization. The authors acknowledge that it is a heuristic, and there is no explicit convergent result provided. Furthermore, there are many different averaging schemes for Nesterov's accelerated algorithms, and it is unclear whether this adaptive variant makes a significant difference without comparing it to other alternatives.

The experimental results are limited to a toy example with quadratic and linear objectives, which is not convincing enough to demonstrate the advantage of adaptive averaging, especially for the general case with strongly convex functions. The authors should consider adding more examples, including higher-dimensional and non-quadratic objectives, to strengthen their claims.

In conclusion, while the paper has some interesting contributions, its originality and usefulness are concerns. The authors should address these concerns by providing more theoretical backing for their adaptive averaging strategy, comparing it to other alternatives, and demonstrating its effectiveness on a wider range of examples.
