Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization via continuous-time dynamics and their discretizations. Building on the averaging interpretation of accelerated mirror descent introduced by Krichene et al. [11], the authors consider a broad family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) ODEs parameterized by two positive, continuous functions: η(t), governing the rate of dual gradient accumulation, and w(t), defining a weighted averaging of the mirrored dual trajectory to produce the primal iterate. Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), they give sufficient conditions on (η, w, r) ensuring that Lr decreases and that f(X(t)) − f* ≤ C/r(t). This unifies and extends prior analyses of accelerated dynamics (e.g., Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan) and connects acceleration explicitly to generalized averaging, including the accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex.

The authors further propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts a(t) = w(t)/W(t) online to make the derivative of the Lyapunov function as negative as possible while preserving the rate guarantee. They also introduce a complementary decreasing “energy” function Er that captures potential-plus-kinetic energy. In discrete time (for r(t)=t^2), they present a practical algorithm that preserves the quadratic rate and compare it empirically to popular adaptive restarts (gradient and speed restarts). Experiments (notably on simplex-constrained problems) indicate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as restarting, sometimes substantially better.

Related work beyond the above includes the large literature on inertial/accelerated dynamics and Lyapunov analyses (e.g., Attouch, Peypouquet, and collaborators), and adaptive step-size methods such as Adagrad and Adam, which the authors correctly note target non-accelerated regimes.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The averaging view of acceleration is extended via a clean Lyapunov framework with explicit, verifiable sufficient conditions.
- Generality: The AMD family subsumes known ODEs and admits rates faster than 1/t^2 in continuous time by appropriate choices of r(t) and weights.
- New examples: An accelerated version of the replicator dynamics on the simplex is derived and interpreted.
- Adaptive averaging: The heuristic is principled (directly tied to Lyapunov decrease), simple, and empirically competitive with strong baselines.
- Additional insights: The energy function Er provides useful intuition and diagnostics complementary to Lr.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Discrete-time theory: While the continuous-time analysis is rigorous, the discrete-time guarantees are either limited or deferred. The main text would benefit from a clearer, self-contained statement (and sketch) that the proposed discretization preserves the O(1/k^2) rate, with precise assumptions on ψ, R, and step sizes. For p>2, only higher-order discretizations appear to preserve the rate; this limitation should be highlighted more prominently.
- Verifiability of conditions: Condition (2) in Theorem 2 is trajectory-dependent and not checkable a priori. Although the corollary provides a constructive sufficient condition (η = a r with a ≥ r′/r), more discussion on practical choices and sensitivity (including the role of t0 and scaling of w) would improve clarity.
- Scope of experiments: The empirical study is informative but small-scale (R^3 visualizations, simplex constraints). Including higher-dimensional benchmarks, non-simplex constraints, and sensitivity to ψ, R, and step sizes would strengthen claims of robustness.
- Comparison details: Please specify hyperparameter tuning and computational overhead for all methods (including the cost of evaluating/adjusting a(t) or ak) to ensure a fair comparison.
- Literature coverage: The connection to inertial gradient-like systems and vanishing viscosity (e.g., Attouch–Peypouquet–Redont) deserves explicit discussion and citation in the main text, given the shared Lyapunov/inertial structure and results on accelerated rates and restart strategies.

Quality
- The continuous-time analysis appears technically sound, with clear derivations of existence/uniqueness, Lyapunov decrease, and rate guarantees. The adaptive averaging rule is well-motivated by inequality (3) and preserves the prescribed rate when η ≥ r′ and a ≥ ηr.
- The discrete-time algorithm is plausible and aligns with known accelerated mirror descent schemes, but the theoretical treatment in the main text is minimal. Providing a theorem and proof outline (or pointing explicitly to the supplement) would strengthen the contribution.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. Some notation is heavy; a short “recipe” summarizing how to choose r, then a, w, η would aid readability.
- Minor typos to fix (e.g., “w,r,too 1/t^2” → “w.r.t. 1/t^2”; “authors continues” → “authors continue”). Clarify the reference to Krichene et al. [11] when discussing prior averaging-based acceleration.

Originality
- The generalized averaging framework and the adaptive averaging heuristic are novel and provide a fresh angle on designing accelerated dynamics beyond restarting.
- The accelerated replicator example is a nice, new instantiation with potential impact in game-theoretic and constrained optimization applications.

Significance
- The work advances our understanding of acceleration via averaging and may inform new algorithmic designs. If the discrete-time guarantees are fully developed and broadened (especially beyond quadratic rates and beyond the simplex), the impact could be substantial.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, unifying continuous-time analysis with clean Lyapunov guarantees.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with empirical gains and theoretical justification.
- Meaningful new examples (accelerated replicator) and insightful energy diagnostics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete discrete-time theory in the main paper; faster-than-1/t^2 rates currently realized only in continuous time or via higher-order discretizations.
- Limited empirical scope and insufficient details on tuning/overheads.

Overall, this is an interesting and carefully executed paper. With clearer discrete-time guarantees and a slightly expanded empirical evaluation, it would be a strong contribution. I recommend citing and discussing related inertial dynamics work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors to better position the paper within the literature.
