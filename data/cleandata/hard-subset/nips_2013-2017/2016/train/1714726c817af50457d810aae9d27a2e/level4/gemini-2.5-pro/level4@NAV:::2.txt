This paper builds upon prior work connecting accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) with continuous-time Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs). The authors propose an extension of the ODE dynamics using a generalized averaging scheme and introduce a novel adaptive averaging strategy for its discretization. The resulting AMD variant demonstrates superior performance over existing AMD algorithms with restarting in some test cases. The adaptive averaging approach is notably novel and well-motivated. However, despite the paper's overall quality, significant concerns remain about its originality and practical utility.

-   Much of the technical framework, such as the Lyapunov function and discretization method, is directly inherited from a previous NIPS paper [7]. The extension from a simple to a generalized averaging scheme is a relatively minor contribution, and its usefulness is questionable since the authors' analysis is confined to the quadratic rate. Achieving faster rates would demand higher-order methods, which are not broadly applicable.
-   While some results—including the energy function interpretation, the primal form reformulation, and the reduction to replicator dynamics—contribute to a deeper understanding of the AMD algorithm, they do not represent a major advance for the machine learning field.
-   The adaptive averaging strategy is motivated by the conditions required for the ODE's convergence, but it lacks a complete theoretical grounding due to approximations introduced during discretization. Consequently, no explicit convergence result is provided, rendering the method a heuristic, as the authors themselves note. Furthermore, numerous alternative averaging schemes for Nesterov's accelerated algorithms exist, and without a comparative analysis, it is difficult to determine if this adaptive variant offers a significant advantage.
-   The experimental evaluation, conducted on a toy example with only a quadratic/linear objective, is not sufficiently compelling to demonstrate the advantages of adaptive averaging, especially for the general case of strongly convex functions.

== Post-Rebuttal Comments ==
I have reviewed the authors' rebuttal. They have committed to incorporating the following in a revised manuscript:
-   Additional experiments on higher-dimensional and non-quadratic examples.
-   A formal theoretical proof of convergence for the adaptive averaging scheme.
-   An implementation of adaptive averaging for Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method.
-   A discussion comparing their method to other adaptive averaging strategies.
