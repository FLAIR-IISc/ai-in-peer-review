This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, building on prior work connecting these dynamics to continuous-time ODEs. The authors generalize a previous ODE formulation with a broader averaging scheme and propose a novel adaptive averaging heuristic for its discretization. They provide sufficient conditions for convergence in continuous time and demonstrate, through numerical experiments on simple problems, that their adaptive averaging approach performs at least as well as, and sometimes better than, existing adaptive restarting heuristics.

The core idea of adaptively adjusting the averaging weights based on the rate of decrease of a Lyapunov function is intuitively appealing and represents a logical extension of previous work on acceleration and averaging. The authors clearly articulate the connection between their work and earlier results, particularly referencing their own NIPS 2016 paper [11]. The theoretical analysis, establishing conditions for convergence based on the Lyapunov function, is a solid contribution. The exploration of the replicator dynamics as a special case and the formulation in primal form add to the understanding of the algorithm.

However, the paper’s originality and significance are somewhat limited. While the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, its direct link to prior work (particularly [11]) and the relatively incremental nature of the generalization raise concerns. The authors acknowledge that many elements follow directly from their previous paper. The focus on quadratic convergence rates restricts the potential impact, as faster rates often necessitate higher-order methods. The initial experiments, while demonstrating competitive performance, are limited in scope and do not convincingly showcase a substantial advantage over existing techniques, particularly in more complex scenarios. The discretization of the heuristic lacks a fully developed theoretical justification and, as the authors concede, remains a heuristic in practice. Moreover, the landscape of adaptive methods for accelerated algorithms is already populated, and a thorough comparison to other alternatives would strengthen the contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and well-motivated problem statement, building on established connections between accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics.
*   Intuitive and novel adaptive averaging heuristic with a sound theoretical basis in Lyapunov stability.
*   Solid theoretical analysis establishing sufficient conditions for convergence.
*   Demonstration of competitive performance compared to existing heuristics on tested examples.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited originality, with many components directly stemming from the authors' previous work [11].
*   Focus on quadratic convergence restricts broader applicability and potential impact.
*   Initial experimental evaluation lacks robustness, using only simple quadratic and linear objective functions.
*   Discretization of the heuristic lacks a complete theoretical guarantee of convergence.
*   Insufficient comparison to other existing adaptive averaging schemes.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a logically sound extension of existing work on accelerated methods.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution with intuitive appeal and demonstrable performance.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed dynamics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The limited originality and incremental nature of the contribution may not meet the high bar for NIPS.
*   The lack of a strong experimental validation with more complex problems weakens the claim of practical significance.
*   The heuristic nature of the discretization and lack of comparison to other adaptive schemes leave room for improvement.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile, but ultimately incremental, contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The authors have built logically on their previous work and propose a sensible adaptive averaging heuristic. However, the limited originality, scope of experimental validation, and the heuristic nature of the discretization currently prevent it from being a standout submission. I believe the paper could be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by expanding the experimental evaluation and providing a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the discretized heuristic.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. I would be willing to change this to Accept if the authors address the concerns raised in the rebuttal and provide the promised additions (higher dimensional examples, theoretical convergence proof, comparison to other methods). The authors' commitment to these improvements in the rebuttal is encouraging.
