Building on earlier work that ties accelerated mirror descent to continuous-time ODEs, the paper generalizes the accelerated dynamics via a broader averaging scheme and introduces an adaptive averaging strategy for one of its discretizations. In several cases, the AMD variant with adaptive averaging compares favorably to AMD with restarts. The adaptive averaging idea is fresh and motivated by a clear intuition. Despite the generally strong presentation, I remain concerned about originality and practical impact.

- Many components (e.g., the Lyapunov construction, discretization, etc.) are derived directly from prior NIPS work [7]. The move from a simple to a generalized averaging schedule feels largely straightforward and of limited utility, since the analysis and experiments ultimately focus on the quadratic rate; obtaining faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which are not always practical.
- Some contributions—such as the energy function interpretation, the primal reformulation, and the connection to replicator dynamics—do improve conceptual understanding of accelerated mirror descent, but they do not seem sufficiently substantial to shift the state of the art in machine learning.
- Although adaptive averaging is designed to enforce the sufficient conditions ensuring ODE convergence, its theoretical footing is weakened by discretization approximations. No explicit convergence guarantees are provided, so it remains a heuristic (as the authors acknowledge). Moreover, there are several alternative averaging schemes for Nesterov-type accelerations; without head-to-head comparisons with these alternatives, it is hard to assess whether the proposed variant offers a meaningful advantage.
- The experimental study uses toy problems with quadratic/linear objectives, which is not compelling evidence of the method’s benefits, especially for more general settings and in the strongly convex regime.

I have read the authors’ response. They commit to include (i) additional higher-dimensional and non-quadratic experiments, (ii) a theoretical convergence proof for adaptive averaging, (iii) an implementation of adaptive averaging within Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method, and (iv) a discussion comparing to other adaptive averaging strategies. I have adjusted my scores accordingly.
