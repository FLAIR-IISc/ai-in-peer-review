This paper introduces a generalized framework for accelerated mirror descent dynamics in continuous time, characterized by two functions: one for gradient accumulation (`η`) and another for averaging (`w`). Using a Lyapunov function, the authors derive sufficient conditions on these functions to guarantee a specific convergence rate. The main contribution is a novel adaptive averaging heuristic, which adaptively sets the averaging weights to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function. A key theoretical result is that this heuristic preserves the `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate, a guarantee that is not established for existing restarting heuristics in the non-strongly convex setting. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through numerical experiments on low-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness and Guarantees (Quality, Originality):** The primary strength of this work is its strong theoretical foundation. The use of a Lyapunov function to both analyze a general class of accelerated dynamics and to derive a new adaptive heuristic is elegant. The resulting guarantee that the adaptive method preserves the optimal convergence rate for non-strongly convex functions is a significant advantage over popular restarting heuristics, which are often motivated by intuition and lack such guarantees.

2.  **Novel Heuristic (Originality, Significance):** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel and intuitive idea. Rather than restarting the dynamics when progress stalls, it adjusts the averaging scheme to emphasize parts of the trajectory that are making progress. This provides a new perspective on how to empirically improve the performance of accelerated methods.

3.  **General Framework (Clarity, Significance):** The paper presents a unified framework (AMDw,η) that encompasses and generalizes previous continuous-time dynamics for accelerated methods. The demonstration of how this framework can be used to derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics is a compelling example of its utility and provides new insights into connections between different fields.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Intuition:** While the paper is technically sound, its accessibility could be improved for a broader NIPS audience. The core dynamics in Equation (1) and the structure of the Lyapunov function in Equation (2) are introduced with limited intuition. A more detailed explanation of why this specific coupling of primal-dual variables is chosen, and what the physical or geometric interpretation of the Lyapunov function and its components is, would help the reader better appreciate the technical contributions.

2.  **Discussion of Related Adaptive Methods:** The paper briefly mentions adaptive learning rate methods like Adagrad and Adam but dismisses them as "not designed for accelerated methods." This discussion is too brief and misses an opportunity to situate the work more broadly. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison:
    *   How does adaptive averaging, which modifies the "memory" of the algorithm through `w(t)`, conceptually differ from methods that adapt the step-size based on gradient statistics?
    *   What are the potential trade-offs? For instance, are Adagrad-style methods better suited for sparse problems, while adaptive averaging is more suited for dense, non-strongly convex problems?
    *   Could these approaches be complementary or even combined?

3.  **Experimental Validation and Significance:** The practical significance of the method is not fully established by the experiments. The evaluations are performed in `R^3`, which serves as a good proof-of-concept for visualization but does not demonstrate how the method scales to the high-dimensional problems typically encountered in machine learning. To make a more compelling case for the method's impact, I would encourage the authors to include experiments on higher-dimensional, standard benchmark problems (e.g., logistic regression or SVM on a real-world dataset). This would provide crucial evidence of whether the observed performance gains over restarting heuristics hold in more practical settings.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In Figure 1, the caption refers to a "solid line" showing the trajectory of the primal variable `X(t)`. However, the figure contains multiple solid lines. It would be clearer to use distinct line styles (e.g., dotted, dashed) or colors to differentiate the trajectories of `∇ψ∗(Z(τ))` and `X(t)`.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a novel and theoretically well-founded contribution to the literature on accelerated optimization. The main weakness is the limited experimental validation, which makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed heuristic for the machine learning community. A more extensive experimental section and a deeper discussion of related adaptive methods would make this a much stronger paper.
