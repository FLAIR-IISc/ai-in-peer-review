- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated constrained convex optimization that preserves original convergence rates, even without strong convexity.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees supported by a Lyapunov analysis and convergence proofs.  
- Empirical results corroborate the theoretical claims and show the heuristic’s effectiveness versus existing methods.  
- The paper is technically dense and may be hard to follow for readers outside the subfield; adding introductory background would help.  
- Equations on lines 33–34 and 46–47 need more intuitive explanations to improve understanding.  
- The connection between this adaptive averaging approach and adaptive methods for non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) is not explored and would be valuable.  
- Practical significance is unclear because experiments are limited to low-dimensional (R^3) settings; scalability to high dimensions is not demonstrated.  
- Figure 1’s legend/line style is ambiguous—distinguish the primal trajectory with a dotted line or different colors.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical framework with clear mathematical notation enabling reproducibility.  
- Strength: novelty in addressing limitations of existing restarting heuristics.  
- Strength: empirical validation supports claims of improved performance.  
- Weakness: accessibility issues due to specialized terminology and technical depth.  
- Weakness: limited experimental scale raises questions about real-world applicability.  
- Weakness: missing comparisons or discussion linking to adaptive non-convex optimization methods.  
- Argument for acceptance: technically sound, novel, and empirically validated contribution with meaningful theoretical guarantees.  
- Argument against acceptance: insufficient demonstration of scalability and missing exploration of potential synergies with non-convex adaptive methods.  
- Recommendation: accept, but address scalability experiments and connections to non-convex adaptive methods in revisions.
