- Extends Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) by introducing a principled adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates while empirically outperforming existing methods.  
- Generalizes continuous-time accelerated mirror descent to a family of dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t).  
- Provides a Lyapunov-based sufficient condition (Theorem 2) guaranteeing convergence at rate 1/r(t).  
- Corollary 1 gives a constructive way to choose valid weight functions; adaptive scheme dynamically adjusts w(t) to minimize the Lyapunov derivative.  
- Adaptive heuristic guarantees rate preservation, unlike common restart-based approaches.  
- Continuous-time analysis is rigorous and clearly presented; primal representation and examples (e.g., simplex/replicator dynamics) strengthen theory-practice link.  
- Algorithm 1 (discrete adaptation) is well-motivated and validated empirically across multiple problem classes.  
- Experiments comprehensively compare to restarts, showing consistent superiority and 1.5–3× speedups in key cases.  
- Empirical results confirm Lyapunov function monotonicity and the energy-function analysis (Theorem 3) provides mechanistic insight.  
- Main weakness: discrete-time convergence proof is absent from the main text; the claim of O(1/k^2) for Algorithm 1 should be stated and proved explicitly.  
- Secondary weakness: limited discussion of broader connections, notably to Conjugate Gradient methods for strongly convex quadratics.  
- Recommendation: strong paper with high significance; accept conditional on adding the discrete-time convergence result to the main text.  
- Pros summarized: novel rate-preserving adaptive heuristic, robust empirical gains, clear theoretical framework, high practical relevance.  
- Cons summarized: missing discrete convergence proof (critical), sparse discussion of CG connections (minor).
