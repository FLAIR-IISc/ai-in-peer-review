This paper presents an adaptive averaging method for accelerating constrained convex optimization. A key contribution is the theoretical guarantee that the method preserves the baseline convergence rate when the objective function is not strongly convex. The authors establish this as a significant advantage over existing restarting heuristics, supporting their claims with both theoretical analysis and experimental results.

While the paper is likely accessible to experts in this specific subfield, I found it difficult to follow due to a lack of background and context. The paper's accessibility could be significantly improved for a broader audience by including more introductory material. The notation and terminology may be standard for specialists, but a brief primer would be beneficial. In particular, the derivations around lines 33-34 and 46-47 would benefit from a more intuitive explanation to clarify the underlying mechanics of the proposed method.

Furthermore, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of its relationship to other adaptive methods, particularly those prevalent in non-convex optimization like Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]. While the problem setting is different, these methods also employ adaptive averaging principles. Clarifying the connections or key differences would help place this work in a broader context and highlight its unique contributions.

A final concern relates to the practical significance of this work. The experiments are conducted in R^3, which represents a low-dimensional, proof-of-concept setting. To establish the method's utility, it is crucial to understand its performance on more realistic problems. The authors should provide evidence or discussion on how the proposed method, as well as related approaches, is expected to scale to the high-dimensional problems commonly found in machine learning.
