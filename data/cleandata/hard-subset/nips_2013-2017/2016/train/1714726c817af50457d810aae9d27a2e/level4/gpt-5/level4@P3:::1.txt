Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies a broad family of accelerated mirror descent dynamics in continuous time, parameterized by two positive weight functions: η(t), the rate at which a dual variable aggregates gradients, and w(t), the averaging weights used to produce the primal iterate as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory. Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f⋆) + Dψ∗(Z, z⋆), the authors give simple sufficient conditions on η and w under which Lr is nonincreasing, yielding convergence f(X(t)) − f⋆ ≤ O(1/r(t)). This unifies and extends the ODE perspective on acceleration initiated by Su, Boyd, and Candès (NIPS 2014), the mirror-descent ODE viewpoint and accelerated mirror descent of Krichene, Bayen, and Bartlett (NIPS 2015), and the variational formulation of Wibisono, Wilson, and Jordan (2016).

The paper then proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts the effective weight ratio a(t) = w(t)/W(t) along trajectories to further decrease d/dt Lr whenever possible, while preserving the rate implied by the Lyapunov analysis. The authors also provide a second “energy” function that is guaranteed to decrease under the same conditions. In the primal representation, the paper derives concrete dynamics for common constraint sets, including a particularly nice accelerated version of the replicator dynamics on the simplex. A discrete-time algorithm for the quadratic rate r(t) = t^2 is proposed, and experiments compare the adaptive averaging rule to popular adaptive restart heuristics (gradient and speed restarts). Empirically, adaptive averaging performs at least as well as restarts and can yield significant improvements, especially for strongly convex problems.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The Lyapunov analysis is clean and transparent. The main condition η(t) ≥ r′(t) together with the trajectory-dependent term ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w) ≤ 0 yields a clear, verifiable path to a desired rate. The corollary that sets η = w r/W and w/W ≥ r′/r provides a constructive recipe for schedules achieving O(1/r(t)). The existence/uniqueness discussion is careful about initialization at t0 > 0.
- Heuristic and guarantees: The adaptive rule increases a(t) when doing so decreases d/dt Lr, thereby guaranteeing that the Lyapunov-based rate is preserved and potentially improving the constant via a faster decrease of Lr. The second energy function offers additional insight into the dynamics.
- Discrete-time: A discrete algorithm is given for r(t) = t^2, with a proof sketch relegated to the supplement. Given the practical importance of discrete-time guarantees, it would strengthen the paper to present the full proof that the O(1/k^2) rate is preserved under the adaptive rule in the main text, and to clarify assumptions (step size, smoothness constants, and mirror map regularity).
- Experiments: The experiments convincingly illustrate the qualitative behavior in R^3 and show favorable performance relative to restart strategies. Larger-scale tests and broader tasks (e.g., constrained logistic regression or regularized problems) would further support significance.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. The Lyapunov argument is easy to follow, especially for readers familiar with the ODE view of acceleration. Suggestions for clarity:
- Make the role of a(t) = w/W central earlier, as it streamlines the conditions and proofs.
- Clearly state in the main text what is proved about the discrete algorithm (rate, constants, and conditions) and what is deferred to the supplement.
- Provide guidance on choosing β, step size s, and t0 in practice, and discuss sensitivity.
- Minor notation/typo cleanups (e.g., ensure “adaptive” is consistently spelled; unify r, w, η definitions across sections).

Originality
The work is an elegant extension of prior ODE analyses of acceleration. The generalized averaging framework, the trajectory-aware adaptive weighting that provably preserves the Lyapunov-based rate, and the accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex are novel and useful contributions. While the techniques build on established continuous-time analyses, the adaptive averaging rule is conceptually simple, implementable, and distinct from adaptive restarts.

Significance
Acceleration is pervasive in optimization and machine learning. A principled, low-overhead heuristic that can only help (with respect to the Lyapunov decrease) and often improves over adaptive restarts is valuable. If the discrete-time guarantees are made more prominent and the empirical scope broadened, the impact could be substantial.

Pros
- Clear Lyapunov framework yielding simple, general conditions for accelerated rates with generalized averaging.
- Adaptive averaging rule with a guarantee that it preserves the base rate and can strictly decrease the Lyapunov function faster.
- Primal representations and concrete examples; accelerated replicator dynamics are of independent interest.
- Empirical results show consistent gains over adaptive restarts on several objectives.
- Conceptually simple and likely easy to adopt in practice.

Cons
- Discrete-time theory is largely in the supplement; bringing the main guarantees into the paper would strengthen the contribution.
- Experimental scope is modest (low-dimensional, deterministic); larger-scale and stochastic settings are not explored.
- Guidance on hyperparameters (β, step size) and robustness is limited.
- The trajectory-dependent condition in Theorem 2 is not directly checkable; the paper relies on the adaptive rule to enforce it—this is fine in practice but worth discussing more explicitly.

Suggestions for improvement
- Move the discrete-time convergence proof (preservation of O(1/k^2) under the adaptive rule) into the main text and state precise constants and assumptions.
- Add higher-dimensional experiments and tasks closer to typical NeurIPS applications; include wall-clock and iteration counts, and sensitivity to β and step size.
- Discuss stochastic gradients and composite objectives; does adaptive averaging play well with variance, and can it be combined with proximal operators beyond the current setting?
- Clarify the dominance guarantee: formally compare the derivative of Lr under adaptive a(t) versus the baseline a(t) = η(t)r(t) to make the improvement explicit.
- Explore connections to conjugate gradient on strongly convex quadratics: does adaptive averaging implicitly generate CG-like directions in continuous or discrete time?
- Provide brief implementation guidance for mirror maps and the choice of ψ∗ on common constraint sets.

Overall assessment
This is a clear, technically sound, and practically relevant contribution. The generalized averaging viewpoint and the adaptive rule are simple yet powerful, with convincing theoretical underpinnings and promising empirical behavior. I recommend acceptance, contingent on strengthening the discrete-time presentation in the main text and modestly broadening the empirical evaluation.
