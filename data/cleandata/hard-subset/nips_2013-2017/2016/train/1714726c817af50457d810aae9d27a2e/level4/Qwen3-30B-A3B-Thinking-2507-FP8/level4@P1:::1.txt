

This paper extends the continuous-time perspective of Nesterov's acceleration developed by Krichene et al. [11] at NIPS 2015 to propose a theoretically grounded adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated mirror descent methods. The authors formulate a generalized family of accelerated mirror descent dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), and establish sufficient conditions for achieving desired convergence rates using Lyapunov analysis. They then introduce an adaptive averaging strategy that dynamically computes weights to accelerate convergence while preserving the quadratic convergence rate. The empirical evaluation demonstrates consistent improvements over existing heuristics, including adaptive restarting, across a range of convex optimization problems on the simplex, including strongly convex, weakly convex, and linear objectives.

The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a theoretically justified approach to adaptively improving Nesterov's acceleration without sacrificing convergence guarantees. The authors clearly establish how their adaptive averaging method outperforms fixed-schedule approaches while maintaining the same convergence rate, addressing an important gap in the literature where many empirical comparisons use Nesterov's method with fixed weights rather than adaptive versions. The connection to the replicator dynamics on the simplex is particularly valuable, as it provides a concrete application in evolutionary game theory and demonstrates the method's utility for well-known optimization dynamics.

Key strengths of the paper include:
1. A theoretically sound adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance
2. Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing consistent improvements across multiple problem types
3. Clear exposition of the continuous-time dynamics and their relationship to discrete optimization
4. Insightful application to the replicator dynamics, extending previous work on mirror descent

The technical development is elegant and well-structured, with the Lyapunov analysis providing a unified framework for understanding the convergence properties of the generalized averaging scheme. The paper successfully demonstrates how the adaptive averaging heuristic can be implemented in discrete time while maintaining theoretical guarantees, as evidenced by the numerical experiments showing the Lyapunov function remains decreasing.

A minor weakness is that while the continuous-time analysis is thorough, the discrete-time convergence guarantees are not fully developed in the main paper (though referenced in the supplementary material). A more detailed analysis of the discretized process would strengthen the technical contribution, though the authors note this should be straightforward given their continuous-time framework.

I would also be interested to see whether the adaptive averaging principle could be extended to higher-order accelerated methods, such as those proposed by Wibisono et al. [23], which achieve faster convergence rates for strongly convex functions.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to optimization theory with significant practical implications. The adaptive averaging heuristic is both theoretically well-founded and empirically effective, addressing an important practical limitation of Nesterov's acceleration. Given the widespread use of accelerated methods in machine learning, this work has substantial potential impact. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a simple yet powerful technique to improve the performance of accelerated optimization methods.
