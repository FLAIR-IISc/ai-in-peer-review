This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization through a continuous-time primal–dual viewpoint. Building on the work of Krichene et al. [7], where Nesterov’s method arises as a discretization of a coupled ODE system, the authors consider a broader family of averaging schemes in which the primal trajectory is formed by averaging the mirror image of the dual variable. They derive convergence rates for these generalized averages under conditions on the weighting coefficients (Theorem 2) and show, in continuous time, that certain choices yield rates faster than 1/t^2. They also propose an adaptive rule for selecting weights with theoretical guarantees and validate it empirically, including comparisons with restart heuristics.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and a coherent extension of the primal–dual continuous-time framework for acceleration.
- A rigorous analysis of generalized averaging schemes that goes beyond standard Nesterov-type constructions.
- The main theoretical result is noteworthy: it identifies conditions under which continuous-time trajectories can converge faster than 1/t^2, a benchmark rate in many accelerated settings.
- The adaptive weighting scheme is well motivated and accompanied by empirical evaluation, including comparisons to practical restart strategies.

Weaknesses and limitations
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the continuous-time dynamics; no convergence guarantees are provided for the discretized algorithm. Given that practical methods are discrete, this is a significant gap. It remains unclear whether the faster-than-1/t^2 behavior survives discretization, under what step-size or regularity conditions, or whether it degrades to O(1/k^2).
- The paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of how the improved continuous-time rate avoids conflict with known oracle lower bounds for first-order methods. Clarifying the role of constraints, mirror geometry, time scaling, or potential functions in enabling faster rates in continuous time would preempt confusion.
- The conditions on the averaging/weight coefficients that yield improved rates could be made more transparent. In particular, it would help to spell out practical, verifiable sufficient conditions and to discuss trade-offs (e.g., stability vs. speed, sensitivity to problem geometry).
- The relationship to the broader literature on inertial and accelerated ODEs could be expanded. In particular, related contributions by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors on inertial dynamics, Lyapunov function constructions, and rates for continuous-time systems appear closely connected and should be explicitly discussed.
- The empirical section is useful but cannot compensate for the missing discrete-time theory. It would help to include ablation results that isolate the effect of the new averaging scheme versus restarts, as well as sensitivity analyses for the adaptive weights.

Questions and requests for clarification
- What are the minimal regularity assumptions on the objective and feasible set (e.g., smoothness, Lipschitz gradients, strong convexity, mirror map properties) required for the main theorem? Are there examples beyond smooth objectives where the improved rates hold?
- Can the authors provide an explicit example of a weight sequence satisfying their conditions and outline a discretization that is numerically stable? Are there step-size or momentum choices that provably prevent oscillations or divergence?
- How does the adaptive weighting scheme behave under noisy or stochastic gradients? Is there a robustness analysis or empirical evidence in that setting?
- For constrained problems, how is feasibility enforced along the trajectory in both continuous and discrete time (projection, Bregman proximal steps), and does this affect the attainable rate?
- Is the faster-than-1/t^2 rate tight in the proposed framework? If not, what are the conjectured limits?

Presentation and minor comments
- The exposition is generally clear and well written. A brief, high-level proof sketch for Theorem 2 (e.g., the Lyapunov function and key inequalities) would aid readability.
- Please correct typos and grammar (e.g., “authors continues” → “authors continue,” “initiated in by” → “initiated by,” “w,r,too 1/t^2” → “with respect to 1/t^2”).
- Clearly define the averaging weights, indexing, and any normalization factors early in the text, and restate the assumptions in a self-contained manner.
- Add a short related-work subsection situating the contribution relative to Attouch–Peypouquet and related inertial ODE analyses.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and technically solid paper that advances the continuous-time analysis of accelerated methods through a novel and well-justified generalization of averaging schemes. The main theorem is significant and the adaptive weighting strategy is practically relevant. However, the absence of a discrete-time theoretical analysis is a substantial limitation for a venue focused on algorithms implemented in practice. I would view the paper favorably if the authors can (i) add a principled discretization with partial guarantees (e.g., conditions under which the improved rate is preserved or nearly preserved), or at least (ii) provide a thorough discussion clarifying why the continuous-time gains may or may not transfer, together with stronger empirical evidence. Subject to these points and the suggested literature connections, I lean toward acceptance.
