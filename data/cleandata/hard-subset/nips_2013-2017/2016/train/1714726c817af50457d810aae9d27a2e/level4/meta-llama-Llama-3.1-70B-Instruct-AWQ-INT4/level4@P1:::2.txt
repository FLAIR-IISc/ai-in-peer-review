The authors extend the connection between accelerated Mirror Descent and continuous-time ODE dynamics with a more general averaging scheme and propose a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one of its discretizations. They demonstrate that this new variant of AMD with adaptive averaging outperforms existing AMD algorithms with restarting in some cases. The proposed adaptive averaging strategy is interesting and intuitive.

The paper builds upon an early work that connects accelerated Mirror Descent and continuous-time ODE. However, the originality and usefulness of the paper remain a concern. Most elements, such as the Lyapunov function and discretization, directly follow from an early NIPS paper [7]. The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging is not particularly significant, as the authors only consider the case with a quadratic rate. Faster rates would require higher-order methods, which are not always applicable.

Some results, such as the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation into primal form, and reduction to replicator dynamics, promote a better understanding of the accelerated Mirror Descent algorithm. However, these contributions are not crucial to advancing machine learning.

The adaptive averaging strategy is introduced to satisfy the conditions sufficient to guarantee convergence of the ODE. However, it is not fully theoretically grounded due to the approximation during discretization. Unfortunately, there is no explicit convergent result provided, making it a heuristic, as referred to by the authors. Moreover, there exist various averaging schemes for Nesterov's accelerated algorithms, and without comparing to other alternatives, it is unclear whether this adaptive variant makes a significant difference.

The experiment on a toy example with only quadratic/linear objectives is not convincing enough to demonstrate the advantage of adaptive averaging, especially for the general case with strongly convex functions. To strengthen the paper, the authors should provide additional higher-dimensional and non-quadratic examples, a theoretical proof of convergence of adaptive averaging, and a discussion comparing their adaptive averaging to other alternatives.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel adaptive averaging strategy for accelerated Mirror Descent.
* The authors demonstrate that their new variant of AMD with adaptive averaging outperforms existing AMD algorithms with restarting in some cases.

* Most elements directly follow from an early NIPS paper [7].
* The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging is not particularly significant.
* The adaptive averaging strategy is not fully theoretically grounded.
* There is no explicit convergent result provided.
* The experiment is not convincing enough to demonstrate the advantage of adaptive averaging.
