The paper builds on earlier work that connects accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) with continuous-time ODEs. It extends the ODE dynamics via a more general averaging scheme and proposes a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one of its discretizations. In some cases, the new AMD variant with adaptive averaging outperforms existing AMD algorithms with restarts. The adaptive averaging idea is genuinely new and comes with a clear intuition. While the paper is well presented overall, I have significant concerns about originality and practical usefulness.

- Many core elements (e.g., the Lyapunov function, discretization approach, etc.) follow directly from the earlier NIPS paper [7]. The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging seems largely trivial and of limited value, since the paper ultimately focuses on the quadratic-rate case; achieving faster rates would require higher-order methods, which are not always applicable.

- Some results—such as the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation into a primal form, and reduction to replicator dynamics—do improve conceptual understanding of AMD. However, these insights do not represent significant advances for machine learning practice.

- Although the adaptive averaging is introduced to meet sufficient conditions for ODE convergence, the discretization introduces approximations that weaken the theoretical grounding. No explicit convergence guarantee is provided; by the authors’ own characterization, the method remains heuristic. In addition, there exist numerous alternative averaging schemes for Nesterov-type accelerated methods. Without empirical or theoretical comparisons to these alternatives, it is unclear whether this adaptive variant provides a meaningful advantage.

- The experimental evaluation is limited to a toy example with quadratic/linear objectives and does not convincingly demonstrate the benefits of adaptive averaging, especially in the general strongly convex setting.

Post-rebuttal: I have read the authors’ response. They commit to adding (i) additional higher-dimensional and non-quadratic experiments, (ii) a theoretical proof of convergence for the adaptive averaging, (iii) an implementation of adaptive averaging within Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method, and (iv) a discussion comparing to other adaptive averaging schemes. I have updated my scores accordingly.
