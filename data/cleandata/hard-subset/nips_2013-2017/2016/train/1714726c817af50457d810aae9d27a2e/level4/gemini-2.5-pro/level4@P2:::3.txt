This paper investigates continuous-time accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization. It extends the work of Krichene et al. (2015), which established a connection between Nesterov's accelerated method and a specific ordinary differential equation (ODE) system. In that framework, the primal trajectory is an average of the mirrored dual trajectory. The central contribution of this paper is to generalize this concept by studying a broader family of "Accelerated Mirror Descent" (AMD) dynamics parameterized by arbitrary weight functions for averaging (`w(t)`) and gradient accumulation (`η(t)`).

Using a Lyapunov function, the authors derive sufficient conditions on these weight functions to guarantee a desired convergence rate of O(1/r(t)). A key theoretical result (Theorem 2) demonstrates that specific choices of `w` and `η` can yield convergence rates faster than the standard O(1/t^2). Building on this analysis, the paper introduces a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. This heuristic dynamically adjusts the averaging weights to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function, thereby preserving the theoretical convergence rate while improving empirical performance. The proposed method is compared numerically against common adaptive restart heuristics, showing favorable results.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous and unified analysis of a general class of accelerated dynamics. The Lyapunov-based framework is elegant and provides clear, interpretable conditions for achieving a given convergence rate. The finding that different averaging schemes can yield rates faster than O(1/t^2) is a novel and important contribution that deepens the community's understanding of acceleration.

2.  **Novel and Well-Guaranteed Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a direct and clever consequence of the continuous-time analysis. A major strength is that, unlike many restart heuristics whose theoretical properties are not fully understood (especially for non-strongly convex problems), this method is guaranteed by construction to preserve the O(1/r(t)) convergence rate.

3.  **Clarity and Insight:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and effectively builds intuition. The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained problems is a particularly insightful example that connects the abstract framework to a well-known dynamical system in evolutionary game theory.

4.  **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments are well-designed and effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of adaptive averaging. The comparison against established restarting techniques shows that the proposed method performs competitively and, in some cases, significantly better, particularly for strongly convex objectives. The visualizations of the Lyapunov function and energy function provide valuable insight into the behavior of the different heuristics.

**Weaknesses (Areas for improvement):**

1.  **Gap between Continuous and Discrete Analysis:** The theoretical analysis, especially regarding rates faster than O(1/t^2), is performed exclusively in the continuous-time setting. While a discrete algorithm is presented, its theoretical analysis is deferred to the supplementary material and does not fully address how these faster rates can be achieved in practice. The paper notes that discretizing these dynamics might lead to higher-order methods, but a more detailed discussion on the practical implications and algorithmic realization of these faster rates would significantly strengthen the paper's impact.

2.  **Discussion of Related Work:** While the paper is well-situated, the connection to the extensive body of work on inertial dynamics by Attouch, Peypouquet, and collaborators could be strengthened. Their work also uses Lyapunov analysis for continuous-time ODEs to understand and design accelerated optimization algorithms. A more detailed comparison of the methodologies and results would provide valuable context for the paper's contributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution to the literature on accelerated optimization methods. The generalization of the averaging scheme and the development of an adaptive heuristic with theoretical guarantees are valuable. The main weakness is the limited discussion on translating the faster continuous-time rates into practical discrete-time algorithms. Despite this, the paper's strengths in theoretical novelty, clarity, and empirical results make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
