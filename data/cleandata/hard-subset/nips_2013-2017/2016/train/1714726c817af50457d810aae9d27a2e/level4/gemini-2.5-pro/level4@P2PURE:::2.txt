Building upon prior work connecting accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ODEs, this paper extends the ODE dynamics using a more general averaging scheme. The authors then propose a novel adaptive averaging strategy for a discretization of this extended ODE. The resulting AMD variant, equipped with this adaptive averaging, is shown to outperform existing AMD algorithms with restarting in certain scenarios. The proposed adaptive averaging strategy is novel and its underlying intuition is a key strength.

Despite being well-presented, the paper's originality and practical utility are significant concerns:
- A significant portion of the technical machinery, including the Lyapunov function and discretization approach, is directly adopted from the prior NIPS paper [7]. The primary theoretical contribution—the extension from simple to generalized averaging—appears incremental. Its practical utility is also questionable, as the authors ultimately focus only on the case with a quadratic rate. Achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which have limited applicability.
- Several results, such as the interpretation of the energy function, the reformulation into a primal form, and the reduction to replicator dynamics, offer valuable insights into the accelerated Mirror Descent algorithm. However, these contributions are primarily of theoretical interest and may not represent a significant advance for the broader machine learning community.
- The adaptive averaging scheme is motivated by the need to satisfy conditions for the convergence of the continuous-time ODE. However, this theoretical grounding is weakened by the approximations made during discretization. Crucially, the paper does not provide a convergence proof for the resulting discrete algorithm, which the authors themselves classify as a heuristic. Furthermore, the literature contains several alternative averaging schemes for Nesterov-style accelerated algorithms. A comparison against these alternatives is missing, making it difficult to assess whether the proposed adaptive variant offers a significant advantage.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to a toy example with quadratic and linear objectives. These experiments are not sufficient to convincingly demonstrate the advantage of the adaptive averaging scheme, particularly for the important general case of strongly convex objectives.

== Post-rebuttal Update ==
I have read the author rebuttal. The authors have committed to addressing the main weaknesses identified in the review by incorporating the following into the final version:
- Additional experiments on higher-dimensional and non-quadratic problems.
- A theoretical proof of convergence for the adaptive averaging algorithm.
- An implementation of their method on Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method.
- A discussion and comparison with other adaptive averaging schemes.

These proposed additions, if executed well, would substantially strengthen the paper. I have raised my score in light of these commitments.
