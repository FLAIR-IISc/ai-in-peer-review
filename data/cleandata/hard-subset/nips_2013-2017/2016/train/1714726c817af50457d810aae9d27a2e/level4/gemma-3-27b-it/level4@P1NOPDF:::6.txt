This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate constrained convex optimization. A key strength of the proposed method is its theoretical guarantee of maintaining the original convergence rate even when dealing with non-strongly convex objective functions – an advantage over existing restarting heuristics, demonstrated both theoretically and empirically. 

I found portions of the paper challenging to follow, largely due to the work falling somewhat outside my area of expertise. While I assume the authors’ use of abbreviations and specialized terminology is appropriate for the target audience, I would suggest including some brief background information to broaden accessibility. Specifically, the equations presented between lines 33-34 and 46-47 could benefit from a more intuitive explanation to aid understanding.

Furthermore, I’m interested in understanding how this work relates to adaptive averaging methods commonly used in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]. Exploring these connections would strengthen the paper's positioning within the broader optimization landscape. 

Finally, I question the practical significance of the results as presented. The experiments are conducted in R<sup>3</sup>, which feels like a rather limited and simplified setting. It remains unclear how well this method – and the related work it builds upon – scales to problems with much higher dimensionality, a crucial consideration for real-world applications.

A minor comment regarding the presentation: the reference to the “solid line” in Figure 1 is ambiguous. Consider using a different line style (e.g., dotted line) or distinct colors to more clearly illustrate the trajectory of the primal variable.
