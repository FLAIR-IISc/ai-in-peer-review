This paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, analyzing them in both continuous and discrete settings. The work builds upon the study initiated by Krichene et al. [7], which interprets Nesterov's method as a discretization of an ordinary differential equation (ODE) system that couples primal and dual trajectories. In that framework, the primal trajectory is obtained by performing a specific averaging of the mirror of the dual variable.

The main contribution of the present paper is to explore more general averaging schemes. The primary theoretical result, Theorem 2, derives convergence rates for these schemes under suitable conditions on the averaging coefficients. Significantly, the analysis demonstrates the existence of averaging schemes in the continuous setting that achieve convergence rates faster than the classical O(1/t^2) rate. The authors also propose and analyze an adaptive scheme for choosing these weights, which includes convergence guarantees. This adaptive scheme is then used in the experimental section, where its performance is compared with standard restarting techniques known to improve the practical convergence of accelerated methods.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-written paper. The analysis of the new averaging scheme for accelerated methods is presented rigorously for the continuous-time setting. While the analytical framework for continuous-time dynamics is becoming well-established through a series of recent papers, this work provides new and valuable contributions.

**Strengths:**
*   The generalization of the averaging scheme is a natural and insightful extension of prior work.
*   The main theoretical result, which demonstrates that convergence rates faster than O(1/t^2) are achievable in the continuous-time model, is a relevant and potentially impactful finding.
*   The development of an adaptive weight selection strategy with convergence guarantees is a useful step toward making these theoretical ideas practical.

**Areas for Improvement:**
*   The most significant limitation is the absence of a theoretical analysis for the discrete version of the algorithm. The continuous-time analysis is compelling, but it is crucial to understand if the improved convergence rates can be preserved after discretization. Without this analysis, the practical implications of the main theoretical result remain speculative. The paper's impact would be substantially greater if it could bridge this gap.
*   The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging and discussing the extensive body of work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and their coauthors on the topic of continuous-time dynamics for optimization. Their contributions are highly relevant to the analytical techniques employed here and should be appropriately cited.

In summary, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The demonstration of continuous-time dynamics with rates faster than O(1/t^2) is a notable achievement. The key open question, which I encourage the authors to address in future work, is whether this theoretical promise can be translated into a practical, discrete algorithm with provably superior performance.
