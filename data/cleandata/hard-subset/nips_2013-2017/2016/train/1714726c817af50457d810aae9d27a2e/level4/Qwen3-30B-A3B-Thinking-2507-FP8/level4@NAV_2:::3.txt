

The paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization in both continuous and discrete settings. It extends the work of Krichene et al. [7] by generalizing the averaging scheme used in accelerated mirror descent dynamics, establishing a unified Lyapunov function framework to characterize convergence rates under suitable conditions on the averaging weights and gradient accumulation rates. The main theoretical contribution (Theorem 2) provides sufficient conditions for achieving a quadratic convergence rate (1/t²) in continuous time, with an illustrative example demonstrating how replicator dynamics can be accelerated through appropriate averaging. The authors further propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate guarantees while empirically outperforming existing restarting techniques in numerical experiments across multiple problem types.

The paper is well-structured and technically rigorous, with a clear presentation of the continuous-time analysis that builds upon established connections between Nesterov's accelerated method and continuous-time ODEs, as shown in Su et al. [20] and Krichene et al. [7]. The theoretical framework is sound, and the numerical experiments are comprehensive, comparing adaptive averaging with gradient restarting [17] and speed restarting [20] across strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, and KL divergence objectives. The results consistently demonstrate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as, and often significantly better than, existing heuristics.

However, the paper lacks a formal convergence rate analysis for the discrete-time implementation of the adaptive averaging heuristic, which is a notable limitation given that most practical applications occur in discrete time. While the authors briefly present Algorithm 1 and provide a supplementary bound, the discrete-time analysis is incomplete. Additionally, the paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of connections to the work of Attouch, Peypouquet, and Redont [1, 2] on the convergence rates of accelerated methods, which directly relates to the main theoretical contribution.

Strengths:
- Rigorous continuous-time analysis with well-justified Lyapunov function approach
- Clear connection to established literature on accelerated methods
- Comprehensive numerical experiments demonstrating consistent performance improvements
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rate guarantees
- Well-written with excellent technical exposition

Weaknesses:
- Absence of formal discrete-time convergence analysis for the adaptive averaging method
- Insufficient contextualization of the main theoretical result within the broader literature on accelerated methods
- Limited theoretical exploration of whether the continuous-time convergence rate can be preserved in discrete time

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by generalizing the averaging framework for accelerated methods
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is both theoretically grounded and empirically effective
- The work builds upon and extends established results in a meaningful way
- The continuous-time analysis is rigorous and well-executed

Con-acceptance arguments:
- The lack of discrete-time convergence analysis limits the practical impact of the theoretical contributions
- The paper would be strengthened by better contextualizing its main theoretical result within the literature
- The numerical experiments would benefit from more theoretical explanation of why adaptive averaging outperforms restarting techniques

Overall, the paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of accelerated optimization with a promising heuristic. While the discrete-time analysis is a limitation, the continuous-time results are strong and the empirical validation is compelling. The work would be improved by addressing the connections to Attouch and Peypouquet's work and providing a more complete discrete-time analysis. Given the quality of the continuous-time analysis and the promising experimental results, I recommend acceptance.
