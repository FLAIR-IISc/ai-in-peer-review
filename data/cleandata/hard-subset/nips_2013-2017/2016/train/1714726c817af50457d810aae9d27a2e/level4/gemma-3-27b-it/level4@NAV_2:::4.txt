This paper studies a class of continuous-time ODEs describing accelerated mirror descent dynamics with a generalized averaging scheme. Using a Lyapunov argument, the authors provide sufficient conditions on the dual variable’s accumulation rate and the averaging weights to guarantee a desired convergence rate. They then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function, providing theoretical guarantees in continuous-time and demonstrating quadratic convergence in discrete-time. Numerical experiments compare this adaptive approach with existing heuristics like adaptive restarting, showing comparable or improved performance. This work builds on a significant body of research focused on understanding and accelerating first-order methods, particularly in the context of large-scale machine learning [6, 9, 5, 20, 2, 1, 3, 11, 23]. The connection established between Nesterov’s accelerated methods [14, 15] and continuous-time ODEs is well-established [20, 11], and this paper extends that connection by exploring the impact of different averaging schemes. 

The strengths of this paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and the insightful connection between continuous-time dynamics and adaptive averaging. The Lyapunov argument provides a solid foundation for the convergence guarantees, and the demonstration of quadratic convergence in discrete-time is a valuable result. The comparison with adaptive restarting provides a practical benchmark, and the experimental results are encouraging. The paper is also well-motivated, clearly articulating the problem and relating it to existing work on acceleration and averaging [8]. The exploration of dynamics on the probability simplex and the derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamic (with connections to evolutionary game theory [22] and viability theory [4]) add to the novelty and significance of the contribution.

However, there are some areas for improvement. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the conditions for the Lyapunov function’s decay (Theorem 2) might be difficult to verify in practice. A more detailed discussion of the practical implications of these conditions, and potentially some relaxation or approximation strategies, would be beneficial. The numerical experiments are limited to a few examples, and a more extensive evaluation on a wider range of problems, including larger-scale datasets, would strengthen the empirical validation. While the paper mentions related adaptive methods like Adagrad [7] and Adam [10], a more detailed discussion of how adaptive averaging differs from and potentially complements these techniques could be insightful. The link to work on cubic regularization of Newton's method [16] could also be further explored.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation based on Lyapunov analysis.
*   Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with provable convergence guarantees.
*   Connections to replicator dynamics and evolutionary game theory.
*   Encouraging numerical results demonstrating comparable or improved performance over existing heuristics.
*   Clear and well-written presentation, placing the work within the context of relevant literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Conditions for Lyapunov function decay may be difficult to verify in practice.
*   Limited scope of numerical experiments.
*   Could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other adaptive methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam).



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. The theoretical rigor, novel approach, and promising experimental results make it a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
