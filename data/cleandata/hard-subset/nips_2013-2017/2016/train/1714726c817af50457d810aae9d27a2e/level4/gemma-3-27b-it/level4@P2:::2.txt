This paper extends previous work connecting accelerated Mirror Descent to continuous-time ODEs by introducing a generalized averaging scheme within the ODE dynamics and proposing a novel adaptive averaging strategy for its discretization. The authors demonstrate, through experiments, that this adaptive averaging can outperform existing accelerated methods employing restarting heuristics in certain cases. The core idea of adaptively adjusting averaging weights is intuitively appealing. However, the originality and overall impact of the work remain a significant concern.

The paper builds heavily on a prior NIPS publication by the authors [11]. While the extension to generalized averaging is mathematically straightforward, its practical utility is limited, as the authors primarily focus on achieving a quadratic convergence rate – improvements beyond this would necessitate higher-order methods, which are not always applicable. Several results, such as the interpretation of an associated energy function, reformulation into a primal form, and reduction to the replicator dynamics in specific cases, deepen our understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent. However, these contributions, while valuable, do not represent a substantial advancement for the machine learning community.

The adaptive averaging strategy is motivated by satisfying sufficient conditions for ODE convergence; however, the discretization process introduces approximations, lacking a fully rigorous theoretical grounding. Consequently, the proposed method remains a heuristic, as acknowledged by the authors, and lacks explicit convergence guarantees. Moreover, numerous averaging schemes already exist for Nesterov's accelerated algorithms; without a comparative analysis against these alternatives, the advantage of this adaptive variant remains unclear. 

The experimental evaluation, limited to relatively simple quadratic and linear objective functions, is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of adaptive averaging, particularly in more general, strongly convex scenarios. 

**(Post-Rebuttal Update):** Having reviewed the authors’ rebuttal, I acknowledge their commitment to address the identified concerns by including: (1) additional experiments in higher dimensions and with non-quadratic objectives, (2) a theoretical proof of convergence for the adaptive averaging scheme, (3) implementation of the adaptive averaging technique in conjunction with Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method, and (4) a discussion comparing the proposed approach to other adaptive averaging methods. These additions strengthen the paper and warrant an updated assessment.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel idea with intuitive appeal.
*   The paper provides a deeper understanding of the connections between continuous-time dynamics and accelerated methods.
*   The initial experimental results suggest potential performance benefits.
*   The authors have committed to significant improvements based on reviewer feedback.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The work builds heavily on prior work by the authors, limiting its overall originality.
*   The theoretical analysis is incomplete, and the method remains a heuristic without explicit convergence guarantees.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited and lacks a comprehensive comparison to existing methods.
*   The generalization of the averaging scheme is limited by the focus on achieving only a quadratic rate.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a reasonable extension of existing work with an interesting heuristic. However, its initial impact is limited by the lack of strong theoretical guarantees and comprehensive experimental validation. The authors’ commitment to addressing these issues in the revision process is encouraging. I recommend acceptance contingent on the successful incorporation of the promised improvements.



**Review Criteria Breakdown:**

*   **Quality:** Technically sound, but lacks a complete theoretical analysis. The experimental results are preliminary.
*   **Clarity:** Generally well-written and organized, although the mathematical notation could benefit from occasional clarification.
*   **Originality:** Incremental. The core ideas build heavily on previous work.
*   **Significance:** Moderate. While the adaptive averaging scheme is interesting, its potential impact is unclear without further investigation.
