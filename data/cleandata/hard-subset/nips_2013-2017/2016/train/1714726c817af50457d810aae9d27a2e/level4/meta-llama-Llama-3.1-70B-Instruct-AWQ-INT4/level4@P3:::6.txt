This paper presents an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerating constrained convex optimization. The method provides a guarantee that it preserves the original convergence rate when the objective function is not strongly convex, which is an advantage over existing restarting heuristics. The authors demonstrate this both theoretically and experimentally.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction provides a good background on the problem of minimizing a convex function over a feasible set, and the authors' motivation for studying continuous-time, first-order dynamics for optimization. The main contribution of the paper is the introduction of a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, which is a generalization of the accelerated mirror descent method.

The authors provide a unified study of this ODE using an appropriate Lyapunov function, and give sufficient conditions on the weight functions to guarantee a given convergence rate in continuous time. They also show how the replicator ODE can be accelerated by averaging, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate.

The experiments compare the performance of the adaptive averaging heuristic to existing restarting heuristics, and the results show that the adaptive averaging heuristic performs at least as well as the other methods, and in some cases considerably better.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it assumes a significant amount of background knowledge in convex optimization and dynamical systems. While the authors provide some references to relevant work, it may be helpful to include more background information or explanations for readers who are not familiar with these topics.

Additionally, the authors could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relation to adaptive averaging methods used in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad or Adam. This would help to provide more context for the contribution of the paper and its potential impact on the field.

Finally, the authors could provide more information on the practical significance of their work. While the experiments were carried out in R^3, it would be helpful to know how the method scales to much higher dimensions.

Minor comment: The reference to the solid line in Figure 1 is ambiguous. Maybe use a dotted line or colors to show the trajectory of the primal variable?
