

This paper extends the connection between accelerated mirror descent (AMD) and continuous-time ordinary differential equations (ODEs) by introducing a generalized averaging scheme and proposing an adaptive averaging heuristic for discretized versions of the dynamics. The authors build upon prior work that established a continuous-time interpretation of Nesterov's accelerated methods, particularly the connection between accelerated gradient descent and ODEs demonstrated in Su et al. (2014) and extended to constrained problems in Krichene et al. (2015).

The main contribution is the development of a unified framework for accelerated descent dynamics using a Lyapunov function approach, with the key innovation being an adaptive averaging strategy that dynamically computes weights to preserve convergence rates. The authors provide sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing, derive conditions for specific convergence rates (particularly quadratic convergence), and demonstrate how the replicator dynamics on the simplex can be accelerated through their approach. The numerical experiments compare adaptive averaging against restarting heuristics on simple constrained optimization problems, showing favorable performance in certain cases.

The paper has several strengths. The theoretical development is sound, with careful analysis of the continuous-time dynamics and clear sufficient conditions for convergence. The connection between the continuous-time framework and discrete optimization algorithms is well-motivated and provides valuable theoretical insights. The reformulation of AMD in primal space and the derivation of accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex are technically solid and offer new perspectives on well-known optimization methods.

However, the paper faces significant concerns regarding originality and significance. The core framework builds directly upon Krichene et al. (2015), which was presented at NIPS 2015, and the extension to generalized averaging appears trivial. The authors only consider the quadratic convergence rate case (r(t) = t²), while faster rates would require higher-order methods that are not applicable to many practical problems. The adaptive averaging heuristic, while interesting, is presented as a heuristic without rigorous theoretical guarantees for the discrete version. The paper states that "the adaptive averaging heuristic preserves the convergence rate," but provides no explicit convergence proof for the discretized algorithm, making it difficult to assess whether the adaptive approach truly offers theoretical advantages over existing methods.

The experimental evaluation is limited and unconvincing. The authors test only simple problems on the simplex (R³) with quadratic and linear objectives, which are insufficient to demonstrate the method's utility for more complex machine learning problems. The absence of experiments on non-quadratic objectives or higher-dimensional problems weakens the claim of practical significance. The comparison is limited to restarting heuristics from the literature, without evaluating against other adaptive averaging schemes that have been proposed for accelerated methods.

The paper's most significant limitation is its failure to clearly demonstrate how the proposed approach advances the state of the art. While the theoretical analysis is technically correct, the incremental nature of the contribution is not sufficiently justified. The connection to replicator dynamics is mathematically interesting but does not translate to meaningful advances in machine learning applications. The authors acknowledge the heuristic nature of their adaptive averaging approach, yet the paper does not convincingly argue why this particular heuristic is superior to others or why it should be adopted in practice.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should provide a more compelling justification for the significance of their generalized averaging framework, including theoretical convergence guarantees for the discrete algorithm. Additional experiments on non-quadratic objectives and higher-dimensional problems would be necessary to demonstrate practical relevance. A more thorough comparison with alternative adaptive averaging strategies would also help establish the novelty of their approach.

In summary, while the paper makes a technically correct contribution to the understanding of accelerated descent dynamics, the incremental nature of the extension, lack of theoretical guarantees for the discrete algorithm, and limited experimental evaluation significantly reduce its impact. The work does not substantially advance the state of the art in a way that would justify acceptance at NIPS.

Pros: Sound theoretical framework, clear connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete optimization, technically solid derivation of accelerated replicator dynamics.

Cons: Incremental contribution that builds directly on prior NIPS work, trivial extension to generalized averaging, lack of theoretical guarantees for discrete algorithm, limited experimental evaluation with insufficiently challenging problems, failure to demonstrate clear practical significance.
