

This paper proposes an adaptive reweighting heuristic for Nesterov's acceleration, building on the ODE interpretation of Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The method adaptively adjusts iterate weights to accelerate convergence, offering a guaranteed improvement over standard fixed-schedule averaging—a key distinction from prior adaptive averaging approaches. Empirical results demonstrate significant speed-ups across a broad class of functions, including non-strongly convex settings. The mathematical exposition is elegant and accessible, particularly for readers familiar with Krichene et al.'s work, and the empirical validation is compelling.  

The paper’s most notable contribution lies in its practical impact. Nesterov’s acceleration is ubiquitous in machine learning and optimization, yet many empirical studies benchmark new methods against Nesterov’s with fixed weights, overlooking the potential gains from adaptivity. This work demonstrates that a minor modification—adaptive reweighting—can yield dramatic performance improvements, challenging the common practice of using non-adaptive baselines. This insight has broad implications for both theory and practice, as it reveals a simple yet powerful way to enhance a foundational optimization technique.  

While the technical development extends Krichene et al.’s framework, the paper’s value stems from its clarity, practical relevance, and the significance of its core finding: adaptive reweighting is not merely beneficial but *guaranteed* to outperform fixed-schedule averaging. This provides a strong theoretical foundation for adopting adaptive strategies in applications where Nesterov’s method is standard.  

To further strengthen the contribution, the authors should include an analysis of the discretization error for the adaptive scheme. Specifically, a proof showing that the convergence rate is preserved under discretization (e.g., via Euler or higher-order methods) would solidify the theoretical claims. Given the paper’s reliance on continuous-time ODEs, this is a natural extension and should be feasible with standard techniques.  

Additionally, exploring potential connections between the adaptive averaging mechanism and the Conjugate Gradient (CG) method for strongly convex quadratics would deepen the paper’s theoretical scope. While CG is typically viewed as a distinct solver, the adaptive weights here might relate to CG’s step-size selection or residual minimization, offering a new perspective on both methods. This could be a fruitful direction for future work.  

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a clear, impactful contribution to adaptive optimization. The empirical results are robust, the theoretical justification is sound, and the practical implications are significant. The minor technical additions suggested would elevate it to a high-impact publication. I recommend acceptance.
