This paper presents an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerating constrained convex optimization. The presented method provides the guarantee that it preserves the original convergence rate when the objective function is not strongly convex, which is an advantage over existing restarting heuristics. The authors demonstrate this advantage both theoretically and experimentally.

The paper discusses the problem of minimizing a convex function over a feasible set, focusing on continuous-time, first-order dynamics for optimization. It studies a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by two positive, continuous weight functions. The authors provide a unified study of this ODE using an appropriate Lyapunov function and give sufficient conditions on the weight functions to guarantee a desired convergence rate. They also propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes the weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function.

The experiments show that the adaptive averaging heuristic compares favorably to existing restarting heuristics, with a significant improvement in the strongly convex case. The results also confirm that under the adaptive averaging heuristic, the Lyapunov function is decreasing, which is not the case for the restarting heuristics.

The paper is well-written and provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method. However, I had some difficulties following the content, as it is out of my area of expertise. I assume that the used abbreviations and terms are known to those readers that are addressed by the paper, but I would suggest including some background information to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.

In particular, the equations between lines 33, 34, and 46, 47 could benefit from a more intuitive explanation. Additionally, I would like to know the relation to adaptive averaging methods used in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad or Adam. While the authors mention that these methods are not designed for accelerated methods, a more detailed comparison would be helpful.

Furthermore, I am wondering about the practical significance of this work. The experiments were carried out in R^3, which is obviously a toyish setup. How does the proposed method (and related work) scale to much higher dimensions?

Minor comment: The reference to the solid line in Figure 1 is ambiguous. Maybe use a dotted line or colors to show the trajectory of the primal variable?

Strengths:

* The paper presents a new adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerating constrained convex optimization.
* The method provides a guarantee that it preserves the original convergence rate when the objective function is not strongly convex.
* The experiments show that the adaptive averaging heuristic compares favorably to existing restarting heuristics.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes a high level of expertise in the field and could benefit from more background information.
* The equations could be explained more intuitively.
* The relation to adaptive averaging methods used in non-convex optimization is not fully explored.
* The practical significance of the work is not fully demonstrated, as the experiments were carried out in a low-dimensional setup.

Acceptance:

Based on the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the comments mentioned above to make the paper more accessible and to provide a clearer understanding of the proposed method and its practical significance.
