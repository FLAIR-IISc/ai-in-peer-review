- Study of coupled primal–dual continuous-time accelerated descent with dual accumulation η(t) and primal averaging w(t), targeting rate 1/r(t) via Lyapunov Lr(X,Z,t).
- Derivation of sufficient conditions on η and w for continuous-time convergence and a primal-only representation; connects to replicator dynamics and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic.
- Discrete-time proposal: first-order method with adaptive averaging (focus on quadratic rate) and empirical comparisons to adaptive restarting.
- Builds on prior continuous-time acceleration and accelerated mirror descent work ([20],[23],[11]) and relates to adaptive heuristics (restarting, Adagrad, Adam).
- Strength: unified continuous-time Lyapunov framework giving flexible recipe for accelerated dynamics and clear rate guarantees.
- Strength: primal-only formulation clarifies mirror map roles and yields concrete dynamics on common domains, including an instructive replicator connection.
- Strength: energy interpretation (potential vs. kinetic) complements Lyapunov analysis and gives intuitive insight.
- Strength: principled adaptive averaging rule derived from controlling the Lyapunov derivative, with favorable empirical performance vs. restarting.
- Strength: careful ODE analysis establishing existence, uniqueness, and feasibility preservation under reasonable assumptions.
- Weakness: incremental originality; much of the structure mirrors prior work and generalization mainly enables the adaptive heuristic.
- Weakness: discrete-time theory in the main text is incomplete—adaptive averaging lacks full convergence guarantees there.
- Weakness: experiments are narrow (low-dimensional, quadratic/linear/KL on simplex) and omit higher-dimensional constrained convex tasks and sensitivity analyses.
- Weakness: limited baselines—comparison mainly to two restarting heuristics and not to other averaging or recent ODE-inspired methods.
- Weakness: some components (energy view, primal reformulation, replicator link) are mainly pedagogical without advancing algorithms.
- Quality: continuous-time analysis is technically sound; discrete-time method plausible but lacks a complete proof; experiments clear but limited.
- Clarity: generally well written but notation-heavy in parts; recommend roadmaps and clearer separation of continuous vs. discrete claims and t0 role.
- Originality: moderate—adaptive averaging is novel, but analytical machinery is close to prior literature.
- Significance: potential if adaptive averaging shows robust benefits and discrete guarantees are provided; currently contribution is incremental.
- Pros for acceptance: clear Lyapunov framework, principled adaptive averaging, pedagogical primal formulations, and reported empirical gains.
- Cons against acceptance: incomplete discrete-time theory, limited experiments and baselines, incremental novelty, and limited high-dimensional/strongly convex evidence.
- Key suggestions: provide a formal discrete-time convergence theorem for adaptive averaging with step-size/parameter conditions.
- Key suggestions: expand experiments to higher dimensions, diverse constraints/mirror maps, composite and non-quadratic objectives, and sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Key suggestions: compare with alternative averaging schedules and recent restart variants; discuss numerical stability near t0 and effect of w(t) scale-invariance.
- Key suggestions: clarify extension to stochastic or variance-reduced gradients.
- Post-rebuttal: authors pledged to add higher-dimensional/non-quadratic experiments, a discrete convergence proof, tests in cubic-regularized Newton, and comparisons to other adaptive strategies; these revisions would substantially strengthen the paper.
