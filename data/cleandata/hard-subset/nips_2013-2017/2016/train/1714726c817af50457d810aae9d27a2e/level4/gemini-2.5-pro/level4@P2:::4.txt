This paper studies a general class of continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order optimization, referred to as Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD). The authors formulate these dynamics as a coupled system involving a dual variable that accumulates gradients at a rate η(t) and a primal variable that is a weighted average of the dual trajectory, with weights w(t). The central theoretical contribution is a unified analysis via a Lyapunov function. This approach yields elegant and simple sufficient conditions on the functions w(t) and η(t) that guarantee a desired convergence rate, for instance, O(1/t^2).

Building on this theoretical framework, the paper introduces its primary contribution: a novel adaptive averaging heuristic. The core idea is to adaptively select the averaging weights w(t) to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, thereby speeding up convergence in practice. A crucial feature of this heuristic is that, by construction, it is guaranteed to preserve the underlying theoretical convergence rate of the non-adaptive method. This provides a formal advantage over many popular restarting heuristics. The authors present a discrete-time version of the algorithm, prove it maintains the O(1/k^2) convergence rate, and provide numerical experiments comparing its performance against standard gradient and speed restarting schemes.

This work is well-situated within the literature connecting Nesterov's acceleration to ordinary differential equations (ODEs), extending the averaging perspective of previous work. It presents a new, theoretically-grounded alternative to empirical heuristics designed to improve the practical performance of accelerated methods.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The main contribution, the adaptive averaging heuristic, is both original and significant. While restarting heuristics are common, the concept of adaptively modifying the averaging weights based on the behavior of a Lyapunov function is a fresh and insightful approach. The key advantage is its preservation of the theoretical convergence rate, a guarantee often missing for restarting schemes on general convex problems. This provides a principled way to improve practical performance without sacrificing worst-case guarantees.
*   **Theoretical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The use of a Lyapunov function to provide a unified analysis for a broad class of accelerated dynamics is elegant and powerful. The framework clearly explains how the choice of averaging and learning rate schedules impacts the convergence rate. The provided proofs and derivations are clear and well-supported.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It begins with a clear motivation for studying continuous-time dynamics, logically develops the theoretical framework, and then transitions smoothly to the proposed heuristic and its empirical validation. The example showing how the replicator dynamics can be accelerated is a nice, insightful application of the general theory.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The paper presents a discrete-time algorithm that can be readily implemented. The numerical experiments, while limited in scope, effectively demonstrate that the proposed heuristic performs competitively with or, in some cases, significantly better than established restarting methods.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for improvement):**

*   **Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments in the main text are conducted in a low-dimensional (R^3) setting for visualization purposes. To bolster the claims of practical utility, it would be beneficial to include or summarize results from higher-dimensional problems that are more representative of modern machine learning tasks. While the authors mention such experiments in the supplementary material, their inclusion in the main paper would strengthen the argument for the heuristic's effectiveness.
*   **Comparison with Other Adaptive Optimizers:** The paper briefly mentions popular adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam but does not compare against them. While the paper's focus is on improving Nesterov-style acceleration, an empirical comparison or a more detailed discussion of the relative merits of adaptive averaging versus adaptive learning rates would provide valuable context for practitioners.
*   **Potential for Stronger Guarantees:** The analysis guarantees that adaptive averaging *preserves* the O(1/k^2) rate. However, the experiments on strongly convex problems suggest that the heuristic might achieve a faster (e.g., linear) rate in such settings, similar to how restarting does. A discussion on the potential for proving faster rates under stronger assumptions, or the challenges in doing so, would be a valuable addition.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a novel and well-motivated contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is elegant, theoretically sound, and shows promising empirical performance. It provides a valuable alternative to existing heuristics for accelerating first-order methods. The weaknesses are minor and mainly concern the scope of the experimental validation and opportunities for future theoretical extensions. The paper is a solid piece of work that is highly suitable for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
