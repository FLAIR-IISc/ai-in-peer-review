- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic for constrained convex optimization that preserves the original convergence rate when the objective is not strongly convex, outperforming restarting heuristics theoretically and experimentally.  
- Frames the problem via continuous-time, first-order dynamics and reviews mirror maps as background context.  
- Studies a family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) ODEs parameterized by weight functions w and η and provides a unified Lyapunov-based analysis.  
- Derives sufficient conditions on η, w, and r for a Lyapunov energy to decrease and exhibits a second energy function that also decreases under those conditions.  
- Provides constructive methods and examples to build weight functions w, η that satisfy the theoretical conditions for simple constraint sets.  
- Introduces an adaptive averaging rule to compute weights w online, proving it preserves convergence rates and often outperforms existing heuristics.  
- Numerical experiments on simplex-constrained problems in R^3 (entropy DGF) show adaptive averaging compares favorably to restarting heuristics, with large gains in the strongly convex case.  
- Paper is well-written, with thorough analysis and clear experimental comparisons.  
- Paper assumes high domain expertise and could be hard to follow for non-experts; more background would improve accessibility.  
- Equations around lines 33–34 and 46–47 lack intuitive explanation and should be clarified.  
- Relation to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) is not discussed and should be addressed.  
- Practical significance and scalability are unclear because experiments are limited to toy (R^3) settings; need discussion or experiments in higher dimensions.  
- Minor figure issue: reference to the solid line in Figure 1 is ambiguous; recommend using dotted lines or color to show the primal trajectory.  
- Recommendation: Accept with revisions to add background, clarify key equations, discuss relation to adaptive non-convex methods, and address empirical scalability.
