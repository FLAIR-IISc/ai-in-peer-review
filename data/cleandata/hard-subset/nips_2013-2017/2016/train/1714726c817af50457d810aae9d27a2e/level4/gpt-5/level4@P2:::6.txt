Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated first-order dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a generalized averaging view of accelerated mirror descent (AMD). It introduces a family of continuous-time ODEs parameterized by a gradient accumulation rate η(t) and averaging weights w(t), and analyzes them via a Lyapunov function Lr that yields sufficient conditions on η and w to attain a target rate 1/r(t). This framework recovers known accelerated rates and includes, as a special case, an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics on the simplex. Building on this, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses weights to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative whenever possible, thereby preserving the nominal rate (e.g., quadratic in time) and offering an alternative to adaptive restarting heuristics. A discrete-time instantiation is provided, and experiments (primarily in R^3) compare adaptive averaging to gradient- and speed-restarting, showing comparable or better performance.

The work is well-positioned within the literature connecting Nesterov’s acceleration to continuous-time dynamics (e.g., Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan), proximal/constraint-aware acceleration (Nesterov; Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett), and empirical restart heuristics (O’Donoghue–Candès). The paper also discusses relationships to adaptive-step-size methods such as AdaGrad (Duchi et al., 2011) and Adam (Kingma and Ba, 2015), though these target step-size adaptation rather than accelerated averaging.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: The AMDw,η family and the Lyapunov analysis cleanly characterize when generalized averaging preserves a desired convergence rate. The explicit conditions on η, w, and r(t) are clear and reusable.
- Novel heuristic with guarantees: The adaptive averaging rule is simple, preserves the continuous-time Lyapunov decrease, and, in the provided discretization, maintains the quadratic rate typical of accelerated first-order methods.
- Connections and examples: The primal representations and the accelerated replicator example on the simplex are informative and potentially useful for constrained problems with natural mirror maps.
- Energy viewpoint: The auxiliary energy Er helps interpret dynamics (potential vs. kinetic terms) and aligns with observed monotonicity in experiments.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Theory-to-discretization bridge: While the continuous-time guarantees are clear, the discrete-time rate preservation relies on a specific scheme. Please state all assumptions (smoothness, Lipschitz continuity, strong convexity where used, properties of ψ/ψ∗, step-size choices) and emphasize precisely what is guaranteed in discrete time versus heuristic.
- Scope of experiments: The empirical evaluation is limited to 3D toy problems. Demonstrate scalability on higher-dimensional benchmarks (e.g., l2-regularized logistic regression, Lasso, portfolio/simplex problems), report iteration complexity and wall-clock time, and compare to adaptive restarting and standard accelerated baselines. Include sensitivity to β, step size s, and the choice of ψ.
- Overhead and robustness: Clarify the computational cost of implementing adaptive averaging (e.g., monitoring the sign of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ or its discrete analogue) and robustness to noise/inexact gradients.

Clarity
- Accessibility: The paper is dense for readers outside the area. Include a brief primer in the main text on mirror maps, Bregman divergence, and the roles of η(t) and w(t) in AMDw,η. Provide intuition around the derivative bound (inequality (3)) and the two conditions of Theorem 2—why they are natural and how they guide the construction of w and η.
- Notation and exposition: Unify notation across X, Z, Ž, X̌ and define all variables at first use. In Algorithm 1, explicitly define R, γ, λk, and ak, and briefly explain their roles.
- Figures: The “solid line” description in Figure 1 is ambiguous; consider distinct line styles or color-coding to distinguish primal and dual trajectories.

Originality
- The generalized averaging family and adaptive averaging heuristic appear novel within the acceleration-via-ODEs line of work. It would help to more explicitly contrast your conditions and guarantees with inertial-dynamics-based analyses (e.g., Attouch–Peypouquet–Redont) and with variational perspectives (Wibisono et al.), highlighting what is new here.

Significance
- If the method scales and preserves accelerated behavior in realistic settings, it could be a practical alternative to restarts with a principled guarantee. A more comprehensive empirical study would strengthen the case for impact. Discussion of extensions to composite (proximal) settings and to stochastic gradients would also broaden relevance.

Relation to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization
- Please clarify how adaptive averaging differs from AdaGrad/Adam in design and goals, and whether your heuristic can be combined with per-coordinate or moment-based adaptations (e.g., adapt a(t) globally while using coordinate-wise preconditioning). If relevant, comment on behavior for sparse gradients and in non-convex settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and general Lyapunov-based conditions linking averaging and acceleration.
- Simple adaptive heuristic with theoretical guarantees in continuous time and a principled discretization preserving the quadratic rate.
- Insightful examples (e.g., accelerated replicator dynamics) and useful energy interpretation.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation on low-dimensional problems; unclear scalability and real-world performance.
- Dense presentation; some derivations and notation require more intuition and clarification.
- Discrete-time guarantees and assumptions could be stated more explicitly and prominently.

Questions for the authors
- How should β, η(t), and the discretization step size be selected in practice? How sensitive is performance to these choices?
- What is the computational overhead of adaptive averaging relative to restarting?
- Can the approach handle composite objectives with proximal steps and stochastic gradients while retaining guarantees?

Minor comments
- Figure 1: use distinct line styles or colors to disambiguate trajectories.
- Ensure all symbols in Algorithm 1 are defined where they appear.
- Wherever possible, move key background (mirror maps/Bregman divergence) from the supplement into the main text for readability.
