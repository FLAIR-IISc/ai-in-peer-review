

This paper investigates a family of continuous-time accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, characterized by a dual variable accumulating gradients at rate η(t) and a primal variable formed as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). The authors establish sufficient conditions on η and w to achieve a desired convergence rate using a Lyapunov argument, with a focus on the replicator dynamics as a concrete example. The key contribution is the adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes weights to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function while preserving the quadratic convergence rate. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing theoretical guarantees in continuous time and demonstrating through numerical experiments that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as adaptive restarting, with notable improvements in certain cases.

The work relates well to previous NIPS contributions, particularly building upon Su et al.'s connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs [20], and extending the analysis from [11] on accelerated mirror descent. The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a rigorous Lyapunov analysis that establishes clear convergence guarantees. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized presentation of the mathematical framework, examples (including the replicator dynamics on the simplex), and numerical experiments that effectively illustrate the method's advantages.

The originality is substantial, as the adaptive averaging heuristic provides a new perspective on accelerating first-order methods that differs from existing restart heuristics. While restart methods have been previously studied (e.g., [17, 20]), this paper establishes theoretical guarantees that these heuristics do not necessarily preserve the convergence rate, whereas adaptive averaging does. The significance is high, as the method has practical implications for speeding up optimization algorithms used in machine learning, particularly for constrained problems.

Strengths:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear Lyapunov arguments
- Well-motivated examples, including the replicator dynamics on the simplex
- Comprehensive numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging with existing heuristics
- Strong connection to continuous-time dynamics and their discrete counterparts
- Clear demonstration that adaptive averaging preserves the convergence rate

Weaknesses:
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is presented primarily in continuous time, with only a brief sketch of the discrete implementation
- The numerical experiments, while promising, could be expanded to include more diverse problem settings
- The connection to related work on adaptive first-order methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) could be more thoroughly discussed

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization dynamics, with both theoretical and practical significance. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a meaningful advancement over existing methods, with clear theoretical guarantees and empirical support. I recommend acceptance as it meets all four criteria for high-quality scientific contributions: quality (rigorous analysis), clarity (well-structured presentation), originality (novel heuristic with theoretical guarantees), and significance (practical impact on optimization algorithms).
