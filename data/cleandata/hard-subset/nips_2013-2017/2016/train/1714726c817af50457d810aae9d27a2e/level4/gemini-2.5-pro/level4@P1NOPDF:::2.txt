This paper builds upon prior work connecting accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The authors extend the ODE dynamics with a more general averaging scheme and, for a discretized version of this ODE, propose a novel adaptive averaging strategy. The experiments show that, in some cases, this new variant of AMD with adaptive averaging can outperform existing AMD algorithms that use restarting. The proposed adaptive averaging strategy is novel and well-motivated.

While the paper is well-presented, its originality and practical significance are significant concerns.

-   The core theoretical elements of the paper, including the Lyapunov function and the discretization approach, are directly adapted from a prior NIPS paper [7]. The extension from simple averaging to a generalized averaging scheme appears to be an incremental step. Its utility is also questionable, as the authors ultimately focus their analysis on the quadratic rate case. Achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which are not broadly applicable.

-   Several results, such as the interpretation of the energy function, the reformulation into a primal form, and the reduction to replicator dynamics, provide deeper insight into the accelerated Mirror Descent algorithm. However, their impact on advancing machine learning is limited.

-   The adaptive averaging strategy is motivated by the need to satisfy conditions sufficient to guarantee the convergence of the continuous-time ODE. However, due to approximations made during discretization, this strategy lacks a rigorous theoretical justification in the discrete setting. The paper does not provide an explicit proof of convergence for the resulting algorithm, meaning the adaptive scheme remains a heuristic, as the authors themselves acknowledge. Furthermore, several alternative averaging schemes for Nesterov's accelerated methods already exist. Without a comparative analysis, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed adaptive variant offers a meaningful advantage over these alternatives.

-   The experiments, which are limited to a toy example with a quadratic/linear objective, are not sufficiently convincing. They fail to demonstrate the advantage of the adaptive averaging scheme, particularly for the more general class of strongly convex problems.
