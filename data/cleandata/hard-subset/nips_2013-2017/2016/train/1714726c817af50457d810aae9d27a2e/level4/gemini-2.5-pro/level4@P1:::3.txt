This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, focusing on their continuous-time dynamics. The work extends the study initiated by Krichene et al. in [11], where Nesterov's accelerated method was connected to an ODE system that couples a primal and a dual trajectory. In that framework, the primal trajectory is constructed as a specific weighted average of the mirrored dual variable's trajectory.

The main contribution of this paper is to generalize this framework to a much broader family of averaging schemes. The authors introduce a class of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two functions: an averaging weight function `w(t)` and a gradient accumulation rate `η(t)`. The central theoretical result, Theorem 2, uses a Lyapunov argument to derive sufficient conditions on `w` and `η` to achieve a desired convergence rate of O(1/r(t)). A key finding is that in the continuous-time setting, this framework allows for convergence rates faster than the standard O(1/t^2).

Building on this analysis, the authors propose a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. This scheme adaptively chooses the weights to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, thereby speeding up convergence in practice while provably preserving the theoretical convergence rate. The paper presents numerical experiments comparing this adaptive scheme to established restarting heuristics, demonstrating competitive or superior performance. Finally, as a compelling example, the paper shows how the well-known replicator dynamics can be accelerated using this averaging framework.

**Strengths**

*   **Rigorous and General Framework:** The paper presents a rigorous and unified analysis for a general class of accelerated dynamics. The use of a Lyapunov function to derive sufficient conditions for convergence is elegant and provides clear insights into the role of the averaging and rate functions.
*   **Novel Theoretical Results:** The demonstration that averaging schemes can achieve convergence rates faster than O(1/t^2) in continuous time is a significant and interesting theoretical contribution.
*   **Practical and Guaranteed Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a valuable practical contribution. A key advantage over existing restarting heuristics is that it is constructed to preserve the Lyapunov function's decrease, thus offering stronger theoretical guarantees on maintaining the convergence rate.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics is a nice, concrete example that illustrates the power of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Gap between Continuous and Discrete Analysis:** The primary weakness is the gap between the continuous-time theory and the discrete-time implementation. While the paper shows that faster-than-O(1/t^2) rates are achievable for the continuous ODE, it is unclear if this advantage can be preserved under discretization. The discrete algorithm presented (Algorithm 1) and its analysis (deferred to the supplement) only appear to cover the O(1/k^2) case.
*   **Positioning Relative to Prior Work:** While the analysis builds on the established connection between continuous dynamics and acceleration, the paper could be strengthened by more explicitly discussing its relationship to the extensive work on inertial dynamics and Hessian-driven damping by Attouch, Peypouquet, and co-authors.

**Detailed Comments**

This is an interesting and well-executed paper. The analysis of the generalized averaging scheme for accelerated methods is a solid contribution, extending the continuous-time perspective on optimization in a meaningful way. The main ideas of the analysis are rooted in a now well-established line of work, but the paper successfully introduces new results and contributions within this context.

The most significant result is that faster convergence rates are possible with different averaging schemes. It would be highly valuable for the authors to discuss the prospects of translating these faster rates into practical discrete-time algorithms. Is it possible to design a discretization that preserves, for instance, an O(1/k^p) rate for p > 2? This would substantially increase the paper's impact.

The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and the experimental results are promising. The comparison with restarting techniques is fair and effectively highlights the benefits of the proposed approach, particularly its stability, as evidenced by the monotonically decreasing Lyapunov function in the experiments.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to our understanding of accelerated methods and provides a novel heuristic with both theoretical backing and empirical support. Addressing the connection between the continuous-time potential and discrete-time reality would make the paper even stronger.
