- Proposes a unified continuous-time family (AMD_{w,η}) of accelerated mirror descent dynamics parameterized by averaging w(t) and accumulation η(t).  
- Core theoretical contribution: Lyapunov-based unified analysis giving sufficient conditions on w(t) and η(t) for 1/r(t) convergence.  
- Derives an accelerated replicator dynamic for optimization on the probability simplex, linking mirror descent and replicator equations.  
- Introduces a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that greedily selects w(t) to decrease the Lyapunov function and preserve continuous-time rates.  
- Provides a discrete-time algorithm implementing the adaptive averaging heuristic and compares it experimentally to adaptive restarting.  
- Strength: clear, well-written continuous-time analysis offering elegant and insightful generalization of averaging and gradient accumulation.  
- Strength: the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, principled, and motivated directly by Lyapunov decrease rather than momentum resets.  
- Strength: in continuous time, the heuristic provably preserves the O(1/t^2) convergence rate for general convex functions.  
- Weakness: heavy dependence on prior NIPS work [11]; extension to general w(t) is natural but incremental rather than groundbreaking.  
- Weakness: theoretical guarantees for the discrete-time algorithm are not presented in the main paper and are deferred to the supplement.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited to low-dimensional (3D simplex) toy problems, insufficient to demonstrate practical scalability or impact.  
- Weakness: comparisons are restricted to adaptive restarting; lacks positioning against broader adaptive averaging or step-size methods.  
- Recommendation summary: solid theoretical contribution with a novel heuristic, but incremental and weakened by limited experiments and the omission of main-text discrete-time proofs.  
- Acceptance arguments: unified framework, novel adaptive averaging with continuous-time guarantees, and new insights like accelerated replicator dynamics.  
- Rejection arguments: incremental over prior work, weak empirical validation, and key discrete-time proof relegated to the appendix.
