- Paper extends continuous-time ODE view of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with a more general averaging scheme and proposes a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one discretization.  
- Adaptive AMD can outperform existing AMD with restarting in some cases; adaptive averaging idea is new and intuitively motivated.

- Strength: Clear, novel formulation of an adaptive averaging strategy with good intuition.  
- Strength: Insights such as energy-function interpretation, primal reformulation, and reduction to replicator dynamics improve conceptual understanding.  
- Strength: Empirical evidence shows adaptive averaging sometimes outperforms AMD with restarting.

- Weakness: Core elements (Lyapunov function, discretization, continuous-time viewpoint) largely follow prior work [7], so originality and practical value are limited.  
- Weakness: Extension from simple to generalized averaging appears straightforward and may not yield substantive practical gains without higher-order methods.  
- Weakness: Discretization approximations are not fully addressed; no explicit convergence proof for the discrete algorithm and method is treated as a heuristic.  
- Weakness: Lacks empirical/theoretical comparisons to many existing averaging schemes for accelerated methods.  
- Weakness: Experiments are limited to toy quadratic/linear problems and do not compellingly demonstrate advantage in general strongly convex settings.

- Overall: Well written with appealing intuition but incremental relative to [7], lacking discrete-time guarantees, comparison to alternative averaging, and broad experiments, which reduces impact.

- Post-rebuttal: Authors commit to adding higher-dimensional and non-quadratic experiments.  
- Post-rebuttal: Authors commit to providing a theoretical convergence proof for the adaptive averaging scheme.  
- Post-rebuttal: Authors will implement adaptive averaging within Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method.  
- Post-rebuttal: Authors will add a discussion comparing to other adaptive averaging strategies.
