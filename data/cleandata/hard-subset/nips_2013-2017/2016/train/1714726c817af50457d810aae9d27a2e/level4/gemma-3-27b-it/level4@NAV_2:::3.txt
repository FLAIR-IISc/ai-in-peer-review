The paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization in both continuous and discrete settings. The authors build upon their prior work [7], which demonstrated that Nesterov’s method can be viewed as a discretization of an ODE system coupling a primal trajectory with a dual one, where the primal trajectory is obtained through a specific averaging of the mirror of the dual variable. This paper extends that work by investigating more general averaging schemes. Theorem 2 establishes convergence rates under appropriate conditions on the averaging coefficients, deriving rates faster than 1/t<sup>2</sup> in the continuous setting. An adaptive choice of weights with convergence guarantees is also proposed and evaluated experimentally, comparing its performance against restarting techniques known to improve the convergence of accelerated methods. The theoretical analysis focuses on the continuous-time setting, with the discrete version left for future work. 

This is a well-written and interesting paper. The analysis of the novel averaging scheme for accelerated methods is rigorous and presented clearly. While the core ideas surrounding the analysis of continuous dynamics are now well-established in a growing body of literature, this work offers new contributions and results. In this context, related work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors [1, 2, 3] should be acknowledged. The main theorem, demonstrating the existence of averaging schemes achieving convergence rates faster than 1/t<sup>2</sup>, is a significant contribution, though it would be valuable to investigate whether this improved rate is preserved upon discretization.

**Strengths:**

*   **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the proposed averaging schemes, building on established results in continuous-time optimization dynamics.
*   **Novel Contribution:** The identification of averaging schemes potentially leading to faster convergence rates than standard accelerated methods is a noteworthy contribution.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the technical details accessible.
*   **Experimental Validation:**  The numerical experiments provide evidence that the adaptive averaging heuristic performs favorably compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discrete Analysis:** The theoretical analysis primarily focuses on the continuous-time setting. A corresponding analysis for the discrete version of the algorithm is lacking, leaving open the question of whether the improved convergence rates translate to practical implementations.
*   **Related Work:** While the paper references relevant literature, including the authors' previous work, it could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work in adaptive optimization methods, particularly those outside the realm of accelerated gradient descent (e.g., Adagrad, Adam [7]).
*   **Conditions for Faster Rates:** The conditions required to achieve convergence rates faster than 1/t<sup>2</sup> might be difficult to verify in practice.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel theoretical analysis of averaging schemes in accelerated descent dynamics.
*   The identified potential for faster convergence rates is a significant contribution to the field.
*   The experimental results support the effectiveness of the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a corresponding discrete analysis limits the immediate practical impact of the theoretical results.
*   The conditions for achieving faster convergence rates may be challenging to satisfy in real-world applications.
