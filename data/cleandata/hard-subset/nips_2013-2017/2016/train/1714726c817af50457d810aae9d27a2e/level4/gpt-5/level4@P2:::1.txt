Summary:
The paper builds on the ODE interpretation of Nesterov-style acceleration (notably Krichene et al., NIPS 2015; Su et al., 2016; Wibisono et al., 2016) to study accelerated mirror descent with generalized averaging. The authors couple a dual trajectory that accumulates gradients at a time-varying rate η(t) with a primal trajectory obtained by weighted averaging via weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov argument, they provide simple sufficient conditions on (η, w) to achieve a target rate 1/r(t) in continuous time, recovering the familiar quadratic rate for r(t) = t^2. They also give a primal representation and concrete examples, including an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics on the simplex.

The main algorithmic contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses weights w(t) to decrease the Lyapunov derivative whenever possible. This guarantees that the Lyapunov function is non-increasing and thus preserves the nominal convergence rate. The authors present a discrete-time instantiation and show it maintains the quadratic rate of accelerated first-order methods. Empirically, the adaptive averaging scheme matches or outperforms popular adaptive restarting heuristics across several convex objectives, with notable gains in some strongly convex cases.

Relation to prior work:
The work is a natural extension of the continuous-time analysis of accelerated mirror descent (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015) and the variational/ODE views of acceleration (Su et al., Wibisono et al.). It contrasts with adaptive restarts (O’Donoghue & Candès; Su et al.) by adapting weights continuously rather than resetting momentum, and it differs from Adagrad/Adam by targeting accelerated dynamics rather than step-size adaptation. The replicator example connects to classic mirror descent on the simplex and evolutionary game dynamics.

Strengths:
- Clear, unified Lyapunov framework that yields easily verifiable sufficient conditions for achieving a desired rate and offers interpretability via an energy function.
- A simple adaptive weighting rule with provable monotonicity of the Lyapunov function, ensuring no loss in convergence rate relative to fixed schedules.
- Concrete primal formulations and illustrative examples (e.g., accelerated replicator dynamics).
- Empirical comparisons showing that adaptive averaging is competitive with or better than adaptive restarts on several objectives.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Discrete-time guarantees: While the paper states that the quadratic rate is preserved under the proposed discretization, the analysis appears to be largely in the supplement. A concise, self-contained proof sketch in the main text (or clearer exposition of assumptions and constants) would strengthen the contribution and aid reproducibility.
- Empirical scope: Most experiments are on simplex-constrained problems in low dimensions for visualization. Including higher-dimensional benchmarks and additional constraint sets (e.g., boxes or Euclidean balls) would better demonstrate robustness and practical impact.
- Parameterization and sensitivity: More guidance on selecting η(t), the target rate r(t), and regularizers would help practitioners. An ablation on the sensitivity of performance to these choices would be valuable.
- Strongly convex setting: The method empirically excels there; a theoretical treatment clarifying when and how adaptive averaging can match or improve accelerated linear rates (or constants) would be useful.
- Connections to classical methods: For strongly convex quadratics, it would be interesting to explore links between adaptive averaging and Conjugate Gradient, including whether the heuristic implicitly induces CG-like directions or orthogonality properties.

Quality:
The theoretical development is technically sound and well-grounded in the Lyapunov framework. The conditions for stability and rate are transparent, and the adaptive rule is justified by direct control of the Lyapunov derivative. The discrete-time algorithm is reasonable, though the main text could more prominently present its analysis and assumptions.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and organized. The continuous-time analysis, the role of the Lyapunov function, and the interpretation of the energy function are easy to follow. The examples (positive orthant and simplex) help build intuition. Minor improvements could include consolidating core discrete-time results into the main text and adding more implementation details.

Originality:
Conceptually, the work is a focused extension of prior ODE-based analyses of acceleration, but the adaptive averaging idea with guarantees is novel and practically meaningful. The accelerated replicator formulation is a nice instantiation of the framework.

Significance:
Because accelerated first-order methods are ubiquitous, a principled and simple way to adaptively weight iterates without sacrificing guarantees is potentially impactful. The empirical gains and generality of the approach make it a promising addition to the toolbox.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid and transparent theory; simple heuristic with guarantees.
- Preserves convergence rates while often improving practical performance.
- Clear exposition and useful examples; connects to widely-used accelerated methods.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Discrete-time analysis is not emphasized in the main text.
- Empirical evaluation could be broader and higher-dimensional.
- Novelty is incremental relative to Krichene et al. (2015), focusing on adaptive weighting rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic paradigm.

Additional questions for the authors:
- Can the adaptive rule be tuned or regularized to favor linear rates under strong convexity, and can you bound constants relative to restarts?
- For quadratics, is there a formal connection to CG (e.g., through the induced averaging weights or implicit polynomials)?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of mirror map and regularizer, and can you provide practical defaults?

Overall evaluation:
I found the paper well-executed, clear, and practically relevant. Despite being a close extension of prior ODE-based acceleration, the adaptive averaging heuristic with guarantees is a compelling contribution, and the experiments are encouraging. Strengthening the discrete-time analysis in the main body and expanding the empirical scope would further increase its impact.
