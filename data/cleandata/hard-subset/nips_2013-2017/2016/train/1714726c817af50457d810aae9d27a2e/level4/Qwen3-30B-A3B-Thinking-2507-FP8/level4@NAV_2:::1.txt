

The paper extends the ODE interpretation of Nesterov's acceleration from Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) to develop a natural adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated mirror descent dynamics. The authors formalize a broad family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, and provide sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate using a Lyapunov argument. They demonstrate how this framework can accelerate the replicator dynamics on the simplex, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes the weights to accelerate convergence while preserving the quadratic convergence rate. This work builds upon and extends the continuous-time analysis of accelerated methods initiated by Su et al. (NIPS 2014) and Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015), which established connections between Nesterov's acceleration and continuous-time ODEs.

The paper excels in several key aspects. First, the mathematical development is elegant and well-motivated, with a clear Lyapunov function approach that provides intuitive insights into the acceleration mechanism. The connection to replicator dynamics and its acceleration on the simplex is particularly compelling and well-executed. Second, the empirical results are thorough and convincing, demonstrating consistent improvements over existing heuristics like adaptive restarting across a variety of objective functions (strongly convex quadratics, weakly convex quadratics, linear functions, and KL divergence). Third, the paper makes an important contribution to the field by showing that simple adaptive modifications to Nesterov's method can yield significant practical improvements, addressing a common gap in the literature where adaptive methods are often compared to non-adaptive baselines.

The main weakness of the paper is the lack of a formal analysis of the discretized process to show that the convergence rate is preserved in discrete time. While the continuous-time guarantees are compelling, the practical utility of the method depends on the discrete implementation, and the authors should provide a rigorous analysis of the discrete version to complement their continuous-time results. The supplementary material mentions a bound on the convergence rate, but a more detailed analysis in the main text would strengthen the paper significantly.

The paper scores highly across all four evaluation criteria. Quality: The theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported by experiments, and the authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach. Clarity: The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the mathematical concepts. Originality: While building on previous work, the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and provides a significant extension to the existing framework. Significance: The results have clear practical implications for accelerating many first-order optimization methods, which are widely used in machine learning and related fields.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a valuable contribution that addresses an important practical gap in the literature, and the paper's theoretical and empirical results are strong. The only major improvement needed is a more detailed analysis of the discrete-time convergence rate preservation, which the authors should address in the camera-ready version.
