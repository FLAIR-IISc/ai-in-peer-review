This paper presents an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerating constrained convex optimization. The presented method provides the guarantee that it preserves the original convergence rate when the objective function is not strongly convex. This is an advantage over existing restarting heuristics and the paper shows this both theoretically and experimentally.

The authors start by summarizing the main ideas of the paper and relating these ideas to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. They discuss the problem of minimizing a convex function f over a feasible set X, a closed convex subset of E = Rn. They also introduce the concept of continuous-time, first-order dynamics for optimization and provide a brief review of the definition and basic properties of mirror maps.

The authors then propose a new heuristic that provides guarantees on the convergence rate of the method. They study a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, given by a set of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) parameterized by two positive, continuous weight functions w and η. They provide a unified study of this ODE using an appropriate Lyapunov function and give sufficient conditions on η, w, and r for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing. They also exhibit a second energy function that is guaranteed to decrease under the same conditions.

The authors then describe a method to construct weight functions w, η that satisfy the conditions of the corollary and provide several examples of these dynamics for simple constraint sets. They also propose an adaptive averaging heuristic for adaptively computing the weights w. They show that this heuristic preserves the convergence rate and seems to perform at least as well as other heuristics for first-order accelerated methods, and in some cases considerably better.

The authors also provide numerical experiments to compare their adaptive averaging heuristic to existing restarting heuristics. They consider simplex-constrained problems and take the distance generating function ψ to be the entropy function. They perform the experiments in R3 and consider different objective functions. The results show that adaptive averaging compares favorably to the restarting heuristics on all these examples, with a significant improvement in the strongly convex case.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the method and its relation to previous work. The numerical experiments are also well-presented and provide a clear comparison between the proposed method and existing heuristics.

However, I had a hard time following the content of the paper as it is out of my area of expertise. I assume that the used abbreviations and terms are known to those readers that are addressed by the paper, but I would suggest including background information to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience. The equations between lines 33,34 and 46,47 could benefit from a more intuitive explanation.

Furthermore, I would like to know the relation to adaptive averaging methods used in non-convex optimization such as Adagrad [1] or Adam [2]. Finally, I am wondering about the practical significance of this work. The experiments were carried out in R^3 which is obviously a toyish setup. How does your method (and related work) scale to much higher dimensions?
