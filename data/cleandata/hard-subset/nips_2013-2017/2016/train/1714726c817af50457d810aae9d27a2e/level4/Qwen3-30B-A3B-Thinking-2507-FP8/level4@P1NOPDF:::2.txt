

The paper builds on foundational work linking accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ODEs by introducing a generalized averaging scheme and a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one discretization variant. While the adaptive averaging strategy is intuitively appealing and demonstrates improved empirical performance over restarted AMD variants in specific cases, the paper's core contribution faces significant originality concerns.  

The primary extension—generalizing simple averaging to a broader scheme—appears relatively straightforward and lacks substantial novelty. The authors ultimately restrict analysis to quadratic rates, where higher-order methods (which enable faster convergence) are often inapplicable. Consequently, this extension offers limited practical significance. Furthermore, key components (Lyapunov functions, discretization techniques, etc.) are directly derived from prior work [7], with the generalized averaging merely extending a well-established framework without introducing transformative insights.  

While certain results—such as the energy function interpretation, primal reformulation, and connection to replicator dynamics—enhance understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent, they do not meaningfully advance machine learning methodology. The theoretical foundation for the adaptive averaging strategy is also incomplete. Although designed to satisfy sufficient conditions for ODE convergence, the strategy lacks rigorous theoretical grounding due to discretization approximations. Crucially, no explicit convergence guarantee is provided for the discrete algorithm, rendering it a heuristic—as the authors acknowledge. Moreover, the paper fails to compare this adaptive scheme against established alternatives in Nesterov’s accelerated methods, leaving unclear whether it offers a meaningful improvement.  

The experimental evaluation is particularly weak. The toy example uses only quadratic or linear objectives, which fails to convincingly demonstrate the adaptive averaging’s advantage, especially for strongly convex problems where the method’s utility is most relevant. This narrow scope undermines the empirical claims.  

== Post-rebuttal update ==  
After reviewing the authors’ rebuttal, I note their commitment to addressing key concerns in the revision:  
- Adding higher-dimensional and non-quadratic examples.  
- Providing a formal convergence proof for the adaptive averaging strategy.  
- Implementing the adaptive averaging for Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method.  
- Including comparative analysis against other adaptive averaging schemes.  

These revisions substantially strengthen the paper’s theoretical and empirical foundation. I have accordingly updated my recommendation to recommend acceptance.
