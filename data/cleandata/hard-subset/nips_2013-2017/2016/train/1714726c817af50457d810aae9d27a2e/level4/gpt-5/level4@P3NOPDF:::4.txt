Summary
The paper studies a class of optimization dynamics expressed as ODEs with a generalized averaging scheme. Using a Lyapunov framework, the authors provide sufficient conditions on the “dual learning rate” and averaging weights to guarantee a prescribed convergence rate. Building on this, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic intended to accelerate the decay of the Lyapunov function along solution trajectories. The paper offers both theoretical guarantees for the non-adaptive scheme and numerical comparisons that suggest the adaptive variant can yield additional speedups. The observation that adaptively averaging weights along the trajectory can improve performance, viewed through the ODE lens, is insightful and potentially impactful.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual framing: The ODE viewpoint and Lyapunov analysis provide a clean, interpretable route to rate guarantees under generalized averaging.
- Sufficient conditions: The paper states explicit conditions on the dual learning rate and weights that can be checked to obtain target rates, which is useful for design.
- Practical angle: The adaptive averaging heuristic is simple and appears to yield improved Lyapunov decay empirically, without substantial algorithmic overhead.
- Empirical support: Numerical results align with the theoretical intuition and show competitive performance against baselines.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Scope and assumptions: It is not entirely clear under which problem classes the guarantees hold (e.g., smooth convex, strongly convex, composite, or constrained). Stating assumptions (smoothness moduli, strong convexity constants, regularity) up front and tying them to the rate statements would improve clarity.
- Novelty relative to prior ODE analyses: There has been substantial prior work connecting continuous-time dynamics and acceleration. The paper would benefit from a tighter positioning versus existing ODE-based analyses of accelerated methods and averaging, clarifying what is genuinely new (e.g., the specific generalized averaging conditions, the adaptive averaging rule, or the Lyapunov construction).
- Discretization gap: The main guarantees seem to be at the ODE level. It would help to state clearly whether the sufficient conditions translate into discrete-time algorithms with matching rates, and under what discretization (e.g., explicit, implicit, or higher-order schemes). Are there stability conditions or step-size restrictions needed to preserve the Lyapunov decrease after discretization?
- Adaptive heuristic theory: While the adaptive averaging speeds up Lyapunov decrease empirically, it is not clear whether it admits theoretical guarantees (e.g., non-asymptotic rates or at least monotonic Lyapunov decrease). Even a weaker statement—such as conditions under which adaptivity does not harm the base rate—would strengthen the contribution.
- Parameter selection: The “dual learning rate” and weight schedules seem critical. Can they be set without knowing problem constants? How sensitive is performance to misspecification? Is there a principled way to tune them online?
- Robustness and stochasticity: The current analysis appears to assume exact gradients. How does the method behave with stochastic or inexact gradients, common in large-scale settings? Is the Lyapunov argument robust to gradient noise or numerical error in integration/discretization?
- Experimental details: The numerical section would be more convincing with additional details on:
  - Which baselines are included (e.g., classical and adaptive accelerated methods, restarts, dual averaging variants).
  - Tuning protocols for all methods to ensure fairness.
  - Ablations isolating the effect of adaptive averaging versus other design choices.
  - Sensitivity plots for key hyperparameters.

Questions for the authors
- Can you formalize the conditions under which the adaptive averaging heuristic preserves the base convergence rate? Is there a simple sufficient condition ensuring that the adaptive rule yields a non-increasing Lyapunov function discretely?
- How do your sufficient conditions compare to those for known accelerated dynamics under standard assumptions (e.g., L-smooth convex vs. μ-strongly convex)? Do you recover the typical O(1/k^2) or linear rates in these regimes?
- What is the precise relationship to dual averaging in discrete time? Can your generalized averaging ODE be viewed as the continuous-time limit of a known dual-averaging-type method?
- Do you have results or empirical evidence for robustness under stochastic gradients? If not, what are the main obstacles to extending the Lyapunov argument to the noisy setting?
- How do the constants in the rate bound depend on the weight schedule and dual learning rate? Are there choices that optimize these constants in practice?

Suggestions for improvement
- State the problem setting and assumptions explicitly, and present a theorem that maps assumptions to rates, with a brief proof sketch highlighting the key Lyapunov construction.
- Add a section discussing discretization: propose at least one concrete discrete algorithm induced by your ODE, state implementable step sizes/weights, and provide a discrete-time convergence guarantee (even if under slightly stronger assumptions).
- Provide intuition for the adaptive rule (e.g., as approximate line-search in the Lyapunov landscape, or as a mechanism to track the “best” averaging schedule), and include a simple sufficient condition for safe adaptation.
- Expand related work to more clearly differentiate from prior ODE-based analyses and adaptive restart schemes, and to situate your contributions relative to dual averaging and momentum methods.
- Strengthen experiments with broader baselines, clear tuning protocols, and ablations. Include failure or sensitivity cases to guide practitioners.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and promising paper. The ODE-based perspective on generalized averaging, the accompanying Lyapunov analysis, and the adaptive averaging heuristic are well-motivated and potentially useful. The main areas for improvement are clarifying the novelty relative to existing ODE analyses, bridging from continuous- to discrete-time guarantees, and providing theory for the adaptive variant. With these additions, the work would make a solid contribution to our understanding and practical deployment of accelerated methods via averaging.
