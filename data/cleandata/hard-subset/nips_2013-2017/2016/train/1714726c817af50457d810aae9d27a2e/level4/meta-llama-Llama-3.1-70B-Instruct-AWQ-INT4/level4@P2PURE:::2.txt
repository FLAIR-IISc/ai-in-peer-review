Building upon earlier work that establishes a connection between accelerated Mirror Descent and continuous-time ODE, the authors propose an extension of the ODE dynamics by incorporating a more general averaging scheme and introduce a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one of its discretizations. In certain scenarios, the new variant of AMD with adaptive averaging outperforms existing AMD algorithms with restarting, and the proposed strategy is intuitively appealing. However, despite the paper's overall coherence, its originality and usefulness remain a concern. 

Most components, such as the Lyapunov function and discretization, are direct consequences of an earlier NIPS paper [7]. The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging appears to be relatively straightforward and may not be particularly useful, as the authors only consider the case with a quadratic rate; exploring faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which are not always applicable. 

Some results, including the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation into primal form, and reduction to replicator dynamics, contribute to a deeper understanding of the accelerated Mirror Descent algorithm. However, these findings are not significantly impactful in advancing the field of machine learning. 

Although the adaptive averaging strategy is introduced to satisfy the conditions necessary for guaranteeing the convergence of the ODE, its theoretical foundation is incomplete due to the approximations made during discretization. Moreover, the authors do not provide explicit convergence results, rendering the strategy a heuristic, as acknowledged by the authors. Furthermore, there exist multiple averaging schemes for Nesterov's accelerated algorithms, and without comparing the proposed adaptive variant to these alternatives, it is unclear whether it offers substantial improvements.

The experimental results on a toy example with only quadratic/linear objectives are not convincing enough to demonstrate the advantages of adaptive averaging, particularly for the general case involving strong convexity.

After reviewing the authors' rebuttal, I have updated my assessment. The authors have committed to including additional higher-dimensional and non-quadratic examples in the revised manuscript, providing a theoretical proof of convergence for the adaptive averaging strategy, implementing adaptive averaging on Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method, and discussing comparisons with other adaptive averaging schemes. I have accordingly revised my scores.
