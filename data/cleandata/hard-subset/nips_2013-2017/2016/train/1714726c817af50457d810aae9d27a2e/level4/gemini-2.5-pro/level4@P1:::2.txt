This paper builds upon previous work connecting accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The authors generalize the corresponding ODE dynamics with a more flexible averaging scheme and, based on this, propose a novel adaptive averaging heuristic for a discrete-time version of the algorithm. The core idea is to adaptively set the averaging weights to accelerate the decrease of a Lyapunov function. Experiments show that this adaptive variant of AMD can outperform existing AMD algorithms that use restarting heuristics.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed adaptive averaging strategy is novel and well-motivated by the goal of decreasing the continuous-time Lyapunov function as quickly as possible.
*   The paper provides several useful interpretations, such as the energy function analogy, the primal-space reformulation, and the connection to replicator dynamics, which contribute to a deeper understanding of accelerated mirror descent.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses concerning its originality, theoretical contributions, and empirical evaluation.

*   **Originality:** The core framework (Lyapunov function, discretization) is adopted directly from the authors' previous work [11]. The main extension—the generalization of the averaging scheme—appears to be an incremental step. Its utility is also limited, as the analysis and experiments ultimately focus only on achieving a quadratic convergence rate. Achieving faster rates would require higher-order methods, which are not always practical.
*   **Significance:** While the paper's interpretations of the energy function and its connection to replicator dynamics are insightful, their broader impact on advancing machine learning methods is not made clear.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated in the continuous-time setting. However, its theoretical justification in the discrete-time setting is incomplete. The paper does not provide a formal convergence proof for the proposed discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1), meaning it remains a heuristic, as the authors acknowledge. Furthermore, the paper fails to compare or contrast the proposed method with other adaptive schemes for Nesterov acceleration, making it difficult to assess its relative contribution.
*   **Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is limited to a low-dimensional toy problem with quadratic and linear objectives. These experiments are not sufficient to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of adaptive averaging, especially for more general or large-scale strongly convex problems common in machine learning.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**

I have read the authors' rebuttal. They have committed to addressing the main concerns raised in this review by making the following additions to the paper:
*   Additional experiments on higher-dimensional and non-quadratic problems.
*   A theoretical proof of convergence for the adaptive averaging algorithm.
*   An implementation of adaptive averaging for Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method.
*   A discussion comparing their method to other adaptive schemes.
