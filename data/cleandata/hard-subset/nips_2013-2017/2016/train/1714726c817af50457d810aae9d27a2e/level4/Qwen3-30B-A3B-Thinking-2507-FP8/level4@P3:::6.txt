

This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, presenting a unified framework for analyzing accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with generalized averaging. The authors extend prior work on continuous-time optimization (Su et al., 2016; Krichene et al., 2016) by introducing a Lyapunov-based analysis that characterizes sufficient conditions on weight functions η(t) and w(t) to achieve desired convergence rates. Their main contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes weights to accelerate convergence while preserving the theoretical guarantees of the original method.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a general framework for AMD dynamics, showing that the replicator dynamics (a mirror descent method on the simplex) can be accelerated using simple averaging. Second, it establishes a theoretical guarantee that their adaptive averaging heuristic preserves the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated methods in discrete time. Third, it demonstrates through numerical experiments that adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics, particularly for strongly convex problems.

Regarding quality, the paper makes a technically sound contribution. The Lyapunov analysis (Theorems 2-3) is rigorous, and the adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated by the desire to minimize the Lyapunov function's derivative. The numerical experiments (Section 5) are well-designed, showing adaptive averaging outperforms gradient restart and speed restart heuristics across multiple objective functions. However, the experiments are limited to R³, which raises questions about scalability to high-dimensional problems common in machine learning applications. The paper would benefit from additional experiments in higher dimensions to better establish practical significance.

The clarity of the paper is mixed. The theoretical framework is well-developed but dense, particularly in Sections 2-3. The connection between the continuous-time formulation and the discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) could be clearer. For instance, the relationship between the continuous-time weight function w(t) and the discrete parameter ak needs more explicit explanation. The supplementary material would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with mirror descent or Bregman divergence. The authors could improve clarity by adding a brief intuitive explanation of the key equation (3) and the rationale behind the adaptive averaging condition (8).

The originality is strong. The paper builds on Krichene et al. (2016) but extends it substantially by introducing generalized averaging and adaptive weights. The connection to replicator dynamics (Section 3.2) is particularly novel, showing how evolutionary game theory concepts can be accelerated using their framework. While the paper references Adagrad and Adam in Section 4, it could better clarify how adaptive averaging differs from these methods. Adagrad and Adam adapt learning rates based on gradient history, while adaptive averaging specifically targets the convergence rate preservation in accelerated methods, making it complementary rather than competitive.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by providing theoretical guarantees for adaptive methods that restarting heuristics lack. The ability to accelerate optimization while preserving convergence rates is valuable for practitioners. However, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the low-dimensional experiments. The authors should address scalability concerns more thoroughly, perhaps by discussing computational complexity or providing a brief analysis of how the method might scale to high dimensions. The connection to replicator dynamics could also be expanded to discuss potential applications in game theory or evolutionary algorithms.

Strengths:
- Theoretical guarantees for adaptive averaging preserving convergence rates
- Novel connection between accelerated optimization and replicator dynamics
- Comprehensive analysis of sufficient conditions for Lyapunov functions
- Strong experimental results showing improvements over restarting heuristics
- Clear presentation of the continuous-time framework

Weaknesses:
- Limited numerical experiments to R³, raising scalability concerns
- Some technical sections (e.g., equations 33-47) lack intuitive explanations
- Insufficient comparison with adaptive methods in non-convex optimization
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges
- The adaptive averaging heuristic's computational overhead compared to restarting methods is not addressed

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS as it makes a significant theoretical contribution to a growing area of continuous-time optimization dynamics. While the experimental validation could be strengthened with higher-dimensional examples, the theoretical advances are compelling. The authors have made a valuable contribution to understanding how to accelerate optimization methods while preserving convergence guarantees.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper should address scalability concerns in the experiments and provide clearer explanations of the key equations. The connection to non-convex adaptive methods (Adagrad, Adam) should be expanded to clarify how this work relates to those approaches. With these improvements, the paper would make an excellent contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
