This paper presents a novel adaptive heuristic for accelerated first-order optimization methods, building upon the continuous-time ordinary differential equation (ODE) interpretation of Nesterov's acceleration. The authors first generalize the accelerated mirror descent dynamics from Krichene et al. (2015) to a broader family of methods parameterized by two time-varying functions: `η(t)` which controls the rate of gradient accumulation, and `w(t)` which defines a generalized averaging scheme. Using a Lyapunov function, they derive sufficient conditions on these functions to achieve a desired convergence rate. The main contribution is an "adaptive averaging" heuristic that dynamically adjusts the averaging weights `w(t)` to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function at each step. This principled approach is shown to preserve the original O(1/t^2) convergence rate, a guarantee not always available for other popular heuristics like adaptive restarting. The authors present a discrete-time version of their algorithm and provide compelling numerical experiments on constrained convex problems, showing that adaptive averaging performs comparably to or significantly better than adaptive restart schemes.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is of high quality. The theoretical development, based on a Lyapunov analysis of the continuous-time dynamics, is technically sound, elegant, and easy to follow. The connection between the conditions on the weight functions and the resulting convergence rate is clearly established. The proposed adaptive heuristic is a natural and well-motivated consequence of this analysis. The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, progressing logically from the general continuous-time framework to the specific adaptive algorithm and its empirical validation.

The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method against the standard non-adaptive accelerated method and two widely-used restarting heuristics across a range of convex objective functions. The visualizations of the trajectories and the Lyapunov/energy functions provide valuable insight into the behavior of the different methods.

One point for improvement concerns the analysis of the discrete-time algorithm. The authors state that a proof of the convergence rate for Algorithm 1 is provided in the supplementary material. While this is acceptable, the guarantee that the heuristic preserves the convergence rate in the discrete setting is a crucial part of the paper's contribution. Including a formal theorem and a brief proof sketch in the main body would significantly strengthen the paper and make it more self-contained.

**Originality and Significance**

While the work is a direct extension of the ODE framework from Krichene et al. (2015), it demonstrates significant originality in its contribution. The novelty lies not in the ODE interpretation itself, but in leveraging that framework to design a new, principled adaptive heuristic. Unlike many ad-hoc restarting schemes, adaptive averaging is derived directly from the goal of optimizing the decrease of a Lyapunov function, which provides a strong theoretical motivation. The guarantee that the convergence rate is preserved is a key differentiator from prior work.

The significance of this work is potentially very high. Nesterov's accelerated gradient method is a cornerstone algorithm in machine learning and large-scale optimization. However, its performance can be sensitive to its parameters and can be hampered by oscillations. Practitioners often resort to heuristics like restarting to improve empirical performance, but these can lack theoretical guarantees. This paper provides a simple, easy-to-implement modification that offers dramatic empirical speedups while provably maintaining the theoretical convergence rate. This provides the community with a more robust, adaptive, and theoretically-grounded version of a fundamental algorithm. New methods are often compared against a fixed-schedule Nesterov method; this work makes a strong case that adaptive variants like this one should serve as a more competitive baseline in future research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Principled Heuristic with Guarantees:** The paper introduces a novel adaptive averaging heuristic derived elegantly from a Lyapunov analysis. Crucially, it is guaranteed to preserve the O(1/t^2) convergence rate of the underlying accelerated method.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The numerical experiments convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method is competitive with, and in several cases significantly outperforms, popular adaptive restarting heuristics.
3.  **High Potential Impact:** Given the ubiquity of Nesterov's method, a simple modification that reliably improves its performance is a significant contribution that will be valuable to a wide audience of researchers and practitioners.
4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making a sophisticated argument clear and accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance / Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The primary weakness is that the technical framework is an incremental development of prior work (Krichene et al., 2015). However, the application of the framework to derive a new and effective adaptive method is a valuable contribution in its own right.
2.  **Discrete-Time Analysis:** As mentioned, the analysis of the practical, discrete-time algorithm should be more prominent in the main paper to fully substantiate the claims.
3.  **Connection to Other Methods:** For further insight, it would be interesting if the authors could comment on the potential connections between their adaptive averaging scheme and classical optimization methods like the Conjugate Gradient (CG) algorithm, particularly for the case of strongly convex quadratic optimization where CG is optimal.

Overall, this is an excellent paper. The strengths, particularly the high potential impact and strong results, far outweigh the minor weakness of its incremental nature. I strongly recommend acceptance.
