

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending the connection between continuous-time dynamics and accelerated first-order methods. The authors present a generalized family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), which they analyze using Lyapunov functions. They establish sufficient conditions on these weight functions to guarantee a desired convergence rate, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights to preserve the convergence rate while improving empirical performance. The work builds upon prior NIPS contributions by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) and Su et al. (NIPS 2014), extending their continuous-time analysis to a broader class of averaging schemes and providing a theoretically grounded adaptive heuristic.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a unified Lyapunov analysis for a broad class of accelerated dynamics, generalizing previous results on continuous-time acceleration. The sufficient conditions for convergence (Theorem 2) are well-motivated and technically sound, with clear connections to existing work on Nesterov's methods. Second, the authors demonstrate how the replicator dynamics (a fundamental ODE in evolutionary game theory) can be accelerated through their averaging scheme, establishing an interesting connection between optimization and game theory. Third, the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is theoretically grounded (it preserves the Lyapunov function and convergence rate) and empirically outperforms existing restarting heuristics in the experiments.

The paper is strong in quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful attention to conditions for existence and uniqueness of solutions (Theorem 1). The Lyapunov function approach is well-executed and provides clear convergence guarantees. The experimental section is well-designed, with a thoughtful comparison against relevant baselines (gradient and speed restarting heuristics). The numerical results clearly demonstrate that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as restarting methods, with significant improvements in some cases.

The paper is somewhat challenging to follow due to its dense mathematical presentation. Some key equations (particularly those in Section 2 relating to the Lyapunov function) would benefit from more intuitive explanations. The connection to adaptive methods used in non-convex optimization (such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]) is not adequately addressed, despite being mentioned in the paper. The authors note that adaptive averaging is "not designed for accelerated methods" but do not explain why their approach differs from these existing adaptive methods.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the experimental scope. The numerical experiments are conducted in R³, which is a highly constrained setting that may not reflect real-world optimization challenges. The paper does not address scalability to high-dimensional problems, which is a critical consideration for practical applications in machine learning. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the practical utility of the method remains unclear without evidence of performance on larger-scale problems.

The paper's originality is solid. The generalized averaging scheme extends previous work on accelerated mirror descent, and the adaptive heuristic provides a theoretically grounded alternative to existing heuristic restart approaches. The connection to replicator dynamics for the simplex constraint is novel and well-motivated.

Strengths:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees
- Theoretical grounding of the adaptive averaging heuristic
- Interesting connection to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory
- Comprehensive comparison with existing heuristics in experiments
- Well-executed mathematical proofs and derivations

Weaknesses:
- Limited experimental scope (R³ only, no high-dimensional testing)
- Insufficient explanation of connection to adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam
- Dense mathematical presentation that could benefit from more intuitive explanations
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations
- Ambiguous reference to "solid line" in Figure 1 (should use different line styles or colors)

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization dynamics, particularly in the constrained setting. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a theoretically sound approach that preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance. However, the lack of high-dimensional experiments significantly limits the practical significance of the work.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the following points:
1. Clarify the relationship between adaptive averaging and existing adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam
2. Provide more intuitive explanations for key mathematical constructs
3. Expand the experimental section to include high-dimensional problems
4. Revise Figure 1 to eliminate ambiguous line references
5. Discuss practical implementation considerations more thoroughly

The paper advances our understanding of accelerated optimization dynamics and provides a theoretically grounded heuristic with clear practical benefits. While the experimental scope is limited, the theoretical contributions are substantial and well-executed.
