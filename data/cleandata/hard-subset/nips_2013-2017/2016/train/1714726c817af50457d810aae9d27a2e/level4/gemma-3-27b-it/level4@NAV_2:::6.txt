This paper investigates adaptive averaging in accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization. The core idea is to couple a dual variable accumulating gradients with a primal variable obtained as a weighted average of the dual trajectory, and to adaptively compute these weights to accelerate convergence. The authors provide sufficient conditions for convergence and demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that their adaptive averaging heuristic preserves the quadratic convergence rate, a benefit over existing restarting heuristics, particularly when dealing with non-strongly convex objectives.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the connection between Nesterov’s accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs established in prior work [20, 2, 11]. The theoretical analysis, based on a Lyapunov argument, is rigorous and clearly presented. The introduction effectively contextualizes the work within the broader landscape of accelerated optimization methods. A significant strength lies in the preservation of the convergence rate guarantee, a notable contribution given the empirical nature of many existing acceleration heuristics. The connection to replicator dynamics and its acceleration provides a concrete example and highlights potential applications in evolutionary game theory [22]. The numerical experiments, while limited to R^3, provide initial validation of the proposed heuristic and demonstrate competitive performance against adaptive restarting [20].

However, the paper's technical depth may present a barrier to entry for readers less familiar with the intricacies of mirror descent and Lyapunov analysis. While the authors likely target a specialized audience, some expansion on foundational concepts could broaden accessibility. The equations surrounding lines 33-34 and 46-47, defining the dynamics, could benefit from more intuitive explanations to aid understanding. Furthermore, a discussion of the relationship between this adaptive averaging approach and existing adaptive methods in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad [7] and Adam [10], would be valuable. While the authors note the differences, a more detailed comparison could clarify the unique contributions of their work. Finally, while the experiments are well-executed, the limitation to R^3 raises questions about scalability. A discussion of the expected performance and challenges in higher dimensions would strengthen the paper. The reference to the "solid line" in Figure 1 is indeed ambiguous and should be clarified, perhaps using distinct line styles or colors.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The Lyapunov-based analysis provides strong guarantees on convergence.
*   **Novelty:** The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a principled approach to acceleration with a provable convergence rate.
*   **Significance:** Preserving the convergence rate in the presence of adaptation is a valuable contribution.
*   **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively builds upon and extends previous research in accelerated optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Accessibility:** The technical depth may limit readership.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The limited scope of the numerical experiments raises questions about performance in high-dimensional settings.
*   **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The connection to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization could be explored more thoroughly.

Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, and the adaptive averaging heuristic offers a promising approach to improving convergence. While some aspects could be improved to enhance accessibility and address scalability concerns, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
