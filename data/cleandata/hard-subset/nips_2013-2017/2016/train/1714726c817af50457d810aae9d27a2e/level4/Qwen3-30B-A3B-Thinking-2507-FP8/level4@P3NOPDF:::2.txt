

The paper extends recent work on accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) by introducing a generalized averaging scheme and a novel adaptive averaging strategy for its discretization. While the adaptive strategy is intuitively appealing and shows empirical improvements over restarted AMD in specific cases, the contribution faces significant concerns regarding originality, theoretical grounding, and practical relevance.  

First, the core technical extension—generalizing simple averaging to a broader scheme—is largely trivial. The paper builds directly on prior NIPS work [7], with most elements (Lyapunov functions, discretization, convergence analysis) inherited from that foundation. The extension to generalized averaging is only meaningful in the quadratic-rate setting, which is already well-covered by existing AMD variants. Crucially, the authors do not address scenarios requiring faster rates (e.g., non-quadratic objectives), where higher-order methods would be necessary but are explicitly acknowledged as "not always applicable." This limits the practical significance of the extension.  

Second, the theoretical justification for the adaptive averaging strategy is incomplete. The authors frame it as satisfying conditions for ODE convergence, but the discretization approximation introduces unaddressed gaps. The paper lacks an explicit convergence proof for the discrete adaptive variant—despite the authors’ own admission that it functions as a "heuristic." Without rigorous guarantees, the strategy’s theoretical foundation remains weak. Furthermore, the paper fails to compare the adaptive scheme against existing alternatives in the literature (e.g., other adaptive averaging approaches for Nesterov-type methods), making it unclear whether the proposed method offers a meaningful advantage.  

Third, the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claimed benefits. The only non-trivial example uses a quadratic objective, which is precisely the regime where the extension is least novel (as the quadratic-rate case is already covered by prior work). The absence of experiments on non-quadratic, high-dimensional problems—especially those with strong convexity, which the paper claims to address—renders the empirical results unconvincing. A "toy example" with linear/quadratic objectives cannot demonstrate the strategy’s value for the broader class of problems where acceleration matters most.  

While the paper’s reformulation of the energy function and connection to replicator dynamics offer incremental insights into AMD’s mechanics, these contributions do not advance core machine learning challenges. They are descriptive rather than prescriptive, failing to provide new algorithmic tools or practical improvements for real-world applications.  

**Post-rebuttal update:** The authors’ rebuttal addresses key concerns by promising to: (1) include higher-dimensional and non-quadratic examples; (2) provide a theoretical convergence proof for adaptive averaging; (3) implement the strategy on Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method; and (4) compare against alternative averaging schemes. These additions would substantially strengthen the paper’s technical contribution and empirical validity.  

**Recommendation:** The paper currently lacks sufficient novelty and theoretical rigor to justify publication. However, with the promised revisions—particularly the convergence proof and expanded experiments—the work could become a valuable contribution. I recommend **weak acceptance** contingent on the authors delivering all promised revisions in the final version. Without these, the marginal novelty and unresolved theoretical gaps would remain problematic. The current version is not compelling enough for acceptance, but the authors’ engagement with the critique suggests a path to improvement.
