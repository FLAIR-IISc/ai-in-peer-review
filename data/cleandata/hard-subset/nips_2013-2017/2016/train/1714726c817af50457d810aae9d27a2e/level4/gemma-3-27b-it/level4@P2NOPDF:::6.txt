This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate constrained convex optimization problems. A key strength of the proposed method is its theoretical guarantee of maintaining the original convergence rate even when dealing with non-strongly convex objective functions – a notable improvement over existing restarting heuristics, as demonstrated both theoretically and empirically.

While I found the core ideas compelling, I confess some difficulty fully engaging with the technical details due to the work falling somewhat outside my primary area of expertise. I assume the authors’ use of specific abbreviations and terminology is appropriate for the intended audience. However, including additional background information or clarifying definitions would enhance accessibility for a broader readership. 

Specifically, the equations presented between lines 33-34 and 46-47 would benefit from more intuitive explanations to aid understanding.  Beyond the immediate scope of this work, I am curious about its relationship to adaptive averaging techniques utilized in the non-convex optimization landscape, such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2].  Exploring these connections could provide valuable insights and potentially broaden the applicability of the presented method.

My primary concern regards the practical impact of this work. The experimental evaluation is currently limited to problems in R<sup>3</sup>, which feels somewhat simplistic.  A crucial question remains: how well does this method – and the related work it builds upon – scale to significantly higher dimensional problems, more representative of real-world applications? Addressing this scalability question would substantially strengthen the paper’s contribution.

Finally, a minor stylistic point: the reference to the “solid line” in Figure 1 is ambiguous. I suggest using a different line style, such as a dotted line, or employing distinct colors to more clearly illustrate the trajectory of the primal variable.
