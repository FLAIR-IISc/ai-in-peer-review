This paper presents an accelerated continuous-time dynamic for solving constrained convex optimization problems. The authors utilize a Lyapunov function-based analysis to formally establish the convergence rate of their proposed method. The core contribution is a novel adaptive averaging heuristic, which is designed to practically accelerate convergence by monitoring the evolution of the Lyapunov function. The empirical results presented suggest that this adaptive averaging scheme performs comparably to, and in some cases better than, the well-known adaptive restarting technique.

While my expertise is not directly in the analysis of continuous-time dynamics for optimization, I found the central idea of using an adaptive averaging scheme to be both interesting and promising. The paper makes a solid contribution to the literature on acceleration methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging scheme is a novel and intuitive approach for accelerating convergence. It offers a compelling alternative to adaptive restarting, which is a more commonly studied technique. The connection between the decay rate of the Lyapunov function and the averaging strategy is elegant.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The use of a Lyapunov function provides a rigorous theoretical underpinning for the algorithm's convergence properties. This analysis adds significant weight to the paper's claims.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are encouraging. By benchmarking against adaptive restarting, the authors provide a relevant and strong baseline for comparison. The finding that adaptive averaging is competitive or superior is a significant result.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

*   **Intuition Behind Averaging:** While the Lyapunov analysis provides the theoretical guarantee, the paper could benefit from a more detailed intuitive explanation of *why* the adaptive averaging scheme is effective. A clearer discussion of how this method estimates and adapts to the local convergence behavior, and how that conceptually differs from a "restart," would improve the paper's clarity and impact for a broader audience.
*   **Comparison to Restarting:** The paper demonstrates empirically that averaging can outperform restarting. It would be valuable to add a more in-depth discussion on the conceptual advantages and disadvantages of each approach. Are there specific problem structures or scenarios where one would be theoretically preferred over the other? For example, is one more robust to noise or misspecification of parameters?
*   **Scope and Limitations:** The analysis is focused on constrained convex optimization. It would be helpful for the authors to discuss the potential limitations of their approach. For instance, how does the performance depend on the geometry of the constraint set? Could the framework be extended to other problem classes, such as non-smooth or stochastic optimization, and what would be the primary challenges in doing so?

Overall, this is a well-motivated paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is promising, and the results are supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence. Addressing the points above, particularly regarding the intuition and a deeper comparison with restarting, would further strengthen this work.
