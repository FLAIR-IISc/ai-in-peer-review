This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic to improve the practical convergence speed of accelerated first-order methods. The work builds directly on the continuous-time ordinary differential equation (ODE) framework for accelerated mirror descent, previously established in Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The authors propose a generalized averaging scheme where the primal iterate is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory. The key contribution is an adaptive strategy for choosing the averaging weights to greedily decrease a known Lyapunov function, which in turn accelerates convergence. The authors provide sufficient conditions for this scheme to preserve the O(1/t^2) convergence rate in continuous time. A discrete-time algorithm is then proposed and empirically evaluated against standard acceleration and popular adaptive restarting heuristics.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a highly important problem. Nesterov's accelerated methods are foundational in modern optimization and machine learning, and developing simple, effective, and theoretically-grounded heuristics to improve their practical performance is a significant contribution. The proposed method shows potential for wide adoption.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical development is sound and based on an elegant Lyapunov argument. The central idea of using the time derivative of the Lyapunov function to guide the adaptation of the averaging weights is intuitive and principled. Crucially, this approach guarantees that the accelerated convergence rate is preserved, a property that is not guaranteed for many other adaptive heuristics. The empirical evaluation is convincing, demonstrating that adaptive averaging performs favorably against established restarting schemes across several function types.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is well-written and logically organized. The motivation for generalizing the averaging scheme is clear, and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow. The connection between the continuous-time theory and the proposed discrete-time algorithm is well-motivated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Originality:** The primary weakness is that the technical framework is a direct and incremental extension of the one presented in Krichene et al. [11]. The core ODE formulation and the structure of the Lyapunov function are adopted from this prior work. However, the specific idea of an adaptive averaging scheme guided by the Lyapunov function's derivative is novel and provides a new perspective on designing adaptive accelerated algorithms.
*   **Analysis of the Discretized Algorithm:** The paper states that the convergence rate proof for the discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) is provided in the supplementary material. This is a critical result for validating the practical algorithm. The main paper would be significantly strengthened by including a proof sketch or a more detailed discussion of this analysis, as it bridges the gap between the continuous-time theory and the implemented method.
*   **Contextualization:** The paper could be improved by discussing the relationship between the proposed adaptive scheme and other classical optimization methods. For instance, for the specific case of strongly convex quadratic optimization, how does this adaptive averaging technique relate to the Conjugate Gradient method, which is known to be optimal in that setting? Providing such connections would help to better position the contribution within the broader optimization landscape.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper introduces a simple, principled, and effective heuristic for a widely used class of optimization algorithms. The method is supported by a solid theoretical analysis in continuous time and compelling empirical results that show clear improvements over other common heuristics. The work has a high potential for practical impact.
*   **Arguments Against Acceptance:** The technical novelty is somewhat limited, as the work is a direct extension of a previously published framework.

In summary, the paper's strengths in terms of significance, quality, and potential impact are substantial and appear to outweigh the limited scope of its novelty.
