

The paper extends Krichene et al.'s NIPS 2015 work on continuous-time accelerated mirror descent to propose an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerating constrained convex optimization. The authors formalize a broad class of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions η(t) and w(t), establishing sufficient conditions for quadratic convergence rates via Lyapunov analysis. Crucially, they demonstrate that their adaptive averaging heuristic—which dynamically adjusts weights to minimize the Lyapunov function's time derivative—preserves the 1/t² convergence rate while outperforming existing restarting heuristics in experiments across simplex-constrained problems (including accelerated replicator dynamics for evolutionary game theory).  

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The continuous-time analysis is technically sound, with rigorous Lyapunov arguments (Theorems 2–3) establishing convergence guarantees under minimal assumptions. The adaptive heuristic is well-motivated and provably preserves the quadratic rate, addressing a key gap in prior work where restarting heuristics often lack theoretical guarantees for non-strongly convex objectives. The empirical comparison (Section 5) convincingly shows adaptive averaging outperforms gradient/speed restarting across diverse objectives (strongly convex quadratics, linear functions, KL divergence), with significant speedups in the strongly convex case.  
*Clarity:* The exposition is exceptionally clear, building logically from continuous-time dynamics to primal representations and concrete examples (e.g., simplex-constrained replicator dynamics in Section 3). The connection between adaptive weights and trajectory progress (Section 4) is intuitively explained, and the discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-justified.  
*Originality:* While extending Krichene et al. (2015), the adaptive averaging mechanism is novel—it provides a theoretically grounded alternative to heuristic restarting, directly addressing a practical limitation in Nesterov acceleration. The replicator dynamics acceleration (Section 3.2) offers a fresh application in evolutionary game theory, distinct from prior NIPS work.  
*Significance:* Nesterov acceleration is ubiquitous in machine learning; this work demonstrates a simple yet powerful modification to enhance its practical efficacy. The paper resolves a common empirical shortcoming: many studies compare new methods against fixed-schedule Nesterov acceleration, ignoring adaptive improvements. The adaptive heuristic requires only minor changes to existing implementations but yields substantial gains, with broad applicability beyond the tested simplex-constrained problems.  

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**  
The primary technical gap is the lack of discrete-time convergence rate analysis. While the continuous-time guarantees are rigorous, the paper’s practical impact hinges on preserving the rate in discrete implementations (e.g., Algorithm 1). The authors note this in the supplementary material but do not provide a self-contained proof. *Recommendation:* Include a concise discrete Lyapunov argument (e.g., adapting the continuous-time bound in Theorem 2) to confirm that adaptive averaging preserves the 1/t² rate in discrete time. This would strengthen the quality assessment and address a critical reviewer concern.  

The discussion of connections to Conjugate Gradient (CG) for strongly convex quadratics (mentioned in the draft) is underdeveloped. While CG and adaptive averaging both exploit problem structure, their mechanisms differ: CG uses exact Hessian information, whereas adaptive averaging relies on trajectory-based weight adaptation. *Recommendation:* Briefly clarify this distinction or omit the reference to avoid misleading comparisons.  

The paper could better emphasize *why* adaptive averaging dominates restarting heuristics. The experiments show superior performance, but the theoretical basis (Section 4) should explicitly link the Lyapunov decay condition to the absence of oscillations in trajectories (Figure 2), contrasting with restarting’s "velocity reset" approach. This would deepen the significance argument.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a high-quality, impactful contribution that advances both theory and practice of accelerated optimization. The technical depth is appropriate for NIPS, the empirical validation is thorough, and the problem addressed (practical acceleration of Nesterov methods) is highly relevant. The minor omission of discrete-rate analysis is the only barrier to acceptance, and it is readily addressable. The work exemplifies how continuous-time insights can yield practical, theoretically grounded improvements to widely used methods—exactly the kind of contribution NIPS seeks.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper’s significance is amplified by its accessibility: the adaptive heuristic requires minimal implementation changes yet delivers tangible gains, making it immediately useful to practitioners. The connection to replicator dynamics further broadens its appeal beyond standard optimization. With the discrete-rate analysis added, this will be a valuable addition to the NIPS 2016 proceedings.
