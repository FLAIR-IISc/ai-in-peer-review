This paper studies accelerated mirror descent through the lens of continuous-time ordinary differential equations (ODEs). Building on previous work that connected Nesterov-style acceleration to a specific ODE with a weighted averaging scheme [11], this work generalizes that framework to a broader family of accelerated dynamics. These dynamics are parameterized by two functions: one for the gradient accumulation rate, η(t), and another for the averaging weights, w(t). The authors employ a Lyapunov function to derive sufficient conditions on η(t) and w(t) to guarantee a desired convergence rate. The main contribution is a novel adaptive averaging heuristic derived from this analysis, which adaptively tunes the weights to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function. The authors propose a discretization of this heuristic (Algorithm 1) and present numerical experiments comparing it to standard restarting heuristics on several constrained convex problems.

**Strengths of the paper:**
*   The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and offers an elegant, intuitive alternative to the more common restarting schemes. Its motivation, which is to greedily decrease the continuous-time Lyapunov function, is well-founded in the theoretical analysis.
*   The paper provides valuable theoretical insights that enhance the understanding of accelerated mirror descent. The primal-space representation of the dynamics and the explicit connection drawn to the replicator dynamics for optimization on the probability simplex are particularly insightful.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The theoretical development is easy to follow, and the motivation for the proposed heuristic is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses of the paper:**
*   **Originality:** The paper's core theoretical framework, including the form of the Lyapunov function and the overall analytical approach, is a direct extension of the authors' prior work [11]. The generalization from a specific averaging scheme (w(t) ∝ t^(β-1)) to a general function w(t) feels like an incremental, though natural, step.
*   **Significance:** While the generalized ODE framework is interesting, its practical utility is not fully demonstrated. The analysis and experiments ultimately focus on achieving the standard O(1/t^2) convergence rate. The potential for achieving faster rates, which the framework theoretically allows, is mentioned but not explored, as it would require higher-order methods. Similarly, while the connections to other dynamics are elegant, their immediate impact on advancing machine learning methods is not clearly established.
*   **Quality of Theoretical Claims:** The adaptive heuristic is rigorously motivated for the continuous-time ODE, ensuring the Lyapunov function is non-increasing. However, a formal proof of convergence for the proposed discrete-time algorithm is not provided in the main text. Without a formal guarantee on its convergence rate, the proposed algorithm remains a heuristic, similar in theoretical standing to the restarting schemes it is compared against.
*   **Quality of Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is limited to low-dimensional (R^3) toy problems with simple objective functions (quadratic, linear, KL-divergence). While these experiments serve as a good proof of concept, they are not convincing enough to demonstrate a clear advantage for large-scale, practical machine learning tasks. A more compelling evaluation would include higher-dimensional problems and a comparison on strongly convex objectives, where restarting schemes are known to perform well.
*   **Context and Comparisons:** The paper's comparison is limited to restarting heuristics. The literature contains other adaptive schemes and alternative averaging strategies for Nesterov's accelerated methods. A discussion and comparison against a broader set of related techniques would better contextualize the contribution and clarify the specific advantages of adaptive averaging.
