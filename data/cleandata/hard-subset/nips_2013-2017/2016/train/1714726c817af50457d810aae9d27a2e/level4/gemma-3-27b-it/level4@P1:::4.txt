This paper studies a class of ODEs describing accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, featuring a generalized averaging scheme. Using a Lyapunov argument, the authors provide sufficient conditions on the dual learning rate and the averaging weights to achieve a desired convergence rate. Furthermore, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function, accompanied by theoretical guarantees and numerical comparisons. This work builds on a long line of research aiming to empirically speed up the convergence of accelerated methods. A key contribution is the observation, made through the lens of ODEs, that adaptively averaging the weights along the solution trajectories can also achieve acceleration. I found this approach particularly interesting.

The paper begins by framing the problem of minimizing a convex function over a closed convex set, assuming differentiability and a Lipschitz constant for the gradient. It motivates the study of continuous-time dynamics as a valuable tool for understanding and designing optimization algorithms, referencing previous work connecting Nesterov’s accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs [6, 9, 5, 20, 2, 1, 3, 11, 23, 14, 15]. The authors appropriately highlight the benefits of analyzing continuous-time dynamics – simpler analysis, guidance for algorithm design, and improved intuition. They build on their previous work [11] to formulate Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics as a coupled system of dual and primal variables.

The core contribution lies in analyzing AMD dynamics parameterized by weight functions *w* and *η*. The authors present sufficient conditions, based on a Lyapunov function, to guarantee a quadratic convergence rate. They then introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate the decrease of this Lyapunov function, providing guarantees on its performance in continuous and discrete time. This is followed by a primal representation of the dynamics and an illustrative example applying the approach to the replicator dynamics on the probability simplex. Finally, the paper presents numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging to existing restarting heuristics [17, 20].

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The theoretical analysis is sound and the Lyapunov argument is well-motivated. The connection between acceleration and averaging is clearly articulated. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel contribution and the provided convergence guarantees are valuable. The numerical experiments offer compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness, demonstrating performance comparable to or exceeding that of adaptive restarting. The paper is well-structured and provides sufficient detail for reproducing the results. The authors also acknowledge and relate their work to existing literature in adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam [7, 10], correctly noting their different focus.

However, there are a few points for consideration. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the conditions for guaranteeing convergence might be somewhat restrictive in practice. Further exploration of the robustness of the adaptive averaging heuristic to variations in these conditions would be beneficial. The numerical experiments are limited to a few examples; expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of problems and datasets would strengthen the claims. Additionally, the discussion of related work could be slightly expanded to include recent advances in the analysis of accelerated methods, such as the work by Attouch et al. []. While the paper mentions the theoretical convergence rate, a more detailed discussion on the implications of the choice of *r(t)* and its impact on the discretization scheme would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to accelerating mirror descent.
*   Clear and well-motivated Lyapunov analysis.
*   Promising numerical results demonstrating comparable or superior performance to existing heuristics.
*   Significant contribution to the understanding of the relationship between averaging and acceleration in optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Convergence guarantees might be restrictive.
*   Limited scope of numerical experiments.
*   The link to recent advancements in accelerated methods analysis could be more explicit.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The authors present a novel and theoretically grounded approach to accelerating mirror descent, supported by compelling numerical evidence. Despite a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh its shortcomings. I recommend acceptance.
