

This paper introduces a generalized framework for accelerated descent dynamics in constrained convex optimization, using a continuous-time perspective to analyze and improve upon existing accelerated methods. The authors study a family of ODEs they call "Accelerated Mirror Descent" (AMD), parameterized by two weight functions w and η that govern the dual variable's gradient accumulation rate and the primal variable's averaging scheme, respectively. They provide a unified Lyapunov analysis that establishes sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate, generalizing previous results by Su et al. (2014) and Krichene et al. (2015) on continuous-time accelerated methods.

The authors demonstrate that their framework can accelerate the replicator dynamics (a key ODE in evolutionary game theory) by applying an appropriate averaging scheme. They then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes the weights w(t) to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function while preserving the convergence rate guarantee. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the continuous-time case, proves that the adaptive method preserves the quadratic convergence rate of discrete accelerated methods, and includes numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging with existing heuristics like adaptive restarting.

The paper's strengths are substantial. It makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a unified Lyapunov analysis covering a broad class of accelerated methods, including special cases from previous work. The sufficient conditions derived are general enough to encompass prior results while offering new insights into the role of averaging in acceleration. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, as it directly targets the decrease of the Lyapunov function. The paper provides strong experimental evidence showing that adaptive averaging outperforms existing heuristics across multiple test cases, particularly for strongly convex problems.

The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with convex optimization. The connection between continuous-time analysis and discrete algorithms is well-explained, and the numerical experiments are well-designed and clearly presented. The supplementary material enhances clarity with additional details on mirror maps and primal representations.

In terms of originality, the paper builds upon recent work on continuous-time interpretations of accelerated methods (Su et al., 2014; Krichene et al., 2015), but makes a key conceptual contribution by generalizing the averaging scheme and providing a principled adaptive heuristic. While adaptive methods have been explored in other contexts (e.g., Adagrad, Adam), the authors clearly distinguish their approach as specifically designed for accelerated methods, which is a novel contribution.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses an important practical problem: how to empirically speed up the convergence of accelerated optimization methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a theoretically sound alternative to existing restarting heuristics, which lack strong convergence guarantees for non-strongly convex problems. The numerical results suggest adaptive averaging can provide significant improvements, particularly for strongly convex problems, making this work valuable to practitioners implementing accelerated methods in constrained optimization settings.

A minor limitation is that the theoretical guarantees are primarily for continuous-time dynamics, though the authors do provide a discrete-time algorithm and experimental results. The paper could have provided more detailed analysis of the discrete-time convergence rate for the adaptive method, but this is a minor point given the strong empirical evidence presented.

Another consideration is that the numerical experiments focus primarily on simplex-constrained problems, which is reasonable given the connection to replicator dynamics. However, additional examples with different constraint sets would further demonstrate the broader applicability of their method.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods through a continuous-time lens. It provides both theoretical insights and practical improvements that advance the state of the art in accelerated optimization. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and well-executed, with clear connections to previous work and a compelling experimental evaluation.

Strengths:
- Unified theoretical framework for accelerated descent dynamics
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantees
- Strong experimental results demonstrating advantages over existing methods
- Clear writing and well-organized presentation
- Good connection between continuous-time analysis and discrete algorithms

Weaknesses:
- Limited theoretical analysis of the discrete-time adaptive method
- Experiments primarily focused on simplex-constrained problems

Recommendation: Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization, with clear theoretical insights and practical improvements to accelerated methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic is both theoretically grounded and empirically effective, making it a valuable addition to NIPS.
