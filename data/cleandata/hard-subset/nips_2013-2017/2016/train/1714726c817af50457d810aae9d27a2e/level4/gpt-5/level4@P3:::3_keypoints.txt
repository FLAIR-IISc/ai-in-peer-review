- Paper studies accelerated mirror descent ODEs via a unified “averaging” view, deriving Lyapunov conditions that yield convergence rate 1/r(t).
- Builds on prior continuous-time acceleration work (Krichene et al., Su–Boyd–Candès, Wibisono et al.) and clarifies averaging’s role (cf. Flammarion–Bach).
- Presents primal representations and examples (positive orthant, simplex) including an accelerated replicator dynamics obtained by averaging.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that locally reduces the Lyapunov function while preserving the target 1/r(t) rate; continuous-time guarantees provided.
- Empirical comparison shows adaptive averaging competitive with or better than adaptive restarting heuristics and yields monotone Lyapunov decrease in discrete runs.
- Continuous-time theory is rigorous: clear Lyapunov construction, flexible sufficient conditions (Theorem 2), and useful corollary η = w r/W with w/W ≥ r′/r.
- Primal formulations and examples are correct and illuminating for constrained/mirror settings.
- Discrete-time analysis is only sketched and deferred to the supplement; a formal main-text statement (assumptions, theorem, proof sketch) is needed.
- Minor technical typo noted: for r(t)=t^2, a(t) should be β/t (β ≥ 2), not β t; subsequent formulas align with β/t.
- Clarity suggestions: explicitly summarize discrete-time results, clarify t0 selection and initialization effects, and fix notational inconsistencies in Algorithm 1.
- Bibliography needs completion and stronger explicit placement of Attouch–Peypouquet and related inertial/Lyapunov literature.
- Originality: generalized averaging perspective and provable adaptive averaging are novel; acceleration of replicator dynamics is a nice, accessible example.
- Significance: offers a flexible template for designing accelerated methods under constraints and mirror maps, with potential practical impact if discrete-time theory is strengthened.
- Strengths include a unifying Lyapunov framework, general sufficient conditions allowing various rates, provable adaptive heuristic, and instructive primal examples.
- Weaknesses: main-text discrete-time theory is underemphasized; experiments are small-scale and limited to 3D simplex; computational overhead of adaptive averaging vs. restarts is not discussed.
- Suggested extensions: add higher-dimensional and more varied feasible-set experiments, clarify overhead, relate conditions to Wibisono et al.’s variational parameters, and investigate discrete-time linear rates under strong convexity.
- Arguments for acceptance: solid continuous-time theory, novel adaptive heuristic with practical benefits, and broad applicability to constrained/mirror settings.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient discrete-time guarantees in the main text, limited experimental evaluation, and minor clarity/notation/reference issues.
- Overall: interesting and carefully executed with valuable theoretical insights; acceptance recommended conditional on strengthening discrete-time analysis and broadening experiments.
