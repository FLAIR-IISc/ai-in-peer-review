This paper builds on prior work establishing a connection between accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) and continuous-time Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs). The authors extend this framework by introducing a generalized averaging scheme within the ODE dynamics and, crucially, propose a novel adaptive averaging strategy for discretization. While this adaptive variant of AMD demonstrates performance improvements over existing AMD algorithms with restarting in certain cases, the paper’s overall originality and practical impact are questionable.

The core contributions feel incremental. The work largely follows directly from a foundational NIPS paper [7], with the generalization from simple to generalized averaging offering limited benefit. The authors ultimately focus solely on quadratic convergence rates; achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which may not be universally applicable. Several results—including the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation into a primal form, and reduction to replicator dynamics—contribute to a deeper understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent, but these insights do not represent a significant advancement for the machine learning field.

A key motivation for the adaptive averaging strategy is to satisfy conditions sufficient for ODE convergence. However, the theoretical foundation remains incomplete due to approximations introduced during discretization. Critically, the paper lacks an explicit convergence result for the adaptive scheme itself, rendering it largely heuristic, as acknowledged by the authors. Furthermore, numerous averaging schemes already exist for Nesterov's accelerated algorithms; without a comparative analysis against these alternatives, the value and potential benefits of this specific adaptive variant remain unclear.

The experimental evaluation, limited to a toy example with quadratic or linear objectives, is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of adaptive averaging, especially in the context of strongly convex, general optimization problems.
