This paper builds upon a previous work [7] that establishes a connection between accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) and continuous-time ordinary differential equations (ODEs). The authors extend this framework by introducing a more general averaging scheme for the ODE dynamics and propose a novel adaptive averaging strategy for a specific discretization of this ODE. The resulting algorithm is presented as a new variant of AMD that, in some cases, can outperform existing AMD algorithms that rely on restarting.

The primary strength of this paper lies in the novel adaptive averaging strategy, which is well-motivated and intuitive. The connection to the continuous-time dynamics provides a clear rationale for its design. Additionally, the paper offers several valuable interpretations—such as the reformulation into a primal form, the connection to replicator dynamics, and the analysis of the energy function—that contribute to a deeper understanding of AMD.

Despite these positive aspects, the paper has several weaknesses concerning its originality, theoretical grounding, and empirical evaluation.

1.  **Limited Originality and Contribution:** The overall originality of the work is a significant concern. The core theoretical framework, including the Lyapunov function and the discretization method, is heavily borrowed from the preceding work [7]. The main theoretical extension—generalizing the simple averaging scheme—feels incremental, particularly since the analysis and experiments ultimately focus on the quadratic rate. Achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which have limited applicability, thus questioning the practical utility of the more general formulation. While the paper provides interpretations that enhance understanding, these insights do not appear to be significant enough to represent a major advance for the machine learning community.

2.  **Incomplete Theoretical Guarantees:** The theoretical grounding for the proposed adaptive averaging scheme is incomplete. While the strategy is motivated by conditions that ensure the convergence of the continuous-time ODE, the authors do not provide a proof that these properties hold for the discretized algorithm. The approximations introduced during discretization create a gap between the continuous-time intuition and the discrete-time implementation. As the authors themselves acknowledge, the adaptive scheme remains a heuristic without a formal convergence guarantee in its current form.

3.  **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation and Comparison:** The empirical evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method. The experiments are limited to a toy example with quadratic and linear objectives, which may not be representative of more complex machine learning problems. The performance on general strongly convex objectives, a key setting for accelerated methods, is not explored. Furthermore, the paper misses a crucial comparison against other adaptive or heuristic averaging schemes for Nesterov's accelerated methods. Without this context, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed adaptive variant offers a significant improvement over existing alternatives.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**

I have read the authors' rebuttal. The authors have committed to addressing the main weaknesses identified in this review by including the following in the final version:

1.  Additional experiments on higher-dimensional and non-quadratic problems.
2.  A theoretical proof of convergence for the discrete-time algorithm with adaptive averaging.
3.  An application of the adaptive averaging scheme to Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method.
4.  A discussion and comparison with other adaptive averaging schemes.

These proposed additions, if executed well, would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution. My updated score reflects the expectation that these crucial elements will be incorporated into the revision.
