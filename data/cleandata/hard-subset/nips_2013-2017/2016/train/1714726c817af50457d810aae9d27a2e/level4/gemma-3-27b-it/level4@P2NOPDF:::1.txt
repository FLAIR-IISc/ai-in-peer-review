This paper presents a compelling and practical approach to accelerating Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent by adaptively reweighting iterates, building upon the ODE interpretation introduced by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The authors cleverly leverage this ODE framework to design a heuristic that demonstrably outperforms standard fixed-schedule averaging techniques, with a theoretical guarantee of dominance.

The core contribution lies in the adaptive averaging scheme, which offers a simple yet effective modification to Nesterov’s method. Empirical results across a range of functions demonstrate consistent speed-ups, validating the efficacy of the proposed approach. I found the paper to be well-written and enjoyable to read; the mathematical arguments are clear and accessible, particularly for those familiar with the prior work of Krichene et al. The combination of theoretical justification and strong empirical performance is particularly encouraging.

A significant strength of this work is its practical relevance. Nesterov’s acceleration is a cornerstone of modern optimization, and it is common practice to benchmark new methods against it using fixed parameters. This paper convincingly argues – and demonstrates – that a minor adaptation can unlock substantial performance gains from Nesterov’s method itself. This observation has the potential to significantly shift the landscape of empirical evaluations, prompting researchers to consider adaptive implementations of foundational algorithms like Nesterov’s. 

While the technical development is a logical and elegant extension of existing work, strengthening the theoretical foundation would further enhance the paper. Specifically, an analysis of the discretized process to confirm the preservation of the convergence rate would be valuable. This appears to be a tractable extension of the current analysis. 

Furthermore, exploring potential connections between this adaptive averaging scheme and the Conjugate Gradient method – particularly in the context of strongly convex quadratic optimization – could offer additional insights and broaden the understanding of this approach. Such a connection might illuminate the underlying mechanisms driving the observed speed-ups and potentially lead to further improvements.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a clear contribution and considerable potential impact. The method is innovative, well-motivated, and empirically validated. Addressing the suggested areas for improvement would solidify its contribution and further elevate its impact within the optimization community.
