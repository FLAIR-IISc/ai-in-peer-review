This paper studies a class of continuous-time ODEs describing accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, focusing on a generalized averaging scheme. The authors provide sufficient conditions, based on a Lyapunov argument, on the dual variable’s learning rate and the averaging weights to guarantee a desired convergence rate. They then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to further accelerate convergence by adaptively computing these weights, providing both theoretical guarantees and numerical comparisons to existing methods like adaptive restarting. This work builds on a significant body of research exploring the connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics, notably Su et al. [20] and the authors’ previous work [11].

The core idea – that adaptive averaging can improve convergence – is insightful and well-motivated. The paper convincingly frames accelerated methods as a coupling of dual and primal variables and demonstrates how varying the averaging scheme can impact performance. The Lyapunov-based analysis provides a solid theoretical foundation, and the connection to replicator dynamics (as an example of mirror descent on the simplex) is a valuable illustration of the framework's applicability. The numerical experiments further strengthen the claims, showing comparable or improved performance compared to adaptive restarting, especially in the strongly convex case.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The Lyapunov argument provides a rigorous framework for analyzing the convergence of the proposed dynamics.
*   **Novelty:** The adaptive averaging heuristic presents a fresh perspective on accelerating first-order methods, moving beyond established techniques like restarting.
*   **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively links its contributions to the broader landscape of accelerated optimization, referencing relevant literature (e.g., [6, 9, 5, 14, 15, 20, 23]).
*   **Practical Relevance:** The numerical results demonstrate the potential benefits of the proposed approach, indicating its practical viability.
*   **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally clear and logically structured, facilitating understanding of the concepts and results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The mathematical exposition, while rigorous, can be dense and challenging to follow for readers not deeply familiar with the underlying concepts of mirror descent and Lyapunov stability. Some key definitions (e.g., Bregman divergence) are relegated to the supplementary material, hindering accessibility.
*   **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** While the experiments provide encouraging results, they are somewhat limited in scope. Expanding the evaluation to a broader range of problems and datasets would further strengthen the claims. Specifically, the comparison to more recent adaptive methods like Adam [10] could be insightful.
*   **Discussion of Discretization:** The transition from continuous-time analysis to a discrete algorithm (Algorithm 1) feels somewhat abrupt. A more detailed discussion of the discretization process and its potential impact on the convergence guarantees would be beneficial. The reference to Wibisono et al. [23] is somewhat isolated; a clearer explanation of the relationship would be helpful.
*   **Minor Technical Detail:** The statement concerning the rate of convergence of Nesterov’s method (referencing Attouch et al.) seems to be a recent finding and may benefit from more precise attribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to accelerating first-order optimization methods.
*   The Lyapunov-based analysis provides strong guarantees on convergence.
*   The numerical results demonstrate the practical potential of the proposed heuristic.
*   The work builds on and extends a significant body of research in the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The mathematical complexity may limit the accessibility of the paper to a wider audience.
*   The limited scope of the numerical experiments leaves some questions unanswered.
*   The discussion of the discretization process could be more detailed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of accelerated optimization. While the mathematical complexity and limited scope of the numerical experiments are minor concerns, the novelty of the approach, the rigorous theoretical analysis, and the promising experimental results outweigh these drawbacks. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance at NIPS 2016.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
