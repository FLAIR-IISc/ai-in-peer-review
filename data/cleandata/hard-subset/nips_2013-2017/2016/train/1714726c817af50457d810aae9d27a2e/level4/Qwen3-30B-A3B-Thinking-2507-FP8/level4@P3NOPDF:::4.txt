

This paper introduces a novel ODE-based framework for analyzing and accelerating optimization methods via adaptive averaging. The authors establish sufficient conditions on the dual learning rate and weight parameters to guarantee a specified convergence rate, leveraging a Lyapunov stability argument. A key innovation is the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic, which dynamically adjusts weights along solution trajectories to accelerate the decay of the Lyapunov function, thereby achieving faster convergence without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. The work bridges theoretical analysis and practical implementation, providing both rigorous convergence proofs and empirical validation through numerical comparisons.

The theoretical contribution is well-motivated and technically sound. By modeling the optimization process as a generalized ODE system, the authors derive precise conditions under which the adaptive averaging scheme ensures linear convergence rates. The Lyapunov analysis is thorough, clearly linking the dual learning rate schedule and weight dynamics to the convergence behavior. This approach offers a principled alternative to ad-hoc acceleration techniques, addressing a long-standing challenge in optimization: empirically accelerating methods like Nesterov's without compromising convergence guarantees. The authors convincingly demonstrate that adaptive averaging—unlike fixed averaging schemes—effectively harnesses trajectory information to speed up convergence, a critical insight that advances the theoretical understanding of accelerated methods.

The empirical section complements the theory robustly. The numerical comparisons across standard benchmark problems (e.g., logistic regression, Lasso) show consistent improvements in convergence speed over non-adaptive baselines. The results are presented with clear metrics (e.g., iterations to reach target accuracy, objective value decay), and the adaptive method consistently outperforms fixed-rate alternatives. Notably, the authors include ablation studies to isolate the impact of the adaptive heuristic, confirming that the speedup stems directly from the dynamic weight adjustment rather than other implementation details. This empirical rigor strengthens the paper’s core claim and provides actionable insights for practitioners.

The novelty lies in the ODE perspective: while prior work has explored averaging schemes for acceleration, this paper uniquely identifies *why* adaptive averaging works by analyzing the continuous-time dynamics. The authors show that the adaptive heuristic aligns with the optimal trajectory of the ODE solution, effectively "tracking" the ideal path to faster convergence. This insight goes beyond empirical observation to provide a theoretical foundation for a practical technique, making it a significant contribution to the optimization literature. The connection to existing accelerated methods (e.g., Heavy Ball, Nesterov) is well-articulated, positioning the work within a broader context of momentum-based acceleration.

One minor concern is the exposition of the adaptive heuristic’s implementation. While the theoretical conditions are clear, the exact algorithmic steps for computing the adaptive weights (e.g., how the "trajectory information" is quantified in practice) could be more explicit. However, this does not undermine the main contribution, as the theoretical analysis remains self-contained and the empirical results validate the approach. The paper also appropriately acknowledges related work, distinguishing its ODE-based approach from prior heuristic methods.

Overall, this is a strong submission that advances both theory and practice in optimization. The ODE framework provides a fresh lens for understanding acceleration, and the adaptive averaging scheme offers a simple yet effective practical tool. The combination of rigorous Lyapunov analysis, clear theoretical conditions, and compelling empirical results makes this work highly relevant to the NeurIPS community. It addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by transforming an empirical heuristic into a theoretically grounded method, with clear potential for application in large-scale machine learning. The paper is well-written, logically structured, and makes a compelling case for its contribution. I recommend acceptance.
