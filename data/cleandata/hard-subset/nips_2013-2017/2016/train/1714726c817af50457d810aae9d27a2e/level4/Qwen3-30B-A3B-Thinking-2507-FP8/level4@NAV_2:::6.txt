

This paper investigates a generalized averaging scheme for accelerated mirror descent dynamics in constrained convex optimization, extending previous work on continuous-time representations of accelerated methods. The authors build on the connection between Nesterov's accelerated methods and ODEs established by Su et al. (NIPS 2014) and Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015), who demonstrated that acceleration can be interpreted as a dual-primal coupling with averaging. The paper's main contribution is a unified Lyapunov-based framework for accelerated descent dynamics with generalized averaging, which provides sufficient conditions for achieving desired convergence rates. The authors demonstrate how this framework can accelerate the replicator dynamics (a key ODE in evolutionary game theory) and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates while empirically outperforming existing restarting heuristics.

Strengths:
- Quality: The theoretical analysis is sound, providing rigorous conditions for the Lyapunov function to decrease, with clear proofs for both continuous and discrete-time cases. The adaptive averaging heuristic preserves the quadratic convergence rate without sacrificing guarantees for non-strongly convex functions, which is a significant advantage over existing restarting heuristics.
- Originality: The paper offers a novel generalization of the averaging scheme in accelerated mirror descent, demonstrating how it can be applied to the replicator dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic is a meaningful contribution that differs from previous work on restarting strategies.
- Significance: The approach provides a theoretically grounded method for accelerating constrained optimization that works for non-strongly convex functions, addressing an important gap in the literature. The experimental results show consistent improvement over existing heuristics, particularly in the strongly convex case.

Weaknesses:
- Clarity: Some technical sections (particularly the derivation of the Lyapunov conditions in Sections 2-3) would benefit from more intuitive explanations to help readers unfamiliar with the mathematical framework. The connection to adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam (which are designed for non-accelerated first-order methods) could be more explicitly addressed.
- Significance: The numerical experiments are limited to R³, which does not adequately demonstrate scalability to high-dimensional problems common in machine learning applications. The paper would be stronger with experiments on larger-scale problems to establish practical relevance.
- Clarity: The description of the primal representation (Section 3) and the adaptive averaging heuristic (Section 4) could be improved with more intuitive explanations of the key equations.

Pros for acceptance: Theoretical contribution is solid with clear guarantees for convergence rate preservation, the adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate consistent improvement over existing methods.

Cons for acceptance: Limited scalability demonstration in experiments, some technical sections would benefit from clearer explanations for broader accessibility, and the practical significance could be better established with larger-scale experiments.
