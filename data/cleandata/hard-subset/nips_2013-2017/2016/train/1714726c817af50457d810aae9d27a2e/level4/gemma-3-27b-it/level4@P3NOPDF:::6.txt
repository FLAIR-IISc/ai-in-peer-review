## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - please fill in]

This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate constrained convex optimization problems. A key strength of the proposed method is its theoretical guarantee of maintaining the original convergence rate even when dealing with non-strongly convex objective functions. This distinguishes it favorably from existing restarting heuristics, a difference demonstrated through both theoretical analysis and experimental results.

While the core idea appears promising, I found aspects of the presentation challenging to follow due to the technical nature of the field, which lies somewhat outside my primary expertise. I suspect the authors’ use of specialized abbreviations and terminology will be readily understood by the target audience, however, providing some brief background context for key concepts would broaden accessibility and improve the paper’s overall clarity. Specifically, elaborating on the problem setting and the motivations behind constrained convex optimization would be beneficial.

The equations presented between lines 33-34 and 46-47, while likely correct, would significantly benefit from a more intuitive explanation. Connecting these mathematical formulations to the underlying algorithmic steps, and perhaps offering a simplified illustrative example, would help readers grasp the practical implications of these expressions. 

Furthermore, it would be valuable to position this work within the broader context of adaptive averaging techniques. Specifically, discussing the relationship to methods commonly employed in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2], would be insightful. Are there connections in terms of inspiration or performance? What distinguishes the approach presented here, tailored for convex optimization, from these established techniques? A comparative discussion would strengthen the paper’s contribution and highlight its specific advantages.

My primary concern relates to the practical scalability and significance of the proposed method. The experiments are currently conducted in R<sup>3</sup>, which, while useful for initial validation, feels somewhat limited. It’s unclear how the method – and the related work it builds upon – will perform as dimensionality increases. Convex optimization problems frequently arise in high-dimensional settings; demonstrating (either theoretically or empirically) the method's behavior in such cases is crucial for establishing its practical impact. Addressing this scalability question would significantly strengthen the paper's overall impact.

Finally, a minor point regarding the presentation of results: the reference to the "solid line" in Figure 1 is somewhat ambiguous. Consider using a different line style (e.g., dotted line) or employing distinct colors to clearly delineate the trajectory of the primal variable and enhance the readability of the figure.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of constrained convex optimization. While the current presentation presents some accessibility challenges, and the scalability to high-dimensional problems remains an open question, the theoretical guarantees and demonstrated advantages over existing methods warrant further consideration. Addressing the points raised above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
