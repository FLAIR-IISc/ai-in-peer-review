This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated first-order methods in constrained convex optimization. The core idea is to dynamically adjust averaging weights based on the decrease of a Lyapunov function, offering a theoretical guarantee of preserving the quadratic convergence rate even when the objective function lacks strong convexity – a key advantage over existing restarting heuristics. The authors provide both theoretical analysis and numerical experiments to support their claims, demonstrating competitive performance with established techniques like adaptive restarting.

The paper builds upon a connection between Nesterov’s accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs, framing the optimization process as coupled dynamics of dual and primal variables. This continuous-time perspective allows for a Lyapunov-based analysis, leading to the proposed adaptive averaging scheme. The authors rigorously establish sufficient conditions for convergence and demonstrate that the heuristic can be effectively applied to specific cases, such as accelerating the replicator dynamics on the probability simplex. 

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a solid theoretical foundation, including a Lyapunov argument and convergence guarantees. The preservation of the quadratic convergence rate is a significant result, particularly in non-strongly convex settings.
* **Novelty:** The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a novel approach to accelerating optimization algorithms, differing from existing restarting methods. The connection to replicator dynamics offers an interesting application.
* **Clarity of Presentation (in parts):** The core idea of adapting weights based on Lyapunov function decrease is clearly articulated. The organization of the paper, progressing from general theory to specific examples, is logical.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments, while limited in dimensionality, provide evidence that the proposed method performs favorably compared to existing heuristics.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility:** The paper assumes a high level of familiarity with optimization theory and dynamical systems. While the authors likely target a specialized audience, increased introductory context would broaden accessibility. Key definitions (e.g., mirror map, Bregman divergence) could benefit from brief explanations.
* **Equation Clarity:** Some equations, particularly those defining the ODEs (lines 33-34 and 46-47), could benefit from more intuitive explanations. Connecting the mathematical notation more directly to the underlying optimization process would enhance understanding.
* **Related Work Discussion:** The discussion of related work could be expanded. While Adagrad and Adam are mentioned, a more detailed comparison—highlighting similarities and, crucially, *differences* in design goals and applicability to accelerated methods—would be valuable. These methods adapt learning rates for non-convex problems with sparse gradients; how does this differ from the adaptive averaging approach in the context of accelerated convex optimization?
* **Scalability Concerns:** The numerical experiments are performed in R<sup>3</sup>, raising concerns about scalability. The paper would benefit from a discussion of how the method and its theoretical guarantees extend to higher dimensions, or a acknowledgement that scalability is a limitation. Are there computational bottlenecks that would arise in larger-scale problems?
* **Figure Clarity:** The reference to the "solid line" in Figure 1 is ambiguous. Utilizing distinct line styles (e.g., dotted, dashed) or color coding would improve clarity in visualizing the trajectories.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in optimization – accelerating convergence while maintaining theoretical guarantees.
* The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and well-motivated.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights.
* The experimental results, while limited, are promising and support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper's technical depth may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The lack of scalability analysis is a concern.
* The connection to other adaptive methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) could be explored more thoroughly.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. While the paper could benefit from increased accessibility and a more comprehensive discussion of scalability and related work, the novelty and rigor of the approach are commendable. I believe the paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program, particularly for researchers working in optimization and dynamical systems.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. Specifically, I recommend:

1.  Adding brief explanations of key concepts and notation to enhance accessibility.
2.  Providing a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the proposed method and existing adaptive optimization techniques like Adagrad and Adam.
3.  Addressing scalability concerns, either through analysis or acknowledgement of limitations.
4.  Improving the clarity of Figure 1 by using more distinct line styles or colors.
