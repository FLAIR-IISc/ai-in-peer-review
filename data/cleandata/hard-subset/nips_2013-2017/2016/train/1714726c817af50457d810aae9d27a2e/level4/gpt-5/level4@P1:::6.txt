Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization in continuous time, extending the accelerated mirror descent (AMD) viewpoint. The authors consider a broad family of dynamics parameterized by two positive weight functions: η(t), which controls the rate at which the dual variable accumulates gradients, and w(t), which defines the averaging that produces the primal variable. Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), they derive simple sufficient conditions on η and w that guarantee a target convergence rate 1/r(t). This unifies and extends prior ODE-based analyses of accelerated methods (e.g., Su, Boyd, and Candès; Wibisono, Wilson, and Jordan) and connects acceleration to averaging in the mirror descent framework (Krichene, Bayen, and Bartlett).

The paper also develops a primal representation of the dynamics and illustrates the method on standard constraint sets, notably deriving an accelerated version of replicator dynamics on the simplex. Motivated by the Lyapunov analysis, the authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses w(t) (equivalently a(t) = w(t)/W(t)) to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative whenever possible. They prove that adaptive averaging preserves the same convergence rate as the baseline accelerated method in continuous time and, in a specific discretization, retains the quadratic rate O(1/k^2) while improving empirical performance. Experiments compare the approach to adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient and speed restarts) and show that adaptive averaging is competitive and sometimes substantially better.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: acceleration via generalized averaging with explicit Lyapunov-based sufficient conditions on η and w. This connects and extends prior work on accelerated ODEs and mirror descent.
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is principled: it directly targets the Lyapunov derivative and therefore preserves the original rate even without strong convexity, in contrast to common restarting heuristics for which such guarantees are limited.
- The paper provides both continuous-time guarantees (existence/uniqueness, Lyapunov decrease, energy monotonicity) and a discrete-time algorithm with a corresponding rate argument.
- The accelerated replicator example is interesting and may broaden the impact to communities studying dynamics on the simplex (evolutionary game theory, viability theory).

Weaknesses and questions
Quality:
- While the continuous-time analysis is solid and the conditions are simple to state, parts of the presentation are hard to follow. In particular, the derivations around the averaging dynamics and the Lyapunov derivative (the equations around lines 33–34 and 46–47) would benefit from more intuition to guide readers who are less familiar with mirror maps and Bregman divergences.
- The comparison to restarting heuristics is empirical and limited to low-dimensional problems. It is therefore hard to assess the practical significance and robustness of the proposed heuristic at scale.

Clarity:
- The paper assumes familiarity with several abbreviations and concepts (AMD, mirror maps, Bregman divergence, replicator dynamics). A short background subsection summarizing the necessary definitions and intuition would make the work more accessible.
- Figure 1: the reference to the solid line is ambiguous. Consider using dotted lines or colors to distinguish the primal trajectory clearly.

Originality:
- The generalized weighting and the adaptive averaging heuristic appear novel within the accelerated mirror descent/ODE literature. The connection to accelerated replicator dynamics is also interesting. It would help to more clearly articulate how the proposed conditions and heuristic differ from, or subsume, the ODE families studied by Su et al. and by Wibisono et al.

Significance:
- The paper argues that adaptive averaging preserves the baseline rate without strong convexity and often improves empirical performance over restarts. However, the experiments are in R^3, which is essentially a toy setting. Please comment on computational overhead and scaling to high dimensions: memory/compute costs of maintaining the dual/primal variables, evaluating the mirror map, and implementing the adaptive weights, as well as performance on larger problems and datasets.

Relation to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization:
- The paper briefly mentions Adagrad and Adam but does not elaborate on similarities/differences. It would be useful to clarify:
  - Conceptual differences: Adagrad/Adam adapt learning rates using gradient statistics and are not designed to preserve accelerated Lyapunov rates; your method adapts averaging weights to control the Lyapunov derivative.
  - Practical interactions: Could adaptive averaging be combined with per-coordinate adaptivity (as in Adagrad/Adam) in constrained mirror-mapped settings? Are there theoretical obstacles or promising directions?
  - Scope: Do your guarantees extend (or can they be adapted) to non-convex settings, perhaps ensuring energy decrease even if convergence rates are not preserved?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a concise background section on mirror descent, mirror maps, and Bregman divergences, with a brief intuitive explanation of the averaging formulation and its link to acceleration.
- Provide more intuition for the key equations (around lines 33–34 and 46–47): a schematic derivation and a high-level explanation of how the conditions on η and w cancel the problematic terms in the Lyapunov derivative.
- Expand the experimental section to higher dimensions and include timing/iteration counts, sensitivity to β and step sizes, and comparisons on standard constrained benchmarks. This would clarify practical significance and scalability.
- Improve Figure 1 by using different line styles or colors for trajectories to avoid ambiguity.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, Lyapunov-based framework for acceleration via averaging; simple sufficient conditions.
- Adaptive averaging is principled, preserves rates without strong convexity, and shows promising empirical gains over restarts.
- Interesting application to replicator dynamics and clear connections to recent ODE-based analyses of acceleration.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation (R^3) leaves practical significance and scalability unclear.
- Parts of the exposition are hard to follow without additional background and intuition.

Minor comment
- Figure 1: use a dotted line or color to clearly indicate the primal trajectory.
