This paper investigates adaptive averaging within the framework of accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, building upon the authors’ prior work [11] establishing a connection between Nesterov’s acceleration and continuous-time ODEs. The core idea is to develop a heuristic for adaptively computing weights in a weighted average of mirrored dual trajectories, aiming to improve convergence speed. The authors provide sufficient conditions for convergence and demonstrate, through numerical experiments, that this adaptive averaging scheme performs at least as well as, and often outperforms, existing heuristics like adaptive restarting [20].

The paper is well-motivated and clearly situates itself within the existing literature on accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics. The connection drawn between averaging and acceleration is insightful, and the proposed heuristic feels natural given the established theoretical framework. The Lyapunov analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the convergence properties of the proposed method. The inclusion of examples – the accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex – helps to concretize the theoretical results and illustrates the applicability of the method. The numerical experiments are well-designed and provide compelling evidence supporting the effectiveness of the adaptive averaging scheme.

A key strength of this work lies in its theoretical guarantees. Unlike many adaptive heuristics, the authors demonstrate that their approach preserves the original convergence rate, a significant advantage. The connection to existing work, particularly [11, 20], is clearly articulated and builds upon a strong foundation. The paper’s clarity is also commendable; the authors explain complex concepts in an accessible manner. 

However, some areas could benefit from further elaboration. While the paper mentions discretization in passing, a more detailed analysis of the convergence rate of the *discrete* implementation of the adaptive averaging heuristic would strengthen the results. The authors acknowledge this as a potential direction in the conclusion and it would be valuable to see this explored further. Although the numerical experiments are well-conducted, it would be beneficial to include a wider range of problem instances and potentially larger dimensions to assess the scalability and robustness of the proposed method. Finally, the suggestion of connections to the Conjugate Gradient method for strongly convex quadratic optimization, raised by the reviewer, is a potentially fruitful avenue for future work and could be briefly discussed. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous Lyapunov analysis and convergence guarantees.
*   **Novel Heuristic:** The adaptive averaging scheme is a natural and well-motivated extension of existing accelerated methods.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to established heuristics.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and accessible.
*   **Potential Impact:** The work addresses a fundamental aspect of Nesterov’s acceleration and could influence practical implementations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Analysis of Discretization:** A more detailed analysis of the discrete implementation’s convergence rate is lacking.
*   **Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments, while solid, could be expanded to cover a broader range of problems and dimensions.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The authors address an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. The empirical results are promising and the clear presentation makes the paper accessible to a broad audience. While further analysis of the discrete case and expanded experiments would be valuable, the paper, as it stands, meets the criteria for acceptance to NIPS. I recommend accepting this paper.
