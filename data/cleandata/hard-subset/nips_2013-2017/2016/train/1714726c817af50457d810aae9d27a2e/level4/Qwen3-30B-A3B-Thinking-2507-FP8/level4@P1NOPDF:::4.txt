

This paper investigates a class of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) equipped with a generalized averaging scheme. The authors establish sufficient conditions on the dual learning rate and weight parameters to achieve a specified convergence rate, using a Lyapunov-based analysis. They further introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic to accelerate the decay of the Lyapunov function. The work provides both theoretical guarantees and numerical comparisons.  

While numerous prior studies have sought empirical acceleration of accelerated optimization methods, this paper offers a key theoretical insight: adaptive averaging of weights along solution trajectories—formulated through ODEs—serves as a viable mechanism for achieving convergence speedups. This approach is notably insightful, as it bridges continuous-time dynamics with practical optimization, revealing a novel perspective on acceleration that transcends purely empirical heuristics. The technical contribution is well-motivated, and the Lyapunov analysis is rigorous and clearly presented. The numerical experiments effectively support the theoretical claims, demonstrating consistent improvements over baseline methods.  

The adaptive averaging technique is particularly compelling, as it dynamically adjusts the weighting scheme based on trajectory evolution, avoiding the need for manual tuning of hyperparameters. This represents a meaningful step toward unifying continuous-time and discrete-time acceleration frameworks. The paper’s focus on ODEs as a unifying lens is well-justified, and the derived conditions for convergence are both non-trivial and practically relevant.  

One minor concern is the clarity of the adaptive heuristic’s implementation in the numerical section; a brief pseudocode or explicit description of the adaptive rule would strengthen reproducibility. However, this does not detract from the paper’s core contribution. Overall, the work makes a significant and timely contribution to the understanding of accelerated methods, offering both theoretical depth and practical utility. The results are well-supported, and the writing is generally clear, though some technical passages could benefit from slightly more concise phrasing. This paper should be of strong interest to the optimization and machine learning communities.
