This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate constrained convex optimization. A key strength of the proposed method is its theoretical guarantee of maintaining the original convergence rate even for non-strongly convex objective functions – a clear advantage over existing restarting heuristics, as demonstrated both theoretically and empirically. While I found the paper challenging to fully grasp due to its specialized nature, I presume the employed abbreviations and terminology are standard within the target audience. Nevertheless, including some background information would likely broaden its accessibility. The equations spanning lines 33-34 and 46-47 would benefit from more intuitive explanations to aid understanding. Furthermore, it would be valuable to discuss the relationship between this work and adaptive averaging techniques utilized in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]. Finally, I question the practical impact of this work; the experiments were conducted in R^3, a rather limited setting. How does the method – and the related work it builds upon – perform as dimensionality scales to more realistic problem sizes? A minor suggestion: the reference to the solid line in Figure 1 is unclear. Consider using a dotted line or distinct colors to more clearly depict the trajectory of the primal variable. [2] Kingma, Diederik, and Jimmy Ba. "Adam: A method for stochastic optimization." International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR). (2014).
