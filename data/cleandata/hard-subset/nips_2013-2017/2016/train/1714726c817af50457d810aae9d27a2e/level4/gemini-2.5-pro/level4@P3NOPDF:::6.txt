### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate constrained convex optimization. The primary contribution is a method that, unlike many existing restarting heuristics, is theoretically guaranteed to preserve the original convergence rate when the objective function is not strongly convex. The authors support this significant claim with both theoretical analysis and experimental results.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Theoretical Contribution:** The proposed adaptive heuristic addresses an important problem in convex optimization. The theoretical guarantee that the method does not degrade the convergence rate for non-strongly convex functions is a key advantage over standard restarting schemes and represents a solid contribution.
2.  **Clear Advantage:** The paper effectively demonstrates, both in theory and practice, the advantage of this rate preservation property, which is a common pitfall for other acceleration methods.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification**

While the paper appears technically sound, several areas could be improved to strengthen the work and clarify its contributions.

1.  **Accessibility and Background:** The paper assumes a high level of domain-specific knowledge from the reader. While abbreviations and terms may be standard within a niche community, the NIPS audience is broad. Including a brief background section or providing more context for the specialized terminology would significantly improve the paper's readability and impact, making it accessible to a wider audience of optimization and machine learning researchers.

2.  **Intuition Behind Formulations:** The mathematical derivations, particularly the equations presented between lines 33-34 and 46-47, would benefit greatly from more intuitive explanations. While formally correct, the text does not provide much insight into *why* these specific update rules were chosen or what geometric or functional properties they are designed to exploit. A high-level, intuitive description accompanying the formal mathematics would help readers grasp the core mechanism of the heuristic.

3.  **Relation to Other Adaptive Methods:** The discussion would be substantially enriched by contextualizing the proposed method with respect to well-known adaptive methods used in non-convex and stochastic optimization, such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]. Although these methods operate in a different setting, they also employ adaptive averaging or momentum-like principles. A discussion on the conceptual similarities and differences would be valuable. For instance, does the analysis in this convex setting offer any insights that might be relevant to those methods, or vice versa?

4.  **Practical Significance and Scalability:** A major concern is the practical utility of the proposed method, as the experimental validation is limited. The experiments were conducted in a very low-dimensional space (R^3), which feels like a toy problem and may not be representative of the high-dimensional settings common in machine learning. It is crucial for the authors to demonstrate how their method (and related approaches) scales to much higher dimensions. I strongly encourage the authors to include experiments on more complex, higher-dimensional problems to convincingly establish the practical significance of their work.

### **Minor Comments**

*   **Figure 1 Clarity:** The reference to the "solid line" in Figure 1 is ambiguous, especially if multiple lines share this style. To improve clarity, I suggest using distinct line styles (e.g., solid, dashed, dotted) or colors to differentiate the trajectories of the different variables, and ensuring these are clearly marked in the caption and legend.
