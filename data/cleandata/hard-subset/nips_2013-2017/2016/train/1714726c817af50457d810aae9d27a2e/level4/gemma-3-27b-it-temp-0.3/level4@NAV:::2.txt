This paper builds upon prior work linking accelerated Mirror Descent to continuous-time ODEs, extending the ODE dynamics with a generalized averaging scheme and introducing a novel adaptive averaging strategy for discretization. The adaptive variant of AMD demonstrates performance improvements over existing restarting AMD algorithms in certain instances, and the adaptive averaging strategy itself presents a novel and intuitively sound approach. However, the overall originality and practical impact of the work remain questionable. 

The core elements – Lyapunov function, discretization approach, and so on – are largely derived from a previous NIPS publication [7]. The generalization from simple to generalized averaging feels incremental, and its practical benefit is limited as the authors primarily focus on quadratic convergence rates; achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods which aren’t universally applicable. While the paper offers valuable insights – such as the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation in primal form, and reduction to replicator dynamics – these contributions, while enhancing understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent, do not represent a substantial advancement for the field of machine learning.

The adaptive averaging strategy, intended to satisfy conditions for ODE convergence, lacks complete theoretical justification due to approximations introduced during discretization. Critically, the paper does not provide an explicit convergence result, leaving the method as a heuristic, as acknowledged by the authors. Furthermore, given the existing landscape of averaging schemes for Nesterov’s accelerated algorithms, the benefit of this specific adaptive variant remains unclear without comparison to alternative approaches. The experimental evaluation, limited to a toy example with quadratic or linear objectives, is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of adaptive averaging, particularly in the context of strongly convex, general-case problems.
