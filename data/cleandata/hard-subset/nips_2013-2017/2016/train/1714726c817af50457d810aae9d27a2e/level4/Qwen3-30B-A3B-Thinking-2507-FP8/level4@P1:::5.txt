

This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending recent work on continuous-time optimization dynamics. The authors present a unified framework for Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), which generalizes prior continuous-time formulations such as those in [11, 20]. Using a Lyapunov argument, they establish sufficient conditions on these weight functions to achieve desired convergence rates, demonstrating that the replicator dynamics on the simplex can be accelerated through a simple averaging scheme. The key contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically computes weights to accelerate convergence while preserving the quadratic convergence rate of accelerated first-order methods.

The paper builds upon and extends several important works in continuous-time optimization. The authors connect their framework to Su et al.'s interpretation of Nesterov's method as a second-order ODE [20], and generalize the proximal version of Nesterov's method studied in [11]. Their work also relates to the broader literature on continuous-time dynamics for optimization [6, 9, 5, 23], while providing a more general theoretical foundation for accelerated methods.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish a clear Lyapunov function framework that unifies various acceleration schemes, and they provide precise sufficient conditions for convergence rates. The theoretical results are well-supported with detailed proofs, including Theorem 2 which gives sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing. The paper also provides a primal representation of the dynamics and demonstrates how it generalizes previous ODE families [23]. The numerical experiments in Section 5 are well-designed and provide compelling evidence that adaptive averaging outperforms existing heuristics like adaptive restarting in multiple problem settings.

The paper is well-written with clear organization and logical flow. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the technical contributions are presented in a way that makes the theoretical framework accessible. The introduction effectively contextualizes the work within the literature, and the examples (particularly the replicator dynamics on the simplex) provide concrete applications of the theoretical framework. The paper would benefit from slightly more detailed discussion of the adaptive averaging heuristic's implementation details, but the current presentation is sufficiently clear for experts.

The originality of this work lies in the generalization of averaging schemes for accelerated mirror descent and the development of a theoretically grounded adaptive heuristic. While the connection between averaging and acceleration was noted in [8], this paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework that encompasses previous approaches and introduces a new adaptive method with convergence guarantees. The paper properly cites related work in NIPS proceedings, including [11] which is directly relevant to the current contribution.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides deeper theoretical understanding of acceleration mechanisms in optimization, and the adaptive averaging heuristic offers a practical improvement to existing accelerated methods. The authors demonstrate that their approach preserves convergence rates while improving empirical performance, which is particularly valuable for constrained optimization problems where acceleration is challenging. The replicator dynamics example shows potential applications in evolutionary game theory and viability theory, expanding the scope of the theoretical framework.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear Lyapunov analysis
- Novel adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence rates
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple problem types
- Clear connection to existing literature and generalization of previous work
- Well-structured presentation with detailed mathematical proofs

Weaknesses:
- The paper assumes familiarity with mirror descent and continuous-time dynamics, which may limit accessibility for some readers
- The adaptive averaging heuristic could benefit from more detailed discussion of computational complexity
- The connection to practical machine learning applications could be strengthened

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theory of accelerated optimization methods. It advances our understanding of the relationship between averaging and acceleration, and provides a theoretically grounded heuristic that improves upon existing methods. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for constrained convex problems.

The authors have successfully extended previous work on continuous-time dynamics to a more general framework, and their adaptive averaging heuristic represents a meaningful advance in practical optimization methods. The paper would be appropriate for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental question in optimization with clear theoretical and practical implications.

I recommend acceptance for the following reasons: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods, provides a novel adaptive heuristic with convergence guarantees, and demonstrates strong empirical performance. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in optimization with clear relevance to machine learning applications. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing both theoretical foundations and practical improvements to acceleration techniques.
