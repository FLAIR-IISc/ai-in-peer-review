Summary
The paper builds on prior work that connects accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ODEs. It extends the ODE dynamics with a more general averaging scheme and proposes a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one particular discretization. In some cases, this adaptive AMD variant outperforms existing AMD algorithms with restarting. The adaptive averaging idea is new and intuitively motivated.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of an adaptive averaging strategy that is novel and supported by good intuition.
- Some results—such as the interpretation of the energy function, the reformulation into a primal form, and the reduction to replicator dynamics—enhance conceptual understanding of AMD.
- Empirical evidence indicates that, in certain cases, the adaptive averaging variant can outperform AMD with restarting.

Concerns and weaknesses
- Originality and usefulness: Most core elements (e.g., Lyapunov function, discretization, and the overall continuous-time viewpoint) directly follow from an earlier NIPS paper [7]. The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging appears largely straightforward and of limited practical value, especially since the paper ultimately focuses on the quadratic rate; achieving faster rates would require higher-order methods that are not always applicable.
- Theoretical grounding: Although the adaptive averaging is introduced to satisfy sufficient conditions for ODE convergence, the discretization introduces approximations that are not fully addressed. No explicit convergence result is provided for the discrete algorithm, and the method is characterized as a heuristic by the authors.
- Relation to alternative averaging schemes: There are numerous averaging schemes for Nesterov’s accelerated algorithms. Without empirical or theoretical comparisons to these alternatives, it is unclear whether the proposed adaptive averaging provides a substantial advantage.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are limited to toy problems with quadratic/linear objectives and are not compelling enough to demonstrate a clear advantage of the adaptive averaging strategy, particularly for the more general strongly convex setting.

Overall assessment
The paper is well written and presents an appealing idea with solid intuition. However, the incremental nature of the main contributions relative to [7], the lack of rigorous discrete-time convergence guarantees, the absence of comparisons with other averaging strategies, and the limited experimental scope weaken the case for impact on the machine learning community.

Post-rebuttal update
I read the authors’ rebuttal. They commit to adding:
- Additional higher-dimensional and non-quadratic experiments.
- A theoretical proof of convergence for the adaptive averaging scheme.
- An implementation of adaptive averaging within Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method.
- A discussion comparing their approach to other adaptive averaging strategies.
