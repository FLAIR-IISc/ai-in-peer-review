This paper builds on earlier work linking accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ODEs. The authors generalize the ODE dynamics with a broader averaging scheme and introduce a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one discretization. In some cases, the AMD variant with adaptive averaging outperforms existing AMD algorithms with restarts. The adaptive averaging idea is interesting, novel, and intuitively motivated. However, I have substantial concerns about originality and usefulness.

- Many components (e.g., Lyapunov function, discretization) follow directly from the earlier NIPS paper [7]. The move from simple averaging to generalized averaging is nearly trivial and of limited practical value, since the paper ultimately focuses on the quadratic-rate case; achieving faster rates would require higher-order methods that are not always applicable.
- Some results—such as the interpretation of the energy function, the primal reformulation, and the reduction to replicator dynamics—enhance understanding of AMD but are not significant advances for machine learning.
- Although adaptive averaging is introduced to satisfy sufficient conditions for ODE convergence, the theory is not fully grounded due to approximations introduced by discretization. No explicit convergence guarantees are provided; as the authors note, the method is essentially heuristic. Furthermore, there are numerous averaging schemes for Nesterov’s accelerated methods; without comparisons to these alternatives, it is unclear whether this adaptive variant offers a substantial improvement.
- The experiment is limited to a toy example with quadratic/linear objectives and does not convincingly demonstrate the benefits of adaptive averaging, especially in more general strongly convex settings.

Post-rebuttal: I have read the authors’ response. They commit to adding (i) additional higher-dimensional and non-quadratic experiments, (ii) a theoretical proof of convergence for adaptive averaging, (iii) an implementation of adaptive averaging in Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method, and (iv) a discussion comparing to other adaptive averaging schemes. I have updated my scores accordingly.
