This paper analyzes a family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics with generalized averaging for constrained convex optimization. The authors couple a dual trajectory Z accumulating gradients at rate η(t) with a primal variable X obtained as a weighted average of the mirrored dual path, with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z, z*), they provide sufficient conditions on η and w that guarantee f(X(t)) − f* = O(1/r(t)). They also present a primal-only formulation, instantiate the dynamics on common domains (positive orthant, simplex), and derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics. Building on this, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts weights along the trajectory to further decrease the Lyapunov function while preserving the nominal rate. A discrete-time scheme is given that maintains the quadratic convergence rate typical of first-order accelerated methods. Experiments compare adaptive averaging with adaptive restarting heuristics and show competitive or improved performance.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to the ODE viewpoint on acceleration (e.g., Su, Boyd, and Candès; Wibisono, Wilson, and Jordan) and to classical mirror descent. It generalizes the averaging-based interpretation of accelerated dynamics (e.g., Krichene et al.) by allowing flexible weight schedules and provides a unifying Lyapunov analysis. The proposed adaptive averaging complements restart-based heuristics (O’Donoghue and Candès; Su et al.) by targeting the Lyapunov decrease directly. The discussion of adaptive gradient methods (AdaGrad, Adam) clarifies conceptual differences.

Strengths
- Quality: The Lyapunov analysis is clean and general. Theorem 2 offers transparent sufficient conditions for rate guarantees; the existence/uniqueness result is carefully stated with the t0 > 0 initialization nuance. The additional “energy” function Er provides insight into the dynamics beyond rates.
- Originality: The adaptive averaging heuristic is a natural yet novel alternative to restarts, grounded in minimizing the time derivative of a Lyapunov function. The accelerated replicator dynamics is an appealing example linking optimization and evolutionary dynamics.
- Significance: The framework unifies and extends accelerated mirror descent in continuous time and yields a practical discrete-time algorithm with provable rate preservation. The approach may inform design of new adaptive accelerations beyond restarts.
- Clarity (parts): The high-level ideas and structure are clear, and the examples on the orthant and simplex are instructive.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Verifiability of conditions: Condition (2) of Theorem 2 depends on the (unknown) trajectory, making a priori verification difficult. While Corollary 1 offers a remedy, it would help to provide additional, readily checkable sufficient conditions or canonical schedules beyond power-law rates.
- Potential typo/clarity in Example 1: For r(t) = t^2, r′(t)/r(t) = 2/t. The text appears to set a(t) = β t in one place but uses expressions consistent with a(t) = β/t later (yielding w(t) ∝ (t/t0)^(β−1)). Please correct and standardize the choice of a(t) and the resulting w(t), η(t).
- Discrete-time guarantees: The discrete algorithm and its rate guarantee are deferred to the supplement. A succinct theorem in the main text (assumptions, statement, and a brief proof sketch) would strengthen the paper, as would guidance on step size s and parameter β selection (and their sensitivity).
- Experimental scope: Most experiments are low-dimensional (R^3 simplex) for visualization. To bolster significance, include larger-scale and higher-dimensional tests, additional constraint sets (e.g., box, l1 ball), and objectives (logistic, regularized problems). Report wall-clock time and gradient evaluations to corroborate practical speedups.
- Comparisons: Consider comparing against recent continuous-time inspired accelerations (e.g., Bregman Lagrangian discretizations) and momentum variants; also test whether adaptive averaging complements restarts (e.g., combining both).
- Clarity and readability: Some notation is heavy (multiple time-varying functions a, w, W, η, r). A consolidated table summarizing choices and their implications would help. The role of t0 > 0 could be explained more intuitively (e.g., how to choose t0 in practice).
- Strongly convex case: The paper notes improvements in strongly convex settings. It would be valuable to state explicit linear-rate guarantees (if available) or discuss whether adaptive averaging can achieve faster-than-quadratic rates in discrete time under strong convexity (cf. Attouch–Peypouquet–Redont).

Quality, clarity, originality, significance (brief evaluation)
- Quality: Strong theoretical backbone with sensible examples; discrete-time analysis should be more visible in the main text.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but some typos/inconsistencies and dense notation hinder readability. More guidance on parameter choices would help practitioners.
- Originality: Solid; the adaptive averaging via Lyapunov descent is new and conceptually compelling.
- Significance: Potentially impactful; a practical, principled alternative to restarts with promising empirical results.

Pros for acceptance
- Unified Lyapunov framework giving flexible rate guarantees in accelerated mirror descent.
- Novel adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical justification and practical benefits.
- Clear links between acceleration and averaging, including an accelerated replicator dynamics example.
- Empirical evidence of improved or comparable performance to standard restart heuristics.

Cons for acceptance
- A priori verifiability of key conditions is limited; some results require trajectory-dependent checks.
- Discrete-time guarantees and implementation details appear mostly in the supplement.
- Experimental evaluation is relatively narrow in scale and diversity.
- Minor presentation issues (e.g., possible typo in Example 1) should be corrected.

Overall assessment
This work offers a thoughtful and rigorous extension of accelerated mirror descent with a principled adaptive averaging mechanism. With strengthened discrete-time exposition, expanded experiments, and minor clarifications, it would make a valuable contribution to NIPS.
