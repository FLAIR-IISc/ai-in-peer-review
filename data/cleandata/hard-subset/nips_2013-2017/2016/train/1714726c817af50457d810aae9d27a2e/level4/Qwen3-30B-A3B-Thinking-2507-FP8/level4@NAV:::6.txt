

This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic to accelerate constrained convex optimization, demonstrating that the method maintains the original convergence rate when the objective is not strongly convex—offering an advantage over existing restarting heuristics, as validated both theoretically and experimentally. While the technical contribution appears sound, I found the exposition challenging to follow due to its specialized nature, suggesting the inclusion of more background for broader accessibility. Specifically, the equations between lines 33–34 and 46–47 would benefit from more intuitive explanations. Additionally, the relationship to adaptive averaging methods in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad [1] or Adam [2]) requires clarification. The practical significance is also unclear: experiments are conducted in ℝ³, a highly simplified setting; scalability to high-dimensional problems must be addressed. Finally, the reference to the "solid line" in Figure 1 is ambiguous—consider using a dotted line or color-coding to clarify the primal variable trajectory. [1] Duchi, John, Elad Hazan, and Yoram Singer. "Adaptive subgradient methods for online learning and stochastic optimization." Journal of Machine Learning Research 12.Jul (2011): 2121-2159. [2] Kingma, Diederik, and Jimmy Ba. "Adam: A method for stochastic optimization." International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR). (2014).
