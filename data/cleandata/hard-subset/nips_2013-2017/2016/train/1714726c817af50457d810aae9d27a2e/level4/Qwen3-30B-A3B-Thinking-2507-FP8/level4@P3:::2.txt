

# Review of "Adaptive Averaging in Accelerated Descent Dynamics"

The paper extends the connection between accelerated optimization methods and continuous-time dynamics, building on prior work by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016) that established the relationship between Nesterov's accelerated methods and a family of continuous-time ODEs. The authors develop a generalized averaging scheme for accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), and provide sufficient conditions using a Lyapunov function to achieve desired convergence rates. They demonstrate how this framework can accelerate replicator dynamics on the simplex, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights to minimize the Lyapunov function's time derivative. The authors provide numerical experiments comparing their method to existing heuristics like adaptive restarting.

**Quality** - The paper demonstrates technical soundness in its continuous-time analysis, with careful Lyapunov arguments establishing sufficient conditions for convergence. The theoretical framework is well-constructed, extending prior work from Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016) to a more general setting. However, the discrete-time implementation lacks rigorous theoretical guarantees. While the authors provide a heuristic for discretization, they do not establish convergence rates for the discrete algorithm, which is a significant gap given the paper's focus on practical optimization. The numerical experiments show promising results but are limited to simple examples (quadratic and linear objectives on the simplex), which don't sufficiently demonstrate the method's advantages for more complex machine learning problems.

**Clarity** - The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The mathematical derivations are clear and logically presented, with appropriate notation and careful explanations of the connections between continuous-time and discrete-time dynamics. The connection to replicator dynamics is particularly well-motivated and illustrated. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results, with comprehensive proofs and supplementary material.

**Originality** - The main contribution is the generalized averaging scheme and the adaptive heuristic. However, the extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging is relatively straightforward, as the authors ultimately restrict their analysis to quadratic convergence rates (r(t) = t²), which corresponds to the case already studied in Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016). The adaptive heuristic is interesting but is presented as a natural extension of the continuous-time framework rather than a novel approach. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work in the NIPS proceedings, particularly from the authors' own previous work.

**Significance** - While the paper provides a unified theoretical framework that enhances understanding of accelerated mirror descent dynamics, the practical significance is limited. The experimental validation is restricted to simple problems on the simplex, which are not representative of typical machine learning applications. The adaptive averaging heuristic shows promise but is not convincingly demonstrated to provide significant advantages over existing methods for broader classes of problems. The connection to replicator dynamics is mathematically interesting but does not clearly advance machine learning practice.

**Strengths** - The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to understanding the relationship between continuous-time dynamics and accelerated optimization. The Lyapunov function approach provides a unified framework that clarifies the connection between averaging and acceleration. The extension to replicator dynamics on the simplex is a compelling application that demonstrates the framework's utility.

**Weaknesses** - The most significant weakness is the limited theoretical grounding of the adaptive averaging heuristic in discrete time. The paper lacks a formal convergence proof for the discrete algorithm, which undermines the claim that the method preserves the quadratic convergence rate. The experimental evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive, with no tests on non-quadratic or higher-dimensional problems. The paper also fails to adequately compare the adaptive averaging approach to other adaptive methods beyond restarting heuristics.

**Arguments for acceptance**: The paper provides a theoretically sound framework for understanding accelerated mirror descent dynamics, with clear connections to previous work. The adaptive heuristic is well-motivated and shows promising empirical results in the provided experiments. The writing is excellent and the mathematical presentation is clear.

**Arguments against acceptance**: The originality is questionable as the main contributions build directly on the authors' prior NIPS 2016 work without significant new insights. The theoretical justification for the discrete-time implementation is incomplete, and the experimental evaluation is limited to simple examples that do not convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages for general machine learning problems.

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization dynamics, but falls short in demonstrating its significance for practical machine learning applications. With stronger theoretical grounding for the discrete-time algorithm and more comprehensive experimental validation, this work would be significantly more compelling. As it stands, the paper's contributions are incremental rather than transformative.
