

The paper extends prior work linking accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ODEs by introducing a generalized averaging scheme and a novel adaptive averaging strategy for its discretization. While the adaptive variant demonstrates improved performance over restarted AMD in certain cases and offers intuitive motivation, the contribution's originality and significance warrant substantial concern.  

The core technical elements—Lyapunov functions, discretization approaches, and convergence analysis—largely derive from the foundational NIPS work [7], with the extension to generalized averaging appearing trivial. The authors ultimately restrict analysis to the quadratic convergence rate case, which limits practical relevance; faster rates typically require higher-order methods (e.g., cubic regularization), which are not universally applicable. Consequently, this extension provides minimal theoretical or algorithmic novelty.  

Some results—such as the energy function interpretation, primal reformulation, and reduction to replicator dynamics—enhance understanding of AMD but do not substantially advance machine learning practice. These insights are largely conceptual and lack concrete implications for broader ML applications or algorithmic improvements.  

Critically, the adaptive averaging strategy is not fully theoretically grounded. While proposed to satisfy ODE convergence conditions, the discretization introduces approximations that undermine rigorous guarantees. The paper provides no explicit convergence proof for the adaptive discretization, rendering the approach heuristic despite the authors' claims. Furthermore, the work fails to compare the adaptive scheme against existing alternatives (e.g., other adaptive averaging strategies for Nesterov-type methods), leaving unclear whether this variant offers meaningful advantages.  

The experimental validation is weak: the toy example uses only quadratic or linear objectives, which cannot demonstrate the adaptive strategy's efficacy for general strongly convex problems. This limitation severely weakens the empirical claims, particularly given the theoretical shortcomings.  

The authors' rebuttal addresses these concerns by promising to:  
1) Add higher-dimensional and non-quadratic examples,  
2) Provide a formal convergence proof for adaptive averaging,  
3) Implement the strategy on Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method, and  
4) Include comparative analysis with alternative adaptive averaging schemes.  

These revisions would substantially strengthen the paper. Pending these additions, the current work lacks sufficient novelty and theoretical depth to justify publication. The proposed adaptive averaging remains a promising heuristic without rigorous justification or compelling empirical evidence for general settings. The incremental nature of the extension, combined with the absence of a convergence guarantee, significantly diminishes the paper’s contribution to the field.  

**Decision:** Major Revision (Pending Rebuttal Promises)  
*Note: The above assessment reflects the pre-rebuttal evaluation. The authors’ commitment to addressing the concerns in the revision justifies a conditional acceptance if all promised additions are successfully incorporated.*
