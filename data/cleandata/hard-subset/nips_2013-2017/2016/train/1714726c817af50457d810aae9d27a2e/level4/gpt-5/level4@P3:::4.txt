Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization via a continuous-time viewpoint. It introduces a broad family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) ODEs parameterized by two positive, continuous functions: η(t), which scales accumulation of gradients in the dual variable Z(t), and w(t), which determines how the primal variable X(t) is formed as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory. The authors analyze these dynamics with a Lyapunov function Lr(X, Z, t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ∗(Z, z*), and provide sufficient conditions on (η, w, r) under which Lr is decreasing, implying a convergence rate O(1/r(t)). This unifies and extends continuous-time analyses of accelerated methods (Su, Boyd, and Candès; Wibisono, Wilson, and Jordan) and connects acceleration with averaging (cf. Flammarion and Bach).

The paper also provides (i) a primal representation of the dynamics and concrete examples for the positive orthant and the simplex, yielding an “accelerated replicator dynamics,” and (ii) a new adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses weights along the trajectory to further decrease the Lyapunov function. The heuristic preserves the target rate in continuous time and, under a specific discretization, preserves the O(1/k^2) rate of first-order accelerated methods. Experiments compare the proposed adaptive averaging against adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient and speed restarts) and show comparable or improved performance, sometimes significantly, while maintaining a decreasing Lyapunov function.

Quality:
- Theory: The Lyapunov-based analysis is sound and clearly identifies sufficient conditions for monotonic decrease of the energy. The condition in Theorem 2 is intuitive and the constructive Corollary 1 gives implementable choices of (w, η) for a target r(t). The additional “energy” function Er is a nice complement.
- Discretization: The proposed discrete algorithm aligns with the continuous-time design and comes with a rate guarantee (details are deferred to the supplement). The connection to known accelerated schemes is clear.
- Experiments: The experiments are informative, especially the simplex case and visualization. However, they are small scale and mostly in R^3, with some higher-dimensional results in the supplement. Comparisons focus on adaptive restarts; broader baselines could strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and well organized, with helpful derivations and specific examples (orthant, simplex).
- Notational load is high in places; a summary table of symbols (X, Z, Ž, a(t) = w/W, etc.) would help.
- Minor but important: in Example 1 and Example 2, it appears that a(t) should be β/t (not β t) to satisfy a(t) ≥ r′(t)/r(t) for r(t) = t^p; this also matches the subsequent calculations resulting in w(t) ∝ (t/t0)^{β−1}. Please correct this typo.
- Consider avoiding the term “quadratic convergence rate” for O(1/k^2), which is sometimes reserved for Newton-type quadratic convergence; “accelerated O(1/k^2) rate” is clearer.

Originality:
- The generalized averaging formulation of AMD with explicit conditions tying (η, w, r) and the adaptive averaging heuristic are, to the best of my knowledge, novel. The accelerated replicator dynamics example is an elegant and original contribution linking optimization and evolutionary dynamics.
- The work builds thoughtfully on continuous-time interpretations of acceleration (Su et al.; Wibisono et al.) and on practical heuristics (O’Donoghue and Candès).

Significance:
- Conceptually unifying and practically relevant. The idea that acceleration can be achieved (and potentially improved) by adaptively weighting a mirror-averaged trajectory could influence the design of new adaptive accelerated methods beyond restarts.
- The continuous-time guarantees, coupled with a discretization that preserves O(1/k^2), make the contribution actionable. Wider empirical validation would further establish impact.

Strengths:
- Clear Lyapunov framework yielding simple, constructive sufficient conditions for acceleration with generalized averaging.
- Insightful primal representation and concrete examples, including accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical guarantees (preserves target rate; decreases Lyapunov and energy).
- Empirically competitive with or superior to adaptive restart heuristics while maintaining monotone Lyapunov decrease.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Experimental scope is limited. Suggestions:
  - Include larger-scale tests (higher dimensions) and additional geometries (e.g., Euclidean ball, ℓ1 ball).
  - Compare against standard accelerated methods with modern restart schemes (e.g., FISTA variants) and tuned constant momentum baselines.
  - Report sensitivity to β and step size s, and provide guidance for choosing these in practice.
  - Assess robustness under noisy/stochastic gradients; can adaptive averaging be combined with stochastic mirror descent?
- Theorem 2’s condition (2) depends on the trajectory and is hard to check a priori. While Corollary 1 gives a tractable design, more discussion on how often the adaptive heuristic achieves the “negative inner product” condition in practice would be helpful.
- Clarify the role of t0 > 0 for existence/uniqueness and its practical implications (if any) for discretization.
- Provide a concise overview of the discrete proof in the main text or a clear roadmap to the supplement; define R and Dψ when first used in Algorithm 1 and simplify steps 6–8 for readability.
- Tighten references (some entries are incomplete) and fix the noted typos.

Questions for the authors:
1. Can the framework yield linear rates for strongly convex f by choosing r(t) exponential (and corresponding η, w)? What changes in the discretization would be needed?
2. How sensitive is adaptive averaging to the choice of β and the schedule for ak updates? Any theoretical guidance or adaptive β?
3. In experiments, Er appears non-increasing even for restart heuristics. Can this be formalized, at least for certain settings?
4. Does adaptive averaging extend naturally to composite objectives (proximal operators) and constrained problems beyond mirror maps induced by entropic ψ?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated adaptive mechanism for acceleration with clear continuous-time guarantees.
- Unifying Lyapunov analysis that connects acceleration, averaging, and mirror maps.
- Concrete new dynamics (accelerated replicator) and promising empirical results.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is relatively narrow and small-scale; broader benchmarks and baselines would strengthen the case.
- Some clarity issues (typos, trajectory-dependent condition) and missing details in the main text.

Verdict:
I lean toward acceptance. The theoretical framework and the adaptive averaging idea are original and potentially impactful. Strengthening the experiments and addressing clarity issues would make the paper even more compelling.
