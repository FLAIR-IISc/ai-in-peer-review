- Paper proposes accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization via coupling a dual variable (accumulating gradients at rate η(t)) and a primal variable (weighted average of mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t)).  
- Uses a Lyapunov argument to derive sufficient conditions on η and w to achieve target convergence rates.  
- Demonstrates acceleration of replicator dynamics (mirror descent on the simplex) using a simple averaging scheme.  
- Introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights online to speed up Lyapunov decrease.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees for adaptive averaging in continuous time and shows preservation of quadratic accelerated rates in discrete time.  
- Presents numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging to adaptive restarting, showing comparable or improved performance in some cases.  
- Reviewer has limited expertise but finds the core idea of adaptive averaging for constrained convex optimization interesting and promising.  
- Quality: technically sound with a solid Lyapunov foundation and careful continuous/discrete analysis, though practical implications of the η and w conditions need more discussion.  
- Novelty: adaptive averaging and its theoretical guarantees are a novel contribution, though the work builds on substantial prior literature in accelerated methods.  
- Impact: potentially high for large-scale convex optimization and applications like machine learning and evolutionary game theory if the heuristic is robust in practice.  
- Clarity: generally well-written and organized but some sections are dense; more intuition and discussion of related adaptive optimizers (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) and links to Wibisono et al. would help.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical foundation, clear convergence guarantees, promising experiments, novel adaptive averaging approach, and well-defined problem/solution.  
- Arguments against acceptance: η and w conditions may be hard to satisfy in practice, related-work discussion could be deeper, some sections lack intuition, and practical improvements may be marginal in many cases.
