This paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic to accelerate constrained convex optimization. The method's primary advantage, demonstrated both theoretically and experimentally, is its guarantee to preserve the original convergence rate for objective functions that are not strongly convex. This feature distinguishes it from existing restarting heuristics, and the paper effectively supports this claim.

However, the paper has several areas that could be improved:

**Clarity and Presentation:**
The paper's accessibility could be enhanced. While the terminology and abbreviations may be standard for experts in this subfield, including additional background information would make the work more approachable for a broader audience. Specifically, the equations presented around lines 33-34 and 46-47 would benefit from more intuitive explanations to help the reader build a better understanding of the underlying mechanism.

**Relation to Other Work:**
The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of its relationship to adaptive methods used in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]. While the setting is different (convex vs. non-convex), these popular methods also employ adaptive schemes, and clarifying the connections or key differences would provide valuable context.

**Practical Significance and Scalability:**
A significant concern is the practical relevance of the proposed method. The empirical validation is performed in R^3, which is a low-dimensional setting. This raises questions about the scalability and applicability of the algorithm to problems commonly found in machine learning. The authors should comment on how their method, as well as related approaches, is expected to perform in much higher-dimensional spaces. Including experiments on higher-dimensional problems would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

**Minor Comment:**
The reference to the "solid line" in Figure 1 is ambiguous. To improve clarity, consider using distinct line styles (e.g., dotted vs. solid) or colors to differentiate the trajectory of the primal variable.
