Summary:
The paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization and uses a Lyapunov function to establish convergence rates. Building on this analysis, the authors introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic intended to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function and thereby improve practical convergence. Empirical results suggest that adaptive averaging performs on par with, or better than, adaptive restarting.

Strengths:
- Addresses constrained convex optimization, which is highly relevant in practice.
- Employs a Lyapunov framework to justify convergence rates, offering a principled analysis.
- Proposes a simple adaptive averaging mechanism that appears effective in experiments.
- The comparison to adaptive restarting is appropriate and helps position the contribution.
- If easy to implement, the heuristic could be broadly useful.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Assumptions: Please clearly state the precise assumptions under which the Lyapunov-based rate holds (e.g., smoothness, Lipschitz gradients, strong convexity or general convexity, properties of the constraint set, availability and cost of projections or prox operators). It would help to specify whether the results extend to composite problems with a nonsmooth regularizer via proximal steps.
- Rates and constants: What exact rate is obtained (e.g., O(1/k^2) in function value for general convexity; linear under strong convexity)? Are the constants explicit, and are they comparable to known accelerated methods? Is there any gap between the Lyapunov decrease and the practical error metrics users care about (objective gap, gradient mapping norm, feasibility)?
- Role of averaging: Provide more intuition for how adaptive averaging interacts with the Lyapunov decrease. Does the heuristic target a provable surrogate decrease, improve constants, or merely yield empirical acceleration? If there are partial theoretical guarantees (e.g., monotonicity of a potential under certain conditions), please state them.
- Comparison to related techniques: Please position the proposed averaging relative to existing averaging schemes in convex optimization (e.g., weighted/primal/dual averaging, averaging in mirror descent) and to established adaptive restart criteria in accelerated methods. What is fundamentally new here—using averaging in a constrained accelerated setting, the specific adaptive rule, or the Lyapunov construction?
- Practical details: The paper would benefit from a clear algorithm box/pseudocode specifying step sizes, momentum/acceleration terms, projection/prox operations, and the adaptive averaging rule (including initialization, update triggers, and weights).
- Sensitivity and overhead: How sensitive is the method to hyperparameters (step sizes, averaging weights)? What is the computational overhead of the averaging heuristic compared to adaptive restart? Is there any adverse effect on per-iteration cost or memory?
- Robustness and scope: How does the approach behave with inexact projections, noisy gradients, or constraints that are expensive to project onto? Does it scale to high dimensions and large datasets?
- Experiments: Please provide more details on the experimental setup: types of constrained problems, dimensions, stopping criteria, and evaluation metrics. Include multiple baselines (standard accelerated methods with and without restart), and report variance across runs if randomness is involved. An ablation isolating the effect of adaptive averaging (versus plain averaging and versus restart) would strengthen the empirical case.

Quality:
The theoretical framing via a Lyapunov function appears sound and appropriate for establishing rates. To fully assess quality, the paper should:
- State all assumptions explicitly and discuss their necessity.
- Provide complete proofs or clear outlines with pointers to the appendix.
- Clarify the relationship between Lyapunov decrease and standard performance measures under constraints.
- Include explicit constants and a discussion of tightness or potential room for improvement.

Novelty:
The combination of accelerated dynamics, constraints, and adaptive averaging seems potentially novel, especially if the averaging rule is new and tailored to the Lyapunov decrease. However, the paper should more clearly differentiate its contributions from prior work on (i) accelerated projected/proximal methods, (ii) adaptive restart schemes, and (iii) averaging techniques in convex optimization. A concise related-work section that maps similarities and differences would help establish novelty.

Impact:
If the method consistently matches or improves upon adaptive restarting across a range of constrained problems, with minimal tuning and computational overhead, it could have meaningful practical impact. The Lyapunov-based analysis may also inform future designs of adaptive mechanisms in accelerated methods. Broader impact will depend on clarity of assumptions, generality to composite and non-Euclidean settings, and robustness in large-scale scenarios.

Clarity:
The paper is generally understandable, but clarity can be improved by:
- Providing high-level intuition for the Lyapunov construction and its link to averaging.
- Including algorithmic pseudocode and a concise assumptions box.
- Ensuring notation is consistent and symbols are defined where first used.
- Presenting clear plots of objective gap and feasibility, along with Lyapunov values, to illustrate the claimed acceleration.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a detailed algorithm description and an assumptions summary.
- Provide theoretical insight into the adaptive averaging rule, even if partial.
- Expand experimental comparisons and include ablations and sensitivity analyses.
- Discuss extensions to composite objectives and alternative norms.
- Consider releasing code to support reproducibility.

Overall assessment:
A promising and relevant contribution that proposes an intuitive adaptive averaging mechanism within a Lyapunov-analyzed accelerated framework for constrained convex optimization. With clearer assumptions, stronger positioning relative to existing methods, and more comprehensive empirical evidence, the paper could make a solid addition. My confidence is limited due to lack of deep expertise in this subarea, but I see potential if the above concerns are addressed.
