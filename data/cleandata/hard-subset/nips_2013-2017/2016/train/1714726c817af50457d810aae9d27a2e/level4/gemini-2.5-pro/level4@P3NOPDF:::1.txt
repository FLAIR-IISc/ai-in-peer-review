This paper presents a novel adaptive averaging scheme for Nesterov's accelerated gradient method. Building upon the ordinary differential equation (ODE) interpretation of acceleration by Krichene et al. (2015), the authors propose a natural and intuitive heuristic for adaptively reweighing the algorithm's iterates to improve its convergence speed. A key theoretical contribution is that, in contrast to previous adaptive heuristics, this method is guaranteed to dominate the standard fixed-schedule averaging scheme. The efficacy of the proposed method is demonstrated empirically, showing significant speed-ups on a general class of functions.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read. The core mathematical argument is elegant, simple, and easy to follow, particularly for readers familiar with the foundational work of Krichene et al.

2.  **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating promising speed-ups across a general class of functions. This provides strong evidence for the practical utility of the proposed adaptive scheme.

3.  **High Potential Impact:** Despite the technical development being an incremental extension of prior work, the potential impact of this paper is substantial. Nesterov's accelerated method is a cornerstone algorithm in optimization and machine learning. Often, novel adaptive methods are benchmarked against a standard, non-adaptive implementation of Nesterov's algorithm. This paper convincingly shows that a simple, well-motivated modification is all that is required to make the classic method itself adaptive, leading to dramatic performance improvements. This finding could have a significant influence on how optimization algorithms are implemented and benchmarked in practice.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Analysis of the Discretized Algorithm:** The current analysis is performed in the continuous-time ODE setting. To make the theoretical guarantees more rigorous and directly applicable to the implemented algorithm, the paper would be significantly strengthened by an analysis of the corresponding discretized process. Such an analysis should formally establish that the optimal rate of convergence is preserved under the proposed adaptive scheme. Given the existing framework, this analysis appears to be a tractable and valuable addition.

2.  **Connection to Conjugate Gradient:** It would be interesting to see a discussion on the potential connections between this adaptive averaging scheme and the Conjugate Gradient (CG) method, particularly in the context of strongly convex quadratic optimization. Since both methods are known to achieve acceleration, exploring how this adaptive reweighing relates to the coefficient choices in CG (e.g., those derived from the Lanczos process) could provide deeper insights into the nature of acceleration.

Overall, I enjoyed reading this paper and believe it makes a valuable practical contribution. The proposed method is simple, effective, and addresses a highly relevant problem, making it of broad interest to the community.
