

This paper investigates a class of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) with a generalized averaging framework, establishing sufficient conditions on the dual learning rate and weight parameters to achieve a specified convergence rate via Lyapunov analysis. The authors propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that accelerates the decay of the Lyapunov function, thereby enhancing convergence speed. The work provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation through numerical comparisons.  

While numerous prior studies have sought empirical acceleration for optimization methods, this paper offers a novel perspective by demonstrating—through ODE analysis—that adaptively averaging weights along solution trajectories achieves acceleration. This insight bridges the gap between continuous-time modeling and practical acceleration techniques, revealing a fundamental mechanism for improving convergence without modifying the core optimization dynamics. The adaptive averaging strategy is particularly compelling as it leverages trajectory-dependent information to dynamically adjust weighting, avoiding the need for manual tuning of hyperparameters.  

The theoretical contribution is well-motivated and rigorously supported. The Lyapunov argument is sound, with clear conditions for convergence rate guarantees that explicitly account for the interplay between learning rates and weight updates. The adaptive heuristic is intuitively justified and shown to significantly reduce the Lyapunov function's decay time in experiments. Numerical results corroborate the theoretical claims, demonstrating consistent speedups across benchmark problems compared to non-adaptive baselines.  

A key strength lies in the paper's ability to distill a complex empirical observation (acceleration via adaptive weighting) into a precise continuous-time framework. This not only validates the heuristic but also opens avenues for further theoretical exploration of adaptive schemes in optimization. The clarity of the ODE formulation and its connection to discrete optimization algorithms is commendable, making the results accessible to both continuous and discrete optimization communities.  

The presentation is concise and focused, with minimal redundancy. The comparison to related work is appropriately contextualized, emphasizing how this approach differs from existing acceleration methods that rely on momentum or adaptive step sizes. One minor suggestion for future work would be to explore the robustness of the adaptive scheme under noisy or non-convex settings, though this is not required for the current contribution.  

Overall, this is a strong submission that makes a significant, well-executed contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization. The core idea—using trajectory-dependent adaptive averaging to achieve acceleration via ODE analysis—is both novel and impactful. The combination of rigorous theory and empirical evidence effectively supports the claims. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. I recommend acceptance.
