This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate constrained convex optimization algorithms. The core contribution is a method that, unlike standard restarting heuristics, provably preserves the original convergence rate for objective functions that are not strongly convex. The authors support this key advantage with both theoretical analysis and experimental validation.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work is its theoretical guarantee. The authors demonstrate that their adaptive averaging scheme maintains the baseline O(1/k) convergence rate for non-strongly convex functions. This is a notable improvement over traditional restarting methods, which can fail to converge at the optimal rate in such settings. This theoretical advantage is well-supported by the provided experimental results, which corroborate the claims made in the paper.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
While the paper presents a technically sound contribution, its accessibility and practical significance could be substantially improved.

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper's presentation could be improved to make it more accessible to a broader audience within the optimization community. While the core ideas are likely clear to experts in this specific subfield, the heavy use of specialized terminology and abbreviations without sufficient background may hinder understanding for other readers. Including a brief preliminary section or providing more in-line definitions for key concepts would significantly enhance the paper's clarity and impact.

2.  **Intuition Behind Equations:** The derivation could benefit from more intuition. Specifically, a high-level explanation of the equations presented between lines 33-34 and 46-47 would help readers grasp the underlying mechanism of the proposed method.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** A significant concern is the limited empirical evaluation. The experiments are conducted in a low-dimensional setting (R^3), which may not be representative of the high-dimensional problems typically encountered in machine learning. This makes it difficult to assess the practical significance and scalability of the proposed method.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the relationship between your adaptive averaging method and those used in non-convex stochastic optimization, such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]? Does your proposed technique share any underlying principles with these methods, or is the similarity in naming purely terminological? A discussion contextualizing your work within this broader family of adaptive methods would be valuable.

2.  How is the performance of the proposed method, as well as related restarting heuristics, expected to scale to much higher dimensions (e.g., thousands or millions of dimensions)? A discussion on the computational complexity and potential scalability challenges, even if not accompanied by new experiments, would greatly strengthen the paper by addressing its practical applicability.
