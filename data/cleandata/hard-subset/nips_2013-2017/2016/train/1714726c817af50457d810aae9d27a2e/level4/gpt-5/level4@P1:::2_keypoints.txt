- Paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) via generalized averaging coupling dual Z (gradient accumulator) and primal X (weighted average), proving convergence rate 1/r(t) via Lyapunov Lr and energy Er functions.
- Framework recovers known AMD/ODE results (Su et al. 2014; Wibisono et al. 2016; Krichene et al. 2015) and emphasizes acceleration-as-averaging, focusing in practice on r(t)=t^2.
- Provides a primal representation showing how ∇^2ψ*∘∇ψ shapes flows and gives examples including an accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex.
- Introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that in continuous time preserves the Lyapunov decrease and presents a discrete implementation compared empirically to restarting heuristics.
- Strength: Clean, unified Lyapunov analysis with clear sufficient conditions for target rates across a broad class of AMD dynamics.
- Strength: The energy function Er complements Lr and connects to Hamiltonian/inertial intuitions.
- Strength: Primal reformulation yields concrete examples and the accelerated replicator dynamics are of independent interest.
- Strength: Adaptive averaging is intuitive and the continuous-time theory supports it; discrete implementation shows competitive empirical performance versus restarting.
- Weakness: Limited originality—analysis closely follows prior AMD/ODE work and generalized averaging is an incremental extension centered on quadratic rate.
- Weakness: Practical usefulness of the generalized averaging conditions is unclear because key conditions depend on trajectory quantities like ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ that are not checkable a priori.
- Weakness: Discrete adaptive averaging is presented as a heuristic without a formal convergence proof or rate guarantee in the submission.
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation is weak—limited to low-dimensional/quadratic/linear/KL problems and missing higher-dimensional, non-quadratic, and strongly convex tests or comparisons to other averaging strategies.
- Weakness: Limited practical guidance—insufficient discussion on selecting η(t), w(t), β, step sizes; no ablations or stability/sensitivity analysis.
- Quality assessment: Strong continuous-time analysis but discrete-time adaptive averaging lacks formal guarantees; sufficient conditions may be hard to use constructively beyond standard schedules.
- Clarity assessment: Generally clear and organized though notation is heavy and relation to key prior work could be sharpened; minor typos remain.
- Originality assessment: Moderate—generalized averaging and adaptive heuristic add some novelty but are close in spirit to existing adaptive/restarting ideas.
- Significance assessment: Potentially useful if supported by stronger discrete-time theory and broader experiments; current evidence insufficient to establish impact.
- Suggested improvement: Provide a rigorous convergence and rate analysis for the discrete adaptive averaging algorithm under standard assumptions.
- Suggested improvement: Expand experiments to higher dimensions, non-quadratic objectives, strongly convex and merely convex cases, include hyperparameter sensitivity and ablations, and compare to more baselines.
- Suggested improvement: Clarify practical construction/selection of η and w for general r(t) and discuss limitations when faster rates require higher-order methods.
- Suggested improvement: Strengthen related-work discussion to better differentiate contributions from prior AMD and ODE-based acceleration papers.
- Pros for acceptance: Unified continuous-time analysis with interpretable Lyapunov/energy functions, interesting accelerated replicator dynamics, and an intuitive adaptive averaging idea with promising empirical comparisons to restarting.
- Cons for acceptance: Incremental novelty vis-à-vis prior NIPS work, missing discrete-time convergence guarantees, and limited low-dimensional experiments with incomplete baseline comparisons.
- Post-rebuttal commitments: Authors plan to add higher-dimensional and non-quadratic experiments, provide a theoretical convergence proof for discrete adaptive averaging, implement adaptive averaging for cubic-regularized Newton, and compare with other adaptive averaging approaches.
