This paper analyzes accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, considering both continuous-time and discrete-time settings. The work builds upon the research initiated by Krichene et al. [7], which interprets Nesterov's accelerated method as a discretization of a system of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) that couples primal and dual trajectories. In that framework, the primal trajectory is generated by a specific averaging of the mirror of the dual variable. This paper generalizes this approach by investigating a broader class of averaging schemes.

A key contribution is Theorem 2, which establishes convergence rates for these general schemes, contingent on certain conditions imposed on the averaging coefficients. In the continuous-time setting, the authors derive convergence rates that are faster than the classical O(1/t^2) rate. The paper also proposes an adaptive scheme for selecting these averaging weights, provides corresponding convergence guarantees, and demonstrates its performance in the experimental section. This adaptive scheme is benchmarked against standard restarting techniques, which are known to empirically improve the convergence of accelerated methods.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-written paper. The analysis of the novel averaging scheme for accelerated methods in the continuous-time setting is rigorous and well-presented. Although the high-level analytical framework for continuous-time dynamics is now well-established, this work provides novel contributions and theoretical results. In this respect, the influential work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and their coauthors on this topic should be cited and discussed to better contextualize the contribution.

The main theorem demonstrating the existence of averaging schemes with convergence rates faster than O(1/t^2) is a significant contribution. However, the theoretical analysis is confined to the continuous-time setting; a formal analysis of the discrete-time version of the algorithm is not included. This raises a crucial open question: is the improved convergence rate demonstrated for the continuous dynamics preserved after discretization? Understanding whether this theoretical gain translates to a practical, discrete-time algorithm would greatly strengthen the paper's impact.
