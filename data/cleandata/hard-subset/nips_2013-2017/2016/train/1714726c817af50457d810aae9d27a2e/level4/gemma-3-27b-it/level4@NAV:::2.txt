This paper builds upon prior work linking accelerated Mirror Descent to continuous-time ODEs, extending the ODE dynamics with a generalized averaging scheme and introducing a novel adaptive averaging strategy for discretization. While the proposed adaptive averaging demonstrates performance improvements over existing AMD algorithms with restarting in certain scenarios, and the strategy itself is intuitively appealing, concerns regarding the overall originality and practical impact remain. 

The work largely follows directly from a previous NIPS publication [7], with the generalization from simple to generalized averaging providing limited benefit as the authors focus exclusively on quadratic convergence rates; achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods that may not be universally applicable.  Several results – including the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation in primal form, and reduction to replicator dynamics – enhance our understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent, but do not represent a substantial advancement for the field of machine learning. 

The adaptive averaging is motivated by conditions sufficient for ODE convergence, but lacks rigorous theoretical justification due to approximations introduced during discretization. Critically, the paper does not provide explicit convergence guarantees, and thus remains largely heuristic, as acknowledged by the authors. Furthermore, given the multitude of existing averaging schemes for Nesterov’s accelerated algorithms, the benefit of this adaptive variant is unclear without comparative analysis.  The experimental evaluation, limited to a toy example with quadratic/linear objectives, is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of adaptive averaging, particularly in the context of general strongly convex problems.
