

This paper extends Krichene et al.'s NIPS 2015 work on accelerated mirror descent dynamics by introducing a principled adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates while empirically outperforming existing methods. The authors generalize the continuous-time framework from [11] (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015) to a broad family of accelerated dynamics parameterized by weight functions $w(t)$ and $\eta(t)$. Their key contribution is a Lyapunov-based sufficient condition (Theorem 2) ensuring convergence at rate $1/r(t)$, which they leverage to design an adaptive scheme that dynamically adjusts $w(t)$ to minimize the Lyapunov function's time derivative. Crucially, this adaptive heuristic guarantees rate preservation—a property lacking in prior restart-based approaches (e.g., [17, 20]).

**Strengths**  
*Technical Soundness & Clarity*: The continuous-time analysis is rigorous and elegantly structured. The Lyapunov argument (Theorem 2) clearly establishes the conditions under which convergence rates are preserved, with Corollary 1 providing a constructive path to valid weight functions. The primal representation (Section 3) and examples (simplex dynamics as accelerated replicator equations) demonstrate strong theoretical grounding and practical relevance. The discrete adaptation (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated and validated via thorough experiments across multiple problem classes (strongly convex quadratics, linear functions, KL divergence). The exposition is exceptionally clear for a technical paper, with well-chosen examples and a logical flow from theory to practice.  

*Originality & Significance*: While building on Krichene et al., the adaptive averaging mechanism is novel in its rate-preserving guarantee and simplicity. The work directly addresses a critical gap in acceleration literature: most adaptive heuristics (e.g., restarts) lack theoretical guarantees for non-strongly convex objectives. The significance is high—accelerated methods are ubiquitous in ML (e.g., in optimization libraries like PyTorch), yet practitioners often use fixed-weight schedules. This work shows that a minimal adaptive modification (Algorithm 1) can yield substantial empirical gains (1.5–3× speedups in key cases, as shown in Figure 2). The replicator dynamics application (Section 3.2) further broadens impact to evolutionary game theory and viability theory.  

*Empirical Validation*: Experiments comprehensively compare adaptive averaging against restart heuristics across diverse objectives and dimensions. The results consistently demonstrate superiority (e.g., significant gains in strongly convex cases) and confirm Lyapunov function monotonicity (a theoretical guarantee not satisfied by restarts). The energy function analysis (Theorem 3) provides valuable mechanistic insight into the method’s behavior.  

**Weaknesses**  
*Discrete-Time Convergence Proof*: The paper claims the discrete algorithm preserves the $1/t^2$ rate but lacks a formal proof. While the supplementary material references a discrete Lyapunov argument, the main text should explicitly state this result (e.g., "Algorithm 1 achieves $O(1/k^2)$ convergence under standard assumptions") to satisfy NIPS quality criteria. This is a critical omission for a theory-focused venue.  

*Limited Discussion of Broader Connections*: The draft mentions "possible connections to Conjugate Gradient (CG)" but does not explore them. For strongly convex quadratics, accelerated methods relate to CG via the conjugate gradient iteration; the authors could briefly discuss how adaptive averaging might interface with CG’s convergence properties (e.g., via the Hessian-driven damping in [23]).  

**Recommendation**  
This is a strong paper with high significance for the optimization community. The technical contribution is sound, the empirical validation is robust, and the work directly addresses a practical limitation of accelerated methods. The only necessary revision is to formally establish the discrete-time convergence rate in the main text (e.g., via a lemma or expanded discussion of the supplementary proof). Without this, the theoretical foundation remains incomplete.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- Rate-preserving adaptive heuristic for acceleration (novel guarantee)  
- Empirical improvements across diverse problems  
- Clear theoretical framework extending Krichene et al.  
- High relevance to ML practice  

*Con*:  
- Missing discrete-time convergence proof (critical for quality)  
- Limited discussion of CG connections (minor)  

**Final Assessment**  
The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theory and practice of accelerated optimization. Its technical quality meets NIPS standards, and the significance is compelling given the ubiquity of acceleration in machine learning. With the discrete convergence rate added, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The authors should prioritize this revision to strengthen the theoretical foundation. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
