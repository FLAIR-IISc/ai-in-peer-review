

Review:  
This paper extends prior work connecting accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ODEs by introducing a generalized averaging scheme and a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one of its discretizations. While the adaptive averaging strategy is intuitively appealing and demonstrates improved performance over existing AMD variants with restarting in certain cases, the paper's originality and significance remain questionable.  

The core technical contributions—such as the Lyapunov function, discretization approach, and generalized averaging extension—largely derive from an earlier NIPS paper [7]. The extension to generalized averaging appears trivial, as the authors ultimately restrict analysis to quadratic rates; faster convergence rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which are not universally applicable. Additionally, while results like the energy function interpretation, primal reformulation, and reduction to replicator dynamics enhance understanding of AMD, they do not substantially advance machine learning.  

The adaptive averaging strategy is introduced to satisfy ODE convergence conditions, but it lacks full theoretical grounding due to discretization approximations. Crucially, no explicit convergence guarantee is provided for the discrete algorithm, rendering it merely heuristic, as acknowledged by the authors. Moreover, without comparative analysis against alternative averaging schemes for Nesterov’s accelerated algorithms, it remains unclear whether this adaptive variant offers meaningful improvements.  

The experimental evaluation, limited to a toy example with only quadratic/linear objectives, fails to convincingly demonstrate advantages for the general strongly convex case.  

Post-rebuttal update: I have read the authors' rebuttal and their commitment to address these concerns in revision, including: (1) additional higher-dimensional and non-quadratic examples; (2) a theoretical convergence proof for adaptive averaging; (3) implementation on Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method; and (4) a comparative discussion of alternative adaptive averaging strategies. I have updated my score accordingly.
