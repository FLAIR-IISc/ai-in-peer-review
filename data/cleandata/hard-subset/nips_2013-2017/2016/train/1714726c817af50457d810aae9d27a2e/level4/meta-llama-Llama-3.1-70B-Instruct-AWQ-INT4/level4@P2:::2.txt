This paper proposes an extension of the accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics with a more general averaging scheme and introduces a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one of its discretizations. The authors build upon an early work that connects AMD and continuous-time ODEs. The paper is well-written and provides a good intuition behind the proposed adaptive averaging strategy.

The authors provide a unified study of the proposed ODE using an appropriate Lyapunov function and give sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing. They also provide a primal representation of the ODE and study some examples, including the replicator dynamics. The adaptive averaging heuristic is proposed to satisfy the conditions sufficient to guarantee convergence of the ODE. The authors also provide some numerical experiments to compare the performance of the proposed adaptive averaging with existing restarting heuristics.

However, the originality and usefulness of the paper remain a concern. Most elements, such as the Lyapunov function and discretization, directly follow from an early NIPS paper. The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging is not particularly significant, as the authors only consider the case with quadratic rate. Faster rates would require higher-order methods, which are not always applicable.

Some results, such as the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation into primal form, and reduction to replicator dynamics, promote a better understanding of the AMD algorithm. However, these contributions are not significantly important to advance machine learning.

The adaptive averaging heuristic is not fully theoretically grounded due to the approximation during discretization. There is no explicit convergent result provided, meaning it is again just a heuristic, as referred to by the authors. Additionally, there exist a number of different averaging schemes of Nesterov's accelerated algorithms; without comparing to other alternatives, it is unclear whether this adaptive variant makes a significant difference.

The experiment on a toy example with only quadratic/linear objective is not interesting or convincing enough to demonstrate the advantage of adaptive averaging, especially for the general case with strongly convex functions.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should consider adding more substantial contributions, such as theoretical proof of convergence of adaptive averaging, implementation of adaptive averaging on Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method, and discussion comparing to other adaptive averaging schemes. They should also provide more comprehensive experiments to demonstrate the advantage of adaptive averaging in various scenarios.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

1. The paper proposes a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one of the discretizations of the proposed ODE.
2. The authors provide a unified study of the proposed ODE using an appropriate Lyapunov function and give sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to be decreasing.
3. The paper promotes a better understanding of the AMD algorithm through the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation into primal form, and reduction to replicator dynamics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. The originality and usefulness of the paper remain a concern, as most elements directly follow from an early NIPS paper.
2. The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging is not particularly significant, as the authors only consider the case with quadratic rate.
3. The adaptive averaging heuristic is not fully theoretically grounded due to the approximation during discretization.
4. The experiment on a toy example with only quadratic/linear objective is not interesting or convincing enough to demonstrate the advantage of adaptive averaging.
