- Studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with generalized averaging: dual accumulates gradients at rate η(t) and primal is a weighted average w(t); uses Lyapunov arguments to derive conditions for a target convergence rate r(t).
- Provides an equivalent primal formulation and interprets an energy/Lyapunov function that decreases under those conditions.
- Shows on the simplex the dynamics yield an accelerated replicator equation, linking optimization and evolutionary dynamics.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses weights to reduce the Lyapunov derivative, with continuous-time guarantees and an argued discretization preserving quadratic rates.
- Empirically compares adaptive averaging to adaptive restarting and reports comparable or improved performance on low-dimensional simplex-constrained tasks.
- Builds on prior ODE/AMD literature (Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan; Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett) and frames adaptive averaging as an alternative to restart heuristics.
- Strength: Clear Lyapunov-based framework that unifies several accelerated dynamics and gives simple sufficient conditions for target rates.
- Strength: Interpretable energy function and primal reformulation that improve intuition about AMD and mirror-map constraint handling.
- Strength: Accelerated replicator dynamics provide a neat illustrative connection between optimization and evolutionary dynamics.
- Strength: Adaptive averaging rule is simple and in continuous time preserves the desired rate by design.
- Strength: Empirical results show the heuristic performs at least as well as adaptive restarting on reported tasks.
- Weakness: Novelty is limited; much of the setup closely follows prior AMD/ODE work and generalized averaging feels incremental.
- Weakness: Several insights (energy interpretation, primal form, replicator reduction) may not, by themselves, substantially advance ML practice.
- Weakness: Discrete adaptive averaging is presented as a heuristic; no explicit discrete-time convergence guarantees are given in the current draft.
- Weakness: Experiments are narrow (toy, low-dimensional, simplex-constrained quadratic/linear objectives) and omit other averaging schemes and higher-dimensional/strongly convex tests.
- Quality: Continuous-time theory is technically sound; the discrete algorithm currently lacks a complete convergence proof.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized but needs a sharper statement of novelty versus prior work and broader experiments.
- Originality: Moderate—adaptive averaging is interesting but the overall framework is incremental relative to existing ODE analyses and restart heuristics.
- Significance: Potentially useful if adaptive averaging extends reliably to practical discretizations and broader problems; significance is uncertain given current limits.
- Arguments for acceptance: Clean unified continuous-time analysis, intuitive heuristic with promising empirical behavior, and interesting connections (replicator).
- Arguments against acceptance: Incremental novelty, heuristic discrete method, insufficient experimental scope and comparative baselines.
- Post-rebuttal: Authors promise higher-dimensional/non-quadratic experiments, a discrete convergence proof, cubic-regularized Newton implementation, and comparisons to other adaptive averaging schemes; satisfactory additions would mitigate main concerns.
