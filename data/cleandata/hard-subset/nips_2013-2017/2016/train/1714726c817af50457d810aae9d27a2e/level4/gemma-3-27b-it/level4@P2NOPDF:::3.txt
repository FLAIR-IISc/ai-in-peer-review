This paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization problems, considering both continuous and discrete settings. Building upon the work of Krichene et al. [7], which demonstrated a connection between Nesterov’s method and the discretization of an ordinary differential equation (ODE) system involving primal and dual trajectories, this paper generalizes the averaging schemes used to construct the primal trajectory. Specifically, the authors explore more flexible averaging strategies based on the dual variable’s mirror map. Theorem 2 establishes convergence rates under appropriate conditions on the averaging coefficients.

In the continuous setting, the paper derives convergence rates exceeding the standard 1/t<sup>2</sup> rate, representing a notable improvement.  An adaptive weighting scheme, accompanied by convergence guarantees, is also proposed and empirically evaluated. This adaptive approach is then benchmarked against restarting techniques—a common practical strategy for enhancing the convergence of accelerated methods.  Importantly, the theoretical analysis focusing on the discrete version of the algorithm is currently omitted. 

Overall, this is a well-written and interesting contribution. The analysis of the proposed averaging schemes is rigorous and presented clearly within the established framework of continuous dynamics analysis, a field now well-developed through a series of recent publications. While the core ideas draw on existing literature, the paper provides new contributions and results in this area. In this context, acknowledging related work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and their collaborators would be beneficial. The main theoretical result – demonstrating the existence of averaging schemes that achieve faster convergence than 1/t<sup>2</sup> – is significant.  However, a key area for future work would be to investigate whether this improved convergence rate is maintained when transitioning from the continuous model to the discrete implementation of the algorithm.
