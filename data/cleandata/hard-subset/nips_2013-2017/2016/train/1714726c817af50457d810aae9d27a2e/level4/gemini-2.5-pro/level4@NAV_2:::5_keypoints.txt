- Presents a unified continuous-time framework for accelerated mirror descent, modeling dual gradient accumulation η(t) and primal averaging w(t) and deriving 1/r(t) rates via a Lyapunov function.  
- Uses the Lyapunov approach to give sufficient conditions linking η(t) and w(t) to provable convergence rates, generalizing prior accelerated ODE results.  
- Proposes a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic that greedily adjusts w(t) to decrease the Lyapunov function and empirically accelerate convergence.  
- Provides a discrete-time implementation and empirical comparisons showing adaptive averaging matches or outperforms popular restarting heuristics in experiments.  
- Guarantees that adaptive averaging preserves the baseline 1/r(t) convergence rate, addressing a theoretical gap of some restart methods.  
- Gives an instructive application: an accelerated replicator dynamics for simplex-constrained problems connecting to evolutionary game theory.  
- Lacks theoretical guarantees on how much adaptive averaging can improve rates (e.g., no proof it attains faster linear rates on strongly convex problems).  
- Experimental validation is limited to low-dimensional (R^3) simplex problems, so practical performance on high-dimensional ML tasks is untested.  
- Detailed convergence proof for the discrete algorithm is deferred to the supplement; the main text omits a brief discrete Lyapunov sketch.  
- Overall assessment: high technical quality, clear exposition, original contribution, and significant potential impact as a rate-preserving alternative to restarting heuristics.
