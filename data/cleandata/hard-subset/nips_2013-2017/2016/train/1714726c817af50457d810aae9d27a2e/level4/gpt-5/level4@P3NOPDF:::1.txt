This paper builds on the ODE interpretation of Nesterov’s acceleration introduced by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) to design an adaptive averaging scheme that reweights iterates in order to accelerate convergence. In contrast to prior adaptive averaging heuristics, the proposed method is guaranteed to dominate the standard fixed-schedule averaging. Empirically, it yields consistent speed-ups across a broad class of objective functions.

I found the paper clear, well motivated, and enjoyable to read. The mathematical development is simple and easy to follow, particularly for readers familiar with Krichene et al. The empirical results are compelling and suggest that a modest modification to the standard accelerated method can deliver substantial practical gains. Given how widely Nesterov’s acceleration is used, the potential impact is significant: many empirical comparisons pit sophisticated adaptive methods against a baseline using a fixed averaging schedule for Nesterov’s method. This paper convincingly argues that making the accelerated method itself adaptive is both straightforward and effective.

Strengths:
- Clear and principled derivation from the ODE viewpoint of accelerated methods.
- A simple adaptive reweighting rule with a clean guarantee that it dominates fixed-schedule averaging.
- Broad and convincing empirical evaluation showing consistent speed-ups.
- High practical relevance due to the ubiquity of Nesterov’s acceleration in applications.

Limitations and suggestions:
- The technical contribution is, by design, a close extension of Krichene et al. While this does not diminish the practical value, it would help to better delineate what is new here beyond the domination guarantee and the specific adaptive rule.
- The paper would be strengthened by an explicit discrete-time analysis showing that the convergence rate of the discretized algorithm matches the continuous-time intuition. If I understand correctly, this should be relatively straightforward under standard smoothness/convexity assumptions, and would make the theoretical story more self-contained.
- It would be interesting to discuss potential connections to the Conjugate Gradient method for strongly convex quadratic objectives. Both methods can be seen as generating iterates that implicitly exploit curvature information; your adaptive averaging might have an interpretation related to CG’s residual orthogonality or to polynomial acceleration. Even a short discussion or empirical comparison on quadratics would add insight.
- To aid practitioners, consider clarifying the precise sense in which the adaptive method “dominates” fixed averaging (e.g., iteration-wise objective value, running average, or after a fixed computational budget), and whether the guarantee holds uniformly across smooth convex and strongly convex settings.
- A brief discussion of computational overhead and implementation details (e.g., how to maintain the adaptive weights efficiently and any sensitivity to hyperparameters) would make the method easier to adopt.

Minor comments:
- Ensure consistency when referencing Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) and clearly contrast their ODE framework with your adaptive weighting contribution.
- If space permits, include ablations isolating the effect of the adaptive reweighting from other implementation choices (e.g., step-size tuning), to attribute the observed gains more precisely.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-written, practically important paper that offers a simple, effective improvement to a widely used baseline. While the theoretical novelty is incremental, the guarantee against fixed-schedule averaging and the strong empirical performance make the contribution valuable. With an added discrete-time convergence analysis and a brief discussion of links to Conjugate Gradient on quadratics, the paper would be even stronger. I recommend acceptance.
