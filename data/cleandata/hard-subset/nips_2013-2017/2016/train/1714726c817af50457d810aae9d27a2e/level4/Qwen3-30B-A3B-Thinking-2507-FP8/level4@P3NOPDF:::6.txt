

This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate constrained convex optimization. The key contribution lies in its theoretical guarantee that the method preserves the original convergence rate when the objective function lacks strong convexity—a significant advantage over existing restarting heuristics. The authors substantiate this claim through rigorous theoretical analysis and empirical validation, demonstrating the method's efficacy in both convex and non-strongly convex settings.  

The paper’s core technical contribution is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. By avoiding the need for strong convexity assumptions, the approach offers greater flexibility for practical optimization tasks where strong convexity is often unavailable. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, though the scope of the experiments warrants further discussion.  

However, the paper’s presentation poses challenges for readers outside the immediate optimization community. The exposition assumes familiarity with specialized notations and abbreviations (e.g., "dual" or "primal" in specific contexts), which may hinder accessibility for broader NIPS audiences. To enhance clarity, the authors should include a brief introductory section outlining foundational concepts relevant to the method, such as the role of averaging in convex optimization and the implications of non-strong convexity. This would help contextualize the contributions for readers encountering the techniques for the first time.  

Specifically, the equations between lines 33–34 and 46–47 require more intuitive explanation. For instance, the derivation of the adaptive averaging step (line 34) and the convergence rate bound (line 47) would benefit from a high-level verbal description alongside the mathematical formulation. A sentence or two explaining *why* the averaging rule is structured as it is—e.g., "The weight adjustment compensates for the diminishing gradient norms observed in non-strongly convex regimes"—would significantly improve readability.  

The connection to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad [1], Adam [2]) also needs elaboration. While the paper focuses on convex settings, it would strengthen its positioning to clarify whether the adaptive averaging principle extends to non-convex scenarios or draws inspiration from these methods. For example: "Unlike adaptive gradient methods that adjust step sizes via coordinate-wise scaling, our approach dynamically weights historical iterates to preserve convergence guarantees under weaker convexity assumptions." This would situate the work within a broader landscape and address potential reader questions about its relationship to established techniques.  

The practical significance of the method is another critical concern. The experiments are conducted in ℝ³, a simplified setting that does not reflect real-world optimization challenges. The authors should explicitly address scalability: How does the method perform on high-dimensional problems (e.g., ℓ₁-regularized regression with 10⁴–10⁶ features)? How does its computational cost compare to state-of-the-art methods like proximal gradient descent or ADMM in such regimes? Without this analysis, the paper’s applicability to modern machine learning applications remains unclear. A brief discussion of empirical scaling (e.g., runtime vs. dimension) would substantially bolster the work’s impact.  

Minor but important issues include ambiguous figure labeling. The reference to "the solid line" in Figure 1 is unclear without additional visual cues. To distinguish trajectories, the authors should use distinct line styles (e.g., solid vs. dashed) or colors for the primal variable path versus the objective function value. This would align with standard visualization practices in optimization literature.  

To summarize, the paper presents a theoretically sound method with clear advantages over existing techniques for constrained convex optimization. Its primary limitations lie in accessibility (due to assumed background knowledge), insufficient contextualization of key equations, and limited experimental validation beyond toy examples. Addressing these points would significantly elevate the paper’s contribution and appeal to the NIPS community.  

**Recommendation:** Major revision. The work has merit but requires substantial clarification of technical exposition, deeper engagement with related methods (including non-convex adaptive techniques), and more realistic experimental validation to demonstrate scalability and practical utility. The authors should also strengthen the narrative around the method’s novelty by explicitly contrasting it with existing heuristics and adaptive methods. With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the optimization literature.
