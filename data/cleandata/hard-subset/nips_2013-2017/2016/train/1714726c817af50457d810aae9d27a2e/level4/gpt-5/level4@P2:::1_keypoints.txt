- Extends ODE interpretation of Nesterov acceleration to accelerated mirror descent with dual accumulation rate η(t) and primal weights w(t).
- Uses a Lyapunov argument to give simple sufficient conditions on (η,w) to achieve a target continuous-time rate 1/r(t), recovering r(t)=t^2.
- Provides a primal representation and concrete examples, including accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex.
- Main algorithmic contribution: an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses w(t) to decrease the Lyapunov derivative and keep the Lyapunov function non-increasing.
- Shows a discrete-time instantiation claimed to preserve the quadratic (accelerated) convergence rate.
- Empirically, adaptive averaging matches or outperforms popular adaptive restarting heuristics on several convex objectives, with strong gains in some strongly convex cases.
- Positions work as a natural extension of Krichene et al./Su et al./Wibisono et al.; differs from restarts by continuous weight adaptation and from Adagrad/Adam by targeting accelerated dynamics.
- Strength: clear, unified Lyapunov framework yielding interpretable energy function and easily verifiable sufficient conditions.
- Strength: simple adaptive weighting rule with provable monotonicity of the Lyapunov function and no loss in nominal convergence rate.
- Strength: concrete primal formulations and illustrative examples that build intuition (positive orthant, simplex).
- Weakness: discrete-time guarantees are mostly in the supplement; main text needs a concise, self-contained proof sketch and clearer assumptions/constants.
- Weakness: empirical scope is narrow (low-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems); needs higher-dimensional benchmarks and other constraint sets.
- Weakness: limited guidance on selecting η(t), target rate r(t), and regularizers; lacks sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Weakness: theoretical treatment of the strongly convex setting is missing; unclear when/if adaptive averaging can match or improve accelerated linear rates.
- Weakness: unexplored connections to classical methods like Conjugate Gradient for quadratics (e.g., induced directions or orthogonality).
- Quality: theoretical development is technically sound and the adaptive rule is justified by direct control of the Lyapunov derivative.
- Clarity: continuous-time analysis and role of Lyapunov are clear; discrete-time results and implementation details should be consolidated into the main text.
- Originality: conceptually incremental over prior ODE-based analyses but introduces a novel, guaranteed adaptive averaging heuristic and a useful accelerated replicator instantiation.
- Significance: potentially impactful—principled, simple adaptive weighting that preserves guarantees and yields empirical gains.
- Arguments for acceptance: solid transparent theory, simple guaranteed heuristic, preserves rates while often improving practice, clear exposition.
- Arguments against acceptance: discrete-time analysis underemphasized, limited empirical breadth, novelty is incremental relative to prior work.
- Questions for authors: can the adaptive rule be tuned/regularized to favor linear rates under strong convexity and bounded relative to restarts?
- Questions for authors: for quadratics, is there a formal connection to Conjugate Gradient (via induced averaging weights or implicit polynomials)?
- Questions for authors: how sensitive is performance to the choice of mirror map/regularizer and can practical default choices be provided?
- Overall evaluation: well-executed, clear, and practically relevant; emphasizing discrete-time analysis and expanding experiments would increase impact.
