Building upon prior work establishing a connection between accelerated Mirror Descent and continuous-time ODEs, the authors generalize the ODE dynamics with a broader averaging scheme and introduce a novel adaptive averaging strategy for its discretization. This adaptive variant of AMD outperforms existing AMD algorithms with restarting in certain cases, and the adaptive averaging strategy itself is a conceptually interesting contribution. However, the paper’s overall originality and practical impact are questionable. Many core components – including the Lyapunov function and discretization method – directly follow from a previous NIPS publication [7]. The extension from simple to generalized averaging feels incremental and offers limited benefit, as the authors focus solely on quadratic convergence rates; achieving faster convergence would necessitate higher-order methods, which are not universally applicable. 

While the paper’s results, such as the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation into primal form, and reduction to replicator dynamics, enhance the understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent, these contributions do not represent a substantial advancement for the machine learning field. Although the adaptive averaging is motivated by ensuring conditions sufficient for ODE convergence, its theoretical foundation remains incomplete due to approximations introduced during discretization. Critically, the paper lacks explicit convergence guarantees, rendering it a heuristic approach as acknowledged by the authors. Moreover, given the existing landscape of averaging schemes for Nesterov’s accelerated algorithms, the value of this adaptive variant is unclear without comparison to alternative methods. The experimental evaluation on a limited toy example with only quadratic or linear objectives is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of adaptive averaging, particularly in the context of general strongly convex problems.
