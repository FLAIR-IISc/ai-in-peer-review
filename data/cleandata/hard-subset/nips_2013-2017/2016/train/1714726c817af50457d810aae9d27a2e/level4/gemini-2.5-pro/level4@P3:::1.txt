### **Summary**

This paper studies a family of continuous-time dynamics for accelerated first-order convex optimization. Building on the work of Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015), which established a connection between Nesterov's accelerated method and a specific ordinary differential equation (ODE) with a weighted averaging scheme, this work generalizes that framework. The authors introduce a broader class of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by two continuous functions: `η(t)`, which controls the rate of gradient accumulation, and `w(t)`, which defines the weights for the averaging scheme.

The core theoretical contribution is a unified analysis of these dynamics using a Lyapunov function. The authors derive simple, sufficient conditions on `η(t)` and `w(t)` that guarantee a convergence rate of `O(1/r(t))` for a desired rate function `r(t)`. Based on this analysis, the paper proposes a novel and intuitive "adaptive averaging" heuristic. Instead of using a fixed, predetermined schedule for the weights `w(t)`, this heuristic adaptively adjusts them to maximize the decrease of the Lyapunov function at each step. A key strength of this approach is that it is guaranteed to preserve the theoretical convergence rate of the non-adaptive method, while potentially offering significant empirical speed-ups. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for this heuristic in continuous time, propose a discrete-time algorithm that preserves the quadratic convergence rate, and present numerical experiments comparing its performance against standard restarting heuristics for accelerated methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Potential Impact:** The paper addresses a highly relevant problem. Nesterov's accelerated methods are ubiquitous in machine learning and optimization, but their performance can be sensitive to parameter tuning and can be improved by adaptive schemes. This work provides a principled and effective method for adaptivity that is simple to implement. The observation that standard, non-adaptive accelerated methods are often used as baselines when they could be easily improved with such heuristics is an important one for the community. The proposed adaptive averaging method has the potential to become a standard, go-to enhancement for accelerated gradient methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is sound, elegant, and provides a clear intuition for the proposed heuristic. The use of a Lyapunov function to analyze a general class of dynamics and then to motivate the adaptive scheme is a strong and principled approach. A crucial advantage of this method over many other heuristics is the formal guarantee that the theoretical convergence rate is preserved.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, and the mathematical arguments are clear and easy to follow, particularly for readers familiar with the ODE interpretation of acceleration. The progression from the general continuous-time framework to the specific adaptive heuristic and its discretization is logical and well-motivated. The experiments are presented clearly and effectively illustrate the behavior of the different methods.

4.  **Empirical Results:** The numerical experiments convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic. It performs competitively with or significantly better than popular restarting schemes across a range of convex problems (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear). The visualizations of the trajectories, objective values, and Lyapunov functions provide valuable insight into the dynamics of the different methods.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality:** The primary weakness is that the technical framework is a direct and incremental extension of prior work, specifically Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). The generalized ODE formulation and the Lyapunov analysis are natural next steps from that work. However, the introduction and analysis of the adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel and valuable contribution that builds effectively on this foundation.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Analysis of the Discretized Algorithm:** The paper states that an analysis of the discretized algorithm (Algorithm 1), proving it preserves the quadratic convergence rate, is provided in the supplementary material. This is a critical result that bridges the gap between the continuous-time theory and the practical algorithm. To strengthen the paper and make it more self-contained, I would strongly recommend including the main theorem statement for the discrete case and a high-level proof sketch in the main text. This would provide the reader with greater confidence in the proposed algorithm's guarantees without needing to consult the appendix.

2.  **Connection to Conjugate Gradient:** For the special case of strongly convex quadratic minimization, Nesterov’s accelerated method is known to be closely related to the Conjugate Gradient (CG) method. It would be insightful if the authors could add a brief discussion on the potential relationship between their adaptive averaging scheme and CG in this setting. For instance, does the adaptive choice of weights cause the iterates to more closely follow the CG path? Does the condition `⟨∇f(x(k)), x(k+1) - x(k)⟩ > 0` (which triggers a weight reset in the gradient restart heuristic) have a known interpretation in the context of CG? Exploring this connection could provide deeper intuition for why the adaptive method is so effective.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a simple, well-motivated, and effective heuristic for one of the most important classes of optimization algorithms. The combination of elegant theory, strong guarantees, and compelling empirical results makes it a valuable contribution to the field.
