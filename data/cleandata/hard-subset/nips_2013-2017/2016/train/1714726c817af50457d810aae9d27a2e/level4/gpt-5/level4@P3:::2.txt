Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a coupled primal–dual continuous-time system. A dual variable Z(t) accumulates gradients at a rate η(t), and a primal variable X(t) is produced as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X) − f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors derive sufficient conditions on η and w to achieve a target convergence rate 1/r(t). They give a primal-only representation, show how the replicator dynamics on the simplex arise and can be “accelerated” via this averaging, and introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic that aims to decrease the Lyapunov derivative along the trajectory. In discrete time (focusing on the quadratic rate), they propose a first-order method with adaptive averaging and compare it empirically with adaptive restarting schemes, reporting competitive or improved performance.

This work builds on the continuous-time viewpoint of acceleration (Su, Boyd, Candès [20]; Wibisono, Wilson, Jordan [23]) and the accelerated mirror descent formulation and discretization in Krichene, Bayen, Bartlett [11]. It also connects to adaptive heuristics such as adaptive restarting (O’Donoghue, Candès [17]) and to adaptive step-size methods (Adagrad [7], Adam [10], though those target different goals).

Strengths
- Unified continuous-time framework: The conditions linking η(t), w(t), and r(t) give a clear, flexible recipe for constructing accelerated dynamics with guaranteed continuous-time rates. The Lyapunov analysis is clean and general.
- Primal formulation and examples: The primal-only representation clarifies the role of the mirror map and yields concrete dynamics on common domains (positive orthant, simplex). The connection to replicator dynamics is pedagogically valuable.
- Energy interpretation: The decreasing energy function complements the Lyapunov analysis and offers intuitive insight into the dynamics (potential vs. kinetic energy).
- Adaptive averaging idea: Choosing weights by directly controlling the Lyapunov derivative is simple and principled. Empirically, adaptive averaging compares favorably to restarting on the reported tests.
- Careful ODE well-posedness: Existence/uniqueness and feasibility preservation are established under reasonable assumptions.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Incremental originality: Much of the structure (Lyapunov function, rate construction via η and w, discretization of accelerated mirror descent) traces closely to [11, 20, 23]. The generalization from specific to generalized averaging is natural and, in the presented form, offers limited new theoretical leverage beyond enabling the adaptive heuristic.
- Discrete-time theory is incomplete in the main text: While the continuous-time guarantees are clear, the adaptive averaging scheme is ultimately a discrete algorithm; its convergence guarantees are not fully developed in the main submission (beyond the claim that it preserves the quadratic rate in a discretization). This weakens the scientific contribution of the proposed practical method.
- Experimental scope: Experiments are mainly low-dimensional and focus on quadratic/linear or KL objectives on the simplex. The evaluation does not include standard higher-dimensional constrained convex tasks (e.g., sparse logistic regression with simplex or orthant constraints, portfolio/simplex problems, composite objectives), nor sensitivity to mirror map choices or parameter tuning. Comparisons are limited to two restarting heuristics; other averaging/weighting schedules or recent ODE-inspired accelerations are not benchmarked.
- Limited significance of some components: The energy interpretation, primal reformulations, and the replicator linkage mainly improve intuition but do not, by themselves, advance the algorithmic frontier.

Quality
Technically sound in continuous time: the Lyapunov and energy analyses are correct and informative, and the ODE well-posedness is handled properly. The discrete-time method is plausible but lacks a complete, self-contained convergence proof in the paper. Experimental methodology is clear but too narrow to demonstrate broad utility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with precise definitions and clear statements. Some parts are notation-heavy; adding brief roadmaps and clarifying the role of t0 > 0 and its discrete analogue would improve readability. Explicitly separating continuous-time guarantees from discrete-time claims would avoid confusion.

Originality
Moderate. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and intuitively compelling, but most of the analytical machinery and the averaging perspective are close to prior work [11, 20, 23]. The accelerated replicator example is nice but not conceptually surprising given the mirror descent framework.

Significance
Potentially useful, but current evidence is limited. If adaptive averaging can be shown to yield robust improvements over standard restarting across a range of problems and mirror maps, and if discrete-time guarantees are established, the impact would be meaningful. As presented, the contribution is more incremental.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, unified Lyapunov framework for accelerated dynamics with generalized averaging.
- Simple and principled adaptive averaging rule tied to Lyapunov decrease.
- Pedagogically valuable primal formulations and replicator connection.
- Empirical gains over adaptive restarting in reported tests.

Cons against acceptance
- Discrete-time convergence theory for the proposed adaptive scheme is incomplete in the submission.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scale, diversity, and baselines.
- Incremental novelty relative to [11, 20, 23]; generalized averaging conditions are close to known schedules.
- Limited evidence for benefits in strongly convex or high-dimensional settings.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a formal discrete-time convergence theorem for the adaptive averaging algorithm, including conditions on step sizes and parameter choices (β, s), and clarify whether the Lyapunov construction carries over discretely without asymptotic gaps.
- Expand experiments to higher dimensions, diverse constraint sets and mirror maps, and include composite objectives and non-quadratic strongly convex functions. Report sensitivity to β and step size and ablation on the weighting policy.
- Compare with alternative weighting/averaging schedules (e.g., schedules from [23], step-size-based averaging in [8]) and more recent restart variants.
- Discuss numerical stability near t0 and the effect of the scale-invariance in w(t) on discretization.
- Clarify whether the approach extends to stochastic gradients or variance-reduced settings.

Post-rebuttal update
The authors committed to (i) adding higher-dimensional and non-quadratic experiments, (ii) providing a convergence proof for the discrete adaptive averaging scheme, (iii) demonstrating adaptive averaging within Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method, and (iv) comparing with other adaptive averaging strategies. These additions would address the main concerns about scope and theory and would substantially strengthen the paper. Contingent on the inclusion and quality of these revisions, my assessment improves.
