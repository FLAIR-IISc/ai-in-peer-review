Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies continuous-time accelerated mirror descent (AMD) with generalized averaging. The dynamics couple a dual variable that accumulates gradients at rate η(t) and a primal variable that is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov argument, the authors derive sufficient conditions on η and w to achieve a desired convergence rate r(t). They present an equivalent primal formulation, interpret an energy function that decreases under the same conditions, and show that, on the simplex, these dynamics yield an accelerated version of the replicator equation. They then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adjusts the weights to further decrease the Lyapunov function, provide continuous-time guarantees and argue that a discretization preserves the quadratic rate of accelerated first-order methods. Experiments compare adaptive averaging to adaptive restarting heuristics and show comparable or improved performance on low-dimensional simplex-constrained problems.

This work builds on a line of research connecting Nesterov’s acceleration to ODEs (e.g., Su–Boyd–Candès; Wibisono–Wilson–Jordan) and on the AMD formulation in continuous and discrete time (Krichene–Bayen–Bartlett). The proposed adaptive averaging is positioned as an alternative to restart heuristics (e.g., O’Donoghue–Candès; Su–Boyd–Candès), with an emphasis on maintaining a Lyapunov decrease.

Strengths
- Clear Lyapunov-based framework that unifies several accelerated dynamics and yields simple sufficient conditions for a target rate.
- Interpretable energy function and primal reformulation that improve intuition about AMD and its constraints-handling via mirror maps.
- Accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex provide a neat example linking optimization and evolutionary dynamics.
- Adaptive averaging has a simple, intuitive rule (choose weights to reduce the Lyapunov derivative) and, in continuous time, preserves the desired rate by design.
- Empirical results show the heuristic performs at least as well as adaptive restarting on the reported tasks and sometimes better.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: much of the setup (Lyapunov function, averaging view, discretization strategy) closely follows prior NIPS work on AMD and variational ODE formulations. Extending from fixed averaging to generalized averaging appears incremental, especially since the paper ultimately focuses on quadratic rates; faster rates would require higher-order methods not always practical.
- Several contributions (energy interpretation, primal form, and replicator reduction) are insightful but may not, on their own, substantially advance ML practice.
- The discrete adaptive averaging is presented as a heuristic; the theoretical guarantees provided are for the ODE, and the discretization introduces approximations. The current draft does not state explicit convergence guarantees for the discrete method.
- Experimental evaluation is narrow: primarily toy, low-dimensional, simplex-constrained problems with quadratic/linear objectives. Comparisons omit other averaging schemes for accelerated methods, leaving uncertain how the proposed heuristic fares more broadly, especially for strongly convex problems and higher dimensions.

Quality
- Technically sound in continuous time with clearly stated sufficient conditions; proofs align with established Lyapunov analyses.
- The discrete algorithm lacks a complete convergence proof in the current version; thus its theoretical status remains heuristic.
- Experimental evidence is limited in scope and does not fully validate broad claims of advantage.

Clarity
- Generally well written and well organized. The Lyapunov analysis, energy function, and primal forms enhance readability.
- The paper would benefit from a sharper delineation of what is new relative to prior AMD/ODE work and from a more comprehensive empirical section.

Originality
- Moderate. The adaptive averaging idea is interesting, but the generalized averaging framework and examples feel incremental given existing ODE-based analyses and restart heuristics.

Significance
- Potentially useful if adaptive averaging consistently improves accelerated methods without costly restarts and if guarantees extend to practical discretizations. As presented, significance is uncertain due to limited experiments and missing discrete-time theory.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clean and unified continuous-time analysis with simple, verifiable conditions for target rates.
- Intuitive heuristic with promising empirical behavior and connections to well-known dynamics (replicator).
- May inspire further developments in adaptive mechanisms for acceleration beyond restarts.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to prior ODE and AMD work; main new element (adaptive averaging) is currently heuristic in discrete time.
- Experimental evaluation is too narrow to support strong claims.
- Lack of comparison to other adaptive averaging/weighting schemes for accelerated methods.

Post-rebuttal comments
The authors commit to adding higher-dimensional and non-quadratic experiments, a convergence proof for the discrete adaptive averaging, an implementation on Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method, and a discussion comparing to other adaptive averaging approaches. If these additions are delivered satisfactorily, they would substantially mitigate the main concerns about scope and theory and improve the paper’s significance. I have adjusted my evaluation accordingly.
