The paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization in both continuous and discrete settings. The authors build upon their previous work [11], which demonstrated that Nesterov’s method can be viewed as a discretization of an ODE system coupling a primal and dual trajectory, where the primal trajectory is obtained via a specific averaging of the mirror of the dual variable. In this paper, they investigate more general averaging schemes. Theorem 2 derives convergence rates under suitable conditions on the averaging coefficients. In the continuous setting, convergence rates faster than 1/t<sup>2</sup> are established. An adaptive choice of the weights, with convergence guarantees, is also studied and then evaluated experimentally. This adaptive scheme is compared with existing restarting techniques, which are known to empirically improve the convergence of accelerated methods. The theoretical analysis of the discrete version of the algorithm is not fully developed in this work.

I find this to be an interesting and well-written paper. The analysis of the new averaging scheme for accelerated methods is presented rigorously in the continuous setting. While the core ideas behind analyzing continuous dynamics are now well-established in a series of papers, this work presents novel contributions and results. In this respect, the related work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and collaborators [1, 2, 3] should be mentioned. The main theorem, demonstrating the existence of averaging schemes with convergence rates potentially faster than 1/t<sup>2</sup>, is a significant result. It would be interesting to investigate whether this improved rate is preserved when transitioning to a discrete procedure. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the proposed averaging schemes within a continuous-time framework.
*   **Novelty:** The exploration of generalized averaging schemes and the derivation of convergence rates represent a valuable contribution to the field.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making the complex ideas accessible.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments provide evidence that the adaptive averaging heuristic performs favorably compared to existing methods.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper builds logically on the authors’ prior research and connects to related work in the literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discrete Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on the continuous-time analysis. A more thorough analysis of the discrete-time version of the algorithm and its convergence properties would be beneficial.
*   **Related Work Gaps:** While the authors cite relevant work, acknowledging recent advances by Attouch, Peypouquet and collaborators in analyzing inertial dynamics would strengthen the related work section.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The practical implications and scalability of the proposed method to very large-scale problems are not fully addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the understanding of accelerated methods.
*   The results have the potential to lead to more efficient optimization algorithms.
*   The experimental validation supports the claims made in the paper.
*   The clarity and organization of the paper make it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a comprehensive analysis of the discrete-time version limits the immediate practical impact of the work.
*   The computational cost of the adaptive heuristic could be a concern for very large-scale problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. While the limited discrete analysis is a drawback, the theoretical insights and experimental results are compelling. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS.
