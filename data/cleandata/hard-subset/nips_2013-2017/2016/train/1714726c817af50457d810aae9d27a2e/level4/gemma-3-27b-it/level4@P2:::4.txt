This paper investigates a class of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) describing accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, focusing on a generalized averaging scheme. The authors establish sufficient conditions on the dual variable’s learning rate and the averaging weights, leveraging a Lyapunov argument to guarantee a desired convergence rate. A novel adaptive averaging heuristic is then proposed to accelerate the decrease of the Lyapunov function, supported by both theoretical guarantees and numerical comparisons. This work builds upon a significant body of research aiming to empirically improve the convergence of accelerated methods. A key contribution lies in demonstrating, through the lens of ODEs, that adaptively adjusting the averaging weights along solution trajectories can effectively achieve acceleration.

The paper begins by framing the problem of convex minimization and connecting it to recent work establishing links between Nesterov’s accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs [20, 2, 11]. The authors clearly articulate the motivation for studying continuous-time dynamics – simpler analysis, algorithm design guidance, and intuitive understanding of discrete processes. They successfully position their work within this context, building on their prior results [11] to explore the impact of different averaging schemes. The introduction effectively highlights the connection between acceleration and averaging, observed previously in simpler cases [8]. 

The core contribution lies in the analysis of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions *w* and *η*. The Lyapunov function approach is well-motivated and the sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) for guaranteeing a quadratic convergence rate are clearly stated. The derivation of an equivalent primal representation (AMD′<sub>w,η</sub>) is a valuable step, facilitating analysis and providing concrete examples.  The application of the framework to the probability simplex, resulting in an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics, is a compelling illustration of the method’s applicability. 

The adaptive averaging heuristic proposed in Section 4 is a particularly strong aspect of the paper. The authors provide a sound rationale for adaptively computing weights based on the rate of decrease of the Lyapunov function, and convincingly demonstrate that this heuristic preserves the convergence rate. This is a notable improvement over existing heuristics like adaptive restarting [17, 20], which often lack such guarantees.  The theoretical analysis linking the heuristic to the Lyapunov function is well-presented.

The numerical experiments in Section 5 provide empirical support for the proposed method. Comparing adaptive averaging to existing heuristics on simplex-constrained problems, the authors demonstrate comparable or improved performance, with significant gains in specific cases. The visualization of solution trajectories and Lyapunov/energy function values provides valuable insights into the behavior of the algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is rigorously grounded in convex analysis and Lyapunov stability theory.
*   **Novel Contribution:**  The adaptive averaging heuristic is a novel approach with theoretical guarantees, distinguishing it from empirical heuristics.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the concepts accessible.
*   **Strong Connection to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately contextualizes its contributions within the broader literature on accelerated methods and continuous-time optimization.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposed method has the potential to improve the efficiency of accelerated optimization algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments are limited to simplex-constrained problems. Expanding the experiments to other constraint sets and objective functions would strengthen the empirical validation.
*   **Discretization Details:** The discretization of the adaptive averaging heuristic (Algorithm 1) could benefit from more detailed justification and analysis. 
*   **Computational Cost:** The overhead associated with adaptively computing the weights could be significant in certain applications. A discussion of the computational complexity would be beneficial.
*  **Reference to recent work**: The related work section could benefit from including more recent work on accelerated methods, such as the work by Attouch et al. [citation needed].



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to accelerating convex optimization.
*   The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a significant improvement over existing methods by providing convergence guarantees.
*   The numerical results support the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The work addresses a challenging and important problem in optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scope of the numerical experiments is limited.
*   The computational cost of the adaptive averaging heuristic is not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed method is novel and well-motivated, and the numerical results are encouraging. While the scope of the experiments is somewhat limited, the potential impact of this work justifies acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper.
