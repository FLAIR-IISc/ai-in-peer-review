Summary
The paper builds on prior work linking accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ODEs. It extends the ODE dynamics via a more general averaging scheme and proposes a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one discretization. Empirically, the adaptive averaging variant sometimes outperforms AMD with restarting. The paper also offers interpretive contributions (e.g., energy/Lyapunov function, primal reformulation, connection to replicator dynamics).

Strengths
- The adaptive averaging strategy is a fresh idea with intuitive motivation, and it is simple to implement.
- The continuous-time perspective and associated Lyapunov analysis are clearly presented and help demystify AMD.
- The reformulations (e.g., primal form, replicator dynamics) may be pedagogically valuable to the community.
- In some settings, the adaptive averaging variant improves over restart-based AMD.

Main concerns
- Originality and incremental contribution: Many technical components (Lyapunov construction, ODE/discretization framework, core analysis) follow closely from prior NIPS work [7]. The generalization from simple to generalized averaging feels relatively straightforward, and its practical value is limited given that the paper ultimately focuses on quadratic-rate behavior. Faster rates would likely require higher-order methods, which are not always practical or broadly applicable.
- Theoretical grounding of the adaptive scheme: The adaptive rule is designed to satisfy continuous-time conditions, but the discretization introduces approximations that are not fully controlled. As presented, the method remains largely heuristic: there is no rigorous convergence or rate guarantee for the discrete algorithm.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are restricted to toy problems with quadratic/linear objectives and limited dimensionality. This is insufficient to demonstrate consistent benefit, especially in strongly convex or more realistic settings. It is also hard to assess robustness and sensitivity of the adaptive rule.
- Context and comparisons: There exist various averaging and restart strategies in accelerated methods. Without careful empirical and conceptual comparison to alternative schemes, it is unclear when and why the proposed adaptive averaging is preferable.

Significance and usefulness
The interpretive results (energy function interpretation, primal reformulation, link to replicator dynamics) improve understanding of AMD, but their impact on mainstream machine learning practice appears modest. The adaptive averaging idea is promising, yet in its current form it lacks theoretical guarantees and convincing empirical evidence beyond toy settings. As a result, the overall usefulness remains uncertain.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Provide a discrete-time analysis establishing convergence (and, if possible, rates) for the proposed adaptive averaging scheme under clear assumptions (e.g., smooth/strongly convex settings). Clarify precisely which continuous-time conditions are preserved after discretization and what step-size/regularity choices are required.
- Expand empirical evaluation:
  - Higher-dimensional, non-quadratic, and strongly convex problems; include composite objectives if possible.
  - Compare against multiple baselines: classic Nesterov averaging, different weighted averaging choices, adaptive restart variants, and other practical heuristics.
  - Report not only iteration counts but also wall-clock time, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and robustness across problem classes.
  - Include ablations isolating the effect of the adaptive averaging schedule.
- Clarify scope and limitations: When does generalized averaging yield tangible benefits over simple averaging? Are there problem structures where the adaptive scheme is provably advantageous or, conversely, harmful?
- If faster-than-quadratic rates are out of scope, state this explicitly and discuss to what extent higher-order methods could be integrated with the proposed averaging in practice (e.g., computational trade-offs, oracle assumptions).
- Strengthen the connection to replicator dynamics by indicating whether it leads to new algorithmic insights or concrete improvements in discrete-time performance.

Clarity and presentation
The paper is generally well written. It would help to:
- Streamline the exposition to reduce overlap with [7], emphasizing what is genuinely new here.
- Clearly separate continuous-time results from discrete-time claims and mark heuristic steps.
- Improve notation consistency around averaging weights and discretization choices, and gather assumptions in one place.

Post-rebuttal update
I appreciate the authors’ commitment to:
- Add higher-dimensional and non-quadratic experiments.
- Provide a theoretical convergence proof for the adaptive averaging scheme.
- Implement the adaptive averaging in Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method.
- Include a discussion and comparison to other adaptive averaging approaches.

These additions, if delivered with rigor and strong empirical evidence, would address my primary concerns about theoretical grounding and the breadth of evaluation. I have updated my scores accordingly, reflecting a more positive assessment of the paper’s potential impact contingent on these revisions.

Overall evaluation
- Originality: Moderate; several ideas build directly on [7], but the adaptive averaging is novel.
- Quality: Currently limited by the lack of discrete-time guarantees and narrow experiments; promised revisions would significantly improve this.
- Clarity: Good.
- Significance: Unclear at present; could be strengthened with proofs and broader comparisons.
