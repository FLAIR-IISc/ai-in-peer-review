Summary
The paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic to accelerate first-order methods for constrained convex optimization. A key contribution is a theoretical guarantee that the heuristic does not degrade the convergence rate when the objective is not strongly convex, addressing a known weakness of common restarting strategies. The paper provides both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence supporting the claim.

Strengths
- Theoretical guarantee: The method preserves the baseline convergence rate in the non–strongly convex setting, which is a meaningful improvement over heuristic restarts that may harm worst-case rates.
- Conceptual simplicity: The heuristic appears simple to implement and potentially broadly applicable across first-order constrained methods.
- Evidence of benefit: The experiments, while limited in dimension, indicate acceleration relative to standard baselines.

Main concerns and questions
1) Clarity and accessibility
- The presentation is difficult to follow for readers not deeply specialized in this subarea. Many abbreviations and terms are used without definition. Please add brief background on the optimization setting, the class of algorithms targeted, and the role of averaging in accelerated methods.
- The equations around lines 33–34 and 46–47 would benefit from an intuitive explanation. For example, provide geometric or variational interpretations of the updates, a short derivation of how the adaptive rule interacts with the primal sequence, or a schematic illustrating the effect on iterates.
- Consider including a concise algorithm box summarizing the heuristic and its inputs/outputs, and a short paragraph on computational overhead per iteration.

2) Relation to prior work
- The paper contrasts with “restarting heuristics,” but the related work could be strengthened. Please clarify how your approach compares theoretically and empirically to popular adaptive restart schemes (e.g., function/gradient-based restarts). Under what conditions would your method strictly dominate, match, or be inferior?
- Non-convex adaptive methods: Although your setting is deterministic convex optimization, readers will naturally ask about connections to AdaGrad [1] and Adam [2]. Please clarify:
  - Conceptual differences (per-coordinate stepsize adaptation vs. averaging schedule adaptation).
  - Whether any insights or guarantees extend to stochastic or non-convex settings.
  - If your averaging rule can be combined with AdaGrad/Adam-like stepsizes in convex problems and what is theoretically known (or not).

3) Scope and practical significance
- Experimental setup: All experiments are in R^3, which limits conclusions about scalability and practical utility. Please evaluate in higher dimensions (e.g., 10^2–10^5), using standard convex tasks with constraints such as:
  - Lasso or constrained least squares
  - Logistic regression with box or simplex constraints
  - Portfolio optimization or TV-regularized problems with feasible sets
- Metrics: Report iteration counts, number of projections, and wall-clock time to a target accuracy. Since constrained methods often hinge on projection cost, the runtime impact of the adaptive rule should be quantified.
- Scaling: Discuss how performance changes with dimension, condition number, and constraint geometry (e.g., simple boxes vs. general polyhedral or spectral sets). If projections are expensive, can the heuristic be adapted to projection-free methods?
- Robustness and tuning: Provide sensitivity analyses for any hyperparameters in the adaptive rule, and discuss robustness to noise or inexact gradients (if applicable).

Technical points to clarify
- Assumptions: State clearly the assumptions on the objective (smoothness, Lipschitz continuity) and feasible set (closed, convex, projection availability). Are Slater-type conditions needed for any part of the analysis?
- Strong convexity: While the focus is non–strongly convex objectives, what happens when the problem is strongly convex? Does the heuristic preserve or improve linear rates, or can it hinder them?
- Complexity overhead: Quantify extra cost per iteration (beyond a gradient and projection), memory requirements, and any stopping or switching conditions introduced by the heuristic.
- Generality: Is the method applicable to both primal and primal–dual schemes? Any constraints on step-size policies it can be paired with?

Suggestions to improve exposition
- Provide a brief “background and intuition” section explaining averaging in accelerated methods and why naive restarts can hurt rates in the non–strongly convex case.
- After the critical equations (lines 33–34 and 46–47), add a paragraph with a geometric intuition and a short proof sketch illustrating why the adaptive averaging cannot degrade the base rate.
- Include pseudocode and a flowchart-like diagram highlighting when and how the averaging weights are adapted.
- Expand the related work to cover:
  - Adaptive restarts and their known guarantees/failure modes.
  - Connections and distinctions to AdaGrad/Adam and recent deterministic adaptive step-size/averaging schemes in convex optimization.

Empirical recommendations
- Add high-dimensional experiments with:
  - Multiple problem families and constraint sets.
  - Comparisons to: baseline accelerated methods without adaptation, standard restart heuristics, and (optionally) coordinate-adaptive methods when applicable.
  - Plots of error vs. iterations and error vs. time; ablations isolating the contribution of the adaptive rule.
- Report initialization details, stopping criteria, and hyperparameter choices to aid reproducibility.

Minor comments
- Figure 1: The reference to the “solid line” is ambiguous. Consider using distinct line styles or colors, and include a clear legend indicating the trajectory of the primal variable.
- Define all abbreviations on first use.
- Proof readability: consider moving technical lemmas to an appendix and adding a high-level proof roadmap in the main text.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a promising and well-motivated heuristic with a valuable worst-case guarantee in the non–strongly convex regime. However, the current presentation is hard to follow, and the experimental evaluation is too limited to establish practical impact. With clearer exposition, stronger positioning relative to adaptive restarts and AdaGrad/Adam-style methods, and substantive high-dimensional experiments, the paper would be significantly strengthened.
