This paper builds on the ODE interpretation of Nesterov’s acceleration developed by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015) to design and evaluate a natural heuristic that adaptively reweights iterates to speed up convergence. In contrast to previous adaptive averaging heuristics, the proposed scheme is guaranteed to dominate the standard fixed-schedule averaging. The empirical evaluation shows clear speed-ups across a broad class of convex objectives.

I found the paper enjoyable to read. The mathematical development is simple and easy to follow, particularly for readers familiar with Krichene et al.’s framework. The experiments are convincing and indicate very promising improvements. While the technical contribution is a relatively close extension of Krichene et al., the potential impact is high because Nesterov’s acceleration is ubiquitous in applications. Too often, even in empirical studies, new (and possibly highly adaptive) methods are compared against Nesterov’s method with a fixed weight sequence. This paper confirms that making Nesterov’s method adaptive requires only a small modification and can yield dramatic gains.

Suggestions for improvement:
- From a technical standpoint, I recommend providing an analysis of the discretized process showing that the convergence rate is preserved. To me, this seems relatively straightforward.
- I would also be interested in possible connections between the proposed adaptive averaging for Nesterov’s method and the Conjugate Gradient method for strongly convex quadratic objectives.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear and well-motivated extension of the ODE viewpoint on acceleration.
- A principled adaptive averaging rule with a guarantee that it does not underperform fixed-schedule averaging.
- Empirical results demonstrating consistent and sometimes substantial speed-ups.
- High potential impact due to the pervasiveness of Nesterov’s methods and the simplicity of the proposed modification.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The technical development closely follows prior work by Krichene et al., with limited novelty in theory.
- The paper would be stronger with a complete analysis of the discrete-time algorithm establishing that the convergence rate is indeed preserved.
- Connections to related accelerated methods (e.g., Conjugate Gradient in the quadratic case) are not explored and could deepen the understanding of when and why the heuristic excels.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-written and practically valuable contribution that refines a widely used accelerated method with a simple, effective adaptive mechanism and a guarantee of non-degradation relative to fixed schedules. The empirical gains are compelling. Strengthening the discrete-time analysis and clarifying links to established methods like Conjugate Gradient would further enhance the paper.
