## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission [Paper ID]

This paper builds upon existing work connecting accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs), extending the ODE dynamics with a generalized averaging scheme and proposing a novel adaptive averaging strategy for discretization. While the work is technically sound and presents some interesting connections to related dynamics, its overall originality and practical impact are questionable.

The core contribution lies in the proposed adaptive averaging strategy. The intuition behind this strategy is well-motivated, and in certain cases, the resulting AMD variant outperforms existing AMD algorithms with restarting. However, the paper relies heavily on a prior NIPS publication [7], with many elements – including the Lyapunov function and discretization approach – directly stemming from this work. The generalization from simple averaging to the considered generalized averaging appears incremental; the authors ultimately focus exclusively on the quadratic convergence rate, which limits the potential impact. Achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which, as acknowledged, are not universally applicable.

Several results within the paper contribute to a deeper understanding of AMD. The interpretation of the energy function, the reformulation into primal form, and the reduction to replicator dynamics offer valuable insights. However, these insights, while theoretically interesting, do not appear to represent a significant advancement for the machine learning community. 

A key concern is the theoretical grounding of the adaptive averaging strategy. Although introduced to satisfy sufficient conditions for ODE convergence, the discretization introduces approximations that are not fully addressed. Critically, the paper lacks an explicit convergence guarantee for the adaptive scheme, leaving it, as the authors concede, as a heuristic. Furthermore, the landscape of averaging schemes for Nesterov’s accelerated algorithms is already populated. Without comparison to alternative schemes, it’s difficult to assess whether this adaptive variant provides a substantial improvement. 

The experimental evaluation, currently limited to a toy example with quadratic and linear objectives, is insufficient to demonstrate the advantages of adaptive averaging, particularly in the context of more complex, strongly convex problems. The lack of broader empirical validation weakens the claim of practical benefit.

**Post-Rebuttal:**

I have carefully reviewed the authors’ rebuttal and their proposed revisions. The commitment to include: 1) additional experiments with higher dimensionality and non-quadratic objectives; 2) a theoretical proof of convergence for the adaptive averaging scheme; 3) implementation of the adaptive averaging on Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method; and 4) a comparative discussion with other adaptive averaging schemes, is encouraging. These additions would substantially address my concerns regarding the paper’s practical validation and theoretical foundation. Consequently, I have updated my scores to reflect this potential for improvement.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically competent exploration of AMD and its connection to ODEs. The adaptive averaging strategy is a novel idea, but its initial presentation lacked sufficient theoretical justification and empirical validation. The authors’ commitment to addressing these shortcomings in the revision process offers hope that the paper will become a more compelling contribution. However, the fundamental originality remains a concern, and the ultimate impact will depend on the strength of the revised experimental results and theoretical analysis.
