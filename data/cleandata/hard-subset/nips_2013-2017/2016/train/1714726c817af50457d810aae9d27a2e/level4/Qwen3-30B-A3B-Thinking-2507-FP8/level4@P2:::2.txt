

This paper extends the connection between accelerated mirror descent (AMD) and continuous-time ordinary differential equations (ODEs) by introducing a generalized averaging scheme for the dual variable. The authors build upon Su et al.'s work [20] and Krichene et al.'s earlier NIPS 2016 paper [11] that established the relationship between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics. They propose a novel adaptive averaging heuristic for the discrete-time implementation of the accelerated dynamics, which adaptively computes weights to accelerate convergence while preserving the quadratic convergence rate.

The paper's strengths are evident in its technical quality and clarity. The authors provide a rigorous Lyapunov analysis for the continuous-time dynamics under a broad family of generalized averaging schemes, establishing sufficient conditions for convergence. The connection between the continuous-time dynamics and the discrete-time algorithm is well-motivated, and the primal representation of the dynamics offers valuable insights into the behavior of accelerated methods. The paper also makes a useful contribution by showing how replicator dynamics (important in evolutionary game theory) can be accelerated through their proposed framework.

Regarding originality, the paper's main contribution appears to be the adaptive averaging heuristic. However, the extension from simple to generalized averaging is somewhat limited since the authors ultimately focus on the quadratic convergence rate case (r(t)=t²), which was already covered in their prior NIPS 2016 work. The theoretical novelty is somewhat constrained as the adaptive heuristic essentially follows the conditions already established for the Lyapunov function in the continuous-time setting. While the adaptive scheme provides an interesting interpretation of how to modify weights to accelerate convergence, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish it from other existing adaptive methods for accelerated optimization.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited. The experimental evaluation focuses on simple quadratic and linear objectives on the simplex, which are not representative of the more complex, high-dimensional problems typical in machine learning applications. The paper demonstrates that adaptive averaging outperforms restarting heuristics in these specific cases, but the experimental scope is narrow and lacks comparison with other adaptive methods beyond restarting. The authors acknowledge this limitation in their rebuttal, promising to include higher-dimensional examples and comparisons with alternative adaptive schemes in the revision.

The paper's technical quality is strong, with careful theoretical analysis and proper citations to related work. The writing is clear, and the connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is well-explained. However, the theoretical grounding of the adaptive averaging heuristic in discrete time is incomplete. The paper states that the adaptive heuristic preserves the convergence rate but does not provide a formal convergence guarantee for the discrete-time implementation, relying instead on the continuous-time analysis. This is a significant gap, as the discretization process can alter convergence properties, and the authors do not address this carefully.

The authors' claim that their method "performs at least as well as adaptive restarting, with significant improvements in some cases" is not fully supported by the experiments, which are limited to toy examples. The paper would be strengthened by comparing with other adaptive methods (e.g., Adagrad, Adam) and demonstrating performance on more challenging, non-quadratic problems.

To summarize:
- Quality: Strong theoretical foundation, but discrete-time convergence guarantee is missing.
- Clarity: Well-written with clear exposition of the ODE framework.
- Originality: Limited novelty in the generalized averaging approach, though the adaptive heuristic is interesting.
- Significance: Experiments are limited in scope and do not convincingly demonstrate broad applicability to machine learning problems.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods through continuous-time dynamics, but the significance and novelty are constrained by the narrow scope of experiments and the limited theoretical justification for the discrete-time implementation. The authors' promise to address these issues in the revision (adding higher-dimensional examples, theoretical convergence proof, and comparisons with other adaptive schemes) is encouraging, but the current version does not fully satisfy the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.

Given the current state of the paper, I would recommend rejection, but with the understanding that substantial revisions addressing the theoretical gaps and experimental limitations could make it a strong contribution. The paper's technical quality is good, but it does not advance the field sufficiently in terms of either novelty or significance for NIPS.
