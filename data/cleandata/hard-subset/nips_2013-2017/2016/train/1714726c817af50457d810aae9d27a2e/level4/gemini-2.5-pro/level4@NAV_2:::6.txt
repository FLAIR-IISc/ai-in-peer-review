This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization from the perspective of continuous-time dynamics. The authors introduce a general family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, which are characterized by a coupling between a dual variable that accumulates gradients and a primal variable represented as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory. Building on prior work that connects Nesterov's acceleration to specific ODEs, this work generalizes the averaging scheme. The main theoretical contribution is a Lyapunov-based analysis providing sufficient conditions on the averaging weights `w(t)` and gradient rate `η(t)` to achieve a desired convergence rate `1/r(t)`. This framework is then used to propose a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively tunes the weights to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function. A key result is that this heuristic is guaranteed to preserve the O(1/t^2) convergence rate, a property not formally established for other heuristics like adaptive restarting, particularly for non-strongly convex functions. As a concrete example, the paper shows how this framework can be used to derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and principled adaptive heuristic for accelerated optimization. The guarantee that the adaptive scheme preserves the theoretical convergence rate of the underlying method is a significant and valuable contribution, addressing a known gap in the analysis of popular restarting heuristics. The unification of various accelerated dynamics under a single framework with generalized averaging is an elegant conceptual contribution.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound and rigorous. The use of a Lyapunov function to derive sufficient conditions for a given convergence rate is a powerful and insightful approach. The proofs are clear, and the connection between the continuous-time analysis and the discrete-time algorithm is well-motivated.
*   **Clarity:** The paper does a good job of motivating the study of continuous-time dynamics and clearly situates the work relative to key prior results (e.g., Su et al. 2014, Krichene et al. 2015). The derivation of an accelerated replicator dynamics is a compelling example that makes the abstract framework more concrete.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Significance and Empirical Evaluation:** The primary weakness of the paper is the limited empirical validation. The experiments are performed on low-dimensional (R^3) problems, which, while useful for visualizing trajectories, are insufficient to demonstrate the practical utility and scalability of the proposed heuristic. To strengthen the paper's claims of significance, the authors should provide experiments on higher-dimensional problems or benchmark datasets relevant to the machine learning community. While the supplement is mentioned to contain higher-dimensional experiments, summarizing those key findings in the main text would be beneficial.
*   **Clarity and Accessibility:** While well-written for experts, the paper's dense technical presentation could be challenging for a broader audience. The core concepts, particularly the specific form of the AMD dynamics (Eq. 1) and the Lyapunov function (Eq. 2), would benefit from more high-level intuition. A brief discussion explaining *why* this particular weighted averaging scheme is key to acceleration would significantly improve readability.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper's discussion of other adaptive optimization methods like Adagrad and Adam is very brief. The authors state these methods are "not designed for accelerated methods," but a more detailed comparison would be valuable. Clarifying the fundamental differences and potential connections between adaptive averaging (which tunes the memory of the trajectory) and adaptive learning rates (which rescale gradients) would better contextualize the paper's contribution within the broader landscape of adaptive optimization.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with strong theoretical contributions. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, well-motivated, and provides a convergence guarantee that is lacking in related heuristics. The main drawback is the unconvincing experimental evaluation. If the authors can provide stronger empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness on more realistic, higher-dimensional problems, this would be a very strong contribution to the conference.
