

This paper proposes a continuous-time approach to constrained convex optimization using accelerated descent dynamics, leveraging Lyapunov analysis to establish convergence rates. The key contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate the convergence rate of the Lyapunov function, which the authors demonstrate performs comparably or superiorly to adaptive restarting techniques. The core idea—using adaptive averaging to achieve faster convergence without manual tuning—presents a promising simplification for practitioners, particularly in settings where restart parameters are difficult to calibrate.

The technical approach is sound. The authors establish a rigorous Lyapunov-based convergence proof, showing that the adaptive averaging rule ensures the Lyapunov function decays at the optimal rate. This is a notable strength, as it provides a unified theoretical framework for analyzing both standard and accelerated dynamics under constraints. The empirical comparison against adaptive restarting is well-executed, with results consistently supporting the claim that adaptive averaging achieves comparable or better performance across benchmark problems. The simplicity of the heuristic (which requires no additional hyperparameters beyond standard optimization settings) is particularly compelling for practical adoption.

However, the paper would benefit from greater clarity in two areas. First, the motivation for choosing adaptive averaging over other acceleration techniques (e.g., Nesterov-style momentum) is underdeveloped. While the authors contrast their method with adaptive restarting, a brief discussion of how adaptive averaging relates to established acceleration paradigms would strengthen the novelty argument. Second, the connection between the continuous-time dynamics and discrete optimization algorithms needs explicit elaboration. The manuscript states the approach is applicable to constrained convex problems but does not clarify how the continuous-time solution translates to a practical discrete implementation (e.g., via Euler discretization), which is critical for assessing real-world impact.

The writing is generally clear, though some technical terms could be better defined for broader readership. For instance, the precise mechanism by which "adaptive averaging" modulates the Lyapunov decay rate is described mathematically but could be supplemented with an intuitive explanation. Minor grammatical issues (e.g., "speed up the convergence rate of Lyapunov") should be corrected for precision. The empirical section is concise but sufficient; however, including runtime comparisons would further support the claim of practical efficiency.

Regarding evaluation criteria:  
**Quality**: The theoretical analysis is rigorous and correctly establishes the convergence rate. The experimental validation is appropriate and well-justified.  
**Novelty**: The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a novel application within Lyapunov-based acceleration for constrained optimization, distinct from existing restart-based methods.  
**Impact**: If validated in broader contexts (e.g., non-convex or large-scale settings), this approach could simplify the implementation of accelerated constrained solvers, reducing reliance on delicate parameter tuning.  
**Clarity**: The core ideas are well-presented, but the paper would improve with clearer exposition of the heuristic’s operational principles and discrete-time translation.

Overall, this work makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of accelerated constrained optimization. The adaptive averaging technique is elegantly designed, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While the manuscript could strengthen its case for novelty by better contextualizing the method against prior acceleration techniques, the approach is sufficiently distinct and promising to warrant publication. The simplicity of the heuristic and its demonstrated performance advantages over adaptive restarting are significant practical merits. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to clarify the relationship between continuous-time dynamics and discrete implementations, and to more explicitly position the method within the broader landscape of acceleration techniques. The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature and offers a compelling path toward more robust, user-friendly constrained optimization algorithms.
