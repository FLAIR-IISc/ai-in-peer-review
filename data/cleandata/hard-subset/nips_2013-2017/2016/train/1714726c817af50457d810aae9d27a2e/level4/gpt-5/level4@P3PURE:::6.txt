Summary:
The paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic to accelerate constrained convex optimization. A key contribution is the guarantee that, when the objective is not strongly convex, the method preserves the original convergence rate. This is an advantage over existing restarting heuristics, and the paper supports this claim with both theoretical analysis and experiments.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual motivation: adaptivity for acceleration without sacrificing convergence guarantees in the non-strongly-convex setting.
- Theoretical and empirical evidence that the method maintains the convergence rate where many restart-based approaches can degrade it.

Clarity and presentation:
- I found the paper difficult to follow, as it is outside my primary area of expertise. While I assume the intended audience will recognize the abbreviations and terminology, I still recommend including more background to make the paper self-contained for a broader set of readers.
- The equations around lines 33–34 and 46–47 would benefit from more intuitive explanations. High-level intuition about why the adaptive averaging behaves as claimed would help substantially.

Relation to prior work:
- Please clarify the relationship between your approach and adaptive averaging methods used in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]. In particular:
  - Are there conceptual or technical connections in how adaptivity is introduced?
  - Do your guarantees or heuristics extend in any way to non-convex settings, or are there obstacles that make such extensions inappropriate?

Practical significance and scalability:
- The experiments are conducted in R^3, which feels like a toy setting. I am concerned about practical significance in higher-dimensional problems.
- How does your method—and, for context, closely related approaches—scale to much higher dimensions? Empirical results or at least a discussion of computational and memory complexity in higher dimensions would strengthen the paper.

Minor comments:
- In Figure 1, the reference to the “solid line” is ambiguous. Consider using a dotted line or colors to clearly indicate the trajectory of the primal variable.
