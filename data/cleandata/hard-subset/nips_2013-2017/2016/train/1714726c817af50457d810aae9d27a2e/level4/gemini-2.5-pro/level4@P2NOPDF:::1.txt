This paper presents a novel and intuitive heuristic for adaptively reweighting the iterates in Nesterov's accelerated method to achieve faster convergence. The approach builds directly upon the Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) interpretation of Nesterov acceleration proposed by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). A key contribution is that, in contrast to previous adaptive averaging heuristics, the proposed method is theoretically guaranteed to dominate the standard fixed-schedule averaging scheme. This theoretical guarantee is well-supported by empirical evaluations, which demonstrate significant speed-ups on a general class of functions.

The paper is well-written and a pleasure to read. The mathematical argument is elegant, simple, and easy to follow, especially for those familiar with the foundational work of Krichene et al. The theoretical simplicity is complemented by promising empirical results that show substantial performance gains.

While the technical development is a direct extension of prior work, I believe this paper has the potential for high impact. Nesterov's accelerated method is a cornerstone of modern optimization, and its applications are pervasive. It is common practice in the literature for new, highly adaptive methods to be compared against a standard implementation of Nesterov's method that uses a fixed weight sequence. This paper convincingly demonstrates that making Nesterov's method adaptive requires only a small modification and can lead to dramatic improvements. This insight is a valuable contribution to the community and provides a much stronger baseline for future comparisons.

To further strengthen the paper from a technical standpoint, I would recommend the following:

1.  The current analysis is based on the continuous-time ODE interpretation. It would be beneficial to include an analysis of the discretized process to formally show that the algorithm's rate of convergence is preserved. While I suspect this analysis would be relatively straightforward, its inclusion would solidify the paper's theoretical foundation.

2.  I would also be interested in a discussion of the potential connections between this adaptive averaging scheme and the Conjugate Gradient (CG) method, particularly for the case of strongly convex quadratic optimization. Exploring this relationship could offer deeper insights and better contextualize the proposed method within the broader optimization literature.
