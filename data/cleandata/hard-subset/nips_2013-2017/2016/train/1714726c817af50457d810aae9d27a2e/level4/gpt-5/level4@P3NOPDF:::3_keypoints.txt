- Paper studies accelerated first-order constrained convex optimization via a continuous-time primal–dual framework, generalizing Krichene et al. and averaging mirror images of the dual to derive convergence rates and adaptive weights, with empirical validation.

- Strength: Clear motivation and coherent extension of the primal–dual continuous-time framework for acceleration.

- Strength: Rigorous analysis of generalized averaging schemes that extends beyond standard Nesterov constructions.

- Strength: Main theoretical result identifies conditions under which continuous-time trajectories can converge faster than 1/t^2.

- Strength: Adaptive weighting scheme is well motivated and empirically evaluated, including comparisons with restart heuristics.

- Weakness: Analysis is limited to continuous time; no convergence guarantees are provided for the discretized algorithm, a significant practical gap.

- Weakness: Unclear whether faster-than-1/t^2 behavior survives discretization, and under what step-size or regularity conditions it might degrade to O(1/k^2).

- Weakness: Need clearer discussion reconciling improved continuous-time rates with known oracle lower bounds for first-order methods.

- Weakness: Conditions on averaging/weight coefficients are not made sufficiently transparent or practically verifiable; trade-offs (stability vs speed) need discussion.

- Weakness: Limited engagement with related literature on inertial/accelerated ODEs (e.g., Attouch–Peypouquet) and Lyapunov constructions.

- Weakness: Empirical results are useful but cannot substitute for discrete-time theory; lacking ablation and sensitivity analyses isolating the averaging scheme vs restarts.

- Question: What minimal regularity assumptions (smoothness, Lipschitz, strong convexity, mirror map properties) are required for the main theorem, and do examples beyond smooth objectives exist?

- Question: Can the authors give an explicit weight sequence meeting their conditions and outline a numerically stable discretization with step-size/momentum choices that avoid oscillations/divergence?

- Question: How does the adaptive weighting scheme perform under noisy or stochastic gradients — any robustness analysis or empirical evidence?

- Question: For constrained problems, how is feasibility enforced in continuous and discrete time (projection, Bregman proximal steps), and does enforcement affect achievable rates?

- Question: Is the faster-than-1/t^2 rate tight in this framework, and if not, what are the conjectured limits?

- Presentation: Include a brief, high-level proof sketch for Theorem 2 (Lyapunov function and key inequalities) to aid readability.

- Presentation: Fix typos/grammar and clearly define averaging weights, indexing, normalization, and restate assumptions self-containedly.

- Presentation: Add a short related-work subsection situating contributions relative to Attouch–Peypouquet and inertial ODE analyses.

- Overall: Interesting and technically solid work with a significant main theorem and practical adaptive rule, but the lack of discrete-time guarantees is a substantial limitation; acceptance recommended if discrete-time discretization or thorough discussion and stronger empirical evidence are added.
