This paper builds upon the ODE interpretation of Nesterov’s accelerated methods, established by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015), to introduce and evaluate a novel adaptive averaging heuristic. The core idea is to dynamically reweight iterates, aiming to accelerate convergence. A key strength is the theoretical guarantee that this adaptive approach will not perform worse than standard fixed-schedule averaging. The empirical results demonstrate promising speed-ups across a range of functions. 

The paper is well-written and the mathematical arguments are clear and accessible, particularly for readers familiar with the prior work of Krichene et al. The experimental results are compelling. This work has the potential for significant impact, given the widespread use of Nesterov’s acceleration in various applications. Often, empirical evaluations compare new methods against Nesterov’s acceleration using a fixed weighting scheme; this paper highlights the benefit of making even established methods adaptive with a minimal modification.

Several aspects particularly stand out. The connection to replicator dynamics when applied to the simplex constraint is insightful. The paper also correctly identifies a gap in the literature regarding guarantees on the convergence rates of existing heuristics like adaptive restarting. 

To further strengthen the work, I recommend providing an analysis of the discretized process to demonstrate preservation of the convergence rate. Given the existing Lyapunov analysis, this appears achievable. Exploring connections between the proposed adaptive averaging scheme and the Conjugate Gradient method for strongly convex quadratic optimization would also be a valuable addition, potentially illuminating the relationship between these well-established optimization techniques. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds on strong prior work (Krichene et al., NIPS 2015) and provides a clear theoretical justification for the proposed heuristic.
*   **Promising Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over standard methods.
*   **Practical Significance:**  The work addresses a common practice in empirical evaluation (using fixed schedules for Nesterov's method) and offers a simple, effective improvement.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-structured and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incremental Advance:** While valuable, the work is an extension of existing research rather than a completely novel breakthrough.
*   **Lack of Discretization Analysis:** The absence of a theoretical analysis for the discrete-time implementation leaves a minor gap in the justification of the proposed method’s practical utility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with clear potential to be influential. The authors successfully leverage existing theoretical tools to develop a practical and effective improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm. While the contribution is incremental, it is a *useful* increment. I recommend acceptance.
