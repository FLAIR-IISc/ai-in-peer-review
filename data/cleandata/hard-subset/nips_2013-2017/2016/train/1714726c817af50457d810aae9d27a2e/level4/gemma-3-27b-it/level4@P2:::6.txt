This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated constrained convex optimization. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that this method preserves the quadratic convergence rate even when the objective function is not strongly convex, a key advantage over existing restarting heuristics. The authors support this claim with both theoretical analysis and empirical results. While the paper’s depth and technicality reflect a specialized area, its connection to broader optimization concepts could be strengthened with additional contextualization.

The paper builds upon recent work linking Nesterov’s accelerated methods to continuous-time ODEs, framing optimization as a coupled dual-primal dynamic. By carefully analyzing a family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, parameterized by weight functions *w* and *η*, the authors derive sufficient conditions – presented in Theorem 2 – to guarantee a desired convergence rate using a Lyapunov argument. A novel adaptive averaging heuristic is then proposed, aiming to dynamically compute these weights to speed up convergence. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that this heuristic preserves the quadratic convergence rate, and numerical experiments suggest it performs competitively with, and sometimes outperforms, adaptive restarting techniques. The extension to the replicator dynamics on the simplex is a particularly interesting result, demonstrating the method’s applicability to a well-known problem in evolutionary game theory.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a solid theoretical foundation, utilizing Lyapunov analysis to establish convergence guarantees.
*   **Novelty:** The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a novel approach to accelerating first-order methods, offering a theoretical advantage over existing heuristics in non-strongly convex settings.
*   **Clear Presentation of Core Ideas:** The main concepts – AMD dynamics, Lyapunov function, and the adaptive averaging heuristic – are clearly articulated, even if the underlying details require specialized knowledge.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments provide supporting evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method, demonstrating competitive performance with existing techniques.
*   **Connection to Replicator Dynamics:** The application of the method to the replicator equation highlights its potential in different domains.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Accessibility:** The paper assumes a high level of prior knowledge in convex optimization and dynamical systems. Providing more background information on key concepts and notations would improve readability for a broader audience. For instance, clarifying the role of the mirror map and its relationship to Bregman divergences could be beneficial.
*   **Equation Clarity:** The equations presented between lines 33-34 and 46-47, defining the AMD dynamics, could benefit from more intuitive explanations. A more detailed walkthrough of the derivation and the meaning of each term would aid understanding.
*   **Related Work Discussion:** The relationship between this work and adaptive learning rate methods commonly used in *non-convex* optimization, such as Adagrad [1] and Adam [2], is not fully explored. A discussion of the differences in methodology and applicability would be valuable.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The experiments are conducted in R<sup>3</sup>, which, as the authors acknowledge, is a simplified scenario. It’s unclear how well the proposed method scales to high-dimensional problems commonly encountered in machine learning. Addressing this scalability concern, even with preliminary analysis or discussion, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Figure Clarity:** The reference to the “solid line” in Figure 1 is ambiguous. Using distinct line styles (e.g., dotted, dashed) or colors would improve visual clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of accelerated optimization.
*   The theoretical guarantees and empirical results demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.
*   The work addresses a relevant problem – accelerating convex optimization – and offers a practical solution with clear advantages over existing heuristics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper’s accessibility is limited by its technical depth and reliance on specialized knowledge.
*   The lack of scalability analysis raises concerns about the practical applicability of the method to real-world, high-dimensional problems.
*   The relationship to related work in adaptive learning rates for non-convex optimization could be more thoroughly explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant technical merit. While the limited accessibility and scalability concerns are valid points, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the promising experimental results outweigh these drawbacks. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance, with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the points raised regarding accessibility and scalability in a revised version.
