### **Summary**

This paper investigates continuous-time dynamics for accelerated, constrained convex optimization. Building on prior work that connects Nesterov's accelerated methods to second-order ordinary differential equations (ODEs), particularly the averaging interpretation of accelerated mirror descent (AMD), the authors introduce a generalized family of dynamics, termed AMDw,η. This framework is parameterized by two functions: η(t), which controls the rate of gradient accumulation for a dual variable, and w(t), which defines a generalized weighted averaging scheme for the primal variable.

The core of the theoretical analysis relies on a Lyapunov function, Lr, which combines the objective function value with a Bregman divergence term. The authors derive sufficient conditions on the functions w(t) and η(t) that guarantee the Lyapunov function is non-increasing, thereby ensuring a convergence rate of O(1/r(t)). As a concrete example, they show how this framework can be used to derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics, a well-known ODE in evolutionary game theory.

The main practical contribution is an "adaptive averaging" heuristic. Motivated by the Lyapunov analysis, this heuristic adaptively adjusts the averaging weights w(t) to maximize the decrease of the Lyapunov function at each step. The authors prove that this adaptive scheme preserves the theoretical O(1/t^2) convergence rate in continuous time and provide a discrete-time algorithm that also maintains this rate. Numerical experiments in a low-dimensional setting compare this new heuristic to existing adaptive restart schemes, suggesting it performs competitively and, in some cases, provides significant improvements.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Solid Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a clean and unified framework (AMDw,η) for studying a broad class of accelerated dynamics through a generalized averaging scheme. The use of a Lyapunov function to derive sufficient conditions for convergence is elegant and provides clear theoretical insight into the role of the averaging and gradient accumulation rates.

2.  **Well-Motivated Heuristic with Guarantees:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is directly derived from the theoretical analysis, aiming to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function. A key advantage, and a significant contribution, is the proof that this adaptivity does not compromise the worst-case O(1/t^2) convergence rate for general convex functions. This stands in contrast to many popular restarting heuristics, for which such guarantees are often limited to more restricted function classes (e.g., quadratic or strongly convex).

3.  **Interesting Connection to Replicator Dynamics:** The application of the framework to derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics is a compelling example. It demonstrates the utility of the generalized formulation and connects the abstract theory of accelerated optimization to a concrete and important dynamical system from another field.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Experimental Validation and Significance:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its empirical evaluation. All experiments are conducted in R^3 on the probability simplex. While this is useful for visualization, it is a low-dimensional, "toy" setting that fails to demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed method for problems typically encountered in machine learning. To make a stronger case for the method's utility, experiments on higher-dimensional, more realistic optimization problems (e.g., logistic regression, LASSO, or SVMs on benchmark datasets) are necessary. It is unclear how the performance gains observed in 3D will translate to settings where N >> 3.

2.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is technically dense and assumes significant familiarity with continuous-time optimization dynamics, convex duality, and mirror descent. While this is appropriate for a specialized audience, the clarity could be improved for a broader NIPS readership. In particular, the core ODEs in equations (1) and (6) could benefit from more intuitive explanations. A brief, high-level description of what the primal (X) and dual (Z) variables represent and how their coupling leads to acceleration would help ground the subsequent technical analysis.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison with Related Adaptive Methods:** The paper briefly mentions other adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam but dismisses them as "not designed for accelerated methods." While true, a more detailed discussion of the relationship would be valuable. These methods also adapt to the geometry of the problem using gradient information. A deeper comparison exploring the differences in mechanism (e.g., adapting learning rates per-coordinate vs. adapting a global averaging schedule) and their respective strengths would better position this work within the broader context of adaptive optimization.

### **Suggestions for Improvement and Questions for the Authors**

*   **Experiments:** As mentioned above, the most critical improvement would be to include experiments on higher-dimensional problems to assess the scalability and practical relevance of adaptive averaging. How does the method perform on sparse problems or standard machine learning classification/regression tasks?
*   **Intuition:** I would encourage the authors to add more intuition behind the key equations, especially the primal-dual dynamics in Section 1 and the primal representation in Section 3. For instance, what is the geometric intuition behind using a weighted average of the *mirrored dual trajectory* for the primal variable?
*   **Relation to Adam/Adagrad:** Could the authors elaborate on the conceptual differences between adaptive averaging and adaptive learning rate methods? Are there scenarios where one is clearly preferable to the other, or could they potentially be combined?
