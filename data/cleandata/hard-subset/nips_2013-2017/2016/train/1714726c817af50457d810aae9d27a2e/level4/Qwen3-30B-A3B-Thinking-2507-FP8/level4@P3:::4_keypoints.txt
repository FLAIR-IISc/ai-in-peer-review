- Studies a generalization of accelerated mirror descent using flexible averaging parameterized by dual learning rate η(t) and averaging weights w(t).  
- Provides a unified Lyapunov analysis establishing sufficient conditions on η and w to achieve a target convergence rate r(t).  
- Theorem 2 is the key theoretical result showing Lr is a Lyapunov function and f(X(t)) - f* ≤ C / r(t) under those conditions.  
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts weights to minimize the Lyapunov derivative while preserving theoretical rates.  
- Supplies a discrete-time version of the adaptive method with convergence guarantees in the supplementary material.  
- Empirically compares adaptive averaging to restart heuristics across several objectives (strong/weak convex quadratics, linear, KL on simplex).  
- Numerical results show adaptive averaging consistently matches or outperforms existing restarts, with notable gains on strongly convex problems.  
- Demonstrates acceleration of replicator dynamics (mirror descent on the simplex), connecting results to evolutionary game theory.  
- Theoretical analysis is sound, with a carefully constructed Lyapunov argument and clear convergence conditions.  
- Properly situates the work relative to prior continuous-time ODE approaches (Su et al., Krichene et al.) and prior averaging/acceleration links.  
- Claims originality by generalizing averaging frameworks and introducing the first adaptive method that preserves theoretical rates while improving empirical performance.  
- Paper is well-written, mathematically consistent, and maintains the continuous-to-discrete connection; figures effectively illustrate results.  
- Significance: provides a theoretically grounded, practical alternative to restart heuristics with broader applicability to first-order optimization.  
- Strength: rigorous theory, novel adaptive heuristic, comprehensive experiments, strong literature connection, clear presentation.  
- Weakness: adaptive heuristic validated mainly on simplex-constrained problems; broader constraint sets need exploration.  
- Weakness: the discrete-time analysis in the supplement could be more directly tied to the main continuous-time results.  
- Weakness: related work discussion could contrast more thoroughly with recent adaptive restart methods (e.g., [23]).  
- Pro for acceptance: significant theoretical advance, novel practical method with strong empirical and theoretical support.  
- Con for acceptance: experimental scope limited mainly to simplex/constrained convex problems; could expand to more diverse problem classes.
