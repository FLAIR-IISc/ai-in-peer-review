

This paper introduces a novel adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerating constrained convex optimization, building upon continuous-time accelerated descent dynamics. The authors present a unified framework for Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics parameterized by weight functions w and η, and provide a Lyapunov-based analysis that establishes sufficient conditions for achieving desired convergence rates. The main contribution is an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the quadratic convergence rate without requiring strong convexity assumptions—a significant advantage over existing restarting heuristics that often fail to maintain convergence rates for non-strongly convex problems.

The paper demonstrates theoretical soundness through rigorous analysis, including Theorem 2 which provides sufficient conditions for the Lyapunov function to decrease, guaranteeing convergence at the specified rate. The authors also establish connections to previous work, particularly extending the continuous-time analysis of accelerated methods initiated by Su et al. (NIPS 2014) and Krichene et al. (NIPS 2015). Their approach to accelerating replicator dynamics (a well-known ODE in evolutionary game theory) provides an interesting application that demonstrates the framework's versatility.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical contributions and the careful analysis of convergence guarantees. The adaptive averaging heuristic successfully preserves the convergence rate while offering practical improvements over existing methods, as demonstrated through numerical experiments comparing with gradient and speed restarting heuristics. The experimental results show that adaptive averaging consistently outperforms restarting heuristics, particularly for strongly convex problems, and maintains the Lyapunov function's monotonic decrease where restarting methods fail.

However, the paper's clarity would benefit from additional background explanations. The authors assume significant familiarity with continuous-time optimization and mirror descent concepts, which may challenge readers outside the optimization community. Several key equations (particularly those around lines 33-47 in the original manuscript) would be more accessible with intuitive explanations of their physical or algorithmic meaning. For instance, the primal representation in Section 3 could be better motivated by explaining how the transformation ∇²ψ*∘∇ψ ensures feasibility in constrained optimization.

The paper's relation to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization (such as Adagrad and Adam) is not sufficiently addressed. While the authors briefly mention these methods in Section 4, they fail to clarify how adaptive averaging differs in its theoretical guarantees and application scope. A more detailed discussion of this relationship would strengthen the paper's positioning within the broader optimization literature.

Regarding significance, the current experiments conducted in R³ represent a limited testbed that does not convincingly demonstrate the method's practical utility for real-world optimization problems. The authors should provide more compelling evidence of scalability to higher dimensions, which is critical for machine learning applications. The paper would benefit from additional experiments on larger-scale problems or theoretical analysis of computational complexity.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods, particularly in the constrained setting. The adaptive averaging heuristic provides a principled approach to improving convergence rates while maintaining rigorous guarantees—a clear advantage over heuristic restarting methods. The connection to replicator dynamics also offers an interesting interdisciplinary application.

The main weaknesses are the limited experimental validation in higher dimensions and the lack of sufficient explanation for technical concepts that would help readers outside the optimization specialty. The authors should consider adding a brief section to explain key concepts like mirror maps and continuous-time optimization for readers who may not be familiar with these topics.

In terms of originality, the paper distinguishes itself from previous NIPS papers on continuous-time accelerated methods (notably Krichene et al., NIPS 2015) through its generalized averaging framework and adaptive heuristic. The theoretical conditions provided in Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 offer a more flexible approach to acceleration than previous work.

Overall, this paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the field of optimization with clear potential for practical applications. With improved clarity through additional background explanations and more comprehensive experimental validation, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a meaningful advancement over existing methods, particularly in its ability to preserve convergence rates without strong convexity assumptions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity and scalability concerns raised above.
