This paper builds upon an earlier work that connects accelerated Mirror Descent and continuous-time ODE, extending the ODE dynamics with a more general averaging scheme and proposing a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one of its discretizations. In some cases, the new variant of AMD with adaptive averaging outperforms existing AMD algorithms with restarting. The proposed adaptive averaging strategy is interesting and comes with a good intuition.

However, the originality and usefulness of the paper remain a concern. Most elements, such as the Lyapunov function and discretization, directly follow from an earlier NIPS paper [7]. The extension from simple averaging to generalized averaging is relatively straightforward and not particularly impactful, as the authors only consider the case with a quadratic rate. Faster rates would require higher-order methods, which are not always applicable.

Some results, such as the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation into primal form, and reduction to replicator dynamics, promote a better understanding of the accelerated Mirror Descent algorithm. However, these contributions are not significantly important to advance machine learning.

Although the adaptive averaging is introduced to satisfy the conditions sufficient to guarantee convergence of the ODE, it is not fully theoretically grounded due to the approximation during discretization. Furthermore, there is no explicit convergent result provided, making it a heuristic, as acknowledged by the authors. Additionally, there exist various averaging schemes for Nesterov's accelerated algorithms, and without comparing to other alternatives, it is unclear whether this adaptive variant makes a significant difference.

The experiment on a toy example with only quadratic/linear objectives is not convincing enough to demonstrate the advantage of adaptive averaging, especially for the general case with strong convexity.

After reading the authors' rebuttal, I have updated my assessment. The authors promise to add higher-dimensional and non-quadratic examples in the revision, provide a theoretical proof of convergence of adaptive averaging, implement adaptive averaging on Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method, and discuss comparisons to other adaptive averaging schemes. These additions will help address some of the concerns raised in this review.
