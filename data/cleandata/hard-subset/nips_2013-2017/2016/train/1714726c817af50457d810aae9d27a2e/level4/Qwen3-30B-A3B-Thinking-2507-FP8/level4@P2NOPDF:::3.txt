

This paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, extending the continuous-time framework introduced by Krichene et al. [7]. The authors build on their prior work by generalizing the averaging scheme used to couple primal and dual trajectories, moving beyond Nesterov's method as a specific discretization of an ordinary differential equation (ODE) system. Theorem 2 establishes convergence rates under conditions on the averaging coefficients, demonstrating rates faster than $1/t^2$ in the continuous setting. An adaptive weight selection strategy with theoretical guarantees is developed and empirically validated, showing competitive performance against restarting techniques commonly used to accelerate practical implementations. However, the theoretical analysis of the discrete counterpart of the proposed algorithm is omitted, which is a significant gap given the practical relevance of discrete methods.  

The paper is well-written and makes a technically sound contribution to the continuous-time analysis of accelerated methods. The new averaging schemes yield meaningful improvements over existing rates, and the continuous-time analysis is rigorous. While the core techniques for analyzing such dynamics are now well-established (e.g., via Lyapunov functions), the specific results on faster-than-$1/t^2$ convergence for general averaging schemes represent a clear advancement. The authors should explicitly acknowledge related work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors [e.g., *Journal of Optimization Theory and Applications*, 2016], which develops similar continuous-time perspectives on acceleration.  

A critical unresolved question concerns the preservation of the improved convergence rate when transitioning from the continuous to the discrete setting. The absence of discrete analysis leaves open whether the theoretical benefits observed in the continuous case translate to practical algorithms. This is particularly relevant given that discrete methods dominate real-world applications. While the adaptive weights are well-motivated and experimentally validated, the lack of discrete guarantees weakens the paper's impact for practitioners.  

The experimental section effectively compares adaptive weights against restarting, but the absence of discrete convergence rates in the theory limits the interpretability of these results. For instance, it remains unclear whether the observed empirical gains stem from the continuous-time insight or are attributable to other factors. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of potential discrete adaptations or a clear statement about the theoretical limitations of the current framework.  

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution to continuous-time acceleration theory. The results are significant, and the writing is clear. However, the omission of discrete analysis is a notable shortcoming, especially for a venue like NIPS where algorithmic practicality is highly valued. Addressing this gap—either through future work or a more explicit acknowledgment of its necessity—would substantially strengthen the paper. The connection to Attouch et al. should also be formalized in the related work.  

In summary, the paper advances the continuous-time understanding of acceleration via general averaging schemes, but its practical relevance is partially undermined by the lack of discrete convergence analysis. The core theoretical contribution is valid and interesting, yet the work would be significantly more impactful with discrete guarantees or a clearer roadmap for their derivation. I recommend acceptance with the expectation that the authors address the discrete analysis gap in revision.
