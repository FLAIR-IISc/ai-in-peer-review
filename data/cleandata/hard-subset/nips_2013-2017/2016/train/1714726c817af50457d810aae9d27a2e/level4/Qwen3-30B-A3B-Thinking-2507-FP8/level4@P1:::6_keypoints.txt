- Studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization via a generalized accelerated mirror descent (AMD) family parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t), analyzed with Lyapunov functions.

- Establishes sufficient conditions on weight functions (Theorem 2) that guarantee desired convergence rates and connect to Nesterov-style acceleration.

- Proposes a theoretically grounded adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the Lyapunov function and convergence rate while improving empirical performance.

- Extends prior continuous-time acceleration work (Krichene et al. 2015; Su et al. 2014) to a broader class of averaging schemes.

- Provides a unified Lyapunov analysis that generalizes previous continuous-time acceleration results.

- Demonstrates acceleration of replicator dynamics on the simplex, creating a novel connection between optimization and evolutionary game theory.

- Theoretical analysis is rigorous, including existence and uniqueness results (Theorem 1) and careful Lyapunov-based convergence proofs.

- Experiments compare adaptive averaging to gradient and speed restarting heuristics and show adaptive averaging performs at least as well and often better.

- Presentation is mathematically dense and would benefit from more intuitive explanations for key equations (notably Section 2).

- The paper mentions but does not adequately explain the relationship between adaptive averaging and adaptive optimization methods like Adagrad and Adam.

- Experimental scope is limited to low-dimensional (R^3) problems, raising concerns about scalability and practical utility in high-dimensional settings.

- Practical implementation details and discussion are limited and should be expanded for real-world applicability.

- Some presentation issues (e.g., ambiguous "solid line" reference in Figure 1) need correction for clarity.

- Originality is solid: generalized averaging scheme and adaptive heuristic are novel and the replicator dynamics link is well-motivated.

- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify adaptive-method relations, add intuitive explanations, include high-dimensional experiments, fix figures, and expand implementation discussion.
