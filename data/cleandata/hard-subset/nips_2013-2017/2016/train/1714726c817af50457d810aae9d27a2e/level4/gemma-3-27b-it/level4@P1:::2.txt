This paper builds upon prior work establishing a connection between accelerated Mirror Descent and continuous-time ODEs, extending the ODE dynamics with a more general averaging scheme and proposing a novel adaptive averaging strategy for its discretization. The authors demonstrate that this new variant of AMD with adaptive averaging can outperform existing AMD algorithms with restarting in certain cases. The proposed adaptive averaging strategy is intuitively appealing and presents a fresh approach. However, concerns regarding originality and practical impact remain.

Most elements of the paper – including the Lyapunov function, discretization strategy, and overall framework – closely follow from an earlier NIPS publication [11]. The extension from simple averaging to a generalized averaging scheme feels incremental and lacks substantial impact, especially given that the authors primarily focus on the quadratic convergence rate case; achieving faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which are not universally applicable. While results like the interpretation of the energy function, reformulation into a primal form, and reduction to the replicator dynamics enhance understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent, they do not represent significant advancements for the machine learning community. 

Although the adaptive averaging strategy is designed to satisfy sufficient conditions guaranteeing ODE convergence, its theoretical foundation remains incomplete due to approximations introduced during discretization. Critically, the paper lacks an explicit convergence proof, positioning it as a heuristic, as the authors themselves acknowledge. Furthermore, numerous alternative averaging schemes exist for Nesterov’s accelerated algorithms; without comparison to these alternatives, it’s unclear whether this adaptive variant offers a significant improvement.

The experimental evaluation, confined to toy examples with quadratic or linear objectives, is not sufficiently compelling to demonstrate the advantages of adaptive averaging, particularly in more general and strongly convex scenarios.
