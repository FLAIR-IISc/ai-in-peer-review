

This paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic to accelerate constrained convex optimization, demonstrating that it preserves the original convergence rate without requiring strong convexity—a key advantage over existing restarting techniques, validated both theoretically and empirically. While the technical contribution appears sound, the paper's accessibility requires significant improvement. The writing assumes specialized familiarity with optimization terminology and notation, which may alienate readers outside the immediate field. To enhance clarity, the authors should provide a brief foundational context for key concepts (e.g., the role of adaptive averaging in constrained settings) and explicitly define all acronyms upon first use.  

The exposition of equations between lines 33–34 and 46–47 lacks intuitive motivation, making their derivation and significance difficult to grasp. A concise verbal explanation of the geometric or algorithmic rationale behind these steps would greatly aid comprehension. Additionally, the paper omits a critical connection to adaptive methods in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]), despite their widespread use. Clarifying how this work relates to or diverges from such approaches—particularly regarding convergence guarantees under non-convexity—would strengthen the theoretical positioning.  

The experimental evaluation presents a notable limitation: all tests are conducted in $\mathbb{R}^3$, a trivially low-dimensional setting that fails to demonstrate practical utility. The authors should address scalability to high-dimensional problems (e.g., $d \gg 100$), which is essential for real-world machine learning applications. Without evidence of performance in larger-scale scenarios, the claimed "acceleration" remains unconvincing. A discussion of computational complexity (e.g., per-iteration cost, memory requirements) relative to baselines would further contextualize the method’s practicality.  

Figure 1 contains an ambiguous reference to a "solid line" without clear labeling. To improve readability, the authors should adopt distinct line styles (e.g., dotted vs. solid) or colors to differentiate trajectories of the primal variable from other elements.  

**Summary of key concerns:**  
1. **Accessibility:** Insufficient background for non-specialists; requires foundational context.  
2. **Clarity:** Equations lack intuitive explanations; critical terms need definition.  
3. **Theoretical Context:** Missing comparison to adaptive non-convex methods (Adagrad, Adam).  
4. **Practical Relevance:** Experiments confined to $\mathbb{R}^3$; scalability unaddressed.  
5. **Presentation:** Ambiguous figure elements; needs improved visual labeling.  

**Recommendation:** The paper’s core idea is promising but currently lacks sufficient rigor and accessibility for NIPS. To merit acceptance, the authors must:  
- Add a concise section on necessary background concepts.  
- Revise equations to include intuitive explanations.  
- Discuss connections to adaptive non-convex optimization literature.  
- Expand experiments to higher dimensions ($d \geq 100$) or provide scalability analysis.  
- Clarify Figure 1 with explicit line-style differentiation.  

Without these revisions, the work’s contribution remains difficult to assess and its practical impact unclear. The current draft would benefit from substantial reworking to align with NIPS’ standards for technical depth and reproducibility.
