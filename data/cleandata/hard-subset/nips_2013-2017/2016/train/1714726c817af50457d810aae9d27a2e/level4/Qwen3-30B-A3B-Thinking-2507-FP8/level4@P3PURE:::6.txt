

This paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerating constrained convex optimization, demonstrating theoretically and empirically that the method preserves the original convergence rate when the objective function is not strongly convex—a key advantage over existing restarting heuristics. While the technical contribution appears sound within its domain, the paper's presentation presents significant accessibility challenges. As the method falls outside my expertise, I found the content difficult to follow without additional context; the paper assumes familiarity with specific abbreviations and terminology that may not be widely accessible to the broader NIPS audience. I recommend including foundational background to improve readability. Additionally, the equations between lines 33–34 and 46–47 would benefit from more intuitive explanations to clarify their role in the proposed framework. The connection to adaptive averaging methods in non-convex optimization (e.g., Adagrad [1] and Adam [2]) warrants explicit discussion, as this could strengthen the paper's positioning within the wider literature. Regarding practical significance, the experimental validation in $\mathbb{R}^3$ constitutes a toy problem that does not adequately address scalability; the authors should clarify how their method (and related approaches) perform in significantly higher dimensions. Finally, the reference to the "solid line" in Figure 1 is ambiguous; using distinct line styles (e.g., dotted vs. solid) or colors would better convey the trajectory of the primal variable. [1] Duchi, John, Elad Hazan, and Yoram Singer. "Adaptive subgradient methods for online learning and stochastic optimization." Journal of Machine Learning Research 12.Jul (2011): 2121-2159. [2] Kingma, Diederik, and Jimmy Ba. "Adam: A method for stochastic optimization." International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR). (2014).
