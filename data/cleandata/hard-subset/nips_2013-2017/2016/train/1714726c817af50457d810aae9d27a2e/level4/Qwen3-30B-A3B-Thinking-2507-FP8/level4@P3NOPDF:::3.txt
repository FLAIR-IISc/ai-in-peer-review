

The paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, extending the continuous-time framework introduced by Krichene et al. [7]. The authors generalize the primal trajectory construction in [7], which uses a specific averaging of the mirror map of the dual variable, by studying broader averaging schemes. The core contribution is Theorem 2, which establishes convergence rates under suitable conditions on the averaging coefficients. In the continuous setting, the authors derive rates faster than $O(1/t^2)$, and propose an adaptive weight selection strategy with theoretical guarantees, validated empirically against restarting techniques. However, the discrete counterpart of the algorithm lacks theoretical analysis, a notable omission given the practical relevance of discrete implementations.

The continuous-time analysis is rigorous and well-motivated. The authors convincingly demonstrate that certain averaging schemes achieve rates beyond the standard $O(1/t^2)$, a significant theoretical advance. This result is relevant for understanding the fundamental limits of acceleration in constrained settings. The adaptive weight choice is practically insightful, and the comparison with restarting techniques provides useful empirical context. The paper is clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to results.

That said, the omission of discrete analysis weakens the paper's impact. While the continuous dynamics offer valuable intuition, the primary utility of accelerated methods lies in their discrete implementations. The authors should explicitly discuss the gap between continuous and discrete analyses, and whether the faster convergence rate is preserved in practice. This is particularly important given that restarting techniques—which the authors compare against—are discrete procedures. Without discrete convergence guarantees, the practical significance of the continuous results remains partially speculative.

The work builds on established continuous-time frameworks (e.g., the ODE interpretation of Nesterov’s method), but the specific averaging schemes and their convergence properties constitute a novel contribution. However, the authors should acknowledge related work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and Redont (2016), which develops a similar continuous-time perspective for proximal algorithms and accelerated methods. This omission is significant, as Attouch et al. provide foundational tools for analyzing such dynamics, including convergence rates and stability arguments. Citing this work would better position the paper within the existing literature.

A minor point: the phrase "faster w,r,too 1/t^2" in the draft is a typo and should read "faster than $O(1/t^2)$." The improved rate is stated as "faster than $1/t^2$," but the precise claim should specify it as $o(1/t^2)$ or $O(1/t^{2+\epsilon})$ for some $\epsilon > 0$, depending on the exact result. The draft should clarify this to avoid ambiguity.

The experimental section effectively compares adaptive weights with restarting, supporting the practical viability of the proposed scheme. However, without discrete convergence analysis, the experiments only partially validate the theoretical claims. It would strengthen the paper to include preliminary discrete results or a discussion of discretization challenges (e.g., step-size selection, stability).

Overall, the paper makes a solid contribution to the continuous-time analysis of accelerated methods, with a novel averaging framework and improved convergence rates. The writing is clear, and the technical content is sound. The main limitations are the absence of discrete analysis and the oversight of key related work by Attouch et al. These are addressable in revision.

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should: (1) add citations to Attouch, Peypouquet, and Redont (2016) and related works on continuous-time acceleration; (2) explicitly discuss the gap between continuous and discrete analyses and its implications; (3) clarify the precise nature of the improved convergence rate (e.g., $o(1/t^2)$ vs. $O(1/t^{2+\epsilon})$); and (4) briefly address how the adaptive weights might be discretized. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution to the literature.
