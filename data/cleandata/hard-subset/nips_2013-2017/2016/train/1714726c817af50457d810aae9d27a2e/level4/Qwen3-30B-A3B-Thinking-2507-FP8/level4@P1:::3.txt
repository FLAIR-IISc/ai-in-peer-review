

This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization through the lens of continuous-time dynamics, extending the work of Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016) by introducing a generalized family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics. The authors establish a unified framework for understanding accelerated methods via continuous-time ODEs, parameterized by two weight functions: one governing the averaging scheme (w(t)) and the other determining the gradient accumulation rate (η(t)). Using a Lyapunov analysis, they derive sufficient conditions on these weight functions to guarantee specific convergence rates (e.g., 1/t² for the quadratic rate), and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves these convergence guarantees while improving empirical performance. The paper demonstrates how this framework can accelerate the replicator dynamics on the simplex—a significant contribution given the replicator dynamics' importance in evolutionary game theory—and provides thorough numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging with existing restarting heuristics.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a rigorous Lyapunov analysis for a broad class of accelerated dynamics, extending the theoretical understanding of Nesterov's methods beyond the specific case studied in prior work. The conditions derived in Theorem 2 are general and applicable to various constraint sets, offering a unified perspective on accelerated methods. Second, the adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, preserving the convergence rate while adaptively improving performance. Third, the connection to replicator dynamics is particularly compelling, demonstrating how the framework can be applied to an important class of ODEs in evolutionary game theory. Finally, the experimental results are thorough and convincing, showing adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics in all tested scenarios, with significant improvements for strongly convex problems.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with careful attention to technical detail. The theoretical framework is presented logically, building from the continuous-time analysis to the adaptive heuristic and then to empirical validation. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature, including foundational works on continuous-time optimization dynamics (Su et al., NIPS 2014; Krichene et al., NIPS 2016) and related work on restarting heuristics (O'Donoghue & Candès, Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 2015).

However, the paper has a notable limitation: the discrete-time counterpart of the proposed method is not developed in the main text (though mentioned in the supplementary material). While the continuous-time analysis is rigorous and insightful, NIPS primarily focuses on discrete algorithms, making this omission significant. The authors should address how the continuous-time insights translate to practical discrete algorithms, particularly since the adaptive averaging heuristic is intended for discrete implementation. Additionally, the connection to the work of Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors (e.g., "Fast convergence of an inertial gradient-like system with vanishing viscosity," 2015) is underdeveloped. These papers explore similar continuous-time dynamics and convergence rates, and a more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper's positioning within the literature.

Strengths:
- Rigorous and general Lyapunov analysis for continuous-time dynamics
- Unified theoretical framework extending prior work on accelerated methods
- Practical adaptive heuristic with strong empirical validation
- Significant application to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory

Weaknesses:
- Missing discrete-time analysis (critical for NIPS audience)
- Insufficient discussion of related work on continuous-time dynamics
- Limited technical detail on discretization of the adaptive heuristic

The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding accelerated optimization through continuous-time dynamics, with clear theoretical insights and compelling empirical results. While the absence of discrete-time analysis is a notable gap, the continuous-time framework provides meaningful insights that can guide the design of new discrete algorithms. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a significant problem in optimization. Given the paper's quality, relevance to NIPS, and the importance of its contributions to both theory and practice, I recommend acceptance.
