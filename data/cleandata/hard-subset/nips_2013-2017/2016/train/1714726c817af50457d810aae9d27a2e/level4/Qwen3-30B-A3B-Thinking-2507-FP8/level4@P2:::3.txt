

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending the continuous-time perspective initiated by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016) and Su et al. (NIPS 2014) that connects accelerated first-order methods with ordinary differential equations. The authors generalize the averaging scheme used in the primal trajectory, demonstrating that different weight functions in the averaging can achieve desired convergence rates. Using a Lyapunov argument, they derive sufficient conditions on the weight functions to guarantee convergence rates in continuous time, showing that accelerated mirror descent can be interpreted as a coupling of primal and dual variables where the primal trajectory is a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory. The paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function, providing theoretical guarantees for preserving the quadratic convergence rate in discrete time, and compares this approach with existing restarting heuristics through numerical experiments.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a unified theoretical framework for accelerated mirror descent dynamics with generalized averaging schemes, establishing sufficient conditions for convergence using a Lyapunov function. This extends previous work by Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016) and Su et al. (NIPS 2014) by considering a broader family of weight functions. Second, the authors demonstrate how the replicator dynamics (a key ODE in evolutionary game theory) can be accelerated using their averaging scheme. Third, they propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate while outperforming existing restarting techniques in numerical experiments.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed, with careful attention to the conditions required for the Lyapunov function to be valid. The authors clearly establish the connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms, and the proof techniques are sound. However, the paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the discrete version of the algorithm, which is a significant omission given the practical importance of discrete implementations. While the continuous-time analysis is solid, the discrete version's theoretical guarantees would strengthen the paper's contribution.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The writing is precise, well-organized, and accessible to readers familiar with convex optimization. The authors provide clear explanations of technical concepts, including the role of the Bregman divergence and the mirror map. The examples, particularly the connection to replicator dynamics, effectively illustrate the theoretical results. The figures and experimental results are well-presented and complement the theoretical contributions.

The originality of the work is noteworthy. While the connection between accelerated methods and continuous-time dynamics has been explored in previous NIPS papers, this paper makes a novel contribution by generalizing the averaging scheme and providing a systematic analysis of the conditions required for convergence. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a meaningful extension beyond the restarting techniques previously studied in the literature. The paper clearly differentiates itself from Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016) by providing a broader family of averaging schemes with theoretical guarantees. The authors could strengthen this section by more explicitly citing and contrasting with recent related work on continuous-time optimization, particularly the contributions by Attouch and Peypouquet (e.g., "The rate of convergence of Nesterov's accelerated forward-backward method" and "Fast convergence of an inertial gradient-like system with vanishing viscosity"), which provide complementary perspectives on continuous-time acceleration.

The significance of the work is substantial. Accelerated optimization methods are widely used in machine learning and other applications, and the authors provide both theoretical insights and practical improvements to these methods. The adaptive averaging heuristic demonstrates significant performance gains over existing techniques in numerical experiments, particularly for strongly convex problems. The connection to replicator dynamics also provides interdisciplinary relevance to evolutionary game theory. The work advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded approach to adaptive averaging that preserves convergence rates, addressing an important practical challenge in implementing accelerated methods.

The main weakness of the paper is the absence of a theoretical analysis for the discrete version of the adaptive averaging algorithm. While the continuous-time analysis is rigorous, the discrete implementation is crucial for practical applications. The authors state that their heuristic preserves the convergence rate in discrete time, but provide no theoretical justification for this claim. The supplementary material mentions a bound on the convergence rate, but this is not presented in the main paper, limiting the paper's impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical framework for accelerated mirror descent with generalized averaging
- Rigorous continuous-time analysis with clear theoretical guarantees
- Demonstrated practical improvements over existing heuristics
- Well-written and well-organized presentation
- Significant connection to replicator dynamics in evolutionary game theory

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing theoretical analysis of the discrete algorithm
- The main contribution (faster convergence rates) might not be preserved in discrete implementation
- The adaptive averaging heuristic is presented without a clear comparison to other adaptive methods like Adagrad or Adam

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes significant theoretical contributions to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods. While the lack of discrete-time analysis is a notable weakness, the continuous-time results are rigorous and the practical implications are compelling. The paper advances the state of the art in optimization theory and provides valuable insights for practitioners. I recommend acceptance, as the theoretical contributions are substantial and the practical relevance is clear, despite the minor omission in the discrete analysis.
