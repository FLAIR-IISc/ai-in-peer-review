

The paper extends prior work connecting accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) to continuous-time ODEs by introducing a more general averaging scheme and proposing a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one of its discretizations. In certain cases, the proposed AMD variant with adaptive averaging demonstrates improved performance over existing AMD algorithms employing restarting. The adaptive averaging strategy is well-motivated and presents an interesting new approach. However, the paper's originality and practical utility remain significant concerns.  

- Most technical components (e.g., Lyapunov functions, discretization methods) are directly derived from an earlier NIPS paper [7]. The extension from simple to generalized averaging is trivial and lacks substantial utility, as the authors ultimately restrict analysis to the quadratic rate case; faster rates would necessitate higher-order methods, which are not universally applicable.  
- While results such as the energy function interpretation, primal form reformulation, and reduction to replicator dynamics enhance understanding of accelerated Mirror Descent, they do not substantially advance machine learning.  
- Although the adaptive averaging is designed to satisfy ODE convergence conditions, it lacks full theoretical grounding due to discretization approximations. Crucially, no explicit convergence guarantee is provided, rendering it a heuristic—consistent with the authors' own characterization. Additionally, without comparison to alternative averaging schemes for Nesterov's accelerated algorithms, it remains unclear whether this adaptive variant offers meaningful improvements.  
- The experimental validation on a toy example with only quadratic/linear objectives is unconvincing and fails to demonstrate advantages for the general strongly convex case.  

== Post-rebuttal update ==  
After reviewing the authors' rebuttal, I note their commitment to including: (1) additional higher-dimensional and non-quadratic examples; (2) a theoretical convergence proof for adaptive averaging; (3) implementation on Nesterov's cubic-regularized Newton method; and (4) a comparative discussion with other adaptive averaging schemes. I have updated my evaluation accordingly.
