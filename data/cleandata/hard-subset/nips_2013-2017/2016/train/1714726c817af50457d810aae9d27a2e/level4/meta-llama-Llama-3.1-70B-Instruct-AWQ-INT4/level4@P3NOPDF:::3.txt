The paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization in both continuous and discrete settings. The authors continue the study initiated by Krichene et al. in [7], where Nesterov's method is shown to be an appropriate discretization of an ODE system, coupling a primal trajectory with a dual one. In particular, the primal trajectory is obtained by performing a specific averaging of the mirror of the dual variable. This paper generalizes this concept by studying more general averaging schemes. Theorem 2 derives convergence rates under suitable conditions on the averaging coefficients, demonstrating faster convergence rates of order 1/t^2 in the continuous setting. An adaptive choice of weights with convergence guarantees is also explored and utilized in the experimental section. This adaptive choice is compared with restarting techniques, which are known to improve the convergence of accelerated methods in practice. Notably, the theoretical analysis of the discrete version of the algorithm is not included in the paper.

Overall, I find this to be an interesting and well-written paper. The analysis of the new averaging scheme for accelerated methods is presented in the continuous setting and is rigorous. Although the main ideas of the analysis of the continuous dynamics are now well-established in a series of papers, there are new contributions and results. In this respect, I believe that the work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors should be mentioned. The main theorem, which shows that there are averaging schemes with faster convergence rates than 1/t^2, is a relevant one. However, it would be interesting to understand if this improved rate is preserved when passing to the discrete procedure.

One potential area of improvement is the inclusion of a more detailed discussion on the implications of the results in the discrete setting. While the paper provides a comprehensive analysis in the continuous setting, the lack of a theoretical analysis in the discrete setting may limit the applicability of the results. Nevertheless, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of accelerated first-order methods, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
