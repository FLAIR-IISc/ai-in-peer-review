

This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate constrained convex optimization. The proposed method ensures the preservation of the original convergence rate when the objective function lacks strong convexity, representing an advantage over existing restarting heuristics. The authors substantiate this claim both theoretically and experimentally. However, I found the paper difficult to follow due to its technical depth outside my expertise. While I assume the abbreviations and specialized terms are appropriate for the intended audience, I recommend including supplementary background information for broader accessibility. The equations presented between lines 33–34 and 46–47 would benefit from more intuitive explanations to enhance clarity. Additionally, the relationship to adaptive averaging methods in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad [1] or Adam [2], warrants explicit discussion. Regarding practical significance, the experiments were conducted in ℝ³—a clearly toy example. I seek clarification on how the proposed method (and related approaches) scales to higher-dimensional settings. Minor comment: The reference to the "solid line" in Figure 1 is ambiguous; using a dotted line or distinct colors to illustrate the primal variable trajectory would improve readability. [1] Duchi, John, Elad Hazan, and Yoram Singer. "Adaptive subgradient methods for online learning and stochastic optimization." Journal of Machine Learning Research 12.Jul (2011): 2121-2159. [2] Kingma, Diederik, and Jimmy Ba. "Adam: A method for stochastic optimization." International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR). (2014).
