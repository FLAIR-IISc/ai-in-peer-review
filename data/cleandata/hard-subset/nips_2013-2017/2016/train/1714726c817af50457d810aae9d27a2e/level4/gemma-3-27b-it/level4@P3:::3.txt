This paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization, building upon the connection between Nesterov’s method and continuous-time ODEs established in prior work, notably by Krichene et al. [7]. The authors generalize the averaging scheme used to obtain the primal trajectory from the dual variable, analyzing the resulting dynamics and providing convergence rate guarantees. Specifically, Theorem 2 offers sufficient conditions on the averaging coefficients to achieve convergence. The paper introduces an adaptive weighting scheme with associated convergence guarantees and compares its performance to existing restarting techniques. While the analysis focuses on the continuous setting, experimental results demonstrate competitive performance.

This is a well-written and interesting paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of accelerated methods. The analysis of generalized averaging schemes in the continuous setting is rigorous and extends the existing body of work in this area. While the fundamental approach of analyzing continuous dynamics to inform discrete algorithms is now well-established – evidenced by a growing body of literature – this paper presents novel contributions and results. The key theorem demonstrating the existence of averaging schemes potentially achieving convergence rates faster than 1/t² is a significant finding. It would be insightful to explore whether this improved rate is maintained upon discretization, a direction for future work. 

**Strengths:**

* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the proposed averaging schemes, grounded in Lyapunov arguments and building upon established techniques in optimization and dynamical systems.
* **Novel Contribution:** The exploration of generalized averaging schemes and the demonstration of potentially faster convergence rates represent a genuine contribution to the field.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the complex concepts accessible to readers familiar with optimization theory.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments provide evidence supporting the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the adaptive averaging heuristic.
* **Connection to Related Work:** The paper appropriately contextualizes its contributions within the broader landscape of accelerated methods and continuous-time optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Discrete Analysis:**  The paper primarily focuses on the continuous-time analysis. A corresponding theoretical analysis of the discrete algorithm, including convergence guarantees, would strengthen the contribution. While the authors acknowledge this is a direction for future work, it remains a limitation of the current manuscript.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to specific problem settings (simplex-constrained problems) and might not generalize to all convex optimization problems. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of problems would enhance the credibility of the results.
* **Missing Related Work:** The work of Attouch, Peypouquet, and their co-authors on fast convergence of inertial dynamics and related topics [e.g., Attouch et al., 2015, 2016] is not adequately referenced, especially given its relevance to the analysis of continuous-time optimization methods.  These works present complementary perspectives on acceleration and inertial dynamics that would be valuable to situate this paper within.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and rigorous analysis of generalized averaging schemes for accelerated methods.
* The theoretical results are significant, demonstrating the potential for faster convergence rates.
* The experimental results support the theoretical findings and highlight the practical benefits of the proposed heuristic.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to researchers in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a corresponding discrete analysis limits the practical impact of the theoretical results.
* The scope of the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited.
* The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work, particularly regarding recent advances in inertial dynamics.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field. The theoretical insights and experimental results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors address the weaknesses identified above in future work, specifically including a theoretical analysis of the discrete algorithm and a more comprehensive discussion of related literature.
