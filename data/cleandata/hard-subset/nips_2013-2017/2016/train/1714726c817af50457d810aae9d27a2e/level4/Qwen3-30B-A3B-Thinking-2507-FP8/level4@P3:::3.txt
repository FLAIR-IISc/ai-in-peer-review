

This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization through a continuous-time dynamics perspective. The authors extend their prior work [11] on accelerated mirror descent by introducing a generalized averaging scheme for continuous-time dynamics, providing rigorous convergence guarantees, and proposing an adaptive averaging heuristic with theoretical and empirical support. The paper builds on a growing body of work connecting continuous-time ODEs to accelerated optimization methods, particularly the foundational connection established by Su et al. [20] between Nesterov's method and second-order ODEs.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it generalizes the accelerated mirror descent dynamics by introducing two weight functions η(t) and w(t) that control the dual trajectory accumulation and primal averaging, respectively. The authors establish a unified Lyapunov analysis framework to derive sufficient conditions for achieving desired convergence rates, including the quadratic rate 1/t². Theorem 2 provides precise conditions under which the Lyapunov function decreases, guaranteeing convergence, and Corollary 1 demonstrates how to construct weight functions satisfying these conditions. The paper's analysis of the replicator dynamics on the simplex (Example 2) provides a concrete application showing how the general framework can accelerate established methods in evolutionary game theory.

A key innovation is the adaptive averaging heuristic (Section 4), which adaptively computes the weights to maintain the Lyapunov function's decreasing property. The authors prove that this heuristic preserves the convergence rate while improving empirical performance, as demonstrated through comprehensive numerical experiments (Section 5) comparing adaptive averaging with restarting techniques. The experiments show adaptive averaging consistently outperforms or matches restarting heuristics across various objective functions, with particularly significant improvements for strongly convex problems.

The paper excels in technical quality and theoretical rigor. The Lyapunov analysis is thorough and well-justified, with careful attention to the conditions required for convergence. The connection to continuous-time dynamics provides valuable theoretical insights that can guide discrete algorithm design. The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The experimental section is appropriately detailed, including visualizations of solution trajectories and energy function behavior.

The paper demonstrates strong originality by extending previous continuous-time analyses of accelerated methods to a broader family of dynamics with generalized averaging. The adaptive averaging heuristic represents a novel contribution that addresses a practical challenge in accelerated optimization. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior NIPS contributions, particularly [11, 20], and acknowledge related work on restarting heuristics [17, 20] while clearly differentiating their approach.

The significance of the work is substantial. The continuous-time perspective provides a unified framework for understanding and designing accelerated methods, with potential applications across machine learning and optimization. The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a practical improvement to existing accelerated methods without sacrificing convergence guarantees, addressing a well-known practical challenge. The results have the potential to influence both theoretical and applied research in optimization.

The paper has two main limitations. First, while the continuous-time analysis is rigorous, the discrete-time counterpart is only briefly mentioned in Algorithm 1 without a formal convergence analysis. This omission is notable given the primary interest in discrete optimization algorithms. Second, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the adaptive averaging heuristic compared to restarting methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

Strengths:
- Rigorous continuous-time analysis with novel convergence guarantees
- Clear connection between theory and practical heuristic
- Comprehensive numerical experiments demonstrating clear improvements
- Well-written with appropriate technical depth
- Strong theoretical foundation building on prior NIPS work

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of discrete-time convergence guarantees
- Insufficient analysis of computational complexity of adaptive averaging
- Some related work on continuous-time acceleration (e.g., Attouch, Peypouquet [3, 4]) could be more thoroughly compared

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for those interested in the theoretical foundations of accelerated methods. The continuous-time perspective provides important insights that can guide the design of better discrete algorithms. The adaptive averaging heuristic addresses a practical challenge in accelerated optimization while maintaining theoretical guarantees, which is particularly relevant for practitioners. The work is well-positioned to influence future research in both continuous and discrete optimization.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's theoretical contributions are significant, its experimental validation is thorough, and it makes a clear step forward in understanding accelerated optimization through continuous-time dynamics. While the discrete-time analysis could be strengthened, the continuous-time results are sufficient to establish the paper's scientific contribution. The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on high-quality theoretical and applied machine learning research.
