

This paper studies a generalization of accelerated mirror descent dynamics using a flexible averaging scheme, with theoretical guarantees on convergence rates via Lyapunov analysis and an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves these rates while empirically improving convergence. The work builds upon recent connections between Nesterov's accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs (Su et al., 2014; Krichene et al., 2015), extending their framework to encompass a broader family of dynamics parameterized by dual learning rate η(t) and averaging weights w(t).

The authors present a unified Lyapunov analysis for Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics, establishing sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate r(t). The key theoretical contribution is Theorem 2, which provides conditions on η and w to ensure Lr is a Lyapunov function, guaranteeing f(X(t)) - f* ≤ C/r(t). The authors then propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that dynamically adjusts weights to minimize the time derivative of the Lyapunov function, preserving the convergence rate while empirically accelerating convergence. This approach contrasts with existing restart heuristics (e.g., gradient restart in [17] and speed restart in [20]), which lack theoretical guarantees for non-strongly convex problems.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it generalizes the connection between acceleration and averaging observed in previous work (e.g., [8]) to a broader family of dynamics. Second, it provides the first adaptive method that preserves the theoretical convergence rate while improving empirical performance. Third, it demonstrates how the replicator dynamics (a mirror descent variant on the simplex) can be accelerated through this framework, offering a concrete application in evolutionary game theory.

The theoretical analysis is sound and well-executed. The Lyapunov argument is carefully constructed, with clear conditions for convergence rates. The paper properly references related work, including Krichene et al. (2015) on accelerated mirror descent, and correctly situates its contributions relative to existing literature. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated, and the authors provide a discrete-time version with convergence guarantees in the supplementary material. The numerical experiments are comprehensive, comparing adaptive averaging against multiple restart heuristics across various objective functions (strongly convex quadratic, weakly convex quadratic, linear, and KL divergence) on the simplex. Results consistently show adaptive averaging outperforms or matches existing methods, with significant improvements in strongly convex cases.

The paper excels in clarity. The mathematical formulation is consistent throughout, with well-motivated notation and careful definitions. The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is maintained across sections, and the supplementary material provides additional context for the primal representation. The figures (e.g., Figure 2) effectively illustrate the comparative performance of different methods.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a clear contribution by generalizing the averaging framework beyond previous work. While the connection between acceleration and averaging has been observed before (e.g., [8]), this paper provides a rigorous framework for understanding how different averaging schemes affect convergence rates, and introduces the adaptive averaging heuristic as a novel method for improving performance while preserving theoretical guarantees. The work is sufficiently distinct from recent NIPS papers on accelerated methods, particularly those focusing on discrete-time algorithms rather than continuous-time dynamics.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a theoretically grounded method for accelerating first-order optimization that could be applied broadly to many optimization problems. The adaptive averaging heuristic offers a practical alternative to restart heuristics with stronger theoretical guarantees, and the numerical results suggest it could be valuable in applications where convergence speed is critical. The connection to replicator dynamics also opens new avenues for applying optimization techniques to evolutionary game theory.

Strengths:
1. Clear theoretical contribution with rigorous Lyapunov analysis
2. Novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves convergence rates
3. Comprehensive numerical experiments demonstrating empirical advantages
4. Strong connection to existing literature on accelerated methods
5. Well-written and well-organized presentation

Weaknesses:
1. The adaptive averaging heuristic is primarily validated on simplex-constrained problems; extension to other constraint sets could be further explored
2. The discrete-time analysis in the supplementary material could be more directly connected to the main continuous-time results
3. The discussion of related work could more thoroughly contrast with recent methods like adaptive restarts in [23]

Pro and con arguments for acceptance:
Pro: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding accelerated optimization through continuous-time dynamics, introduces a novel adaptive heuristic with strong empirical performance, and provides comprehensive theoretical and experimental validation. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and advances the field of optimization.

Con: The scope is somewhat limited to constrained convex optimization on the simplex; while the framework is general, the experimental validation could be expanded to more diverse problem classes.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances our understanding of accelerated optimization through continuous-time dynamics. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with a novel solution that provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical benefits. It makes a clear contribution to NIPS' focus on optimization methods in machine learning, and I recommend acceptance.
