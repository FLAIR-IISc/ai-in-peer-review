

This paper introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic designed to accelerate constrained convex optimization. The key contribution is a method that preserves the original convergence rate when the objective function lacks strong convexity—a significant advantage over existing restarting heuristics, as demonstrated both theoretically and empirically.  

While the technical contribution appears sound, the paper’s readability poses challenges for readers outside the immediate domain. The authors assume familiarity with specialized terminology and abbreviations, which may hinder accessibility. To improve clarity, providing a brief background section on foundational concepts would help contextualize the approach for broader audiences. Additionally, the equations between lines 33–34 and 46–47 would benefit from more intuitive explanations, as their derivation and significance are not immediately apparent.  

The authors should also clarify the relationship between their method and adaptive averaging techniques in non-convex optimization, such as Adagrad [1] or Adam [2]. While these methods operate in different settings, discussing potential parallels or distinctions would strengthen the paper’s positioning within the wider literature.  

Regarding practical relevance, the experimental validation in three-dimensional space (R³) appears limited. This setup constitutes a toy example, raising questions about scalability to high-dimensional problems common in modern machine learning. The authors should address how their method (and comparable approaches) performs in higher dimensions, including computational complexity and empirical results on larger-scale datasets. Without this, the work’s applicability to real-world scenarios remains unclear.  

Finally, Figure 1’s reference to a “solid line” is ambiguous. To enhance interpretability, consider using a dashed line, distinct color-coding, or explicit labeling for the primal variable trajectory.  

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically grounded heuristic with a promising advantage over existing methods. However, clarifying the exposition, expanding the experimental scope, and contextualizing the work relative to adaptive optimization literature would substantially strengthen its contribution.
