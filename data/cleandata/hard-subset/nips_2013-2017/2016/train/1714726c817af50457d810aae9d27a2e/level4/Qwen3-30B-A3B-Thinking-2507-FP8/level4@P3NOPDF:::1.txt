

This paper presents a compelling extension of Krichene et al.'s ODE-based interpretation of Nesterov's acceleration (NIPS 2015) to develop an adaptive reweighting heuristic for Nesterov's method. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that this simple adaptive averaging strategy provably outperforms standard fixed-schedule averaging while maintaining the theoretical guarantees of the underlying accelerated method. The authors establish that the adaptive reweighting, though conceptually straightforward, yields significant practical improvements across a broad class of convex optimization problems, as evidenced by their empirical results.

The technical argument is remarkably clean and accessible, building directly on the established framework of Krichene et al. This accessibility is a major strength, as it allows the contribution to be understood without requiring deep familiarity with the nuances of continuous-time optimization. The empirical evaluation convincingly shows convergence speedups on benchmark problems (e.g., logistic regression, Lasso), with improvements that are both statistically significant and practically meaningful. Crucially, the paper addresses a pervasive issue in optimization literature: the common practice of comparing new adaptive methods against Nesterov's acceleration with a *fixed*, suboptimal weight sequence. As the authors convincingly demonstrate, this comparison is often misleading, as the fixed-schedule Nesterov method itself is not the optimal baseline for adaptive settings. Their adaptive heuristic requires minimal modification to standard Nesterov acceleration yet delivers substantial gains, highlighting an important practical insight often overlooked.

The potential impact of this work is substantial. Nesterov's acceleration is ubiquitous in machine learning and optimization applications, yet its standard implementation frequently uses a fixed weight schedule that is not tailored to the problem at hand. This paper provides a principled, low-cost solution to this widespread limitation. By showing that adaptive averaging is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, the authors offer a practical tool that could immediately improve the performance of numerous existing algorithms without requiring major re-engineering. The simplicity of the approach makes it highly accessible for adoption in diverse settings.

While the technical development is a close extension of Krichene et al., its significance stems from resolving a critical practical gap. The paper successfully bridges the theoretical understanding of accelerated methods with a simple, deployable heuristic that directly addresses a common weakness in real-world applications. The clarity of the exposition and the strength of the empirical validation make the contribution highly valuable.

To further strengthen the paper, two minor technical points merit attention. First, a rigorous analysis of the *discretized* adaptive algorithm (i.e., the actual implementation used in experiments) would be beneficial. The continuous-time ODE analysis is elegant, but demonstrating that the convergence rate is preserved under discretization (e.g., via standard Euler or implicit schemes) would solidify the theoretical foundation for practical use. Given the close relationship to the continuous case, this analysis should be relatively straightforward and would significantly enhance the paper's technical completeness.

Second, the authors might explore a potential connection between their adaptive averaging scheme and the Conjugate Gradient (CG) method for strongly convex quadratic problems. For quadratic objectives, CG is optimal and achieves convergence in finite steps, while Nesterov acceleration is typically suboptimal. The adaptive reweighting strategy could offer insights into how to dynamically adjust Nesterov's weights to better align with CG's behavior, potentially leading to a unified perspective or even faster convergence for quadratic problems. A brief discussion of this connection, even if preliminary, would add depth and broaden the paper's relevance.

In summary, this is a strong, well-motivated paper that makes a clear, impactful contribution to the optimization literature. The adaptive reweighting heuristic is simple, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated, addressing a genuine practical limitation in the widespread use of Nesterov's acceleration. The writing is clear, the arguments are sound, and the potential impact is significant. The minor suggested additions would further elevate the work but are not essential for acceptance. This paper deserves publication in NIPS.
