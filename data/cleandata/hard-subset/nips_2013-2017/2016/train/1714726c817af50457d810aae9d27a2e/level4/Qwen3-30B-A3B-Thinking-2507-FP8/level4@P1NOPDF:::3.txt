

This paper investigates accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization in both continuous and discrete settings. It extends the work of Krichene et al. [7], which demonstrated that Nesterov's method corresponds to a specific discretization of an ODE system coupling primal and dual trajectories, where the primal trajectory arises via a particular averaging of the mirror map applied to the dual variable. The authors generalize this approach by studying broader averaging schemes. Theorem 2 establishes convergence rates under suitable conditions on the averaging coefficients. In the continuous setting, rates faster than \(1/t^2\) are derived, and an adaptive weight selection with theoretical guarantees is proposed and employed in experiments. This adaptive choice is compared with restarting techniques, which empirically enhance accelerated methods' convergence. However, the theoretical analysis of the discrete algorithm version is omitted.  

This is an interesting and well-written paper. The continuous-time analysis of the new averaging scheme is rigorous and presents meaningful contributions. While the core analytical framework for continuous dynamics is now well-established through recent work, the authors provide novel results, particularly in Theorem 2, which demonstrates the existence of averaging schemes achieving rates beyond \(1/t^2\). This finding is significant. The paper would benefit from acknowledging related work by Attouch, Peypouquet, and coauthors, which has explored similar continuous-time perspectives on accelerated methods.  

A key unresolved question remains: whether the improved convergence rate in the continuous setting persists when the method is discretized. The absence of discrete analysis prevents assessing this critical link between theory and practice. The experimental section, while supporting the adaptive weight choice, does not address this gap. The comparison with restarting techniques is relevant but lacks theoretical justification for why the adaptive weights might outperform restarting in the discrete regime.  

Overall, the paper advances understanding of continuous-time interpretations of acceleration and offers a compelling new averaging framework. The continuous analysis is sound, and the main result is relevant. However, the omission of discrete convergence guarantees limits the work's applicability to practical implementations, which typically operate in discrete time. Including even a high-level discussion of the discrete case’s challenges or a conjecture about rate preservation would strengthen the contribution. The suggestion to cite Attouch et al. is essential for proper contextualization within the existing literature on continuous-time acceleration. Despite these gaps, the paper presents a valuable theoretical insight into the structure of accelerated methods.
