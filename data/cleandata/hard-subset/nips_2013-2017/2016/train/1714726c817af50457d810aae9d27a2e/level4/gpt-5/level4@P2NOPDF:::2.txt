This paper builds on prior work linking accelerated mirror descent (AMD) to continuous-time ODEs. It extends the ODE dynamics with a more general averaging scheme and proposes a novel adaptive averaging strategy for one discretization. In some settings, the adaptive-averaging variant of AMD outperforms existing AMD methods with restarting. The adaptive averaging mechanism is genuinely interesting and well motivated. Overall, the paper is clearly written and technically competent. However, I have significant concerns about originality, practical impact, and theoretical grounding.

- Originality and scope: Many core elements (e.g., the Lyapunov/energy function, discretization approach) closely follow the earlier NIPS paper [7]. The move from simple to generalized averaging seems rather incremental and of limited utility, especially since the analysis and experiments ultimately focus on the O(1/t^2) “quadratic” rate; achieving faster rates would typically require higher-order methods, which are not always applicable or practical.

- Conceptual contributions: The interpretations offered—energy function perspective, primal reformulation, and reduction to replicator dynamics—do clarify AMD and may aid intuition. Still, they do not appear to deliver substantial advances for machine learning practice or broaden the algorithm’s applicability in a meaningful way.

- Theoretical guarantees: Although adaptive averaging is designed to satisfy conditions that ensure ODE convergence, the discretization introduces approximations that weaken these guarantees. The paper provides no formal convergence results for the discrete algorithm, and the method remains a heuristic by the authors’ own admission. Moreover, there exist several alternative averaging schemes in Nesterov-style accelerated methods; without empirical or theoretical comparisons to these variants, it is hard to judge the benefit of the proposed approach.

- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are limited to a toy example with quadratic/linear objectives. This does not convincingly demonstrate the advantage of adaptive averaging, particularly for strongly convex problems or more complex, high-dimensional, and non-quadratic settings.

Post-rebuttal: I appreciate the authors’ commitments to (i) add higher-dimensional and non-quadratic experiments, (ii) provide a theoretical convergence proof for adaptive averaging, (iii) implement adaptive averaging within Nesterov’s cubic-regularized Newton method, and (iv) include a discussion and comparisons with other adaptive averaging schemes. These planned improvements address my main concerns, and I have updated my scores accordingly.
