- Paper proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic to accelerate first-order constrained convex optimization with a guarantee that it does not degrade convergence in non–strongly convex settings.  
- Provides theoretical analysis and limited empirical evidence showing acceleration over standard baselines.  
- Strength: preserves baseline convergence rate in non–strongly convex problems, improving over heuristic restarts that may harm worst-case rates.  
- Strength: heuristic is conceptually simple and easy to implement across first-order constrained methods.  
- Strength: experiments in low dimension indicate empirical acceleration versus baselines.  
- Concern: presentation is hard to follow for non-specialists; many terms and abbreviations are undefined.  
- Concern: key equations (lines 33–34 and 46–47) lack intuitive/geometric explanations or short derivations.  
- Suggestion: include a concise algorithm box and a short paragraph on per-iteration computational overhead.  
- Concern: related work section underdeveloped regarding comparison to popular adaptive restart schemes (function/gradient-based restarts).  
- Request: clarify when the method strictly dominates, matches, or is inferior to existing restart schemes both theoretically and empirically.  
- Concern: readers will ask about connections to AdaGrad/Adam; clarify conceptual differences and applicability to stochastic/non-convex settings.  
- Request: state whether averaging rule can be combined with AdaGrad/Adam-like stepsizes and what guarantees, if any, extend.  
- Concern: experiments only in R^3, limiting conclusions about scalability and practical utility.  
- Request: evaluate in higher dimensions (10^2–10^5) on standard constrained convex tasks (Lasso, constrained logistic, portfolio, TV-regularized problems).  
- Suggestion: report iteration counts, projection counts, and wall-clock time; quantify runtime impact since projection cost matters.  
- Request: analyze scaling with dimension, condition number, and constraint geometry; consider projection-free adaptations.  
- Suggestion: provide sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters and robustness to noise or inexact gradients.  
- Technical: explicitly state assumptions on objective smoothness/Lipschitzness, feasible set properties, and any Slater-type requirements.  
- Technical: clarify behavior under strong convexity—does the heuristic preserve/improve linear rates or can it hinder them?  
- Technical: quantify extra per-iteration cost, memory requirements, and any stopping/switching conditions.  
- Technical: clarify generality—applicability to primal vs. primal–dual schemes and compatible step-size policies.  
- Exposition: add a background/intuitive section on averaging in accelerated methods and why naive restarts can hurt rates.  
- Exposition: add a high-level proof sketch and move technical lemmas to an appendix for readability.  
- Empirical recommendations: include diverse high-dimensional experiments, comparisons to baseline, restart heuristics, and ablations; provide plots error vs iterations/time and reproducibility details.  
- Minor: clarify Figure 1 line styles and legend, define all abbreviations on first use, and improve proof readability with a roadmap.  
- Overall: promising heuristic with a valuable worst-case guarantee, but needs clearer exposition, stronger positioning versus related adaptive methods, and substantive high-dimensional experiments to establish practical impact.
