This paper studies continuous-time accelerated descent for constrained convex optimization and introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic.

Builds on connections between accelerated methods and ODEs, extending Krichene et al. (2016) accelerated mirror descent analysis.

Proposes a generalized averaging framework AMD_{w,η} parameterized by weight functions w(t) and η(t).

Provides sufficient conditions for desired convergence rates via a Lyapunov argument (Theorem 2).

Introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses weights to minimize the time derivative of the Lyapunov function.

Shows replicator dynamics on the simplex can be accelerated through simple averaging, linking to evolutionary game theory.

Presents a discrete-time implementation (Algorithm 1) grounded in the continuous-time analysis.

Theoretical contributions: unified framework for accelerated mirror descent with clear convergence conditions and rigorous Lyapunov analysis.

Empirical contributions: the adaptive averaging heuristic preserves convergence rates and empirically outperforms restart heuristics, especially for strongly convex problems.

Numerical experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, with positive-orthant and simplex examples illustrating applicability.

Supplementary material strengthens theoretical justification and experimental details.

Main weakness: limited discussion of computational complexity and per-iteration cost of the adaptive weight computation.

Experiments could be improved by reporting computational-efficiency metrics (iterations, function evaluations) alongside convergence rates.

Minor concern: insufficient comparison/discussion relative to adaptive first-order methods like Adagrad and Adam.

Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and uses consistent notation; discrete-continuous connections are properly established.

Originality and significance: meaningful extension of continuous-time accelerated methods and a novel adaptive heuristic with broad applicability.

Overall assessment: technically sound, clear, original, and significant; meets NIPS quality criteria with convincing experiments and theoretical interest.
