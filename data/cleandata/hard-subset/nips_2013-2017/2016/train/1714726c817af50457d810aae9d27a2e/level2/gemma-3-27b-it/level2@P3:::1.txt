This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, framing the dynamics as a coupling of dual and primal variables. The core contribution lies in providing sufficient conditions for convergence, demonstrating acceleration of replicator dynamics via averaging, and proposing an adaptive averaging heuristic with convergence guarantees. The work builds upon prior research connecting Nesterov’s acceleration to continuous-time ODEs, specifically generalizing earlier findings [11, 20].

The strengths of the paper are significant. The Lyapunov argument provides a rigorous framework for analyzing accelerated descent, and the extension to general averaging schemes is a valuable contribution. Demonstrating acceleration of the replicator dynamics is a concrete example highlighting the applicability of the proposed framework. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated, and the proof of its convergence rate preservation is a substantial result. The numerical experiments provide supporting evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing performance comparable to, and sometimes exceeding, adaptive restarting. The connection drawn to related work, including evolutionary game theory and existing acceleration heuristics, contextualizes the findings effectively. The paper is well-structured, building logically from theoretical foundations to practical applications.

However, there are areas where the paper could be improved. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the presentation could benefit from increased clarity. The jump between continuous-time ODEs and the discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) requires more detailed explanation. Specifically, the choice of the step size ‘s’ and how it relates to the continuous-time parameters isn’t thoroughly discussed. The connection between the discretization and the preservation of the convergence rate is stated but not fully illuminated, leaving the reader to refer to the cited supplementary material. While the numerical experiments are informative, they are somewhat limited in scope. More comprehensive experimentation, including varying problem dimensions and different objective function landscapes, would strengthen the empirical validation. The discussion of related work, while present, could be more exhaustive. Mentioning recent advances in adaptive methods beyond Adagrad and Adam, particularly those specifically designed for accelerated methods, would enhance the context. The notation can be dense; defining all variables upon first use, even if seemingly obvious to experts, would improve readability.

Arguments *for* acceptance: The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for analyzing and accelerating first-order optimization methods. The convergence guarantees for the adaptive averaging heuristic are a significant contribution. The numerical results support the theoretical findings and demonstrate practical potential.

Arguments *against* acceptance: The presentation lacks some clarity, particularly in the transition from continuous-time to discrete-time. The numerical experiments are limited in scope. The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a solid theoretical foundation and promising experimental results. Addressing the clarity and scope issues outlined above would further enhance its impact. It advances the state of the art in accelerated optimization and is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in the field. The paper's focus on a fundamental aspect of optimization – averaging – and its rigorous analysis make it a valuable contribution.
