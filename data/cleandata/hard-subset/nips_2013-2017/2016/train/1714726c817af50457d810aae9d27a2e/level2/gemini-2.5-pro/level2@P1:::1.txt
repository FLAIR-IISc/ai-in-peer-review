This paper studies accelerated first-order methods for constrained convex optimization from the perspective of continuous-time dynamics. Building on prior work that connects Nesterov's accelerated method to a second-order ODE with a specific averaging scheme [11, 20], this work introduces a generalized family of Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics. These dynamics are parameterized by two time-varying functions: `η(t)`, which controls the rate of gradient accumulation for a dual variable, and `w(t)`, which defines the weights for averaging the mirrored dual trajectory to produce the primal variable.

The core of the paper's theoretical contribution is a unified analysis of this family of ODEs using a Lyapunov function. The authors derive simple and elegant sufficient conditions on the functions `w(t)` and `η(t)` that guarantee a convergence rate of O(1/r(t)) for a given rate function `r(t)`. This framework is shown to recover previous results as a special case. As a compelling example, the paper demonstrates how these dynamics can be used to derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics, a well-known ODE in evolutionary game theory, for optimization over the probability simplex.

Based on this theoretical framework, the paper proposes a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The key idea is to adaptively choose the averaging weights `w(t)` to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function along the solution trajectory. A major advantage of this approach is that, by construction, it is guaranteed to preserve the O(1/r(t)) convergence rate. The authors present a discrete-time version of this heuristic (Algorithm 1) and provide strong empirical evidence, through a series of numerical experiments, that it performs competitively with, and in some cases significantly better than, popular restarting heuristics for accelerated methods.

**Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis in Section 2 is sound, and the Lyapunov-based argument provides a clear and insightful way to understand the conditions required for acceleration. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical proofs and numerical experiments. The derivation of the primal form of the dynamics and the connection to the replicator equation is a nice contribution that adds depth to the work. The paper presents a complete piece of research, moving from a general theoretical framework to a concrete algorithm and its empirical validation.

**Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The progression from the general continuous-time dynamics to the specific adaptive heuristic and its discrete implementation is logical and easy to follow. The notation is clear and consistent. The authors do a good job of providing intuition for the dynamics, for example by drawing an analogy between the terms in their proposed energy function and potential/kinetic energy. The figures in the experimental section are particularly effective, as they visualize not only the objective function value but also the solution trajectories and the behavior of the Lyapunov and energy functions, providing valuable insight into how the different heuristics work.

**Originality**
The paper's contributions are original and significant. While the study of optimization through ODEs has gained traction, the generalization of the averaging scheme in accelerated mirror descent is a novel extension. The main contribution, the adaptive averaging heuristic, is a genuinely new idea for improving the practical performance of accelerated methods. It provides a principled alternative to the widely used restarting schemes. Unlike restarting, which completely resets the algorithm's "momentum," adaptive averaging modulates the influence of the trajectory's history, which is a more subtle and potentially more powerful mechanism. The demonstration that the replicator dynamics can be accelerated is also a novel and interesting finding.

**Significance**
The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners. The unified theoretical framework deepens our understanding of the role of averaging in acceleration. The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a practical contribution that is simple to implement and, based on the experiments, appears to be a very effective alternative to existing heuristics. The fact that it comes with a guarantee of preserving the theoretical convergence rate—a guarantee that is often missing for restarting schemes on general convex problems—is a major strength. This work could inspire further research into other adaptive schemes for accelerated methods that are grounded in a continuous-time analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and elegant generalization of the continuous-time dynamics for accelerated mirror descent.
*   The Lyapunov-based analysis offers a unified and insightful perspective on the conditions for acceleration.
*   Introduces "adaptive averaging," a new and well-motivated heuristic for improving the practical performance of accelerated methods.
*   The proposed heuristic is backed by a theoretical guarantee that it preserves the original convergence rate, a key advantage over many existing heuristics.
*   The numerical experiments are well-designed and demonstrate that the proposed method is highly competitive with state-of-the-art restarting schemes.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation is performed on low-dimensional problems. While effective for visualization and demonstrating the core idea, showcasing the method's performance on higher-dimensional machine learning tasks would strengthen the paper's practical claims.
*   The connection between the continuous-time condition for adapting the weights (Eq. 8) and the specific rule used in the discrete algorithm could be justified more formally.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes strong contributions on both theoretical and practical fronts. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to potential extensions than to flaws in the current work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
