Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a general continuous-time formulation that couples a dual variable Z accumulating gradients at a rate η(t) and a primal variable X formed as a weighted average of the mirrored dual trajectory with weights w(t). A Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*) is introduced to derive sufficient conditions on η and w that guarantee a target convergence rate 1/r(t). This generalizes an earlier accelerated mirror descent formulation where the averaging weights are fixed to a specific polynomial schedule (e.g., t^{r−1}) and clarifies the role of averaging in acceleration (cf. Krichene et al., NIPS 2015 [11], and the ODE perspective of Su et al., NIPS 2014 [20]).

The paper provides: (i) general sufficient conditions (Theorem 2) under which Lr is decreasing, implying f(X(t))−f* ≤ const/r(t); (ii) a primal representation and examples, including an accelerated variant of the replicator dynamics on the simplex; (iii) an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses a(t)=w(t)/W(t) to strictly decrease the Lyapunov derivative whenever possible, thereby preserving the 1/r(t) rate; (iv) a discrete-time algorithm (for r(t)=t^2) with a proof sketch in the supplement that the accelerated 1/k^2 rate is preserved; and (v) empirical comparisons against adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient restart [17], speed restart [20]). The adaptive averaging heuristic performs at least as well as restarting, and sometimes substantially better, while maintaining a decreasing Lyapunov function. The work connects to the variational ODE family of Wibisono et al. [23] and to practical heuristics that improve accelerated methods [17,20].

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean Lyapunov-based framework that isolates the roles of η(t), w(t), and the target rate r(t), yielding simple, verifiable sufficient conditions.
- Generalization and unification: recovers known accelerated schedules as special cases (e.g., r(t)=t^p with w(t)∝t^{β−1}, η(t)=β t^{p−1}) and relates to Wibisono et al. via an explicit parameter mapping.
- Novel adaptive heuristic: adaptive averaging that preserves the continuous-time Lyapunov decrease and has a concrete discrete-time instantiation. In contrast to restarting, the rate guarantee is maintained in the non-strongly convex setting.
- Interesting example: an accelerated form of the replicator dynamics on the simplex derived via the mirror map, broadening the toolbox for game-theoretic and entropy-regularized problems.
- Empirical behavior: in all reported cases, adaptive averaging matches or improves on restarting, with reduced oscillations and monotone Lyapunov decrease.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of discrete-time guarantees: the proof that the discrete algorithm preserves the quadratic rate is deferred to the supplement and focused on r(t)=t^2. It would help to state precise assumptions (smoothness constants, strong convexity of R, choice of β and step size s) and the explicit convergence bound and constants in the main text. Extension to composite objectives (proximal setting) is mentioned but not developed here.
- Condition (2) in Theorem 2 is trajectory-dependent. The adaptive rule replaces continuous-time sign checks of ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ with a discrete surrogate (comparing f(x̃^{k+1}) and f(x̃^k)). The correspondence between these criteria should be clarified theoretically (e.g., via a discrete Lyapunov descent lemma) to bridge the gap between the continuous analysis and Algorithm 1’s triggers.
- Experimental scope: experiments are mainly 3D simplex problems for visualization. Conclusions would be stronger with higher-dimensional benchmarks (e.g., logistic regression with simplex constraints, entropy-regularized OT, portfolio/simplex projections), wall-clock comparisons, and sensitivity to β and step size s. Since adaptive methods like Adagrad/Adam [7,10] are mentioned, a brief discussion on how adaptive averaging interacts with stochastic gradients would be valuable, even if deferred as future work.
- Assumptions on ψ and ψ*: the text assumes Lipschitz ∇ψ* and sometimes twice differentiability for the primal representation. The impact of relaxing these assumptions (e.g., non-smooth ψ leading to standard mirror maps) on existence/uniqueness and the Lyapunov analysis could be further discussed.
- Strongly convex case: the abstract notes significant empirical gains there. A theoretical treatment (e.g., linear rates or acceleration beyond 1/k^2 under strong convexity) would substantially increase impact; related continuous-time analyses exist (e.g., Attouch & Peypouquet).

Quality
The analysis is technically sound and clearly motivated. The Lyapunov derivations (Theorem 2) and the corollary construction of admissible weights are correct and useful. Existence/uniqueness (Theorem 1) is standard and well-justified. The energy function (Theorem 3) provides additional insight. The discrete-time analysis is less visible in the main text; elevating key lemmas from the supplement would strengthen the scientific completeness.

Clarity
Overall clear and well organized. Notation is dense (r, w, η, a, W), and multiple equivalent forms (primal/dual) are presented; adding a small summary table of symbols and a running example would help. The link to Wibisono et al. is briefly given; a more explicit mapping would aid readers. Algorithm 1 is readable, though a short paragraph explaining the role of each step (z-update, x̃-update, mixing via λ_k) and the meaning of ak would improve accessibility.

Originality
The general Lyapunov framework for arbitrary weights and the adaptive averaging heuristic appear novel relative to prior accelerated mirror descent and restarting heuristics. The accelerated replicator dynamics example is a nice contribution.

Significance
Moderate. The framework may influence the design of accelerated methods with principled, rate-preserving adaptivity, and the replicator acceleration could be of interest in game theory and entropic modeling. The current empirical scope is limited; broader experiments and stronger discrete-time theory (including composite/strongly convex settings) would raise significance.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Bring key parts of the discrete-time analysis (assumptions, theorem statement, rate with constants) into the main paper for r(t)=t^2; clarify the discrete surrogate for ⟨∇f, Ẋ⟩>0 used in Algorithm 1.
- Expand experiments to higher-dimensional problems and report wall-clock time, sensitivity to β and s, and robustness across mirror maps.

Optional:
- Discuss extensions to composite objectives (prox/proximal mirror maps) and to stochastic gradients.
- Elaborate the mapping to Wibisono et al.’s ODE family and contrast with restarting heuristics in terms of theoretical guarantees.
- Provide guidance on choosing ψ and on verifying ∇ψ* Lipschitz/Twice differentiable assumptions in common settings.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, general Lyapunov-based conditions that unify and extend accelerated mirror descent with averaging.
- A simple, principled adaptive heuristic that preserves the target rate and improves practical behavior compared to popular restarting methods.
- Novel accelerated replicator dynamics and insightful continuous-time/discrete-time connections.

Arguments against acceptance
- Discrete-time guarantees are not prominently presented in the main text and are limited to the quadratic-rate case.
- Experimental evaluation is small-scale and focused on low-dimensional simplex problems, limiting evidence of broader impact.
- Strongly convex and composite settings are not theoretically treated despite promising empirical indications.

Overall recommendation
Promising and well-motivated contribution with solid continuous-time theory and a practical adaptive idea. Strengthening the discrete-time theory presentation and broadening experiments would make the case for acceptance stronger.
