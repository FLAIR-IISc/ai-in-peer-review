**Summary**

This paper investigates continuous-time accelerated mirror descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization. It introduces a general family of dynamics, AMDw,η, parameterized by two functions: η(t), which controls the rate of gradient accumulation for a dual variable, and w(t), which defines the weights for averaging the mirrored dual trajectory to produce the primal variable. The core of the analysis is a Lyapunov function, Lr, which is used to derive sufficient conditions on η and w to achieve a desired convergence rate of 1/r(t). This framework is shown to recover previous work as a special case and provides a novel accelerated version of the replicator dynamics for optimization on the simplex.

The main practical contribution is a new heuristic called "adaptive averaging." This heuristic is derived directly from the Lyapunov analysis and adaptively modifies the averaging weights w(t) to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function. A key advantage of this approach is that it provably preserves the O(1/t^2) convergence rate, a guarantee that is often missing for other heuristics like adaptive restarting. The paper presents a discrete-time version of the algorithm and provides numerical experiments comparing its performance to standard accelerated methods and restarting heuristics on several simplex-constrained problems.

**Strengths**

1.  **Unified Theoretical Framework:** The paper provides an elegant and unifying perspective on accelerated mirror descent dynamics. The formulation in terms of generalized averaging (w) and gradient scaling (η) is general, and the use of a Lyapunov function (Lr) to derive sufficient conditions for convergence (Theorem 2) is a clean and powerful analytical approach.

2.  **Novel and Principled Heuristic:** The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is a significant contribution. Unlike many ad-hoc heuristics, it is directly motivated by the continuous-time theoretical analysis. The goal of greedily decreasing the Lyapunov function is a well-founded principle. Most importantly, it comes with the guarantee of preserving the original O(1/t^2) convergence rate, which is a strong theoretical advantage over competing restarting schemes, particularly for non-strongly convex problems.

3.  **Clear Connection to Known Dynamics:** The work does an excellent job of connecting its general framework to existing concepts. By deriving a primal representation of the dynamics, it demonstrates how its framework relates to the ODE family from Wibisono et al. [23]. The derivation of an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics is a compelling and concrete example of the framework's utility.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments are well-designed and convincing. They not only demonstrate that adaptive averaging is competitive with and sometimes superior to restarting heuristics in terms of objective value, but they also provide insight by plotting the Lyapunov and energy functions. The plots compellingly illustrate the theoretical claim that adaptive averaging maintains a decreasing Lyapunov function while restarting methods do not.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Analysis of the Heuristic's Rate:** The analysis guarantees that adaptive averaging *preserves* the base O(1/t^2) convergence rate. A natural and important question is whether it can be proven to *improve* this rate under stronger assumptions, such as strong convexity. Restarting heuristics are known to achieve linear convergence in such settings. While the conclusion mentions this as future work, a brief discussion in the main text about the potential and challenges of proving such a result would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Clarity of the Discrete Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 introduces a regularizer `R(x̃, x(k))` and a parameter `γs` without defining them in the main text. While experts may infer their form from context, explicitly defining these terms (e.g., stating that `R` is a Bregman divergence) would improve the clarity and self-containedness of the paper. Furthermore, the main result on the convergence rate of the discrete Algorithm 1 is stated to be in the supplementary material. Including a formal statement of this theorem in the main body of the paper would make the claims about the discrete-time version more concrete.

3.  **Comparison with Other Adaptive Methods:** The introduction briefly mentions Adagrad and Adam but dismisses them as "not designed for accelerated methods." While true, a slightly deeper discussion could be beneficial. For example, are there fundamental incompatibilities between adaptive learning rates and Nesterov-style momentum that this work circumvents? Expanding on this point could better position the contribution relative to this very popular class of methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution by unifying a broad class of accelerated mirror descent dynamics under a single, elegant framework.
*   The proposed adaptive averaging heuristic is novel, theoretically principled, and comes with stronger guarantees than popular competing heuristics.
*   The work successfully bridges continuous-time theory and discrete-time algorithms, with strong experimental results validating the proposed method.
*   The paper is very well-written, clear, and makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The theoretical analysis for the adaptive heuristic does not show an improved rate of convergence (e.g., for strongly convex functions), only that the base rate is preserved.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes strong contributions on both theoretical and practical fronts. The development of a principled adaptive heuristic for accelerated methods with rate-preservation guarantees is a significant step forward. The weaknesses identified are minor and can be addressed with small revisions. The work is technically sound, original, clearly presented, and its results are likely to be of high interest to the optimization and machine learning communities at NIPS.
