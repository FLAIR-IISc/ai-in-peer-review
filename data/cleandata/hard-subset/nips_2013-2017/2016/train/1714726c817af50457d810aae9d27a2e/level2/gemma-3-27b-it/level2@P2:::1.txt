This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, framing it as a coupling of dual and primal variables via averaging. The authors provide sufficient conditions for convergence using a Lyapunov argument and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic to speed up convergence, proving guarantees in continuous and discrete time, and supporting these with numerical experiments.

The paper is well-motivated, building on the connection between Nesterov’s accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs, a line of work the authors themselves have contributed to [11, 20]. The formulation using a Lyapunov function is a principled approach, and the connection to mirror descent and the replicator dynamics is interesting. The adaptive averaging heuristic seems reasonable and the guarantees provided – particularly preserving the quadratic convergence rate – are a notable contribution. The experimental validation comparing adaptive averaging to adaptive restarting is also a strong point. The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.

However, there are several weaknesses. While the Lyapunov argument is solid, the conditions for its application (specifically those in Theorem 2) appear quite restrictive and potentially difficult to verify in practice. The discussion around these conditions lacks intuition – why are they sufficient, and what happens when they are not met? The connection to existing adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam [7, 10] feels somewhat superficial; the authors acknowledge they are designed for different problems, but a deeper discussion of the differences and potential synergies would be valuable. 

The numerical experiments, while supportive, are limited in scope. The comparison is primarily on the simplex constraint and doesn’t generalize to other, more complex constraints or objective functions. The discussion of the experimental results is also slightly hand-wavy – "significant improvements in some cases" is vague. A more thorough statistical analysis (e.g., reporting standard deviations, p-values) would be stronger.  Furthermore, the discrete version of the heuristic (Algorithm 1) is not fully explained – how are the parameters β and s chosen?

The paper references [23] in relation to higher-order accelerated methods, but doesn’t clearly articulate how the proposed approach compares or complements these methods. Additionally, while the paper highlights the benefit of preserving the Lyapunov function, a deeper discussion of its limitations in non-strongly convex settings would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Principled Approach:** Using Lyapunov analysis provides a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Convergence Guarantees:** The preservation of the quadratic convergence rate is a significant result.
*   **Connection to Replicator Dynamics:** Demonstrating acceleration of this well-known dynamic is insightful.
*   **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Restrictive Conditions:** The conditions for Lyapunov stability are potentially difficult to satisfy.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The numerical results lack breadth and statistical rigor.
*   **Superficial Comparison to Existing Methods:** The discussion of Adagrad/Adam could be more insightful.
*   **Incomplete Algorithm Explanation:** Algorithm 1 lacks crucial implementation details.
*   **Lack of Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the method’s weaknesses.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to accelerating mirror descent.
*   The convergence guarantees are a valuable contribution.
*   The experimental results, though limited, are encouraging.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The restrictive conditions for the theoretical results may limit practical applicability.
*   The limited scope of the numerical experiments weakens the empirical validation.
*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison to related work and a deeper discussion of its limitations.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is borderline accept. The theoretical contributions are solid, but the practical implications are somewhat limited by the restrictive conditions and the lack of comprehensive experimental validation. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically providing more intuition for the conditions on η and w, expanding the numerical experiments, and clarifying the implementation details of Algorithm 1. A more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly in non-strongly convex scenarios, would also be valuable.
