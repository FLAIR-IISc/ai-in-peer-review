

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, extending the continuous-time perspective on acceleration. The authors introduce a generalized averaging scheme for mirror descent, parameterized by weight functions w and η, and provide a Lyapunov function analysis to establish sufficient conditions for achieving desired convergence rates. They show that the replicator dynamics (mirror descent on the simplex) can be accelerated through a simple averaging scheme, and propose an adaptive averaging heuristic that adaptively computes the weights to speed up the decrease of the Lyapunov function. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the adaptive approach in continuous-time, proves it preserves quadratic convergence rates in discrete-time, and includes numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging with existing heuristics like adaptive restarting.

The paper builds upon the authors' prior work at NIPS 2016 on accelerated mirror descent dynamics, extending the framework to a broader class of averaging schemes. It relates to Su et al. (NIPS 2014) and Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016) by connecting continuous-time ODEs to accelerated methods and providing a unified theoretical framework. The paper makes a clear contribution by generalizing the averaging scheme and providing a principled adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence rates.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with well-constructed theorems and proofs. The Lyapunov function approach is well-suited to the problem, and the conditions for convergence rates are clearly derived. The adaptive averaging heuristic is well-motivated by the continuous-time analysis and provides a theoretically grounded approach that addresses a practical problem in optimization. The numerical experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method with existing heuristics across multiple problem types (strongly convex, weakly convex, linear, KL divergence), and clearly demonstrating advantages in convergence speed. The paper carefully acknowledges limitations, noting that the Lyapunov function may not converge to zero when multiple minimizers exist.

The clarity is generally good. The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and consistent presentation of concepts. The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is well-explained. Some sections (particularly Section 2 on the Lyapunov analysis) might benefit from additional intuitive explanations for readers unfamiliar with mirror descent to make the contributions more accessible. The supplementary material appears to provide additional context that would help readers understand the technical details.

The originality is strong. The generalized averaging scheme extends previous work on accelerated mirror descent, and the adaptive averaging heuristic represents a novel contribution. The connection to replicator dynamics on the simplex is a particularly interesting application that demonstrates the generality of the approach. The paper properly contextualizes its contribution against related work, noting how it differs from existing methods like adaptive restarting and other adaptive first-order methods (Adagrad, Adam). The paper is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS papers (e.g., Krichene et al. 2016) as it provides a broader theoretical framework and a new adaptive heuristic.

The significance is high. The paper provides a unified theoretical framework for accelerated optimization with a generalized averaging scheme, which advances our understanding of acceleration in continuous-time. The adaptive averaging heuristic has clear practical value for speeding up convergence in optimization algorithms, with numerical results showing at least comparable, and often better, performance than existing heuristics. The connection to replicator dynamics opens up new applications in evolutionary game theory. The theoretical guarantees that the adaptive heuristic preserves the convergence rate are particularly valuable, as many existing heuristics lack such guarantees.

The main weaknesses are that the paper could more explicitly connect the continuous-time analysis to discrete-time algorithms beyond the quadratic case, and the numerical experiments might benefit from more diverse problem instances. The paper mentions that the adaptive averaging heuristic could be applied to other accelerated methods beyond the quadratic case, but doesn't provide examples of such applications.

In summary, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the understanding of accelerated optimization methods with a novel adaptive heuristic that preserves convergence rates. The theoretical analysis is sound, the numerical experiments are well-designed, and the significance is high for both theoretical understanding and practical applications. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical foundation with novel adaptive heuristic, clear experimental validation showing advantages over existing methods, significant connection to replicator dynamics, and well-motivated extension of previous NIPS work.

Arguments against acceptance: Some technical sections could be made more accessible to a broader audience, and the paper could provide more examples of applying the adaptive heuristic beyond the specific cases studied.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that advances our understanding of accelerated optimization methods with significant theoretical and practical contributions. I recommend acceptance.
