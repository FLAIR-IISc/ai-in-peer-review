

This paper investigates accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization, focusing on continuous-time formulations and introducing an adaptive averaging heuristic. The work builds on prior connections between accelerated methods and continuous-time ODEs, particularly extending the analysis of accelerated mirror descent dynamics presented in Krichene et al. (NIPS 2016). The authors propose a generalized averaging framework for accelerated mirror descent (AMDw,η) characterized by two weight functions w(t) and η(t), and provide sufficient conditions for achieving a desired convergence rate using a Lyapunov argument. The paper demonstrates that the replicator dynamics on the simplex can be accelerated through a simple averaging scheme, and proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that computes weights to minimize the time derivative of the Lyapunov function.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a unified theoretical framework for accelerated mirror descent dynamics with generalized averaging, extending previous work by establishing clear sufficient conditions for convergence rates. The Lyapunov analysis is rigorous and well-motivated, with Theorem 2 offering a clear characterization of the conditions needed for the dynamics to achieve the specified convergence rate. Second, the paper develops a novel adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the convergence rate while empirically outperforming existing restart heuristics. The discrete-time implementation in Algorithm 1 is well-motivated and properly grounded in the continuous-time analysis. Third, the paper provides a valuable connection between accelerated optimization and replicator dynamics, which has applications in evolutionary game theory and viability theory.

The paper's strengths include its clear mathematical presentation, thorough theoretical analysis, and comprehensive numerical experiments. The examples of positive-orthant-constrained dynamics and simplex-constrained dynamics (replicator equation) effectively illustrate the applicability of the framework to specific optimization problems. The numerical experiments comparing adaptive averaging with existing restart heuristics are well-designed and demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method, particularly for strongly convex problems. The supplementary material provides additional theoretical justification and experimental details that strengthen the paper.

The paper's main weakness is the limited discussion of the computational complexity of the adaptive averaging heuristic. While the discrete-time algorithm is presented, the paper does not address how the adaptive weight computation affects the per-iteration computational cost compared to standard accelerated methods or restart heuristics. The numerical experiments in Section 5 are informative but could be strengthened by including more detailed comparisons of computational efficiency (e.g., number of iterations, function evaluations) alongside convergence rates.

Another minor concern is that the paper does not sufficiently address the relationship between the adaptive averaging heuristic and existing adaptive first-order methods like Adagrad and Adam. While the paper notes these methods are not designed for accelerated methods, a more detailed comparison would help clarify the unique contribution of adaptive averaging in the context of accelerated optimization.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with logical flow from theoretical foundations to practical implementation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, making the paper accessible to the optimization community. The connection between continuous-time dynamics and discrete algorithms is properly established, with the discrete-time version of the adaptive heuristic grounded in the continuous-time analysis.

In terms of originality, the paper extends the recent work on continuous-time accelerated methods (Su et al., NIPS 2014; Krichene et al., NIPS 2016) by introducing a generalized averaging scheme and a novel adaptive heuristic. The specific application to replicator dynamics on the simplex represents a meaningful extension of the framework to an important class of constrained optimization problems with applications in evolutionary game theory.

The significance of the paper is strong, as it advances our understanding of accelerated optimization through continuous-time dynamics while providing a practical heuristic that improves upon existing methods. The theoretical results are general enough to apply to a wide range of constrained convex optimization problems, and the adaptive averaging heuristic has potential for immediate application in optimization algorithms for machine learning and other domains.

The paper meets all four NIPS quality criteria: It is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis, clear in its presentation, original in its adaptive averaging approach, and significant in both theoretical and practical contributions to optimization. The numerical experiments provide convincing evidence of the heuristic's effectiveness, and the connection to replicator dynamics adds theoretical interest. The paper's contribution represents a meaningful advancement in the understanding of accelerated optimization methods, particularly for constrained problems.
