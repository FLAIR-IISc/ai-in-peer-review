

This paper studies accelerated descent dynamics for constrained convex optimization through a generalized averaging scheme, proposing a unified framework for Accelerated Mirror Descent (AMD) dynamics. The authors establish sufficient conditions on weight functions η(t) and w(t) using Lyapunov analysis to achieve desired convergence rates, demonstrate how replicator dynamics can be accelerated on the simplex, and introduce an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves quadratic convergence while improving empirical performance.

The theoretical contribution is strong, with clear Lyapunov arguments (Theorems 2 and 3) establishing sufficient conditions for convergence. The connection to replicator dynamics provides an insightful application in evolutionary game theory. The adaptive averaging heuristic is novel and well-motivated, with the key insight that decreasing the Lyapunov function's derivative accelerates convergence while preserving theoretical guarantees. The numerical experiments in Section 5 provide compelling evidence that adaptive averaging outperforms existing restarting heuristics, particularly for strongly convex problems, and correctly show the Lyapunov function remains decreasing.

However, the paper's discrete-time implementation (Algorithm 1) lacks complete theoretical justification for its convergence properties, despite the continuous-time guarantees. The relationship to other adaptive methods like Adagrad and Adam could be more thoroughly discussed, as they share the spirit of adaptive parameter selection but are not designed for accelerated methods. The authors also could better clarify how the adaptive heuristic differs from the restarting techniques they compare against.

The paper demonstrates significant theoretical novelty by unifying and generalizing acceleration mechanisms, with potential applications in constrained optimization and game theory. The experimental results are well-designed and support the theoretical claims. While the discrete-time analysis could be strengthened, the continuous-time framework provides a solid foundation for future work.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances our understanding of acceleration in optimization. The paper meets NIPS standards for technical soundness, originality, and significance. The theoretical insights are valuable, and the adaptive heuristic shows promise for practical implementation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the discrete-time convergence guarantees more thoroughly.
