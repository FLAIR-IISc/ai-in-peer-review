- Summary: Introduces AMDw,η, a continuous-time accelerated mirror descent family parameterized by η(t) and w(t), with a Lyapunov Lr giving sufficient conditions for 1/r(t) convergence and a discrete algorithm plus experiments; proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves O(1/t^2).

- Strength 1: Provides a unified, elegant theoretical framework using generalized averaging and gradient scaling, with a clean Lyapunov-based convergence analysis (Theorem 2).

- Strength 2: Proposes a novel, principled adaptive averaging heuristic motivated by Lyapunov decrease that provably preserves O(1/t^2), unlike many ad-hoc restarting schemes.

- Strength 3: Clearly connects to known dynamics, recovers prior ODE families, and derives an accelerated replicator dynamics as a concrete example.

- Strength 4: Strong experimental validation showing adaptive averaging is competitive with or better than restarting methods and empirically maintains a decreasing Lyapunov function.

- Weakness 1: Analysis only proves rate preservation, not improvement (e.g., linear rates under strong convexity); discussion of potential/obstacles for improved rates is missing.

- Weakness 2: Discrete Algorithm 1 lacks explicit definitions for R(x̃, x(k)) and γs in the main text, and its discrete-time convergence theorem is relegated to the supplement.

- Weakness 3: Comparison to adaptive methods like Adagrad/Adam is brief; a deeper discussion of compatibility with accelerated/Nesterov-style schemes would help contextualize the contribution.

- Argument for acceptance 1: Unifies a broad class of accelerated mirror descent dynamics under a single analytical framework.

- Argument for acceptance 2: Adaptive averaging is novel, theoretically principled, and has stronger guarantees than common heuristics.

- Argument for acceptance 3: Bridges continuous-time theory and discrete algorithms with convincing empirical support.

- Argument for acceptance 4: Clear, well-written, technically sound, and of high interest to optimization/ML communities.

- Argument against acceptance: Does not demonstrate improved convergence rates for the heuristic under stronger assumptions (e.g., strong convexity).

- Overall assessment: High-quality, original work with minor fixable issues; merits acceptance.
