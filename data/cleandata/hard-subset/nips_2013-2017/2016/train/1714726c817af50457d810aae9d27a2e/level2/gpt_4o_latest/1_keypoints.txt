- Introduces an adaptive averaging heuristic for accelerated descent in constrained convex optimization that adaptively computes weights to speed convergence.
- Leverages a Lyapunov framework to derive sufficient conditions and theoretical convergence guarantees for the proposed method.
- Novelty: the adaptive averaging is a significant innovation compared to existing heuristics (e.g., adaptive restarting) while preserving quadratic accelerated rates.
- Theoretical rigor: mathematical analysis via Lyapunov functions is solid, with clear and generalizable conditions (e.g., Corollary 1).
- Strong connection to prior continuous-time optimization work (Nesterov, replicator dynamics) and meaningful extension to constrained/adaptive settings.
- Practical relevance: numerical experiments show the heuristic outperforms restarting heuristics, especially in strongly convex problems.
- Clarity of numerical results: experiments are well-designed and visualizations (trajectories, Lyapunov/energy plots) elucidate method behavior.
- Limited empirical scope: experiments are mainly low-dimensional (R^3); higher-dimensional and real-world tests are lacking.
- Missing practical implementation details: computational overhead and trade-offs versus simpler schemes are not thoroughly discussed.
- Generality concerns: claimed "significant improvements" are not consistently observed in weakly convex or non-convex settings.
- Accessibility issue: exposition is mathematically dense, particularly Sections 2–3, which may hinder non-expert readers.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to expand empirical evaluation and improve clarity; overall a valuable contribution.
