- Studies accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics where the primal is a weighted average of a mirrored dual trajectory, parameterized by η(t) and w(t), with Lyapunov Lr giving f(X)−f* ≤ O(1/r(t)).
- Unifies prior continuous-time acceleration analyses and shows acceleration can be achieved via appropriate averaging schedules; recovers the standard t^2 rate.
- Proposes an adaptive averaging heuristic a(t)=w(t)/W(t) that preserves Lyapunov decrease and aims to reduce the Lyapunov derivative.
- Introduces an energy function Er that decreases under the same conditions as Lr.
- Provides a primal representation and examples, including a novel accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex.
- Presents a discrete-time algorithm for r(t)=t^2 (details deferred to the supplement) and empirical comparisons suggesting gains over adaptive restarting heuristics.
- Connects and builds on Su et al., Krichene et al., Wibisono et al., clarifying the role of averaging and linking to mirror descent and replicator dynamics.
- Strength: conceptual clarity showing a continuum of averaging schedules can achieve accelerated rates, not just canonical polynomial weights.
- Strength: clean Lyapunov analysis with simple sufficient conditions (Theorem 2, Corollary 1) and a recipe to design schedules for a desired r(t).
- Strength: adaptive averaging has formal continuous-time guarantees and a discrete instantiation claimed to preserve the quadratic rate.
- Strength: useful primal formulation and illustrative examples; accelerated replicator dynamics is an interesting, novel contribution.
- Strength: empirical comparisons indicate practical benefits over restart heuristics in several settings.
- Weakness: key condition (2) depends on trajectory information via ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩ which is not known a priori; robustness to noisy gradients or numerical differentiation is unclear.
- Weakness: discrete-time guarantees are in the supplement; the main text should include the primary theorem, assumptions, and a proof sketch (regularity of f, ψ and ranges of β, s).
- Weakness: experimental scope is limited (mostly low-dimensional simplex problems); lacks higher-dimensional/non-simplex tests, wall-clock/runtime reporting, and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: comparison to adaptive restart heuristics is not systematic—restart threshold tuning and robustness are not detailed.
- Weakness: Lyapunov Lr uses z* tied to a known minimizer x*, so Lr is not a usable runtime diagnostic on real problems—this should be clarified.
- Evaluation (quality): theoretical developments are sound under stated assumptions (smooth convex f with Lipschitz gradient; smooth ψ*), but discrete-time analysis needs more prominence.
- Evaluation (clarity): generally well written with a clear thread, though some notation is dense; recommend restating a(t)=w/W early in Section 2.
- Evaluation (originality): generalized averaging perspective and adaptive averaging heuristic are novel and interesting; accelerated replicator example adds originality.
- Evaluation (significance): advances understanding of acceleration via averaging and offers a principled alternative to restarting, with potential high impact if validated in discrete-time at scale.
- Suggestions: move the main discrete-time theorem and proof sketch into the paper and state precise assumptions for O(1/k^2) with adaptive averaging.
- Suggestions: add ablation studies isolating adaptive averaging vs fixed schedules on larger-scale problems (e.g., logistic regression) and include runtime/sensitivity metrics.
- Suggestions: discuss robustness to stochastic/minibatch gradients and potential extensions of the adaptive rule to noisy settings.
- Suggestions: clarify computational overhead and parameter choices (β, step size s); provide sensitivity plots.
- Suggestions: expand comparison to Wibisono et al. (2016) variational framework, articulating similarities/differences in roles of r(t), a(t), and η(t).
- Pros for acceptance: solid theoretical unification/extending of accelerated dynamics via averaging, principled adaptive heuristic with guarantees, and clear ODE/mirror descent connections.
- Cons for acceptance: discrete-time guarantees and empirical validation need strengthening; dependence on trajectory information raises practical concerns.
- Minor comments: fix bibliography details and formatting, ensure consistent notation for AMD variants and define a(t)=w/W consistently, and clean footnotes/headers.
- Recommendation: Accept if discrete-time guarantees are moved into the main text and experiments modestly expanded; otherwise borderline but positive.
