Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies accelerated first-order dynamics for constrained smooth convex optimization through a generalized averaging view of accelerated mirror descent (AMD). The dynamics couple a dual trajectory Z accumulating gradients at rate η(t) with a primal trajectory X defined as a weighted average of the mirrored dual path with weights w(t). Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors provide sufficient conditions on η and w ensuring Lr decreases and f(X(t))−f* = O(1/r(t)). This unifies and extends earlier continuous-time analyses linking Nesterov’s acceleration to ODEs (e.g., Su-Boyd-Candès; Wibisono-Wilson-Jordan), and connects to the authors’ prior work on AMD via averaging.

The paper further gives: (i) a primal-only reformulation; (ii) examples for specific feasible sets, including an accelerated version of replicator dynamics on the simplex; (iii) a second “energy” function Er whose monotonicity is guaranteed under the same conditions; and (iv) an adaptive averaging heuristic that chooses the weights to further decrease the Lyapunov derivative. A discrete-time algorithm is proposed (for r(t)=t^2), with a proof sketch (in the supplement) that preserves the O(1/k^2) rate, and experiments on simplex-constrained problems comparing to adaptive restarting heuristics (gradient and speed restarts).

Strengths:
- Clear and general Lyapunov framework: Sufficient conditions on η and w that recover known accelerated rates and suggest new designs.
- Conceptual link between acceleration and averaging is sharpened; provides interpretable levers (η, w, r) and a route to guarantee monotone Lyapunov/energy.
- Primal representation and concrete examples (positive orthant; simplex/replicator) make the dynamics more accessible and highlight applications.
- Adaptive averaging heuristic is simple, principled (directly targets Lyapunov decrease), and in experiments performs on par with or better than restarts.
- Existence/uniqueness and feasibility guarantees are carefully addressed.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- The key condition in Theorem 2 involves the trajectory-dependent term ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩(r − ηW/w), which is difficult to verify or enforce a priori. The main tractable case (Corollary 1) ties η and w in a specific way; this may limit design flexibility.
- There appears to be a notational inconsistency: to cancel the second term in (3), one needs η(t) = r(t) w(t)/W(t), i.e., η = a r with a = w/W. In multiple places (Corollary 1, Example 1/2) the text seems to write η(t) = w(t) r(t) W(t). Please confirm and correct throughout; the examples (yielding η=β t for r=t^2 and a=β/t) support η = r w / W.
- Discrete-time guarantees are mostly deferred to the supplement; the main text would benefit from a concise theorem with assumptions, rate, and constants. The phrase “preserves the quadratic convergence rate” could be confusing—consider “O(1/k^2) rate” to avoid conflation with Q-quadratic convergence of Newton-type methods.
- Experimental scope is limited (low-dimensional simplex, deterministic smooth objectives). It is unclear how the method scales or behaves with larger n, different geometries (e.g., box/L1-ball), or stochastic gradients.
- Sensitivity and tuning: performance likely depends on β, step size s, and choices of R and ψ. The paper does not analyze robustness to these choices or provide guidelines.
- Relation to Wibisono et al. (variational perspective) could be sharpened: to what extent is the proposed family strictly more general, and how do the Lyapunov conditions compare to the Bregman Lagrangian framework?

Quality:
Technically sound in continuous time. The Lyapunov derivations and examples are convincing, and the Er energy monotonicity is a nice addition. The discrete-time analysis is plausible but should be stated more explicitly in the main text. Clarify the η/w/W relation to avoid confusion.

Clarity:
Generally well-organized and readable, with helpful intuition. Minor notational inconsistencies (η vs w/W) should be fixed. It would help to summarize the discrete-time theorem in the main body and to provide a short algorithm box with clearly defined parameters and assumptions.

Originality:
The generalized averaging perspective and the adaptive averaging heuristic that preserves the Lyapunov rate are novel and differentiable from existing restart heuristics. The accelerated replicator dynamics is a neat application. The work builds on and extends several recent threads (Su-Boyd-Candès; Wibisono et al.; Krichene et al.).

Significance:
Provides a flexible, principled way to derive accelerated dynamics with guaranteed rates and a practical heuristic that competes with (and sometimes outperforms) popular restarts. With stronger discrete-time results and broader experiments, the impact could extend to practical accelerated methods beyond the simplex.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, unified continuous-time analysis with clear sufficient conditions.
- Simple adaptive heuristic with guarantees and promising empirical performance.
- New insights and an accelerated variant of replicator dynamics.
- In-scope and timely given ongoing interest in ODE views of acceleration.

Cons for acceptance:
- Discrete-time theory and experimental validation are limited in scope.
- The main condition in Theorem 2 can be hard to use beyond the special coupling (Corollary 1).
- Notational inconsistency regarding η, w, and W; needs correction for clarity.

Questions for the authors:
1. Please clarify and correct the relation η, w, W in Corollary 1 and Examples 1–2. Is η(t) = r(t) w(t)/W(t) the intended identity?
2. Can you state (in the main text) a precise discrete-time theorem for Algorithm 1: assumptions (L-smooth, convex), choice of β and s, and the O(1/k^2) bound with constants?
3. How sensitive is adaptive averaging to β and s? Any guidelines or adaptive schemes for these?
4. Can your framework capture or improve rates under strong convexity (e.g., linear rate) while retaining the Lyapunov monotonicity?
5. What is the per-iteration cost relative to FISTA/accelerated mirror descent? Any overhead due to computing ak or extra proximal steps?

Suggestions:
- Broaden experiments: higher-dimensional problems, other geometries (Euclidean mirror map, box/L1-ball), and comparisons to monotone FISTA/restart variants.
- Include an ablation on the behavior of a(t): constant vs β/t, and the effect on weight growth and performance.
- Provide implementation details and, if possible, code.

Minor:
- Ensure references are complete (venues, years, page ranges), and harmonize citation formats.
- Consider replacing “quadratic convergence rate” with “O(1/t^2) (or O(1/k^2)) rate” to avoid ambiguity.
