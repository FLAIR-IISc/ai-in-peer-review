Summary
The paper studies a broad family of accelerated mirror descent (AMD) dynamics for constrained convex optimization where the primal variable is a weighted average of a mirrored dual trajectory. The dynamics are parameterized by two positive, continuous functions: η(t), the gradient accumulation rate in the dual, and w(t), the averaging weights. Using a Lyapunov function Lr(X,Z,t) = r(t)(f(X)−f*) + Dψ*(Z,z*), the authors give sufficient conditions relating η, w, and a desired rate r(t) ensuring f(X(t))−f* ≤ O(1/r(t)). This unifies previous continuous-time acceleration analyses and shows that acceleration can be driven by appropriate averaging schedules; the standard t^2 rate is recovered as a special case.

They propose an adaptive averaging heuristic: adapt a(t) = w(t)/W(t) along the trajectory to (i) preserve the Lyapunov decrease and hence the convergence rate, and (ii) reduce the Lyapunov derivative whenever possible. They also introduce an “energy” function Er that decreases under the same conditions. A primal representation is given, with concrete examples, including an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics on the simplex. A discrete-time algorithm for r(t)=t^2 is proposed (details in the supplement), and empirical comparisons with adaptive restarting heuristics suggest that adaptive averaging performs at least as well, with notable gains in some cases.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on and generalizes continuous-time views of Nesterov acceleration (Su et al., 2014; Krichene et al., 2015; Wibisono et al., 2016), as well as practical heuristics such as adaptive restart (O’Donoghue & Candès, 2015). It clarifies the role of averaging in acceleration (cf. Flammarion & Bach, 2015) and connects to mirror descent and replicator dynamics. Compared to restart heuristics that may lack guarantees for general convex problems, the adaptive averaging rule preserves the Lyapunov decrease and the target rate under verifiable conditions.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: shows that accelerated rates can be achieved by a continuum of averaging schedules, not just the canonical polynomial weights.
- Clean Lyapunov analysis with simple sufficient conditions (Theorem 2, Corollary 1). Recovers known accelerated schedules and offers a recipe to design new ones for a desired r(t).
- Adaptive averaging heuristic with formal guarantees in continuous time (preserves Lr decrease and Er monotonicity), and a discrete instantiation that claims to preserve the quadratic rate.
- Useful primal formulation and illustrative examples; the accelerated replicator dynamics on the simplex is both interesting and novel in this context.
- Empirical comparison suggests practical benefits over restart heuristics in several scenarios.

Weaknesses and questions
- The key condition (2) in Theorem 2 depends on the trajectory via ⟨∇f(X), Ẋ⟩, which is not known a priori; the paper mitigates this via adaptive selection of a(t) but the precise robustness of the rule is not fully explored. Sensitivity to noisy gradient estimates or numerical differentiation of Ẋ is unclear.
- Discrete-time guarantees are deferred to the supplement. It would strengthen the paper to include the main theorem, assumptions, and proof sketch in the main text, including the precise regularity assumptions on f and ψ, and the range of β, s for which O(1/k^2) is preserved.
- Experimental scope is limited (mostly low-dimensional simplex problems). It would be valuable to include higher-dimensional and non-simplex constraints, and to report wall-clock/run-time and sensitivity to hyperparameters (β, step size s).
- The comparison to adaptive restart heuristics could be made more systematic: how are restart thresholds tuned, and are results robust across settings?
- The Lyapunov Lr uses z* corresponding to a minimizer x*; while fine for analysis and experiments (where x* is known), this implies Lr is not usable as a runtime diagnostic on real problems. Clarifying this point would help.

Evaluation per criteria
- Quality: The theoretical developments are sound under the stated assumptions (smooth convex f with Lipschitz gradient; smooth ψ*). The Lyapunov derivations are careful, and the existence/uniqueness argument is standard and appropriate (with the technicality around t0>0 clearly stated). The discrete-time part would benefit from more detail in the main paper.
- Clarity: Generally well written, with a clear thread from continuous-time analysis to algorithm design. Some notation is dense; briefly restating the role of a(t)=w/W early in Section 2 would help readability. Minor formatting issues in references.
- Originality: The generalized averaging perspective and the adaptive averaging heuristic with guarantees are new and interesting extensions of prior continuous-time acceleration frameworks. The accelerated replicator example is a nice contribution.
- Significance: The results add to our understanding of acceleration via averaging and suggest a practical alternative to restarting that is principled and easy to implement. The potential impact could be high if validated more broadly in discrete-time and at scale.

Suggestions for improvement
- Bring the main discrete-time theorem and its proof sketch into the paper; state precise assumptions ensuring the O(1/k^2) rate with adaptive averaging.
- Provide an ablation study isolating the effect of adaptive averaging versus fixed schedules on larger-scale problems (e.g., logistic regression with simplex or orthant constraints).
- Discuss robustness to stochastic gradients and whether the adaptive rule can be extended to noisy or mini-batch settings.
- Clarify computational overhead and parameter choices (β, s); include sensitivity plots.
- Expand the comparison to the variational framework of Wibisono et al. (2016): articulate similarities/differences in the role of r(t), a(t), and η(t), and how your construction fits beyond the special case η=a r.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution unifying and extending accelerated dynamics via generalized averaging.
- Simple, principled adaptive heuristic with guarantees; promising empirical behavior.
- Clear connection to important ODEs (replicator dynamics) and mirror descent.

Cons for acceptance
- Discrete-time guarantees and experimental validation are not as comprehensive as desirable.
- Dependence on trajectory information for the condition (2) raises practical questions not fully addressed.

Minor comments/typos
- Some references lack complete bibliographic details; standardize formatting.
- Ensure consistent notation between AMDw,η and AMD′w,η, and consistently define a(t)=w/W when used.
- Footnote formatting and the “th Conference…” line need cleanup.

Overall recommendation: Accept if the discrete-time guarantees are moved into the main text and experiments are modestly expanded; otherwise borderline but positive due to conceptual clarity and generality.

Reviewer confidence: High on the theoretical part; medium on the discrete-time and empirical claims pending access to the supplement.
