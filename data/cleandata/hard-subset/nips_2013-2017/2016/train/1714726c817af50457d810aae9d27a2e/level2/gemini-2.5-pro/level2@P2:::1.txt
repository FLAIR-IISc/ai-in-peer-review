### Summary

This paper studies continuous-time accelerated first-order dynamics for constrained convex optimization. It generalizes the accelerated mirror descent (AMD) framework from prior work [11] by introducing a dynamics parameterized by a gradient accumulation rate `η(t)` and an averaging weight function `w(t)`. The main theoretical contribution is a Lyapunov-based analysis that provides simple, sufficient conditions on `η(t)` and `w(t)` to achieve a desired convergence rate of `1/r(t)`. This framework is shown to be general enough to derive an accelerated version of the replicator dynamics, a well-known ODE for optimization on the probability simplex.

Building on this analysis, the paper proposes a novel "adaptive averaging" heuristic. The core idea is to adaptively choose the averaging weights `w(t)` to greedily decrease the Lyapunov function, thereby speeding up convergence while provably preserving the `O(1/t^2)` rate. A discrete-time version of this heuristic is presented and empirically compared against standard accelerated mirror descent and popular restarting heuristics [17, 20] on several simplex-constrained problems. The experiments suggest that adaptive averaging performs at least as well as restarting, and offers significant improvements in some cases, particularly for a strongly convex objective.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is sound and elegant. The use of the Lyapunov function `Lr` provides a clear and unified way to analyze the convergence of the entire class of `AMDw,η` dynamics. The derivation leading to the main conditions in Theorem 2 is straightforward and well-presented. A key strength of this work is that the proposed adaptive averaging heuristic comes with a formal guarantee: it preserves the original convergence rate by construction. This is a significant advantage over many existing heuristics (like restarting), for which such guarantees are often absent or limited to specific problem classes (e.g., strongly convex).

The experimental validation is solid and convincingly supports the paper's claims about the practical performance of adaptive averaging. The comparison with established restarting heuristics is fair and the results are presented clearly. The inclusion of plots for both the Lyapunov function `Lr` and the energy function `Er` provides valuable insight into the behavior of the different methods.

A minor weakness is that the detailed analysis of the discrete-time algorithm (Algorithm 1) and its convergence guarantee are deferred to the supplementary material. While understandable due to space constraints, a brief sketch of the discrete Lyapunov argument in the main text would have made the paper more self-contained.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The introduction effectively situates the work within the context of continuous-time analysis of accelerated methods. The mathematical derivations are clean, and the notation is consistent. The progression from the general continuous-time framework to the specific adaptive heuristic and its discretization is logical. Figure 1 provides a useful conceptual illustration of the dynamics. The primal representation in Section 3 and the examples of dynamics on the positive orthant and the simplex are insightful and help build intuition.

One area for potential improvement is the connection between the continuous-time adaptive strategy in equation (8) and its discrete implementation in Algorithm 1. The continuous heuristic relies on the sign of `<∇f(X(t)), Ẋ(t)>`, while the discrete algorithm checks `f(x̃(k+1)) > f(x̃(k))`. While these conditions are related (both detect a lack of progress), the exact correspondence could be elaborated on for greater clarity.

### Originality

The paper makes several original contributions.
1.  The `AMDw,η` framework is a novel and meaningful generalization of the dynamics presented in [11] and provides a unified perspective on averaging in accelerated methods.
2.  The adaptive averaging heuristic is a new and clever idea. It provides an alternative to the dominant paradigm of restarting for empirically improving accelerated methods. The mechanism—modifying the averaging weights rather than resetting the state—is fundamentally different.
3.  Demonstrating how to accelerate the replicator dynamics is an interesting and novel application that connects the abstract optimization framework to a classic ODE from evolutionary game theory.

The work clearly builds upon the recent line of research connecting Nesterov's methods to ODEs [20, 11, 23], but it pushes this direction forward with new theoretical tools, a new algorithm, and new insights.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant for both theory and practice.
-   **Theoretically**, the unified Lyapunov analysis for a general class of averaging schemes is a valuable contribution to our understanding of acceleration. It provides a principled way to design and analyze new accelerated dynamics.
-   **Practically**, adaptive averaging appears to be a powerful and robust heuristic. The fact that it comes with a convergence rate guarantee makes it a "safer" choice than other heuristics, which can sometimes fail to converge or slow down convergence on certain problems. The strong empirical performance suggests it could be a valuable tool for practitioners. The ideas presented could also inspire further research into other forms of adaptive, rate-preserving heuristics for optimization algorithms, potentially including the higher-order methods mentioned in the conclusion.

While the experiments are conducted on low-dimensional problems for visualization purposes, the principles are general. If the strong performance holds up in higher-dimensional machine learning settings, the impact could be substantial.

### Arguments for acceptance:
*   Presents a novel and elegant theoretical framework for analyzing a general class of accelerated dynamics.
*   Introduces adaptive averaging, a new, practical heuristic for accelerating convergence that is fundamentally different from restarting.
*   Crucially, the proposed heuristic is backed by a formal guarantee that it preserves the theoretical `O(1/t^2)` convergence rate.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and clear.
*   Empirical results are promising and show the new method is competitive with or superior to existing state-of-the-art heuristics.

### Arguments against acceptance:
*   The main paper's experimental validation is limited to low-dimensional problems. Including results from the supplementary material on higher-dimensional problems would strengthen the claims.
*   The connection between the continuous-time heuristic and its discrete implementation could be slightly better motivated.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a complete piece of work with strong theoretical foundations, a novel and practical algorithm, and convincing empirical results. It is an excellent contribution to the field.
