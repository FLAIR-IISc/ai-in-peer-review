This paper presents Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The work extends the Generative Moment Matching Network (GMMN) model, which learns a joint distribution by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between real data and generated samples. The key challenge in the conditional setting is that for a mini-batch of inputs `x_i`, the target distributions `p(y|x_i)` are all different. A simple application of MMD on the aggregated set of `y` samples would be incorrect.

The authors' core contribution is to propose a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective to address this challenge. This objective measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective kernel mean embedding operators. The proposed model generates samples by feeding both the conditional variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` through a deep neural network. The authors demonstrate that the CMMD objective can be optimized efficiently via backpropagation. The method is evaluated on a diverse set of tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, conditional image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the important and non-trivial problem of extending moment-matching techniques to conditional generation. This is a significant step forward from the original GMMN, greatly expanding the applicability of this class of models.
*   The proposed CMMD objective is a principled approach to comparing conditional distributions in the RKHS framework.
*   The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating the model's versatility across predictive, generative, and knowledge distillation tasks, with competitive results.

**Weaknesses:**
The main weakness of the paper lies in its clarity and the lack of intuition provided for its core technical contribution.

1.  **Lack of Intuitive Motivation:** The paper immediately dives into the formal mathematics of kernel mean embedding operators and Hilbert-Schmidt norms. While this formalism is likely necessary for the theoretical grounding, it makes the core ideas difficult to access. The original MMD objective used in GMMN has a very clear and powerful intuition: if two distributions are identical, all of their moments must match. The paper fails to provide a similarly intuitive explanation for the proposed CMMD objective. The reader is presented with the final formula (Equation 2) without a clear, high-level understanding of what it is actually computing or why it is the correct way to measure the distance between two families of conditional distributions.

2.  **Insufficient Detail on Practical Computation:** Following from the first point, the paper does not clearly explain the practical steps involved in computing the CMMD objective (Equation 2) and its gradient. It is not immediately apparent how to translate this formula, which involves matrix inversions and traces of matrix products, into an efficient algorithm for mini-batch training. The reader is left wondering about the computational complexity and potential bottlenecks. While some details are deferred to the appendix, a clearer exposition in the main text is needed to understand what computations are actually being performed during training and how scalable the method is.

To significantly improve the paper, I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the presentation to address these clarity issues. Specifically, it would be beneficial to answer the following questions:

*   How can one intuitively arrive at the CMMD objective in Equation (2)? Could the authors provide a more accessible explanation that bridges the conceptual gap between the standard MMD and the proposed CMMD, perhaps by explaining what the weighting matrices `K̃⁻¹` represent in an intuitive sense?
*   What are the precise computational steps required to implement the CMMD loss for a single mini-batch update? A clear, step-by-step description or pseudo-code in the main paper would greatly improve reproducibility and the reader's understanding of the algorithm's practical implications. A discussion of the computational complexity with respect to the batch size would also be valuable.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and potentially significant extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. However, its impact is currently limited by a presentation that prioritizes mathematical formalism over intuitive explanation and practical clarity. Improving the exposition of the CMMD objective and its implementation would make the work much stronger and more accessible to the wider NIPS community.
