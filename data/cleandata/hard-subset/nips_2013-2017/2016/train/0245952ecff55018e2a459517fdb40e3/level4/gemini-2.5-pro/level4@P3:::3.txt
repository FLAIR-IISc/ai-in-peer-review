### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the previously proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN). While GMMN learns a joint distribution by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model-generated samples and real data, CGMMN learns a conditional distribution p(y|x). The core technical contribution is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective function. This objective is derived from the theory of kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, measuring the discrepancy between the conditional embedding operators of the true data distribution and the generative model. The model itself consists of a deep neural network that takes the conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to generate a sample `y`. The network's parameters are learned via backpropagation by minimizing the CMMD loss. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their framework on a range of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual image generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a logical and important extension of the GMMN framework to the conditional setting. This significantly broadens the applicability of moment-matching approaches, providing a direct alternative to other conditional generative models like conditional GANs and conditional VAEs. The non-adversarial nature of the MMD-based objective is an appealing feature, potentially offering more stable training than the min-max optimization of GANs.

2.  **Versatility of Applications:** The authors do an excellent job of demonstrating the model's flexibility. Applying a single framework to supervised classification, conditional generation, and the novel task of "Bayesian dark knowledge" distillation is a key strength. The distillation experiment, in particular, showcases an interesting and practical use case for learning a fast, deterministic "student" network that approximates the predictive distribution of a complex Bayesian "teacher" model.

3.  **Soundness of Approach:** The proposed CMMD loss is well-grounded in the theory of kernel embeddings. The paper builds cleanly upon established work in this area (e.g., Gretton et al., Song et al.) to formulate a principled objective function for conditional density estimation.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Connection (Remark 1):** In Remark 1, the authors draw an interesting connection between CMMD and a weighted version of MMD. However, this connection is not made sufficiently clear. The expression `Tr(Ld · 1)` is ambiguous; it is unclear if the dot `·` represents standard matrix multiplication or an element-wise Hadamard product. Furthermore, the choice of `C` for the weight matrices `C1, C2, C3` is potentially confusing, as `C` is already used to denote the conditional embedding operator. A more detailed derivation or explanation of how Equation (2) maps to this weighted form would greatly improve the reader's intuition.

2.  **Missing Proof for Theorem 3:** Theorem 3, which states that equality of the conditional embedding operators implies equality of the conditional distributions, is a cornerstone of the paper's theoretical justification. Unlike Theorems 1 and 2, which are cited, Theorem 3 is presented without a proof or a reference. For a result of this importance, the authors should either provide a proof (e.g., in the appendix) or cite a publication where the proof can be found.

3.  **Scope of Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The application to distilling Bayesian models is novel and promising. However, its evaluation on only the Boston housing dataset with one type of Bayesian model (PBP) makes it difficult to assess the general utility of this technique. Demonstrating its effectiveness on more complex models or additional datasets would substantially strengthen this contribution.
    *   The generative modeling experiments on MNIST and Yale Faces successfully prove the concept. However, to establish CGMMN as a competitive method in the rapidly advancing field of generative models, results on more complex and diverse datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, LSUN) are needed. This would better demonstrate the model's ability to scale and handle the challenges posed by natural images, providing a more compelling comparison against state-of-the-art conditional GANs.

4.  **Supervised Learning Application:** While the authors' stated goal of using classification tasks is to highlight the model's versatility, this remains one of the weaker applications. Generative classifiers are typically outperformed by their discriminative counterparts, and the results here reflect that. This is a minor point, as the authors acknowledge this, but the space might have been better used to bolster the more novel applications.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a well-motivated and theoretically sound extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. The resulting CGMMN framework is elegant and demonstrated across a compelling variety of tasks. The main weaknesses lie in the need for greater clarity on some theoretical details (Remark 1, Theorem 3) and a more ambitious experimental evaluation to fully demonstrate the significance and scalability of the approach, particularly in the domain of high-resolution image generation. Despite these points, the paper makes a solid and valuable contribution to the literature on deep generative models.
