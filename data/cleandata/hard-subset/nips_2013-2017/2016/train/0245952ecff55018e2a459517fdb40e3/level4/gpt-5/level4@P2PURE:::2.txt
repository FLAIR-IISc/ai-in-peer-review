This paper extends moment matching networks to the conditional setting. Unlike models that update on single examples—where the extension is trivial by simply feeding the conditional into the network—the conditional extension here is non-trivial. Training requires matching the distributions of a large number of generated samples (per mini-batch) to a large number of input conditionals, and these distributions are no longer the same (unless one uses the same conditional for a given update, which would be inefficient).

The main issue is exposition: the paper launches into heavy formalism rather than building intuition, and it does not make clear what computations the algorithm actually performs or how efficient they are. In particular, the paper does not explain the details of the final objective being optimized (Eq. (2)): what assumptions are required to make it work, how it is computed in practice, and what its computational cost is. As a result, it is hard for the reader to see the concrete operations carried out by the algorithm. Similarly, the non-conditional version can be explained (and is, in the original paper) without advanced machinery such as reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces, because the minimized objective is intuitive; an equally clear, intuitive explanation is needed for the conditional case.

It would help to address the following:
- How one would intuitively derive Eq. (2).
- What the exact steps are from Eq. (2) to the practical training algorithm (some of this is in the supplement, but it is hard to follow).
