Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN [19] from modeling joint distributions to modeling conditional distributions P(Y|X). The key idea is to train a deep conditional generator by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) between conditional mean embeddings of the data and model. The conditional mean embedding operator CY|X is estimated from paired data (x, y) using kernel methods [29], and the CMMD objective compares two empirical operators via Hilbert–Schmidt norms, yielding a simple, differentiable loss. As with GMMN, sampling from a base noise distribution and passing [x, h] through a neural network defines a flexible generator; gradients are obtained by backpropagation. The paper evaluates CGMMN on classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional image generation (MNIST, Extended Yale B), and distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (Bayesian “dark knowledge” [15]) into a compact student.

This work connects several lines: kernel two-sample tests and mean embeddings [7, 27, 29], deep generative models trained by MMD [3, 19], and conditional generative models such as conditional GANs [21] and conditional VAEs [28]. The paper’s main conceptual contribution is to replace unconditional MMD with CMMD so that a single conditional generator can be trained without separately solving many small conditional problems.

Strengths:
- A principled, simple objective: CMMD reduces conditional matching to a trace form involving Gram matrices, closely mirroring MMD (Remark 1), and is amenable to backpropagation.
- Solid theoretical grounding: using operator equality in a universal RKHS as a sufficient condition for conditional distribution equality (Theorem 3) is natural in the mean embedding framework.
- Versatility: the same training recipe applies to classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation. The classification results are competitive with strong DGM baselines (MMVA/CMMVA), and the distillation experiment shows no loss relative to the PBP teacher on Boston housing.
- Practicality: the generator architecture is flexible (MLP/CNN), and the training loop is straightforward.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Baselines and ablations: For discrete conditioning (e.g., MNIST labels), a strong naïve baseline is to train one unconditional GMMN per class (sharing lower layers if desired). This tests whether CMMD gives tangible gains over simpler decompositions when X takes few values. In conditional generation, comparisons to conditional GAN [21] and conditional VAE [28] are also important. For distillation, comparing against the original “dark knowledge” student [15] would clarify advantages.
- Scalability and mini-batch sensitivity: CMMD requires inverting Kx + λI on each mini-batch (O(B^3)), and computing O(B^2) Gram matrices. The paper notes a mini-batch variant but does not quantify computational cost, memory footprint, or the effect of batch size on stability and accuracy. Empirical sensitivity analyses (batch size vs. performance), and practical tricks (Cholesky solves instead of explicit inverses, Nyström/Random Features approximations, block-diagonal kernels, or caching Kx when x’s repeat) would strengthen the case for scalability.
- Kernel choices and hyperparameters: Performance often hinges on kernel selection and bandwidths. The paper should specify kernels used for X and Y in each task, bandwidth selection (median heuristic? CV?), and whether kernels are characteristic. For labels, clarify whether a delta kernel is used; for images, specify whether RBF on pixels or features is used.
- Clarity: Sections 2.3 and 3.1 are hard to follow without prior familiarity with conditional embeddings. A more self-contained derivation (with consistent centering, regularization, and operator notation), a brief algorithm box showing minibatch CMMD computation, and an intuitive explanation of CMMD as “MMD with input-dependent weights” (as hinted in Remark 1) would help.
- Quantitative generation evaluation: Beyond visual samples, include standard quantitative proxies (e.g., Parzen scores in feature space, FID, or retrieval-based metrics) and nearest-neighbor analyses for all datasets.

Technical comments and minor issues:
- MMD estimator unbiasedness (Sec. 2.2, around line 77): The squared MMD has both a biased V-statistic and an unbiased U-statistic estimator [7]. The expression L̂^2 = || 1/N ∑ φ(xi) − 1/M ∑ φ(yj) ||^2 corresponds to the biased V-statistic (including diagonals). It is not unbiased, though it is consistent. Please either use the U-statistic form or revise the statement to “consistent” (not “unbiased”).
- Conditional embedding operator (Sec. 2.3, around line 90): The paper defines CXY as a centered cross-covariance operator and then states CY|X = CYX C−1XX. The empirical estimator ĈY|X = Φ (K + λI)−1 Υ⊤ typically follows from a regression view with appropriate centering (H) and scaling [29]. As written, the estimator seems to omit centering, which yields a different operator unless μX = 0. Please reconcile the definition of CXY (centered vs. uncentered), the conditions under which CY|X = CYX C−1XX holds, and the empirical estimator (with regularization and centering), or explicitly state that you use the uncentered variant and why.
- Notation: Several symbols are overloaded (Φ, Υ, K, L, Ksd, Lds). A small notation table would help.
- Typos/consistency: “MINIST” → “MNIST”; “ReLu” → “ReLU”; fix references formatting and line breaks (e.g., “th Conference…”). Clarify whether λ is scaled by N in regularization.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically sound, with a reasonable objective and implementation. Some derivational details and estimator claims need clarification/correction (unbiasedness; operator consistency). Computational complexity and kernel choices should be discussed more deeply.
- Clarity: Overall clear high-level story; however, Sections 2.3 and 3.1 would benefit from a more self-contained and consistent derivation and more implementation detail.
- Originality: Using CMMD to train deep conditional generators appears novel, distinct from GMMN [19] and conditional GAN/CVAE [21, 28], and well-grounded in conditional mean embeddings [29].
- Significance: Potentially useful for a broad range of conditional modeling tasks and offers an alternative to adversarial training. Current experiments are promising but somewhat limited; stronger baselines and scalability analysis would increase impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add the “naïve conditional-GMMN” baseline (per-class GMMNs on MNIST) and comparisons to conditional GAN/CVAE on conditional generation.
- Provide computational complexity, wall-clock comparisons, and batch-size sensitivity; consider approximate kernels or linear-time CMMD variants.
- Specify kernel choices and bandwidth selection procedures; discuss characteristic kernels.
- Clarify estimator properties (biased vs. unbiased MMD) and reconcile conditional operator definitions/centering.
- Include additional quantitative generation metrics and more thorough distillation experiments (e.g., larger datasets or comparisons to [15]).

Pros:
- Simple, principled objective; avoids adversarial min–max.
- Theoretically motivated with clear connection to mean embeddings.
- Versatile across tasks; competitive results; neat application to Bayesian distillation.

Cons:
- Missing key baselines/ablations; limited quantitative evaluation for generation.
- Scalability and mini-batch sensitivity not fully addressed.
- Some notation/derivation inconsistencies; minor technical inaccuracies.

Overall, CGMMN is a timely and interesting contribution; with the above clarifications and stronger empirical comparisons, it would be a solid addition to the literature on conditional generative modeling.
