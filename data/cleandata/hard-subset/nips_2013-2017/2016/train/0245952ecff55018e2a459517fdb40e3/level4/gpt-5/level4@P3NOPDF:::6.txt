Summary
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), extending GMMNs to model conditional distributions for prediction and conditional generation. The method leverages kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional MMD (CMMD) objective to match conditional distributions in RKHS without requiring explicit likelihoods. Experiments cover classification, conditional generation, and distillation of Bayesian models.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Framing conditional generation via conditional distribution embeddings is elegant and avoids the data sparsity problem inherent in per-condition models.
- Objective function: The CMMD objective has a clean formulation. As noted in Remark 1, it can be computed in a way closely analogous to the unconditional MMD, with kernel matrices independent of network parameters so that gradients propagate only through samples from the model.
- Practical relevance: Applying CGMMN to classification and Bayesian model distillation is interesting and demonstrates versatility.
- Implementation detail: Emphasizing that all K matrices in Eq. (2) are fixed with respect to model parameters simplifies backpropagation and is helpful for practitioners.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Baselines: The paper does not compare against a simple “naive” conditional baseline—training a separate (or shared-parameter) GMMN per condition. While this approach suffers from data sparsity in continuous conditioning spaces, it can be competitive when the conditioning variable is discrete with few categories (e.g., MNIST digits). Including this baseline would strengthen the empirical case, especially on datasets with small, discrete condition sets.
- Minibatch variance and computational cost: The CMMD estimator seems sensitive to minibatch size; small batches may introduce high variance that obscures the learning signal. Conversely, large batches make the kernel computations and linear solves expensive. The need to invert (or solve with) kernel matrices can become a bottleneck. Please discuss:
  - The variance–batch size trade-off and any techniques you use to stabilize training (e.g., moving-average/streaming estimators, control variates, or regularization).
  - Practical complexity per iteration and wall-clock times for different batch sizes.
  - Numerical strategies: using Cholesky solves instead of explicit inverses; iterative solvers (CG) with warm starts; low-rank/Nyström approximations; random Fourier features; block or linear-time MMD variants; or caching Gram matrices for discrete conditions.
- Clarity and self-containment: Sections 2.3 and 3.1 are difficult to follow without consulting prior work. The paper would benefit from:
  - A concise, self-contained derivation of the conditional embedding operator used, including operator domains/codomains, explicit definitions of the cross-covariance operators, and the role of regularization.
  - A summary of assumptions (e.g., characteristic kernels), and a brief discussion of how kernel hyperparameters are chosen (bandwidth selection) and their sensitivity.
  - Clear notation for all matrices in Eq. (2), their sizes, and how they are constructed in minibatches.
- Experimental analysis depth: While the results are promising, the empirical section would be stronger with:
  - The naive conditional baseline described above; where conditions are discrete and well-represented, how much does CGMMN gain?
  - Sensitivity to the regularization parameter and batch size.
  - Ablations on kernel choice for both inputs and outputs, and the effect of bandwidth selection on performance and stability.
  - Runtime and memory comparisons versus the unconditional GMMN and other conditional generative baselines.

Specific comments and minor issues
- Line 77: The statement that the MMD estimator is “unbiased” is inaccurate as written. The U-statistic estimator for MMD^2 is unbiased (though it can be negative due to variance), whereas commonly used finite-sample estimators can be biased. Please clarify the estimator used and its properties.
- Line 90: The definition of the conditional embedding operator appears inconsistent with reference [29]. To obtain your form, it seems a different definition of the cross-covariance operator is required. Please reconcile the notation and provide a short derivation that aligns with [29], or explicitly state any deviations and their implications.
- Remark 1: It would help to explicitly state the exact matrices that are independent of model parameters and to clarify how gradients are computed through the sampled outputs.
- Notation: Clearly distinguish between population operators and their empirical (regularized) estimates, and state the regularization used in the conditional embedding (e.g., ridge parameter λ) along with its selection procedure.

Questions for the authors
- How does CGMMN perform when the conditioning space is high-dimensional and continuous with limited coverage? Do you observe degradation relative to discrete or well-covered conditions?
- What batch sizes were ultimately used in your best-performing models, and how sensitive are results to reducing batch size?
- Do you reuse kernel matrices across steps for discrete conditions, and if so, how much speedup do you obtain?
- Can you report per-iteration complexity and memory usage, and provide training time comparisons with alternative conditional generative models?
- For the Bayesian distillation task, how does CGMMN compare to distillation via conditional VAEs or cGANs, and what is the calibration quality of the distilled model?

Overall evaluation
This is a solid and timely contribution that integrates conditional kernel embeddings with deep generative modeling. The CMMD objective is elegant and practically implementable, and the applications are compelling. The main gaps are empirical: a stronger baseline comparison (especially the naive conditional GMMN in discrete settings), clearer reporting of computational costs, and improved exposition of the conditional embedding machinery. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and its impact.
