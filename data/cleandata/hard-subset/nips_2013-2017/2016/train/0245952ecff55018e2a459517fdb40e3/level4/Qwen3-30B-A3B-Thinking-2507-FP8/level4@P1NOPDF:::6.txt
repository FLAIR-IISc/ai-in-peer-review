

This paper introduces conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMNs), extending generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core innovation lies in kernel embedding of conditional distributions and a conditional MMD (CMMD) metric for measuring conditional distribution discrepancies. The authors evaluate CGMMNs on classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation.  

The naive extension of GMMNs to conditional settings would train a separate GMMN per condition, sharing a single neural network across all conditional distributions. However, this approach suffers from severe data sparsity: when conditioning variables are continuous, each conditional distribution may contain only a single example, making reliable estimation infeasible. The proposed CGMMN addresses this by treating all conditional distributions as a unified family, directly matching the model to the conditional embedding operator rather than individual distributions. While the approach appears compelling, the naive method achieves acceptable performance in discrete-conditioning scenarios (e.g., MNIST with 10 discrete conditioning classes). A comparison against this baseline would strengthen the evaluation.  

The CMMD objective’s formulation is elegant and computationally efficient, as noted in Remark 1. Crucially, all kernel matrices in Equation 2 are independent of model parameters, enabling straightforward backpropagation identical to unconditional MMD. A notable concern, however, is the CMMD’s potential need for large minibatches to mitigate stochastic error—yet large minibatches incur significant computational cost, particularly for expensive matrix inversions. This trade-off merits explicit discussion.  

Overall, the CMMD framework provides a theoretically sound approach for modeling conditional distributions. The classification and Bayesian model distillation results are promising, though the practical impact of the computational constraints should be clarified.  

The presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 lacks clarity, requiring readers to consult referenced papers for full understanding. Specific improvements are needed:  
(1) Line 77 incorrectly claims the MMD estimator is unbiased; it is unbiased only for finite samples under specific kernel choices.  
(2) The conditional embedding operator (line 90) conflicts with the definition in reference [29]. The paper’s formulation requires a distinct cross-covariance operator definition to align with its derivation.  

The technical contributions are sound, and the CMMD framework offers a valuable extension to conditional generative modeling. Addressing the minor technical inconsistencies and clarifying the computational trade-offs would significantly strengthen the paper.
