

This paper introduces a novel loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables, extending generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) via conditional kernel mean embeddings. The proposed method leverages maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) to define a conditional loss, demonstrating efficacy across classification, handwritten digit and face generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation.  

This is my second review of the paper. The authors have addressed all prior concerns: the rationale for low-dimensional latent spaces is now justified, scalability to higher dimensions is discussed, and comparative utility against existing methods is clarified. However, two technical points require resolution. First, Remark 1’s mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form remains ambiguous. Specifically, the dot notation in the equation is unclear—does it denote matrix multiplication or Hadamard product? A brief elaboration would clarify this critical step. Second, the notation choice for \(C\) is problematic: \(C\) is simultaneously used to define the conditional embedding and as a constant (e.g., in Equation 4), risking confusion. The authors should adopt distinct symbols for these distinct concepts.  

Additionally, Theorem 3 lacks a citation to a proof source or an included proof, while Theorems 1 and 2 reference prior work. A proper citation or proof must be added for Theorem 3 to meet academic standards.  

The empirical results are adequate but not transformative. As noted in the prior review, the supervised learning application (e.g., classification) appears suboptimal, though the authors correctly position it as a demonstration of model versatility rather than a primary contribution. The Bayesian knowledge distillation experiment is intriguing but limited to a single dataset and model architecture, making its broader applicability unclear. For this approach to gain traction, the strongest evidence should focus on pure generative modeling, where the method’s performance is comparable to the original GMMN paper on standard benchmarks (digits and faces). While these datasets suffice to validate the core idea, adoption by the community would require compelling results on more challenging tasks like CIFAR-10 or LSUN. The current experiments, though sufficient for proof-of-concept, do not yet establish a clear advantage over state-of-the-art generative models on complex data.  

In summary, the paper makes a technically sound contribution to conditional generative modeling, with minor revisions to notation and theorem presentation needed. The core methodology is well-motivated and validated on relevant tasks, but the limited scope of empirical evaluation—particularly the absence of results on high-complexity datasets—prevents a strong recommendation for acceptance at this stage. The authors should prioritize addressing the notational ambiguities and expanding experimental validation to more challenging benchmarks before the final decision.
