This paper develops conditional generative moment-matching networks (CGMMN), a neural network approach based on a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The proposed technique is evaluated on standard benchmark tasks to assess both its discriminative and generative capabilities. Overall, this is an interesting extension within the sub-area of deep learning research focused on building generative models. The paper is reasonably clear, although, as is common in this field, the mathematical details can be challenging to follow. The tasks used in the experiments are relatively small in scale, but comparable to those found in other recent work. Given that the algorithm for training the model involves computationally expensive steps, I would appreciate comments in the experimental section regarding training time compared to existing approaches like DSN. The paper would be significantly strengthened by the public release of reference code, facilitating reproducibility of the reported experiments. A practitioner might find it difficult to precisely replicate the evaluated models without such code.

The paper begins by presenting a thorough overview of relevant background, including Hilbert space embeddings, maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), and kernel embedding of conditional distributions. The core contribution lies in extending generative moment matching networks (GMMN) to the conditional setting, enabling the learning of flexible conditional distributions. The CMMD criterion is well-motivated and leverages kernel methods to avoid strong distributional assumptions. The authors clearly articulate the motivation for conditional modeling, highlighting applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and building large networks. 

The strengths of this work include the theoretical soundness of the proposed method, the clear presentation of the CMMD criterion, and the empirical validation on a diverse set of tasks. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), generative modeling (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The use of both MLP and CNN architectures provides some insight into the flexibility of the proposed approach. 

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the theoretical foundation is solid, the assumptions required for the CMMD objective to be well-defined are not fully discussed, and their practical impact is not thoroughly investigated. The experiments, while demonstrating competitive performance, are limited in scope. A more extensive evaluation on larger and more challenging datasets would further strengthen the claims. The comparison with existing methods is not always comprehensive; for example, more recent generative models like variational autoencoders with more advanced architectures could have been included. Furthermore, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method, particularly with respect to the kernel computations. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of GMMN to the conditional setting, addressing a practically relevant problem.
*   Theoretically sound approach based on kernel methods and maximum mean discrepancy.
*   Competitive empirical performance on a diverse set of tasks.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method and experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of the experiments and lack of comparison to more recent and advanced generative models.
*   Insufficient discussion of the theoretical assumptions and their practical implications.
*   Lack of analysis of the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method.
*   Absence of publicly available code hinders reproducibility.

In conclusion, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling. While some weaknesses remain, the novelty of the approach, its theoretical soundness, and the promising experimental results suggest that this work is worthy of acceptance, contingent upon addressing the concerns regarding experimental scope, computational cost, and code availability.
