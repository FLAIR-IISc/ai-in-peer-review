Summary
This paper proposes a neural network approach based on a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The method, termed a conditional generative moment-matching network (CGMMN), is evaluated on standard small benchmark tasks to assess both discriminative and generative performance. Overall, the work presents an interesting extension within the deep learning sub-area concerned with generative modeling.

Strengths
- The central idea—optimizing a conditional discrepancy measure to learn a conditional generative model—is conceptually clear and well aligned with current efforts in deep generative modeling.
- The experimental evaluation covers both discriminative and generative tasks, providing a broad view of the method’s capabilities.
- The paper is reasonably clearly written; while some mathematical derivations are challenging (as is common in this area), the main ideas are understandable.

Weaknesses
- The datasets and tasks are relatively small. While this is consistent with some prior work, stronger evidence on larger-scale tasks would be compelling.
- The training algorithm appears to involve computationally expensive steps. The paper would benefit from explicit reporting of training time and computational setup, and direct comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., DSN) in terms of wall-clock time and resource usage.
- Reproducibility is a concern: from the model section alone, practitioners may find it difficult to exactly reproduce the reported models and results. The paper would be substantially strengthened by releasing reference code and providing more detailed implementation specifics (architectures, hyperparameters, training schedules).

Questions for the authors
- How long does the model take to train on the reported tasks, and how does this compare to baselines such as DSN under comparable hardware and optimization settings?
- Can you provide more detailed architectural and training hyperparameters to facilitate replication?
- Do you plan to release reference code for the experiments?

Evaluation by criteria

Quality
- The approach appears technically sound, and the claimed capabilities are supported by empirical results on both discriminative and generative tasks.
- However, the computational cost of the training procedure is not quantified. Given the potentially expensive steps involved, explicit runtime and resource analyses are necessary to fully assess practicality relative to existing approaches (e.g., DSN).
- The experiments are on small benchmarks; while informative, the conclusions would be stronger with larger or more diverse datasets.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and reasonably clear. Some mathematical details may be difficult to follow, but this is common in related work.
- To improve clarity and reproducibility, the authors should include more concrete implementation details and training configurations, and ideally provide code.

Originality
- The paper presents an interesting extension within the domain of deep generative models by employing a conditional discrepancy objective. This direction aligns with ongoing research in conditional generative modeling and is a natural development of moment-matching ideas.

Significance
- The method is promising and could be useful to researchers working on conditional generative modeling. The current experiments support competitive performance on small benchmarks.
- The significance would be enhanced by demonstrating scalability, reporting training efficiency, and ensuring that others can reproduce the results via released code.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually appealing extension of moment-matching methods to conditional settings.
- Demonstrates both discriminative and generative capabilities with competitive performance on standard small benchmarks.
- Reasonably clear presentation of the main ideas.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scale of experiments; broader empirical validation is desirable.
- Lack of training-time comparisons and resource usage details makes it hard to judge practical efficiency against strong baselines (e.g., DSN).
- Reproducibility concerns due to sparse implementation detail and absence of reference code.

Recommendation
Borderline. The core idea is interesting and the results are promising, but the paper would benefit significantly from:
- Reporting training times and computational resources, especially compared with DSN and other baselines.
- Providing sufficient implementation detail and releasing reference code to ensure reproducibility.
If these issues are addressed, the contribution would be much stronger.
