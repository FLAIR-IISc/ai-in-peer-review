This paper presents Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), an extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction applications. The key to the proposed method lies in the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric for measuring the discrepancy of conditional distributions. The authors test the proposed CGMMN on classification, conditional generation, and distilling Bayesian models.

A naive approach to extending GMMNs to the conditional setting would be to estimate a GMMN for each conditional distribution, with all these conditional distributions sharing parameters through the use of the same neural network. However, this approach has a significant drawback: each conditional distribution has very few examples, and in the case of continuous conditioning variables, each conditional distribution may have only one single example, resulting in a data sparsity problem. In contrast, the proposed approach treats all the conditional distributions as a family and attempts to match the model with the conditional embedding operator directly, rather than matching each individual conditional distribution. The advantage of the proposed approach seems clear, but it is still possible to envision scenarios where the naive approach performs reasonably well, such as in conditional generation where the conditioning variable takes one of a limited number of values, as in MNIST. A comparison with such a naive approach as a baseline would be an interesting addition.

The conditional MMD objective has a nice formulation and can be easily computed almost in the same way as the unconditional MMD, as pointed out in Remark 1. Moreover, all the K matrices in equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, and backpropagation can be done in the same way as before. One concern about the proposed approach is that the CMMD objective may require large minibatches; otherwise, the stochasticity of the minibatches may dominate the error. However, large minibatches make the computation of the objective expensive, particularly the matrix inverses, which could be prohibitively expensive to compute.

Overall, I think the CMMD provides a nice way to model conditional distributions. The experiment results on classification and distillation of Bayesian models are also interesting. However, the presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 was not easy to follow, and I had to refer to the referenced papers to gain a deeper understanding of the material. There are a couple of minor issues: (1) on line 77, it is mentioned that the MMD estimator is unbiased, which is not accurate. (2) On line 90, the conditional embedding operator is not consistent with the one given in reference [29]. To obtain the form used in this paper, a different definition of the cross-covariance operator seems to be required.
