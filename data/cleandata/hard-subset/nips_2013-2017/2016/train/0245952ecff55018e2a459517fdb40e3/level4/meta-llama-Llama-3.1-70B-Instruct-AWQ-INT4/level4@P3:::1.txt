This paper develops a neural network approach based on a conditional maximum mean discrepancy criterion. The proposed technique is evaluated on standard benchmark tasks for both discriminative and generative capabilities. Overall, this is an interesting extension for the sub-area of deep learning research focused on building generative models.

The paper text is reasonably clear, although, like most papers in this area, the math can be a bit difficult to follow at times. The authors do a good job of explaining the underlying concepts and providing relevant background information, making the paper accessible to readers with a basic understanding of deep learning and generative models.

The tasks used in experiments are somewhat small but not entirely unreasonable when compared against other recent work in this area. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several datasets, including MNIST, SVHN, and Yale Face, and show that their model is competitive with state-of-the-art methods.

One potential concern is the computational cost of training the model. As the algorithm involves some computationally expensive steps, it would be helpful to include some comments in the experiment section on how long the model takes to train compared with existing approaches like DSN. This would provide a more complete understanding of the trade-offs involved in using this approach.

Additionally, making reference code available for the experiments would significantly strengthen the paper. As it stands, a practitioner may have difficulty reproducing the exact models evaluated in the paper, which could limit the impact of the work.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the authors have done a good job of addressing the criteria outlined in the NIPS review guidelines. The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors have also been careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, pending some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above. With some additional information on computational cost and the release of reference code, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of deep learning and generative models.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and interesting extension to the field of deep learning and generative models.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several benchmark tasks.
* The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the underlying concepts.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The computational cost of training the model is not well-documented.
* Reference code is not available, making it difficult for practitioners to reproduce the results.
* The tasks used in experiments are somewhat small compared to other recent work in this area.
