### Summary

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep generative model for learning conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The work is a direct and significant extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which focused on modeling joint distributions. The core technical contribution is the development of a novel training objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). CMMD measures the dissimilarity between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective kernel mean embedding operators in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This provides a principled, moment-matching-based criterion for training the network. The generative process involves feeding both the conditional variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` through a deep neural network to produce a sample `y`. The network's parameters are then optimized using backpropagation to minimize the CMMD objective between the model's output distribution and the true data distribution. The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN on a range of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (generating handwritten digits and faces conditioned on labels), and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Novel Objective Function:** The primary strength of this paper is the introduction of the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. It is a well-motivated and non-trivial extension of the MMD criterion to the conditional setting, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed model. This adds a valuable new tool to the family of moment-matching techniques for training deep generative models.

2.  **Stable Training Dynamic:** A key advantage of the moment-matching framework, inherited from GMMN and extended here, is its stable training objective. Unlike Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), which rely on a difficult-to-solve min-max optimization problem, CGMMN training involves minimizing a single, well-defined loss function. This avoids issues like mode collapse and training instability often associated with adversarial training.

3.  **Demonstrated Versatility:** The paper effectively showcases the flexibility of the CGMMN framework by applying it to three distinct and relevant problem domains: standard supervised learning (prediction), conditional generation, and a more novel application in model compression ("Bayesian dark knowledge"). This broad evaluation strengthens the claim that CGMMN is a general-purpose tool for conditional density estimation.

4.  **Competitive Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are solid. On the predictive tasks for MNIST and SVHN, CGMMN-CNN achieves error rates that are competitive with other state-of-the-art deep generative models like CMMVA. The qualitative results for conditional generation are convincing, showing that the model can generate diverse and realistic samples conditioned on class labels.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The CMMD objective, as formulated in Equation (2), requires the inversion of kernel Gram matrices (`(K + λI)⁻¹`), an operation that scales cubically with the mini-batch size (`O(B³)`). While the use of mini-batches makes training feasible, this is a significant computational bottleneck compared to models like Conditional VAEs or Conditional GANs, whose per-batch complexity is typically linear or quadratic in `B`. The paper would be much stronger if the authors included a discussion on the computational complexity and provided empirical comparisons of training times against key baselines (e.g., CMMVA, DSN). This is a critical practical consideration for potential users of this method.

2.  **Reproducibility:** While the paper describes the network architectures, crucial details for reproducing the results appear to be missing. For example, the choice of kernel functions and their parameters (e.g., bandwidth for an RBF kernel) for `x` and `y` variables in the CMMD calculation is not specified. The value of the regularization parameter `λ` is also not mentioned. A practitioner would likely find it difficult to replicate the experiments precisely without this information. Providing source code would significantly enhance the paper's impact and reproducibility.

3.  **Clarity of Mathematical Details:** The paper is reasonably clear, but the derivation of the empirical CMMD estimator could be elaborated. The jump from the definition of CMMD as the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of operator differences to the final matrix trace form in Equation (2) is quite dense. A more detailed derivation, perhaps in an appendix, would improve clarity and help readers fully grasp the mechanics of the objective function.

4.  **Scale of Experiments:** The chosen benchmarks (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Faces) are standard but relatively small in scale and complexity. While sufficient for a proof of concept, the generative modeling field is rapidly moving towards more challenging, higher-resolution datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA, LSUN). Demonstrating the model's generative capabilities on at least one of these more complex datasets would have provided a more compelling case for its significance and scalability.

### Final Assessment

Overall, this is a well-written and interesting paper that makes a solid contribution to the literature on deep generative models. The extension of the moment-matching framework to conditional distributions is a logical and valuable step forward. The proposed CMMD objective is theoretically sound and the model demonstrates strong performance across a variety of tasks. The primary concerns are the practical issues of computational scalability and the lack of detail needed for full reproducibility. Despite these weaknesses, the strengths of the paper—particularly its novel, stable, and versatile framework—make it a worthwhile contribution to the NIPS community.
