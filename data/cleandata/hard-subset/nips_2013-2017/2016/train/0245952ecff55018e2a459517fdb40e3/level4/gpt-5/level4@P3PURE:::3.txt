This paper proposes a loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables. The method extends generative moment matching networks (GMMN), which use an unconditional loss based on the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion. By leveraging conditional kernel mean embeddings, the authors derive a conditional loss and demonstrate results on several generative tasks, including classification, generating handwritten digits and faces, and distilling knowledge in Bayesian models.

This is my second time reviewing the paper. The previous round raised minor concerns—namely, why a low-dimensional space was used and whether the method scales to higher-dimensional latent spaces, the practical utility relative to alternatives, and various clarifications. Based on the current text, these points appear to have been addressed directly.

Technical clarity:
- Regarding Remark 1, the mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form is not clear. In particular, the dot notation is ambiguous: does it denote matrix multiplication or the Hadamard (elementwise) product? Please add more detail so the mapping is explicit.
- The choice of the symbol C is somewhat unfortunate given that C is already used to denote the conditional embedding. Consider using different notation to avoid confusion.
- Theorems 1 and 2 are tied to prior work, but Theorem 3 has no reference. A citation to a paper containing a proof should be provided, or a proof should be included.

Empirical evaluation:
- The results are decent. Although earlier reviewers noted that supervised learning might not be the most compelling application for this approach, the authors have argued that it serves to illustrate the model’s versatility; I find this acceptable.
- The distilled Bayesian inference application is interesting, but it is demonstrated on only one dataset with one model, so its broader utility remains unclear.
- This approach seems most likely to gain traction in pure generative modeling. The experiments here are comparable to those in the original GMMN paper. Using digits and faces is sufficient to demonstrate that the idea works, but broader adoption will likely require compelling results on more challenging datasets such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN.
