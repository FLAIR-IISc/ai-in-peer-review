This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMNs), a framework for learning conditional distributions with deep generative models. The core contribution is a novel training objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which extends the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) criterion used in unconditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMNs). Drawing upon the theory of kernel mean embeddings for conditional distributions, CMMD measures the distance between two conditional distributions in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The model itself is a deep neural network that takes both conditional variables and a source of random noise as input to generate samples. The parameters of this network are learned by minimizing the CMMD between the model's output distribution and the empirical data distribution, using stochastic gradient descent with backpropagation. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach on a range of tasks, including supervised classification, conditional image generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** This work presents a well-motivated and natural extension of the moment-matching framework for deep generative models. By introducing a conditional version (CGMMN), the authors significantly broaden the applicability of GMMNs, which were previously limited to modeling joint distributions. This fills an important gap in the literature, offering a compelling alternative to other conditional deep generative models like conditional GANs and conditional VAEs.

2.  **Methodological Simplicity:** A key advantage of the proposed framework is its conceptual elegance and the simplicity of its training procedure. Unlike the adversarial min-max optimization required for GANs, which can be unstable and difficult to tune, CGMMN training involves minimizing a single, well-defined CMMD objective. This optimization can be performed straightforwardly using standard backpropagation, making the model potentially more stable and easier to train.

3.  **Demonstrated Versatility:** The authors effectively showcase the model's flexibility by applying it to a diverse set of tasks: standard supervised prediction (classification on MNIST and SVHN), conditional generation of images (digits and faces), and the novel application of distilling a complex Bayesian model into a simpler student network. This breadth of experiments successfully highlights the general-purpose nature of the proposed framework.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Connection (Remark 1):** The connection drawn in Remark 1 between the CMMD objective (Equation 2) and a weighted MMD objective is insightful but lacks sufficient detail for the reader to fully grasp. The authors should explicitly define the notation used (e.g., is the dot product a standard matrix multiplication or a Hadamard product?) and provide a more thorough derivation to make the mapping transparent. Additionally, the reuse of the letter `C` for the weight matrices is potentially confusing, given that `C` also denotes the conditional embedding operator throughout the paper. Employing a different notation for the weight matrices would improve the paper's clarity.

2.  **Missing Proof or Citation for Theorem 3:** Theorem 3 is a cornerstone of the paper's theoretical foundation, as it states that the equality of conditional embedding operators implies the equality of the corresponding conditional distributions. However, this theorem is presented without a proof or a reference to prior work. For a claim of this importance, it is essential to either include a proof (e.g., in the appendix) or cite the established work from which this result is derived to ensure the paper is theoretically self-contained and sound.

3.  **Scope of Experimental Validation:** While the experiments demonstrate the model's potential, their scope could be expanded to make a more compelling case for its significance and practical utility.
    *   **Bayesian Distillation:** The application to "Bayesian dark knowledge" is intriguing. However, the evaluation is limited to a single regression dataset (Boston housing) with one teacher model (PBP). To convincingly establish the general utility of this approach, it would be beneficial to see results on additional datasets (e.g., classification tasks) and when distilling other types of Bayesian models.
    *   **Generative Modeling:** The generative modeling results on MNIST and the Yale Face dataset provide a solid proof-of-concept. However, to rigorously assess the significance and competitiveness of CGMMNs against state-of-the-art conditional generative models, evaluations on more complex and diverse datasets such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN are needed. Such experiments would be crucial for positioning the method as a strong alternative to established models and for encouraging its adoption by the community.

### **Summary and Recommendation**

This paper presents a solid and valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling. The proposed CGMMN is an elegant and well-motivated extension of the GMMN framework to the conditional setting. The primary weaknesses relate to minor issues in theoretical clarity that can be easily addressed and the need for a more extensive experimental validation on challenging benchmarks to fully demonstrate the model's practical impact. These points do not detract from the core quality of the idea. The paper is well-written and the proposed method is a technically sound and useful addition to the toolbox of deep generative models.
