

This paper introduces conditional generative moment-matching networks (CGMMN), which extend generative moment-matching networks (GMMN) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core contribution lies in the development of conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the discrepancy between conditional distributions using kernel mean embedding of conditional distributions. CGMMN was evaluated across classification, conditional generation, and distillation of Bayesian models, demonstrating competitive performance in all tasks.

The authors address a significant limitation of the naive approach to conditional generation—estimating a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution—which suffers from severe data sparsity, especially when conditioning variables are continuous (where each conditional distribution may have only one example). Instead, CGMMN treats all conditional distributions as a family and directly matches the model with the conditional embedding operator, avoiding per-condition distribution estimation. While this approach seems theoretically sound, it would be valuable to include a comparison with the naive approach as a baseline, particularly for cases where conditioning variables take discrete values (e.g., 10 classes in MNIST), as the naive method might still perform reasonably well in such settings.

The CMMD objective is well-formulated and computationally efficient, with the key advantage that all kernel matrices in Equation (2) are independent of model parameters, enabling straightforward backpropagation. This similarity to the unconditional MMD framework, as noted in Remark 1, is a significant practical benefit. However, a potential limitation is that CMMD may require large minibatches to avoid stochasticity dominating the error, which could make computation expensive due to the need for matrix inverses, particularly with larger datasets.

The experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are promising and demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. The presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1, however, would benefit from clearer explanations, as the technical details required consulting referenced papers to fully understand the methodology. Additionally, two minor technical issues should be addressed: (1) the claim on line 77 that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect, and (2) the conditional embedding operator definition on line 90 is inconsistent with the formulation in reference [29], suggesting a need for clarification regarding the cross-covariance operator definition used in this work. Overall, CGMMN provides a valuable extension to moment-matching networks for conditional modeling with a sound theoretical foundation and practical implementation.
