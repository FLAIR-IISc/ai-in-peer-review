

The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to model conditional distributions via conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). Building on Song et al.'s kernel embedding framework for conditional distributions [29], CGMMN learns a generative model \(y = f(x, h | \theta)\) where \(h \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)^d\) and \(x\) is the conditioning variable. The authors formalize CMMD as \(\| \hat{C}_{Y|X}^d - \hat{C}_{Y|X}^s \|^2_{\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{G}}\) to match empirical conditional distributions, enabling training via backpropagation. The method is evaluated on predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing).

**Strengths:**  
The conditional extension of GMMN is conceptually clean and technically well-motivated. The CMMD criterion properly adapts MMD to conditional settings via kernel mean embeddings, avoiding the need for explicit density modeling. The generative architecture is straightforward—combining input \(x\) with latent noise \(h\) through a deep network—and the training procedure (Algorithm 1) is practical. The experiments cover diverse applications, and the Bayesian dark knowledge application (Section 4.3) is particularly novel, demonstrating that CGMMN can distill predictive distributions from Bayesian models without performance degradation (Table 3). The theoretical grounding in RKHS embeddings is sound, and the paper properly references related work on conditional kernel embeddings [29, 32].

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Technical gap in \(\hat{C}_{Y|X}\) definition:** The definition of \(\hat{C}_{Y|X}\) (Line 90) omits terms involving \(\mu_X\) and \(\mu_Y\) (or their estimators), which are standard in the cross-covariance operator formulation [29]. The assumption that \(\mu_X = \mu_Y = 0\) (data centered in RKHS) is unusual and requires justification. Since \(\hat{C}_{Y|X}\) is central to the method, this omission affects gradient computation and experimental validity. The authors should clarify how this assumption impacts implementation and whether it introduces bias.

2. **Limited experimental comparison:** The predictive performance (Tables 1-2) shows CGMMN-CNN achieving 3.13% error on SVHN versus CMMVA’s 3.09% and DSN’s 1.92%, yet the paper does not compare CGMMN against conditional generative models like cGAN [21] or conditional VAEs [28]. Without such comparisons, it is unclear whether CGMMN generates higher-quality samples than established methods (e.g., Fig. 2 shows noisy MNIST samples; Fig. 4 improves with autoencoders, but this is not compared to cGAN). The generative results lack quantitative metrics (e.g., FID scores) for objective evaluation.

3. **Understated limitations:** The paper claims competitive performance but overlooks that CGMMN’s predictive errors are consistently higher than SOTA (e.g., +1.2% on SVHN vs. CMMVA). The generative experiments use simple MLPs, while cGANs [21] achieve sharper samples with similar architectures. The absence of ablation studies (e.g., impact of latent dimension \(d\)) also weakens the evaluation.

**Typos and Clarity Issues:**  
Minor but distracting errors include "Frobeniu norm" (Line 48), "an universal" (Line 65), and "which the" (Line 238). The phrase "a large network" (Line 37) should be "large networks" for grammatical correctness.

**Overall Assessment:**  
CGMMN is a technically sound extension of GMMN to conditional distributions, with clear theoretical foundations and promising applications. However, the omission in \(\hat{C}_{Y|X}\) and the lack of rigorous comparisons against state-of-the-art conditional generative models (e.g., cGAN) undermine the paper’s claims of competitiveness. The experimental results are not sufficiently compelling to justify acceptance over existing methods. While the Bayesian dark knowledge application is novel, the predictive and generative performance falls short of the strongest baselines.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel conditional extension of MMD-based generative models; theoretically grounded; successful application to Bayesian distillation.  
*Con:* Critical technical gap in \(\hat{C}_{Y|X}\) definition; weak experimental comparisons; predictive performance lags behind SOTA.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper addresses an important gap in conditional generative modeling but requires significant revisions to resolve the technical ambiguity in \(\hat{C}_{Y|X}\) and provide fair comparisons against cGAN/conditional VAEs. As written, the evidence for competitive performance is insufficient. Given the authors’ prompt rebuttal addressing the \(\hat{C}_{Y|X}\) issue, I would support acceptance if these concerns are resolved in the camera-ready version. For now, I recommend rejection pending clarifications.
