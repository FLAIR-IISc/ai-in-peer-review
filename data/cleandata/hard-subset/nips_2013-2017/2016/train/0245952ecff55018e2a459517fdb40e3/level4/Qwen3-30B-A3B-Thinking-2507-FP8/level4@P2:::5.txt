

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions. The authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to measure the distance between empirical conditional distributions and generative models, enabling training via back-propagation. The approach builds upon kernel mean embedding techniques for conditional distributions, as pioneered by Song et al. (2009), and extends the GMMN framework to address predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution by formalizing conditional kernel embeddings and demonstrating how they can be leveraged for conditional generation. The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with careful attention to the mathematical properties of conditional moment matching. The authors correctly identify that conditional distributions are crucial for many applications, including predictive modeling (where p(y|x) is more direct than p(x,y)), contextual generation (e.g., class-conditional image generation), and building hierarchical generative models. The paper's connection to recent work on conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional variational autoencoders (Soň et al., 2015) is appropriate, though the relationship to conditional maximum mean discrepancy techniques (Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012) could be more explicitly discussed.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality through its rigorous derivation of CMMD and the conditional embedding operator. The mathematical presentation is mostly clear, though a minor error appears in Equation (1) where the conditional embedding operator definition is missing the cross-covariance term involving μ_X and μ_Y (the mean embeddings of the input distribution). This omission is critical as the conditional embedding operator C_{Y|X} = C_{YX}C_{XX}^{-1} relies on properly centered data. While the authors note that in practice the empirical estimator Φ(K+λI)^{-1}Υ^T is used, the theoretical foundation would be strengthened by clarifying how this addresses the missing terms, particularly for gradient computation.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-structured sections that progressively build the theoretical foundation. The experimental design is appropriate for the claims made, though the presentation of results could be improved. The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN datasets), contextual generation (MNIST and Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing regression). The experimental setup is well-documented, with sufficient detail about network architectures and hyperparameters.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses an important gap in the literature by extending moment-matching networks to conditional settings. The ability to model conditional distributions without the min-max optimization challenges of GANs is theoretically appealing and has practical utility for applications requiring conditional generation. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of direct comparison with state-of-the-art conditional generative models, particularly conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), which have been widely adopted for conditional generation tasks.

The originality of the work is moderate. While the conditional extension of GMMN is novel, the approach builds upon well-established kernel embedding techniques and follows the pattern of extending GANs to conditional settings. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on moment-matching networks, but the novelty is incremental rather than transformative. The authors would strengthen the originality claim by more thoroughly contrasting their approach with related conditional generative models.

The experimental results present a mixed picture. For predictive modeling, CGMMN (both MLP and CNN variants) achieves competitive results but falls short of state-of-the-art methods like CMMVA and DSN on both MNIST and SVHN. For contextual generation, the samples appear reasonable but lack comparison with alternative conditional generative models. The Bayesian dark knowledge distillation experiment shows promise but is limited to a single regression task. The paper's claim that CGMMN "obtains competitive performance in all these tasks" is somewhat overstated given the comparative results presented.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical foundation for conditional moment matching is sound and well-developed
2. The paper correctly identifies the importance of conditional distributions for practical applications
3. The experimental design covers a diverse set of applications
4. The approach maintains the simplicity of training via back-propagation

Weaknesses:
1. The theoretical derivation of the conditional embedding operator contains a critical omission
2. The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over state-of-the-art methods
3. Lack of comparison with conditional GANs and other contemporary conditional generative models
4. Some grammatical errors (e.g., "Frobeniu norm" should be "Frobenius norm")
5. The significance claim is overstated given the comparative results

The paper represents a technically sound contribution that extends the GMMN framework to conditional settings. While the approach is not revolutionary, it provides a valuable addition to the toolkit of conditional generative models. The work would be significantly strengthened by addressing the theoretical gap in the conditional embedding definition, providing more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art conditional generative models, and tempering the claims about performance competitiveness.

In summary, the paper makes a legitimate contribution to the field of generative modeling, with solid technical foundations but limited experimental evidence to demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The theoretical contribution is significant, but the practical significance is somewhat limited by the experimental results. With minor revisions addressing the theoretical gap and experimental comparisons, this paper could be an acceptable contribution to NIPS.
