This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), building upon Generative Moment Matching Networks to address conditional generation and prediction tasks. A core contribution lies in the use of kernel embeddings to represent conditional distributions, coupled with a conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric for evaluating differences between these distributions. The method is evaluated across classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation. A straightforward extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would involve estimating a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters via a common neural network. However, this approach suffers from data sparsity, particularly with continuous conditioning variables where individual conditional distributions may be supported by limited data. The proposed CGMMN instead treats the set of conditional distributions as a unified family, directly matching the model to the conditional embedding operator rather than individual distributions. While the benefits of this approach are apparent, the simpler, per-conditional-distribution approach can perform adequately in certain scenarios – for instance, conditional generation with a limited number of conditioning variable values, such as the 10 digits in MNIST. Including this as a baseline comparison would be valuable. The paper presents a well-formulated conditional MMD objective (Remark 1) that can be computed efficiently, mirroring the unconditional case, and leverages the independence of the K matrices in equation 2 from model parameters to enable standard backpropagation. A potential limitation is the need for large minibatches to mitigate stochastic error in the CMMD objective; however, larger batches increase computational cost, especially regarding the matrix inversions. Despite this, the CMMD offers a compelling framework for modeling conditional distributions, and the experimental results demonstrating its application to classification and Bayesian model distillation are promising. The explanations in sections 2.3 and 3.1 were somewhat difficult to follow, requiring reference to external papers for full comprehension. There are also a few minor technical issues: (1) the claim of an unbiased MMD estimator on line 77 is inaccurate, and (2) the conditional embedding operator on line 90 does not align with the definition in reference [29], seemingly requiring a revised definition of the cross-covariance operator to achieve the paper’s formulation.
