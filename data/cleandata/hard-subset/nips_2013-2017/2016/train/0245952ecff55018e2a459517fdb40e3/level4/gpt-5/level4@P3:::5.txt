Summary
This paper extends Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMNs) to the conditional setting. The key idea is to represent conditional distributions via conditional kernel mean embeddings and to learn a conditional generator by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The generator takes conditioning variables x and noise h as input to a deep network y = f(x, h; w), and training proceeds by back-propagation through a closed-form CMMD estimate expressed with Gram matrices and their regularized inverses. The approach is evaluated on (1) predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), (2) contextual conditional generation (MNIST digits, Yale Faces), and (3) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge”).

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on: (i) MMD for training deep generators without likelihoods (Li et al., 2015; Dziugaite et al., 2015); and (ii) conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012), which provide a nonparametric representation of P(Y|X) as a linear operator in RKHS. It complements other conditional deep generative models such as conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), and supervised/max-margin DGMs (Li et al., 2015) by offering a stable, single-objective training criterion that avoids adversarial min–max games and amortized variational bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clean and principled extension of MMD training to conditional distributions via CMMD, with a sufficient condition (Theorem 3) for equality of conditional distributions when conditional embedding operators match.
- Practicality: Training reduces to kernel Gram computations on x and y and back-propagation through the generator; the inverses of Gram matrices on x do not depend on model parameters, simplifying gradients.
- Generality: The method is architecture-agnostic (MLP/CNN), handles both discrete and continuous conditioning variables, and is applicable to conditional generation and prediction.
- Applications: Showcases diverse uses, including Bayesian model distillation, where CGMMN approximates a teacher’s predictive distribution without expensive posterior sampling at test time.

Weaknesses and questions
- Estimator clarity (addressed in rebuttal): In the empirical estimator of the conditional embedding operator ĈY|X, the manuscript uses Φ(K + λI)−1Υ⊤. In conditional embedding theory, cross-covariance operators include mean terms. Please clarify in the final version that the adopted estimator is the standard regularized conditional mean operator obtained via kernel ridge regression of Y-features on X-features; it does not require explicit centering of φ(X) and φ(Y), and is consistent with Song et al. (2009). Also clarify why centering terms do not affect gradients (the K̃−1 factors are constant w.r.t. w).
- Comparative evaluation for conditional generation: The paper lacks quantitative or side-by-side comparisons to conditional GANs and conditional VAEs on the same tasks. Nearest-neighbor visual checks are informative but limited. Even small-scale FID/Inception-style scores (or task-specific metrics in 2016) or human preference studies would strengthen claims.
- Predictive performance: On MNIST and SVHN, CGMMN underperforms strong discriminative baselines and also lags behind the max-margin DGM (CMMVA). It would help to analyze whether the gap is due to kernel choices, batch sizes (cubic cost in batch), or architectural capacity, and whether CMMD brings complementary benefits (e.g., calibrated uncertainty).
- Scalability/sensitivity: The cubic dependence on minibatch size for Gram inverses is a practical limitation. Discussion of low-rank approximations, random feature approximations, or blockwise estimators would be helpful. An ablation on kernel choices for x and y, λ-regularization, and noise dimensionality would clarify robustness.
- Presentation: A few typos and minor grammatical issues (e.g., “Frobeniu norm,” “an universal”) should be corrected. Some derivations could benefit from pointers to appendices when assumptions (e.g., existence of CY|X in F ⊗ G) are invoked.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and grounded in established RKHS theory. The CMMD objective is derived cleanly and optimized with standard deep learning techniques. Empirical evaluations are competent, but the predictive experiments do not surpass the best discriminative baselines, and the conditional generation benchmarks would benefit from stronger comparisons and metrics. The Bayesian distillation example is appealing; the distilled model matches the teacher’s RMSE on Boston Housing, suggesting practical utility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, with clear statements of preliminaries (MMD, conditional embeddings) and the CMMD objective. The estimator and its relation to centered operators needed clarification (now addressed in rebuttal). Including a brief gradient sketch and a complexity note for minibatch CMMD improves readability.

Originality
Extending MMD training to conditional distributions via the operator norm between conditional mean embeddings is a natural but nontrivial step. To my knowledge, this is the first deep conditional generator trained with CMMD, distinct from adversarial and variational approaches.

Significance
The contribution broadens the applicability of moment-matching generators to conditional tasks. While current predictive accuracy does not set a new SOTA, the approach offers a stable alternative to adversarial training and a practical tool for distilling Bayesian models. With stronger empirical comparisons and scalability tweaks, it could have sustained impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and principled conditional MMD objective with theoretical justification.
- Simple, stable training; compatible with standard deep nets.
- Diverse applications including Bayesian distillation; competitive results.

Cons for acceptance
- Predictive performance lags behind strong baselines.
- Missing quantitative comparisons to cGAN/CVAE for conditional generation.
- Computational costs of kernel matrices may limit scale without approximation.

Update after rebuttal
The authors clarified the conditional operator estimator, explaining that their Φ(K + λI)−1Υ⊤ form is the standard regularized conditional mean embedding (kernel ridge regression view) and does not require explicit centering; they also clarified how centering terms do not affect gradients since K̃−1 is constant w.r.t. network parameters. Typos will be fixed. They provided additional discussion on comparisons and practicalities. With these clarifications, my concerns are addressed, and I raise my overall assessment.
