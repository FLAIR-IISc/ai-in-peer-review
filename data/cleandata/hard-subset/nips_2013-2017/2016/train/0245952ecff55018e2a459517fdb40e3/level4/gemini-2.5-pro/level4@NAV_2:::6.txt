This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the GMMN framework designed to model conditional distributions. The core contribution is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective, which leverages kernel embeddings of conditional distributions to measure the discrepancy between the model's output distribution and the true data distribution, conditioned on a set of input variables. This approach elegantly addresses the data sparsity issue that would arise from a naive extension of GMMN, where one might learn a separate model for each conditioning value, a strategy that is particularly problematic for continuous conditioning variables. The proposed CGMMN is evaluated on a diverse range of tasks, including predictive modeling, contextual image generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper's main strength lies in its principled and theoretically grounded method for extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. By matching the conditional embedding operators directly, the model effectively shares statistical strength across all conditioning contexts.
*   The CMMD objective function is well-formulated and represents an intuitive extension of the standard MMD, as highlighted in Remark 1. The fact that the kernel matrices involving the conditioning variables are independent of the model parameters allows for straightforward optimization via back-propagation, similar to the original GMMN.
*   The experiments are comprehensive and showcase the model's versatility. The application to classification, conditional generation, and particularly the novel use for distilling Bayesian models, effectively demonstrates the broad applicability of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by the inclusion of a more direct baseline. For tasks with discrete conditioning variables, such as MNIST generation, a comparison against a simpler approach—for example, training ten separate GMMNs with shared parameters—would help to empirically validate the benefits of the more sophisticated CMMD operator-matching approach.
*   There is a potential concern regarding the computational complexity and scalability of the CMMD objective. The estimation of the conditional embedding operator may require large minibatches to ensure stable gradients, yet the cost of computing the objective, especially the matrix inversions, scales poorly with the minibatch size. A discussion on this trade-off between statistical stability and computational feasibility would be valuable.
*   The clarity of the presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 could be improved. The concepts of conditional mean embeddings and the CMMD objective are introduced very concisely, making them difficult to fully grasp without prior expertise or consulting the cited references. Providing more intuition behind the formulations would enhance the paper's accessibility.
*   There are a couple of minor technical inaccuracies that should be addressed:
    1.  On line 77, the paper claims the MMD estimator is unbiased, which is incorrect for the commonly used squared MMD V-statistic estimator.
    2.  The form of the conditional embedding operator presented on line 90 does not appear to directly match the one in the cited reference [29]. This might stem from a different definition of the cross-covariance operator and should be clarified.

Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution by providing a theoretically sound and practical method for conditional density estimation with moment-matching networks. The CMMD objective is an elegant solution to a significant problem, and the experimental results are promising. Addressing the concerns about experimental comparisons and improving the clarity of the technical exposition would further strengthen the paper.
