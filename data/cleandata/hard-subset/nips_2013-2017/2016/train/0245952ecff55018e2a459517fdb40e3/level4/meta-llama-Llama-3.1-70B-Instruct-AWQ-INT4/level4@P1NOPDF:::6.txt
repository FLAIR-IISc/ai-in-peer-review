This paper introduces conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMNs), an extension of generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction applications. The core of the proposed method lies in the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the conditional maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) metric for measuring the discrepancy between conditional distributions. The authors evaluate the proposed CGMMN on classification, conditional generation, and distilling Bayesian models.

A naive approach to extending GMMNs to the conditional setting would be to estimate a GMMN for each conditional distribution, with all these conditional distributions sharing parameters through the use of the same neural network. However, this approach has a significant drawback: each conditional distribution often has only a few examples, and in the case of continuous conditioning variables, each conditional distribution may have only a single example, resulting in a data sparsity problem. In contrast, the proposed approach treats all the conditional distributions as a family and attempts to match the model with the conditional embedding operator directly, rather than matching each individual conditional distribution. The advantage of the proposed approach seems clear, but in some cases, the naive approach can still achieve reasonable results, for instance, in conditional generation where the conditioning variable takes one of a limited number of values, such as in MNIST. It would be interesting to compare the proposed approach to this naive approach as a baseline.

The conditional MMD objective has a nice formulation and can be easily computed almost in the same way as the unconditional MMD, as pointed out in Remark 1. Since all the K matrices in equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, backpropagation can be performed in the same way as before. One concern about the proposed approach is that the CMMD objective may require large minibatches; otherwise, the stochasticity of the minibatches may dominate the error. On the other hand, large minibatches make the computation of the objective expensive, particularly the matrix inverses, which could be prohibitively expensive to compute.

Overall, I think the CMMD provides a nice way to model conditional distributions. The experiment results on classification and distillation of Bayesian models are also interesting. However, the presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 was not easy to follow, and I had to dive into the referenced papers to understand the details. There are a couple of minor issues: (1) on line 77, it is mentioned that the MMD estimator is unbiased, which is not true. (2) on line 90, the conditional embedding operator was not consistent with the one given in reference [29]. To get the form used in this paper, it seems we need a different definition of the cross-covariance operator.
