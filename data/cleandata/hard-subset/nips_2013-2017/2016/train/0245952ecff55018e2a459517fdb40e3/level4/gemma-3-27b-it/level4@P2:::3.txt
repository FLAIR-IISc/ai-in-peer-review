This paper introduces conditional generative moment-matching networks (CGMMN) as a novel approach to learning conditional distributions, extending the capabilities of generative moment matching networks (GMMN). The core idea leverages conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD), building upon existing work in kernel mean embeddings, to define a loss function suitable for directed generative models. The authors demonstrate the method’s effectiveness across a range of tasks—predictive modeling, contextual generation (digit and face generation), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation—showing competitive performance in each. This is a resubmission, and the authors have addressed previous reviewer concerns regarding latent space dimensionality and model utility.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the common need for conditional generative models in tasks beyond joint distribution estimation. The extension from GMMN to CGMMN is a logical progression, offering a potentially simpler alternative to approaches like GANs, while retaining the benefits of deep learning. The experimental validation is commendable in its breadth, covering diverse applications. The results on MNIST and Yale Face datasets are solid and demonstrate the viability of the proposed method. The application to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation is particularly interesting, suggesting a practical use case beyond typical generative modeling.

However, a few points require further clarification. While Remark 1 connects CMMD to MMD, the mapping from Equation (2) to a “weighted MMD form” remains unclear. Explicitly stating whether the dot notation represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product would significantly improve readability. The authors should provide more detail to make this connection more intuitive. The use of ‘C’ to denote both the conditional embedding operator and in other contexts creates potential for confusion; a different notation might be preferable. Theorem 3 lacks a citation. Providing a reference to a source containing the proof, or including the proof itself within the paper, is crucial for completeness and rigor. 

While the results are competitive, the evaluation is somewhat limited. The Bayesian dark knowledge distillation experiment utilizes only one dataset and model configuration. Expanding this evaluation to other datasets and Bayesian models would strengthen the claim of its general applicability. Although the authors acknowledge that applying CGMMN to supervised learning may not be its strongest suit, focusing on more challenging generative modeling datasets like CIFAR-10 or LSUN would showcase the method’s scalability and potential for broader impact.  

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extends GMMN to conditional distributions, offering a new approach to generative modeling.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Builds on established concepts like kernel mean embeddings and MMD, providing a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Versatility:** Demonstrated effectiveness across multiple tasks, including classification, generation, and knowledge distillation.
*   **Clarity (Improved):** Addresses previous reviewer comments, resulting in a more understandable presentation.
*   **Competitive Performance:** Achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art methods on the tested datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Clarity in Remark 1:** The connection between Equation (2) and weighted MMD is not sufficiently explained.
*   **Missing Citation:** Theorem 3 requires a reference or proof.
*   **Limited Evaluation of Distillation:** The Bayesian dark knowledge distillation experiment lacks broader validation.
*   **Dataset Scope:** The datasets used, while adequate, might not fully demonstrate the scalability of the approach.
*   **Potential Notation Confusion:** Overuse of ‘C’ can create ambiguity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of GMMN.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the versatility and competitiveness of the proposed method.
*   The authors have addressed previous reviewer concerns effectively.
*   The work contributes to the growing body of research on generative modeling and offers a potentially valuable alternative to existing techniques.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of clarity in certain sections (Remark 1) and the missing citation (Theorem 3) detract from the paper’s rigor.
*   The limited scope of the evaluation, particularly for the Bayesian dark knowledge distillation experiment, weakens the claims of general applicability.
*   Without evaluation on more complex datasets, the potential for wider adoption remains uncertain.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is **borderline accept/reject**. The core idea is novel and the results are promising. However, addressing the identified weaknesses – particularly clarifying the mathematical details, providing a citation for Theorem 3, and expanding the evaluation – would significantly strengthen the paper and justify acceptance. I recommend the authors carefully address these points before final consideration.
