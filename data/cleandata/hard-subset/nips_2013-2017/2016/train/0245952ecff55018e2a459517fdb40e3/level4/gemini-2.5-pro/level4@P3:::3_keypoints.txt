- Paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) that learn p(y|x) via a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective using kernel mean embeddings; model is a neural net taking x and noise h to generate y and is trained by minimizing CMMD, demonstrated on classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.

- Strength: Novel and significant extension of GMMN to conditional settings, offering a non-adversarial alternative to conditional GANs/VAEs that may yield more stable training.

- Strength: Versatile framework applied to supervised classification, contextual image generation, and Bayesian “dark knowledge” distillation, with the distillation experiment a particularly practical use case.

- Strength: CMMD objective is well-grounded in kernel embedding theory and builds cleanly on established prior work.

- Weakness: Remark 1’s claimed connection between CMMD and a weighted MMD is unclear—notation (e.g., Tr(Ld · 1)) and the role of C for weight matrices are ambiguous and need a clearer derivation.

- Weakness: Theorem 3 (equality of conditional embedding operators implies equality of conditional distributions) is stated without proof or citation and should be proven or referenced.

- Weakness: Experimental scope is limited—Bayesian distillation is only tested on Boston housing with one Bayesian model (PBP), so generality is unclear.

- Weakness: Generative modeling experiments are limited to MNIST and Yale Faces; results on more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, LSUN) are needed to demonstrate scalability and competitiveness with state-of-the-art conditional GANs.

- Weakness: Supervised/classification application is weak since generative classifiers lag discriminative ones; the paper could reallocate that space to strengthen novel experiments.

- Conclusion: CGMMN is an elegant, theoretically sound extension of moment-matching networks, but clarity on theoretical details and more ambitious experimental validation are needed to fully demonstrate impact.
