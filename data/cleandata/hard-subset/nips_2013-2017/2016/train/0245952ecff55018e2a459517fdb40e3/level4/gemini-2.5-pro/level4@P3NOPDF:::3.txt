This paper proposes a novel loss function for training directed generative models with conditional variables. The method is a principled extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs), which use an unconditional loss function based on the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) criterion. By leveraging the work on conditional kernel mean embeddings, the authors develop a conditional MMD loss suitable for this setting. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated on a variety of tasks, including supervised classification, generating handwritten digits and faces, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

Having reviewed a previous version of this manuscript, I am pleased to see that the authors have directly addressed the minor issues raised by the reviewers. These included questions about the use of a low-dimensional latent space, the model's scalability, its utility compared to other approaches, and other clarifying points. The current revision is substantially improved as a result.

However, there are still several areas that would benefit from further clarification and strengthening.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Connection:** In Remark 1, the connection between Equation (2) and the weighted MMD form is not immediately obvious. The derivation would be more accessible if the authors provided a more detailed explanation of how to map from one to the other. Specifically, the "dot notation" used is ambiguous; it is unclear whether it represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product. Clarifying this notation and expanding on the steps would significantly improve the reader's understanding of this key formulation.

2.  **Theoretical Justification:** While Theorems 1 and 2 are properly supported by references to existing work, Theorem 3 is presented without a citation or a proof. For a central theoretical claim, it is crucial to either include a proof in the appendix or provide a reference to a paper containing the proof.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical results are decent and demonstrate that the proposed method is viable. However, the evaluation could be more compelling.
    *   **Supervised Learning:** The authors justify the application to supervised learning as a way to highlight the model's versatility. While this is a reasonable point, this application is unlikely to be the primary reason researchers would adopt this model over specialized classifiers.
    *   **Distilled Bayesian Inference:** The application to knowledge distillation is interesting and novel. However, its evaluation on a single dataset with a single model makes it difficult to gauge its generalizability. Showing that this technique is useful across a wider range of models and datasets would make this contribution much stronger.
    *   **Generative Modeling:** The performance on generative modeling tasks appears comparable to that of the original GMMN paper. The datasets used (handwritten digits and faces) are sufficient to demonstrate proof-of-concept. However, to truly showcase the advantages of this conditional framework and drive wider adoption, the method needs to be tested on more challenging, higher-dimensional datasets such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN. Compelling results on such benchmarks would provide a much stronger signal of the method's practical utility.

**Minor Issues**

*   The choice of the variable `C` for the weighting matrix in Remark 1 is unfortunate, as `C` is already used to define the conditional embedding operator. Using a different symbol would prevent potential confusion and improve the paper's readability.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically well-motivated and valuable extension of MMD-based training to conditional generative models. The authors have diligently addressed the concerns from the previous review cycle. The primary remaining weaknesses lie in the clarity of some theoretical details and the ambition of the empirical evaluation. By providing the missing proof or reference for Theorem 3, clarifying the derivation in Remark 1, and benchmarking the model on more complex datasets, the authors could significantly strengthen the paper and its potential impact on the field. The core idea is strong, and with these improvements, the paper would represent a solid contribution.
