

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions. The authors develop a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for learning conditional distributions, building on the kernel mean embedding approach from GMMN. While GMMN learns unconditional distributions using MMD, CGMMN enables conditional generation by measuring the difference between kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions. The authors demonstrate their approach on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, showing competitive performance across these tasks.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with strong theoretical grounding. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for CMMD (Theorem 3) and demonstrate how the conditional embedding operator can be estimated empirically. The derivation of the CMMD objective (Equation 2) and its implementation in Algorithm 1 is well-justified, showing how the conditional nature of the problem can be addressed without resorting to computationally intensive per-condition evaluations. The paper's technical approach is consistent with the kernel embedding literature (Gretton et al., JMLR 2012; Song et al., ICML 2009), while addressing a significant gap in the GMMN framework.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The writing is precise and well-structured, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and the proposed methodology. The authors effectively use figures to illustrate the generative process (Fig. 1) and the quality of generated samples (Figs. 2-5). The experimental section is thorough, with detailed descriptions of the architectures, hyperparameters, and evaluation metrics for each task. The appendix provides additional implementation details that would be valuable for reproducibility. The paper is accessible to readers familiar with deep learning and kernel methods, though some readers might benefit from a more explicit comparison to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Soň et al., 2015) in the main text.

In terms of novelty, the paper makes a meaningful extension to the GMMN framework. While conditional generative models have been explored in other contexts (e.g., conditional GANs, conditional VAEs), the authors' approach to incorporating conditioning into the moment-matching framework is both novel and well-motivated. The key insight—using the kernel embedding of conditional distributions to define a tractable objective for learning—provides a simpler alternative to the min-max optimization in conditional GANs. The derivation of the CMMD objective (Section 3.1) is particularly noteworthy, as it addresses the challenge of continuous conditioning variables that would require separate MMD tests for each condition value. This represents a clear technical advance over the unconditional GMMN framework (Li et al., ICML 2015).

The significance of the work is substantial. The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN across multiple application domains: predictive modeling (Tables 1-2), contextual generation (Figs. 2-5), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Table 3). For predictive modeling, CGMMN-CNN achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art discriminative models like DSN (Table 2) without requiring data augmentation. For contextual generation, the model produces high-quality samples conditioned on labels (Fig. 2), and the authors provide convincing evidence that the samples are not merely copies of training data (Fig. 3). The Bayesian dark knowledge application is particularly interesting, as it demonstrates how CGMMN can efficiently distill knowledge from Bayesian models without significant performance degradation (Table 3).

The paper's main weakness is the limited comparison to the most recent conditional generative models. While the authors compare to conditional GAN variants (CMMVA) and discriminative models, they could strengthen their position by including more recent conditional generative approaches like conditional VAEs (Soň et al., 2015) or conditional normalizing flows. The authors also note that "CGMMN is compatible with various network architectures" (Section 4.1.2), but they don't explore how architectural choices affect performance beyond the MLP/CNN comparison.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to deep generative modeling. It successfully extends the GMMN framework to conditional distributions with a theoretically sound and practically implementable approach. The experimental results across multiple domains demonstrate the framework's versatility and competitiveness with state-of-the-art methods. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. I believe this work would be of significant interest to the NIPS community, particularly to researchers working on deep generative models, probabilistic modeling, and applications requiring conditional generation.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical extension of GMMN to conditional distributions
- Well-justified derivation of CMMD objective
- Competitive performance across multiple application domains
- Excellent writing and organization
- Practical implementation with Algorithm 1

Weaknesses:
- Limited comparison to recent conditional generative models
- Insufficient exploration of architectural choices beyond MLP/CNN
- Limited discussion of theoretical limitations of the CMMD approach

I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to the field of deep generative modeling.
