

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions. The authors develop a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to learn conditional distributions, enabling applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation. The approach builds on recent advances in moment matching networks [Li et al., 2015] and conditional kernel embeddings [Song et al., 2009], representing a meaningful extension to the GMMN framework.

The paper makes several contributions. First, it formulates a theoretically grounded CMMD criterion for conditional distributions, showing that matching conditional embedding operators ensures equivalence of conditional distributions. Second, it proposes a practical generative architecture that takes both conditional inputs and random noise as inputs to produce target samples. Third, it demonstrates competitive performance across multiple tasks including image classification (MNIST, SVHN), image generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian model distillation (Boston Housing).

The strengths of the paper include a well-motivated extension to conditional distributions, a clean generative architecture that enables simple backpropagation-based training, and comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse tasks. The approach successfully addresses three important application scenarios: predictive modeling (where conditional distributions are more efficient than joint modeling), contextual generation (where samples are conditioned on specific attributes), and Bayesian dark knowledge (where knowledge is distilled from Bayesian models). The experiments show that CGMMN achieves competitive results compared to strong baselines like CMMVA [Li et al., 2015], DSN [Lee et al., 2015], and Network in Network [Lin et al., 2014].

However, the paper has significant weaknesses in clarity and presentation that undermine its contribution. The mathematical formulation (Section 3.1) begins with highly technical Hilbert space embeddings and kernel operators without providing intuitive motivation for the conditional extension. The non-conditional MMD formulation in GMMN [Li et al., 2015] is explained intuitively without heavy mathematical machinery, but the conditional extension lacks this accessibility. The authors should explain how the CMMD formula (equation 2) arises from practical considerations rather than presenting it as a mathematical necessity.

The paper also fails to clearly connect the theoretical CMMD objective to the practical implementation. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented without explaining how the CMMD computation translates to the actual gradient updates. The reader is left wondering: How exactly are the conditional embeddings computed? What is the computational cost of the CMMD objective compared to standard MMD? How do the kernel matrices (Kd, Ks, Ld, Ls) relate to the network architecture? The supplementary material contains some details, but these should be integrated into the main text to improve clarity.

A critical weakness is the lack of discussion about computational efficiency. The CMMD objective involves computing inverse matrices (K̃⁻¹s and K̃⁻¹d) that scale cubically with sample size, which is prohibitive for large datasets. The paper mentions a mini-batch version (Algorithm 1) but doesn't quantify the computational savings or explain how the mini-batch approach affects the theoretical guarantees. Without this discussion, it's unclear whether CGMMN is practically feasible for large-scale applications.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Providing intuitive motivation for the CMMD formula before presenting the mathematical derivation
2. Explicitly mapping the CMMD objective to the practical gradient computation
3. Discussing computational efficiency and scalability considerations
4. Clarifying the relationship between the theoretical assumptions and practical implementation

The authors should answer these questions: Why does the conditional embedding operator need to be matched rather than the conditional distributions directly? How would you explain the CMMD criterion to someone unfamiliar with kernel methods? What are the practical steps from the CMMD objective to the gradient update in Algorithm 1?

The paper's core contribution is valuable - conditional generative modeling is an important direction for generative models. However, the current presentation obscures this contribution with excessive mathematical formalism and insufficient practical explanation. The authors should refocus on making the conditional extension accessible and understandable, rather than emphasizing mathematical rigor at the expense of clarity.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound extension to moment-matching networks with promising empirical results across multiple tasks. However, the presentation fails to adequately convey the intuition behind the approach and the practical considerations of implementation. With improved clarity and more detailed explanation of the connection between theory and practice, this work would make a stronger contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper's potential is somewhat obscured by its current presentation.
