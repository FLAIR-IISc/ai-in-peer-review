

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions via conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). The authors propose a unified approach to handle conditional generation and prediction tasks—such as predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation—by directly matching conditional kernel mean embeddings rather than training separate models per condition. The method leverages backpropagation for efficient optimization, with CMMD formulated as a Hilbert-Schmidt norm between conditional embedding operators. Experiments demonstrate competitive performance across classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional image generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian model distillation (Boston housing).

**Strengths:**  
The core innovation—treating conditional distributions as a family via CMMD—effectively sidesteps the data sparsity problem inherent in per-condition GMMN training (e.g., when conditioning variables are continuous or have high cardinality). The CMMD objective (Equation 2) is elegantly derived and computationally feasible, as all kernel matrices are independent of model parameters, enabling standard backpropagation. The formulation in Remark 1 insightfully connects CMMD to MMD, highlighting how conditional variables weight kernel matrices non-uniformly. Experimental results are comprehensive, showing strong performance in classification (competitive with CMMVA on SVHN), coherent conditional generation (e.g., MNIST digits conditioned on labels), and effective Bayesian distillation (preserving RMSE on Boston housing). The approach’s simplicity and broad applicability are significant advantages.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**  
1. **Clarity and Exposition:** Sections 2.3 (conditional kernel embedding) and 3.1 (CMMD derivation) require substantial background to follow. The exposition would benefit from a concise high-level summary of key operators (e.g., defining $C_{Y|X}$ upfront) and clearer motivation for the cross-covariance operator definition. As-is, readers must consult [29] to resolve minor inconsistencies (e.g., the $C_{XY}$ definition in the paper differs slightly from [29], requiring an adjusted cross-covariance formulation).  
2. **Technical Accuracy:** The claim that the MMD estimator is "unbiased" (line 77) is incorrect; it is *asymptotically unbiased* but biased for finite samples. This should be corrected.  
3. **Baseline Comparison:** The naive baseline—training a separate GMMN per condition (e.g., 10 networks for MNIST labels)—is not evaluated. While the paper acknowledges this approach is infeasible for continuous conditioning, a comparison on discrete-condition tasks (like MNIST label generation) would strengthen the argument for CGMMN’s necessity. The current experiments implicitly assume this baseline is inferior, but this deserves explicit validation.  
4. **Scalability Concerns:** The reliance on matrix inverses (e.g., $K + \lambda I)^{-1}$) for CMMD computation becomes computationally prohibitive for large minibatches. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify the trade-off between minibatch size (to reduce stochasticity) and computational cost. A brief discussion of approximate alternatives (e.g., Nyström methods) would be valuable.

**Recommendation:**  
CGMMN makes a compelling contribution to conditional generative modeling, offering a theoretically sound and practically efficient extension of MMD-based methods. The technical formulation is novel and well-supported by experiments. With minor revisions to address clarity (particularly in the theoretical exposition), correct the MMD bias statement, and include a comparison to the naive per-condition baseline (at least for discrete conditioning), this work would be a strong fit for NIPS. The current version is technically rigorous and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature, but the clarity issues slightly hinder its accessibility. **I recommend acceptance.**
