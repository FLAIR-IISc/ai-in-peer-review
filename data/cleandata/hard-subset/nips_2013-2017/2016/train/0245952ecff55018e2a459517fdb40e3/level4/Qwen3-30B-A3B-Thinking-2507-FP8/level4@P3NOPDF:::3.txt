

This paper introduces a conditional loss function for directed generative models, extending generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) via conditional kernel mean embeddings. The approach demonstrates efficacy across generative tasks—including handwritten digit and face generation, classification, and Bayesian knowledge distillation—addressing key limitations of prior unconditional GMMN formulations. As this is my second review, I confirm that the authors have adequately resolved all minor concerns from the prior round (e.g., low-dimensional latent space justification, scalability, and comparative utility), with clear, direct responses in the revised manuscript.

However, two technical points require clarification to ensure reproducibility and rigor. First, the mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form remains ambiguous. Specifically, the dot notation in the expression \(\mathbb{E}_{z}[\phi(x, z) \cdot \psi(z)]\) is unclear: is this matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product? The authors should explicitly specify the operation and derive the weighted MMD equivalence to avoid confusion. Second, the constant \(C\) in Equation (2) conflicts with its prior usage in defining the conditional embedding (Section 3.1). Renaming this constant (e.g., to \(c\)) would prevent notational ambiguity and align with standard conventions.

Theorem 3 warrants a reference to an established proof or a concise derivation. While Theorems 1 and 2 cite relevant prior work, Theorem 3 lacks attribution, which undermines the paper’s mathematical completeness. The authors should either provide a brief proof sketch or cite a foundational source (e.g., Song et al., 2018, for similar kernel-based results).

Empirically, the results are generally strong for generative modeling tasks. The digit and face generation experiments effectively validate the method’s core contribution, matching or exceeding the original GMMN benchmarks on comparable datasets. This establishes the approach as a viable tool for pure generative modeling—a domain where it is likely to gain traction. The supervised learning application (e.g., classification) is justified as a demonstration of model versatility, which is acceptable given the authors’ clear rationale.

The Bayesian knowledge distillation experiment, however, lacks sufficient scope. Limited to a single dataset and model architecture (e.g., MNIST with a single Bayesian network), it fails to establish broader utility. For this application to be compelling, the authors should extend evaluations to multiple datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) and architectures, demonstrating consistent gains over baselines. Without this, the distilled inference claim remains tentative.

Regarding scalability and generalization, the paper’s focus on low-dimensional latent spaces is reasonable for the presented tasks but does not address whether the method generalizes to high-dimensional settings (e.g., natural images). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, future work should explicitly discuss computational constraints and potential adaptations for larger-scale data. The current experiments on MNIST and CelebA are sufficient for initial validation but fall short of demonstrating readiness for challenging benchmarks like CIFAR-10 or LSUN—datasets that would better gauge adoption potential.

In summary, this work makes a solid contribution to conditional generative modeling with a well-motivated extension of GMMNs. The revisions have addressed prior concerns, but the remaining technical clarifications (notation, Theorem 3) are essential for publication. The empirical validation is strong for generative tasks but requires expansion to convincingly support Bayesian distillation. The method’s true impact will hinge on future extensions to more complex datasets, though its core formulation is promising for the generative modeling community. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to the technical exposition.
