This paper presents Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), an extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core contribution is the application of the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the corresponding Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a metric for training the generative model. The authors demonstrate the utility of CGMMNs on a range of applications, including classification, conditional generation, and the distillation of Bayesian models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Novel Approach:** The paper's main strength is its principled extension of the GMMN framework to the conditional setting. The authors correctly identify the data sparsity problem inherent in a naive approach, where one might train a separate model for each conditioning value. This would be especially problematic for continuous or high-cardinality conditioning variables. By instead treating the conditional distributions as a unified family and matching the conditional embedding operator directly, the proposed model can effectively share statistical strength across different conditioning contexts.

2.  **Elegant and Practical Objective Function:** The CMMD objective function is elegant and leads to a practical implementation. As highlighted in Remark 1, its empirical estimate can be computed in a manner very similar to the standard unconditional MMD. The fact that the kernel matrices in Equation 2 are independent of the model parameters is a significant advantage, as it allows for straightforward optimization via standard backpropagation.

3.  **Compelling Applications:** The paper showcases the versatility of the proposed framework through a set of interesting experiments. The application to distilling complex Bayesian models is particularly novel and highlights the potential impact of this work beyond standard generative tasks.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** While the paper argues effectively against the "naive" approach of training a separate GMMN for each condition, this baseline is not empirically evaluated. For tasks with a small number of discrete conditions, such as the MNIST conditional generation experiment (10 classes), this approach could be a reasonable competitor. Including a comparison to such a baseline would better contextualize the performance of the proposed CGMMN and more clearly quantify the practical benefits of the conditional embedding framework.

2.  **Scalability and Computational Concerns:** A potential concern is the computational cost and sample complexity of the CMMD objective. The estimator may require large minibatches to ensure that the variance from stochastic sampling does not dominate the true discrepancy signal. However, large minibatches significantly increase the computational burden, particularly due to the matrix inversions, which can be prohibitively expensive. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss this trade-off, perhaps by providing an analysis of the model's performance as a function of minibatch size or by discussing potential approximations to mitigate the computational cost for large-scale problems.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 was difficult to follow without consulting the cited literature. The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing more self-contained explanations of the key theoretical concepts, such as the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the derivation of the CMMD estimator. This would improve the paper's accessibility and allow readers to fully appreciate the technical contributions without needing to refer to external sources.

**Minor Corrections**

1.  **Line 77:** The text states that the MMD estimator is unbiased. This is only true for the U-statistic estimator. The V-statistic, which is typically used in practice (and appears to be used here), is a biased estimator. Please correct this statement for accuracy.

2.  **Line 90:** The definition of the conditional embedding operator seems inconsistent with the one provided in the cited reference [29]. To arrive at the form used in this paper, a different definition of the cross-covariance operator appears to be necessary. Please clarify this discrepancy or correct the formulation to align with the reference.

**Overall Assessment**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable method for modeling conditional distributions. The CMMD provides an elegant and effective way to extend moment-matching techniques to the conditional setting. The experimental results, especially on model distillation, are interesting and promising. My main concerns relate to the need for a stronger baseline comparison and a clearer exposition of the theoretical background. Addressing these points would substantially improve the quality and impact of the paper.
