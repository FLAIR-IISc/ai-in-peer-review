

The paper extends moment matching networks (MMNs) to a conditional setting, addressing a non-trivial challenge distinct from simpler models where conditional updates are trivial (e.g., simply incorporating the condition into the network). The core difficulty arises because the conditional extension requires matching distributions across *large batches* of samples to *large batches* of inputs, rather than single examples. Crucially, this mismatch occurs because the conditional distribution varies per input—using identical conditions for an entire update batch would be inefficient.  

The primary weakness lies in the paper’s lack of intuitive explanation for the proposed conditional formulation. Instead of first establishing conceptual clarity, the authors immediately present complex mathematical machinery (e.g., reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces) without grounding the reader in *why* the approach makes sense. For instance, the non-conditional MMN algorithm is intuitively explained in the original paper (e.g., via matching moments directly), yet the conditional version (Equation 2) is introduced abstractly. The authors fail to articulate:  
1. **The intuitive motivation** for Equation 2 (e.g., "How would one derive this formula from first principles?").  
2. **The practical algorithmic steps** translating Equation 2 into computation (e.g., "What specific operations are performed per batch?").  
3. **Computational assumptions and efficiency** (e.g., "What approximations enable scalability, and how do they impact performance?").  

While some algorithmic details appear in the supplement, they are difficult to follow due to the absence of a clear narrative. The paper does not clarify whether the optimization in Equation 2 is solved via closed-form updates, iterative methods, or approximations, nor does it address how the conditional moment matching scales with batch size or input dimensionality. This omission leaves readers unable to assess the method’s practicality or understand the *actual computations* performed during training.  

For comparison, the non-conditional MMN’s simplicity stems from its intuitive objective: align the moments of generated and real data distributions. The conditional extension should similarly prioritize conceptual accessibility. A stronger presentation would begin with an intuitive "aha!" moment (e.g., "To conditionally match distributions, we must account for input-dependent variations in the target distribution, which leads to...") before deriving Equation 2. The current approach—starting with formalism—obscures the contribution’s value.  

To address these gaps, the authors should:  
- **Replace Equation 2’s abstract derivation** with a step-by-step intuitive justification (e.g., "Consider how the conditional distribution shifts across inputs; to match it, we need a term that...").  
- **Explicitly map Equation 2 to algorithmic steps** (e.g., "Step 1: Compute conditional moments for each input; Step 2: Minimize the distance between these moments and the target...").  
- **Discuss efficiency assumptions** (e.g., "We assume the kernel can be approximated efficiently via random features, reducing complexity from O(n²) to O(n log n)").  

Without these clarifications, the paper risks being perceived as technically dense but conceptually opaque. The conditional MMN’s potential impact hinges on demonstrating not just *that* the method works, but *why* it works and *how* to implement it efficiently. The current draft falls short of this standard.
