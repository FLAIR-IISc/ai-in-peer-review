This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), building upon Generative Moment Matching Networks to address conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core innovation lies in utilizing kernel embeddings to represent conditional distributions and a conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric to quantify discrepancies between them. The efficacy of CGMMNs is demonstrated through applications in classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation. A straightforward extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would involve estimating a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters via a common neural network. However, this approach suffers from data sparsity, particularly in continuous conditioning variable spaces where each conditional distribution may be supported by only a limited number of samples, or even a single instance. In contrast, the proposed CGMMN treats the set of conditional distributions as a unified family, directly matching the model to the conditional embedding operator instead of individually fitting each distribution. While the benefits of this approach are apparent, the simpler per-conditional-distribution approach can perform adequately in certain scenarios – for example, conditional generation with a small number of discrete conditioning values, such as the 10 digits in MNIST. Inclusion of such a baseline comparison would strengthen the evaluation. The formulation of the conditional MMD objective is well-presented and, as noted in Remark 1, efficiently computed with similarities to the unconditional MMD. Importantly, the K matrices in equation 2 are independent of model parameters, allowing for standard backpropagation. A key consideration is the potential need for large minibatches when optimizing the CMMD objective to mitigate the impact of stochasticity, which simultaneously introduces computational challenges, especially concerning the cost of matrix inversions. Despite this, the CMMD offers a valuable framework for modeling conditional distributions, and the experimental results for classification and Bayesian model distillation are compelling. The explanations in sections 2.3 and 3.1 could be improved for clarity; referencing the original papers was necessary to fully grasp the underlying concepts. Several minor issues were identified: (1) the claim of an unbiased MMD estimator on line 77 is inaccurate, and (2) the conditional embedding operator on line 90 deviates from the definition in reference [29], requiring a revised definition of the cross covariance operator to align with the paper’s formulation.
