This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), an extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction applications. The core of the proposed method lies in the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) metric for measuring the discrepancy of conditional distributions.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMNs on classification, conditional generation, and distillation of Bayesian models. A naive approach to extending GMMNs to the conditional setting would involve estimating a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters through a single neural network. However, this approach suffers from the data sparsity problem, as each conditional distribution has limited examples, particularly in the case of continuous conditioning variables.

In contrast, the proposed approach treats all conditional distributions as a family and directly matches the model with the conditional embedding operator, rather than individual conditional distributions. The advantage of this approach is apparent, although the naive approach may still perform reasonably well in certain cases, such as conditional generation with a small number of discrete conditioning variables (e.g., MNIST). A comparison with this naive approach as a baseline would be an interesting addition.

The conditional MMD objective has a clear formulation, and its computation is similar to the unconditional MMD, as noted in Remark 1. The K matrices in equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, allowing for backpropagation to be performed in the same manner as before. One concern with the proposed approach is that the CMMD objective may require large minibatches to mitigate the stochasticity of the minibatches, which can dominate the error. However, large minibatches can make the computation of the objective expensive, particularly the matrix inverses.

Overall, the CMMD provides a promising approach to modeling conditional distributions. The experimental results on classification and distillation of Bayesian models are also noteworthy. However, the presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 could be improved, as it was not easy to follow without referencing external papers. There are a couple of minor issues: (1) the claim on line 77 that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect, and (2) the conditional embedding operator on line 90 is inconsistent with the definition in reference [29], which appears to require a different definition of the cross-covariance operator.

Recommendations for future work include exploring the trade-offs between minibatch size and computational cost, as well as comparing the proposed approach with the naive baseline in various scenarios. Additionally, clarifying the presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 would improve the overall readability of the paper.
