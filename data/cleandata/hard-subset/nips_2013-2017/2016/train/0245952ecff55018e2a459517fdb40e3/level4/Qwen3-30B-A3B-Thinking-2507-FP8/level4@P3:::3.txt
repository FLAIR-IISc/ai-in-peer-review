

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extends the Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion that leverages kernel mean embedding techniques for conditional distributions to learn flexible conditional generative models. The approach is evaluated across three application domains: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (image synthesis conditioned on labels), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (learning compact student networks from Bayesian teachers).

The work builds upon several key NIPS contributions. It extends GMMN (Li et al., ICML 2015), which provided a simpler alternative to GANs using maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for unconditional generative modeling. The theoretical foundation draws from conditional kernel mean embedding work (Song et al., ICML 2009; Grunewalder et al., ICML 2012), while the practical applications relate to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, ArXiv 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., NIPS 2015).

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with a well-structured theoretical development of CMMD. The authors provide a clear proof of Theorem 3, establishing the connection between CMMD and conditional distribution equality. The experimental design is appropriate for the tasks evaluated, with comprehensive results on MNIST, SVHN, and Yale Face datasets. However, the Bayesian dark knowledge experiment is limited to a single regression task (Boston housing), which restricts the generalizability of the findings. The authors address previous concerns about low-dimensional latent spaces, but the rationale for dimension selection could be more detailed.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical formulations. However, the notation creates confusion with dual use of "C" (for both the conditional embedding operator and regularization matrix). The mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form in Remark 1 requires clarification—specifically, whether the dot notation denotes matrix multiplication or Hadamard product. The authors have improved the presentation from previous versions, but these notational issues should be resolved.

**Originality:** The extension of MMD to conditional distributions represents a meaningful contribution to the field. While conditional GANs have been explored, this work provides a principled alternative to GAN-based conditional modeling that avoids min-max optimization challenges. The CMMD criterion is novel in its application to conditional generative modeling, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from related work.

**Significance:** The method demonstrates competitive performance on classification tasks (comparable to CMMVA, a state-of-the-art DGM for prediction), contextual generation (MNIST and Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the focus on relatively simple datasets (digits and faces) rather than more challenging benchmarks like CIFAR-10 or LSUN. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is interesting but lacks broader empirical validation across multiple datasets and model architectures.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical foundation for conditional generative modeling
- Good experimental validation across multiple tasks
- Addressed previous reviewer concerns effectively
- Provides a principled alternative to conditional GANs
- Demonstrates versatility through diverse applications

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited evaluation on challenging image datasets (CIFAR-10, LSUN)
- Insufficient analysis of scalability to higher-dimensional latent spaces
- Confusing notation with dual use of "C"
- Bayesian distillation experiment lacks breadth
- Supervised learning application, while demonstrating versatility, is not the strongest application

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a technically sound extension to GMMN that addresses an important gap in conditional generative modeling. The quality of the work is high, and the method is well-motivated. However, the significance would be substantially strengthened by demonstrating the approach on more challenging datasets. I recommend borderline acceptance with a strong recommendation for the authors to expand the evaluation to more challenging benchmarks in the camera-ready version.

**Pro-Acceptance Arguments:**
1. Theoretical contribution to conditional generative modeling is novel and well-supported
2. The method is conceptually simple and compatible with existing deep learning frameworks
3. Demonstrates competitive performance across multiple tasks
4. Addresses a clear limitation of previous work (unconditional generative models)

**Con-Acceptance Arguments:**
1. Limited empirical validation on challenging datasets
2. Notable confusion in notation with dual use of "C"
3. Bayesian distillation experiment lacks sufficient breadth
4. Supervised learning application is not the most compelling use case

The authors have made significant improvements from the previous version, addressing most reviewer concerns. With minor revisions to clarify the notation and expand the experimental validation, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
