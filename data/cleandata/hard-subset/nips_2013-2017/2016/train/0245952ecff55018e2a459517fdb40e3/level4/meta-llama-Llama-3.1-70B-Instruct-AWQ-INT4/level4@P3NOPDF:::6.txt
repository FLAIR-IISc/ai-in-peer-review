This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), an extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction applications. The core of the proposed method lies in the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric for measuring the discrepancy between conditional distributions. The authors evaluate the proposed CGMMN on classification, conditional generation, and distillation of Bayesian models.

The naive approach to extending GMMNs to a conditional setting involves estimating a GMMN for each conditional distribution, with all conditional distributions sharing parameters through the use of the same neural network. However, this approach has a significant drawback: each conditional distribution often has only a limited number of examples, and in cases where the conditioning variables are continuous, each conditional distribution may have only a single example. This leads to a data sparsity problem. In contrast, the proposed approach treats all conditional distributions as a family and attempts to match the model with the conditional embedding operator directly, rather than matching each individual conditional distribution. The advantages of the proposed approach are clear, but in some cases, the naive approach can still produce reasonable results, such as in conditional generation where the conditioning variable takes one of a limited number of values (e.g., 10 values in MNIST). A comparison with this naive approach as a baseline would be an interesting addition to the paper.

The formulation of the Conditional MMD objective is a notable contribution, as it can be easily computed in a similar manner to the unconditional MMD, as pointed out in Remark 1. The K matrices in Equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, allowing backpropagation to be performed in the same way as before. However, one concern with the proposed approach is that the CMMD objective may require large minibatches to mitigate the stochasticity of the minibatches, which can dominate the error. On the other hand, large minibatches can make the computation of the objective expensive, particularly when computing matrix inverses.

Overall, the Conditional MMD provides a promising approach to modeling conditional distributions. The experimental results on classification and distillation of Bayesian models are also noteworthy. However, the presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 could be improved, as it was not easy to follow without referencing external papers to gain a deeper understanding of the material.

There are a couple of minor issues that need to be addressed: (1) on line 77, it is stated that the MMD estimator is unbiased, which is not accurate. (2) on line 90, the conditional embedding operator is inconsistent with the one defined in reference [29]. To obtain the form used in this paper, a different definition of the cross-covariance operator appears to be necessary.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

Rating: 8/10
