This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMNs), a method for training deep generative models to represent conditional distributions. The work is a natural and well-motivated extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMNs), which are trained using an unconditional objective based on Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The key contribution is a novel loss function, the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which is derived from the theory of conditional kernel mean embeddings. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their model on a variety of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (handwritten digits and faces), and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

The authors have successfully addressed the minor concerns raised in a previous review cycle, such as clarifying the choice of a low-dimensional latent space and discussing the model's scalability and utility.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Originality and Soundness:** The paper presents a theoretically grounded and logical extension of the GMMN framework to the conditional setting. The proposed CMMD objective is a principled way to measure the discrepancy between conditional distributions in a non-parametric fashion.
2.  **Versatility:** A significant strength is the demonstration of the model's flexibility across a diverse set of applications. Applying a single framework to classification, image generation, and model distillation effectively showcases its wide-ranging potential.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The connection to prior work on MMD and kernel mean embeddings is clearly explained.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Clarity of Remark 1:** Remark 1 provides an interesting intuition connecting CMMD to a weighted form of MMD. However, the connection is not entirely clear from the text. The authors should clarify the notation used in the expression `Tr(Ld · 1)`. Is this standard matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product? Providing more detail on the derivation would make the mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form more explicit and impactful. Additionally, the choice of `C1, C2, C3` for the weighting matrices is potentially confusing, as `C` is already used to denote the conditional embedding operator.
2.  **Missing Proof for Theorem 3:** Theorem 3 is central to the paper's theoretical justification, as it establishes that equality of the conditional embedding operators implies equality of the conditional distributions. While Theorems 1 and 2 correctly cite prior work, Theorem 3 is presented without a proof or a citation. The authors must either provide a proof (e.g., in the appendix) or cite the work from which this result is derived.
3.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:**
    *   The Bayesian model distillation application is novel and intriguing. However, its evaluation on a single dataset (Boston Housing) with only one "teacher" model (PBP) makes it difficult to assess its general utility. Demonstrating this on another task or with a different Bayesian model would strengthen this contribution.
    *   The model's primary impact is likely to be in conditional generative modeling. The experiments on MNIST and the Yale Face dataset are sufficient to demonstrate that the core idea works, with results comparable to the original GMMN paper. However, to establish the significance of this approach and encourage broader adoption, it is crucial to demonstrate compelling performance on more complex and challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN, which have become standard benchmarks for generative models.

**Final Assessment:**
This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution by extending moment-matching networks to the important problem of conditional density modeling. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the proposed CMMD objective is elegant. While the authors' justification for including classification experiments to highlight versatility is reasonable, the model's main strength lies in generative tasks. The current generative experiments are convincing as a proof of concept, but demonstrating the method's effectiveness on more difficult, standard benchmarks would significantly elevate the paper's impact. The suggested clarifications regarding Remark 1 and Theorem 3 are minor but necessary for technical completeness.
