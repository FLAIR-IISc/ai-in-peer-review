Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) that learns conditional distributions p(y|x) using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The model samples noise h from a simple distribution, concatenates it with the conditioning variable x, and maps (x, h) through a deep network to produce y. Training minimizes the Hilbert–Schmidt distance between the conditional mean embedding operators of the empirical conditional distribution and the model-induced conditional, enabling end-to-end optimization by back-propagation.

The work builds on kernel mean embeddings and MMD for two-sample testing, and specifically on conditional mean embeddings (e.g., Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012) to define the CMMD objective. Compared to GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), CGMMN has a single, non-adversarial objective and uses kernels to avoid strong parametric assumptions. Relative to conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), the approach replaces an explicit likelihood/ELBO with a discrepancy objective in RKHS. In predictive modeling, it is compared to max-margin deep generative models (Li et al., 2015). The paper also explores “Bayesian dark knowledge” distillation (Korattikara et al., 2015) by training a CGMMN student to mimic the predictive distribution of a Bayesian teacher (PBP; Hernández-Lobato & Adams, 2015).

Quality
- Theory: The paper defines CMMD as the squared Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional embedding operators and shows that equality of operators implies equality of conditionals for all x under standard assumptions (universal kernels, smoothness). The empirical estimator uses Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ and leads to a computable objective expressed with Gram matrices. The connection to MMD as a special case with uniform weights is informative.
- Algorithms: Training uses SGD on mini-batches; K̃−1 depends only on x and λ (not on network parameters), simplifying differentiation. The minibatch formulation addresses cubic costs in batch size.
- Empirics: Experiments cover classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST digits, Yale faces), and Bayesian distillation (Boston housing). Results are competitive with strong baselines for a non-adversarial generative approach, and qualitative samples are reasonable. The nearest-neighbor analysis suggests the model is not memorizing training examples; using an autoencoder code space improves visual quality.

Some aspects could be strengthened: (i) more detail and sensitivity analysis for kernel choices (type, bandwidth selection), λ, and batch sizes; (ii) complexity/memory profiling for CMMD (e.g., O(B^3) per batch for inverses; whether Cholesky or low-rank approximations are used); (iii) quantitative generation metrics beyond visuals (at least Parzen estimates, common at the time); and (iv) a broader distillation study (e.g., negative log-likelihood, predictive calibration, speedups) beyond RMSE on Boston.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well organized, and readable. The presentation of CMMD, its estimator, and the connection to MMD is helpful. A few passages would benefit from clarification:
- Outline kernel choices and bandwidth selection strategy for K and L, especially for mixed discrete/continuous outputs.
- Explicitly discuss computational costs and numerical stability (conditioning of K+λI; effect of λ).
- In predictive modeling, clarify how predictions are produced (single sample vs. averaging multiple y samples), and whether this affects accuracy or calibration.
- Fix minor typos and grammatical issues (e.g., “This si”, “out network”, “convoluational”).

Originality
Conditioning GMMN is a natural but nontrivial extension. The paper’s contribution is in formulating CMMD via conditional mean embeddings and turning it into a practical, differentiable objective for deep conditional generators. While related ideas exist in conditional mean embeddings and in conditional VAEs/GANs, the specific operator-based discrepancy for conditional learning in deep nets appears novel and useful.

Significance
The method offers a simple, stable alternative to adversarial training for conditional generation and a likelihood-free alternative to VAEs. It broadens the applicability of MMD-based generators to settings where p(y|x) is the target (prediction, contextual generation, and knowledge distillation). The empirical results are competitive, though not state-of-the-art across the board, and the approach should be of interest to researchers and practitioners who prefer non-adversarial training and kernel-based criteria.

Strengths
- Clear, principled derivation of a conditional discrepancy objective grounded in kernel embeddings.
- Simple training with back-propagation; no adversarial optimization.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Competitive results with strong baselines; informative qualitative analyses.

Weaknesses
- Limited discussion of kernel selection, bandwidth tuning, and the sensitivity of results to these choices.
- Computational aspects (time/memory, scaling with batch size) are not quantified.
- Generative evaluation lacks quantitative metrics; comparisons to cGAN and cVAE on generation are missing.
- The distillation experiment is narrow (one dataset, RMSE only), with no calibration or likelihood metrics and no runtime comparison.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please detail kernel choices and bandwidth selection procedures (median heuristic? cross-validation?) for both input and output kernels, and provide a sensitivity study.
- Report computational costs per training epoch and peak memory for typical batch sizes; consider Cholesky or low-rank updates to reduce the cost of (K+λI)−1.
- For classification, evaluate averaging multiple samples from p(y|x) or using the conditional mean embedding to form probabilities, and report calibration (e.g., ECE/NLL).
- Add quantitative generative metrics appropriate for the time (e.g., Parzen estimates) and, if possible, head-to-head comparisons with cGAN/cVAE under similar architectures.
- Expand the distillation study with NLL and calibration, as well as speed and memory benefits relative to the teacher; include additional datasets.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a solid, well-motivated, and generally well-executed paper that extends MMD-based generative modeling to the conditional setting in a principled way. While some experimental and practical details could be fleshed out, the contribution is clear and likely to be useful. I lean toward acceptance.
