

This paper introduces conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMNs), extending generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core contribution lies in kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) metric for measuring conditional distribution discrepancies. The proposed CGMMN framework is evaluated on classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation. The naive approach to extending GMMNs to conditional settings—estimating a separate GMMN per conditional distribution while sharing neural network parameters—suffers from severe data sparsity. Specifically, when conditioning variables are continuous, each conditional distribution may contain only a single example, rendering the naive method ineffective. In contrast, the proposed CGMMN treats all conditional distributions as a family and matches the model directly to the conditional embedding operator, bypassing per-distribution matching. While the proposed approach offers clear advantages, the naive method can still perform reasonably well in cases with discrete conditioning variables (e.g., 10 classes in MNIST conditional generation), suggesting a comparison against this baseline would strengthen the evaluation. The conditional MMD objective is well-formulated and computationally efficient, as noted in Remark 1, with all K matrices in Equation 2 being parameter-independent, enabling standard backpropagation. A key concern is that the CMMD objective may require large minibatches to mitigate minibatch stochasticity, which could overwhelm estimation error; however, large minibatches also increase computational cost, particularly for expensive matrix inverses. Overall, the CMMD provides a compelling framework for modeling conditional distributions, and the experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are promising. However, the presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is difficult to follow without consulting referenced papers. Two minor issues require attention: (1) Line 77 incorrectly states that the MMD estimator is unbiased; (2) The conditional embedding operator on Line 90 conflicts with the definition in reference [29], necessitating a different cross-covariance operator definition for consistency with the paper's formulation.
