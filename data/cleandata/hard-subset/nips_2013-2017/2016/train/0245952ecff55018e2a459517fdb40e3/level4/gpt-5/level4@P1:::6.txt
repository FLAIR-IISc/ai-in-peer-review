Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend GMMNs to conditional modeling via conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). The core idea is to represent conditional distributions using kernel mean embeddings and to learn a conditional generator by minimizing the Hilbert–Schmidt distance between the conditional embedding operators of model and data. Training is carried out with back-propagation; importantly, the Gram matrices over the conditioning variables (the K matrices in Eq. 2) do not depend on model parameters, so gradients propagate as in the unconditional MMD case.

The work builds on MMD-based generative models (GMMN) and kernel embeddings of conditional distributions, and is complementary to GANs and their conditional variants, as well as conditional VAEs. Unlike the naive per-condition GMMN approach—learning a separate model for each conditional value while sharing network parameters—CGMMN matches the family of conditionals jointly via the conditional embedding operator, which helps when each conditional has few examples or when X is continuous.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: modeling p(y|x) directly is often preferable to modeling p(x, y), and conditional generation is broadly useful (e.g., label- or attribute-conditional synthesis, cross-modal generation).
- Sound formulation: CMMD is a natural extension of MMD to conditionals via operator norms in RKHS, with a practical estimator expressible via Gram matrices. Remark 1 usefully connects CMMD to MMD as a non-uniformly weighted variant, where weights reflect the conditioning variables.
- Implementation simplicity: because the K matrices are independent of network parameters, back-propagation proceeds as in GMMN without requiring differentiable kernels on x.
- Empirical scope: the paper demonstrates CGMMN on classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional image generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, with competitive results.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Baseline comparison: A strong baseline is missing. In discrete, low-cardinality settings (e.g., MNIST with 10 classes), a naive per-condition approach could be reasonable—train a GMMN per class (or a shared network conditioned on one-hot labels, but with a standard MMD loss applied per class). Comparing against such a baseline would clarify the empirical gains specifically attributable to CMMD.
- Computational cost and stability: The CMMD objective involves matrix inverses of minibatch Gram matrices, which scale cubically in the batch size. CMMD may require large minibatches to control variance; otherwise, minibatch stochasticity could dominate the signal. This tension raises concerns about both runtime and memory. Some discussion or experiments on batch-size sensitivity, and/or scalable approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features, block-diagonal or cached inverses), would strengthen the paper.
- Clarity: Sections 2.3 and 3.1 are difficult to follow without consulting the cited work. Definitions around the conditional embedding operator and the cross-covariance operator should be presented more self-contained, with consistent notation and assumptions, to make the paper accessible.

Minor issues
1) Line 77: It is stated that the MMD estimator is unbiased. This is not generally correct as written. There are both biased (V-statistic) and unbiased (U-statistic) estimators of MMD^2; the common finite-sample estimator used in practice is often biased. Please clarify which estimator is used and its bias/variance properties.
2) Line 90: The definition of the conditional embedding operator appears inconsistent with [29]. To obtain the form used here, a different definition of the cross-covariance operator seems required. Please reconcile the notation and assumptions with [29], or restate the operator definitions precisely.

Quality
- The CMMD objective is well-motivated and consistent with kernel embedding theory. The observation that the input Gram matrices are parameter-independent is useful for implementation. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of estimator variance, computational complexity, and numerical stability, especially for large-scale problems.

Clarity
- Overall organization is reasonable, but the theory sections (2.3, 3.1) need more explicit definitions and assumptions to stand alone. A brief derivation outline for Eq. 2, the precise role of regularization λ, and a clear statement of when the operator norm is well-defined would help readers avoid having to consult multiple references.

Originality
- The extension from MMD to CMMD for training conditional deep generators is a natural but non-trivial step that, to my knowledge, has not been previously instantiated in deep generative modeling. The approach is distinct from conditional GANs and conditional VAEs.

Significance
- Conditional modeling is widely useful, and a stable, back-propagation-friendly objective is valuable. The experiments on classification and Bayesian distillation are interesting; generation results are reasonable. The impact would be clearer with the suggested baseline and with more analysis of computational trade-offs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a naive baseline for discrete conditionals (e.g., MNIST per-class GMMN or per-class MMD training using a shared network).
- Analyze sensitivity to minibatch size and λ; report wall-clock costs and memory usage.
- Consider scalable approximations for Gram matrix inversion (Nyström, random features) or reusing cached inverses across iterations.
- Clarify the theoretical sections with consistent operator definitions and a brief self-contained derivation, and correct the statements about MMD estimator bias.

Pros
- Conceptually clean extension of MMD to conditional modeling.
- Simple training by back-propagation; no adversarial training instabilities.
- Competitive empirical performance; interesting Bayesian distillation application.

Cons
- Missing naive baseline; unclear empirical advantage in discrete, low-cardinality settings.
- Potentially high computational cost due to matrix inverses; limited discussion of scalability.
- Some theoretical exposition and claims need clarification/correction.

Overall assessment
CGMMN is a neat and potentially useful contribution to conditional generative modeling with kernels and deep networks. With the additional baseline and clearer treatment of computational and theoretical details, the paper would be significantly strengthened.
