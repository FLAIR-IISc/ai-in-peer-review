This paper proposes a novel loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables, building upon the generative moment matching networks framework. Specifically, it leverages the concept of conditional kernel mean embeddings to extend the unconditional loss function based on the maximum mean discrepancy criterion. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a range of generative tasks, including classification, generating handwritten digits and faces, and distilling knowledge in Bayesian models.

I had the opportunity to review this paper previously, and the authors have addressed the minor concerns raised at that time. These concerns included the choice of a low-dimensional space and the potential for scaling to higher-dimensional latent spaces, as well as the utility of this model compared to existing alternatives. Upon reviewing the revised text, it appears that these issues have been adequately addressed.

However, I do have some lingering questions regarding the mathematical formulation. Specifically, I found it unclear how to map Equation (2) onto the weighted MMD form, as described in Remark 1. Could the authors provide more detail on the dot notation used in this context, clarifying whether it represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product? Additionally, the choice of the variable "C" seems unfortunate, given its use in defining the conditional embedding.

I also noticed that Theorems 1 and 2 are properly referenced to existing literature, whereas Theorem 3 lacks a corresponding reference or proof. To maintain the rigor of the paper, I suggest that the authors either provide a reference to a paper containing a proof of Theorem 3 or include the proof itself.

The empirical results presented in the paper are decent, although the authors acknowledge that using this model for supervised learning may not be the most effective application. They argue, however, that this demonstration serves to highlight the versatility of the model, which is a reasonable justification. The application to distilled Bayesian inference is intriguing, but its usefulness in other contexts remains to be seen, given that it is only evaluated on a single dataset with one model.

In my opinion, this approach is most likely to gain traction in the context of pure generative modeling. The experiments presented in this context are comparable to those in the original GMM paper, and the datasets used (involving digits and faces) are sufficient to demonstrate the viability of the idea. Nevertheless, to convince others to adopt this method, more compelling results on challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN, would be necessary.
