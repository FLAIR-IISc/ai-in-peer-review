This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMNs to learn conditional distributions via kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional MMD (CMMD) training objective. The model generates y by passing both the conditioning variables x and noise h through a deep network, then matches the conditional mean embeddings CY|X of the data and model using CMMD. Training uses backpropagation and mini-batch estimates of Gram matrices. The paper evaluates CGMMN on classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional image generation (MNIST digits, Yale faces), and Bayesian “dark knowledge” distillation (Boston Housing), showing competitive performance.

Relative to prior work, CGMMN generalizes GMMN (MMD-trained generators) from joint to conditional distributions by leveraging kernel conditional mean embeddings, a well-studied tool in kernel methods. It provides an alternative to conditional GANs and conditional VAEs, with a simpler objective (no adversarial min–max, no variational bound). Remark 1 neatly shows CMMD reduces to MMD with non-uniform weights determined by x, connecting the approach to standard MMD training.

Quality:
- The formulation is sound: CMMD measures the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional embedding operators constructed from data and model samples. The objective depends on Gram matrices K for x and L for y, and only the L terms involve model parameters, enabling straightforward backpropagation.
- Experiments support the claims: CGMMN is competitive on MNIST and SVHN classification (close to MMVA/CMMVA), produces plausible conditional samples (improved with an autoencoder code space), and distills a Bayesian predictor without degrading RMSE on Boston Housing.
- However, the experimental section could be strengthened by (i) including a “naïve” conditional baseline (e.g., training per-condition GMMNs or a shared network with separate MMD per label on MNIST), (ii) comparisons to cVAE and cGAN where applicable, and (iii) quantitative generation metrics or human evaluation for image quality.

Clarity:
- Sections 2.3 and 3.1 are hard to follow without consulting prior work. Please clarify the definition of cross-covariance and the conditional embedding operator, state assumptions explicitly, and align notation with [29]. A short derivation of Eq. (2) in the main text (or a clearer pointer to the appendix) would help.
- Provide implementation details critical to reproducibility: kernels used for x and y, kernel bandwidth selection, regularization λ, mini-batch sizes for each experiment, whether Gram inverses are cached/reused, and computational cost per iteration.

Originality:
- The core novelty is training deep conditional generators by matching conditional mean embeddings, rather than matching joint distributions or optimizing adversarial/variational objectives. Using CMMD with deep nets is a clean and useful contribution that bridges kernel conditional embeddings and deep generative modeling.

Significance:
- Conditional modeling is central to prediction, conditional generation, and as a building block in larger models. CGMMN offers a principled, simple-to-train alternative to cGAN/cVAE with broad applicability. The distillation application is compelling. Wider empirical comparisons and analyses would increase impact.

Strengths:
- Elegant extension of MMD training to the conditional setting via CMMD.
- Simple, end-to-end training with standard backprop; K matrices do not depend on model parameters.
- Applicable across tasks: classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Competitive results with reasonable architectures; clear qualitative samples, improved via autoencoder code space.

Weaknesses:
- CMMD may require large mini-batches to reduce estimator variance; matrix inversions per batch are cubic in batch size and can be costly.
- Limited baselines for conditional generation and prediction (missing cVAE/cGAN; no “naïve” per-condition GMMN).
- Some theoretical and notational inconsistencies and clarity issues in Sections 2.3 and 3.1.
- Limited quantitative evaluation of generation quality; no ablations on kernel choices or batch size.

Suggestions and questions for the authors:
- Include a naïve conditional baseline on MNIST (e.g., per-class GMMN or shared network with per-class MMD) to illustrate the advantage of CMMD when x has few discrete values.
- Compare to cVAE and cGAN on conditional generation tasks.
- Report kernel choices (for x and y), bandwidth selection strategy, and λ; analyze sensitivity to these hyperparameters.
- Discuss and, if possible, empirically study the bias/variance of the mini-batch CMMD estimator as a function of batch size; show performance vs. batch size.
- Provide computational complexity and wall-clock/runtime per iteration; describe strategies to mitigate cost (e.g., caching K̃−1 when reusing x, low-rank approximations, random Fourier features, conjugate gradients).
- Clarify whether you sample multiple y per x when forming L, and whether that helps variance reduction.
- Improve derivations/notation for the conditional operator and its empirical estimate; align definitions with [29] or explain deviations.

Minor issues:
- The statement that the MMD estimator is unbiased is inaccurate as written. The commonly used V-statistic is biased; there is an unbiased U-statistic form (with diagonals removed) that has higher variance. Please clarify which estimator you use.
- The definition of the conditional embedding operator appears inconsistent with [29]. In [29], CY|X = CYX C−1XX under stated assumptions; please reconcile your definition with the standard cross-covariance operator and ensure assumptions are clearly stated.
- Typos/grammar: several minor typos (e.g., “augumentation”, “convoluational”) and formatting issues could be corrected.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean and principled extension of MMD training to conditional models.
- Simple training procedure with broad applicability.
- Competitive empirical results, including a useful distillation case.

Cons for acceptance:
- Clarity and notational issues in the core methodology sections.
- Limited baselines and quantitative evaluation for generation.
- Potential computational burden of CMMD with large mini-batches and matrix inverses; limited analysis of estimator variance and scalability.

Overall: A solid contribution that meaningfully extends GMMN to conditional modeling. With improved clarity, stronger baselines, and some analysis of computational/scalability trade-offs, the paper would be a strong addition.
