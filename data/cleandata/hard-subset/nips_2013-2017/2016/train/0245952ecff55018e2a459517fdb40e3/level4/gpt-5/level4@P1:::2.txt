Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) to the conditional setting. The proposed Conditional GMMN (CGMMN) models p(y|x) by feeding both the conditioning variables x and auxiliary noise h through a deep network to generate y, and learns parameters by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) between the empirical conditional distribution and the model. The approach builds on kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009/2013) and adapts the MMD-based training of GMMN (Li et al., 2015; Dziugaite et al., 2015). The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and Bayesian “dark knowledge” distillation (Korattikara et al., 2015), reporting competitive results.

Strengths
- A natural and potentially useful extension of GMMN to conditional distributions, broadening its applicability to prediction, conditional generation, and model distillation.
- Training remains simple and stable relative to min–max formulations (e.g., GANs), relying on back-propagation through a straightforward sampling process.
- The CMMD objective is principled (operator norm between conditional mean embeddings) and connects cleanly to prior kernel literature.
- Empirical results are competitive across several tasks and architectures (MLP/CNN), and the approach is compatible with standard optimizers (Adam).

Core concerns and weaknesses
The main weakness is clarity and practicality. The paper starts with heavy formalism and does not adequately explain the intuition behind CMMD or the concrete computations required by Eq. (2). In the conditional case, unlike settings where single-example updates are natural (e.g., purely discriminative models), the algorithm must match distributions over many generated samples to many distinct inputs. As a result:
- It is not clear how one should derive Eq. (2) intuitively, and why this is the right conditional analogue of MMD. The non-conditional GMMN objective can be explained without RKHS machinery; a similarly accessible explanation is missing here.
- The steps from Eq. (2) to a practical, efficient mini-batch algorithm are under-specified. The supplement includes some of this, but it is difficult to follow.
- Computational complexity and efficiency are not discussed in sufficient detail. Gram matrix formation and inversion scale cubically in the batch size; how is this managed in practice (e.g., choice of batch sizes, Cholesky vs. explicit inverse, numerical stability, cost per iteration)?
- Assumptions required for CMMD to be well-defined (e.g., smoothness, regularization, operator Hilbert–Schmidt conditions) are stated but their practical implications are not discussed. How sensitive is training to λ, kernel choice, bandwidth selection, and the conditioning kernel on x?
- The paper notes that K̃−1 for x does not depend on model parameters, but it still must be computed repeatedly per batch; the actual cost and caching strategy are unclear.
- The algorithmic description (Alg. 1) omits critical implementation details (kernel choices for x and y, normalization, kernel bandwidth selection strategy, how many h samples per x, whether x is perturbed, etc.).

Requests for clarification and suggested improvements
To improve clarity and address practical concerns, it would help to:
- Provide an intuitive derivation of Eq. (2). For example: show CMMD as a “weighted MMD” over outputs where weights reflect similarity in x (as hinted in Remark 1), and relate CY|X to kernel ridge regression of output features on x.
- Explicitly explain the steps from Eq. (2) to the implemented mini-batch algorithm: how to construct K and L per batch, how to compute K̃−1 stably (e.g., via Cholesky), and how gradients propagate through L but not through K.
- Include a brief complexity analysis: per-iteration time and memory as functions of batch size B, output dimension, and kernel computations; wall-clock training times; sensitivity to B.
- Detail kernel choices and hyperparameter selection for both x and y (e.g., Gaussian kernels, bandwidth selection via median heuristic or validation). Discuss the effect of using non-differentiable kernels for x.
- Clarify how conditioning inputs x for the “data” and “generated” sets are chosen per iteration (same x or perturbed variants), and justify these choices in terms of estimator variance/bias and efficiency.
- Summarize the above in a short, self-contained section that trades formal derivation for operational clarity.

Quality
The method is technically sound and grounded in established kernel embedding theory. However, the paper underspecifies key computational steps and assumptions, which hinders reproducibility and assessment of efficiency.

Clarity
Writing and organization should prioritize intuition and practical algorithmic detail before formal operator notation. The supplement addresses some issues but remains hard to navigate. Adding the requested explanations and a concise algorithmic blueprint would substantially improve clarity.

Originality
The combination of deep conditional generative modeling with conditional mean embeddings and an operator-norm training criterion appears novel relative to GMMN and cGAN/cVAE contemporaries.

Significance
If clarified and made practical, CMMD-trained CGMMNs could be a useful alternative to adversarial/variational methods for conditional generation and probabilistic prediction, with advantages in stability and simplicity.

Pros
- Principled extension of GMMN to conditional distributions.
- Simple training via back-propagation; no adversarial game.
- Broad applicability demonstrated; competitive results.

Cons
- Insufficient intuitive explanation and practical algorithmic detail for Eq. (2).
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and kernel/hyperparameter choices.
- Supplement is hard to follow; core implementation details should be in the main text.

Actionable questions for the authors
- How would you intuitively derive Eq. (2) as the conditional analogue of MMD?
- What are the exact steps from Eq. (2) to the implemented mini-batch algorithm, and what is the per-iteration computational complexity?
- Which kernels and bandwidths are used for x and y, and how are they selected?
- How sensitive is performance to λ, batch size, and the number/dimension of noise variables h?
- Can you provide wall-clock training times and memory usage for the reported experiments?
