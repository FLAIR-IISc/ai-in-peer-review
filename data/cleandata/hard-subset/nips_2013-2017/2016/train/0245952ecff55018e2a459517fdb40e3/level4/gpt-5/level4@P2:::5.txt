Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper extends Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMNs) to the conditional setting. The proposed Conditional GMMN (CGMMN) learns p(y|x) by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective: the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between the conditional mean embedding operators of the empirical conditional distribution and a deep conditional generator. The approach builds directly on kernel mean embeddings and conditional embeddings (Song et al., 2009; Fukumizu et al.) and on prior MMD-based deep generators (Dziugaite et al.; Li et al.). Training is straightforward via back-propagation because the objective depends on Gram matrices and does not require an adversarial min–max game. Experiments cover predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST digits, Yale Face), and distilling Bayesian “dark knowledge” (Boston Housing) from a teacher (PBP).

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean conditional extension of MMD-based generative learning, with a well-motivated CMMD objective that leverages conditional mean embeddings.
- Simple training pipeline; gradients flow through the generator only, and the inverses of Gram matrices in CMMD do not depend on model parameters.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Practical observations (e.g., minibatch CMMD, use of CNN/MLP backbones, code-space generation with autoencoders) make the method easy to adopt.

Weaknesses and technical concerns:
- Definition of the empirical conditional operator ĈY|X (Line 90): The paper uses ĈY|X = Φ (K + λI)^{-1} Υᵀ, but the earlier definition of CXY includes centering terms (CXY = E[φ(X) ⊗ φ(Y)] − μX ⊗ μY). The estimator for ĈY|X should reflect these mean terms, typically via centered feature maps or centered Gram matrices. Assuming μX = 0 and μY = 0 (i.e., already centered in the RKHS) is unusual unless explicitly stated and implemented via centering. Because CMMD hinges on ĈY|X, please clarify:
  - Whether you center features/Gram matrices in practice, and how (empirical centering, shrinkage, or other).
  - How centering affects Eq. (2) and the gradient computation through L̂CMMD.
  - Any bias/variance implications of omitting centering, and whether results change if centering is applied.
- Empirical evaluation is weaker than the most competitive baselines:
  - Predictive performance: On both MNIST and SVHN, CGMMN/CGMMN-CNN underperforms the state-of-the-art DGM baseline CMMVA.
  - Generative performance: No direct comparison to conditional GANs or conditional VAEs is provided, making it difficult to assess sample quality relative to strong alternatives. The nearest-neighbor sanity check is helpful but insufficient; quantitative metrics (e.g., FID, Inception score) or side-by-side human evaluation would strengthen the case.
- Scalability and design choices: Since CMMD requires inverting batch Gram matrices (cubic in batch size), please discuss runtime/memory tradeoffs, sensitivity to batch size and λ, and kernel choices for X and Y. Clarify whether K^{-1} is recomputed per minibatch, and whether non-differentiable kernels on X are used in practice.
- Minor clarity issues and typos (see below).

Quality:
- The method is technically sound overall, but the handling of centering in ĈY|X is a critical omission that needs explicit treatment. The experimental methodology is reasonable, though limited by the lack of comparative baselines for conditional generation and by trailing predictive results.

Clarity:
- Generally clear exposition of CMMD and its relationship to MMD. Please improve notation consistency (e.g., consistently distinguish datasets Ds and Dd; define all symbols at first use in Eq. (2)). Add implementation details for centering, kernel choices, and computational complexity.

Originality:
- Moderate. The paper combines established conditional kernel embedding theory with deep generators and an MMD-style objective. The conditional extension is natural but useful; the contribution lies in the CMMD training recipe and its application breadth.

Significance:
- Potentially useful for conditional generative modeling without adversarial training, and for distilling Bayesian predictors. Current empirical results suggest promise but fall short of state-of-the-art on prediction and lack strong generative comparisons. Strengthening the evaluation would improve impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clean, principled objective for conditional generative modeling.
- Simple, stable training; broadly applicable to several tasks.
- Attractive alternative to adversarial training and variational bounds.

Cons against acceptance:
- Key technical detail (centering in ĈY|X) not fully specified.
- Predictive results lag behind strong DGM baselines (CMMVA).
- No direct comparisons to conditional GAN/cVAE or quantitative sample quality metrics.
- Limited analysis of scalability and kernel design choices.

Suggestions:
- Clarify and implement proper centering in ĈY|X (or justify omission) and update derivations accordingly.
- Add comparisons to conditional GANs and/or conditional VAEs on MNIST and Yale Faces with standard metrics (FID, Inception score), plus qualitative side-by-sides.
- Report training time, memory, and sensitivity to batch size and λ; discuss kernel selection for X and Y and its impact.
