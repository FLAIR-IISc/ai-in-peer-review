This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional distributions with deep neural networks. The work extends the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework, which learns joint distributions by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between a model distribution and a data distribution. The core contribution of this paper is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective, derived from the theory of kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. This CMMD criterion measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The proposed model generates a sample by passing a conditioning variable and a random noise vector through a deep network. The network parameters are then learned by minimizing the CMMD objective via stochastic gradient descent and back-propagation. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on a variety of tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, contextual image generation on MNIST and the Yale Face dataset, and knowledge distillation from a Bayesian model.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated extension of MMD-based generative models to the conditional setting. This fills an important gap in the moment-matching network literature and provides a compelling alternative to other conditional generative frameworks like conditional GANs and VAEs. The ability to model conditional distributions directly significantly broadens the applicability of this class of models.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed CMMD objective is theoretically well-grounded, building directly upon established work in kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. The connection to the standard MMD is clearly articulated (Remark 1), providing useful intuition.
*   **Experimental Breadth:** The authors evaluate CGMMN on a diverse set of tasks, effectively showcasing its versatility. The experiments on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and particularly the novel application of "Bayesian dark knowledge" distillation, strongly support the claim that this is a flexible and useful framework.
*   **Performance:** The experimental results are competitive. In prediction tasks, CGMMN-CNN achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art deep generative models like CMMVA on both MNIST and SVHN. The qualitative results for image generation are also convincing, demonstrating the model's ability to generate varied and class-conditional samples.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While the overall structure and writing are clear, the details required for full reproducibility are lacking. Key hyperparameters such as the choice of kernel functions for both input and output variables, the kernel bandwidths, and the regularization parameter λ are not specified. Given the sensitivity of kernel methods to these choices, their omission makes it difficult for other researchers to reproduce the results or build upon the work. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including these details in an appendix and, ideally, by releasing the source code.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** The CMMD objective requires the computation and inversion of kernel Gram matrices within each mini-batch, an operation that scales cubically with the batch size. The paper does not provide any analysis of the computational complexity or empirical runtime of the proposed method. Including a discussion of training time, especially in comparison to baselines like CMMVA or conditional GANs, would provide valuable practical insight for potential users of the model.
*   **Scale of Experiments:** The chosen datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Faces) are standard but relatively modest in complexity and scale. To better establish the significance and scalability of CGMMN, it would be beneficial to demonstrate its performance on more challenging, higher-dimensional datasets that are common in recent generative modeling literature, such as CIFAR-10 or CelebA.
*   **Clarity of CMMD Objective:** Section 3.1 is technically dense. While correct, a more intuitive explanation of the final CMMD objective (Equation 2) could improve the paper's accessibility to a broader audience. For instance, explaining how the `K̃⁻¹` terms act to re-weight the sample distances in the output space based on similarities in the input space could be helpful.
