This paper introduces conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMNs), extending GMMNs to conditional generation and prediction. The core idea is to use kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional MMD (CMMD) objective to measure discrepancies between conditional distributions. The method is evaluated on classification, conditional generation, and distillation of Bayesian models.

A straightforward extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would train a GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters through a common neural network. This approach suffers from data sparsity: each condition may have very few examples, and for continuous conditioning variables, some conditions may have only a single example. The proposed method instead treats the set of conditional distributions as a family and directly matches the model to the conditional embedding operator, rather than matching each conditional distribution separately. This strategy addresses sparsity and seems advantageous in general. However, in problems with a small, discrete set of conditions (e.g., MNIST with 10 labels), the naive shared-parameter baseline may still perform reasonably well. Including such a baseline would strengthen the empirical comparisons.

The CMMD objective is well formulated and, as noted in Remark 1, can be computed similarly to the unconditional MMD. The Gram matrices in Equation (2) are independent of the model parameters, allowing backpropagation to proceed as in the unconditional case.

A potential concern is that CMMD may require large minibatches: with small batches, gradient noise from stochasticity could dominate the signal; with large batches, computation becomes expensive, especially due to matrix inverses, which may be prohibitively costly. A discussion of computational complexity, batch-size sensitivity, and any practical tricks used (e.g., regularization, approximations) would be helpful.

Overall, CMMD provides a principled and appealing way to model conditional distributions. The experiments on classification and Bayesian model distillation are promising. That said, parts of the presentation—particularly Sections 2.3 and 3.1—are difficult to follow; I needed to consult prior work to fully understand the derivations and assumptions. More self-contained explanations or additional intuition would improve readability.

Minor issues:
1) Line 77 states that the MMD estimator is unbiased. This is not generally true for the commonly used finite-sample estimator; only the U-statistic form is unbiased. Please clarify which estimator is used and correct the statement.
2) Line 90 defines the conditional embedding operator in a way that appears inconsistent with Reference [29]. Achieving the form used here seems to require a different definition of the cross-covariance operator. Please reconcile the notation and assumptions with [29].

In summary, the paper presents a clear and useful extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting, with a solid objective and interesting applications. Addressing the baseline comparison, computational considerations for CMMD, and clarity of key sections would significantly strengthen the work.
