This paper proposes a novel loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables, building upon the generative moment matching networks approach. The authors leverage conditional kernel mean embeddings to develop this loss function, which is an extension of the unconditional loss function based on the maximum mean discrepancy criterion. The efficacy of the proposed method is demonstrated through a range of generative tasks, including classification, handwritten digit and face generation, and knowledge distillation in Bayesian models.

As I reviewed this paper for the second time, I noticed that the authors have addressed the minor concerns raised in the previous review. These concerns included the choice of a low-dimensional space, the scalability of the method to higher-dimensional latent spaces, and the utility of the model compared to existing approaches. The authors have provided direct responses to these concerns, which is appreciated.

However, I would like to request clarification on Remark 1, specifically regarding the mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form. The dot notation used in this context is unclear, and it would be helpful to specify whether it represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product. Providing additional details to make this mapping more explicit would be beneficial.

Furthermore, I found the choice of notation for the variable C to be somewhat unfortunate, as it is already used to define the conditional embedding. I suggest using a more distinct notation to avoid potential confusion.

The authors refer to Theorems 1 and 2 from other papers, but Theorem 3 lacks a reference. To maintain consistency, it would be best to provide a reference to a paper containing a proof of Theorem 3 or include the proof itself.

The empirical results presented in the paper are satisfactory. Although the authors acknowledge that using this model for supervised learning may not be the most effective application, they argue that it demonstrates the model's versatility. I find this justification reasonable. The application of distilled Bayesian inference is intriguing, but its utility is only demonstrated on a single dataset with a single model. As a result, it is unclear whether this approach will be effective in other scenarios. In my opinion, this method is most likely to gain traction in the context of pure generative modeling.

The experiments conducted in this context are comparable to those in the original GMM paper. The datasets used, which include digits and faces, are sufficient to demonstrate the feasibility of the idea. However, to encourage adoption of this method by others, it would be beneficial to obtain compelling results on more challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN.
