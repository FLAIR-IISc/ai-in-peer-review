This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), an extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core of the proposed method lies in the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) metric for measuring the discrepancy between conditional distributions. The authors evaluate the proposed CGMMN on classification, conditional generation, and distillation of Bayesian models.

A naive approach to extending GMMNs to conditional settings would be to estimate a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters through the use of the same neural network. However, this approach suffers from the data sparsity problem, as each conditional distribution may only have a few examples, or even just one example in the case of continuous conditioning variables. In contrast, the proposed approach treats all conditional distributions as a family and directly matches the model with the conditional embedding operator, rather than matching individual conditional distributions. The advantage of this approach is apparent, but it would be interesting to compare it to the naive approach as a baseline, especially in cases where the conditioning variable takes on a limited number of values, such as in MNIST.

The conditional MMD objective has a nice formulation, and its computation is almost identical to that of the unconditional MMD, as noted in Remark 1. The K matrices in equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, allowing backpropagation to be performed in the same manner as before. One concern with the proposed approach is that the Conditional MMD (CMMD) objective may require large minibatches to avoid stochasticity dominating the error, but large minibatches can make computing the objective expensive, particularly when calculating matrix inverses.

Overall, I believe the CMMD provides a nice way to model conditional distributions. The experimental results on classification and distillation of Bayesian models are also noteworthy. However, the presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 could be improved, as it was not easy to follow and required referencing external papers to understand the underlying concepts. There are a couple of minor issues: (1) the statement on line 77 that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect, and (2) the conditional embedding operator on line 90 is inconsistent with the one defined in reference [29], which would require a different definition of the cross-covariance operator to match the form used in this paper.
