Summary
The paper extends moment matching networks to the conditional setting. Unlike models that can be updated on single examples by simply augmenting inputs with the conditioning variable, this extension is non-trivial because training requires matching distributions of outputs across a mini-batch to a set of diverse conditioning inputs. When the conditioning variables differ across examples, the target distributions are no longer shared, making a naive batch-level moment matching approach inadequate or inefficient.

Strengths
- Addresses a meaningful gap: extending MMD-based training to conditional generative modeling is important and non-trivial.
- The framework appears general and potentially applicable to a range of conditional tasks.
- The idea of leveraging moment matching in conditional spaces is promising and could offer a stable alternative to likelihood-based or adversarial training.

Main weaknesses and clarity issues
- The paper launches into formal derivations without providing intuition for the final objective (Eq. 2). Readers unfamiliar with conditional mean embeddings or RKHS machinery may find it difficult to understand what is being computed and why it is the right quantity to optimize.
- The practical algorithm implied by Eq. (2) is not clearly described. It is unclear how the theoretical objective translates into per-batch computations, how these computations scale, and what approximations are employed.
- Efficiency and complexity are not discussed in sufficient detail. In particular, if the objective involves pairwise kernel evaluations over both inputs and outputs, the naive complexity could be quadratic in batch size; the paper does not clarify whether the method uses random features, mini-batch structuring, or other tricks to reduce this cost.
- The assumptions required to make Eq. (2) tractable and unbiased (or low bias) are not clearly stated. For example, the role of characteristic kernels, independence assumptions, regularization, and whether the method relies on repeated conditionings per batch is unclear.
- The supplemental material contains some of the algorithmic details, but it is hard to follow and does not bridge the gap between the high-level objective and the steps implemented during training.

Requested clarifications and questions to the authors
- Intuitive derivation of Eq. (2): Please provide a step-by-step, non-RKHS explanation of how one would arrive at the conditional objective from first principles. For example:
  - Start from the goal of matching p(y|x) and qθ(y|x) for x drawn from the data distribution.
  - Explain how unconditional MMD applies when x is fixed, and why complications arise when x varies across a mini-batch.
  - Show how the proposed objective aggregates conditional discrepancies across varying x.
- From Eq. (2) to a practical algorithm: Please outline the exact computations performed per training step:
  - Given a batch {(xi, yi)} and generated samples {ŷij ~ qθ(·|xi)}, what matrices/vectors are formed? Which kernels over x and y are used, and how are they combined?
  - Is the objective an unbiased or biased estimator of a population conditional MMD? How is variance controlled?
  - What is the computational and memory complexity in terms of batch size B, number of generated samples per x (m), and feature dimension (or number of random features)?
  - Are there O(B^2) pairwise terms over x and/or y? If so, what approximations or batching strategies are used to keep training efficient?
  - Do you require multiple outputs per conditioning input (m > 1) to form meaningful conditional moment estimates? If yes, how large must m be in practice?
- Handling heterogeneous conditionings within a batch:
  - How do you ensure that matching is carried out “per x” rather than across unrelated conditionings?
  - Do you reuse identical x values across samples in the same update, or does the method leverage a kernel over x to transfer information across nearby conditionings? If the latter, please clarify how the x-kernel bandwidth is chosen and its effect on bias/variance.
- Assumptions and guarantees:
  - What properties of the kernels over x and y are required (e.g., characteristic kernels) for the discrepancy to be a proper metric between conditional distributions?
  - Are there regularization terms (e.g., for kernel matrices) that are critical for stability?
  - Under what conditions does optimization of Eq. (2) imply convergence (or consistent estimation) of qθ(y|x) to p(y|x)?
- Implementation details to add:
  - Pseudocode describing a single update, including sampling strategy, kernel feature computation, objective assembly, and backpropagation.
  - A clear description of how gradients flow through the conditional objective to θ.
  - Any use of random Fourier features or low-rank approximations to reduce cost, and their empirical impact.

Suggestions for improving exposition
- Add an intuition-first section before the formal RKHS treatment. The unconditional MMD objective can be explained without heavy functional analysis; a similar, accessible explanation would greatly help for the conditional case.
- Include a compact derivation that starts from matching conditional expectations of test functions ϕ(y) for each x and then shows how kernelizing over x and y yields Eq. (2).
- Provide a small, didactic example (e.g., 1D x and y) illustrating exactly what is computed in a batch, how the x-kernel couples samples, and how gradients are obtained.
- Report timing and memory usage per iteration versus batch size and m, and compare to a “same-conditional-per-batch” baseline to demonstrate both efficiency and accuracy trade-offs.
- Clarify hyperparameter sensitivity (kernel choices and bandwidths over x and y, number of samples per x).

Minor comments
- Ensure notation is consistent around Eq. (2), especially indices over conditioning variables and generated samples.
- Summarize key steps from the supplement in the main text; the reader should not need the appendix to understand the core algorithm.
- Briefly position the method relative to conditional mean embeddings and prior conditional MMD objectives, highlighting what is novel here.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important and challenging extension of moment matching to conditional models. However, the current presentation obscures the key ideas and practicalities. With a clearer, intuition-driven derivation, explicit algorithmic steps, and a discussion of efficiency and assumptions, the contribution would be much easier to evaluate and likely more impactful.
