This paper introduces conditional generative moment-matching networks (CGMMN), extending GMMNs to conditional prediction and generation. The central idea is to use RKHS embeddings of conditional distributions together with a conditional MMD (CMMD) objective to measure discrepancies between conditional laws. The method is evaluated on classification, conditional image generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. A straightforward extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would be to train a separate GMMN per conditioning value, sharing parameters via a common neural network. However, each conditional slice typically has very few samples—and in the continuous-x case, potentially only a single example—making this naïve strategy data-inefficient. The proposed approach instead treats the set of conditionals as a whole and matches the conditional mean embedding operator directly, rather than matching each conditional distribution independently. This advantage is compelling, though in discrete, low-cardinality settings (e.g., MNIST labels with 10 classes), the naïve baseline might still perform reasonably; including such a comparison would be informative. It is also appealing that CMMD has a clean form and can be computed almost identically to unconditional MMD (as highlighted in Remark 1). In Eq. (2), the K matrices depend only on the conditioning variables and are independent of the model parameters, so gradients can be backpropagated as usual. A practical concern is that CMMD may require large minibatches to reduce stochastic noise; yet large batches make the objective costly, with matrix inversions potentially becoming a computational bottleneck. Overall, CMMD is a nice way to model conditional distributions, and the classification and Bayesian distillation results are interesting. The exposition in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is hard to follow; I had to consult the cited references to fully grasp the details.
