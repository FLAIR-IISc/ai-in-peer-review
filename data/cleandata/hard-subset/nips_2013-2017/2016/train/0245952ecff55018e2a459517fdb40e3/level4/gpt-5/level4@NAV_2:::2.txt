The authors extend moment matching networks to the conditional setting. Unlike models that can be updated on single examples—where conditioning is essentially just feeding the conditioning variables into the network—this extension is nontrivial here because one must match distributions across many samples (a minibatch) to many inputs, and these distributions differ across inputs (unless one reuses the same conditioning variables for a given update, which is inefficient). My primary concern is that the paper leads with heavy formalism instead of first offering intuition for the objective and clearly exposing the computations executed by the algorithm and their computational cost.

The authors extend moment matching networks to a conditional variant. In contrast to single-example update settings where the extension is straightforward, here the challenge is to align distributions for many samples against many conditionings, which are no longer identical across inputs (unless one fixes the same conditioning per update, again inefficient). The main shortcoming is that the paper does not unpack the details behind the final optimized expression (Eq. (2))—its efficiency, and the assumptions required for it to be valid—so the reader cannot see what computations are actually carried out. Similarly, the paper provides formal mathematics first rather than building intuition. The non-conditional version can be explained (as in the original paper) without leaning on reproducing kernel Hilbert space machinery—the minimized statistic is intuitive—and it would help to provide an equally accessible intuition for the conditional case.

It would be valuable to address the following:
- How would one arrive at Eq. (2) from an intuitive perspective?
- What are the exact steps from Eq. (2) to the practical training procedure? Some of this appears in the supplement, but it is difficult to follow.
