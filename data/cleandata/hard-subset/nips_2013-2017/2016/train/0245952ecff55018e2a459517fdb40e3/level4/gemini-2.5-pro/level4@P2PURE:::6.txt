This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), an extension of GMMNs designed for conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core of the proposed method is the use of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions and the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric to measure the discrepancy between them. The CGMMN is evaluated on classification, conditional generation, and the distillation of Bayesian models.

The authors address a key limitation of a naive extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting. Such an approach would estimate a separate GMMN for each condition, with parameters shared through a common neural network. This strategy, however, suffers from data sparsity, particularly when each condition has few associated examples or, in continuous domains, potentially only a single example. In contrast, the proposed approach treats all conditional distributions as a unified family, matching the model to the conditional embedding operator directly rather than to each individual distribution.

While the advantage of the proposed approach is clear, a comparison to the naive baseline would strengthen the paper. For instance, in the conditional MNIST generation task where the conditioning variable is one of 10 discrete values, the naive method might still perform reasonably well and would serve as an important reference.

A significant strength of the method is the elegant formulation of the CMMD objective. As highlighted in Remark 1, it can be computed with a complexity similar to that of the unconditional MMD. Furthermore, because the kernel matrices in Equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, backpropagation can be implemented in the same manner as in a standard GMMN.

One potential concern is that the CMMD objective may require large minibatches to prevent the estimation error from being dominated by stochasticity. Conversely, large minibatches make the objective's computation expensive, as the required matrix inversions could become prohibitive.

Overall, the CMMD framework offers a compelling approach to modeling conditional distributions, and the experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are interesting. However, the presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 lacks clarity, requiring the reader to consult the cited papers to fully grasp the methodology.

I also noted two minor issues:
1.  On line 77, the paper incorrectly states that the MMD estimator is unbiased.
2.  The definition of the conditional embedding operator on line 90 appears inconsistent with that in reference [29]. Achieving the form used in this paper seems to require a different definition of the cross-covariance operator.
