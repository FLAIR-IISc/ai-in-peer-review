This paper introduces a novel loss function tailored for directed generative models with conditional variables. The method builds upon generative moment matching networks, extending their unconditional maximum mean discrepancy (MMD)-based loss to a conditional setting by incorporating techniques from conditional kernel mean embeddings. The effectiveness of this approach is demonstrated across diverse generative tasks, including classification, handwritten digit and face generation, and Bayesian model knowledge distillation.

This is my second review of this manuscript. Previous comments regarding the choice of a low-dimensional latent space and the model's scalability, as well as questions concerning its utility relative to existing methods, have been addressed adequately by the authors. However, a clarification is needed regarding Remark 1: the connection between Equation (2) and the weighted MMD formulation remains unclear. Specifically, the meaning of the dot notation used in this context—whether it represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product—should be explicitly stated to improve readability and understanding. Furthermore, the variable 'C' is used to denote both the conditional embedding and another parameter, which is potentially confusing. Theorems 1 and 2 cite relevant prior work, but Theorem 3 lacks a citation or accompanying proof; a reference to a source containing the proof, or the proof itself, should be included.

The empirical results presented are encouraging. While prior reviews questioned the suitability of applying this model to supervised learning tasks, the authors’ justification – highlighting the model’s versatility – is reasonable. The application to Bayesian inference distillation is intriguing, though its evaluation is currently limited to a single dataset and model, hindering a broader assessment of its generalizability. I anticipate that this approach will find its strongest application within the realm of pure generative modeling, where the experimental performance aligns with that of the original GMM paper. The use of digits and faces provides a sufficient demonstration of the core idea. 

To encourage wider adoption, demonstrating compelling performance on more challenging datasets such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN would be beneficial.
