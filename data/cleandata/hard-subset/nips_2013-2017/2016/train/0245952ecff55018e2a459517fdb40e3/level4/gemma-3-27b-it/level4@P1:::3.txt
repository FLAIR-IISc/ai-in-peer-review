This paper proposes a loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables. The method is an extension of generative moment matching networks (GMMN), which utilizes an unconditional loss function based on the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion. This approach leverages work on conditional kernel mean embeddings to define the loss function, and the results are demonstrated on a variety of generative tasks including predictive modeling, generating handwritten digits and faces, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models. This is my second time reviewing this paper. Previous reviewers raised minor points regarding the dimensionality of the latent space, the model’s utility compared to existing approaches, and some clarifying details, which appear to have been addressed.

Regarding Remark 1, the connection between Equation (2) and the weighted MMD form is not immediately clear. Specifically, the notation used for the dot product requires clarification; is it matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product? Providing more detail to elucidate this mapping would be beneficial. Also, the variable ‘C’ is used to denote both the conditional embedding and a matrix in Equation (2), which could be confusing. Theorem 3 lacks a reference; either a citation to a paper containing a proof should be included or a proof should be provided within the manuscript.

The empirical results are solid. Previous reviews questioned the suitability of using this model for supervised learning, but the authors rightly point out that this application serves to demonstrate the model’s versatility, which is acceptable. The distilled Bayesian inference application is intriguing, but its evaluation is limited to a single dataset and model, making it difficult to assess its generalizability. I believe this approach would be most impactful in the realm of pure generative modeling. The experiments presented, similar to those in the original GMMN paper, demonstrate effectiveness on digits and faces, which is sufficient to establish proof of concept.

For broader adoption, compelling results on more challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN, would be necessary.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a logically sound extension of GMMN to the conditional setting.
*   The technical contributions – the derivation of the CMMD criterion and its efficient implementation via backpropagation – are significant.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the method’s competitiveness across diverse tasks.
*   The authors have addressed concerns raised in previous reviews.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification for Theorem 3 is missing.
*   The connection between the CMMD objective and weighted MMD requires further clarification.
*   The application to Bayesian distillation is limited in scope.
*   Evaluation on more challenging generative datasets is needed to demonstrate scalability and broader impact.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling. The proposed CMMD criterion provides a principled way to learn conditional distributions, and the experimental results are encouraging. Addressing the minor theoretical concerns and expanding the evaluation to more complex datasets would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
