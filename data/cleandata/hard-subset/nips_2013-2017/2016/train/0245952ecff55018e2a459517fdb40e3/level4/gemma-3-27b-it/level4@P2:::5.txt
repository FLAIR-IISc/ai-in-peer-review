This paper introduces conditional generative moment-matching networks (CGMMN) as an extension of prior work on generative moment matching networks (GMMN). The core idea is to learn conditional distributions by matching moments in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), building upon the framework established by Song et al. (2009). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN across a range of tasks including predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. 

The paper clearly outlines the motivation for focusing on conditional distributions, highlighting their advantages over joint distributions in scenarios like predictive modeling (compared to approaches in, e.g., Ng & Jordan, 2001) and contextual generation (building on work like Denton et al., 2015, and Mirza & Osindero, 2014). The formulation using conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) is a natural extension of the MMD criterion used in GMMN (Dziugaite et al., 2015; Li et al., 2015), and the authors explain how to efficiently compute gradients via backpropagation. 

A significant strength of this work is its theoretical grounding in kernel methods and the Hilbert space embedding framework. The authors present a clear derivation of the CMMD criterion and demonstrate its connection to existing theory. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering diverse tasks and datasets.

However, a point requiring clarification initially concerned the definition of the conditional embedding operator (specifically, the term involving $\mu_X$ and $\mu_Y$ in Line 90). The initial assumption of centering the data in the RKHS was somewhat unusual and warranted further explanation regarding its impact on gradient computation and empirical results. (This concern was addressed in the author rebuttal.) Furthermore, while the results are promising, they are not always state-of-the-art. The predictive performance on the MNIST and SVHN datasets is slightly higher than that of comparable methods like CMMVA (Li et al., 2015) and DSN (Lee et al., 2015), and the generative results lack a direct comparison to other conditional generative models like conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014). The paper also contained minor typographical errors (e.g., "Frobeniu norm”, "an universal”, “which the”). (These have been noted in the update after author rebuttal.)

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting is a valuable contribution, offering a new approach to conditional generative modeling.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The work is well-grounded in kernel methods and provides a rigorous mathematical formulation of the CMMD criterion.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The method is demonstrated across multiple tasks and datasets, suggesting its potential for wider application.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Performance:** While competitive, the results are not consistently state-of-the-art on all tasks.
*   **Comparison to Alternatives:** The lack of direct comparison to other conditional generative models limits the assessment of the method’s advantages.
*   **Initial Clarification Needed:** The original definition of the conditional embedding operator needed clarification. (Resolved by author rebuttal)
*   **Minor Typographical Errors:** The presence of minor typos detracted slightly from the overall polish of the paper. (Corrected in the author rebuttal)

**Overall:**

This paper presents a significant and well-motivated contribution to the field of generative modeling. While the experimental results are not uniformly superior to existing methods, the theoretical soundness and broad applicability of the approach make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS program. The authors have thoughtfully addressed initial concerns raised regarding the conditional embedding operator, further strengthening the paper. I recommend acceptance.
