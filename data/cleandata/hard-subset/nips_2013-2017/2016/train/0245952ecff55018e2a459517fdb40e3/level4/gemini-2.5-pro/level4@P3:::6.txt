### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMNs), a novel framework for learning conditional probability distributions. The work extends the recently proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMNs), which learn joint distributions by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model and data distributions. The core contribution of this paper is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric. This metric measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective kernel mean embedding operators. The CGMMN model is a deep neural network that takes a conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to generate a sample `y`. The network's parameters are learned via backpropagation by minimizing the CMMD objective. The authors demonstrate the versatility and effectiveness of their approach on a diverse set of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual image generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

### Strengths of the Paper

1.  **Novel and Principled Contribution:** The paper addresses a clear and important problem: extending the moment-matching framework for deep generative models to the conditional setting. The proposed CMMD objective is a natural and elegant generalization of MMD, based on the solid theoretical foundation of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions. This provides a compelling alternative to other conditional generative models like conditional GANs and VAEs.

2.  **Stable Training Objective:** A significant advantage of the moment-matching framework, which this work inherits and extends, is the avoidance of the unstable min-max optimization required for training GANs. The CGMMN is trained by minimizing a single, well-defined CMMD objective, which can be optimized using standard stochastic gradient descent and backpropagation.

3.  **Broad Applicability and Strong Empirical Results:** The authors validate their method on a wide range of applications, successfully demonstrating its flexibility. The competitive classification error rates on MNIST and SVHN show its effectiveness as a predictive model. The qualitative results on conditional image generation are convincing, and the application to distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model is an interesting and novel use case.

4.  **Clear Intuition:** Remark 1 provides an excellent and intuitive explanation of the relationship between CMMD and MMD. It clarifies that CMMD can be viewed as applying non-uniform weights (derived from the conditioning variables) to the Gram matrices, in contrast to the uniform weighting implicit in MMD. This insight helps connect the new theory to more familiar concepts.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Sections:** The presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1, which introduces the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the CMMD metric, is quite dense. For readers not already expert in this specific area, it is difficult to fully grasp the concepts without consulting the cited papers. The manuscript would be significantly improved by adding more intuition and a more self-contained explanation of the operators and the derivation of the final CMMD estimator (Equation 2).

2.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** A practical concern with the proposed CMMD objective is its computational complexity. The calculation requires inverting N x N kernel matrices within each minibatch, an operation that scales cubically (O(N³)) with the minibatch size N. This creates a difficult trade-off: a larger minibatch is desirable for a more stable estimate of the CMMD gradient, but it quickly becomes computationally prohibitive. The authors should explicitly discuss this limitation and its implications for scaling the model. Mentioning potential approximation techniques (e.g., random Fourier features, Nyström method) to mitigate this cost would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** For the conditional generation tasks on MNIST (where the conditioning variable is a discrete class label), a natural and straightforward baseline is missing. One could train a separate GMMN for each of the 10 classes, possibly sharing some network parameters. A comparison against such an approach would more directly highlight the benefits of the proposed method, which jointly models the entire family of conditional distributions through the embedding operator.

### Minor Issues and Questions for Clarification

*   On line 77, the paper claims that the empirical squared MMD estimator is asymptotically unbiased. The commonly used V-statistic for squared MMD is known to be biased for finite samples, though an unbiased U-statistic estimator does exist. Please clarify which estimator is being referred to or correct the statement for accuracy.
*   On line 90, the formulation of the empirical conditional embedding operator, `Ĉ_Y|X = Φ(K+λI)⁻¹Υᵀ`, appears to differ slightly from the definition in the primary reference [29], which is `Ĉ_YX (Ĉ_XX + λI)⁻¹`. This seems to imply a different definition of the cross-covariance operator. A brief clarification or justification for this formulation would be helpful.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on deep generative models. The proposed CGMMN framework is theoretically sound, practically useful, and demonstrated to be effective across a variety of challenging tasks. While the presentation of the core theory could be made more accessible and a discussion on computational scalability would be beneficial, the strengths of the work far outweigh these weaknesses. The paper introduces an important new tool for probabilistic modeling and is of clear interest to the NIPS community.
