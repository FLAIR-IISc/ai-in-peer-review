Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) that learns conditional distributions p(y|x). The key idea is to introduce a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective that measures the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between the conditional mean embedding operators of the empirical data and the model. A conditional generator takes both x and a noise vector h, passes them through a deep network, and produces y; parameters are trained via backpropagation to minimize CMMD. The approach retains the simplicity of GMMN (no adversarial min–max) while enabling conditional modeling.

This work sits at the intersection of kernel mean embeddings/MMD-based learning [Gretton et al.; Song et al.] and deep generative modeling. It complements conditional variants of GANs and VAEs (e.g., CGAN, CVAE) by offering a kernel-based, likelihood-free objective that does not require a discriminator or variational bound. Compared to prior unconditional GMMN, the paper contributes a conditional formulation via operator-valued embeddings and a practical empirical estimator amenable to SGD.

Methods and experiments:
- Objective: CMMD is defined as the squared RKHS norm between conditional embedding operators, with an empirical estimator expressed using Gram matrices over x and y (with Tikhonov regularization). This yields a differentiable loss in terms of Gram matrices on outputs and inverse Gram matrices on inputs, enabling minibatch training.
- Architecture: A DNN maps [x; h] to y. Both MLP and CNN backbones are used. Sampling is straightforward by drawing h and conditioning on x.
- Tasks:
  1) Predictive modeling: classification on MNIST and SVHN using CNN/MLP backbones; predictions are argmax of generated y.
  2) Conditional generation: class-conditional MNIST images and Yale Face generation; also an autoencoder+CGMMN variant to generate in code space for smoother samples.
  3) Bayesian dark knowledge: distillation of a Bayesian neural network (PBP) on Boston Housing; the student CGMMN matches the teacher’s predictive performance.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity and technical soundness: CMMD is a natural and well-justified conditional analogue of MMD using conditional mean embeddings; the estimator is explicit and differentiable.
- Simplicity and stability: avoids adversarial training and variational bounds; training reduces to standard backprop with minibatches.
- Generality: works with diverse architectures (MLP/CNN) and tasks (classification, conditional image generation, Bayesian distillation).
- Empirical competitiveness: classification error on MNIST/SVHN is comparable to state-of-the-art deep generative classifiers (e.g., MMVA/CMMVA) without data augmentation or ensembling; generation is diverse and label-consistent; distilled student matches teacher RMSE on Boston Housing.
- Practicality: Algorithm 1 is easy to implement; sampling is trivial; the framework can distill any Bayesian teacher that provides predictive samples.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Computational cost and scalability: CMMD involves inverses of input Gram matrices with O(n^3) cost per batch. While minibatching mitigates this, a discussion of memory/time trade-offs and batch-size sensitivity would be helpful, especially for high-dimensional x.
- Kernel choices and hyperparameters: Performance can be sensitive to kernel families, bandwidths, and the regularization λ. The paper would benefit from a principled strategy (e.g., median heuristic, cross-validation) and an ablation on kernel choices for x and y.
- Assumptions and approximation: The theoretical justification assumes the conditional embedding operator lies in the tensor product RKHS; in practice the empirical estimator is used. Clarifying when this approximation is robust, and how λ affects bias/variance, would strengthen the exposition.
- Evaluation depth for generation: Generation quality is shown qualitatively; quantitative metrics (e.g., nearest-neighbor analyses are provided, but no FID/Inception-type metrics) and comparisons to CGAN/CVAE on the same setups would give a more complete picture.
- Limited ablations: More analysis on the role of noise dimensionality, concatenation vs additive corruption of x, and the effect of the number of generated samples per x would inform best practices.
- Predictive modeling details: Clarify the loss used for classification (CMMD only vs any auxiliary losses), calibration of y outputs, and whether temperature scaling or averaging multiple samples at test time helps.

Questions for the authors:
- How are kernel hyperparameters for x and y selected in practice? Do different kernels for inputs vs outputs materially change performance?
- What is the computational complexity per minibatch (time/memory) as a function of batch size and feature dimensions, and how does this compare to standard cross-entropy training?
- For classification, does averaging multiple y samples per x improve accuracy or calibration?
- Can the approach handle structured outputs y (e.g., sequences) via appropriate kernels, and what are the practical limitations?

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The method appears technically sound, with clear derivations and a practical estimator; experiments are sensible and show competitive results. I did not verify every derivation line-by-line.
- Clarity: The paper is well organized and readable; the appendix provides useful details. Minor clarifications as noted above would help.
- Originality: Extending GMMN to conditional distributions via CMMD and operator-valued embeddings is a natural yet nontrivial contribution; the empirical estimator and training recipe are useful additions.
- Significance: The framework broadens the applicability of MMD-based deep generative modeling to conditional tasks and provides a simple alternative to CGAN/CVAE, with practical value for both prediction and distillation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, principled extension of MMD-based generative learning to conditional settings.
- Simple, stable training with broad applicability and competitive results.
- Useful addition to the toolkit for conditional generation and Bayesian model distillation.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Computational overhead of kernel Gram inversions and limited discussion of scalability/hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Generative evaluation could be more comprehensive and comparative.

Overall, this is a clear, technically sound, and practically useful contribution that extends MMD-based deep generative models to conditional settings with a simple and general training criterion.
