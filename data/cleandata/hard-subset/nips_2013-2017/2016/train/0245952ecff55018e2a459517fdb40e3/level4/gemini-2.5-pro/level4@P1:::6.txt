This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) designed to model conditional distributions. The core contribution is a novel training objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which is derived from the kernel embedding of conditional distributions. This metric allows the model to measure the discrepancy between two conditional distributions. The authors demonstrate the utility of CGMMN on a variety of tasks, including classification, conditional generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

The paper effectively addresses a key limitation of a naive approach to conditional GMMNs. A simple extension would be to estimate a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, `p(y|x)`, while sharing parameters across the underlying neural networks. However, this approach suffers from data sparsity, especially when the conditioning variable `x` is continuous, as each specific condition may have very few or even a single training example. The proposed CMMD method elegantly sidesteps this issue by treating the conditional distributions `p(y|x)` as a single family and matching the model's conditional embedding operator directly to the data's, rather than matching each individual conditional distribution.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a principled and novel extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. The development of the CMMD objective is a significant contribution that greatly expands the applicability of GMMN-style models to a wider range of important machine learning problems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** A key strength is the elegant formulation of the CMMD objective (Eq. 2). As noted in Remark 1, its computation is analogous to the standard MMD objective, and the fact that the kernel matrices on the conditioning variables (`K` matrices) are independent of the model parameters simplifies gradient computation via backpropagation.

3.  **Methodological Soundness:** The motivation for moving from matching individual distributions to matching the conditional embedding operator is clear and well-argued, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN across a diverse set of tasks. The results on classification are competitive, and the application to distilling Bayesian models is a particularly interesting and novel use case.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity:** The presentation of the theoretical background could be improved, particularly in Sections 2.3 and 3.1. The concepts of conditional mean embedding and the associated operators are central to the paper's contribution, but the current explanation is quite dense and may be difficult for non-experts to follow. I had to consult the cited references (e.g., [29]) to fully grasp the technical details. Providing more intuition behind the formulation would enhance the paper's accessibility.

2.  **Computational Concerns:** A practical concern is the trade-off between minibatch size and computational cost. The CMMD objective, like MMD, may require large minibatches to obtain a stable gradient estimate, as stochasticity can otherwise dominate. However, the cubic complexity of computing the kernel matrix inverse makes using large minibatches computationally prohibitive. It would be helpful for the authors to discuss this trade-off and any strategies they used to manage it in their experiments (e.g., choice of minibatch size, regularization).

3.  **Missing Baseline:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including a comparison to the "naive" baseline discussed earlier. For instance, in the conditional MNIST generation task where the conditioning variable is a discrete class label, one could train a separate GMMN for each of the 10 classes, with all networks sharing parameters. While the authors argue against this approach on theoretical grounds, an empirical comparison would provide a more concrete demonstration of CGMMN's advantages in a setting where this baseline is feasible.

4.  **Minor Technical Inaccuracies:** There appear to be a couple of minor points that require clarification or correction:
    *   On line 77, the paper states that the MMD estimator is unbiased. This is not true for the biased V-statistic presented in the paper.
    *   The empirical estimator for the conditional embedding operator on line 90 (`ĈY |X = Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤`) does not seem to directly match the definition provided in the cited work [29]. Deriving this form may require a different definition of the cross-covariance operator, which should be clarified for the sake of reproducibility and theoretical correctness.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and well-motivated method for learning conditional distributions. The CMMD framework is an elegant and significant extension of moment-matching techniques. While the experimental results are promising, the paper could be improved by enhancing the clarity of the theoretical sections, addressing practical computational concerns, and including a comparison to a simpler baseline. Despite these points, the contribution is solid and of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
