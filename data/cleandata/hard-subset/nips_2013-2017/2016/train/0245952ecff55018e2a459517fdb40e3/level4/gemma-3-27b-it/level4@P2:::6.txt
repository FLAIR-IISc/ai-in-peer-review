This paper introduces conditional generative moment-matching networks (CGMMNs), extending generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) to address conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core idea leverages kernel embedding of conditional distributions and a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) metric to measure discrepancies between distributions. The authors evaluate CGMMNs on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, demonstrating competitive performance across these applications. 

The paper addresses a key limitation of naively extending GMMNs to conditional settings – data sparsity when estimating GMMNs for each conditional distribution, especially with continuous conditioning variables. Instead of matching individual conditional distributions, CGMMN aims to match the model with the conditional embedding operator, representing all conditional distributions as a family. This approach is conceptually sound and offers a potential advantage, although a comparison to the simpler baseline of individual GMMNs, particularly for tasks like MNIST with a limited number of conditioning classes, would strengthen the evaluation. The CMMD objective function is elegantly formulated and efficiently computable, building upon the existing MMD framework. The authors correctly note that the kernel matrices involved are independent of the model parameters, facilitating standard backpropagation.

A potential concern is the computational cost associated with the CMMD objective. Achieving accurate estimation may necessitate large mini-batches, which can be computationally expensive, particularly the matrix inversions. Further discussion of strategies for mitigating this, perhaps through approximation techniques or efficient linear algebra libraries, would be beneficial. Overall, the CMMD criterion presents a principled approach to modeling conditional distributions. The experimental results, particularly those demonstrating successful Bayesian model distillation, are promising.

However, certain sections of the paper require improvement in clarity. Sections 2.3 and 3.1 are dense and challenging to follow, requiring reference to external papers for complete understanding. More detailed explanations of the underlying concepts would significantly enhance readability. There are a couple of minor technical inaccuracies: on line 77, the claim that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect; it is consistent but not unbiased. Furthermore, on line 90, the conditional embedding operator definition appears inconsistent with the formulation presented in the cited reference [29]. Reconciling this discrepancy or providing a more explicit derivation would enhance the paper’s rigor.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of MMD and kernel methods to conditional generative modeling.
*   Addresses a significant issue of data sparsity in conditional GMMNs.
*   Competitive experimental results across diverse tasks.
*   Elegant formulation of the CMMD objective function.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity issues in sections 2.3 and 3.1.
*   Minor technical inaccuracies regarding MMD bias and the conditional embedding operator.
*   Lack of comparison to a naive baseline of individual GMMNs for conditional distributions.
*   Potential computational challenges associated with large mini-batches.

**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling. While the clarity of certain sections and minor technical points require attention, the novel approach, strong experimental results, and theoretical justification suggest a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above. Specifically, expanding the explanations in sections 2.3 and 3.1 and resolving the discrepancies regarding the MMD estimator and the conditional embedding operator are crucial. A comparison to a baseline of individual GMMNs would further strengthen the paper's contribution.
