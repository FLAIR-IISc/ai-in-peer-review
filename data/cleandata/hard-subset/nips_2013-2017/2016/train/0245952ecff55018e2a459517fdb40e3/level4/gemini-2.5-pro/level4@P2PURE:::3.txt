This paper proposes a loss function for directed generative models that incorporate conditional variables. The method is a direct extension of generative moment matching networks (GMMNs), which use an unconditional loss based on the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion. To achieve this, the authors leverage conditional kernel mean embeddings to formulate the conditional loss function. The approach is demonstrated on a variety of tasks, including classification, generation of handwritten digits and faces, and knowledge distillation for Bayesian models.

This is my second time reviewing this submission. The concerns raised by reviewers in the previous round were minor and appear to have been directly addressed in this revision. These included questions about the use of a low-dimensional latent space, the potential for scaling to higher dimensions, and the model's comparative utility.

While the paper has improved, several points require further clarification:

1.  **Remark 1:** The derivation mapping Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form is unclear. I would suggest the authors provide a more detailed explanation to make this connection more explicit. The dot notation used here is also ambiguous; please clarify whether it represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product.
2.  **Notation:** The choice of 'C' for a constant in the weighted MMD form is unfortunate, as 'C' is already used to denote the conditional embedding operator. Using a different symbol would improve readability.
3.  **Theorem 3:** Theorems 1 and 2 appropriately cite prior work. However, Theorem 3 is presented without a reference or a proof. A citation to a paper containing the proof must be provided, or a proof should be included in the appendix.

The empirical results are solid and serve as a good proof of concept. The authors' justification for including a supervised learning task—to highlight the model's versatility—is reasonable, even if it is not the most compelling application. The distilled Bayesian inference experiment is interesting, but its evaluation on a single dataset with a single model makes it difficult to assess its general usefulness.

The approach seems most promising for pure generative modeling tasks. In these experiments, the model's performance is comparable to that of the original GMMN paper on standard digit and face datasets, which is sufficient to demonstrate that the core idea is effective. For the method to gain wider adoption, however, its utility would need to be demonstrated with compelling results on more challenging, higher-dimensional datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN.
