Summary
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) from modeling joint distributions to modeling conditional distributions p(y|x). The key idea is to train a conditional generator by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective derived from kernel conditional mean embeddings. The generator takes both x and a noise vector h and maps them through a deep network to produce y; training proceeds via back-propagation. The paper evaluates CGMMN on supervised prediction (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST digits conditioned on labels, Yale Faces), and a proof-of-concept application to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (distilling a PBP model on Boston Housing). Results are competitive with prior DGMs and demonstrate the method’s versatility.

Relation to prior work
CGMMN builds directly on MMD-based deep generative modeling (GMMN) by replacing the unconditional MMD loss with a CMMD loss on conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., Fukumizu et al.). Compared to GANs and conditional GANs, CMMD yields a single, non-adversarial objective and simpler optimization. It is also related to conditional VAEs, but uses a kernel-based discrepancy rather than a variational bound. The conditional operator view is standard in the kernel literature; applying it as a training signal for conditional deep generators is a natural but meaningful extension.

Quality
- Theory: The use of CMMD as the objective is principled. However, Theorem 3 (equating equality of conditional embedding operators with equality of conditional distributions) is stated without a citation or proof; a reference to a work providing the proof, or a sketch with assumptions (e.g., universality, measurability, and operator-class conditions) is needed. The discussion around the existence/regularization of the empirical operator is appropriate but could be tightened.
- Algorithm: The minibatch training algorithm is clear. The observation that K̃−1 on x does not depend on network parameters is useful.
- Empirics: Classification results on MNIST/SVHN are competitive with DGM baselines (e.g., MMVA). Conditional generation on MNIST and Yale demonstrates controllable synthesis. The Bayesian distillation example is intriguing but limited to one dataset/model; broader evaluation would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, and most clarifications raised in an earlier review round (latent dimensionality choice, scalability to higher-dimensional latent spaces, and utility relative to alternatives) have been addressed. One part remains unclear:

- Remark 1: The mapping from Eq. (2) to a “weighted MMD” form needs explicit derivation. Please define the “dot” operator unambiguously (matrix multiplication vs. Hadamard product), show the exact weights (C1, C2, C3) in terms of K̃−1 and cross-Gram matrices, and explain the conditions under which this reduces to MMD when weights are uniform. Also, consider avoiding the symbol C for the weighting matrices since C is already used for conditional operators.

Originality
The paper makes a straightforward but novel contribution: it translates unconditional MMD training of deep generators into the conditional setting via conditional mean embeddings and the CMMD loss. While conditional GANs and conditional VAEs exist, this CMMD-based approach is distinct and simplifies training by avoiding adversarial optimization.

Significance
The method is broadly applicable to conditional modeling tasks. Its main practical appeal is a stable, back-propagation-friendly loss for conditional generators. For wide adoption, more evidence on challenging conditional generation benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10 class-conditional, LSUN attributes) and broader distillation studies would be valuable. As it stands, the paper convincingly demonstrates feasibility but stops short of establishing state-of-the-art gains.

Specific comments and questions for the authors
- Remark 1 and Eq. (2): Please provide a step-by-step derivation of the weighted-MMD interpretation. Clarify the “dot” operator, define C1, C2, C3 explicitly, and explain their dependence on K̃−1.
- Notation: Avoid overloading C; use different symbols for conditional operators and weighting matrices.
- Theorem 3: Add a citation to a source containing a proof (or include a proof in the appendix) and list precise regularity assumptions (e.g., universality of kernels on X and Y, conditions ensuring CY|X ∈ F ⊗ G and invertibility/regularization of CXX).
- Computational aspects: Discuss the cubic cost in minibatch size for forming K̃−1 and practical limits. Any use of low-rank approximations, blockwise updates, or Nyström features?
- Kernel choices and regularization: Report sensitivity to kernel type and bandwidth on x and y, and to λ. Are mismatched kernels detrimental? How are bandwidths selected?
- Ablations: Provide ablations on latent dimensionality h and the effect of different ways to combine x and h (concatenation vs. additive noise).
- Evaluation of generation: Beyond nearest neighbors, consider quantitative metrics available at the time (e.g., Parzen-window log-likelihood estimates) and more challenging datasets to demonstrate scalability.
- Distillation: Expand beyond Boston/PBP—e.g., different Bayesian teachers and datasets—to assess generality.

Pros
- Principled extension of GMMN to conditional modeling via CMMD.
- Simple, stable training objective; no adversarial optimization.
- Compatible with both MLP and CNN architectures.
- Demonstrates versatility across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Addresses several concerns from earlier reviews.

Cons
- Theorem 3 lacks citation/proof; some notation is ambiguous/overloaded.
- Limited scope of generative experiments; primarily digits and faces.
- Distillation evaluation is narrow (single dataset/model).
- Computational and kernel-selection trade-offs not fully analyzed.
- Supervised learning may not best showcase the method’s strengths.

Overall
A clear and useful contribution that brings kernel conditional mean embeddings to conditional deep generative modeling with a tractable loss. With improved clarity around Remark 1, a proper reference or proof for Theorem 3, and stronger experiments on more challenging conditional generation and distillation scenarios, this work would be compelling. As is, it demonstrates a solid idea with competitive results and good potential for broader impact.
