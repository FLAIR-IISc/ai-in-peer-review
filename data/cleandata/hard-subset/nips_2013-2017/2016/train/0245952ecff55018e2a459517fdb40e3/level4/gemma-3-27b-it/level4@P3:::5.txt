This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) as an extension of existing Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to learn conditional distributions. The core idea leverages kernel mean embeddings to define and optimize a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion, allowing for straightforward training via backpropagation. The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, demonstrating competitive performance across these tasks.

The paper builds upon the successful application of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for deep generative modeling, initially presented by Gretton et al. (2012), and extends it to the conditional setting. Related work includes Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and their conditional variants (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), as well as GMMNs (Li et al., 2015). The novelty lies in adapting the moment-matching approach to conditional distributions, offering a potentially simpler training process than adversarial methods. The use of kernel embeddings, rooted in the work of Song et al. (2009), provides a theoretically grounded approach to distribution comparison.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting, offering a potentially valuable alternative to GANs for conditional generation.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The method is grounded in established theoretical concepts like kernel mean embeddings and MMD, providing a solid mathematical basis.
*   **Simplicity:** The training process, relying on backpropagation, is relatively straightforward compared to the min-max optimization in GANs.
*   **Versatility:** The evaluation on diverse tasks—predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation—demonstrates the applicability of CGMMN to a range of problems.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and explains the concepts in a relatively accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Results:** While the results are competitive, they don’t consistently outperform state-of-the-art methods. On the MNIST predictive task, CGMMN exhibits higher error rates than CMMVA (Li et al., 2015).
*   **Limited Comparison:** The lack of direct comparison with other conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs) makes it difficult to fully assess the advantages of CGMMN.
*   **Potential Issue with Implementation:** The original submission had an omission in the formulation of  $\hat{C}{Y|X}$ (Line 90) regarding the terms related to $\muX$ and $\muY$, which, while addressed in the rebuttal, suggests a potential area of oversight. This requires careful consideration of the impact on gradient computation and practical results.
*   **Typographical Errors:** The original submission contained minor typographical errors (e.g., "Frobeniu norm," "an universal"), although these are not significant.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of generative modeling.
*   The method is theoretically well-motivated and offers a potentially simpler alternative to GANs.
*   The experimental evaluation, despite not achieving state-of-the-art results, demonstrates the applicability and competitiveness of the approach.
*   The authors responded to the reviewer’s concern and provided clear explanations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental results do not consistently demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The lack of comparison with more recent and relevant conditional generative models limits the assessment of the method’s true potential.
*   The initial implementation detail regarding $\hat{C}{Y|X}$ raises concerns about carefulness, even though it was addressed in the rebuttal.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the NIPS conference. While the experimental results are not groundbreaking, the novelty of the approach, its theoretical foundation, and the clear presentation justify acceptance. The authors have successfully extended the GMMN framework to the conditional setting, offering a potentially valuable tool for researchers in generative modeling. The responsiveness to the review and the clarification of the implementation detail further strengthen the case for acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
