

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the framework of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to learn conditional distributions using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. Building on the work of Li et al. (2015) on GMMN, the authors develop a principled approach for conditional generative modeling that maintains the simplicity of the original MMD-based training while enabling applications requiring conditional generation. The paper makes a clear connection to related work in conditional generative models, including conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional variational autoencoders (Soň et al., 2015), while demonstrating how CGMMN provides a simpler training procedure than GAN-based approaches.

The paper's technical contribution is well-motivated and addresses an important gap in the literature. The authors establish a theoretical foundation for conditional kernel mean embeddings and CMMD, proving that when the conditional embedding operators match, the conditional distributions are identical (Theorem 3). This theoretical development is well-executed and provides a solid basis for their empirical approach. The generative architecture is straightforward: the network takes both conditional inputs and random noise as inputs, passes them through a deep network to produce target samples. The training procedure via stochastic gradient descent with backpropagation is simple and well-suited to deep learning frameworks, avoiding the min-max optimization challenges of GANs.

Regarding quality, the paper demonstrates good technical soundness. The CMMD objective is properly derived, and the authors provide a detailed algorithm for training CGMMN. The experimental evaluation covers multiple relevant application areas: predictive modeling (on MNIST and SVHN datasets), contextual generation (on MNIST and Yale Face datasets), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (on Boston housing dataset). The results are competitive with state-of-the-art methods in each domain, with the CGMMN-CNN model achieving particularly strong performance on SVHN (3.13% error rate compared to CMMVA's 3.09%). The authors properly acknowledge limitations, noting that while the CGMMN framework is compatible with various architectures, performance can be enhanced with more sophisticated structures.

The paper's clarity is generally good, with clear mathematical formulations and well-organized sections. However, the theoretical exposition in Sections 2.2 and 2.3 could be more accessible to readers unfamiliar with kernel embedding techniques. The connection between the theoretical framework and the practical implementation could be strengthened with more explicit explanations of how the CMMD objective is computed in practice.

The originality of this work is significant. While conditional GANs have been explored (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), CGMMN offers a fundamentally different training approach that avoids the instability of adversarial training. The paper clearly articulates how CGMMN differs from previous work, particularly in its use of MMD as a training criterion rather than adversarial learning. The authors appropriately cite relevant prior work, including the foundational MMD work of Gretton et al. (2012) and the GMMN framework of Li et al. (2015).

In terms of significance, CGMMN addresses a practical need in deep learning: the ability to learn conditional distributions without the computational and stability challenges of GANs. The applications demonstrated (predictive modeling, contextual generation, and knowledge distillation) are compelling and relevant to current research directions. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is particularly novel, showing how CGMMN can distill knowledge from Bayesian models without significant loss in predictive performance.

The paper would be strengthened by addressing a few limitations. First, the authors could provide more detailed computational comparisons, particularly regarding training time relative to other methods like DSN, which is mentioned as a strong baseline. The paper's claim that "the training process is simple" could be better substantiated with explicit timing comparisons. Second, the experiments on MNIST and SVHN use relatively small architectures compared to recent state-of-the-art methods, which might limit the perceived impact of the approach. While the authors acknowledge this in the discussion, more thorough comparison with larger architectures would strengthen the significance claim.

The paper's main weakness is the limited discussion of computational complexity. While the authors note that "the computation cost for kernel gram matrix grows cubically with the sample size," they do not provide concrete timing information or discuss practical limitations for larger datasets. This is particularly relevant given the paper's claim of simplicity in the training process. The authors should clarify whether their approach remains practical for large-scale applications.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on deep generative models. The theoretical foundation is solid, the approach is novel compared to existing conditional generative methods, and the empirical results demonstrate competitive performance across multiple applications. The paper would benefit from more detailed computational analysis and potentially larger-scale experiments, but these are minor concerns given the strong technical content and clear contribution to the field. The work represents a valuable extension of the GMMN framework that should be of interest to the NIPS community working on generative models.
