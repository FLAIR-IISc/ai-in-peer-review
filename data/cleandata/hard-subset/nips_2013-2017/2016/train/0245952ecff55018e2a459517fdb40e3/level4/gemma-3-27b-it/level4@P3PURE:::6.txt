This paper presents conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMNs), an extension of generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core idea is to utilize kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) metric to measure discrepancies between them. The authors evaluate CGMMNs on classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation. A naive extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would involve estimating a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters via a common neural network. However, this approach suffers from data sparsity, particularly when conditioning on continuous variables where each conditional distribution may be supported by only a single example. The proposed CGMMN addresses this by treating the collection of conditional distributions as a family and directly matching the model with the conditional embedding operator, rather than individual conditional distributions. While the advantage of this approach is clear, the naive method can perform reasonably well in certain cases, such as conditional generation with a limited number of conditioning values (e.g., 10 values as in MNIST). A comparison to this naive baseline would be a valuable addition to the experimental evaluation. The formulation of the conditional MMD objective is elegant and, as noted in Remark 1, can be computed with comparable ease to the unconditional MMD. Importantly, the K matrices in equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, allowing for standard backpropagation. A potential concern is that the CMMD objective may require large minibatches to mitigate the influence of stochasticity; however, large minibatches can be computationally expensive, especially the matrix inversions required for objective evaluation. Overall, the CGMMN offers a promising approach to modeling conditional distributions, and the experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are compelling. The presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 was somewhat difficult to follow, requiring reference to the cited papers for full comprehension. There are also a couple of minor issues: (1) line 77 states that the MMD estimator is unbiased, which is incorrect; (2) line 90 describes a conditional embedding operator inconsistent with the definition provided in reference [29]. Achieving the form used in this paper appears to necessitate a different definition of the cross-covariance operator.
