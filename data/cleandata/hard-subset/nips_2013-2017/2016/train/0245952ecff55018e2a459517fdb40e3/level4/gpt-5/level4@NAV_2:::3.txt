This paper introduces a loss for conditional directed generative models, extending generative moment matching networks (GMMN) from the unconditional MMD objective to a conditional setting via conditional kernel mean embeddings. The resulting conditional MMD (CMMD) criterion is optimized by backpropagation, and the method is evaluated across multiple tasks, including supervised prediction, conditional generation of handwritten digits and faces, and distillation from Bayesian models.

This is my second review of the submission. Previously raised concerns were relatively minor—why a low-dimensional latent space was used and whether the approach scales to higher-dimensional latents, the comparative utility versus alternative methods, and various clarifications. In the current version, these points appear to be addressed.

I still find Remark 1 insufficiently clear: the mapping from Equation (2) to a “weighted MMD” form is not transparent. Specifically, what does the “dot” notation denote there—standard matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product? Expanding this derivation with explicit definitions would make the correspondence clearer. Also, the choice of the symbol C is unfortunate given its prior use for the conditional embedding operator; a different symbol would reduce confusion. While Theorems 1 and 2 are properly attributed to prior work, Theorem 3 lacks a citation; please provide a reference containing a proof or include a complete proof in the paper.

The empirical results are solid. Although supervised learning may not be the most compelling application for this framework, the authors’ rationale—to demonstrate versatility—is reasonable. The Bayesian distillation experiment is interesting but currently limited to a single dataset and teacher model, leaving its broader utility uncertain. This line of work likely has the strongest potential impact in pure generative modeling. The generation experiments are comparable to those in the original GMMN paper; the digit and face datasets are sufficient to demonstrate feasibility. For wider adoption, results on more challenging benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10 or LSUN) would be persuasive.
