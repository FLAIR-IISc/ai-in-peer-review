This paper proposes a loss function for training directed generative models with conditional variables, extending Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) from the unconditional to the conditional setting. The key idea is to leverage conditional kernel mean embeddings to define a principled conditional MMD objective. The method is evaluated on several tasks, including supervised classification, generation of handwritten digits and faces, and distillation of Bayesian posteriors.

This is my second time reviewing the paper. The previously raised concerns—why low-dimensional latent variables were used, whether the approach scales to higher-dimensional latent spaces, the practical utility relative to alternatives, and several clarifications—appear to have been addressed directly in the revision. The presentation is generally clearer and the empirical section is more complete.

Technical clarity and notation:
- Remark 1: The mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD formulation is not sufficiently clear. In particular, the “dot” notation needs to be disambiguated: is this standard matrix multiplication, an elementwise (Hadamard) product, or a contraction over specific indices? Please spell out the algebraic steps that convert Eq. (2) into the weighted MMD expression, including the precise roles of the conditional embedding operators, any weighting matrices, and how expectations over conditionals are handled. A small, explicit derivation or an appendix with the detailed expansion would make the connection immediate to the reader.
- Notation: The choice of the symbol C is unfortunate since it is already used for the conditional embedding operator. Please rename one of these quantities to avoid overloading, which currently obscures the derivation and proofs.
- Theorems: Theorems 1 and 2 are appropriately attributed to prior work, but Theorem 3 lacks a citation. Please provide a reference to a published proof or include a full proof in the appendix. If Theorem 3 is a straightforward corollary of known results (e.g., from properties of conditional mean embeddings or MMD), make that dependence explicit.

Empirical evaluation:
- The results are decent and support the feasibility of the approach. While supervised learning may not be the most compelling application for a conditional generative loss, the authors’ stated goal—to illustrate versatility—is reasonable, and the results are consistent with that aim.
- The distilled Bayesian inference application is interesting and potentially impactful. However, it is demonstrated on a single dataset with a single model, making it difficult to judge generality. Additional experiments on different models (e.g., deeper Bayesian neural networks or hierarchical models) and datasets would substantially strengthen this claim.
- For pure generative modeling, the experiments on digits and faces are on par with the original GMMN work and demonstrate that the idea works in practice. To encourage broader adoption, it would be valuable to include more challenging benchmarks such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN. Even a subset of these benchmarks, with clear comparisons to strong baselines (GMMN variants, VAEs, GANs with conditional losses), would make the empirical case more compelling.
- Since the prior round raised concerns about scalability to higher-dimensional latent spaces, it would help to report explicit experiments varying latent dimensionality (e.g., reconstruction quality, sample fidelity, and training stability as a function of latent size), along with training time and memory profiles. An ablation on kernel choices and bandwidth selection in the conditional setting would also be informative.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean extension of MMD-based training to conditional generative models using conditional mean embeddings.
- The proposed loss is simple to implement and potentially broadly applicable (supervised prediction, conditional generation, and knowledge distillation).
- The paper is generally well written, and most prior concerns appear to have been addressed.

Limitations and concerns:
- The mapping from the core objective to a weighted MMD form is under-explained, and notation is overloaded, which hampers readability.
- Theoretical coverage is incomplete without a citation or proof for Theorem 3.
- The empirical evaluation, while solid on standard datasets, lacks challenging benchmarks and broader validation for the Bayesian distillation use case.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify Remark 1 with a step-by-step derivation from Eq. (2) to the weighted MMD objective, including unambiguous notation for all operators and products.
- Rename the symbol C (either the constant or the operator) to avoid confusion, and provide a concise notation table.
- Provide a reference or full proof for Theorem 3, and articulate assumptions under which it holds (e.g., kernel properties, boundedness, and regularity of conditional operators).
- Augment experiments with at least one more challenging generative benchmark (e.g., CIFAR-10 or LSUN), including comparisons to strong baselines, and add an analysis of sensitivity to latent dimensionality and kernel hyperparameters.
- Expand the Bayesian distillation section to include additional datasets/models or provide diagnostic analyses (e.g., posterior predictive checks, calibration, and divergence metrics) to support claims of broader applicability.

Overall assessment:
This is a thoughtful and timely extension of MMD-based training to conditional generative models, built on a solid kernel-embedding foundation. The paper is promising and, with improved clarity around the objective’s derivation and strengthened empirical validation—particularly on more challenging datasets and in the Bayesian distillation setting—it could make a useful contribution. I encourage the authors to incorporate the clarifications and additional experiments outlined above.
