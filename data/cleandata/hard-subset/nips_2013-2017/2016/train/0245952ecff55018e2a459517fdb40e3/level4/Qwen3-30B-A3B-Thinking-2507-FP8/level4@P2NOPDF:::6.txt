

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), extending Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core innovation lies in kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric for measuring conditional distribution discrepancies. The authors evaluate CGMMNs on classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation.  

The naive extension of GMMNs to conditional settings involves training separate GMMNs per condition while sharing neural network parameters. However, this approach suffers from severe data sparsity: when conditioning variables are continuous (e.g., a single example per condition), the model lacks sufficient data to estimate individual distributions. The proposed CGMMN addresses this by modeling all conditional distributions as a unified family and directly matching the model to the conditional embedding operator, avoiding per-condition estimation. While this approach appears compelling, the naive method may still perform adequately in cases with discrete, low-cardinality conditioning variables (e.g., MNIST with 10 classes). A baseline comparison against this naive approach would strengthen the evaluation.  

The conditional MMD formulation (Remark 1) is a notable strength: it mirrors the unconditional MMD computation closely, with all K matrices in Equation 2 independent of model parameters. This enables straightforward backpropagation, making the method computationally tractable. However, a significant concern remains regarding the CMMD objective’s minibatch requirements. Small minibatches risk stochasticity dominating estimation error, while large minibatches incur prohibitive computational costs, particularly for expensive matrix inversions. The authors should address this trade-off more thoroughly.  

The experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are promising, demonstrating the method’s practical utility. Yet, sections 2.3 and 3.1 present comprehension challenges. Key concepts rely heavily on referenced papers (e.g., [29]), requiring readers to consult external material to grasp the framework’s mechanics. A clearer self-contained exposition would improve accessibility.  

Two critical minor issues require correction:  
1. Line 77 claims the MMD estimator is unbiased, which is inaccurate (MMD estimators are biased for finite samples).  
2. The conditional embedding operator in line 90 conflicts with the definition in reference [29]. To align with the paper’s formulation, a distinct cross-covariance operator definition is needed, which should be explicitly justified.  

Overall, CGMMNs provide a principled and efficient framework for conditional distribution modeling, with the conditional MMD offering a theoretically sound and computationally feasible solution. The method’s potential is evident, though the lack of a naive baseline comparison and unresolved computational trade-offs warrant attention. The presentation gaps and technical inconsistencies should be addressed in revision. The core contribution is valuable for conditional generative modeling, and the paper makes a solid case for CMMD as a versatile tool. With minor revisions to clarify the methodology, validate claims, and strengthen empirical comparisons, this work would be a strong fit for NIPS.
