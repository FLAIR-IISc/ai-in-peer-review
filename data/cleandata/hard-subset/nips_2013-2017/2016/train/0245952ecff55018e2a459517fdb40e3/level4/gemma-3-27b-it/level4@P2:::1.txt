This paper introduces conditional generative moment-matching networks (CGMMN), a novel neural network approach based on a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for learning conditional distributions. The authors evaluate their technique on several standard benchmark tasks, demonstrating both discriminative and generative capabilities. Overall, this is a valuable extension to the growing body of research on deep generative models. 

The paper is generally well-written and clear, though the mathematical details, as is common in this field, require careful attention. The experimental evaluation uses datasets of reasonable size, aligning with typical benchmarks in recent work. A significant strength is the theoretical grounding in kernel methods and the connection to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD). The authors effectively build upon prior work on GMMNs [19, 3] to address the important problem of conditional generation. 

However, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of computational cost. Given the potentially expensive steps involved in training – particularly the matrix inversions in the CMMD calculation – it would be beneficial to include a comparison of training time with existing approaches like DSN [17].  Reproducibility is also a concern. While the architecture is described, the lack of publicly available code hinders the ability of others to verify and build upon the results. Providing code would substantially increase the impact of this work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting via CMMD is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The work is well-grounded in kernel methods and MMD, providing a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The authors demonstrate competitive performance on a variety of tasks, including predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the core concepts effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** A more thorough discussion of training time and computational complexity is needed.
*   **Reproducibility:** The lack of publicly available code makes it difficult to reproduce the reported results.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the chosen datasets are standard, exploring larger and more complex datasets could further demonstrate the scalability and generalizability of the approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to conditional generative modeling.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate competitive performance.
*   The work addresses an important problem in deep learning – learning conditional distributions – with a novel and effective technique.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of code hinders reproducibility.
*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of computational cost.
*   The experimental evaluation, while solid, could be strengthened with larger datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field of deep generative modeling. While the lack of code and the limited discussion of computational cost are weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and promising experimental results make this a strong submission. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the reproducibility concerns by releasing their code.
