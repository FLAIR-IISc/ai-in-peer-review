This paper introduces a loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables, building upon generative moment matching networks and extending their unconditional maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) loss. The proposed method utilizes recent advances in conditional kernel mean embeddings to formulate this new loss. The authors demonstrate the approach on diverse generative tasks, including classification, handwritten digit and face generation, and Bayesian model knowledge distillation. This is my second review of this work; previous concerns centered on the use of a low-dimensional embedding space and its scalability to higher dimensions, the model’s utility relative to existing methods, and several requests for clarification. These points appear to have been addressed directly in the revised manuscript. However, the connection between Equation (2) and the weighted MMD form presented in Remark 1 remains unclear. Specifically, the meaning of the dot notation used – whether it represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product – requires clarification for a more transparent derivation. I recommend providing additional detail to make this mapping more explicit. Furthermore, the choice of the variable ‘C’ is potentially confusing, as it is already used to denote the conditional embedding. Theorems 1 and 2 cite relevant publications, but Theorem 3 lacks a citation; either a reference to a proof should be provided, or the proof itself included in the paper. The empirical results are promising. While earlier reviews questioned the suitability of applying this model to supervised learning, the authors’ justification – to illustrate the model's versatility – is reasonable. The application to distilled Bayesian inference is intriguing, although its evaluation is limited to a single dataset and model, leaving its broader applicability uncertain. I believe this approach has the greatest potential in the realm of pure generative modeling, where the experimental results are competitive with those reported in the original GMM paper. The use of digit and face datasets is adequate for demonstrating proof-of-concept. To encourage wider adoption, however, the authors should present more compelling results on more challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN.
