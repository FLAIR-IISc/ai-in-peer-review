This paper proposes a loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables, extending generative moment matching networks (GMMs) which originally employed an unconditional loss function based on the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion. The authors leverage recent work on conditional kernel mean embeddings to derive this new loss function, and demonstrate its efficacy on a variety of generative tasks, including classification, handwritten digit and face generation, and knowledge distillation in Bayesian models. This is my second review of this paper. Previous reviewers raised minor concerns regarding the choice of a low-dimensional latent space and its potential scalability, the utility of the model compared to existing methods, and some points requiring clarification. These issues appear to have been addressed directly in the revised manuscript. However, the mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form in Remark 1 remains unclear. Specifically, the notation used for the dot product – is it matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product? – needs further clarification to make this connection more transparent. Furthermore, the choice of ‘C’ to represent both the conditional embedding and another parameter is somewhat unfortunate and could lead to confusion. Theorems 1 and 2 cite relevant papers, but Theorem 3 lacks a reference; either a citation to a paper containing a proof should be provided, or the proof itself included in the appendix. 

The empirical results are encouraging. While previous reviews questioned the suitability of applying this model to supervised learning, the authors appropriately justify this as a demonstration of the model’s versatility, and I find this acceptable. The application to distilled Bayesian inference is interesting, though its evaluation is limited to a single dataset and model, hindering a clear assessment of its generalizability. I believe this approach would likely gain the most traction within the domain of pure generative modeling. The corresponding experiments yield results comparable to those reported in the original GMM paper. The use of digits and faces as datasets is sufficient to establish the feasibility of the proposed method. However, to encourage wider adoption, demonstrating compelling performance on more challenging datasets such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN would be necessary.
