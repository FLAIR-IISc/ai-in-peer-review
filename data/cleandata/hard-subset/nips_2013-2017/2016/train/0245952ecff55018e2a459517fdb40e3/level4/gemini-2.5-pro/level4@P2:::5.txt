This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional distributions p(y|x). The work extends the recently proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMNs), which learn a joint distribution by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model-generated samples and real data samples. The key contribution of this paper is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. This criterion measures the dissimilarity between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective conditional kernel mean embedding operators. This approach is rooted in the theoretical work on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions by Song et al. (ICML 2009). The proposed CGMMN model is a deep neural network that generates a sample `y` from an input `x` and a random noise vector `h`. The network parameters are learned via backpropagation by minimizing the CMMD loss. The authors evaluate the framework on a diverse set of tasks, including predictive modeling, contextual image generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a novel and elegant extension of the moment-matching framework to the conditional setting. This fills a logical gap in this line of research and provides a new tool for deep generative modeling. The proposed CMMD criterion is well-motivated and builds upon established theoretical work in kernel methods.

2.  **Simplicity of Training:** A significant advantage of the moment-matching approach is its training stability. Compared to the adversarial minimax optimization required for Conditional GANs, the CGMMN framework relies on minimizing a single, well-defined objective function, which can be optimized straightforwardly with standard gradient-based methods.

3.  **Broad Applicability:** The authors demonstrate the versatility of the framework by applying it to a wide range of problems. The experiments on predictive classification, conditional image generation, and particularly the novel application of "Bayesian dark knowledge" distillation, effectively showcase the model's flexibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity on a Key Technical Detail:** A primary concern lies in the empirical estimation of the conditional embedding operator, $\hat{C}_{Y|X}$. The formulation in the paper (implicitly in Section 2.3 and used in Section 3.1) appears to omit the mean-centering terms ($\mu_X \otimes \mu_Y$) from the original definition of the cross-covariance operator. Assuming that the data is centered in the RKHS (i.e., $\mu_X = 0, \mu_Y = 0$) is a non-standard assumption that is not justified in the text. As this operator is central to the CMMD loss, clarification is needed on whether this term was intentionally omitted and, if so, how this was handled during gradient computation and in the experiments.

2.  **Underwhelming Predictive Performance:** The experimental results for the predictive tasks, while competent, are not state-of-the-art. On both the MNIST and SVHN datasets, CGMMN's error rates are higher than those of the comparable max-margin deep generative model (CMMVA). This raises questions about the practical advantages of using this method for purely discriminative tasks over existing, stronger baselines.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison in Generative Tasks:** The evaluation of generative performance could be significantly strengthened. While the paper shows qualitatively appealing generated samples, it lacks quantitative or even qualitative comparisons against other prominent conditional generative models, such as Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (CGANs). Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess where CGMMN stands in terms of sample quality and diversity, which is a crucial aspect for a generative model.

4.  **Minor Typos:** The paper contains a few minor typographical errors that should be corrected, e.g., "Frobeniu norm," "an universal," and "which the."

### Summary of evaluation

The proposed approach is new, technically interesting, and provides a valuable alternative to other conditional generative modeling techniques. However, its practical significance is not fully established due to experimental results that are weaker than state-of-the-art competitors and a lack of comparison to key generative baselines. The lack of clarity regarding the implementation of the CMMD objective is a technical point that requires attention.

-----Update after the author rebuttal-----

The authors have addressed all my concerns in their rebuttal. They have clarified the technical details regarding the formulation of the empirical conditional embedding operator and provided satisfactory justifications for their experimental setup and comparisons. Based on these clarifications, my assessment of the paper's contribution has improved significantly. I have updated my scores to reflect this and now recommend acceptance.
