

This paper extends the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework [19] to conditional distributions via a novel conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion, enabling flexible conditional generation. It builds upon prior NIPS work on kernel-based generative models (e.g., GMMN [19], conditional GANs [21]) while avoiding the min-max instability of GANs. The core contribution is deriving a conditional kernel embedding operator (Theorem 2) and its empirical estimator (Eq. 2), which allows training via backpropagation. The authors demonstrate competitive performance across three application domains: predictive modeling (MNIST/SVHN classification), contextual image generation (MNIST/Yale Faces), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing regression).  

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The theoretical derivation of CMMD (Theorems 2–3) is rigorous, and the experimental validation across diverse tasks is comprehensive. The empirical estimator (Eq. 2) is well-justified, and the implementation (Algorithm 1) is straightforward. The Bayesian distillation experiment (Section 4.3) is particularly compelling, showing no performance degradation versus the teacher model.  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear mathematical exposition (Sections 2–3) and intuitive explanations of the generative process (Fig. 1). The appendix provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.  
*Originality:* While conditional extensions are conceptually natural, the derivation of CMMD—specifically the operator-based estimator (Eq. 2) and its connection to non-uniform kernel weighting (Remark 1)—represents a non-trivial technical contribution beyond prior conditional GAN/VAE work [21, 28]. It significantly broadens the applicability of moment-matching networks.  
*Significance:* The framework addresses three important challenges (predictive modeling with fewer assumptions, contextual generation, and scalable Bayesian distillation) with a unified, simple training procedure. The results demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on classification (Table 1) and competitive generation (Fig. 2–4), with clear potential for adoption in practice.  

**Weaknesses:**  
*Quality:* The theoretical assumptions (e.g., universality of RKHS, smoothness for CMMD well-definedness) are not thoroughly validated in the experiments. The paper does not compare against recent conditional GAN variants (e.g., conditional Wasserstein GAN [16]) on generation tasks, which would strengthen the significance claim.  
*Clarity:* The connection between the conditional embedding operator (Section 2.3) and the generative architecture (Section 3.2) could be more explicitly linked to avoid reader confusion about how inputs are processed.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel CMMD derivation, strong empirical results across diverse tasks, clear implementation, and broad applicability to generative modeling.  
*Con:* Limited comparison to contemporary conditional GAN methods and incomplete theoretical validation of assumptions.  

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling by extending MMD-based networks to conditional settings with minimal computational overhead. It is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses important practical challenges. The minor weaknesses do not undermine the paper’s overall quality or significance.
