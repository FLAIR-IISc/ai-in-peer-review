

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions using a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The authors build upon the theoretical foundation of kernel mean embeddings for conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML 2010) and the GMMN approach (Li et al., ICML 2015), creating a novel framework that maintains the simplicity of back-propagation training while addressing conditional learning scenarios. CGMMN demonstrates versatility across predictive modeling (on MNIST and SVHN datasets), contextual generation (of handwritten digits and facial images), and Bayesian knowledge distillation, where it successfully distills predictive distributions from Bayesian models without performance degradation.

The paper excels in theoretical development with clear theorems establishing the CMMD framework, though Theorem 3 lacks a reference to the proof source. The experimental design is well-justified for the application domains tested, with appropriate baselines including state-of-the-art discriminative models (DSN, CMMVA) and generative approaches (VAE, GAN variants). The CMMD objective (Equation 2) is theoretically sound, though the connection to the weighted MMD form in Remark 1 requires clarification regarding the dot notation (matrix multiplication vs. Hadamard product).

Strengths: 
- Theoretical extension of GMMN to conditional distributions is well-motivated and properly contextualized within the literature
- The generative process is simple and consistent with deep learning practices, enabling straightforward implementation
- Demonstrated versatility across multiple application domains (predictive modeling, generation, knowledge distillation)
- Bayesian knowledge distillation application is novel and well-executed, showing no performance degradation

Weaknesses:
- The empirical results on standard digit and face datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Faces) are competent but not sufficiently compelling for broader adoption; more challenging datasets (CIFAR-10, LSUN) would strengthen significance claims
- The limited application of Bayesian dark knowledge (only Boston housing dataset) makes it difficult to assess general utility
- The choice of notation (using C for both the conditional embedding operator and regularization matrix) creates ambiguity, particularly in Remark 1

Quality: The theoretical development is sound, with appropriate mathematical rigor. The experimental evaluation is complete for the tasks presented, though the scope could be expanded.

Clarity: The paper is well-written with clear explanations of the conditional embedding framework. The connection between Equation (2) and the weighted MMD form would benefit from additional clarification regarding the dot product notation.

Originality: The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions represents a meaningful contribution to the literature, distinct from conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Soň et al., 2015).

Significance: While the framework is versatile, the significance is somewhat limited by the narrow range of datasets tested. The Bayesian distillation application is promising but requires more extensive evaluation across diverse datasets to demonstrate broader utility.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, clear extension of existing work, versatile application across multiple domains, well-executed Bayesian knowledge distillation experiment.

Con: Limited experimental scope on challenging datasets, ambiguous notation in Remark 1, limited assessment of Bayesian distillation beyond one dataset.
