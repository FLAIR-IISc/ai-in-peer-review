This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional distributions. The method extends the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) approach by proposing a novel training objective, the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). This objective is derived from the theory of kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, allowing the model to match the conditional distribution of generated data to that of a target dataset. The authors demonstrate the model's versatility on a range of tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, conditional image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model.

**Strengths:**
- **Originality:** The paper presents a novel and logical extension of the GMMN framework to the conditional setting. Grounding the loss function in the established theory of conditional kernel mean embeddings provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approach.
- **Versatility:** The model is successfully applied to a diverse set of problems, from standard prediction and generation to the more novel application of "Bayesian dark knowledge." This breadth effectively showcases the flexibility of the proposed framework.
- **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, and the authors appear to have successfully addressed concerns from previous reviews regarding model design choices.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Theoretical Justification:** Theorem 3, which is central to the justification of the CMMD objective, is stated without a proof or a citation. For the paper to be technically complete, this result should be either formally proven within the paper or attributed to an existing source.
- **Clarity of Key Connection:** Remark 1, which presents an intuition for CMMD as a weighted version of MMD, is difficult to follow. It is not immediately clear how the form `Tr(Ld · C1) + ...` is derived from Equation (2). The dot notation is ambiguous (is it a matrix product or a Hadamard product?), and the choice of `C` for the weighting matrices is potentially confusing given that `C` is also used to denote the conditional embedding operator. Clarifying this connection with more detail would strengthen the paper's exposition.
- **Significance of Experimental Results:** While the experiments demonstrate that the method works, their scope may limit the paper's perceived impact.
    - The generative modeling results on MNIST and Yale Faces are convincing for these datasets but are comparable in difficulty to those in the original GMMN paper. To demonstrate a significant advance and encourage wider adoption, results on more complex, standard benchmarks such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN would be necessary.
    - The Bayesian distillation application is interesting, but its evaluation on a single dataset with a single teacher model makes it difficult to assess its general utility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper proposes a novel, theoretically-grounded, and well-motivated method for conditional generative modeling within the moment-matching framework.
2.  The versatility of the CGMMN is convincingly demonstrated across three distinct and relevant machine learning tasks.
3.  The work fills a clear gap by extending the successful GMMN model to the conditional case, significantly broadening its applicability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The empirical evaluation on generative modeling tasks does not include challenging, modern benchmark datasets, making it difficult to gauge the method's performance relative to the current state of the art.
2.  A key theoretical result (Theorem 3) is presented without a proof or citation, representing a gap in the paper's technical rigor.

Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear contribution. The weaknesses identified are addressable. The application to distilling Bayesian models is particularly interesting, and the core idea of a conditional MMD loss is a valuable addition to the literature.
