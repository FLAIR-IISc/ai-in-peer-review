This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), a compelling extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to the challenging domain of conditional generation and prediction. The core contribution lies in the elegant use of kernel embeddings of conditional distributions, coupled with a conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric for quantifying the distance between these distributions. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of CGMMNs across diverse tasks including classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation.

A significant motivation for this work is the inherent difficulty of naively extending GMMNs to conditional settings. Directly estimating a GMMN for each conditional distribution suffers from severe data sparsity, particularly when dealing with continuous conditioning variables where each conditional distribution might be supported by only a single data point. CGMMNs circumvent this issue by treating the collection of conditional distributions as a unified family and learning an embedding operator that maps conditioning variables to the target distribution, rather than attempting to match each conditional distribution individually. This approach offers a clear advantage, although the paper acknowledges, and I concur, that the simpler, naive approach can perform reasonably well in specific scenarios – for instance, when the conditioning variable takes a limited number of discrete values, as exemplified by the MNIST experiment. A direct comparison against such a naive baseline would strengthen the presented results.

The derivation of the conditional MMD objective is particularly noteworthy. As highlighted in Remark 1, the formulation bears a strong resemblance to the unconditional MMD, enabling efficient computation. The authors also correctly point out that the kernel matrices (K in Equation 2) are independent of the model parameters, allowing for standard backpropagation. 

However, a crucial concern arises regarding the computational cost of the CMMD objective. Achieving stable and accurate gradient estimates likely requires large minibatch sizes. This, in turn, introduces significant computational overhead, particularly concerning the matrix inversions involved. The paper does not fully address this potential bottleneck, and a discussion of strategies for mitigating this issue (e.g., approximations to the matrix inverse, alternative optimization techniques) would be beneficial.

Overall, CGMMNs represent a conceptually sound and promising approach to modeling conditional distributions. The experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are encouraging and demonstrate the method’s potential. However, sections 2.3 and 3.1 could benefit from improved clarity. I found myself needing to consult the cited papers to fully grasp the underlying concepts and notations. Greater self-containment within the manuscript would enhance its accessibility.

I also identified a few minor points requiring attention:

1.  Line 77 states that the MMD estimator is unbiased, which is demonstrably false. The MMD estimator is generally biased, and acknowledging this would increase the paper's precision.
2.  On line 90, the definition of the conditional embedding operator appears inconsistent with that presented in reference [29]. Achieving the form used in this paper requires a modified definition of the cross-covariance operator, and this discrepancy should be clarified.



Despite these minor issues, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling. The proposed CGMMN framework provides a sophisticated and potentially powerful alternative to existing techniques for conditional generation. Addressing the computational concerns and improving the clarity of certain sections would further strengthen the paper’s impact.
