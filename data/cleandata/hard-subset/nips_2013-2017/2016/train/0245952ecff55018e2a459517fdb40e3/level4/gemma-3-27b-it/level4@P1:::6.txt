This paper presents conditional generative moment-matching networks (CGMMNs), an extension of GMMNs to conditional generation and prediction applications. The key idea is to leverage kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) metric for measuring the discrepancy between conditional distributions. The proposed CGMMN is evaluated on classification, conditional generation, and distilling Bayesian models. A naive approach to extending GMMNs to the conditional setting would be to estimate a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution. However, this suffers from data sparsity, especially when the conditioning variables are continuous. The proposed approach addresses this by treating all conditional distributions as a family and matching the model to the conditional embedding operator directly, rather than matching individual conditional distributions. This approach’s advantage is clear, though it’s worth noting that in scenarios where the conditioning variable takes a limited number of values (e.g., 10 classes in MNIST), the naive approach can still perform reasonably well. Comparing against such a baseline would be a valuable addition. The CMMD objective has a well-defined formulation and can be computed similarly to the unconditional MMD, as highlighted in Remark 1. Importantly, the kernel matrices in Equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, enabling standard backpropagation. A potential concern is that the CMMD objective might require large mini-batches to avoid stochasticity dominating the error. However, large mini-batches can make the computation expensive, particularly the matrix inversions. Overall, the CMMD provides a compelling way to model conditional distributions, and the experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are promising.

The presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 was challenging to follow. A deeper understanding requires referencing the cited papers. There are a couple of minor points that require attention: (1) on line 77, the statement that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect; (2) on line 90, the conditional embedding operator presented appears inconsistent with the definition provided in reference [29]. Achieving the form used in this paper seems to necessitate a different definition of the cross-covariance operator.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel extension of GMMNs to conditional generation, addressing the limitations of applying GMMNs directly to conditional settings.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation based on kernel embeddings and CMMD appears solid, and the derivation of the CMMD objective is well-motivated.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across diverse tasks, including predictive modeling, generative tasks, and Bayesian knowledge distillation.
*   **Clarity (overall):** Despite some sections being dense, the overall flow of the paper is logical, and the core ideas are conveyed effectively.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity (specific sections):** Sections 2.3 and 3.1 require significant prior knowledge to fully understand. Improved explanations and more intuitive introductions to the concepts would be beneficial.
*   **Minor Technical Inaccuracies:** The claim of an unbiased MMD estimator and the discrepancy in the conditional embedding operator definition should be addressed.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** The absence of a comparison with a naive baseline (e.g., separate GMMNs for each conditional value) limits the assessment of the proposed method’s advantages.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The potential computational cost associated with large mini-batches for CMMD estimation warrants further discussion or exploration of mitigation strategies.
