

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) to model conditional distributions. The authors propose a novel Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric that directly measures the discrepancy between conditional distributions using kernel embeddings, rather than estimating individual conditional distributions for each condition. The paper demonstrates the utility of CGMMN across three tasks: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation, and distilling Bayesian models.

The work builds upon recent advances in generative modeling, particularly GMMN (Li et al., ICML 2015) and conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014). While conditional GANs have been explored, CGMMN offers a simpler training objective than GANs by avoiding min-max optimization. The paper's technical foundation rests on kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML 2009) and MMD (Gretton et al., JMLR 2012), extending these concepts to develop a principled approach for conditional modeling.

**Strengths:**
1. **Quality**: The CMMD formulation is technically sound and well-motivated. The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for why the proposed approach avoids the data sparsity problem inherent in training separate GMMNs for each condition. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across multiple tasks, including classification on MNIST and SVHN datasets, conditional image generation, and distillation of Bayesian models. The approach effectively addresses a significant limitation of previous conditional generative models.

2. **Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution by extending moment-matching networks to handle conditional distributions in a unified framework. This is distinct from previous work that either used separate models for each condition or relied on GANs' min-max optimization. The paper adequately differentiates itself from related work in conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs, conditional VAEs).

3. **Significance**: The framework has broad applicability across multiple domains including classification, image generation, and knowledge distillation. The Bayesian dark knowledge application demonstrates practical utility for making Bayesian models more computationally efficient. The method's simplicity (training via standard backpropagation) makes it particularly appealing for practitioners.

4. **Clarity**: The paper's core idea is clearly presented, though some sections (particularly Section 2.3 on conditional embedding and Section 3.1 on CMMD) would benefit from clearer exposition. The experimental setup is well-described and the results are thoroughly presented.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity**: The paper's mathematical exposition in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is dense and could be improved with more intuitive explanations. For instance, the relationship between the conditional embedding operator and the CMMD objective would be clearer with a more concrete example. The authors reference Theorem 2 from Song et al. (2009) without adequately explaining how their formulation differs or builds upon it.

2. **Quality**: The claim on line 77 that the MMD estimator is "unbiased" is incorrect (it's asymptotically unbiased but not unbiased). Additionally, the conditional embedding operator definition in Section 2.3 appears inconsistent with the formulation in Song et al. (2009), which would require clarification. The paper does not include a comparison to the naive approach of training separate GMMNs for each condition (e.g., for MNIST with 10 classes), which would provide a stronger baseline for evaluating the proposed method.

3. **Quality**: The computational complexity of the CMMD objective (requiring matrix inverses) is not thoroughly discussed. While the authors note that the method can be implemented with mini-batches, they don't address the practical challenges of implementing this with large datasets or the trade-offs between batch size and computational cost.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:*
- Addresses a significant limitation in conditional generative modeling with a theoretically sound approach
- Demonstrates practical utility across multiple domains including classification, generation, and Bayesian model distillation
- Maintains the simplicity of GMMN training via backpropagation
- Provides a clear mathematical formulation that extends existing work

*Con:*
- Incomplete comparison to the naive baseline approach (training separate GMMNs for each condition)
- Some technical inaccuracies in the mathematical exposition
- Insufficient discussion of computational challenges and trade-offs
- Certain sections require additional explanation to be accessible to the broader NIPS audience

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a well-motivated extension of generative moment-matching networks to conditional settings. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the experimental results demonstrate the method's effectiveness across multiple applications. While the paper contains some technical inaccuracies and would benefit from clearer exposition of certain mathematical concepts, these issues are relatively minor compared to the paper's overall contribution. The approach is technically sound, addresses an important gap in the literature, and demonstrates practical significance through multiple experimental applications.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the mathematical inconsistencies and to include the comparison to the naive baseline approach. The authors should clarify the relationship between their conditional embedding operator and the formulation in Song et al. (2009), correct the claim about MMD unbiasedness, and provide a more thorough discussion of computational considerations.
