This paper presents a neural network approach built upon a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion. The proposed technique is evaluated on standard benchmark tasks to assess both its discriminative and generative capabilities. Overall, this represents an interesting extension within the deep learning research area focused on generative models. The paper is reasonably well-written, although, as is common in this field, the mathematical details can be challenging to follow at times. The experiments utilize relatively small datasets, but this is not uncommon when compared to other recent publications in this area.

Given the computationally intensive nature of the proposed training algorithm, I would appreciate further discussion in the experimental section regarding training time. Specifically, a comparison of training duration with existing approaches, such as DSN, would be valuable. The inclusion of reference code would significantly strengthen the paper. Currently, a practitioner might find it difficult to reproduce the exact models evaluated, hindering replicability and further research. 

Here's a more detailed breakdown of my assessment:

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The application of a conditional MMD criterion within a neural network framework is a potentially valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling.
* **Clear Presentation (Generally):** The core ideas of the paper are conveyed in a reasonably clear manner, making the main contributions accessible.
* **Standard Evaluation:** The use of established benchmark tasks allows for a fair comparison to related work.
* **Sound Motivation:** The paper provides adequate motivation for the proposed approach, grounding it in the challenges of existing generative models.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Mathematical Complexity:** While understandable with effort, the mathematical formulation could benefit from further clarification and potentially more intuitive explanations. A more gradual introduction of the notation might improve accessibility.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The computational cost of the method is acknowledged, but a quantitative comparison of training time to other relevant methods is missing. This is critical for assessing the practical viability of the proposed approach.
* **Lack of Code Availability:** The absence of publicly available code is a significant drawback. Reproducibility is a cornerstone of scientific research, and providing code allows for thorough validation and facilitates future developments. The details within the model section are insufficient for independent implementation without access to the authors' specific configurations.
* **Small Dataset Size:** The experiments, while well-executed, are conducted on relatively small datasets. Demonstrating performance on larger, more complex datasets would provide a more compelling evaluation of the method’s scalability and generalizability.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, and how it scales with dataset size and model parameters?
* What specific hardware (CPU, GPU, RAM) was used for the experiments, and what were the typical training times for each dataset? A direct comparison to DSN or other relevant models, in terms of training time, would be particularly helpful.
* Are there any plans to release the code used in the experiments? This would greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the work.
* How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the kernel bandwidth in the MMD calculation)? Was a systematic hyperparameter search conducted?
* Could you elaborate on the limitations of the proposed approach? Are there specific types of data or generative tasks where it is expected to perform poorly?



**Overall Recommendation:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to generative modeling. The idea is sound, and the initial results are encouraging. However, the lack of code and the limited discussion of computational cost and scalability prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. I believe that addressing these concerns – particularly releasing the code and providing a more detailed analysis of training time – would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more valuable contribution to the field. I would be willing to reconsider the paper if these changes are made.
