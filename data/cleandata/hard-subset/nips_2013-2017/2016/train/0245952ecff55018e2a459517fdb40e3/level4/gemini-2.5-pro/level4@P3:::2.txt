### **Summary**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The core technical contribution is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective function. This objective measures the discrepancy between the true conditional distribution (represented by training data) and the one modeled by the generative network by comparing their respective kernel mean embedding operators in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The network is trained via backpropagation using stochastic gradient descent on this CMMD objective. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach by applying it to a range of tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, conditional image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model. This work extends the applicability of MMD-based deep generative models, placing it alongside other conditional models like conditional GANs and conditional VAEs.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and non-trivial problem of extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. Unlike simple feed-forward networks where conditioning is often a matter of providing an additional input, adapting the MMD objective for this purpose is more complex. In a minibatch, each generated sample `y_i` is conditioned on a different input `x_i`, meaning the model must match a family of conditional distributions rather than a single joint distribution. The proposed CMMD criterion is a principled and novel solution to this problem, significantly broadening the scope and applicability of GMMNs.

2.  **Broad Applicability:** The authors successfully demonstrate the model's flexibility across three distinct and relevant problem domains:
    *   **Prediction:** The model achieves competitive classification error rates on MNIST and SVHN, comparable to other state-of-the-art deep generative models.
    *   **Conditional Generation:** It can generate plausible images conditioned on class labels for both MNIST and the Yale Face dataset.
    *   **Model Distillation ("Bayesian Dark Knowledge"):** The application to distill a complex Bayesian model into a faster CGMMN student network is an interesting and practical use case.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are competitive. The performance on SVHN, in particular, is strong and demonstrates that the method is effective on challenging, real-world datasets.

### **Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of the paper is its lack of clarity and intuition in presenting the core CMMD objective. While the technical derivation appears sound, it is not well-motivated, making it difficult for the reader to grasp the underlying principles.

1.  **Lack of Intuitive Motivation:** The paper immediately delves into the formal mathematical machinery of Hilbert space embeddings of conditional operators (Section 2.3 and 3.1) without first providing an intuitive explanation for the proposed approach. The unconditional MMD objective has a clear intuition: minimize the distance between the mean embeddings of two distributions. The paper fails to build a similar intuitive bridge for the conditional case. It is not clear *why* comparing conditional operators via the Hilbert-Schmidt norm (Equation in Section 3.1, before Equation 2) is the natural way to measure if `P(y|x) = Q(y|x)`.

2.  **Unclear Connection Between Theory and Practice:** The jump from the theoretical definition of CMMD to the final practical objective in Equation (2) is abrupt. Remark 1 attempts to provide some intuition by framing CMMD as a non-uniformly weighted version of MMD, where the weights depend on the conditional variables `x`. This is a crucial insight that should be a central part of the motivation and derivation, rather than an afterthought. The paper would be much stronger if it first explained this weighting concept intuitively and then showed how the formal operator-based derivation leads to it.

3.  **Insufficient Algorithmic and Computational Details:** The paper does not adequately discuss the computational details and efficiency of the proposed algorithm.
    *   The objective in Equation (2) involves forming and inverting kernel matrices (`K_d + λI` and `K_s + λI`). This is an `O(B^3)` operation, where `B` is the minibatch size. This potential scalability bottleneck is not discussed.
    *   It is unclear how the practical mini-batch algorithm (Algorithm 1) relates to the full-batch formula in Equation (2). Are `DsXY` and `DdXY` simply the samples within the current minibatch? This should be made explicit.
    *   The role and selection of the regularization parameter `λ` in the matrix inversions are not discussed.

### **Questions for the Authors**

To improve the paper, I would encourage the authors to address the following points:

1.  Could you provide a more intuitive, step-by-step motivation for the CMMD objective? For example, one could start with the idea of a weighted MMD, where pairs of points `(y_i, y_j)` are weighted based on the similarity of their corresponding conditions `(x_i, x_j)`, and then show how this leads to the formulation in Equation (2).

2.  Please provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational steps within a single training iteration of Algorithm 1. What is the precise computational complexity with respect to the minibatch size? How does this compare to related methods like conditional GANs?

3.  The use of an autoencoder in Section 4.2.1 to generate smoother images is an interesting practical choice. Does this suggest that the CMMD objective struggles with learning distributions on high-dimensional pixel spaces directly? Could you comment on the general applicability of CMMD to high-dimensional outputs?

4.  In Section 3.2, it is noted that non-differentiable kernels can be used for the conditional variables `x`. Could you elaborate on the choice of kernels for both `x` and `y` in your experiments and discuss the sensitivity of the model's performance to these choices?

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a novel and significant extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. The method is principled and supported by strong empirical results across a diverse set of tasks. However, the paper's main weakness is the lack of clarity and intuition in its central technical contribution, the CMMD objective. The connection between the high-level theory and the final implemented objective is not explained well, and important computational details are omitted. I believe the paper is a valuable contribution, but it would require a significant revision to improve its clarity and exposition before it can be fully appreciated by the NIPS community.
