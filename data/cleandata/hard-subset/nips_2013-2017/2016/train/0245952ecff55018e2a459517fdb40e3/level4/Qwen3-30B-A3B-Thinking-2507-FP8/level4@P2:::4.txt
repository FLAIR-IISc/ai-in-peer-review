

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. Building on Li et al.'s (ICML 2015) work that used maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for unconditional generative modeling, the authors develop a conditional MMD (CMMD) criterion to learn flexible conditional distributions. This represents a natural yet non-trivial extension of GMMN, as it requires careful derivation of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions and their empirical estimation. The work relates to other conditional generative approaches like conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., NIPS 2015), but offers a simpler training procedure than GAN-based methods by avoiding min-max optimization.

Regarding quality, the paper presents technically sound contributions with well-justified theoretical derivations. The authors provide a clear definition of conditional kernel embeddings and prove that CMMD serves as a valid criterion for measuring the difference between conditional distributions. The experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance across three distinct tasks: predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN classification), contextual generation (MNIST and Yale Face datasets), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing regression). The results are comprehensive and well-supported by appropriate baselines, with careful attention to model architectures and hyperparameters. The implementation details in Algorithm 1 and the appendix provide sufficient information for reproducibility.

The paper excels in clarity. The writing is precise and well-organized, with a logical flow from theoretical foundations to experimental validation. Key concepts like kernel embeddings and conditional MMD are introduced with sufficient mathematical rigor while remaining accessible to the NIPS audience. The figures effectively illustrate generated samples and experimental results, and the appendix provides valuable supplementary details on implementation specifics and additional experiments.

In terms of originality, while the core idea of extending GMMN to handle conditional distributions is conceptually straightforward, the authors provide a non-trivial derivation of the conditional MMD criterion and its empirical estimator. This represents a meaningful contribution to the literature, as it fills a gap in the moment-matching literature that previously lacked a simple, backpropagation-friendly approach for conditional generation. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work by emphasizing the simplicity of the training procedure compared to conditional GANs and the broader applicability of the framework.

The significance of this work is substantial. CGMMN provides a straightforward, effective tool for practitioners working with conditional generative models, with immediate applicability to predictive modeling, contextual generation, and knowledge distillation. The framework's simplicity (as evidenced by Algorithm 1) and competitive performance across diverse tasks suggest it will be widely adopted in both research and industry settings. The Bayesian dark knowledge application in particular demonstrates how CGMMN can simplify complex probabilistic inference tasks.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel extension to GMMN that addresses an important gap in the literature
- Well-justified theoretical framework with sound mathematical derivations
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple challenging tasks
- Clear writing and well-organized presentation
- Simple implementation with immediate practical utility

Arguments against acceptance:
- The core idea represents a relatively incremental extension to existing work
- Some theoretical details could be clarified (e.g., the smoothness assumptions for CMMD)
- The novelty is somewhat limited to extending an existing framework rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling by providing a simple yet effective method for conditional generation. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a practical need in the community. The framework's ease of implementation and competitive performance across multiple tasks suggest it will be widely adopted. While the extension from unconditional to conditional generation is conceptually straightforward, the careful derivation of CMMD and its empirical estimator represents a non-trivial contribution that fills a meaningful gap in the literature. The paper should be accepted as it advances the state of the art in conditional generative modeling with a simple, practical approach that will benefit many researchers and practitioners.
