This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep generative model designed to learn conditional distributions. The core contribution is the development of a novel training criterion called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). This work is a direct and logical extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which use the standard Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to learn a joint distribution. The proposed CMMD objective measures the difference between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between their respective conditional mean embedding operators. This approach provides an alternative to other popular conditional generative models like conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (cGANs) and conditional Variational Autoencoders (cVAEs), with the potential benefit of a more stable training objective than the min-max formulation of GANs. The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN on a range of tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, contextual image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and principled extension of MMD-based generative models to the conditional setting. This is a valuable contribution, as conditional modeling is fundamental to a wide array of machine learning problems. By extending the moment-matching framework, this work broadens the applicability of this class of generative models, providing a useful new tool for the community.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The proposed CMMD criterion is theoretically well-grounded, building logically on established work in kernel methods, specifically the kernel mean embedding of conditional distributions. The formulation is a natural generalization of MMD and provides a coherent objective function for training the conditional generator network.

3.  **Broad Applicability:** A key strength of the paper is its demonstration of the CGMMN framework across three distinct and relevant application domains: prediction, generation, and knowledge distillation. This showcases the model's flexibility and potential for wide-ranging impact.

4.  **Training Simplicity:** Compared to the often challenging and unstable adversarial training process of GANs, the proposed training method via direct optimization of the CMMD objective using stochastic gradient descent is more straightforward and potentially more stable.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability Analysis:** The CMMD objective requires the computation and, critically, the inversion of kernel Gram matrices within each minibatch, an operation that scales cubically with the batch size. While the paper employs a mini-batch training algorithm, the computational expense could be a significant bottleneck for scaling to larger batch sizes, which are often beneficial for training deep networks. The paper would be much stronger if the authors included a discussion on the computational complexity and provided empirical data on training times, especially in comparison to the baselines they cite (e.g., CMMVA, DSN).

2.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are performed on standard but relatively small-scale datasets (e.g., MNIST, SVHN). While the results are competitive and demonstrate proof-of-concept, the generative modeling field has increasingly moved towards more complex and higher-resolution datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA). Evaluating the performance and scalability of CGMMN on such datasets would provide a more convincing argument for its effectiveness relative to the current state of the art in conditional generative modeling.

3.  **Reproducibility:** The description of the network architectures and training parameters, while present, lacks some of the finer details that would be necessary for another researcher to reproduce the results exactly. For example, details on learning rates, the specific form of regularization used, and the initialization scheme are not fully specified. The contribution's impact would be significantly amplified if the authors made their source code available.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and useful extension of moment-matching networks for conditional density estimation. It successfully demonstrates the model's versatility on several different tasks with competitive results. The approach offers a promising alternative to cGANs with a potentially more stable training objective.

*   **Con-acceptance:** The evaluation is limited to smaller-scale datasets, and potential issues regarding computational scalability are not addressed. The lack of detailed implementation specifics or source code may hinder reproducibility.

Overall, this is a solid and interesting paper that makes a nice contribution to the sub-field of deep generative models. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the scope of the experimental evaluation and the analysis of computational cost, rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed method itself. These points could be addressed to further strengthen the paper.
