This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), extending Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core innovation lies in utilizing kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) metric to assess the divergence between them. The authors evaluate CGMMNs across classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation.

A straightforward, yet problematic, extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would involve estimating a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters via an underlying neural network. This approach suffers from data sparsity, particularly with continuous conditioning variables where individual conditional distributions might be supported by only a single data point. CGMMNs circumvent this by treating the set of conditional distributions as a family, directly matching the model with the conditional embedding operator rather than focusing on individual distributions. While the benefits of this approach are intuitively clear, the performance of the naive per-conditional-distribution approach can be surprisingly strong in certain cases—for instance, in conditional generation with a limited number of conditioning variable values, as demonstrated with MNIST. Including such a baseline comparison would strengthen the evaluation.

The formulation of the conditional MMD objective is elegant and, as highlighted in Remark 1, efficiently computable—almost identically to the unconditional MMD. Notably, the K matrices in Equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, facilitating straightforward backpropagation. However, a potential drawback is the computational cost and minibatch size requirements of the conditional MMD objective. Insufficiently large minibatches can introduce stochasticity that overwhelms the signal, while large minibatches dramatically increase computational expense, especially the matrix inversions. Nevertheless, CGMMNs provide a valuable framework for modeling conditional distributions, and the experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are encouraging.

The presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 requires improvement. Understanding the underlying methodology necessitates consulting the cited references, hindering accessibility. Additionally, there are a couple of minor technical inaccuracies: (1) the claim on line 77 that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect; (2) the conditional embedding operator defined on line 90 does not align with the definition presented in reference [29]. Achieving the form used in this paper appears to require a different definition of the cross-covariance operator.

Overall, this paper presents a conceptually sound and potentially impactful contribution to generative modeling. Addressing the concerns regarding clarity, baseline comparisons, and technical accuracy would significantly enhance its quality.
