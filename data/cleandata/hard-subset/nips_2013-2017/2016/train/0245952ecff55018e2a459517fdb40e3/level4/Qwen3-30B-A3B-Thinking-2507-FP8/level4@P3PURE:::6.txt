

This paper introduces conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMNs), extending generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core contribution lies in kernel embedding of conditional distributions and a conditional MMD (CMMD) metric for measuring discrepancies between conditional distributions. The proposed CGMMN framework is evaluated on classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation.  

The naive extension of GMMNs to conditional settings involves training a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, with shared parameters across conditions via a single neural network. However, this approach suffers from severe data sparsity: each conditional distribution typically has very few examples, and for continuous conditioning variables, a single example may exist per condition, rendering estimation unreliable. The proposed CGMMN addresses this by treating all conditional distributions as a family and directly matching the model to the conditional embedding operator, bypassing per-condition estimation. While the proposed method offers a clear conceptual advantage, the naive approach may still yield reasonable results in discrete conditioning scenarios (e.g., conditional generation with 10 discrete conditioning values as in MNIST). A comparison with this naive baseline would strengthen the evaluation.  

The CMMD objective is well-formulated (Remark 1) and computationally efficient, as all kernel matrices in Equation 2 are independent of model parameters, enabling standard backpropagation. A key concern, however, is the potential need for large minibatches to mitigate stochastic approximation error, which could dominate the objective. While large minibatches reduce this error, they significantly increase computational cost, particularly due to expensive matrix inversions.  

Overall, the CMMD framework provides a principled approach to conditional distribution modeling. The experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are compelling. However, sections 2.3 and 3.1 are difficult to follow without consulting referenced papers for context. Two minor issues require correction: (1) Line 77 incorrectly states the MMD estimator is unbiased; (2) Line 90 uses a conditional embedding operator inconsistent with the definition in reference [29]; the form employed here necessitates a distinct cross-covariance operator definition.
