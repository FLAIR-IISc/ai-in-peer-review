This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), an extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core of the proposed method is the use of the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric to measure the discrepancy between them. The model is evaluated on classification, conditional generation, and the distillation of Bayesian models.

The paper effectively motivates its approach by contrasting it with a naive strategy for extending GMMNs. This naive method would involve estimating a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, with shared parameters across a single neural network. As the authors correctly identify, this approach suffers from data sparsity, particularly when the conditioning variables are continuous, which can result in only a single example per conditional distribution. In contrast, the proposed method treats all conditional distributions as a unified family, matching the conditional embedding operator directly. This provides an elegant solution to the data sparsity problem.

A key strength of the work is that the CMMD objective has a clean formulation and, as noted in Remark 1, can be computed almost as easily as the standard unconditional MMD. Furthermore, because the kernel matrices (K) in Equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, backpropagation can be implemented in the same straightforward manner as in the unconditional case.

Despite these strengths, there are several points that could be addressed to improve the paper:

1.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** While the theoretical advantage of the proposed method over the naive approach is clear, the empirical evaluation would be strengthened by a direct comparison. In settings with a discrete and small set of conditioning variables, such as the 10 classes in MNIST, the naive approach might perform reasonably well. Including this baseline would provide a more complete picture of the proposed method's practical benefits.

2.  **Computational Scalability:** A potential concern is the minibatch size required for the CMMD objective. To ensure the stochastic estimate is reliable and not dominated by noise, large minibatches may be necessary. However, this increases the computational cost of the objective function, particularly the matrix inversions, which could become prohibitively expensive. A discussion of this trade-off and the method's sensitivity to batch size would be valuable.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 was difficult to follow. I had to consult the referenced papers to fully grasp the technical details of what was being implemented. Improving the clarity and self-containedness of these sections would benefit the paper.

There are also a couple of minor technical points that require correction:
*   Line 77 states that the MMD estimator is unbiased, which is not true for the commonly used empirical estimator. This statement should be corrected or clarified.
*   The definition of the conditional embedding operator on line 90 appears inconsistent with the one provided in reference [29]. To arrive at the form used in this paper, a different definition of the cross-covariance operator seems to be required. This discrepancy should be clarified.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable and principled method for modeling conditional distributions using CMMD. The experimental results on classification and model distillation are interesting and promising. Addressing the concerns regarding the experimental baseline, computational scalability, and presentation clarity would further strengthen this work.
