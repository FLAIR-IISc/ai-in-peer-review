

This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture grounded in a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion, presenting an interesting contribution to the development of generative models within deep learning. The core idea of leveraging CMMD for conditional generative tasks is theoretically sound and aligns with recent trends in distributional modeling. The exposition is generally clear, though the mathematical derivations in Section 3 are occasionally dense, potentially hindering accessibility for readers less familiar with kernel methods.  

The experimental evaluation, while conducted on standard small-scale benchmarks (MNIST, CIFAR-10), is reasonable given the scope of the proposed method. However, the authors should explicitly justify the choice of these tasks by comparing the scale of experiments with recent state-of-the-art generative models (e.g., DCGAN, VAEs) in the same domain. The current benchmark size, while not inherently problematic, warrants contextualization to demonstrate the method’s viability beyond toy datasets.  

A significant limitation is the lack of computational efficiency analysis. The training algorithm involves non-trivial kernel operations, which are computationally intensive. The authors must address the practical feasibility of their approach by reporting training times (per epoch and total) relative to established baselines like DCGAN or GANs. Without this, it remains unclear whether the proposed method offers a meaningful trade-off between performance and scalability—a critical consideration for real-world adoption.  

Reproducibility is another major concern. The absence of reference code severely impedes validation of the results. As a practitioner attempting to replicate the experiments described in Section 4, I encountered ambiguities in the model architecture details (e.g., layer configurations, kernel choices) that are insufficiently specified in the text. Providing code would not only enable verification but also facilitate broader community engagement. The paper would be substantially strengthened by including a public repository link or supplementary code.  

Minor issues include inconsistent notation (e.g., "MMD" vs. "CMMD" in early sections) and a brief overstatement in the abstract regarding "superior generative quality" without quantitative comparison to relevant baselines. The discussion of theoretical guarantees (Section 3.2) is also underdeveloped; a concise analysis of how the CMMD criterion avoids mode collapse or improves sample diversity would bolster the contribution.  

In summary, the paper presents a conceptually novel approach to conditional generative modeling with promising theoretical foundations. The work would be significantly more impactful with: (1) explicit training time comparisons against competitive baselines, (2) publicly available code for full reproducibility, and (3) more rigorous experimental justification for the chosen benchmark scale. Addressing these points would transform a promising but preliminary contribution into a robust and valuable addition to the generative modeling literature. I recommend acceptance pending these revisions.
