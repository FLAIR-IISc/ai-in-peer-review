This paper extends moment matching networks (MMNs) to the conditional setting. Unlike models that can update on a single example—where the extension is trivial by feeding the conditional into the network—the conditional MMN case is non-trivial. Here, one must match distributions over a large number of generated samples (a mini-batch) to a large number of conditioning inputs, but these distributions are no longer the same across inputs. Using the same conditional for all samples in an update would make the problem trivial but inefficient.

The main issue with the paper is expository. The presentation begins with heavy formalism rather than building intuition for the final objective, and it does not make clear what computations are actually performed during training or how efficient they are. In particular:
- The paper does not explain the details behind the optimized objective (eq. (2)): what assumptions are required, how the expression is derived, and how the terms translate into concrete computations.
- The computational efficiency of the resulting algorithm (per update and overall) is not discussed clearly enough for the reader to understand the practical cost.
- The steps from the objective to the implementable algorithm are under-specified in the main text; while some material appears in the supplement, it is difficult to follow.

By contrast, the non-conditional MMN can be explained—and was in the original paper—without relying on heavy machinery (e.g., reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces); the minimized quantity there is intuitive. It would strengthen this submission to provide an equally clear, intuitive explanation for the conditional case.

Requests for clarification and improvement:
- Provide an intuitive derivation or motivation for eq. (2): how would one arrive at this objective starting from the goal of matching conditional distributions across a mini-batch with varying conditions?
- Lay out, step by step, how eq. (2) leads to the practical training algorithm: what statistics are computed on each mini-batch, how samples and conditionals are paired or grouped, what assumptions are used to make the estimator workable, and what the computational cost is per update.
- Clarify how the algorithm handles the mismatch between “many samples” and “many conditionals” in a mini-batch without resorting to using the same conditional for all samples (which would be inefficient). Make explicit what is being matched to what, and with which weighting or aggregation.
- Summarize any supplemental derivations that are essential to implementation, ideally with a small, concrete example to illustrate the computations.

Overall, the extension to the conditional setting is interesting and potentially valuable, but the paper should prioritize intuition, spell out the actual computations and assumptions behind eq. (2), and make the efficiency–accuracy trade-offs explicit so that readers can understand and reproduce the algorithm.
