This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional probability distributions. The work is a natural and significant extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) [19, 3], which learn joint distributions. The core technical contribution is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective function. This objective measures the distance between the kernel mean embeddings of two conditional distributions, building upon the theoretical work on Hilbert space embeddings of conditional distributions by Song et al. [29]. The proposed CGMMN model generates samples by passing conditional variables and auxiliary random noise through a deep neural network. The network parameters are then trained to minimize the CMMD between the model's output distribution and the true data distribution using stochastic gradient descent with backpropagation. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach on a range of tasks, including predictive modeling, contextual image generation, and "Bayesian dark knowledge," where a simple CGMMN is trained to mimic a more complex Bayesian model.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a clear and important problem: extending the moment-matching framework for deep generative models to the conditional setting. This is a crucial step that significantly broadens the applicability of this class of models, moving them from simple generation to more complex tasks like prediction, structured output, and contextual generation. This work fills a notable gap in the literature, providing a counterpart to other conditional deep generative models like Conditional GANs [21] and Conditional VAEs [28].

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed CMMD objective is a principled and theoretically grounded extension of the MMD criterion. The formulation is elegant and the connection to the conditional mean operator from prior work [29] is well-motivated. A key advantage of the MMD-based approach, which CGMMN inherits, is the training stability. The objective function is simpler to optimize compared to the adversarial min-max objective of GANs, which can be notoriously difficult to train.

3.  **Versatility and Potential Impact:** The authors do an excellent job of demonstrating the model's flexibility through a diverse set of experiments. The application to predictive classification, conditional image generation, and distilling knowledge from complex Bayesian models showcases the wide-ranging potential of CGMMNs. This suggests that the framework could be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners in various domains.

**Weaknesses and Points for Clarification:**

1.  **Clarity on the Empirical Estimator:** A point of clarification is requested regarding the empirical estimator for the conditional embedding operator, `Ĉ_Y|X`. The theoretical definition of the cross-covariance operator `C_XY` (Section 2.3) involves subtracting the outer product of the mean embeddings, `μX ⊗ μY`. However, the empirical estimator `Ĉ_Y|X = Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤` used to derive the final CMMD objective in Equation (2) does not seem to explicitly account for this term. It is unclear if the data is assumed to be centered in the RKHS, or if this term is omitted for another reason. Clarification on this point and its implications for the gradient computation would strengthen the paper's technical presentation.

2.  **Predictive Performance:** While the predictive results on MNIST and SVHN are promising and demonstrate the model's capability, CGMMN's performance is slightly below the state-of-the-art deep generative model for prediction, CMMVA [18], in the paper's own comparison tables. This suggests that while CMMD is a valid objective for discriminative tasks, it may not be as powerful as the max-margin criteria employed by competing models in this specific setting.

3.  **Generative Performance Comparison:** The qualitative results for conditional image generation on the MNIST and Yale Face datasets are interesting and demonstrate that the model can generate diverse, class-conditional samples. However, the evaluation would be significantly more compelling with a direct comparison to other contemporary conditional generative models, most notably Conditional GANs [21]. Without such a baseline, it is difficult to assess the quality of the generated samples relative to the state of the art in conditional generation.

4.  **Minor Issues:** The paper is generally well-written, but there are a few minor typos that should be corrected in the final version (e.g., "an universal RKHS" in Section 2.2, line 65).

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and significant extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. The training procedure is simpler and more stable than adversarial alternatives. The framework is shown to be highly versatile across a diverse set of important machine learning tasks.
*   **Con-acceptance:** There is a minor lack of clarity in the derivation of the empirical CMMD objective. The experimental results, while solid, do not consistently demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, and the generative experiments lack a comparison to key baselines like Conditional GANs.
