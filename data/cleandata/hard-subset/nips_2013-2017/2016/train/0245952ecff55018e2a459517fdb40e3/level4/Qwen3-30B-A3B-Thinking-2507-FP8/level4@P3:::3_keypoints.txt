- Introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) to model conditional distributions.  
- Proposes a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion using kernel mean embeddings.  
- Evaluated on three domains: predictive modeling (classification), contextual image generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.  
- Builds on prior work (GMMN, conditional kernel mean embeddings) and relates to conditional GANs/VAEs.  
- Technical approach is sound with a clear theoretical development and a proof linking CMMD to conditional distribution equality.  
- Experimental design is appropriate with comprehensive results on MNIST, SVHN, and Yale Face datasets.  
- Bayesian dark knowledge experiment is limited to a single regression task (Boston housing), reducing generalizability.  
- Rationale for low-dimensional latent space choices is insufficiently detailed.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized with clear math formulations.  
- Notation is confusing due to dual use of "C" and unclear mapping in Remark 1 (dot notation ambiguity).  
- Extension of MMD to conditional distributions is novel and a meaningful contribution.  
- Provides a principled, non-adversarial alternative to conditional GANs that avoids min-max optimization.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance but significance is limited by use of relatively simple datasets (no CIFAR-10/LSUN).  
- Strengths: solid theory, diverse experimental validation, addresses prior reviewer concerns, and methodological versatility.  
- Weaknesses: limited challenging-dataset evaluation, scalability/latent-dimension analysis lacking, notation confusion, and narrow Bayesian distillation validation.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept, with a strong request to expand experiments on more challenging benchmarks for camera-ready.  
- Pro-accept points: novel theory, simplicity/compatibility with deep learning, competitive results, and fills a gap from unconditional GMMNs.  
- Con-accept points: limited empirical validation, notation issues, insufficient Bayesian distillation breadth, and supervised application is not the most compelling.
