This paper extends generative moment-matching networks (GMMNs) to the conditional setting by matching conditional distributions rather than joint distributions. The core idea is to embed conditional distributions in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), following the conditional mean embedding framework of Song et al. (2009), and to define a conditional MMD (CMMD) objective. The resulting conditional GMMN (CGMMN) is trained with stochastic gradient descent via backpropagation. The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST and Yale Faces), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

Quality and technical correctness:
- The definition of C_hat(Y|X) (Line 90) appears to omit terms involving mu_X and mu_Y (specifically, their empirical estimators) that arise in the standard definition using cross-covariance operators. It is also unusual to implicitly assume mu_X = 0 and mu_Y = 0 (i.e., that the data are centered in the RKHS). Because C_hat(Y|X) is central to CGMMN, the paper should clarify how these mean terms are handled: whether Gram matrices are centered, whether centering is absorbed into regularization, and how this affects gradient computation and the implementation used in the experiments. Clear guidance on this point is essential for reproducibility.
- Beyond this, the methodological development is sound and the CMMD objective is a neat extension of MMD to conditionals.

Originality:
- Using conditional mean embeddings to define a CMMD training criterion for deep conditional generators is, to my knowledge, novel. It is a natural and elegant extension of prior GMMN work to the conditional case, and contrasts with the min–max formulation in conditional GANs.

Significance and empirical evaluation:
- Predictive modeling: On both MNIST and SVHN, CGMMN has higher error rates than the state-of-the-art max-margin deep generative model (CMMVA). This weakens the case for CGMMN as a competitive predictive model.
- Generative modeling: The paper presents plausible conditional samples, but provides no quantitative or qualitative comparison with other conditional generative methods (e.g., conditional GANs). Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess whether CGMMN produces better or more diverse samples than existing alternatives.
- Dark knowledge distillation: The application is interesting, but the evaluation is limited. While the Boston Housing result suggests no degradation relative to the teacher (PBP), broader evidence would strengthen the claim.

Clarity and presentation:
- The manuscript is generally readable, but there are several typos that should be corrected: “Frobeniu norm” (Line 48) should be “Frobenius norm,” “an universal” (Line 65) should be “a universal,” and “which the” (Line 238) should be revised. Please also proofread for minor grammatical issues.
- As noted above, the treatment of centering in RKHS for the conditional embedding estimator should be made explicit, including its impact on the empirical CMMD and gradients.

Pros:
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMN to conditional distributions using CMMD.
- Simple training via backpropagation; avoids adversarial min–max optimization.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across prediction, conditional generation, and model distillation.

Cons:
- Predictive performance trails a strong baseline (CMMVA) on both datasets.
- No direct comparison to other conditional generators (e.g., conditional GANs) for sample quality/diversity.
- Ambiguity in the definition and implementation of the conditional embedding estimator (handling of mu_X, mu_Y).
