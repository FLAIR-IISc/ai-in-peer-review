

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to conditional settings. The authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to learn conditional distributions p(y|x), addressing the data sparsity problem that arises when training separate GMMNs for each conditional distribution. By treating all conditional distributions as a family and matching the model with the conditional embedding operator directly, CGMMN avoids the limitations of naive approaches while maintaining a simple training procedure via backpropagation.

The work builds on GMMN (Li et al., ICML 2015) and kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML 2009; Grünewälder et al., ICML 2012). While GMMN learns joint distributions p(x,y), CGMMN focuses on the more practical conditional distribution p(y|x), which is advantageous for predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation. The theoretical foundation is sound, with the authors establishing that matching conditional embedding operators (Theorem 3) is sufficient for matching conditional distributions, providing a natural extension of MMD to conditional settings.

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution by extending GMMN to conditional distributions, addressing a significant gap in the literature. Previous work on conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs) has primarily focused on adversarial training, while CGMMN offers a moment-matching alternative that is theoretically grounded and computationally simpler.

2. **Quality**: The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. CGMMN-CNN achieves competitive results on SVHN (3.13% error rate) compared to state-of-the-art CMMVA (3.09%). The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment demonstrates that CGMMN can distill knowledge from Bayesian models without degrading prediction performance (RMSE: 2.574±0.089 vs. 2.580±0.093).

3. **Significance**: The applications evaluated are highly relevant to the NIPS community. The paper demonstrates that CGMMN can be applied to diverse tasks including predictive modeling (where conditional distributions are more direct than joint distributions), contextual generation (e.g., generating samples conditioned on class labels), and efficient Bayesian model distillation.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity**: The presentation of the conditional embedding and CMMD in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is dense and difficult to follow without referring to cited papers. The relationship between CMMD and the naive approach (training separate GMMNs for each conditional value) could be clarified with a simple example, particularly since the authors acknowledge this approach might work well in discrete cases like MNIST (10 classes).

2. **Technical Accuracy**: The paper claims that the MMD estimator is unbiased (line 77), which is inaccurate - MMD is asymptotically unbiased but not unbiased for finite samples. Additionally, the definition of the conditional embedding operator in Section 2.3 appears inconsistent with the one in reference [29] (Grünewälder et al., ICML 2012), requiring a different definition of the cross-covariance operator that the authors should clarify.

3. **Computational Considerations**: The CMMD objective requires computing matrix inverses (K̃^{-1}_s and K̃^{-1}_d) that scale cubically with sample size. While the authors mention a mini-batch approach (Algorithm 1), the paper lacks detail on how these inverses are approximated in practice, which is crucial for large-scale applications. The concern about needing large minibatches to mitigate stochasticity (as noted in the draft) remains unaddressed.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to generative modeling, with a clear theoretical extension of GMMN to conditional settings and strong experimental results across multiple applications. The main concerns can be addressed through relatively straightforward revisions.

**Specific Suggestions:**
1. Clarify the relationship between CMMD and the naive approach (training separate GMMNs) with a simple example or comparison on a small dataset, particularly for discrete conditioning variables.

2. Correct the claim about MMD being unbiased (specify that it's asymptotically unbiased).

3. Resolve the inconsistency in the conditional embedding operator definition (Section 2.3 vs. [29]) and provide a clear justification for the authors' formulation.

4. Provide more detail on how matrix inverses are approximated in practice, especially for large-scale applications.

5. Include a brief discussion of computational complexity compared to other conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs).

The paper's contribution is significant and well-executed, and with these minor revisions, it will be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings. The method is theoretically sound, experimentally validated, and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature on generative models.
