This paper proposes a loss function for conditional directed generative models, extending the work on Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMs) and their unconditional loss based on the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) criterion. The approach leverages conditional kernel mean embeddings to construct the conditional loss function. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness on a variety of tasks, including classification, generating handwritten digits and faces, and distilling knowledge in Bayesian models.

This is my second time reviewing this paper. The authors have effectively addressed the minor issues raised in the previous review cycle, namely the justification for using a low-dimensional latent space, the potential for scaling to higher dimensions, the model's comparative utility, and other points of clarification.

While the manuscript has improved, a few areas still require attention:

1.  **Clarity on Weighted MMD:** In Remark 1, the mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form remains unclear. A more detailed derivation would help make this connection more obvious. Furthermore, the dot notation used in the expression is ambiguous; please specify whether it represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product.

2.  **Notation:** The choice of the variable `C` in the weighted MMD expression is unfortunate, as `C` is already used to denote the conditional embedding operator. Using a different symbol would avoid potential confusion.

3.  **Proof for Theorem 3:** Theorems 1 and 2 appropriately cite external sources. However, Theorem 3 is presented without a reference or a proof. The authors must either provide a citation to a paper containing the proof or include a proof in the manuscript or its appendix.

The empirical results are solid. The authors' justification for including a supervised learning application—to highlight the model's versatility—is reasonable, even if it is not the most compelling use case. The application to distilled Bayesian inference is interesting, but its demonstration on a single dataset with one model makes it difficult to assess its general utility in other scenarios.

The primary strength of this approach appears to be in pure generative modeling. The experiments on digit and face generation are comparable to those of the original GMM paper and are sufficient to demonstrate that the core idea is viable. However, to encourage wider adoption and demonstrate the method's competitiveness, it will be crucial to show compelling results on more challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN. While the current results provide a strong proof of concept, scaling to more complex data distributions is a necessary next step.
