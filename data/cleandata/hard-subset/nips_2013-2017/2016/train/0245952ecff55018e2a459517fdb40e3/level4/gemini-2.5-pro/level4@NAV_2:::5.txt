This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework for modeling conditional distributions. The core idea is to define a training objective based on a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the distance between conditional distributions embedded in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. This approach builds upon prior work on kernel embeddings for conditional distributions by Song et al. (2009). The method is evaluated on a range of tasks, including prediction, conditional generation, and knowledge distillation.

**Strengths:**
*   The proposed framework is a novel and elegant extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. It addresses a significant gap by providing a principled, MMD-based method for training deep conditional generative models, which is a natural and important direction for this class of models.
*   The paper demonstrates the versatility of the proposed CGMMN on a diverse set of applications, highlighting its potential as a general-purpose tool for conditional density estimation.

**Weaknesses:**
*   A key technical concern arises from the empirical definition of the conditional embedding operator, `Ĉ_Y|X`. The formulation appears to omit the terms corresponding to the empirical means (`μ_X` and `μ_Y`) from the cross-covariance operator, which would imply that the data is assumed to be centered in the RKHS. As this operator is fundamental to the proposed CMMD objective, a clarification is needed on whether this term was omitted and how it was handled during gradient computation and in the experiments.
*   The empirical results, while demonstrating feasibility, are not consistently state-of-the-art. In the predictive tasks on both MNIST and SVHN, CGMMN's performance is surpassed by CMMVA, a comparable max-margin deep generative model.
*   The evaluation of the model's generative capabilities is incomplete. While the paper shows qualitative examples of generated samples, it lacks a quantitative or qualitative comparison against other prominent conditional generative models, such as conditional GANs. This makes it difficult to assess the relative strengths and weaknesses of CGMMN for generation tasks.
*   The manuscript could be improved by a thorough proofreading to correct several minor typographical errors (e.g., "Frobeniu norm" in the abstract, "an universal" in Section 2.2).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound method for training conditional generative models based on moment matching.
*   The extension from GMMNs to CGMMNs is a significant and well-motivated contribution to the literature on deep generative models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   There is a lack of clarity regarding a central technical detail in the CMMD estimator.
*   The experimental results do not demonstrate a clear performance advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods.
*   The generative performance is not benchmarked against relevant competing models, limiting the significance of the results.
