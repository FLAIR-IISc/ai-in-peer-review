This paper introduces conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMNs), extending generative moment matching networks to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core idea is to leverage kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) metric to quantify the discrepancy between these distributions. The authors evaluate CGMMNs on classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation. A straightforward extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would involve estimating a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters via a common neural network. However, this naive approach suffers from data sparsity, particularly when conditioning variables are continuous – in such cases, each conditional distribution may be represented by only a single example.  Instead, the proposed CGMMN treats the collection of conditional distributions as a family, directly matching the model to the conditional embedding operator rather than individual distributions. While the benefits of this approach are apparent, the naive method can perform reasonably well in certain scenarios, such as conditional generation with a limited number of conditioning variable values (e.g., 10 values in MNIST), and should be included as a baseline for comparison. The formulation of the conditional MMD objective is elegant and, as noted in Remark 1, computationally similar to the unconditional case. Importantly, the K matrices in equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, allowing for standard backpropagation. A potential drawback of the CMMD objective is its sensitivity to minibatch size: insufficient batch size may lead to error dominated by stochasticity, while large batches increase computational cost, especially due to the expense of matrix inversions.  Overall, CGMMN offers a promising approach to modeling conditional distributions, and the experimental results on classification and Bayesian distillation are compelling. However, sections 2.3 and 3.1 were difficult to follow, requiring reference to external papers for complete understanding. There are also a couple of minor issues: (1) the claim on line 77 that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect; (2) the conditional embedding operator on line 90 is inconsistent with the definition in reference [29], seemingly requiring a modified definition of the cross-covariance operator to achieve the form used in the paper.
