This paper introduces a novel loss function for directed generative models conditioned on variables, building upon the framework of generative moment matching networks (GMMs). The original GMM utilized an unconditional maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion; this work extends this by incorporating concepts from conditional kernel mean embeddings to formulate a loss function suitable for conditional generation. The method is evaluated across diverse generative tasks including classification, handwritten digit and face generation, and Bayesian model distillation. 

As a returning reviewer, I’ve noted the authors have addressed the majority of concerns raised in the previous review cycle. These primarily centered on the dimensionality of the latent space, the model’s utility relative to existing approaches, and requests for clarification – all of which have been comprehensively addressed in this revision.

However, some aspects remain unclear. Specifically, Remark 1’s connection between Equation (2) and the weighted MMD form requires further elaboration. The notation employing a dot (⋅) is ambiguous; it is not immediately apparent whether this represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product. Providing a more detailed explanation of this mapping would significantly improve clarity. Additionally, the choice of the symbol ‘C’ for both the conditional embedding and a separate parameter within the loss function is somewhat unfortunate and could lead to confusion.

Theorems 1 and 2 appropriately cite relevant prior work. However, Theorem 3 currently lacks a citation. Either a reference to a paper containing a proof of this theorem should be provided, or a proof must be included directly within the manuscript. 

The empirical results presented are encouraging. While previous reviews questioned the suitability of employing this model for supervised learning, the authors rightly position this application as a demonstration of the model’s versatility, which I find acceptable. The application to Bayesian inference distillation is particularly intriguing, although its evaluation is currently limited to a single dataset and model configuration. This restricts the ability to assess its generalizability and broader applicability. I believe this approach is most likely to gain traction within the field of pure generative modeling.

The generative modeling experiments – specifically digit and face generation – yield results comparable to those reported in the original GMM paper, demonstrating the method’s effectiveness. The selected datasets are reasonable for an initial demonstration of the proposed technique. However, for wider adoption, the authors should consider evaluating the model on more challenging datasets such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN. Demonstrating competitive or superior performance on these benchmarks would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact and encourage further research in this direction.

In summary, this is a solid contribution that builds logically upon existing work. Addressing the remaining points regarding notational clarity (Equation (2) and the use of ‘C’) and providing a reference or proof for Theorem 3 would further elevate the paper's quality. While the current results are promising, demonstrating performance on more complex datasets is crucial for establishing the long-term potential of this approach.
