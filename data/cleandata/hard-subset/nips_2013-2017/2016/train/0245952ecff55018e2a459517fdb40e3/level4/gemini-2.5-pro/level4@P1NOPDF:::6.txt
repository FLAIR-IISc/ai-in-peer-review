This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), an extension of GMMNs designed for conditional generation and prediction tasks. The method's key contribution is its use of the kernel embedding of conditional distributions, leveraging the conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) to measure the discrepancy between them. The authors demonstrate the proposed CGMMN on tasks including classification, conditional generation, and the distillation of Bayesian models.

A naive approach to extending GMMNs to the conditional setting would be to estimate a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, with parameters shared across a common neural network. However, this approach suffers from data sparsity, as each conditional distribution may be supported by only a few examples. In the case of continuous conditioning variables, this problem is particularly acute, as each slice may contain only a single data point. The proposed method elegantly sidesteps this issue by treating the conditional distributions as a unified family, matching the conditional embedding operator of the model directly to that of the data, rather than matching each conditional distribution individually.

The advantage of this approach is clear. Nevertheless, for some problems, the naive approach might still be a reasonable baseline. For instance, in the MNIST conditional generation task, where the conditioning variable is discrete and takes one of only ten values, the data sparsity issue is less severe. A comparison against such a baseline would be an interesting addition to the paper.

It is commendable that the conditional MMD objective is elegantly formulated and, as noted in Remark 1, can be computed almost as easily as the unconditional MMD. Since the kernel matrices in Equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, backpropagation can be performed in the standard way. However, one potential concern is that the CMMD objective may require large minibatches. With small batches, the stochasticity of the gradient estimate could dominate the training signal. This presents a difficult trade-off, as large minibatches make the objective computationally expensive, particularly due to the matrix inverse operations, which can be prohibitive for very large batches.

Overall, CMMD provides a valuable framework for modeling conditional distributions, and the experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are compelling. The presentation, however, could be improved in a few areas. Specifically, Sections 2.3 and 3.1 were difficult to follow, and I found it necessary to consult the referenced papers to fully understand the methodology.

There are also a few minor technical points to address:
1.  The claim on line 77 that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect. The standard empirical estimator is biased.
2.  The definition of the conditional embedding operator on line 90 appears inconsistent with that in reference [29]. It seems that arriving at the form used in this paper would require a different definition of the cross-covariance operator.
