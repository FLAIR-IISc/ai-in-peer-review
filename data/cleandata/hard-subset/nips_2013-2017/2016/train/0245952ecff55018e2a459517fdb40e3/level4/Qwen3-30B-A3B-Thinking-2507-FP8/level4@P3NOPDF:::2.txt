

The paper extends moment matching networks (MMN) to a conditional setting, addressing a non-trivial challenge not present in standard MMN or single-example update models. While the latter can trivially incorporate conditioning (e.g., by feeding the condition into the network), the conditional MMN requires matching distributions of *large batches* of samples to *large batches* of inputs under *varying conditions*. Crucially, this distribution matching cannot rely on identical conditioning per update (which would be computationally inefficient), making the extension inherently more complex. The core contribution is thus valid and meaningful, but the paper’s presentation significantly undermines its clarity and impact.

The primary weakness lies in the paper’s failure to provide intuitive motivation for the conditional formulation, instead launching directly into formal mathematics. The non-conditional MMN (in the original paper) is effectively explained without heavy machinery—its objective (minimizing the RKHS distance between distributions) is intuitive and grounded in the goal of matching sample distributions. The conditional extension, however, lacks this accessibility. The authors present Equation (2) as a given, without explaining *why* this specific form is chosen or how it addresses the core challenge of batch-wise conditional distribution matching. Key questions remain unanswered:  
1. **Intuition**: How does one *reason* toward Equation (2)? What conceptual leap connects the problem (matching distributions across varying conditions) to this mathematical form?  
2. **Algorithmic Translation**: What are the concrete computational steps from Equation (2) to the practical implementation? The supplementary material hints at these steps but fails to clarify them coherently, leaving readers to reverse-engineer the algorithm.

This omission is particularly problematic because the efficiency and feasibility of the method hinge on these details. The paper states the objective (Eq. 2) but neglects to:  
- Specify computational assumptions (e.g., kernel choices, approximations for scalability),  
- Quantify computational complexity relative to standard MMN or alternatives,  
- Explain *how* the conditional term integrates into the matching process without degenerating into inefficient per-example updates.  

For instance, the claim that "distributions are not the same anymore" due to varying conditions needs elaboration: *Which distributions differ, and why does their mismatch necessitate this specific objective?* Without this, the reader cannot assess whether the formulation is both necessary and sufficient for the problem. The non-conditional case avoids such pitfalls by prioritizing intuition—this conditional extension should follow suit.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by restructuring to first establish the *intuitive problem* (e.g., "We need to align the distribution of generated samples with target samples *for each distinct condition* in the batch, without reprocessing the entire dataset per condition"), then derive Equation (2) as the natural solution. A concise analogy or step-by-step rationale (e.g., "Extending the non-conditional objective requires...") would bridge the gap between the problem and the math. The algorithmic steps should then be explicitly mapped to Equation (2), highlighting computational trade-offs (e.g., "The conditional term adds O(K) complexity per batch, where K is the number of unique conditions").

As it stands, the paper risks being perceived as a technical exercise rather than a principled methodological advance. The conditional extension’s novelty is diluted by the lack of clear communication about *why* it works and *how* to implement it. This is especially disappointing given the elegance of the non-conditional MMN. To justify the extension’s value, the authors must demonstrate not just *that* it works, but *how* and *why* it is the right approach for the conditional setting.

**Recommendation**: Major revision. The paper’s core idea has merit but requires substantial reworking to clarify the conditional formulation’s intuition and algorithmic details. The authors should:  
1. Replace the initial mathematical exposition with a clear problem statement and intuitive motivation for Equation (2).  
2. Explicitly address the two questions above in the main text (not just the supplement).  
3. Provide a brief, step-by-step breakdown of the algorithm’s computational flow, emphasizing efficiency.  
Without these changes, the contribution remains obscured and its practical utility unclear.
