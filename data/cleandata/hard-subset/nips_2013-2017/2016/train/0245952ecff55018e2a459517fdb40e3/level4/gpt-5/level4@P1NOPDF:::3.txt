This paper proposes a loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables. The method extends generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) by moving from an unconditional MMD-based loss to a conditional version derived from conditional kernel mean embeddings. The approach is evaluated on a range of tasks, including classification, generating handwritten digits and faces, and distilling knowledge in Bayesian models.

This is my second time reviewing the paper. Previously, reviewers raised minor concerns: the choice of a low-dimensional latent space and whether the method scales to higher-dimensional latent spaces, the practical utility of the model relative to alternatives, and several clarifications. From the current text, these points appear to have been addressed directly.

I do, however, have a few remaining concerns about clarity in the theory:

- Regarding Remark 1, it is not clear how Equation (2) maps to the weighted MMD formulation. The “dot” notation is ambiguous: is this standard matrix multiplication or the Hadamard (elementwise) product? Please provide explicit definitions and a short derivation or rewriting that makes the mapping to weighted MMD immediate.
- The choice of the symbol C is unfortunate, as C is already used to denote the conditional embedding operator. Reusing the notation risks confusion; please rename one of these quantities.
- Theorems 1 and 2 are tied to prior work and are properly referenced, but Theorem 3 has no citation. Please provide a reference to a source containing a proof, or include a complete proof in the appendix.

The empirical results are solid. Although earlier reviewers questioned the value of using this framework for supervised learning, the authors justified it as a demonstration of versatility, which I find reasonable. The distilled Bayesian inference application is interesting and potentially impactful, but it is demonstrated on only one dataset with one model; as a result, it is difficult to assess how broadly applicable this technique will be. A more diverse set of Bayesian models and datasets would strengthen the case.

I believe this approach has the greatest potential impact in pure generative modeling. The experiments in that setting are comparable to those in the original GMMN paper. The digits and faces datasets are sufficient to show that the idea works in relatively simple regimes. However, to drive broader adoption and convince practitioners of the method’s competitiveness, results on more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10 or LSUN) would be very valuable. Such benchmarks would also help clarify the method’s scalability and stability in higher-dimensional latent spaces and more complex conditional settings.

In summary, this is a well-motivated extension of GMMNs using conditional kernel mean embeddings, with reasonable empirical validation and clear potential. Addressing the notational and derivational ambiguities (especially around Remark 1 and Theorem 3) and adding results on more difficult generative benchmarks would significantly strengthen the paper.
