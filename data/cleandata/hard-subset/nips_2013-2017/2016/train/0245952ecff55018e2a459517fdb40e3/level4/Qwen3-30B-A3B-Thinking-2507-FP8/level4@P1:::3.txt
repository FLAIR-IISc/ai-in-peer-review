

This paper introduces conditional generative moment-matching networks (CGMMN), an extension of generative moment-matching networks (GMMN) that learns conditional distributions using conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). The authors leverage conditional kernel mean embeddings to define a loss function that enables training via backpropagation. The method is evaluated across multiple tasks including classification, conditional image generation (MNIST and Yale Face datasets), and knowledge distillation from Bayesian models.

This is my second review of this paper. The previous reviewers raised several minor concerns, which the authors have addressed directly in the revision: they clarified the choice of low-dimensional latent space (justifying it as sufficient for prediction tasks), explained the model's scalability to higher dimensions, and provided stronger justification for the approach's utility compared to alternatives. These revisions appear satisfactory.

The most significant technical concern pertains to Remark 1 and Equation (2), where the connection to weighted MMD is unclear. The authors state that CMMD "applies non-uniform weights, reflecting the influence of conditional variables," but the mathematical mapping between Equation (2) and this weighted formulation requires further clarification. Specifically, the dot notation used in the equation is ambiguous—does it represent matrix multiplication or Hadamard product? A more detailed explanation of how the CMMD objective reduces to a weighted MMD would strengthen the paper.

The paper also uses the symbol "C" for two distinct purposes: to denote the conditional embedding operator and to represent the regularization matrix. This notational conflict is confusing, particularly since the conditional embedding operator is already denoted by "C" in Section 2.3. The authors should reconsider their notation to avoid this ambiguity.

Additionally, Theorem 3 lacks a proper reference or proof. Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 appropriately cite relevant prior work, but Theorem 3, which establishes the connection between equal conditional embedding operators and identical conditional distributions, requires either a reference to a proof in the literature or a concise proof within the paper.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates the model's versatility across multiple domains. For classification tasks (MNIST and SVHN), CGMMN achieves competitive results, particularly when using CNN architectures. The authors justify using supervised learning as a demonstration of model versatility rather than as a primary application, which is reasonable. The conditional image generation results on MNIST and Yale Face datasets are visually compelling, with samples conditioned on class labels showing clear class-specific patterns. The authors also provide evidence against simple data copying by examining nearest neighbors of generated samples.

The Bayesian knowledge distillation application is particularly interesting, as it demonstrates CGMMN's utility in efficiently approximating complex Bayesian predictive distributions. However, the evaluation is limited to a single regression task (Boston housing dataset) using a single Bayesian model (PBP). While the results show no performance degradation compared to the teacher model, the scope is too narrow to confidently assess the method's broader applicability. A more comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets and Bayesian models would strengthen this contribution.

The authors make a reasonable case that CGMMN's primary value lies in pure generative modeling rather than supervised learning. The experimental results on standard image generation benchmarks (MNIST, Yale Face) are comparable to those reported in the original GMMN paper. However, to demonstrate broader impact, the authors should include results on more challenging datasets such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN, which would better establish the method's potential for wider adoption.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, with a clear theoretical foundation and well-executed experiments. The clarity of presentation is generally good, though the mathematical exposition could be improved by addressing the notational conflicts and clarifying the relationship between CMMD and weighted MMD. The work demonstrates solid originality in extending GMMN to conditional distributions while maintaining the simplicity of the training procedure. The significance is moderate: the approach is a natural extension of existing work with clear applications in conditional generative modeling, though the broader impact is limited by the current scope of experiments.

To summarize the key strengths and weaknesses:

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical extension of GMMN to conditional distributions
- Well-executed experiments across multiple application domains
- Competitive performance on standard classification and generation benchmarks
- Demonstrates practical utility in Bayesian knowledge distillation

Weaknesses:
- Ambiguous notation (confusing use of "C")
- Insufficient explanation of the relationship between CMMD and weighted MMD
- Limited evaluation of Bayesian distillation application
- Lack of results on more challenging datasets (CIFAR-10, LSUN)

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution that extends the GMMN framework to conditional settings with practical value. The paper would benefit from addressing the notational issues and clarifying the mathematical relationships, as well as expanding the experimental evaluation to more challenging generative tasks. As currently written, it makes a meaningful but somewhat incremental contribution to the field of generative modeling.
