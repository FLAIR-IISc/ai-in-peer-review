

This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions. The authors introduce a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to train a conditional generative model that maps input variables to target distributions. CGMMN builds on GMMN (Li et al., 2015), which uses MMD for unconditional generative modeling, but extends it to handle conditional distributions by embedding conditional distributions in a Hilbert space and measuring the discrepancy between empirical conditional embeddings.

The paper's main contribution is a theoretically grounded framework for conditional generative modeling that maintains the simplicity of GMMN's training procedure. The authors demonstrate CGMMN's effectiveness across three application domains: predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN classification), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on labels), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (learning compact models from Bayesian teachers). Results show competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods, including conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and max-margin DGMs (Li et al., 2015).

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality:** The paper makes a clear and significant extension to the GMMN framework, addressing a gap in conditional generative modeling. While conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Kingma et al., 2014) exist, CGMMN provides a distinct approach based on moment matching that avoids the min-max optimization challenges of GANs. The extension to conditional distributions is non-trivial, as the authors correctly note, because the distributions to match depend on the conditional variables, making standard MMD approaches inapplicable.

2. **Significance:** The applications demonstrated (predictive modeling, contextual generation, Bayesian distillation) showcase the framework's versatility. The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment is particularly compelling as it demonstrates practical utility in model compression, a significant challenge in deep learning.

3. **Quality:** The theoretical foundation is sound, with proper mathematical treatment of conditional kernel embeddings. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate consistent performance across multiple datasets and tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity:** The paper's primary weakness is poor explanation of the practical implementation. The authors begin with heavy mathematical formalism (Section 2) without providing intuitive motivation for the CMMD formulation. The key equation (2) is presented without sufficient explanation of how it relates to the underlying intuition. For example, the paper doesn't clearly answer: "How would one intuitively arrive at this formula?" or "What is the actual computational procedure when implementing this?"

2. **Algorithmic Detail:** The connection between the theoretical CMMD objective (equation 2) and the practical algorithm (Algorithm 1) is insufficiently explained. While the supplement provides some details, the main paper should clarify how the CMMD objective is computed efficiently in practice. The paper mentions "kernel tricks" but doesn't specify how the kernel computations scale with batch size or how the inverse matrices are handled during training.

3. **Comparison to Prior Work:** While the paper references conditional GANs, it doesn't sufficiently contrast CGMMN with other conditional moment-matching approaches. The relationship to the conditional mean embedding work (Song et al., 2013) could be clarified.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. The paper would benefit from an intuitive explanation of the CMMD objective before presenting the formal mathematics. For example: "When conditioning on input x, the target distribution changes with x, so instead of comparing two fixed distributions as in MMD, we need to compare distributions that depend on x. This can be achieved by weighting the kernel similarities based on x, which leads to the CMMD objective."

2. The authors should explicitly outline the computational steps for implementing equation (2), including how the kernel matrices are computed, how the inverses are handled (especially with large batches), and the computational complexity.

3. A brief comparison to the conditional VAE (Kingma et al., 2014) would help contextualize CGMMN's advantages, particularly regarding the avoidance of variational inference.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Addresses an important gap in conditional generative modeling with a theoretically sound approach
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance across diverse applications
- Maintains the simplicity of GMMN's training procedure while extending its capabilities
- Provides a new perspective on conditional distribution learning that could benefit practitioners

*Con:*
- Theoretical contribution is substantial but obscured by poor exposition
- Key algorithmic details are not sufficiently explained in the main paper
- The connection between theory and practice is weak, making implementation difficult to follow
- The paper's complexity may limit its accessibility to a broader audience

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to the GMMN framework with practical value across multiple applications. The core idea is novel and significant, but the paper's failure to clearly explain the intuition behind the CMMD objective and the practical implementation details substantially reduces its impact. The authors have made a strong theoretical contribution but have not communicated it effectively to the reader.

The paper would be substantially stronger with a clearer exposition of the intuition behind the CMMD objective and a more detailed explanation of the practical algorithm. As it stands, the theoretical contribution is overshadowed by the paper's poor clarity. With the suggested revisions, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

I recommend a borderline decision: the paper has significant theoretical merit and strong empirical results, but its current presentation is insufficiently clear for NIPS. The authors should substantially improve the exposition of the CMMD objective and its implementation before the paper can be recommended for acceptance.
