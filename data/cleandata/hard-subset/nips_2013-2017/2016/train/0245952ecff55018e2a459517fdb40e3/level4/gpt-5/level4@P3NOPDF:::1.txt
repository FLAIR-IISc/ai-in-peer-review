Summary
The paper proposes a neural network approach trained using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion. The method is evaluated on standard, relatively small benchmarks for both discriminative and generative performance. The work represents a meaningful extension of deep generative modeling techniques.

Strengths
- The core idea—leveraging conditional MMD within a neural network framework for generative modeling—is interesting and timely.
- The paper is generally clear and well written. While some derivations are dense, the narrative is understandable overall.
- The experimental tasks, though modest in scale, are in line with datasets commonly used in related prior work.

Weaknesses
- The datasets and tasks are relatively small, limiting the conclusions about scalability and real-world applicability.
- The training procedure appears computationally intensive, yet the paper lacks a clear accounting of runtime, resource usage, or efficiency compared to established baselines such as DSN.
- Reproducibility is a concern. The model and training details provided are likely insufficient for practitioners to reproduce the reported results, and no reference implementation is made available.

Questions for the authors
- Runtime and resources: How long does training take end-to-end and per epoch? On what hardware (GPU/CPU type, memory)? How does this compare to DSN and other relevant baselines under matched conditions?
- MMD details: Which kernels are used for the (conditional) MMD? How are kernel hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidths) selected or adapted? Do you use biased or unbiased estimators? What is the batch size, and how do you manage the O(n^2) cost of MMD computation (e.g., subsampling, random features)?
- Conditioning mechanism: How is conditioning implemented in the MMD term (e.g., through joint kernels, separate kernels per condition, or feature concatenation)? How sensitive are results to this design?
- Training stability: How sensitive is performance to hyperparameters (learning rates, batch sizes, regularization coefficients)? Are there common failure modes or instability issues?
- Evaluation: What exact metrics and protocols are used for generative quality and discriminative performance? How many runs are averaged, and are standard deviations reported?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a clear runtime analysis: report wall-clock times, number of epochs, and computational cost relative to DSN and at least one additional contemporary baseline, under the same hardware and comparable model capacity.
- Strengthen reproducibility:
  - Release reference code, trained models, and scripts to reproduce tables/figures.
  - Document complete model specifications (architectures, layer dimensions, activations, normalization, regularization, and initialization).
  - Report all optimization details (optimizer type, learning rate schedules, weight decay, early stopping, gradient clipping, number of training steps/epochs, and random seeds).
  - Include data preprocessing and augmentation procedures.
- Clarify mathematical presentation: add a concise algorithmic summary or pseudocode for training, define all symbols upon first use, and move lengthier derivations or proofs to an appendix with a step-by-step walkthrough.
- Add ablations and sensitivity analyses: compare unconditional vs. conditional MMD; vary kernel choices and bandwidths; test alternative conditioning schemes; and examine the impact of batch size on performance and speed.
- Expand empirical scope where feasible: even one or two larger-scale datasets or higher-resolution settings would better demonstrate scalability. If not possible, provide a discussion that analyzes expected computational and performance behavior as data and model size grow.
- Report uncertainty: include means and standard deviations over multiple random seeds, and, where applicable, statistical significance tests.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and relevant contribution to deep generative modeling, proposing a conditional MMD-based training objective with promising results on standard small-scale benchmarks. The paper’s main limitations are the modest empirical scope, incomplete reporting of computational costs, and limited reproducibility due to missing implementation details and lack of released code. Addressing these issues—particularly by adding runtime comparisons (e.g., with DSN), providing full model and training specifications, and sharing reference code—would markedly strengthen the paper’s impact and credibility. Pending these improvements, I view the work as interesting and potentially impactful within its sub-area.
