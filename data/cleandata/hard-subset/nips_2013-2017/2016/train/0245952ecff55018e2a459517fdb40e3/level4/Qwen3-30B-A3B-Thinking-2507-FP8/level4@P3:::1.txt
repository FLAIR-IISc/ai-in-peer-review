

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a novel extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) that learns conditional distributions using Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). The authors address three important application areas: predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. CGMMN extends the work of Li et al. (ICML 2015) on GMMN to handle conditional distributions, which has been a significant limitation in previous moment-matching approaches. Unlike conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), CGMMN maintains a simpler optimization objective without the min-max game theory complexity. The paper also relates to recent work on conditional variational autoencoders (Soň et al., NIPS 2015) and Bayesian dark knowledge (Korattikara et al., NIPS 2015), positioning CGMMN as a complementary approach for learning conditional distributions.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations, with rigorous derivations of CMMD and its connection to kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. The authors provide a clear generative process and training algorithm, making CGMMN straightforward to implement using standard deep learning frameworks. The experimental evaluation covers multiple applications: predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN datasets, contextual generation on MNIST and Yale Face datasets, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation on the Boston housing dataset. Results show competitive performance across all tasks, particularly in contextual generation where CGMMN generates class-specific samples that closely match the target distribution.

**Strengths:**
1. **Quality:** The theoretical framework is sound, with proper mathematical derivations and clear connections to previous work on kernel embeddings (Gretton et al., JMLR 2012; Song et al., ICML 2009). The experimental results are well-supported by comprehensive comparisons against multiple strong baselines, including recent state-of-the-art methods like CMMVA (Li et al., NIPS 2015) and DSN (Lee et al., AISTATS 2015).

2. **Originality:** The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions addresses a significant gap in the literature. While conditional GANs exist, CGMMN offers a simpler training procedure without min-max optimization, making it more accessible for practitioners. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work, particularly in the application of CMMD to conditional distributions.

3. **Significance:** The paper demonstrates CGMMN's applicability across multiple important domains: predictive modeling (where it achieves competitive results with discriminative models), contextual generation (producing high-quality samples conditioned on class labels), and Bayesian dark knowledge (efficiently distilling predictive distributions from Bayesian models without performance degradation). These applications are both theoretically interesting and practically valuable.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity:** While the paper is generally well-organized, some mathematical derivations (particularly in Section 3.1) are dense and would benefit from additional intuitive explanations or examples to help readers grasp the CMMD objective. The connection between Theorem 3 and practical implementation could be made more explicit.

2. **Computational Considerations:** The paper does not provide sufficient information on computational complexity or training time comparisons with competing methods. For instance, it would be valuable to know how CGMMN's training time compares to DSN (which achieves the best results on SVHN) or CMMVA, particularly given the use of kernel-based operations that scale cubically with dataset size. The authors mention a mini-batch version in Appendix A.2.1, but without empirical training time results, it's unclear how practical the approach is for larger datasets.

3. **Reproducibility:** While the paper describes the experimental setup in detail, it would significantly strengthen the contribution to make reference code available. As noted by the authors themselves in the draft review, practitioners would have difficulty reproducing the exact models evaluated without access to implementation details.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**
*Pro:* The paper presents a theoretically sound, well-motivated extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with clear applications across multiple domains. The results are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, and the training procedure is simpler than conditional GANs. The work addresses a meaningful gap in the literature on generative models.

*Con:* The paper lacks sufficient computational complexity analysis and training time comparisons, which would be important for practitioners considering adoption. Reproducibility could be improved with code availability, and some mathematical derivations would benefit from additional clarity.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling by extending moment-matching networks to handle conditional distributions. The theoretical foundation is strong, the applications are relevant, and the results are competitive with existing methods. While the paper would benefit from additional computational complexity analysis and code availability, these are reasonable requests that could be addressed in a revised version. The work is well-positioned to contribute to both theoretical understanding and practical applications in generative modeling, making it suitable for acceptance at NIPS.
