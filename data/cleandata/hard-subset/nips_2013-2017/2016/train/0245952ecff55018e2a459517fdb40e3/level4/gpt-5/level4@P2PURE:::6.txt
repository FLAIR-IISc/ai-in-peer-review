This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), an extension of GMMNs to conditional generation and prediction. The core idea is to use kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional MMD (CMMD) metric to measure discrepancies between conditional distributions. The authors evaluate CGMMN on classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation.

A straightforward extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would fit a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters via a common neural network. The drawback is that each conditional typically has very few examples; with continuous conditioning variables, a given condition may have only a single example, leading to severe data sparsity. The proposed approach instead treats all conditionals as a family and matches the model to the conditional embedding operator directly, rather than fitting each conditional separately. While this advantage is clear, the naive approach could still do reasonably well in cases like MNIST, where the conditioning variable takes only 10 values. It would be useful to include this naive baseline for comparison.

It is appealing that the CMMD objective has a clean formulation and can be computed almost exactly as in the unconditional MMD case (as noted in Remark 1). All K matrices in Equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, allowing backpropagation to proceed as usual.

A concern is that CMMD may require large minibatches; otherwise, minibatch stochasticity could dominate the error. However, large minibatches make the objective expensive to compute, particularly because the required matrix inversions can be prohibitively costly.

Overall, CMMD provides a compelling way to model conditional distributions, and the results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are interesting. That said, Sections 2.3 and 3.1 were difficult to follow; I had to consult the cited papers to fully understand the methodology.

Minor issues:
(1) Line 77 claims the MMD estimator is unbiased, which is not correct.
(2) Line 90 defines the conditional embedding operator in a way that is inconsistent with reference [29]. The form used here appears to require a different definition of the cross-covariance operator.
