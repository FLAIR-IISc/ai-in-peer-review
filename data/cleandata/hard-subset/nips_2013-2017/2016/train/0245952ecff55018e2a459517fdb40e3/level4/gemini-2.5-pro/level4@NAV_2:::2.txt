This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extends the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions. Whereas GMMNs learn a joint distribution by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model samples and data, CGMMNs learn a conditional distribution p(y|x) by minimizing a novel Conditional MMD (CMMD) objective. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach with strong empirical results on a range of tasks, including predictive modeling, conditional image generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

The extension from the unconditional to the conditional setting is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. In a minibatch setting, each input `x` corresponds to a different target distribution `p(y|x)`, making a direct application of MMD challenging. The proposed CMMD objective, which measures the discrepancy between conditional embedding operators in an RKHS, is a novel way to address this problem. The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the model's effectiveness across diverse applications, suggesting its potential significance.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its lack of clarity, particularly concerning the motivation and practical implementation of the core CMMD objective. The presentation begins with a highly technical mathematical formalism (kernel embedding of conditional operators) but fails to provide an intuitive explanation for the final objective in Equation (2). The original GMMN paper provided a more accessible motivation for the MMD loss, which made the method easier to grasp. A similar intuitive build-up for the conditional case is missing here.

Furthermore, the paper does not sufficiently detail the practical computational steps that bridge the gap between the theoretical objective in Equation (2) and the final algorithm. Key details about the algorithm's efficiency, scalability, and the specific assumptions required to make it computationally tractable within a mini-batch gradient descent framework are not clearly explained in the main text. While some information is present in the supplement, it is difficult to follow and would be better integrated into the core paper to improve reproducibility and understanding.

To summarize, here is a list of arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for acceptance (Strengths):**
*   **Originality & Significance:** The paper proposes a novel and non-trivial extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting, filling an important gap in this class of generative models.
*   **Quality:** The proposed method is evaluated on a diverse set of challenging tasks and achieves competitive performance, demonstrating its practical utility and significance.
*   **Motivation:** The problem is well-motivated, as conditional models are fundamental to many machine learning tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance (Weaknesses):**
*   **Clarity:** The core CMMD objective is presented without sufficient intuition, making it difficult to understand *why* the specific form of Equation (2) is a good choice. The reliance on abstract mathematical concepts without a clear conceptual bridge is a major barrier to understanding.
*   **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The paper lacks a clear explanation of the computational steps of the algorithm, its complexity, and the practical approximations made. This makes it difficult for the reader to understand how the method is implemented in practice and to reproduce the results.

I would encourage the authors to revise the paper to address these clarity issues. Specifically, it would be beneficial to:
1.  Provide an intuitive explanation or derivation for the CMMD objective in Equation (2). How does this formula naturally arise from the goal of matching conditional distributions?
2.  Clearly outline the sequence of computations performed in the practical algorithm, starting from Equation (2). What are the computational costs and any assumptions made to ensure efficiency?
