This paper proposes a method for conditional generative modeling by extending the framework of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs). The core contribution is a novel objective function that enables the matching of conditional distributions. This extension is non-trivial; unlike models that can be updated on single examples where conditioning is often a simple matter of augmenting the input, GMMNs operate on mini-batches. This requires a method to match a batch of generated samples to a batch of target samples, where each sample pair is associated with a distinct conditioning variable.

The primary strength of the paper is in tackling this challenging and important problem. The proposed extension to the moment matching framework for conditional generation is a novel contribution to this class of generative models.

However, the paper has two major weaknesses in its current form, primarily centered on clarity and the connection between theory and practice.

First, the paper lacks a clear, intuitive motivation for the proposed objective function (Equation 2). The presentation immediately delves into the formal mathematical framework of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) without first building an intuition for why the proposed objective is a suitable choice for conditional moment matching. The original GMMN paper, by contrast, succeeded in providing a very accessible explanation for its objective function before introducing the kernel trick. This paper would be significantly strengthened by adopting a similar pedagogical approach. As it stands, the reader is presented with a complex formula without the conceptual scaffolding needed to understand its origin and purpose. A clear, high-level explanation would make the work more accessible and impactful.

Second, the paper provides insufficient detail regarding the practical implementation and computational complexity of the proposed algorithm. It does not clearly delineate the concrete computational steps required to optimize Equation 2. This leaves critical questions unanswered: What is the computational cost of evaluating the objective and its gradients for a mini-batch? What assumptions or approximations, if any, are necessary to make the algorithm tractable? While some of these details may be present in the supplementary material, the main paper must provide a clear overview of the algorithm's mechanics and efficiency to allow the reader to assess its practicality. The current presentation makes it difficult to understand the transition from the theoretical objective to a working algorithm.

To improve the paper, I would strongly encourage the authors to address the following questions in a revised manuscript:

1.  Could you provide a more intuitive, step-by-step explanation for the objective function in Equation (2)? For instance, starting from the goal of matching conditional distributions P(X|Y) and P_g(X|Y), how does one arrive at this specific formulation without immediately resorting to the full RKHS formalism? An intuitive bridge between the problem and the proposed mathematical solution is needed.

2.  Could you please provide a clear, step-by-step breakdown of the practical algorithm used for training? Specifically, what are the exact computations performed to evaluate the loss and its gradient for a mini-batch? A discussion of the computational complexity (e.g., in terms of batch size and data dimensionality) would be highly beneficial for understanding the trade-offs of this method.

In summary, while the paper addresses a valuable problem with a novel approach, its potential is hindered by a presentation that prioritizes mathematical formalism over intuitive understanding and practical details. Addressing the issues of motivation and algorithmic clarity would significantly improve the quality and impact of this work.
