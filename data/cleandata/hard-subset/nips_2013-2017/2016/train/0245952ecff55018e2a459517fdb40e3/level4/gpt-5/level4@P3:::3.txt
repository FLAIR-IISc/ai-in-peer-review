Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative model trained by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective. CGMMN extends Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) by moving from matching joint distributions with MMD to matching conditional distributions via kernel conditional mean embeddings. The model generates y by feeding both the condition x and a noise vector h through a neural network, and learns parameters by back-propagation on a closed-form CMMD loss. Empirical results span supervised prediction (MNIST, SVHN), class-conditional generation (MNIST, Yale Faces, with and without an autoencoder code space), and knowledge distillation from a Bayesian teacher (PBP on Boston Housing).

The work builds on: (i) kernel mean embeddings and MMD two-sample testing (Gretton et al.); (ii) conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., Grunewälder et al.); (iii) GMMN for unconditional generation (Dziugaite et al., Li et al.); and relates to conditional deep generators such as cGANs (Mirza & Osindero; Denton et al.) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al.). Compared to GANs, the training objective is a single, differentiable loss without adversarial min–max optimization, and unlike VAEs, it does not require tractable likelihood or variational bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a natural, principled extension of MMD-based learning to conditional distributions using the conditional embedding operator.
- Simple training: closed-form CMMD objective amenable to back-propagation, with the inverse kernel matrices depending only on x.
- Flexible architectures: can incorporate MLPs or CNNs; compatible with autoencoder code spaces.
- Empirical viability: competitive classification on MNIST and SVHN; reasonable conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces; illustrative application to Bayesian dark knowledge.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications
- Remark 1 and Eq. (2): The mapping to a “weighted MMD” form is underspecified. Please define the dot operator (·) explicitly as matrix multiplication (not Hadamard), and write the weighting matrices. From Eq. (2), one can rewrite
  - Tr(Kd K̃d^-1 Ld K̃d^-1) = Tr(Ld C1) with C1 = K̃d^-1 Kd K̃d^-1
  - Tr(Ks K̃s^-1 Ls K̃s^-1) = Tr(Ls C2) with C2 = K̃s^-1 Ks K̃s^-1
  - Tr(Ksd K̃d^-1 Lds K̃s^-1) = Tr(Lds C3) with C3 = K̃s^-1 Ksd K̃d^-1
  This makes the analogy to MMD precise: standard MMD corresponds to C1 = C2 = C3 = J (all-ones) up to constants, while CMMD uses non-uniform, data-dependent weights induced by kernels on x. Adding these identities would greatly improve readability.
- Notation collision: Using C for both conditional operators (e.g., CXY) and weighting matrices C1–C3 is confusing. Please choose distinct symbols.
- Theorem 3: A proof or citation is needed. As stated, it follows from the injectivity of the mean embedding in a universal RKHS and properties of the conditional embedding operator (see Fukumizu et al., 2004/2011; Song et al., 2009; Grunewälder et al., 2012). Please add a formal statement with assumptions and a proof sketch, or cite a source where the result appears.
- Experimental scope: Supervised classification is a serviceable demonstration, but not the strongest use case. Conditional generation is compelling, yet the datasets (MNIST, Yale Faces) are relatively simple. There is no comparison to cGAN or CVAE on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10/LSUN) or in-domain metrics. The Bayesian distillation result is limited to one dataset and one teacher; broader evidence would strengthen the significance claim.
- Practical details and reproducibility: Please specify kernel choices, bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic or cross-validation) for both x and y, the regularization λ, and sensitivity analyses. Clarify the perturbation scheme for x when sampling from the teacher in distillation. Discuss computational complexity: CMMD involves Gram matrix inversions per batch; report batch sizes, amortized costs, and memory footprint.
- Minor clarity issues: The superscripts s/d (real vs. generated samples) appear to switch roles across sections; standardizing the notation would avoid confusion. Briefly justify the choice of low-dimensional noise for classification and report whether higher-dimensional h changes results (you mention it slows training).

Quality
The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The CMMD objective is a principled extension of MMD leveraging conditional mean embeddings. Theoretical assumptions and practical estimators are mostly accurate, but Theorem 3 requires a proof/citation and the discussion of regularity assumptions (e.g., CXX^-3/2 CXY Hilbert–Schmidt) could be tightened. The empirical evaluation is careful for the chosen tasks, though limited in scope for broader claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The main areas to improve clarity are: the explicit derivation in Remark 1, consistent notation for datasets, precise definitions of kernels/hyperparameters, and a reference or proof for Theorem 3.

Originality
Extending GMMN to conditional distributions via CMMD is a natural but nontrivial contribution, combining insights from conditional embeddings with deep generators. While related to cGAN and CVAE, the objective and training dynamics differ meaningfully.

Significance
The idea is potentially useful for conditional generation and knowledge distillation when one desires a stable, likelihood-free training objective. Current experiments demonstrate feasibility but fall short of establishing clear state-of-the-art performance or broad utility across challenging domains. Stronger baselines (cGAN/CVAE), larger datasets, and more thorough distillation studies would increase impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add the explicit derivation connecting Eq. (2) to the weighted-MMD view and define the dot operator.
- Provide a proof or authoritative citation for Theorem 3; restate assumptions clearly.
- Expand comparisons to conditional baselines (cGAN, CVAE) and include at least one more challenging conditional generation benchmark (e.g., CIFAR-10).
- Report sensitivity to kernel choices and λ, and discuss the effect of using non-differentiable kernels on x.
- Clarify notation, list all training hyperparameters, and consider releasing code for reproducibility.
- For Bayesian distillation, include an additional dataset and a different teacher (e.g., HMC/SGHMC or dropout-as-Bayes) to demonstrate generality.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, clear extension of GMMN to conditional settings with a simple, stable training objective.
- Solid theoretical grounding in kernel conditional embeddings.
- Competitive performance on standard tasks; flexible to different architectures.
- Useful perspective for knowledge distillation without adversarial training.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental scope; no comparisons to strong conditional baselines on harder datasets.
- Missing proof/citation for Theorem 3 and incomplete derivation in Remark 1.
- Generative evaluations rely on qualitative visuals and nearest-neighbor checks; limited quantitative assessment.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and generally solid paper. With the clarified derivations and stronger experimental comparisons, it would make a valuable contribution.
