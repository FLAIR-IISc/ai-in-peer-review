This paper proposes a loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables, extending generative moment matching networks to use a conditional loss function based on the maximum mean discrepancy criterion. The authors leverage work on conditional kernel mean embeddings to produce the loss function, demonstrating results on various generative tasks, including classification, generating handwritten digits and faces, and distilling knowledge in Bayesian models.

The main idea of the paper is to learn a conditional distribution given some input variables based on a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The CMMD is defined as the difference between the kernel mean embeddings of the empirical conditional distribution and the generative model. The authors provide a theoretical justification for the CMMD criterion, showing that it is a sufficient condition for two conditional distributions to be the same.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method. The experimental results are decent, demonstrating competitive performance on various tasks. The authors also provide a comparison with other methods, including variational autoencoders and max-margin deep generative models.

However, there are some minor issues that need to be addressed. In Remark 1, the authors mention that the CMMD objective can be expressed as a weighted version of the MMD objective. However, the notation used is unclear, and it would be helpful to provide more details on how to map from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form. Additionally, the choice of notation for the conditional embedding operator is unfortunate, as it is already used to define the conditional embedding.

Theorems 1 and 2 refer to other papers, but Theorem 3 has no reference. A reference should be provided to a paper containing a proof, or a proof should be given.

Overall, the paper proposes an interesting extension to generative moment matching networks, and the experimental results demonstrate its potential. However, some minor issues need to be addressed to improve the clarity and completeness of the paper.
