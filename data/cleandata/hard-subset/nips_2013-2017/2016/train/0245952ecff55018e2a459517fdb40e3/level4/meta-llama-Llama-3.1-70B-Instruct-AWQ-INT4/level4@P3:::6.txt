This paper presents conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMN), an extension of generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction applications. The key to the proposed method is the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) metric for measuring the discrepancy of conditional distributions. The proposed CGMMN was tested on classification, conditional generation, and distilling Bayesian models.

The paper is well-motivated, and the proposed approach addresses the limitations of the naive approach to extending GMMNs to conditional settings. The naive approach would estimate a GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters through the use of the same neural network. However, this approach is prone to data sparsity problems, particularly when the conditioning variables have a continuous domain. In contrast, the proposed approach treats all conditional distributions as a family and tries to match the model with the conditional embedding operator directly, rather than matching each individual conditional distribution.

The advantage of the proposed approach is clear, and the CMMD objective has a nice formulation that can be easily computed almost in the same way as the unconditional maximum mean discrepancy (MMD). The fact that all the K matrices in equation 2 are independent of the model parameters is also a significant advantage, as it allows for backpropagation to be done in the same way as before.

However, one concern about the proposed approach is that the CMMD objective potentially requires big minibatches, as the stochasticity of small minibatches may dominate the error. On the other hand, big minibatches make the computation of the objective expensive, especially the matrix inverses, which could be prohibitively expensive to compute.

The experiment results on classification and distillation of Bayesian models are interesting, and the proposed approach seems to perform competitively with state-of-the-art methods. However, it would be interesting to compare the proposed approach to the naive approach as a baseline, particularly in cases where the conditioning variable takes one of a finite number of values, such as in MNIST.

The presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 could be improved, as it was not easy to follow without diving into the referenced papers. There are also a couple of minor problems: (1) on line 77, it is mentioned that the MMD estimator is unbiased, which is not true; and (2) on line 90, the conditional embedding operator was not consistent with the one given in reference [29].

Overall, I think the CMMD provides a nice way to model conditional distributions, and the proposed approach has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field. However, the presentation and experimental evaluation could be improved to make the paper more compelling.

Strengths:

* The proposed approach addresses the limitations of the naive approach to extending GMMNs to conditional settings.
* The CMMD objective has a nice formulation that can be easily computed.
* The experiment results on classification and distillation of Bayesian models are interesting and competitive with state-of-the-art methods.

Weaknesses:

* The presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 could be improved.
* The proposed approach potentially requires big minibatches, which can be computationally expensive.
* The comparison to the naive approach as a baseline is missing.

Originality:

* The proposed approach is a novel extension of GMMNs to conditional generation and prediction applications.
* The CMMD objective is a new contribution to the field.

Significance:

* The proposed approach has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative models.
* The experiment results on classification and distillation of Bayesian models demonstrate the competitiveness of the proposed approach.

Quality:

* The paper is well-motivated, and the proposed approach is technically sound.
* The experiment results are interesting and competitive with state-of-the-art methods.

Clarity:

* The presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 could be improved.
* The paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations and visualizations to help readers understand the proposed approach.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper, but require the authors to address the minor problems and improve the presentation and experimental evaluation.
