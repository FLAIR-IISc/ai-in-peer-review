This paper presents a novel loss function for training directed generative models that incorporate conditional variables. The proposed method is an extension of the generative moment matching network (GMMN) framework, which originally employed an unconditional loss function rooted in the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion. This work successfully generalizes that approach by leveraging conditional kernel mean embeddings to formulate the loss. The model's efficacy is demonstrated across several tasks, including classification, the generation of handwritten digits and faces, and knowledge distillation for Bayesian models.

Having reviewed a previous version of this manuscript, I can confirm that the authors have directly addressed the minor concerns raised by reviewers, which pertained to the use of a low-dimensional latent space, the model's scalability, its utility relative to other methods, and various requests for clarification.

However, a few points still require attention. In the discussion of Remark 1, the derivation mapping Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form is not immediately obvious. The authors should provide more detail to make this connection explicit. Specifically, the dot notation used is ambiguous; it should be clarified whether this represents a matrix multiplication or a Hadamard product. Furthermore, the notational choice of 'C' is suboptimal, as this symbol is already used in the definition of the conditional embedding, potentially leading to confusion. While Theorems 1 and 2 are properly supported by references, Theorem 3 currently lacks either a citation to a paper containing its proof or a proof itself. The authors must provide one of these.

The empirical evaluation is solid. The authors' justification for including supervised learning experiments—to highlight the model's versatility—is a reasonable response to previous critiques. The application to distilled Bayesian inference is intriguing, but its evaluation on only a single dataset with a single model makes it difficult to ascertain its generalizability. The primary contribution of this work likely lies in the domain of pure generative modeling. In this area, the experimental results on digit and face datasets are comparable to those of the original GMMN paper and are sufficient to serve as a proof-of-concept. For the method to achieve wider adoption, however, it will be necessary to demonstrate compelling results on more challenging benchmarks, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN.
