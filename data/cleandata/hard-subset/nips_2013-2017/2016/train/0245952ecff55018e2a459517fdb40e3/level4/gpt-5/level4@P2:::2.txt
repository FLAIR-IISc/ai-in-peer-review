Summary
The paper extends Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) to the conditional setting by introducing Conditional GMMN (CGMMN), trained via a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The model generates y by passing both the conditioning variables x and noise h through a deep network, and learns by minimizing the discrepancy between the conditional distributions of generated and real data in an RKHS. The authors apply CGMMN to predictive modeling, conditional generation, and distillation of Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge”).

Relation to prior work
CGMMN builds on GMMN and MMD-based training for deep generative models, and is positioned alongside conditional variants of GANs and VAEs. While feeding x into a generator is straightforward, defining a principled objective that compares conditional distributions is non-trivial; the paper proposes CMMD to address this gap.

Strengths
- The conditional extension is meaningful and, unlike naive conditioning, requires addressing how to compare distributions across varying x. This is a non-trivial and pertinent problem.
- The proposed training remains compatible with standard backpropagation and is conceptually simple once the objective is fixed.
- Applications are broad, and the empirical results suggest the approach is competitive across several tasks.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- The exposition leads with formal RKHS machinery and quickly presents Eq. (2), but offers little intuition for the CMMD objective or how it reduces to practical computations. In contrast, the non-conditional MMD objective in prior GMMN work can be explained without heavy functional analysis, making it accessible and interpretable.
- The paper does not clearly articulate the computational steps required to evaluate Eq. (2) in minibatch training: which Gram matrices are built, which inverses are computed, what is reused across iterations, and how the cost scales with batch size and output dimension.
- Assumptions required for the conditional embedding operator (e.g., existence in F ⊗ G, regularization via K+λI) are stated but not unpacked. It is unclear to the reader when these conditions hold in practice and how violations affect training or require additional heuristics.
- The key practical challenge—matching distributions across many inputs in a minibatch when conditionals differ—is acknowledged implicitly but not explained. Using the same conditioning variables across the compared datasets can be inefficient; using different x’s raises questions about statistical efficiency and variance. The paper should clarify what statistics are shared and why CMMD remains a good surrogate under minibatch sampling.

Requests for clarification (to improve readability and reproducibility)
- Intuition for Eq. (2): How would one arrive at CMMD from first principles, starting from MMD? A high-level narrative that CMMD is a “weighted MMD” where weights reflect similarity in x (as hinted by the remark comparing CMMD and MMD) would make the objective more interpretable.
- From Eq. (2) to code: Please provide a concise, step-by-step description of a minibatch update:
  1) Construct K, L, Ksd, Lds from a minibatch of real pairs (x, y) and generated pairs (x, ŷ);
  2) Add λI and invert K blocks; 
  3) Form the traces in Eq. (2); 
  4) Backpropagate through L terms only (i.e., gradients flow through ŷ and network parameters), while K terms are constants w.r.t. parameters;
  5) State the per-iteration complexity (e.g., O(b^3) due to (K+λI)−1 with batch size b) and memory footprint.
- Efficiency: Do you reuse (K+λI)−1 within an iteration or across iterations when x’s repeat? How do batch size, kernel choice for x and y, and λ affect run time and stability?
- Assumptions: What conditions on kernels and data ensure well-behaved estimates of ĈY|X? How sensitive is training to λ and to the conditioning kernel choice?
- Sampling scheme: When X is continuous, how are x’s chosen for the generated set relative to the real minibatch? Do you use the same x’s to reduce variance, or nearby x’s? What are the empirical trade-offs?

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Rework Section 3 to begin with an intuitive derivation: start from MMD, motivate why uniform weighting fails in the conditional case, then show that CMMD corresponds to MMD with non-uniform weights induced by similarity in x. Only afterward present the operator view and Eq. (2).
- Include a compact pseudocode block that spells out the matrices, inverses, and gradient paths, plus a complexity analysis per minibatch.
- Provide an ablation or at least a discussion on kernel choices for x and y, batch size effects, and the impact of reusing identical x’s vs. sampling new x’s.
- Move the essential parts of the supplement (derivation steps from CMMD to Eq. (2), and the minibatch algorithm) into the main paper; the current supplement is hard to follow.

Pros
- Addresses a real gap: principled training for conditional generative models via MMD.
- Simple training loop compatible with backprop; broad applicability.
- Empirical results show competitive performance.

Cons
- Presentation over-relies on formalism; lacks an intuitive bridge from objective to algorithm.
- Practical computation and efficiency are not clearly described.
- Assumptions and their practical implications are under-explained.

Overall, the technical idea is promising and the application scope is appealing. Strengthening the intuitive exposition and detailing the computational pipeline for Eq. (2) would substantially improve clarity and impact.
