Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional distributions p(y|x) using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective. Building on kernel mean embeddings and prior work on MMD-based generative models (GMMN), the authors define CMMD as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between the conditional mean embedding operators of the empirical conditional distribution and the model. The generator maps the concatenation of x and a noise vector h through a deep network to produce y, and training proceeds by back-propagation on the CMMD loss computed from mini-batches.

The paper positions CGMMN relative to: (i) MMD/GMMN for unconditional generation; (ii) conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., Grunewälder et al.) for representing conditional distributions; and (iii) deep conditional generative models such as conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero), Laplacian pyramid GANs, and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al.). Empirically, CGMMN is evaluated on classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional image generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and “Bayesian dark knowledge” by distilling a Bayesian predictor (PBP) into a CGMMN student.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: CMMD gives a direct, stable training criterion without adversarial min-max optimization, while still enabling flexible deep conditional generators.
- Sound grounding in kernel embeddings: the operator-based perspective is natural for conditional distributions; the trace-form CMMD objective is clearly derived and amenable to back-propagation.
- Versatility: applications span discriminative prediction, contextual generation, and Bayesian distillation using the same learning principle.
- Competitive predictive results: CGMMN-CNN is close to strong deep generative baselines (e.g., MMVA) on MNIST/SVHN, without data augmentation or ensembles.
- Practical detail: noting that the inverses in CMMD depend only on x (not on model parameters) is useful, and the minibatch algorithm is straightforward.

Weaknesses
- Scalability and compute: Each CMMD evaluation requires O(b^3) time for Cholesky/inversion of b×b Gram matrices per mini-batch for both inputs and outputs. The paper does not report training time, memory use, or wall-clock comparisons to baselines (e.g., DSN, MMVA, cVAE, cGAN). This omission makes it hard to assess practical efficiency.
- Sensitivity to kernel choices: Results depend on kernels and hyperparameters for both x and y (e.g., bandwidths, regularization λ), but selection procedures and sensitivity analyses are not reported. Since in practice the authors reuse the same x for real and generated pairs in each batch (Algorithm 1), CMMD effectively becomes a non-uniformly weighted MMD over outputs, with weights induced by Kx; the impact of kernel bandwidth on these weights should be discussed.
- Empirical scope for generation: The generative experiments use small datasets and qualitative samples, with limited quantitative evaluation. There is no direct comparison to conditional GANs or conditional VAEs on generation quality; image samples appear somewhat noisy unless combined with an autoencoder.
- Reproducibility: Important training details (kernel types and bandwidths for x and y, λ, noise dimension choices, preprocessing, optimizer settings per task) are scattered or missing, and no code is provided. Practitioners may struggle to replicate the reported numbers.
- Evaluation metrics for probabilistic predictions: For classification and distillation, accuracy/RMSE are reported, but calibration or likelihood-based metrics (e.g., negative log-likelihood, Brier score) would better reflect the quality of the learned conditional distributions.

Quality
- Theoretical development is largely sound and consistent with prior work on kernel conditional mean embeddings. The CMMD estimator and the trace-form expression are clearly stated. Assumptions and the role of regularization are acknowledged, though practical guidance on choosing λ and kernels is limited.
- The minibatch CMMD is a biased, low-variance approximation; discussing this trade-off and any observed training instabilities would strengthen the work.
- Experimental comparisons are reasonable for classification (though not state-of-the-art), but generation lacks quantitative measures and comparisons to cGAN/cVAE.

Clarity
- Generally well written, though notation is dense. It would help to explicitly state kernel choices and hyperparameters per experiment, and to include a brief complexity analysis of CMMD per batch with suggested implementations (Cholesky, caching).
- Clarify that in Algorithm 1 the inputs x are shared between real and generated pairs in each minibatch, and discuss how this special case simplifies CMMD.
- Provide more architectural details (layer types, activation, normalization, dropout) and training hyperparameters to improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The core novelty is combining conditional kernel mean embeddings with deep generators trained via CMMD. While conditional mean embeddings and MMD-based training are known, applying an operator-norm CMMD as a learning objective for deep conditional generators is a meaningful and non-trivial extension of GMMN. Relation and distinctions to cGAN/cVAE are appropriately acknowledged but could be empirically deepened.

Significance
- Moderate. The method offers a stable and conceptually clean alternative to adversarial training for conditional generation and provides a unified approach spanning several tasks. However, empirical gains over strong baselines are limited, and scalability/efficiency remain open.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Report training time and memory for MNIST/SVHN models and the Yale generator, and compare to MMVA/DSN/cVAE/cGAN where possible.
- Detail kernel choices and hyperparameters for x and y (RBF? median heuristic?), the regularization λ, and sensitivity analyses.
- Discuss scaling strategies (e.g., random Fourier features, Nyström approximations, blockwise CMMD, inducing points) to reduce O(b^3) costs.
- Include quantitative generation metrics common at the time (e.g., Parzen window log-likelihood estimates) and/or human evaluation; add comparisons to cGAN/cVAE for conditional generation.
- For Bayesian distillation, add more datasets and report calibration/likelihood metrics; show runtime speedups of the student versus the teacher at test time.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated objective avoiding adversarial min-max.
- Solid theoretical grounding with a neat operator-based loss.
- Versatile framework with competitive predictive performance and a novel application to Bayesian distillation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical scope and missing comparisons on generation quality to contemporary conditional models.
- Unclear computational efficiency and scalability; no training-time results.
- Incomplete reporting of kernel/hyperparameter choices and lack of code hinder reproducibility.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a clean and useful extension of MMD-based training to conditional generative modeling with broad applicability. Strengthening the experimental section with runtime analysis, clearer hyperparameter reporting, and direct comparisons to cGAN/cVAE would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
