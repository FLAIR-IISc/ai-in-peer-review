This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMNs), a principled extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMNs) for modeling conditional distributions p(y|x). The core innovation is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective, which measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions by comparing their kernel mean embedding operators in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This framework allows for the training of a deep generative model that can sample from p(y|x) for a given x. The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMNs on a range of tasks, including classification (predicting a discrete y given an image x), contextual generation (generating an image x given a class label y), and distilling knowledge from complex Bayesian models.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Approach:** The paper's main strength lies in its elegant solution for extending GMMNs to the conditional setting. A naive approach might involve training a separate GMMN for each value of the conditioning variable, which would suffer from severe data sparsity, especially for continuous conditioning variables. Instead, the authors propose matching the *operator* that maps from the input space to the conditional mean embedding of the output. This is a powerful and well-motivated idea that treats the conditional distributions as a unified family, allowing them to share statistical strength.

2.  **Tractable Objective Function:** It is particularly noteworthy that the resulting CMMD objective has a closed-form expression in terms of kernel matrices, as shown in Equation (2). This formulation, as highlighted in Remark 1, bears a strong resemblance to the standard MMD objective but incorporates non-uniform weights determined by the conditional variables. This allows for straightforward optimization via back-propagation, as the kernel matrices involving the conditioning variable x are independent of the generative model's parameters.

3.  **Broad Applicability and Strong Empirical Results:** The paper successfully demonstrates the model's effectiveness across a diverse set of applications. The competitive results on MNIST and SVHN classification show its utility for predictive modeling. The contextual generation experiments on MNIST and Yale Faces showcase its ability to produce varied and realistic samples conditioned on specific attributes. Furthermore, the application to distilling Bayesian models is an interesting and novel use case, demonstrating that CGMMN can efficiently approximate complex predictive distributions.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Exposition:** A primary weakness of the paper is the clarity of the theoretical background in Sections 2.3 and 3.1. The concepts of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions and the derivation of CMMD are presented very densely. For a reader not already deeply familiar with the work of Song et al. [29], these sections are difficult to parse without consulting the original references. The paper would be significantly improved by providing more intuition behind the conditional embedding operator and the Hilbert-Schmidt norm that defines the CMMD objective.

2.  **Computational Concerns and Minibatch Size:** A significant practical concern is the computational trade-off related to minibatch size. The CMMD objective involves inverting kernel matrices (K̃_s and K̃_d), an operation with cubic complexity in the batch size. While small minibatches are computationally feasible, the empirical CMMD estimate may be dominated by stochastic noise, potentially destabilizing training. Conversely, large minibatches that would provide a more stable gradient estimate are prohibitively expensive. The paper would benefit from a discussion of this trade-off and an empirical analysis of how performance varies with minibatch size.

3.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including a comparison to a more straightforward "naive" baseline. For instance, in the MNIST conditional generation task where the conditioning variable is a discrete class label, one could train ten separate GMMNs (one per class), perhaps with shared layers to encourage parameter sharing. While the authors argue against this due to data sparsity, for a problem with 10 classes and thousands of examples per class, this approach might perform reasonably well and would serve as a valuable point of comparison for the proposed CMMD framework.

### Summary of Review Criteria

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound for the most part, but there are some concerns. The computational complexity of the objective function poses practical challenges that are not addressed. There are also a couple of minor technical inaccuracies noted below. The experimental work is solid and covers a good range of tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the theoretical sections (2.3 and 3.1) lack clarity and intuition, making the core technical contribution difficult to fully grasp without external reading. The overall architecture and learning algorithm, however, are clearly explained.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds upon GMMNs and kernel embeddings of conditional distributions, the formulation of the CMMD objective and its application to train a deep conditional generative model is a novel and significant contribution. It clearly differs from prior work on conditional GANs and VAEs by providing a moment-matching perspective.
*   **Significance:** The results are important as they significantly broaden the applicability of moment-matching networks, making them a viable alternative to GANs and VAEs for conditional modeling tasks. The ability to model complex conditional distributions has wide-ranging implications for prediction, structured output, and model compression, making it likely that other researchers will build on these ideas.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Proposes a novel and principled CMMD criterion for learning conditional generative models.
*   Extends the applicability of MMD-based deep generative models significantly.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on a diverse set of important tasks.

### Arguments for Rejection:
*   Clarity of the core theoretical sections is a major issue, hindering reproducibility and understanding.
*   Practical concerns about the computational cost versus statistical stability of the CMMD estimator are not addressed.
*   Lacks a comparison to a simple, direct baseline for conditional modeling.

### Minor Comments and Corrections

1.  **Line 77:** The paper states that the MMD estimator (L̂²_MMD) is unbiased. The V-statistic formulation presented is in fact a biased estimator. An unbiased estimator exists but requires a different formulation that avoids diagonal terms in the kernel matrix expansions.
2.  **Line 90:** The definition of the conditional embedding operator Ĉ_Y|X = Φ(K+λI)⁻¹Υᵀ seems to be based on a definition of the cross-covariance operator that differs from the one in the cited paper [29]. A brief clarification on the specific definition of the cross-covariance operator being used would be helpful.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution. The primary concerns are the clarity of the theoretical sections and the lack of discussion on computational trade-offs. If the authors can address these points, the paper would be an excellent contribution to the conference.
