Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to the conditional setting by introducing Conditional GMMN (CGMMN). The key idea is to train a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w), which takes the context x and noise h, by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) between the empirical conditional distribution P(Y|X) and the model’s conditional distribution. The loss measures the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between two conditional mean embedding operators estimated from samples, leading to a tractable objective (Eq. (2)) expressed through kernel Gram matrices on X and Y.

The work builds on kernel mean embeddings and conditional mean operators (Song et al., 2009/2013), and on GMMN for unconditional generation (Li et al., ICML 2015). It complements conditional deep generative models such as conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Denton et al., NIPS 2015), conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., NIPS 2015), and discriminatively trained DGMs (Li et al., NIPS 2016). Applications include classification (predictive modeling), class-conditional image generation, and distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (Korattikara et al., NIPS 2015).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension: replacing MMD between marginal embeddings with CMMD between conditional embedding operators is principled and aligns well with prior kernel embedding theory.
- Practical training: the objective is single-sided (no adversary), differentiable with respect to the generator, and compatible with standard backprop/SGD.
- Versatility: the same framework supports predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Empirical results: competitive classification on MNIST and SVHN; plausible class-conditional samples on MNIST and Yale; promising Bayesian distillation on Boston Housing without degrading RMSE.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Intuition for CMMD and Eq. (2): The paper jumps quickly into formal operator algebra. A reader-friendly, high-level explanation of CMMD as a “context-weighted MMD” would greatly help. For example, show how Eq. (2) reduces to MMD when CXX is “uniform,” and interpret the three trace terms as within-/cross-dataset similarities reweighted by kernel similarities in X.
- From Eq. (2) to an algorithm: Provide a clear step-by-step description of what is computed per minibatch, including the construction of K and L on inputs/outputs for data and generated samples; the role of λ; and how gradients flow only through L (and possibly cross-terms) but not through K.
- Computational complexity and scalability: Inverting K̃ = K + λI per minibatch is O(b^3). Discuss typical minibatch sizes used, wall-clock costs, and remedies (e.g., caching K̃−1 for repeated x, low-rank Nyström, random Fourier features for Y kernels, conjugate gradient with preconditioning).
- Assumptions and estimator behavior: The operator existence assumptions (universality, EY|X[g|X] ∈ F, etc.) are stated but not discussed practically. Clarify when these fail and why the regularized empirical estimator remains sensible. Discuss bias/variance of minibatch CMMD and its dependence on λ and batch size.
- Kernel choices and hyperparameters: The paper should clearly specify kernels for X (e.g., RBF on pixels, CNN features, or one-hot labels) and for Y (e.g., linear vs RBF on logits or pixel intensities), bandwidth selection (median heuristic?), and sensitivity analyses. These details are essential to reproduce results and understand performance.
- Experimental evaluation for generation: Results are mainly qualitative. Comparative baselines (e.g., cGAN, CVAE) and quantitative conditional generation metrics (e.g., class-conditional Parzen density, classifier-based accuracy of generated digits, or inception-like scores) would strengthen the claims.
- Bayesian distillation: The aim is efficient prediction; please report model size, test-time speedup vs the teacher, and calibration metrics (predictive log-likelihood, NLL, ECE) in addition to RMSE. Also compare to the original “Bayesian dark knowledge” student training objective if possible.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: Note where competitors use data augmentation or multi-model voting. For classification, consider reporting NLL/Brier in addition to error rates to reflect probabilistic quality.
- Ablations: Explore the impact of noise dimension, different ways of combining x and h (concat vs additive noise), effect of λ and batch size on stability, and different kernels.

Quality
The method is technically sound and grounded in established kernel embedding theory. The empirical estimator of CMMD is reasonable with regularization, and the optimization via backprop is straightforward. However, the paper needs clearer algorithmic exposition, complexity analysis, and experimental details (kernels/hyperparameters) to ensure reproducibility and to substantiate efficiency claims.

Clarity
The manuscript would benefit from:
- A motivating, non-technical derivation of CMMD and a connection to MMD (e.g., “CMMD = MMD with context-dependent weights”).
- An explicit minibatch algorithm box with shapes, costs, and a short gradient expression indicating which terms depend on w.
- A consolidated section summarizing kernel choices, bandwidth selection, and regularization.
- Improved organization of experimental details (currently scattered between main text and appendix) and minor proofreading (e.g., MNIST spelling, augmentation).

Originality
The core idea—training deep conditional generators by minimizing the Hilbert–Schmidt distance between conditional mean operators—is a natural but nontrivial extension of GMMN. To my knowledge, this specific CMMD-based training for deep conditional models is novel at the time and distinct from cGAN/CVAE approaches.

Significance
A stable, adversary-free objective for conditional generation and conditional modeling is valuable. The approach could be adopted broadly, especially where adversarial training is brittle. Significance would be strengthened by broader comparisons, quantitative generative metrics, and scalability demonstrations.

Pro/Con arguments
Pros:
- Principled extension of MMD to conditional modeling
- Simple training, no min–max
- Demonstrated across prediction, generation, and Bayesian distillation
- Competitive classification results and plausible conditional samples

Cons:
- Lacks intuitive explanation and clear algorithmic pathway from Eq. (2)
- Missing complexity/scalability analysis and kernel/hyperparameter details
- Limited quantitative evaluation for conditional generation
- Incomplete reporting for distillation (speed, calibration)

Questions and requests for the authors
- Provide an intuitive derivation of Eq. (2): how does CMMD arise from comparing µY|x via CY|Xφ(x), and how does it relate to a context-weighted MMD?
- Spell out the exact per-minibatch computations and gradients, including computational costs and memory footprint.
- Specify all kernels and hyperparameters used (for both X and Y), bandwidth selection strategy, and sensitivity.
- Discuss the role of λ, estimator bias/variance, and stability as a function of batch size.
- Add quantitative comparisons for conditional generation and report predictive calibration metrics.
- For Bayesian distillation, report model size and test-time throughput vs teacher, and compare to the original dark knowledge objective.

Minor comments
- Clarify whether, during training, each x in a minibatch is paired with one or multiple generated y; discuss whether multiple y per x helps variance reduction.
- Consider feature-space kernels on X (e.g., CNN features) to reduce K’s ill-conditioning on high-dimensional pixels.
- Proofread for typos and consistency (e.g., MNIST, augmentation).

Recommendation: Weak accept. The idea is sound and potentially useful, but the paper should improve clarity, provide algorithmic details and complexity analysis, and strengthen empirical evaluation to maximize impact. Confidence: Moderate.
