This paper proposes a novel loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables, extending the generative moment matching networks approach. The method leverages work on conditional kernel mean embeddings to produce the loss function, which is based on the maximum mean discrepancy criterion. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on various generative tasks, including classification, generating handwritten digits and faces, and distilling knowledge in Bayesian models.

As this is my second time reviewing this paper, I noticed that the authors have addressed the minor issues raised by reviewers in the previous round. These concerns included the use of a low-dimensional space, the scalability of the method to higher-dimensional latent spaces, the utility of the model compared to others, and several clarifying issues. Upon reviewing the text, it appears that the authors have directly addressed these concerns.

However, I still have some questions regarding the technical details. Specifically, in Remark 1, the mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form is not entirely clear. The dot notation used in this context is ambiguous, and it would be helpful to specify whether it represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product. Providing more details on this mapping would improve the clarity of the presentation.

Furthermore, the choice of notation for the constant C is somewhat unfortunate, as it is already used to define the conditional embedding. To avoid confusion, it would be better to use a distinct notation for this constant.

Theorems 1 and 2 are properly referenced, but Theorem 3 lacks a reference to a paper containing a proof or a proof itself. It is essential to provide a proof or a reference to a paper that contains one to maintain the rigor of the presentation.

The empirical results presented in the paper are decent. Although using this model for supervised learning may not be the most effective application, the authors' argument that it demonstrates the model's versatility is reasonable. The application to distilled Bayesian inference is interesting, but its usefulness in other cases is unclear, as it is only demonstrated on a single dataset with one model. In my opinion, this approach would be most effective in pure generative modeling.

The experiments in this context are comparable to those in the original GMM paper. The datasets used, which include digits and faces, are sufficient to demonstrate the feasibility of the idea. However, to convince others to adopt this method, more compelling results on challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN, would be necessary.
