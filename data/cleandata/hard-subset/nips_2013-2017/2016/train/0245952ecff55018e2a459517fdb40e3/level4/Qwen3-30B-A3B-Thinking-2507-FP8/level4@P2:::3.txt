

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) that enables learning of conditional distributions using Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). The authors build upon kernel mean embedding techniques to develop a principled approach for conditional generative modeling, demonstrating applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation. The work extends previous NIPS contributions on GMMN (Li et al., 2015) by addressing the important problem of conditional distribution learning, which is crucial for many real-world applications including predictive modeling, contextual generation, and building complex generative models.

The paper makes several significant contributions. Theoretically, it provides a rigorous formulation of CMMD (Theorem 3) and establishes its connection to conditional kernel mean embeddings (Theorem 2). The authors demonstrate that CGMMN can learn conditional distributions while maintaining the simplicity of the GMMN training procedure, which relies on back-propagation rather than the more challenging min-max optimization of GANs. The empirical evaluation covers three application domains: predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN datasets, contextual generation of handwritten digits and faces, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation on the Boston housing dataset.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical foundation is sound and well-justified, with clear connections to previous work on kernel embeddings and MMD.
2. The extension to conditional distributions is novel and addresses a clear gap in the literature, as previous generative models (GANs, VAEs, GMMN) primarily handle unconditional distributions.
3. The applications demonstrate the versatility of the framework, particularly the Bayesian dark knowledge distillation which offers a promising approach to accelerate Bayesian inference.
4. The writing is clear and well-organized, with appropriate mathematical formalization of the key concepts.

Weaknesses:
1. The theoretical development would benefit from greater clarity in Remark 1 regarding the connection between MMD and CMMD. The notation in Equation (2) is ambiguous—the "dot" operator is unclear (matrix multiplication vs. Hadamard product), and the reference to "C" is confusing as it conflicts with the notation for the conditional embedding operator.
2. The experimental evaluation is limited to relatively simple datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Faces, Boston Housing). While these demonstrate the basic viability of the approach, the paper would be significantly strengthened by results on more challenging benchmarks such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN, which would better demonstrate the method's scalability and utility for complex generative tasks.
3. The comparison to state-of-the-art conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs, conditional VAEs) is insufficient. The paper claims "competitive performance" but doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with the most relevant contemporary approaches in the conditional generation domain.
4. The authors address the previous reviewers' concerns about the low-dimensional latent space (discussed in the revision), but the explanation of why this dimensionality is sufficient for predictive tasks could be more thorough.

Regarding the four NIPS review criteria:
- Quality: The technical content is sound, with well-supported theoretical claims and appropriate experimental validation. The paper would benefit from more extensive experiments on challenging datasets.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, but the notation in Remark 1 requires clarification to fully understand the relationship between MMD and CMMD.
- Originality: The extension to conditional distributions represents a meaningful contribution that builds on prior work while addressing a significant gap in the literature.
- Significance: The applications are promising, particularly the Bayesian knowledge distillation, but the significance would be stronger with results on more complex tasks and datasets.

The authors have appropriately addressed previous reviewers' concerns about the low-dimensional latent space and the utility of the model, but the paper would benefit from the following specific revisions:
1. Clarify the notation and mathematical relationships in Remark 1, including a more detailed explanation of how Equation (2) relates to the weighted MMD formulation.
2. Provide a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art conditional generative models, particularly conditional GANs and conditional VAEs.
3. Include experiments on more challenging datasets (CIFAR-10, LSUN) to better demonstrate the method's capabilities and limitations.
4. Add a more detailed discussion of how CGMMN compares to existing conditional generative approaches, including their strengths and limitations relative to CGMMN.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling by extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. While the current experimental validation is sufficient to demonstrate the basic viability of the approach, the paper would be significantly stronger with more comprehensive experiments on challenging benchmarks and a more thorough comparison to relevant contemporary methods. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is particularly promising and deserves further exploration. With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field of deep generative modeling.
