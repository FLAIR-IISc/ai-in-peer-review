This paper presents conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMNs), extending GMMNs to conditional generation and prediction. The core idea is to use kernel embeddings of conditional distributions together with a conditional MMD (CMMD) to measure discrepancies between conditional distributions. The method is evaluated on classification, conditional generation, and distillation of Bayesian models.

A natural but naive extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would be to train a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, tying parameters by sharing the same neural network. However, each conditional distribution may have very few examples; with continuous conditioning variables, some conditionals may have only a single example, leading to severe data sparsity. The proposed approach instead treats all conditional distributions as a family and matches the model to the conditional embedding operator directly, rather than fitting each conditional individually. This advantage is compelling, though there are cases where the naive approach may still perform reasonably well—for example, in conditional generation on MNIST where the conditioning variable takes one of 10 values. Including such a naive baseline would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

The CMMD objective is nicely formulated and, as noted in Remark 1, can be computed almost identically to the unconditional MMD. In particular, all the K matrices in Equation (2) are independent of the model parameters, so backpropagation proceeds as in the unconditional case.

A key concern is that the CMMD objective may require large minibatches; otherwise, minibatch stochasticity could dominate the error. Large minibatches, however, make the computation expensive, and the required matrix inverses may become prohibitively costly. This potential computational burden deserves discussion and, if possible, empirical characterization.

Overall, CMMD provides a clean and principled way to model conditional distributions. The experimental results on classification and distillation of Bayesian models are interesting and suggest the approach is broadly useful.
