Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN). The key idea is to train a conditional generative model by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective derived from kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions. The generator conditions on inputs x and auxiliary noise h, and is trained via backpropagation using stochastic gradient descent. The method is evaluated on predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (class-conditional digit and face synthesis), and distilling Bayesian “dark knowledge” by approximating Bayesian predictive distributions with a student network.

The approach builds directly on GMMN (which uses MMD to match joint distributions) and on the literature on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions, replacing adversarial min–max training with a single, smooth objective. Compared to GANs, the objective is simpler; compared to variational methods, it avoids explicit likelihoods. The paper situates itself appropriately relative to GMMN, conditional kernel mean embeddings, and conditional GANs.

Quality:
- Theory: The use of CMMD to compare conditional distributions via the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean operators is well-motivated. However, Theorem 3, which asserts that equality of conditional mean operators implies equality of conditional distributions for all x, is stated without a reference or proof. Theorems 1 and 2 cite prior work; Theorem 3 should similarly cite a source containing a proof, or the paper should include its own proof.
- Clarity of CMMD formulation: Remark 1 claims a connection between MMD and CMMD as “weighted MMD.” I find the mapping from Equation (2) to this weighted form unclear. Specifically:
  - Please clarify whether the “·” in expressions like Tr(Ld · C1) denotes matrix multiplication or Hadamard (elementwise) product.
  - Provide an explicit derivation showing how the CMMD terms reduce to MMD with non-uniform weights when K̃−1 plays the role of weights induced by the conditioning variables. As written, the correspondence is too terse to follow.
  - Notation: using “C” both for the conditional embedding operators (e.g., CY|X) and for the weighting matrices in Remark 1 is confusing. Consider renaming the latter (e.g., W1, W2, W3) to avoid overloading.
- Practical considerations: The algorithmic description is straightforward. It is helpful that K̃−1 on the conditional variables does not depend on model parameters, so x-kernels need not be differentiable. The mini-batch approximation is reasonable given the cubic cost in the batch size.

Empirical evaluation:
- Predictive modeling: Results on MNIST and SVHN are decent. As noted in prior reviews, supervised learning may not be the most compelling application for this model class, but using it here to demonstrate versatility is acceptable. Performance is competitive with max-margin deep generative models on MNIST/SVHN, though below strong discriminative baselines such as DSN.
- Conditional generation: Class-conditional digits and faces are solid and comparable in scope to prior GMMN-style evaluations. The autoencoder+CGMMN variant improves visual quality, which is a useful practical detail. Nearest-neighbor analyses suggest the model is not merely copying training data.
- Bayesian dark knowledge: The distillation experiment on the Boston Housing dataset is interesting and shows no degradation relative to PBP. However, it is limited to a single dataset and teacher model, making it hard to assess broader applicability. More evidence across models/datasets would strengthen this part.

Originality:
The paper contributes a natural and well-motivated extension of GMMN to conditional modeling via CMMD. While the building blocks (MMD, conditional kernel mean embeddings) are known, their synthesis into a practical deep conditional generator trained end-to-end appears novel and useful.

Significance:
The method is simple, general, and compatible with standard deep architectures. For broader impact and adoption, stronger results on more challenging generative benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, LSUN) would be valuable. As it stands, the paper convincingly demonstrates feasibility on digits and faces, and shows promise for Bayesian distillation, but stops short of compelling state-of-the-art results.

Clarity and presentation:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Addressing the specific issues in Remark 1, resolving the notation collision, and providing a citation/proof for Theorem 3 would substantially improve readability and rigor. I reviewed a previous version; the authors have addressed earlier minor concerns (e.g., low-dimensional latent choice, scalability, and utility comparisons).

Pros:
- Simple, principled conditional extension of GMMN using CMMD.
- End-to-end training with standard backprop; no adversarial min–max.
- Broad applicability: classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Competitive results on MNIST/SVHN (vs. generative baselines) and convincing conditional generations.

Cons:
- Theorem 3 lacks a reference or proof.
- Remark 1’s “weighted MMD” connection is under-explained; notation is confusing.
- Empirical scope is limited on Bayesian distillation (one dataset/teacher).
- No results on more challenging generative datasets, which may limit adoption.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution. Clarifying the theoretical points (Theorem 3, Remark 1) and expanding empirical evaluations—especially on harder conditional generation tasks—would strengthen the paper further.
