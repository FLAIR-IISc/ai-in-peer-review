Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative model trained by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) between the conditional kernel mean embeddings of the data and the model. The generator takes both observed inputs x and auxiliary noise h, passes them through a deep network, and outputs samples y ~ p(y|x). Training uses backpropagation on the CMMD objective, which compares empirical conditional embedding operators estimated from minibatches. This extends GMMN (MMD-based training for joint distributions) to the conditional setting, leveraging prior work on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions (e.g., Song et al.). The approach avoids adversarial min–max optimization (as in GANs) and amortized variational bounds (as in VAEs). Empirically, the paper evaluates three use cases: predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST, Extended Yale Face), and “Bayesian dark knowledge” distillation (from PBP on Boston Housing). Results are generally competitive with strong deep baselines, including comparisons with max-margin DGMs and standard CNNs.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual extension of GMMN to the conditional case via CMMD, with a principled RKHS foundation.
- Simple training pipeline that uses standard backpropagation without adversarial training instability.
- Broad empirical scope: classification, conditional image generation, and Bayesian model distillation.
- Competitive performance on MNIST and SVHN classification (close to CMMVA) and reasonable conditional generations; distillation maintains teacher performance on Boston Housing.
- Theoretical grounding: CMMD uses conditional mean embeddings; the objective reduces to trace terms of Gram matrices, making implementation straightforward.

Weaknesses:
- Scalability and computational cost: CMMD requires inverses of Gram matrices; although minibatches are used, wall-clock training time and memory footprint are not reported. A direct comparison with discriminative baselines (e.g., DSN, CMMVA) would help.
- Limited experimental scale: datasets are small to medium; no large-scale conditional generation benchmarks. Quantitative generation metrics are not provided.
- Reproducibility risk: important training details are scattered or missing (kernel choices for x and y, bandwidth selection, regularization λ, minibatch sizes per task, number of generated samples per x, noise dimension h, combination strategy for x and h). Reference code would substantially strengthen the work.
- Sensitivity/ablation: no analysis of kernel choices (RBF vs. others), bandwidth tuning, effect of λ, mini-batch CMMD approximation, or design choices (concatenation vs. corruption of x).
- Theoretical assumptions behind CMMD (e.g., smoothness conditions ensuring Hilbert–Schmidt norm) are acknowledged but not empirically probed for robustness when violated.

Quality:
- The method is technically sound and grounded in established kernel embedding theory. The derivation of CMMD and the empirical estimator are correct, and the training objective is amenable to SGD.
- Experimental comparisons are reasonable for classification, and the distillation case study is a nice addition. However, the absence of runtime and sensitivity analyses makes it hard to assess the practical efficiency of CMMD at scale.

Clarity:
- The high-level narrative is clear, and the operator-form CMMD objective is well motivated. Some mathematical sections are dense; more intuition for the CMMD weights (vs. uniform weights in MMD) would help.
- The experiments section should include concrete training details (kernels, bandwidths, λ, optimization hyperparameters, number of samples per conditional input, early stopping criteria) to improve reproducibility.

Originality:
- Novelty lies in framing and training a conditional generator via CMMD, extending GMMN to conditional settings. This is distinct from conditional GANs and conditional VAEs, offering a third training paradigm. While the building blocks are known, the combination is original and practically useful.

Significance:
- Provides an alternative to adversarial and variational training for conditional generation and predictive modeling, with competitive performance on standard benchmarks. The approach is promising but its broader impact hinges on demonstrating scalability and providing open-source code.

Pro-accept:
- Conceptually clean extension of MMD-based training to conditional models.
- Avoids adversarial instability; simple backprop-based learning.
- Diverse applications, including an interesting Bayesian distillation use case.
- Competitive results with strong baselines on MNIST/SVHN.

Con-accept:
- Missing runtime and resource comparisons; unclear training efficiency vs. state-of-the-art.
- Limited scale of experiments and lack of quantitative evaluation of generated samples.
- Insufficient details for full reproducibility; no code release.
- No ablations to understand sensitivity to kernel and hyperparameter choices.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report training time, memory, and convergence curves per dataset; compare to DSN/CMMVA and standard CNNs with similar parameter counts.
- Provide full hyperparameter details: kernel types for x and y, bandwidth selection procedure (e.g., median heuristic), λ, minibatch sizes, number of conditional samples per x, noise dimension, and conditioning strategy (concatenation vs. noise injection).
- Include ablations on kernel choice/bandwidth, λ, batch size, and noise dimension; analyze CMMD stability.
- Add quantitative generation metrics and nearest-neighbor analyses for all generative experiments, and show sensitivity to the conditioning variable.
- Release reference code and training scripts to ensure reproducibility.

Overall assessment:
An interesting and well-motivated conditional extension of GMMN with solid theoretical grounding and competitive empirical results on standard benchmarks. With added clarity on training details, runtime analysis, and code availability, the paper would be significantly stronger.
