This work introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), extending the GMMN framework to conditional generation and prediction applications. The method's core contribution lies in using the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and a corresponding conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric to measure their discrepancy. The proposed CGMMN is evaluated on tasks including classification, conditional generation, and the distillation of Bayesian models.

A naive approach to conditional GMMNs would be to estimate a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing parameters across a single neural network. The drawback of this method is the data sparsity problem; each conditional distribution is estimated from very few examples. This issue is particularly severe in continuous domains for the conditioning variables, where each condition might correspond to only a single sample. The proposed method circumvents this by treating the conditional distributions as a unified family, directly matching the model with the conditional embedding operator rather than fitting each distribution individually.

While the advantage of the proposed technique is clear, the naive approach may still perform reasonably in specific cases, such as the conditional MNIST generation task where the conditioning variable is discrete (one of 10 values). It would strengthen the paper to include a comparison against such a naive method as a baseline.

On a positive note, the CMMD objective has an elegant formulation. As highlighted in Remark 1, it can be computed with similar ease to the unconditional MMD. Furthermore, the K matrices in Equation 2 are independent of the model's parameters, allowing backpropagation to be implemented in the standard way.

One concern with the proposed approach is that the CMMD objective may require large minibatches to prevent the stochasticity of the batches from dominating the estimation error. This creates a computational trade-off, as large minibatches make the objective function expensive to compute, with matrix inversions potentially becoming prohibitively costly.

Overall, the CMMD offers a well-founded and effective method for modeling conditional distributions. The experimental results on classification and the distillation of Bayesian models are also compelling. However, the presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 was difficult to follow, requiring me to consult the referenced papers to fully understand the methodology.
