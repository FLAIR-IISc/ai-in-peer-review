This paper introduces conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMNs), extending GMMNs to conditional generation and prediction. The central idea is to use kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and the conditional MMD (CMMD) as a discrepancy measure. The method is evaluated on classification, conditional generation, and distillation of Bayesian models.

A straightforward extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would train a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, with parameters shared via a single neural network. However, this approach suffers from severe data sparsity: each conditional distribution has very few examples, and with continuous conditioning variables, many conditionals may have only a single example. The proposed method addresses this by matching the model to the conditional embedding operator for the entire family of conditionals, rather than fitting each conditional distribution independently. This advantage is clear in principle. That said, in settings with a small discrete set of conditioning values (e.g., MNIST digits taking one of ten labels), the naive approach could still perform reasonably well. Including such a naive baseline would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

It is appealing that the CMMD objective has a clean formulation and can be computed almost identically to the unconditional MMD, as noted in Remark 1. In particular, the K matrices in Equation (2) do not depend on the model parameters, allowing standard backpropagation.

One concern is that CMMD may require large minibatches; otherwise, minibatch stochasticity could dominate the error signal. Large batches, however, make the objective expensive to compute, and the required matrix inverses could be prohibitively costly in practice.

Overall, CMMD provides a compelling framework for modeling conditional distributions, and the results on classification and Bayesian distillation are interesting. However, the presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is difficult to follow; I had to consult the cited papers to understand key steps. Improving clarity and self-containment in these sections would significantly enhance readability.
