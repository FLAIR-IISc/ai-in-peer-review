

The authors propose Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to model conditional distributions. The core innovation is the development of Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the distance between kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions. CGMMN generates samples by passing both conditional variables and random noise through a deep network, then optimizes the CMMD objective via backpropagation. The approach is evaluated across predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge tasks.

This work builds upon Song et al.'s kernel embedding of conditional distributions (2009) and Li et al.'s GMMN (2015), extending them to conditional settings. The authors properly contextualize their contribution within the literature, acknowledging relevant prior work on conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs).

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation is solid, with proper justification for using CMMD to match conditional distributions. The connection to existing kernel embedding techniques is well-established, and the paper demonstrates how to compute gradients efficiently for optimization.
2. **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide a clear generative process that enables efficient sampling for CMMD estimation. The implementation details (including mini-batch training in Algorithm 1) are well-described and practical.
3. **Diverse Applications:** The evaluation across multiple domains (predictive modeling, contextual generation, Bayesian knowledge distillation) demonstrates the framework's versatility and practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Technical Gap in Theoretical Formulation:** The definition of the conditional embedding operator (Equation 3) appears incomplete. The term related to μ_X and μ_Y (specifically, the cross-covariance operator C_XY = E[φ(X)⊗φ(Y)] - μ_X⊗μ_Y) is missing the subtraction of μ_X⊗μ_Y, which is critical for proper centering. This omission affects the theoretical foundation of CMMD. The draft review correctly identifies this issue, which should be addressed in the final version. Without this correction, the gradient computation and empirical implementation may lack theoretical grounding.
2. **Empirical Results Comparison:** The predictive modeling results (Tables 1-2) show CGMMN-CNN achieving higher error rates than state-of-the-art methods like CMMVA and DSN, particularly on SVHN (3.13% vs. 1.92% error). The paper does not adequately explain why CGMMN underperforms these methods, nor does it provide sufficient comparison with other conditional generative approaches (e.g., conditional GANs) in the generation task. This weakens the significance claim.
3. **Clarity Issues:** The manuscript contains several grammatical errors and typos that reduce readability (e.g., "Frobeniu norm" on Line 48, "an universal" on Line 65, "which the" on Line 238). While minor, these detract from the paper's professionalism.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
- Correct the theoretical formulation of the conditional embedding operator to include the full cross-covariance expression. This is essential for the CMMD objective to be properly defined.
- Expand the experimental section to include comparisons with conditional generative models like conditional GANs on the same datasets. This would better demonstrate the relative advantages of CGMMN.
- Provide a more detailed discussion of why CGMMN underperforms state-of-the-art methods in predictive tasks, and whether architectural improvements (e.g., deeper networks) could close this gap.
- Proofread the manuscript to eliminate grammatical errors and typos.

**Evaluation Against NIPS Criteria:**
- **Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with proper theoretical justification, though the missing term in the conditional embedding operator weakens this aspect. The empirical evaluation covers multiple relevant tasks.
- **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured, but contains grammatical errors and typos that reduce clarity. The theoretical formulation requires clarification.
- **Originality:** The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions is a meaningful contribution, though the novelty is somewhat incremental given the existing literature on conditional generative models.
- **Significance:** The framework's versatility across multiple applications is promising, but the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over state-of-the-art methods, limiting the significance claim.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a solid technical contribution that extends an important line of work in generative modeling. While the theoretical foundation requires correction and the empirical results need stronger comparison with relevant baselines, the core idea is sound and potentially valuable for practitioners working with conditional generative models. With minor revisions addressing the theoretical gap and enhancing the experimental comparison, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.

The authors have addressed my concerns in the rebuttal, particularly regarding the theoretical formulation of the conditional embedding operator. I therefore recommend acceptance.
