

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to conditional distributions using Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as the training objective. The authors build upon the work of Li et al. (ICML 2015) who introduced GMMN for learning joint distributions, and extend it to handle conditional scenarios where the model needs to generate samples based on input variables. This addresses a gap in the literature, as previous work on conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs [Mirza & Osindero, 2014] and conditional VAEs [Sohn et al., 2015]) often rely on more complex optimization procedures. CGMMN maintains the simplicity of GMMN's training process while enabling conditional generation through a straightforward generative architecture.

The paper demonstrates CGMMN's effectiveness across three application domains: predictive modeling (on MNIST and SVHN datasets), contextual generation (on MNIST and Yale Face datasets), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (on Boston housing dataset). The experimental results show competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods in each domain, particularly highlighting the method's ability to generate high-quality samples conditioned on class labels.

Strengths: The theoretical foundation of CMMD is well-motivated and properly grounded in Hilbert space embeddings. The extension to conditional distributions is theoretically sound and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. The experimental evaluation covers a diverse set of tasks, demonstrating the versatility of the approach. The paper is well-organized and generally clear, with sufficient mathematical detail for the intended audience.

Weaknesses: The computational complexity of the CMMD objective is not thoroughly discussed, particularly regarding training time compared to other methods like DSN [Lee et al., 2015]. The paper would benefit from more detailed computational comparisons and analysis of training efficiency. The experimental section lacks sufficient information about the exact architecture used for reproduction (e.g., specific layer sizes, activation functions, and hyperparameters for the MNIST and SVHN experiments). Additionally, the paper could better articulate how CGMMN differs from recent conditional generative approaches, particularly conditional VAEs and conditional GANs, to clarify its unique contribution.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical derivations are correct, and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance. However, the paper would be stronger with more detailed computational analysis and better reproducibility information.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and organized, though some mathematical sections could be made more accessible. The description of the conditional embedding operator (Section 2.3) and the CMMD objective (Section 3.1) would benefit from additional intuitive explanations.

Originality: The conditional extension of GMMN is novel and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. While related to conditional GANs and conditional VAEs, CGMMN provides a distinct approach based on moment matching rather than adversarial training or variational inference.

Significance: The approach has potential applications in various domains requiring conditional generation, including predictive modeling and knowledge distillation. However, the evaluation on relatively small benchmark datasets limits the significance of the claims, and the paper would benefit from larger-scale experiments to better demonstrate the method's practical utility.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of generative modeling. With additional computational details, better reproducibility information, and a clearer articulation of the method's unique advantages over recent work, the paper would be significantly strengthened. The authors should consider including training time comparisons and making reference code available to improve the paper's impact and reproducibility.
