

The paper proposes a novel extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMNs) to conditional distribution matching, termed Conditional GMMNs (CGMMN). The core idea leverages reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) embeddings for conditional distributions, building on Song et al. (2009). While the theoretical extension is conceptually clean and well-motivated, several critical issues require clarification before the work can be accepted.

The most significant concern lies in the definition of the conditional covariance operator $\hat{C}_{Y|X}$ (Line 90). The expression omits terms involving the RKHS mean embeddings $\mu_X$ and $\mu_Y$ (or their empirical estimates), which are essential for unbiased estimation in RKHS. This omission is problematic because the estimator's validity hinges on properly accounting for data centering in the feature space. The authors assume $\mu_X = \mu_Y = 0$, implying data is already centered in RKHS—a non-standard assumption that lacks justification. Without these terms, the estimator $\hat{C}_{Y|X}$ is mathematically incomplete, raising questions about its convergence properties and practical implementation. Crucially, the paper does not explain how this omission is handled during gradient computation or in real experiments. The authors must provide a corrected definition, justify the zero-mean assumption (or explain its necessity), and clarify its impact on optimization. This is especially critical as $\hat{C}_{Y|X}$ is central to CGMMN.

The empirical evaluation presents further challenges. For predictive tasks, CGMMN achieves higher error rates than the state-of-the-art CMMVA on both datasets, undermining the claim of competitiveness. The paper fails to compare CGMMN against conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs or conditional VAEs) for sample generation. Without such comparisons, it remains unclear whether CGMMN generates higher-quality samples than existing approaches. The absence of generative benchmarks (e.g., FID, human evaluation) or qualitative sample analysis weakens the contribution. While the authors acknowledge the need for such comparisons in the rebuttal, the current manuscript lacks them.

Minor but distracting issues include: "Frobeniu norm" (Line 48 → "Frobenius norm"), "an universal" (Line 65 → "a universal"), and "which the" (Line 238 → likely "which" or "that"). These typographical errors reduce the paper’s professionalism.

Despite these concerns, the theoretical framework is sound and the conditional extension is novel. However, the unresolved mathematical gap in $\hat{C}_{Y|X}$ and the underwhelming empirical results necessitate revision. The authors must address the missing terms in the estimator, clarify the zero-mean assumption, and provide comprehensive comparisons against relevant baselines (both predictive and generative). 

**Update after author rebuttal:** The authors have satisfactorily addressed all concerns. They provided a corrected definition of $\hat{C}_{Y|X}$, justified the zero-mean assumption via RKHS centering, and added comparisons with conditional GANs in the revised manuscript. The generative results now demonstrate clear advantages over baselines. I have updated my scores accordingly.
