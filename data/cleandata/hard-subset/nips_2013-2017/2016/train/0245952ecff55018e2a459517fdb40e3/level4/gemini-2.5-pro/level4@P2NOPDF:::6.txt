This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), an extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) designed for conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core contribution is the use of the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the corresponding conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) metric to measure the discrepancy between two conditional distributions. The proposed model is evaluated on several tasks, including classification, conditional generation, and the distillation of Bayesian models.

The authors correctly identify a key limitation of a naive approach to conditional GMMNs. Such an approach might involve training a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, with shared parameters across a common neural network. This strategy suffers from data sparsity, as each conditional distribution may be supported by very few samples. In the case of continuous conditioning variables, this problem is exacerbated, with potentially only a single example per condition. The proposed method elegantly sidesteps this issue by treating the collection of conditional distributions as a unified family. Instead of matching each conditional distribution individually, the model is trained to match the conditional embedding operator directly.

The theoretical advantage of this approach is clear. However, it would be beneficial to empirically validate this against the simpler, naive baseline, particularly in settings where the naive approach might be reasonably effective. For instance, in the conditional generation task on MNIST, where the conditioning variable is a discrete class label (one of ten values), the data sparsity problem is less severe. A direct comparison in such a scenario would provide a stronger empirical justification for the proposed method's superiority.

A significant strength of the paper is the elegant formulation of the CMMD objective. As pointed out in Remark 1, the objective can be computed almost as easily as the standard unconditional MMD. The fact that the kernel matrices (`K_X`, `K_Y`, etc.) in Equation 2 are independent of the model parameters simplifies optimization, allowing for straightforward backpropagation as in a standard GMMN. The experiments on classification and the distillation of Bayesian models are also compelling and showcase the versatility of the proposed framework.

Despite these strengths, I have a few concerns. First, the clarity of the presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 could be improved. I found these sections difficult to follow without consulting the cited papers to fully understand the technical details. Enhancing the self-containedness of these sections would greatly improve the paper's readability.

A second concern relates to the computational demands of the CMMD objective. The stochastic estimation of the objective may require large minibatches to mitigate variance and ensure a stable training signal. However, large minibatches significantly increase the computational cost, especially for operations like matrix inversion, which could become prohibitively expensive. It would be valuable for the authors to comment on this trade-off and discuss the practical scalability of their approach.

Finally, I have a couple of minor points regarding technical accuracy:

1.  On line 77, the paper states that the MMD estimator is unbiased. This is incorrect for the commonly used V-statistic estimator; only the U-statistic estimator is unbiased, but it is more computationally complex. This point should be clarified.
2.  On line 90, the definition provided for the conditional embedding operator appears to be inconsistent with the one in reference [29]. The form used in this paper seems to rely on a different definition of the cross-covariance operator. The authors should verify this and correct the definition or citation as needed.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and principled method for modeling conditional distributions. The CMMD objective is a valuable contribution to the generative modeling toolkit. While there are areas for improvement concerning experimental comparison, presentation clarity, and computational considerations, the core ideas are sound and interesting.
