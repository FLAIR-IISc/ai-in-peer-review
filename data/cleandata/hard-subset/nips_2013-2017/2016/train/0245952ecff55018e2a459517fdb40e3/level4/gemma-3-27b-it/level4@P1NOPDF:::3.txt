This paper proposes a loss function for directed generative models with conditional variables. The method extends generative moment matching networks by building upon an unconditional loss function based on the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion. The authors leverage recent work on conditional kernel mean embeddings to derive this loss function, and demonstrate its effectiveness on a variety of generative tasks, including classification, handwritten digit and face generation, and knowledge distillation in Bayesian models. This is my second time reviewing this paper. Previous reviewers raised minor concerns regarding the choice of a low-dimensional latent space and its scalability to higher dimensions, the utility of the model compared to existing approaches, and some clarifying points. These appear to have been addressed directly in the revised manuscript.

However, Remark 1 remains unclear. Specifically, the mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form is not readily apparent. The dot notation used in this section is ambiguous; is it intended to represent matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product? I would appreciate a more detailed explanation to clarify this mapping. Additionally, the choice of the symbol ‘C’ is somewhat unfortunate, as it is already used to define the conditional embedding. Theorems 1 and 2 cite supporting papers, but Theorem 3 lacks a reference. Either a citation to a paper containing a proof should be provided, or the proof itself should be included.

The empirical results are solid. While previous reviews questioned the suitability of applying this model to supervised learning, the authors rightly point out that this application serves to demonstrate the model’s versatility, which I find acceptable. The application to distilled Bayesian inference is interesting, though its evaluation is limited to a single dataset and model, making it difficult to assess its broader applicability. I believe this approach would likely find the most traction within the field of pure generative modeling. In this context, the experiments yield results comparable to those reported in the original GMM paper. The use of digits and faces as datasets is sufficient to demonstrate the feasibility of the proposed method.

To encourage wider adoption, however, I believe the authors would benefit from presenting more compelling results on more challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN.
