This paper extends moment matching networks (MMNs) to the conditional setting. Unlike models that can be updated on single examples—where simply feeding the conditioning variable into the network makes the extension trivial—this case is genuinely non-trivial. In the conditional setting, a mini-batch contains many inputs with different conditionals, so we must match the distribution of a large set of generated samples to a large set of targets whose conditionals differ. Unless the same conditional is used within each update (which is inefficient), the target and model distributions in a batch no longer coincide, complicating the learning signal.

My main concern is the presentation. The paper leads with heavy formalism rather than building intuition, and it does not make clear what is actually computed, how efficient those computations are, or what assumptions are required. In particular, Equation (2)—the objective ultimately optimized—is introduced without explaining:
- the intuition behind it,
- how it reduces to concrete operations in a mini-batch,
- what estimators are used and under what assumptions they are valid,
- and the computational and memory costs per iteration.

By contrast, the non-conditional MMN can be explained (as in the original paper) without resorting to advanced machinery (e.g., RKHS); the minimized objective there is intuitive. The conditional case deserves an equally accessible treatment. Right now, the reader is asked to accept Equation (2) and its derivation without a clear picture of the actual algorithmic steps or their practicality. While some material appears in the supplement, it is difficult to follow and should be summarized more clearly in the main text.

Concrete questions the paper should address:
- How would one arrive at Equation (2) from first principles, intuitively? What mismatch is being minimized, and why is this the appropriate conditional analogue of the unconditional moment-matching objective?
- What are the exact steps from Equation (2) to the implemented algorithm? For example: how are conditionals organized within a batch; how are sample pairs formed; which kernels or features are used; how are gradients computed; and what is the per-iteration time and memory complexity?
- What assumptions are required to make the estimator practical (e.g., unbiasedness of mini-batch estimates, independence across conditionals, specific kernel properties)?
- Why is it insufficient here to “just feed the conditional” as one would in per-example updates, and how does your estimator resolve the mismatch between model and target distributions when conditionals vary within a batch?

Overall, the technical idea—extending MMNs to the conditional setting—is interesting and non-trivial. However, the exposition needs to foreground intuition and concrete computation. A small, worked example (e.g., a batch with two distinct conditionals) showing exactly which empirical quantities are computed and how gradients are obtained would go a long way. Clarifying efficiency and assumptions in the main paper, rather than relying on dense supplemental derivations, would make the contribution much more accessible and convincing.
