

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to learn conditional distributions using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The authors build upon GMMN (Li et al., ICML 2015), which uses MMD to learn joint distributions, by developing a conditional version that can handle predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The paper presents theoretical foundations for CMMD, a conditional kernel embedding approach, and a simple generative architecture that can be trained via backpropagation. The authors evaluate CGMMN on MNIST, SVHN, and Yale Face datasets, demonstrating competitive performance in classification, image generation, and knowledge distillation tasks.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing literature on deep generative models at NIPS. Previous work in this area includes GANs (Goodfellow et al., NIPS 2014), which use min-max optimization but face training instability, and GMMN (Li et al., ICML 2015), which simplifies the objective function but focuses on joint distributions. CGMMN addresses a significant gap by providing a conditional learning framework that maintains the simplicity of GMMN while extending its applicability to a wider range of tasks. The work also relates to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), conditional variational autoencoders (Soň et al., NIPS 2015), and sum-product networks (Poon & Domingos, UAI 2011), showing how CGMMN complements rather than duplicates these approaches.

Strengths:
1. Originality: The paper successfully extends GMMN to conditional distributions using CMMD, which is a novel technical contribution. The application to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Korattikara et al., NIPS 2015) is particularly interesting and demonstrates practical utility.

2. Significance: The approach addresses important real-world problems including predictive modeling (where conditional distributions are more direct than joint distributions), contextual generation, and knowledge distillation from Bayesian models. The experimental results show competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods on multiple datasets.

3. Quality: The theoretical foundation for CMMD is sound, with clear connections to kernel embedding techniques and Hilbert space methods. The experimental design is comprehensive, covering both discriminative (classification) and generative (image synthesis) tasks.

Weaknesses:
1. Clarity: While the mathematical presentation is generally sound, some sections (particularly the derivation of CMMD in Section 3.1) would benefit from more intuitive explanations and visual aids. The connection between the theoretical framework and practical implementation is not always clear.

2. Quality: The paper lacks important implementation details that would facilitate reproduction. Specifically, it does not provide training times compared to competing methods (e.g., DSN, CMMVA), nor does it mention the computational resources required for training. The absence of reference code is a significant drawback, as noted by the authors' own admission that "a practitioner may have difficulty reproducing the exact models evaluated."

3. Significance: While the results are competitive, the paper does not clearly articulate how CGMMN advances the state of the art beyond existing conditional generative models. The comparison with CMMVA (a state-of-the-art DGM for prediction) in Table 2 shows marginal gains, but the paper does not sufficiently explain why CGMMN's approach is superior in the contexts where it outperforms competitors.

4. Quality: The experimental section could be strengthened by including more detailed ablation studies. For example, it would be valuable to understand how the dimensionality of the hidden space affects performance in both predictive and generative tasks.

The paper's greatest strength is its elegant theoretical extension of GMMN to conditional distributions, which maintains the simplicity of the original framework while broadening its applicability. The Bayesian dark knowledge application demonstrates a particularly promising direction for practical use. However, the lack of training time comparisons and reference code significantly limits the paper's impact and reproducibility.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper presents a novel and technically sound extension to an important area of generative modeling. While the experimental section could be strengthened with additional implementation details and training time comparisons, the theoretical contribution and competitive results on standard benchmarks make this a valuable addition to the field. With the inclusion of reference code and more detailed computational comparisons, this work would be even stronger. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the development of conditional generative models that should be of interest to the NIPS community.
