This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework for modeling conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The core contribution is the development of a new training objective, the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the distance between the true conditional distribution and the one learned by the model. This is achieved by comparing their respective kernel mean embedding operators in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The proposed generative model takes a conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to a deep neural network, which then outputs a sample `y`. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach across a diverse set of applications, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, conditional image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models. This work is situated within a growing body of research on deep generative models, extending the moment-matching paradigm in a similar vein to how conditional variants of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) have been developed.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles the important and logical next step for moment-matching networks: extending them to the conditional setting. This significantly broadens the applicability of this class of models. The extension is non-trivial; unlike simpler models where conditioning can be achieved by merely feeding `x` as an input, matching distributions for an entire mini-batch where each sample is conditioned on a different `x` requires a more sophisticated objective function. The proposed CMMD objective is a novel and principled approach to solving this problem.

2.  **Theoretical Grounding:** The CMMD loss function is well-grounded in the existing literature on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. By building on this theoretical foundation, the paper provides a solid mathematical basis for the proposed training criterion.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on a wide range of tasks, from standard classification benchmarks to more complex applications like contextual image generation and Bayesian model distillation. This breadth of application highlights the flexibility and practical utility of the CGMMN framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Intuition:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its exposition. The presentation jumps directly into the formal mathematics of RKHS and conditional embedding operators without first building the reader's intuition. The final objective in Equation (2) is presented without a clear, high-level explanation of why it is a sensible measure for comparing conditional distributions. The original GMMN paper did an excellent job of intuitively motivating the MMD objective before formalizing it; this paper would be substantially improved by adopting a similar pedagogical approach. For example, Remark 1 hints that CMMD can be seen as applying non-uniform weights to the Gram matrices, but this crucial insight is not developed into a clear intuitive explanation.

2.  **Lack of Implementation Details and Computational Analysis:** The paper is sparse on the practical details required to implement the method. The steps from the theoretical definition of CMMD to the final usable formula in Equation (2) are not clearly laid out. More importantly, the computational cost of the proposed objective is not discussed. Equation (2) involves inverting kernel matrices, which is an O(N³) operation, where N is the mini-batch size. This can be prohibitively expensive. While the paper proposes a mini-batch algorithm, it does not explain how this computational challenge is addressed in practice or what the trade-offs are. Crucial details seem to be deferred to the appendix, which weakens the self-containedness of the main manuscript.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

The work is promising, but its impact could be significantly increased by improving its clarity. I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the manuscript to address the following points:

*   **Improving Intuition:** Before introducing the formalism of conditional embedding operators, could you provide an intuitive explanation for the CMMD objective? For instance, could Equation (2) be motivated as a weighted version of the standard MMD, where the weights (`K̃⁻¹`) account for the relationships between the conditioning variables `x`? A simple illustrative example would be highly beneficial.

*   **Clarifying the Derivation:** Please provide a clearer, step-by-step derivation that bridges the gap between the abstract definition of the CMMD norm (`||C_Y|X - C_Z|X||²`) and the final, practical form in Equation (2) that uses Gram matrices. This would help the reader understand what assumptions and approximations are being made.

*   **Computational Complexity:** Please add a discussion about the computational complexity of the CMMD objective, particularly with respect to the mini-batch size due to the matrix inversions. How does this compare to related methods like GANs? What are the practical limits on the mini-batch size, and how was this handled in the experiments?

*   **Role of `λ`:** The regularization parameter `λ` in `K̃ = K + λI` is critical for the stability of the matrix inversion. Could you elaborate on its role and provide guidance on how it was set in your experiments?

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and well-motivated contribution to the field of deep generative models. The primary concerns relate to the clarity of the presentation rather than the technical quality of the work itself. If the authors can substantially improve the intuitive explanation and provide more detail on the practical implementation and computational aspects, I believe this will be a strong paper.
