This paper introduces conditional generative moment matching networks (CGMMNs), an extension of generative moment matching networks (GMMNs) to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core idea is to leverage kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) metric to measure discrepancies between them. The authors evaluate CGMMNs on classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation.

A naive extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting would involve estimating a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, with all distributions sharing parameters through a common neural network. However, this approach suffers from data sparsity, especially when conditioning on continuous variables, where each conditional distribution might be represented by only a single example. The proposed CGMMN addresses this by treating the set of conditional distributions as a family and directly matching the model to the conditional embedding operator, rather than attempting to match each individual distribution. This advantage is clear, though in some cases, the naive approach can still perform reasonably well – for example, in conditional generation tasks where the conditioning variable takes a limited number of discrete values, such as the 10 digits in MNIST. A comparison to this naive baseline would be a valuable addition.

The paper nicely demonstrates a convenient formulation for the conditional MMD objective, which, as noted in Remark 1, can be computed almost as efficiently as the unconditional MMD. Importantly, the K matrices in equation 2 are independent of the model parameters, allowing for standard backpropagation. 

One potential concern is the computational cost of the conditional MMD objective. Accurate estimation may require large minibatches to mitigate the effects of stochasticity; however, increasing the minibatch size significantly raises the computational burden, particularly the cost of matrix inversions. Overall, CGMMNs offer a compelling approach to modeling conditional distributions, and the experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are interesting. 

The presentation in sections 2.3 and 3.1 was somewhat difficult to follow. Understanding the underlying mechanisms required consulting the cited papers. There are also a couple of minor issues: (1) on line 77, the claim that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect. (2) on line 90, the conditional embedding operator does not align with the definition provided in reference [29]. Reaching the form used in this paper appears to necessitate a different definition of the cross-covariance operator.
