This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) to learn conditional distributions, extending the capabilities of existing Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN). While GMMN focuses on joint distributions, CGMMN addresses scenarios requiring conditional modeling, such as predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The core idea is to leverage a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for learning, enabling training via stochastic gradient descent and back-propagation. The authors evaluate CGMMN across these tasks, demonstrating competitive performance.

This work builds upon the recent success of moment matching techniques for generative modeling, notably GMMN [19, 3], which offer a simpler alternative to Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [5]. The paper correctly identifies the need for conditional generative models, referencing previous work utilizing conditional GANs [21] and variational autoencoders [28] for similar purposes. The proposed CMMD criterion provides a theoretically sound approach to extending moment matching to the conditional setting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Conditional generative modeling is a crucial area with broad applications.
*   **Solid theoretical foundation:** The use of kernel mean embeddings and MMD provides a well-established theoretical framework.
*   **Competitive results:** The experimental results demonstrate that CGMMN achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods on diverse tasks.
*   **Clear connection to prior work:** The paper effectively situates CGMMN within the landscape of generative modeling techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Intuition:** The paper prioritizes mathematical formalism over intuitive explanation. The derivation of the CMMD objective (Eq. 2) feels abstract, and the authors don't adequately explain the computational steps involved or the underlying rationale. While the non-conditional MMD can be understood intuitively, the conditional extension lacks a similar accessible explanation.
*   **Computational Details:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the computational complexity and efficiency of the proposed method. Specifically, the computational cost of evaluating and optimizing Eq. (2) is not fully clarified. Critical details, such as implementation choices and the practical challenges of kernel matrix calculations, are relegated to the supplementary material, hindering understanding.
*   **Assumptions:** The paper doesn’t clearly state the assumptions necessary for the CMMD objective to be well-defined and effective, leaving the reader uncertain about the limitations of the approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded extension of GMMN to the conditional setting.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of CGMMN.
*   The work addresses a significant problem in generative modeling with a potentially valuable approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of intuitive explanation and computational clarity hinders understanding and potentially limits the reproducibility of the results.
*   The absence of a clear discussion of the method's limitations and assumptions raises concerns about its generality and robustness.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of generative modeling. The proposed CGMMN offers a promising approach to conditional generation, built upon a strong theoretical foundation. However, the paper would significantly benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the CMMD criterion, a detailed discussion of computational aspects, and a clear articulation of the underlying assumptions. While the technical soundness and experimental results are commendable, the lack of clarity prevents a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **weak accept** pending the authors address the concerns regarding clarity and computational details. Specifically, a section devoted to answering the question "How would you intuitively come up with formula (2)?" and outlining the steps from this formula to the actual implemented algorithm would substantially improve the paper.
