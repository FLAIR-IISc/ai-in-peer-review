

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMNs), extending GMMNs to conditional generation and prediction tasks. The core innovation lies in leveraging kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional MMD (CMMD) metric to measure discrepancies between conditional distributions. The authors evaluate CGMMNs on classification, conditional generation, and distilling Bayesian neural networks.  

The motivation for CGMMNs stems from a key limitation of naive extensions of GMMNs to conditional settings: training a separate GMMN for each condition (with shared neural network parameters) suffers from severe data sparsity. This is especially acute when conditioning variables are continuous (e.g., a single example per conditional distribution). The proposed approach instead treats all conditional distributions as a family, matching the model directly to the conditional embedding operator—bypassing per-condition estimation. While the approach is compelling, the authors should compare against the naive baseline in discrete conditioning scenarios (e.g., MNIST with 10 discrete conditions), where the naive method may remain competitive. Such a comparison would better contextualize the method’s advantages.  

The CMMD formulation (Remark 1) is a significant strength. The objective mirrors unconditional MMD computation, with all kernel matrices in Equation 2 independent of model parameters, enabling straightforward backpropagation. This simplifies implementation and aligns well with existing GMMN frameworks.  

However, a practical concern arises: CMMD likely requires large minibatches to mitigate sampling variance, as small minibatches introduce stochasticity that dominates estimation error. This creates a trade-off—large minibatches increase computational cost (e.g., expensive matrix inversions for kernel matrices), potentially limiting scalability. The authors should discuss this trade-off or propose mitigation strategies (e.g., variance reduction techniques).  

The experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are promising. Yet, the presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 lacks clarity. Key concepts (e.g., the conditional embedding operator) are not sufficiently self-contained, forcing readers to consult referenced work [29] for context. A more intuitive explanation or diagram would improve accessibility.  

Two technical issues require correction:  
1. **Line 77**: The claim that "the MMD estimator is unbiased" is inaccurate. MMD estimators are consistent (converge to the true value as sample size grows) but not unbiased for finite samples. The text should be revised to reflect this.  
2. **Line 90**: The conditional embedding operator definition conflicts with [29]. To align with the paper’s usage, the cross-covariance operator requires a different formulation (e.g., incorporating a conditional kernel). The authors should explicitly reconcile this discrepancy.  

Overall, CGMMNs offer a principled and computationally feasible approach to conditional generative modeling, addressing data sparsity in a theoretically sound manner. The CMMD formulation is elegant and well-integrated into the GMMN framework. While the naive baseline comparison and computational trade-offs warrant further discussion, the core contribution is valuable. Addressing the minor technical errors and clarifying the exposition would significantly strengthen the paper.  

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions (addressing technical errors, clarifying presentation, and adding naive baseline comparison in experiments).
