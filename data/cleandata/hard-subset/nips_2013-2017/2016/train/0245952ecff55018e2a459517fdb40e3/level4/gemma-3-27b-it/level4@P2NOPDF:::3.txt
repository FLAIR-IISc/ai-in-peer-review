This paper introduces a novel loss function for directed generative models conditioned on specific variables. The method builds upon generative moment matching networks, extending their unconditional maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) loss to a conditional setting. The authors achieve this by leveraging recent advances in conditional kernel mean embeddings. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach across diverse generative tasks, including classification, handwritten digit and face generation, and Bayesian model knowledge distillation.

This is my second review of this work. Previous feedback centered on three main points: the motivation for employing a low-dimensional latent space and its scalability to higher dimensions, the practical advantages of this model compared to existing alternatives, and several requests for clarification. The authors have addressed these concerns comprehensively in the revised manuscript. 

However, a minor point of ambiguity remains. In Remark 1, the connection between Equation (2) and the weighted MMD formulation is not entirely clear. Specifically, the notation involving the dot product requires further explanation. It is unclear whether this represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product; greater detail would improve clarity. Additionally, the choice of ‘C’ to represent both the conditional embedding and another variable feels potentially confusing and could be revisited.

Regarding the theoretical contributions, Theorems 1 and 2 cite relevant prior work. However, Theorem 3 currently lacks a citation. A reference to a paper containing a proof of Theorem 3 is essential, or the proof itself should be included in the manuscript.

The empirical results are encouraging. While earlier reviews questioned the suitability of using this model for supervised learning, the authors rightly position this application as a demonstration of the model’s versatility, which I find acceptable. The application to distilled Bayesian inference is particularly interesting, although its evaluation is currently limited to a single dataset and model. Consequently, the generalizability of this benefit remains uncertain. I believe this approach is most likely to gain traction within the field of pure generative modeling. 

In this context, the presented experiments yield results comparable to those reported in the original generative moment matching network (GMM) paper. The datasets used – digits and faces – are sufficient to establish the viability of the proposed method. To encourage broader adoption, however, the authors should consider demonstrating performance on more challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN, to showcase the approach’s capabilities in more complex scenarios.
