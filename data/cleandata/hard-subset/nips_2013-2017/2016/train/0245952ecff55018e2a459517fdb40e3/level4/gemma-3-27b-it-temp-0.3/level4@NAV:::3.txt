This paper introduces a novel loss function tailored for directed generative models operating with conditional variables. The method builds upon generative moment matching networks, extending their unconditional loss – originally based on maximum mean discrepancy – through the application of conditional kernel mean embeddings. The efficacy of this approach is demonstrated across diverse generative tasks, including classification, handwritten digit and face generation, and Bayesian model knowledge distillation. 

This is my second review of this manuscript. Previous concerns regarding the dimensionality of the latent space, scalability, comparative utility, and minor clarifications appear to have been adequately addressed by the authors. However, a point of ambiguity remains in Remark 1: the connection between Equation (2) and the weighted MMD formulation is not immediately clear. Specifically, the meaning of the dot notation used – whether it represents matrix multiplication or the Hadamard product – requires further clarification and elaboration. 

Additionally, the notation choice of ‘C’ is potentially confusing, given its prior use in defining the conditional embedding. Theorems 1 and 2 appropriately cite relevant prior work, but Theorem 3 lacks a corresponding reference; either a citation to a paper containing a proof or the proof itself should be included.

The empirical results presented are encouraging. While prior reviews questioned the suitability of supervised learning as an application, the authors’ justification – to showcase the model’s versatility – is reasonable. The application to distilled Bayesian inference is intriguing, though its evaluation is currently limited to a single dataset and model, hindering broad conclusions about its generalizability. I believe this approach holds the greatest potential within the realm of pure generative modeling, where the experimental performance aligns with that of the original GMM paper. The use of digit and face datasets provides sufficient evidence of concept viability. 

To encourage wider adoption, demonstrating compelling performance on more challenging datasets such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN would be beneficial.
