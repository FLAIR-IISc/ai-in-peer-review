**Summary**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. It extends the recently proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which learn a joint distribution by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model samples and data samples. The core contribution of this work is the formulation of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. This objective measures the distance between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between their respective conditional mean embedding operators, leveraging prior work by Song et al. (2009) on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. The generative model is a deep neural network that takes the conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to produce a sample `y`. The model is trained end-to-end using backpropagation on the CMMD loss. The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN on a wide range of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (generating images conditioned on class labels), and distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model.

The work is well-situated in the context of deep generative models. It provides an alternative to other conditional DGMs like Conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and Conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., NIPS 2015). While cGANs rely on a challenging min-max adversarial objective and CVAEs optimize a variational lower bound, CGMMN offers a simpler, single objective function based on moment matching in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS).

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The extension of MMD-based generative models to the conditional setting is a novel, important, and natural step forward. It significantly broadens the applicability of the moment-matching framework beyond joint density estimation to a host of crucial machine learning problems that rely on conditional modeling. The proposed CMMD objective is a principled criterion for this purpose, with a solid theoretical grounding in the RKHS literature on conditional embeddings.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for conditional modeling is clearly laid out. The theoretical background on kernel embeddings and MMD is concisely reviewed, leading naturally to the definition of CMMD. The authors provide a helpful intuition in Remark 1, contrasting the uniform weighting in MMD with the data-dependent weighting in CMMD. The model architecture and training procedure are described clearly.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed CMMD criterion is technically sound, building directly upon established theoretical results for conditional mean operators. The formulation of the empirical CMMD estimator (Eq. 2) and its integration into a deep learning framework trained with stochastic gradient descent is a solid piece of engineering.

4.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** A major strength of the paper is the demonstration of CGMMN's effectiveness across a diverse set of applications.
    *   The predictive modeling results on MNIST and SVHN are competitive with other state-of-the-art deep generative models from that period, such as max-margin DGMs [18].
    *   The contextual generation experiments on MNIST and Yale Faces successfully show that the model can generate plausible and diverse samples conditioned on class labels.
    *   The "Bayesian dark knowledge" application is an interesting and novel use case, showcasing the model's potential for model compression and distillation.
    This breadth of experiments strongly supports the claim that CGMMN is a flexible and powerful framework.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The CMMD objective as formulated in Eq. (2) requires the computation and inversion of kernel Gram matrices `(K + λI)⁻¹` for each minibatch. This operation has a computational cost of `O(B³)` where B is the batch size. This could be a significant bottleneck for training, potentially limiting the practical batch sizes that can be used. The authors should discuss this limitation. It would strengthen the paper to also discuss or experiment with potential solutions, such as linear-time MMD approximations (e.g., using random Fourier features) and whether they could be adapted to the conditional setting to create a more scalable CMMD estimator.

2.  **Lack of Comparison with Key Competitors:** The most direct competitors for this work are Conditional GANs (cGAN) and Conditional VAEs (CVAE). While the introduction motivates CGMMN as a simpler alternative to GANs, the paper lacks any direct experimental comparison. A qualitative and/or quantitative comparison on a shared task (e.g., conditional image generation on MNIST) would be invaluable. For instance, how does the visual quality of generated samples compare? Is the training of CGMMN demonstrably more stable than that of a cGAN? Such a comparison would more firmly establish the practical advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.

3.  **Visual Quality of Generated Samples:** The generated images on MNIST (Fig. 2) appear somewhat noisy and lack the sharpness often seen in results from contemporary GANs. The authors astutely address this by showing that using an autoencoder to work in a latent "code" space yields much cleaner results (Fig. 4). While this is a good practical workaround, it suggests that the core CGMMN model may struggle to directly model the distribution of sharp natural images. This is a common trait of MMD-based models and is worth noting as a potential limitation.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The empirical CMMD estimator involves a regularization parameter `λ` for the matrix inversion. The choice of `λ`, as well as the kernel function and its bandwidth, can have a significant impact on performance. The paper provides limited details on how these hyperparameters were selected and does not discuss their sensitivity. A brief analysis or discussion of this would improve the paper's reproducibility and practical utility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Proposes a novel and principled extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting, a significant contribution.
*   The CMMD objective is theoretically well-founded and provides a stable, non-adversarial training target.
*   The paper is clearly written and presents its ideas coherently.
*   Demonstrates strong performance across a diverse and compelling set of experiments, showcasing the framework's versatility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Potential scalability issues due to the cubic complexity of the CMMD objective are not addressed.
*   The experimental section lacks a direct comparison to the most relevant competing methods (cGAN, CVAE).

**Recommendation:** Accept.

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practically useful method for conditional density modeling. The weaknesses, while important to note, primarily point towards avenues for future work (e.g., scalability, direct comparison with GANs) rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed approach. The work significantly advances the applicability of MMD-based deep generative models and represents a solid contribution to the NIPS community.
