Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which learn flexible conditional distributions p(y|x) by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) between the empirical conditional distribution and the model. CMMD is defined as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X in tensor RKHSs, estimated from samples using regularized kernel ridge regression. The generator concatenates the conditional input x with noise h and maps it through a DNN to output y; parameters are learned by backprop through the CMMD objective computed from Gram matrices. The paper evaluates CGMMN on (i) predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), (ii) contextual generation (class-conditional MNIST and Yale Faces, with/without an autoencoder), and (iii) Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (replicating PBP regressors on Boston).

The work builds on GMMN (Li et al., ICML’15) which matched joint distributions via MMD, and on kernel conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., ICML’09; Grunewälder et al., ICML’12). It parallels conditional deep generators such as conditional GAN (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAE (Sohn et al., NIPS’15), but differs by using a single, non-adversarial, kernel-based matching loss. Distillation experiments relate to “Bayesian Dark Knowledge” (Korattikara et al., NIPS’15) and PBP (Hernández-Lobato & Adams, ICML’15).

Quality
- Theory: The CMMD criterion is well motivated: if CY|X equals for two distributions in a universal RKHS and regularity holds, then PY|X agrees pointwise (Thm. 3). The empirical estimator is standard via Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ with regularization. The trace-form objective (Eq. 2) is correct and implementable with minibatches. Assumptions (existence of operators; smoothness) are acknowledged, as is the practical use of regularization when assumptions fail.
- Algorithms: The minibatch training procedure is straightforward. A nice observation is that kernels on x need not be differentiable, since the inverses are independent of model parameters.
- Experiments: Predictive results on MNIST and SVHN are competitive with deep generative baselines (CMMVA), though not surpassing strong discriminative models (DSN, Maxout, NiN). Conditional generation results are visually plausible, and the autoencoder variant improves sample smoothness. The distillation case on Boston shows no loss relative to the Bayesian teacher.

That said, several aspects could be strengthened: (1) limited quantitative evaluation of conditional generation (no likelihood surrogate, FID, or diversity metrics); (2) limited ablation on kernel choices, λ, batch size, and noise dimension; (3) scalability and runtime analysis of the O(b^3) per-batch kernel inversions; (4) lack of head-to-head comparison with cGAN/CVAE for contextual generation on the same setups.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The review of kernel embeddings, MMD, and conditional embeddings is concise and correct. CMMD and its connection to MMD are explained well (Remark 1). The generative architecture and training loop are described clearly. However, some important details are missing or relegated to the appendix:
- Exact kernel choices (on x and y), bandwidth selection, and sensitivity.
- Computational complexity and wall-clock runtimes, batch sizes vs. stability, and memory footprint of Gram matrices.
- For classification, how y is handled as output: kernel on logits vs. probabilities; whether calibration is encouraged; any temperature scaling.

Originality
Using the conditional mean embedding operator discrepancy as a training objective for deep conditional generators is, to my knowledge, novel. While conditional embeddings are known and have been used for regression and dynamical systems, and CMMD-like quantities appear in testing/independence literature, this paper is the first to couple it with deep generators as a practical alternative to adversarial or variational training for conditional models. The architectural side (x concatenated with noise) is standard, but the loss and its mini-batched implementation are the main contributions.

Significance
The idea is broadly applicable wherever conditional generative modeling is needed. A stable, non-adversarial objective is appealing. The method achieves competitive accuracy with prior deep generative methods for prediction and yields reasonable conditional samples. The distillation experiment suggests utility as a student model for Bayesian predictors. However, the empirical evidence falls short of demonstrating clear state-of-the-art results or advantages over cGAN/CVAE in conditional generation, and questions remain about scalability to larger datasets and high-resolution outputs.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated objective connecting kernel conditional embeddings to deep generators.
- Avoids GAN min–max instability; trained by standard backprop.
- Flexibility in kernels on x; does not require differentiable kernels for conditioning variables.
- Competitive predictive performance vs. strong DGM baselines; decent qualitative generation; successful Bayesian distillation without degradation.
- Clear derivations and relation to MMD.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation of conditional generation and no direct comparison to cGAN/CVAE on the same tasks.
- Computational scalability: per-batch matrix inversions (O(b^3)) and quadratic memory may limit large-batch/large-data settings; no runtime analysis.
- Sparse ablations: kernel types/bandwidths, λ, noise dimension, number of samples per x, and effect on diversity/calibration are not studied.
- For classification, the choice of kernel on outputs and its impact on accuracy and calibration is under-specified.
- Theory provides sufficiency (CY|X match => PY|X match) but no generalization bounds or finite-sample analysis of CMMD minimization.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative metrics for conditional generation (e.g., class-conditional Inception Score/FID on MNIST; diversity under fixed x).
- Compare to cGAN and CVAE on the same conditional generation tasks and architectures.
- Provide scalability analysis and/or approximations: random Fourier features or Nyström to avoid O(b^3) inverses; preconditioning strategies.
- Ablate kernel choices and hyperparameters; report sensitivity and practical selection procedures (median heuristic, cross-validation).
- For predictive modeling, report calibration (ECE) and uncertainty quality; for regression, heteroscedastic cases would showcase conditional distribution modeling.
- Document runtime, memory, and code/hyperparameters to aid reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and elegant conditional objective bridging kernel embeddings and deep generative modeling.
- Simpler and potentially more stable alternative to adversarial training.
- Broad applicability; solid, competitive results across diverse tasks.
- Clear exposition; contributes a useful tool to the community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation does not yet demonstrate clear superiority over existing conditional generators; missing key baselines and metrics.
- Scalability concerns and limited analysis of computational trade-offs.
- Insufficient ablations and details on kernels/hyperparameters.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The core idea is interesting and technically sound, and the paper would stimulate further work on non-adversarial conditional generative modeling, though the empirical section could be strengthened.
