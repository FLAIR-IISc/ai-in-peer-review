

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to learn conditional distributions. The authors propose a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for training, which generalizes the MMD objective to conditional distributions by measuring the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between kernel mean embeddings of the empirical conditional distribution and the model's conditional distribution. CGMMN generates samples by taking both conditional variables and random noise as inputs to a deep network, with training performed via backpropagation. The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, demonstrating competitive performance across all tasks.

The paper builds on Li et al.'s GMMN (ICML 2015) and relates to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), conditional VAEs (So et al., 2015), and kernel methods for conditional distribution embedding (Song et al., 2009). The authors clearly articulate how CGMMN differs from these approaches by providing a simpler training procedure (using backpropagation) compared to the min-max optimization in GANs.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical extension of MMD to conditional distributions is well-motivated and rigorously derived. The paper provides clear definitions of CMMD and proves necessary theorems supporting the approach.
2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple application domains with appropriate baselines and thorough ablation studies (e.g., MLP vs. CNN architectures).
3. Results demonstrate competitive performance across tasks: CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error rate on SVHN (comparable to state-of-the-art CMMVA at 3.09%), and the Bayesian dark knowledge application shows minimal performance degradation (RMSE 2.580±0.093 vs. PBP's 2.574±0.089).
4. The paper provides sufficient implementation details, including the mini-batch algorithm (Algorithm 1) for efficient training.
5. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is particularly novel and demonstrates a practical use case for CGMMN.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison with conditional GANs is limited, which would have strengthened the evaluation as they are the most direct competitors for conditional generation tasks.
2. The theoretical justification for CMMD could be more thorough, particularly regarding convergence properties and conditions under which CMMD is a valid objective.
3. The connection to Song et al.'s work on Hilbert space embeddings of conditional distributions (ICML 2009) is not sufficiently emphasized, though the paper acknowledges related work.
4. The computational complexity analysis of CMMD compared to other methods is missing, which would help readers understand the trade-offs.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical derivations are clear, and the experiments are thorough and well-designed. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., "we are expected to get better results with more sophisticated structures").

Clarity: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the figures effectively illustrate the model and results. The writing is clear enough that an expert reader could reproduce the results.

Originality: The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions via CMMD is novel. While conditional GANs and conditional VAEs exist, this work provides a distinct approach using moment matching with a simpler training procedure. The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior art.

Significance: The approach addresses important applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and knowledge distillation. The competitive results across multiple tasks demonstrate that CGMMN advances the state of the art in these applications. The Bayesian dark knowledge application shows a particularly valuable use case for distilling Bayesian models efficiently.

Pro and Con Acceptance:
Pros: Novel extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with CMMD criterion; comprehensive evaluation across multiple tasks; competitive results; clear theoretical foundation.
Cons: Limited comparison with conditional GANs; theoretical justification could be more thorough; computational complexity analysis missing.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to generative modeling by extending moment-matching networks to handle conditional distributions. The approach is theoretically sound, well-evaluated, and demonstrates practical utility across multiple applications. While there are minor weaknesses in the comparison to conditional GANs and theoretical justification, these do not undermine the main contribution. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility and advances the state of the art in conditional generative modeling.
