Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper extends GMMN (Li et al., ICML’15) to the conditional setting by introducing Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN). The key idea is to learn a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w) that takes context x and noise h and is trained by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) between the empirical conditional distribution P(Y|X) (estimated from data via kernel conditional mean embeddings) and the model’s conditional distribution. The CMMD objective measures the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between two conditional mean operators and can be computed entirely via Gram matrices on x and y with a regularization λ, allowing optimization by backpropagation. The paper evaluates CGMMN for (i) predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), (ii) conditional generation (label-conditioned MNIST and Yale faces), and (iii) Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (approximating the predictive distribution of a Bayesian neural network).

The work sits at the intersection of kernel mean embeddings for conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML’09; Fukumizu et al.) and deep generative modeling. It is a natural counterpart to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., NIPS’15), offering a stable, likelihood-free training criterion that avoids min–max optimization. The use of autoencoder codes to improve generation mirrors GMMN+AE.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean extension of MMD-trained generators to the conditional setting using conditional mean embeddings, with a clear operator-based CMMD objective.
- End-to-end training by SGD with simple sampling; avoids adversarial instability and variational bounds.
- Empirical versatility across prediction, conditional generation, and model distillation; competitive classification on MNIST/SVHN and plausible conditional samples.
- Practical observations (e.g., K-inverse terms do not depend on w, permitting non-differentiable kernels on x) and mini-batch algorithm.

Weaknesses:
- Theoretical foundations are partial: the sufficiency result (Theorem 3) hinges on strong assumptions, but there is no consistency/generalization analysis for empirical CMMD minimization or for the minibatch, regularized operator estimates used in practice.
- Clarity issues in the theory: in Theorem 3, universality/characteristic properties appear to be required on the kernel over Y (to make mean embeddings injective), yet the text states assumptions on F (the RKHS over X). This mismatch should be corrected/clarified.
- Computational scalability: computing K̃−1 per batch is O(B^3) and CMMD requires several Gram matrices; no analysis of time/memory or comparisons to adversarial/variational alternatives in wall-clock or sample efficiency.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope for the novel aspects. For classification, CGMMN is only on par with prior DGM baselines and trails discriminative CNNs; ablations on kernel choices, λ, bandwidth selection, batch size, and noise dimension are missing. For generation, comparisons to cGAN/cVAE are absent; quantitative metrics are not reported. The distillation experiment is on a single small dataset with RMSE only (no NLL/calibration).
- The autoencoder-aided generation reproduces known GMMN+AE benefits; the conditional novelty is incremental.

Quality:
- Methodologically sound at a high level. The CMMD expression is standard given conditional mean embedding estimators; the training objective is differentiable and implementable.
- Some technical details are underspecified: kernel choices for x and y (especially for high-dimensional y like images), bandwidth selection (median heuristic?), regularization λ selection, stability to ill-conditioned K, and how x is sampled/perturbed when forming the synthetic dataset for CMMD.
- The minibatch CMMD with per-batch inverses yields a biased, high-variance surrogate of the population objective. No discussion of variance reduction, preconditioning, or approximations (e.g., Nyström/RFF) to scale beyond moderate batch sizes.

Clarity:
- Generally well organized; preliminaries provide enough background to follow the method.
- Notational overload between F (X) and G (Y) creates confusion in Theorem 3; please fix the universality/characteristic assumption attribution.
- Algorithm 1 is clear, but important training choices (kernels, λ, bandwidths) should be specified in the main text.
- Figures demonstrate plausible samples; tables are readable, but experimental details (data augmentation, early stopping, hyperparameter search) are sparse.

Originality:
- The core novelty is the CMMD objective for conditional generators, derived from conditional mean embeddings. This is a natural but nontrivial extension of GMMN.
- Positioning relative to conditional GANs/VAEs is appropriate; however, there is no head-to-head comparison with them, and many implementation choices mirror prior work (e.g., AE codes).

Significance:
- Provides a useful alternative to adversarial/variational conditional generative modeling with a simpler, stable loss.
- Potentially impactful for tasks where a principled conditional two-sample criterion is desirable and for distilling predictive distributions. That said, empirical evidence falls short of establishing superiority or clear practical advantages over cGAN/cVAE at this stage.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
- Theory: Clarify the conditions under which CY|X = CZ|X implies PY|X = PZ|X. Typically one needs a characteristic kernel on Y so that equality of conditional mean embeddings for all x implies equality of conditional distributions. Please correct the RKHS attribution and discuss whether universality on X is required (it enters the existence/estimation of CXX−1, but not identifiability in Y).
- Estimation: Discuss estimator bias/variance with finite λ and minibatches; consider showing that minimizing empirical CMMD converges (in probability) to the population objective under standard conditions. Include a sensitivity plot for λ and kernel bandwidths.
- Scalability: Report wall-clock, memory, and batch sizes; consider Nyström or random Fourier features to avoid O(B^3) inversions and to allow larger batches. Analyze how performance scales with batch size.
- Kernels: Specify kernels for x and y for each experiment. For images, raw-pixel RBF can be poor; your AE-code experiment supports that. Consider reporting both. For discrete labels as x, clarify if a delta kernel is used.
- Comparisons: Add baselines to cGAN and cVAE on conditional generation (e.g., class-conditional MNIST) and report quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen NLL on codes, FID/Inception for larger datasets where appropriate).
- Distillation: Evaluate on multiple regression datasets; report NLL and calibration (e.g., reliability diagrams, ECE), since a key advantage of Bayesian teachers is uncertainty. Compare to the original “Bayesian Dark Knowledge” student.
- Prediction: Discuss the benefit of stochastic outputs versus a standard discriminative loss; try training with cross-entropy and compare to CMMD on identical architectures.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled conditional extension of MMD-based deep generators.
- Simple, stable training objective; broad applicability across tasks.
- Competitive classification and reasonable conditional generation; novel application to Bayesian distillation.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond correctness of the objective; unclear assumptions in Theorem 3.
- Scalability and computational aspects not rigorously addressed.
- Empirical evaluation lacks ablations, quantitative generation metrics, and comparisons to cGAN/cVAE; distillation evidence is thin.

Overall: A solid and technically sound contribution that extends GMMN to conditional modeling with an elegant operator-based loss. With stronger theoretical clarity and more thorough empirical comparisons/ablations, the paper would be considerably strengthened. I lean toward weak accept.
