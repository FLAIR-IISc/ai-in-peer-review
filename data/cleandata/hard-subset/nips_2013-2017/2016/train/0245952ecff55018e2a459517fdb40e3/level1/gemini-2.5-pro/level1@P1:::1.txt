### Reviewer Comments

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional probability distributions p(y|x). The work is a direct and logical extension of the recently proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) [Li et al., 2015; Dziugaite et al., 2015], which learn joint distributions p(x) by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model-generated samples and real data samples.

The core technical contribution is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective function. This objective measures the distance between two conditional distributions by calculating the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between their respective conditional mean embedding operators, a concept previously explored in the kernel methods literature [Song et al., 2009]. The generative model itself is a deep neural network that takes the conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to produce a sample `y`. The network's parameters are learned by minimizing the CMMD objective via stochastic gradient descent and back-propagation.

This work fits well within the landscape of deep generative models of its time. It positions itself as an alternative to Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (cGANs) [Mirza & Osindero, 2014] and Conditional Variational Autoencoders (CVAEs) [Sohn et al., 2015]. The main advantage it claims over cGANs is a simpler and more stable training objective that avoids the challenges of min-max optimization, while offering a non-variational, moment-matching alternative to CVAEs. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their model through a diverse set of experiments, including standard predictive and generative tasks, as well as a novel application in distilling knowledge from Bayesian models ("Bayesian dark knowledge").

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and significant extension of the MMD-based generative modeling framework. Moving from unconditional to conditional modeling dramatically increases the applicability of these models to a wide range of important machine learning tasks, such as structured prediction, contextual generation, and discriminative tasks, as rightly pointed out by the authors. The proposed CMMD is a principled and theoretically grounded metric for this purpose.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The CMMD objective is well-founded, building upon established theory of kernel mean embeddings for conditional distributions [Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012]. The paper does a good job of presenting the necessary theoretical background and connecting it to their proposed learning objective. This provides a strong theoretical justification for the approach, which is a desirable quality compared to more heuristic methods.

3.  **Broad and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength. The authors test CGMMN on three distinct and relevant problems:
    *   **Predictive Modeling:** The competitive results on MNIST and SVHN demonstrate that the model can be used for discriminative tasks, performing on par with other state-of-the-art deep generative models like CMMVA.
    *   **Contextual Generation:** The generation of MNIST digits conditioned on class labels and Yale faces conditioned on person identity are convincing proofs of concept.
    *   **Bayesian Dark Knowledge:** This is a particularly clever and novel application. The idea of using CGMMN to learn a fast "student" network that approximates the predictive distribution of a complex Bayesian "teacher" model is compelling and showcases the flexibility of the framework.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for conditional modeling is well-articulated, the CMMD objective is derived step-by-step, and the overall algorithm is easy to understand. The connection between MMD and CMMD highlighted in Remark 1 provides useful intuition.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The CMMD objective in Equation (2) requires computing and inverting kernel Gram matrices (`Kd`, `Ks`) of size `batch_size x batch_size`. This operation has a computational complexity of O(B³) and memory complexity of O(B²), where B is the mini-batch size. This can be a significant bottleneck for large mini-batches, which are often preferred for stable training of deep networks. The paper mentions using a mini-batch algorithm but does not discuss the practical limitations this imposes on the batch size or potential strategies to mitigate this cost (e.g., low-rank approximations like the Nyström method or random Fourier features). A discussion of these scalability aspects would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Qualitative Comparison of Generated Samples:** While the generated samples in Figures 2 and 5 are recognizable, they appear somewhat noisy and less sharp than what was being produced by state-of-the-art GANs around 2016. The paper addresses this by using an autoencoder to learn a code space, which indeed improves sample quality (Figure 4). However, this adds an extra, separate training step. It would be beneficial for the authors to more directly acknowledge this potential limitation in sample quality compared to adversarial methods and perhaps include a qualitative side-by-side comparison with cGANs on one of the generation tasks.

3.  **Details on Hyperparameter Selection:** The choice of kernels and their hyperparameters (e.g., the bandwidth of an RBF kernel) is crucial for MMD-based methods. The paper does not provide much detail on how these were selected for the various experiments. Similarly, the regularization parameter `λ` in the matrix inversion is important for numerical stability but its selection process is not discussed. Providing these details would improve the reproducibility of the work.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

**Pro:**
*   A novel and principled extension of moment-matching networks to the highly relevant conditional setting.
*   The training objective is simpler and potentially more stable than the adversarial objective of cGANs.
*   The work is theoretically well-grounded in the literature on kernel mean embeddings.
*   The experimental validation is extensive and demonstrates the method's effectiveness across a diverse set of important applications, including a novel one in model distillation.

**Con:**
*   Potential scalability issues due to the cubic complexity of kernel matrix inversion in the objective function are not fully addressed.
*   The quality of generated images seems to lag behind contemporary GAN-based approaches without additional components like an autoencoder.
*   Some implementation details regarding kernel selection and hyperparameters are missing, which could hinder reproducibility.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper. It introduces a valuable and timely contribution to the field of deep generative models by providing a theoretically sound and empirically effective framework for conditional density estimation using moment matching. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the CMMD objective for training DGMs and the breadth of the experimental validation, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The work clearly advances the state of the art for MMD-based generative models and provides a compelling alternative to cGANs and CVAEs. I recommend acceptance.
