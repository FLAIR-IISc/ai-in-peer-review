This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional probability distributions p(y|x) using a deep generative model. The work is a direct extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) [19, 3], which learn joint distributions by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model-generated samples and real data samples. The key contribution of this paper is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. CMMD measures the dissimilarity between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective kernel mean embedding operators, a concept built upon prior work on Hilbert space embeddings of conditional distributions [29].

The proposed CGMMN model generates a sample `y` conditioned on an input `x` by passing both `x` and a random noise vector `h` through a deep neural network. The network's parameters are trained by minimizing the empirical CMMD between the true conditional distribution (represented by the training data) and the model's conditional distribution (represented by generated samples). The CMMD objective is differentiable, allowing for end-to-end training with backpropagation. This approach provides a non-adversarial alternative to models like Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (cGANs) [21]. The authors demonstrate the model's versatility across a range of tasks: predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (image synthesis on MNIST and Yale Faces), and model distillation (compressing a Bayesian neural network).

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Theoretical Grounding:** The primary contribution, the CMMD objective, is a novel and principled extension of MMD to the conditional setting. It is well-grounded in the theory of kernel mean embeddings of conditional operators. This work successfully fills a logical gap, providing a moment-matching framework for conditional generation that parallels existing adversarial (cGAN) and variational (cVAE) approaches.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of CMMD and its empirical estimator is technically sound and follows directly from the established theory of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces. The proposed generative architecture is flexible, and the use of stochastic gradient descent for optimization is appropriate. The connection between MMD and CMMD is well-explained, highlighting how CMMD re-weights sample pairs based on the conditional variables.

3.  **Significance and Versatility:** The paper introduces a potentially more stable training framework for conditional generative models compared to the often-unstable min-max optimization of cGANs. The single, non-adversarial objective function is a significant practical advantage. The breadth of experiments is a major strength, showcasing the model's applicability to discriminative tasks (classification), generative tasks (image synthesis), and a novel application in distilling "dark knowledge" from Bayesian models. This demonstrates that the framework is not only a theoretical curiosity but also a versatile tool.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. It effectively builds the reader's understanding by first reviewing MMD and kernel embeddings of conditional distributions before introducing the CMMD criterion. The model architecture and training procedure are described with sufficient clarity.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** A notable weakness of the proposed CMMD objective (Eq. 2) is its computational complexity. The formulation requires the inversion of kernel Gram matrices, which scales as O(B³), where B is the mini-batch size. This cubic scaling can be a significant bottleneck for training with large mini-batches, which are often beneficial for stabilizing training and improving performance in deep learning. The paper does not address this limitation or discuss potential scalable approximations (e.g., using random features or Nyström methods to approximate the kernel matrices), which could be crucial for applying the method to very large-scale problems.

2.  **Details on Kernel Selection:** The performance of MMD-based methods is often sensitive to the choice of kernels and their hyperparameters (e.g., the bandwidth of a Gaussian RBF kernel). The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the kernel choices made for the inputs (`x`) and outputs (`y`) across the different experiments. It is also unclear how hyperparameters were selected or how sensitive the model's performance is to these choices. This omission affects the reproducibility and practical guidance for applying the model.

3.  **Generative Quality and Comparison:** While the generated samples are reasonable, the unconditional samples in Figure 2 appear noisy compared to contemporary results from GAN-based models from the 2016 period. The authors mitigate this by coupling CGMMN with a pre-trained autoencoder, which produces much cleaner samples (Figure 4). However, this requires an additional training stage and complicates the overall pipeline, suggesting that the base CGMMN model may struggle with generating high-fidelity images on its own. A direct quantitative and qualitative comparison against a cGAN on the generative tasks would have strengthened the evaluation.

4.  **Clarity on Estimator Variants:** For the generative task on MNIST, where the conditional variable (class label) is discrete, the authors mention they use a different, more direct estimator for the conditional embedding operator. This important implementation detail is deferred to the appendix. While acceptable, including a brief justification for this choice in the main text would have improved clarity, as it represents a deviation from the general CMMD formula presented earlier.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, theoretically sound, and well-motivated CMMD criterion for training conditional generative models.
*   Provides a non-adversarial alternative to cGANs, with a simpler and potentially more stable training objective.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance and versatility across a diverse set of tasks, including prediction, generation, and model distillation.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The CMMD objective has a cubic computational complexity in the mini-batch size, which raises concerns about scalability that are not addressed.
*   Lack of important implementation details regarding kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning, which impacts reproducibility.
*   The quality of directly generated samples appears lower than that of competing methods, requiring an additional autoencoder pre-training step for improvement.
