Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN by learning conditional distributions p(y|x) using conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). The key idea is to represent conditional distributions via kernel mean embeddings and to measure the discrepancy between the empirical conditional embedding operator of the data and that of a deep generative model. The model samples a noise vector h together with the conditioning input x, passes (x, h) through a neural network y = f(x, h; w), and learns w by minimizing CMMD via back-propagation. The CMMD objective compares conditional embedding operators estimated from Gram matrices on inputs (K) and outputs (L), with regularization (K + λI)−1. Theoretical support is provided via Theorem 3: equality of conditional embedding operators implies equality of conditional distributions under standard assumptions (universality of kernels, etc.). The paper evaluates CGMMN on classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional image generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (from PBP on Boston Housing).

This work builds on MMD and kernel mean embeddings (Gretton et al. 2012; Song et al. 2009; Grunewälder et al. 2012) and extends GMMN (Li et al. 2015; Dziugaite et al. 2015) to the conditional setting, analogous in spirit to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al. 2015), but with a single-objective, non-adversarial criterion grounded in kernel statistics.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The CMMD objective is well-motivated and derived from conditional kernel embedding theory. The empirical estimator ĈY|X = Φ(K + λI)−1Υ⊤ is standard, and the Hilbert–Schmidt norm difference leads to the trace-form objective used in training. The training algorithm is straightforward and implementable via mini-batches; the observation that K−1 terms are independent of model parameters (hence kernels on x need not be differentiable) is correct. The conditions under which CY|X exists (e.g., C−3/2 XX CXY Hilbert–Schmidt) are standard but strong; the paper appropriately uses the regularized empirical operator.
- Aspects needing clarification for reproducibility and robustness:
  - Kernel choices and hyperparameters (kernel types for x and y, bandwidth selection strategy, λ) are not specified in the main text; sensitivity analysis is missing.
  - Computational complexity: per-minibatch inverses of K (O(b^3)) may be non-trivial; runtime/memory and stability with larger batches are not discussed.
  - The mini-batch CMMD estimator’s bias/variance relative to full-batch CMMD is left to the appendix; an empirical study of its effect on training convergence is not provided.
  - For generation, quantitative metrics are absent; evaluation relies on visual inspection and nearest-neighbor checks.
- Experiments: The classification results on MNIST/SVHN are competitive with deep generative baselines (MMVA) but below strong discriminative models (e.g., DSN). Conditional generation on MNIST and Yale demonstrates class-conditioned sampling and diversity; adding an autoencoder improves sample quality. The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment shows that a distilled CGMMN student matches PBP’s RMSE on a small dataset, but broader regression tasks and predictive uncertainty calibration are not explored.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear, with a concise review of kernel embeddings and MMD, a precise definition of CMMD, and a simple learning algorithm. Notation is mostly consistent, though the s/d superscripts (data vs. synthetic) could be clarified earlier, and Eq. (2) would benefit from explicit dimensionality notes. There are minor typographical errors (“MINIST,” “augumentation,” “Through there is a gap”). More detail on kernel choices and training hyperparameters in the main text would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
The extension from unconditional GMMN to conditional CGMMN via CMMD is a natural but nontrivial contribution, leveraging conditional kernel embedding operators to define a practical learning objective. Compared to conditional GAN/CVAE, the approach replaces adversarial/variational objectives with a kernel-based moment-matching criterion. While conditional embeddings are known, integrating them with deep generative networks and demonstrating learning via back-propagation is novel at NIPS scale circa 2016.

Significance:
The contribution provides a principled, simple alternative to adversarial or variational training for conditional generative modeling. The method is broadly applicable (classification, conditional generation, model distillation) and compatible with various architectures (MLP/CNN). Empirically, it achieves competitive performance but does not advance state-of-the-art. The lack of quantitative generative evaluation and limited distillation scope constrain immediate impact, but the framework could be useful for applications where stable training and flexibility with non-differentiable kernels on x are desirable.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, principled objective (CMMD) grounded in established kernel theory; simple and stable training without min-max.
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMN to conditional modeling, filling a gap relative to CGAN/CVAE.
- Versatile demonstrations across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation with competitive results.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is limited: no quantitative metrics for generative quality; small-scale distillation; classification not state-of-the-art.
- Key practical details (kernel choices, bandwidth/λ selection, sensitivity) are omitted from the main text.
- Computational aspects (cost of K inversions, scaling to large datasets/high-dimensional x) are not rigorously analyzed.
- Theoretical section stops at sufficiency; no generalization or consistency analysis for the finite-sample regularized estimator in the deep learning setting.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide kernel specifications (types, bandwidth selection heuristics) and sensitivity analyses; include ablations over λ, minibatch size, and h dimensionality.
- Report training time and memory, and discuss strategies to mitigate O(b^3) costs (e.g., low-rank approximations, random features).
- Add quantitative generative evaluations (e.g., Parzen window estimates as in GMMN, classifier-based scores, or diversity metrics) and comparisons to CGAN/CVAE on conditional generation.
- Expand distillation experiments to more regression/classification datasets and assess predictive uncertainty calibration (e.g., NLL, calibration curves).
- Improve clarity/typos and notation around s/d datasets and Eq. (2).

Overall assessment:
A technically sound and reasonably clear paper proposing a meaningful extension of MMD-based deep generative modeling to conditional distributions. The idea is solid and broadly applicable, but the empirical section and practical details are somewhat underdeveloped. The work is of moderate originality and significance.
