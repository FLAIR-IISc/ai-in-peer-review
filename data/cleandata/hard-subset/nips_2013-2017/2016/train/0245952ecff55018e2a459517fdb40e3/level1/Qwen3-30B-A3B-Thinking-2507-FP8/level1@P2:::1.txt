

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

**Summary:**
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for training, which measures the difference between kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions. CGMMN employs a simple generative process where samples are drawn from a simple distribution and passed through a deep network with conditional variables, trained via backpropagation. The paper evaluates CGMMN on predictive modeling (MNIST/SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST/Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, demonstrating competitive performance across all tasks.

**Quality:**
The paper presents a technically sound extension of GMMN to conditional distributions. The theoretical foundation is solid, with Theorem 3 establishing the connection between CMMD and conditional distribution matching. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly presented, and the implementation details in experiments are thorough. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations regarding the assumptions for kernel embedding operators. However, the paper could have provided more detailed theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the CMMD objective, particularly regarding the empirical estimator's behavior with finite samples.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Section 2 provides a concise yet comprehensive background on kernel mean embeddings, making the paper accessible to the NIPS audience. The figures (Fig. 1-5) effectively illustrate the architecture and results. The experimental section is logically structured with clear presentation of results. The appendix provides sufficient additional details without cluttering the main text. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon.

**Originality:**
CGMMN makes a meaningful contribution by extending GMMN to conditional distributions, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The CMMD criterion is novel, and the paper distinguishes itself from prior work by focusing on moment-matching rather than adversarial learning. While conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Soň et al., 2015) exist, CGMMN offers a simpler training procedure without min-max optimization. The paper adequately references related work, including GMMN (Li et al., 2015), conditional GANs, and Bayesian dark knowledge (Korattikara et al., 2015).

**Significance:**
CGMMN addresses a practical need for conditional generative models across diverse applications. The framework's flexibility enables use in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and knowledge distillation without major modifications. The results demonstrate competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods, particularly noting the simplicity of training (backpropagation vs. GAN's min-max optimization). The paper's contribution significantly broadens the applicability of moment-matching networks, making it a valuable addition to the generative modeling literature.

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical foundation with proper theorems and proofs
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple domains
- Simple and practical training procedure using standard backpropagation
- Demonstrated utility in diverse applications (predictive modeling, generation, knowledge distillation)
- Good comparison with relevant baselines across all tasks

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison with conditional GANs (the most popular alternative for conditional generation)
- Insufficient discussion of computational complexity (matrix inverses in CMMD objective)
- Claims of "competitive performance" without strong evidence of superiority over existing methods
- Theoretical justification for using Hilbert-Schmidt norm could be strengthened

**Recommendation:**
The paper makes a solid contribution to generative modeling by extending moment-matching networks to conditional distributions. The theoretical foundation is sound, the implementation is straightforward, and the experimental results are compelling across multiple tasks. While the comparison with conditional GANs could be more extensive, the paper's focus on a simpler training procedure (vs. min-max optimization) is a significant practical advantage. The paper is well-written and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Clear extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with novel CMMD criterion
- Comprehensive evaluation across multiple application domains
- Practical training procedure using standard deep learning techniques
- Demonstrates real-world applicability in diverse tasks
- Addresses a meaningful gap in the literature on generative models

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with conditional GANs (the most relevant baseline)
- Some theoretical gaps in the analysis of CMMD
- Claims of "competitive performance" without strong evidence of superiority

Given the paper's strong theoretical foundation, comprehensive evaluation, and practical significance, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance. The work advances the state of the art in generative modeling by providing a simpler, more flexible framework for conditional distribution learning that can be applied across multiple domains without the complexities of adversarial training.
