Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend GMMN by learning conditional distributions p(y|x). The core contribution is a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective that measures the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X and CZ|X estimated from data and from the model. Practically, the model concatenates the conditioning variable x with noise h, passes through a deep network y = f(x, h; w), and optimizes CMMD over mini-batches via backprop. The paper demonstrates applications in predictive modeling (classification on MNIST/SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and distilling Bayesian posterior predictive distributions (Bayesian dark knowledge).

The work is well-grounded in kernel mean embeddings, MMD-based generative models (GMMN), and connects to conditional generative modeling, including conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero) and conditional VAEs, as well as to max-margin DGMs for prediction. The main novelty is a principled CMMD objective derived from conditional kernel embeddings (Song et al. 2009; Grunewalder et al. 2012), and its coupling with deep conditional generators trained by SGD.

Quality
- Theory: The CMMD is well-motivated. Theorem 3 gives a sufficient condition (equality of conditional embedding operators) for equality of conditionals, aligning with established RKHS results. The empirical operator estimator Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ is standard and justified. The derivation of the trace/Gram-matrix formula in Eq. (2) enables efficient computation and clear gradients through the output Gram matrices.
- Algorithmic correctness: The training loop is straightforward. Because inverses of K̃ depend only on x, differentiability only requires smooth kernels on y; this is correctly noted.
- Practicality and scalability: Mini-batching is necessary since CMMD entails O(b^3) inversion per batch; this is acknowledged. However, the paper does not quantify runtime/memory or compare different strategies (e.g., Cholesky, Nyström/random features to reduce cubic cost), and it does not analyze sensitivity to λ or kernel bandwidths. These omissions limit reproducibility and understanding of stability.
- Experiments: For classification, CGMMN-CNN is competitive with max-margin DGMs and older CNN baselines on MNIST and SVHN, but lags strong discriminative models. The evaluation for generation is mostly qualitative (images, nearest neighbors), without quantitative metrics; comparisons to conditional GANs or CVAEs are absent. The distillation experiment on Boston Housing shows essentially no degradation in RMSE relative to the teacher PBP, but does not assess uncertainty (NLL, calibration), which is central to Bayesian dark knowledge.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The background on MMD and conditional embeddings is concise and accurate. The CMMD derivations are clear, and Algorithm 1 is easy to follow. Some implementation details are missing or scattered to the appendix: kernel choices for x and y, bandwidth selection procedure, λ selection, the exact mini-batch pipeline for constructing cross-gram matrices, and how perturbations of x are done in distillation. Minor typos (e.g., “MINIST”) appear.

Originality
The idea of using conditional kernel mean embeddings to define a discrepancy between conditional distributions and coupling it with deep generators is novel relative to prior GMMN and complements contemporaneous conditional GAN/CVAE work. While conditional embeddings are established, their integration into a deep conditional generator trained end-to-end appears original and nontrivial. The Bayesian dark knowledge use case is a nice, less-explored application.

Significance
Conceptually, CMMD offers a stable, non-adversarial objective for conditional generative modeling. This is valuable given the optimization difficulties of GANs. The approach is general and could be adopted broadly. However, the empirical section, confined to relatively simple datasets with limited baselines and metrics, underplays potential impact. The classification results are respectable but do not set new state of the art; the generation results are not quantitatively assessed; the distillation experiment is small-scale and does not measure uncertainty preservation.

Pros
- Clear and principled extension of MMD to conditional modeling via operator norms.
- Simple, general training procedure amenable to backprop; no min–max.
- Multiple applications demonstrated, including a compelling Bayesian distillation use case.
- Solid theoretical grounding and correct use of RKHS machinery.

Cons
- Limited empirical validation: mostly small datasets; lack of comparisons to conditional GANs/CVAEs; no quantitative generative metrics.
- Missing analysis of kernel choices, bandwidth selection, regularization λ, and sensitivity.
- Computational aspects of CMMD (O(b^3) per batch) not profiled; no approximations explored.
- For classification, no comparison to the same architectures trained with cross-entropy, making it hard to isolate the value of CMMD as a learning signal.
- Bayesian distillation evaluated only by RMSE; no NLL or calibration.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to conditional GAN and conditional VAE on MNIST/Yale (and ideally CIFAR-10) with quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen as used then, or at least structured prediction accuracy, sharpness/diversity checks).
- Report sensitivity to kernel bandwidths and λ; describe selection procedure; provide ablations for noise dimensionality and combination schemes (concatenation vs additive noise).
- Provide runtime/memory benchmarks and discuss approximations (Nyström, random Fourier features) to reduce cubic cost.
- For classification, include baselines with identical architectures trained with cross-entropy; report NLL and calibration.
- For Bayesian distillation, include NLL, predictive intervals, and calibration plots to demonstrate preservation of uncertainty.

Overall evaluation
The paper meets the quality and clarity bar in theory and method, and presents a conceptually appealing, original extension of MMD-based generative modeling. The empirical evaluation is promising but limited. I lean toward weak accept: the idea is sound, broadly useful, and the missing empirical pieces seem addressable; strengthening experiments and analysis would substantially increase the paper’s impact.
