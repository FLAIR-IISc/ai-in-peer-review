

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a criterion for learning conditional distributions, building upon the kernel mean embedding framework established in previous NIPS work (e.g., Gretton et al., 2012; Song et al., 2009). The approach extends GMMN (Li et al., 2015) to conditional settings by embedding conditional distributions and measuring the discrepancy between empirical conditional embeddings.

The paper makes several meaningful contributions. It provides a theoretically grounded approach to conditional generation using CMMD, which avoids the instability issues of GANs while maintaining the simplicity of the MMD criterion. The authors demonstrate the approach across three application domains: predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN classification), contextual generation (MNIST and Yale Face datasets), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing regression). The results show competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods in all domains.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for CMMD by building upon kernel mean embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012). The extension from MMD to CMMD is logically derived and well-motivated.

2. **Practical Application:** The paper successfully applies CGMMN to multiple practical problems, including classification (where it achieves competitive results with CMMVA on SVHN), contextual generation (producing realistic samples conditioned on labels), and Bayesian knowledge distillation (maintaining predictive performance while reducing computational cost).

3. **Clear Experimental Design:** The experiments are well-structured, with appropriate baselines and thorough evaluation across different datasets and tasks. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is particularly novel and useful for practitioners.

4. **Technical Clarity:** The paper is well-written with clear mathematical formulations and a logical flow from theoretical background to application.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Comparison with Conditional GANs:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), which are the dominant approach for conditional generation. While the paper mentions GANs in the introduction, it doesn't directly compare CGMMN to conditional GANs on the same benchmarks, making it difficult to assess the relative advantages.

2. **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a theoretical foundation for CMMD, it lacks a thorough analysis of the convergence properties of the CMMD objective or generalization bounds for the conditional model. This is a significant gap given the paper's focus on theoretical grounding.

3. **Underdeveloped Discussion of Limitations:** The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach, particularly the noisy samples in Fig. 2. The authors mention using an autoencoder to improve sample quality (Fig. 4), but don't explain why the basic CGMMN produces noisy samples or how this limitation affects the approach's practical applicability.

4. **Unclear Advantages over Existing Methods:** The paper doesn't clearly articulate why CMMD is advantageous over other conditional generative approaches like conditional VAEs (So et al., 2015) or conditional GANs. The paper states CGMMN "largely extends the capability of GMMN" but doesn't demonstrate how it specifically improves upon previous conditional methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a theoretically grounded extension of GMMN to conditional distributions
- Demonstrates practical utility across multiple important application domains
- Offers a viable alternative to conditional GANs that avoids adversarial training instability
- The Bayesian dark knowledge application is novel and valuable for model compression

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Lacks sufficient comparison with the dominant conditional generative approach (conditional GANs)
- Theoretical analysis of the learning process is incomplete
- Doesn't clearly articulate advantages over existing conditional generative methods
- Doesn't sufficiently address limitations of the approach

The paper represents a solid contribution to the field of generative modeling, extending the moment-matching framework to conditional settings with practical applications. However, the lack of direct comparison with conditional GANs and the incomplete theoretical analysis prevent it from making a compelling case for significant advancement over existing approaches.

Overall, the paper is technically sound with well-executed experiments, but the novelty is somewhat incremental given the existence of other conditional generative models. The authors would benefit from addressing the limitations mentioned above, particularly by providing a more thorough comparison with conditional GANs and a deeper theoretical analysis of the CMMD learning process. Given the quality of the work and its relevance to NIPS, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, but with the understanding that the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the comparative analysis with conditional GANs.
