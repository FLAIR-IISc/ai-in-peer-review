## Review of Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) as an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to learn conditional distributions. The core idea is to apply a conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for training, enabling tasks like predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The authors present a theoretically sound framework and demonstrate competitive performance across various experiments.

The work builds directly on the success of GMMN [19] and addresses a key limitation – the inability to model conditional distributions.  This extension is a natural and important step, aligning with broader trends in generative modeling like conditional GANs [21] and conditional VAEs [28]. The use of MMD as a training criterion is well-motivated, offering advantages over adversarial training (GANs) in terms of stability.  The formulation of CMMD and the associated gradient calculation via backpropagation are significant contributions, allowing for efficient training of these models.  The application to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation is a particularly interesting and practical use case. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for CMMD, building on established concepts like kernel mean embeddings and Hilbert space operators.
*   **Novelty:** Extending GMMN to the conditional setting is a novel contribution, filling a gap in the existing literature.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate competitive performance on diverse tasks, including predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), generative modeling (MNIST, Yale Face), and knowledge distillation.
*   **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically structured.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the results are competitive, more detailed ablation studies would be valuable. For example, assessing the impact of different network architectures or kernel choices would strengthen the findings.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t sufficiently discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters, especially the regularization parameter λ in the CMMD objective.
*   **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons are made, some recent and highly competitive baselines (e.g., normalizing flows) are not included. This makes it harder to assess the true advantage of CGMMN.
*   **Scalability concerns**: The computational cost of calculating kernel matrices is highlighted, and while mini-batch approaches are mentioned, a detailed analysis of scalability for larger datasets would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in generative modeling.
*   Presents a theoretically sound and novel approach.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on a range of tasks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks comprehensive ablation studies.
*   Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   Comparison to limited set of baselines.



**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The novelty and theoretical rigor, combined with the encouraging empirical results, outweigh the identified weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance**, but encourage the authors to address the points raised above in a revised version, particularly regarding ablation studies and hyperparameter analysis.
