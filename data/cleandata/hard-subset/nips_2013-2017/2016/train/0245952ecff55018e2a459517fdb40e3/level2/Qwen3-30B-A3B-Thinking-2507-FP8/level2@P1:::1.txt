

The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a novel extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) that learns conditional distributions using Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). The authors present a theoretically sound framework that builds on kernel mean embeddings to address conditional generation tasks, with evaluations across predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a meaningful and well-motivated extension of GMMN to conditional distributions, addressing an important gap in the literature. The CMMD criterion is rigorously derived from kernel embedding theory, with clear theoretical justification for its use in conditional settings.

2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and covers multiple application domains with appropriate datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face, Boston housing). The results demonstrate competitive performance across all tasks, with particular strength in the Bayesian dark knowledge application where the distilled model preserves predictive performance.

3. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with thorough mathematical formulations and effective use of figures to illustrate generated samples. The connection to related work, including conditional GANs and conditional VAEs, is appropriately discussed.

4. The method's simplicity (training via backpropagation without min-max optimization) is a significant practical advantage over conditional GANs, which is well-articulated in the paper.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison with conditional GANs (cGANs) is limited. While the paper references cGANs in the introduction, the experimental comparison is not as thorough as it could be. A direct comparison of sample quality, training stability, and computational efficiency between CGMMN and cGANs would strengthen the paper's position in the conditional generative modeling landscape.

2. The paper mentions that "the assumptions may not hold" for the CMMD objective but doesn't provide empirical evidence of how often these assumptions fail in practice or how performance degrades when they do. A sensitivity analysis would be valuable.

3. The discussion of hyperparameters (particularly λ for regularization) is limited. The paper states λ serves as regularization but doesn't explore its sensitivity or optimal values across different tasks.

4. The ablation study on hidden variable dimensionality is insufficient. The authors note "we did not observe much difference with a higher dimension" but don't systematically investigate how dimensionality affects performance or training time across different datasets.

Constructive suggestions:
1. Include a more detailed comparison with conditional GANs, including quantitative metrics (e.g., FID scores) for image generation tasks to better establish the trade-offs between the two approaches.

2. Expand the discussion of computational complexity, particularly the cost of matrix inversions in the CMMD calculation. The mini-batch approach is mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed in terms of scalability.

3. Provide a more thorough analysis of the hyperparameter λ, including its impact on different tasks and a recommendation for optimal values.

4. Include a quantitative comparison of image quality (e.g., using perceptual metrics) between CGMMN with and without the autoencoder component for generation.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to conditional generative modeling by extending the GMMN framework to conditional distributions while maintaining the simplicity of the training process. The theoretical foundation is sound, the implementation is straightforward, and the empirical results are convincing across multiple domains. While the comparison with conditional GANs could be strengthened, the paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field.

The work is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in generative modeling with a novel approach that builds on established theoretical foundations. The method's practical advantages over conditional GANs (no need for discriminator training, simpler optimization) are particularly relevant for applications where training stability is a concern.

Pro: Clear theoretical extension, strong experimental validation across multiple domains, practical advantages over conditional GANs
Con: Limited comparison with conditional GANs, insufficient hyperparameter analysis, some computational details could be expanded

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a meaningful contribution that advances the state of the art in conditional generative modeling, with solid theoretical grounding and compelling empirical results. The minor weaknesses identified do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.
