This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep generative model for learning conditional distributions p(y|x). The work is a natural and significant extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which utilize Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to learn joint distributions. The core contribution is a novel training objective, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which is derived from the theory of kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions. This objective measures the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between the conditional embedding operators of the true data distribution and the model's generated distribution. The model is trained by minimizing this CMMD loss via back-propagation. This approach presents an alternative to other conditional generative models like Conditional GANs and VAEs, aiming for a simpler and more stable training objective than the adversarial min-max game.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength is the proposal of the CMMD objective, which is theoretically well-founded and provides an elegant method for conditional density estimation in the moment-matching framework. The paper is clearly written, logically structured, and provides sufficient background for the reader to understand the contribution. A major advantage of this work is its demonstrated versatility. The authors successfully apply CGMMN to a diverse set of tasks—predictive modeling, contextual image generation, and the novel application of distilling knowledge from Bayesian models—which compellingly showcases the framework's flexibility and broad potential. The experimental results are solid, achieving performance that is competitive with other state-of-the-art deep generative models on standard benchmarks like MNIST and SVHN.

**Weaknesses:**
While the performance is competitive, the model does not consistently outperform the top methods in all tasks, particularly in prediction, where it falls behind discriminative approaches like DSN. For the generation tasks, the presented images (without an autoencoder) appear somewhat noisy, and a direct quantitative and qualitative comparison against a strong baseline like Conditional GANs would have provided a more conclusive evaluation. Another concern is the computational complexity of the CMMD objective, which scales cubically with the mini-batch size due to the kernel matrix inversion. While this is a known issue for kernel methods, a more thorough discussion of the scalability and the impact of mini-batch approximations would strengthen the paper.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and significant contribution to the field of deep generative models. The proposed CMMD framework is an important addition to the literature, and its strengths in originality and significance outweigh its current limitations in state-of-the-art performance.
