This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the capabilities of GMMNs to learn conditional distributions. The core idea is to leverage a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for training, enabling applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The authors demonstrate competitive performance across these tasks.

The paper is well-motivated, building on recent advances in generative modeling with MMD and GANs. The extension to conditional distributions is a natural and valuable step, addressing limitations of standard GMMNs. The presentation is clear and generally well-organized. The authors connect their work to related areas and cite relevant papers, including prior NIPS publications on GANs, GMMNs, and MMD. The experimental evaluation is fairly comprehensive, covering diverse tasks and datasets, providing convincing evidence for the effectiveness of CGMMN. The use of both MLP and CNN architectures highlights the flexibility of the approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending GMMNs to the conditional setting using CMMD is a significant contribution. While GANs have addressed conditional generation, offering an alternative based on moment matching is valuable, especially considering the training stability issues often associated with GANs.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of CMMD based on kernel embedding theory is rigorous and well-explained. Theorems 3 and 2 provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.
* **Practicality:** The authors explicitly address the computational challenges related to kernel methods, particularly in the context of large datasets, by proposing a mini-batch version of the algorithm.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating competitive performance on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The inclusion of MNIST, SVHN, and Yale Face datasets strengthens the evaluation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with MMD and kernel methods. The diagrams (e.g., Fig. 1) are helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not provide sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning (e.g., the choice of the regularization parameter λ in CMMD). The sensitivity of performance to these parameters isn’t discussed.  It’s not clear if the results are robust or rely on careful parameter selection.
* **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the authors compare to several baselines, some more recent and state-of-the-art methods in conditional generation (e.g., more advanced VAEs or GAN variants) are missing from the comparison. Specifically, in the context of Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, comparison to more recent distillation techniques beyond [15] would be helpful.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies are limited. For example, the paper mentions using autoencoders in conjunction with CGMMN for improved image generation (Fig. 4), but a quantitative comparison to CGMMN without the autoencoder is lacking.  It would be useful to isolate the contribution of each component.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The paper acknowledges that the theoretical assumptions underlying CMMD may not always hold in practice, but it does not fully discuss the implications of this. A more detailed analysis of the robustness of CMMD to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** While the mini-batch approach addresses some computational concerns, the overall computational cost of evaluating and optimizing CMMD, especially for high-dimensional data, remains unclear.  A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to conditional generative modeling.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across a range of tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.
* The extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting addresses a significant limitation of previous work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper lacks detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art baselines is incomplete.
* The ablation studies are limited, hindering a thorough understanding of the contributions of each component.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling. The authors propose a novel and well-motivated approach that addresses a significant limitation of existing methods. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the hyperparameter sensitivity, computational complexity, and comparison to stronger baselines.  A more thorough ablation study would also be beneficial.
