Summary
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to conditional distributions via a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The key idea is to represent conditional distributions by their conditional mean embeddings CY|X in an RKHS, and to learn a neural generator y = f(x, h; w) such that the empirical operator induced by generated samples matches that induced by data, measured by the Hilbert–Schmidt norm ∥ĈdY|X − ĈsY|X∥2. The authors derive a practical CMMD estimator based on Gram matrices (Eq. 2), enabling stochastic training with backpropagation. They evaluate CGMMN on (i) predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), (ii) conditional generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and (iii) Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing), reporting competitive results.

Relation to prior work
CGMMN builds on GMMN (Li et al., 2015; Dziugaite et al., 2015), which used MMD to match joint distributions by training feedforward generators. The paper leverages conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012) to define CMMD, paralleling MMD but with non-uniform weights driven by similarities in the conditioning variables (Remark 1). Compared to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), CGMMN offers a simple, stable objective without min–max games or variational bounds. The paper also connects to recent max-margin DGMs for prediction (Li et al., 2015) and to Bayesian distillation (Korattikara et al., 2015).

Quality
- Theory: The paper provides a sufficient condition (Theorem 3) that equality of conditional mean operators implies equality of conditional distributions under standard assumptions (universal kernel, operator existence). The empirical CMMD estimator is clearly stated and practically computable. Assumptions and the role of regularization λ are discussed.
- Algorithms: Mini-batch training and backprop through the generator are straightforward. The inverse K̃−1 depends only on X, so non-differentiable kernels for X are permissible.
- Empirics: The classification experiments on MNIST/SVHN show competitive accuracy relative to DGMs (MMVA/CMMVA) and are behind strong discriminative CNNs (e.g., DSN). Conditional generation is demonstrated qualitatively, with and without an AE code space. Distillation from PBP on Boston shows no degradation in RMSE.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with helpful connections between MMD and CMMD. A few points would benefit from more explicit detail (kernel choices and bandwidth selection for L/K, computational complexity and memory, and the precise sampling protocols for x in estimating CMMD). The experimental sections could be more explicit about training settings and hyperparameters.

Originality
The formulation of CMMD for training conditional neural generators appears novel in the deep generative modeling literature, combining conditional mean embeddings with backprop-based generators in a simple way. While conditional mean embeddings are established, and GMMN exists for joint distributions, the conditional extension and its practical instantiation are a meaningful contribution.

Significance
A simple, non-adversarial objective for conditional generative modeling is valuable. The method is broadly applicable (prediction, conditional generation, model distillation), and the experiments suggest it can be a viable alternative to cGAN/cVAE in settings where stability and implementation simplicity are priorities. The distillation application is particularly interesting.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean objective (CMMD) grounded in kernel conditional embedding theory.
- Simple training with backprop; avoids adversarial instabilities and variational bounds.
- General applicability: prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Competitive classification performance for a generative-style approach; solid qualitative generation; successful distillation without loss of accuracy.
- Insightful discussion linking CMMD to MMD via non-uniform weighting induced by X.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical comparisons omit direct baselines to conditional VAEs and conditional GANs on conditional generation tasks. This limits the assessment of significance relative to contemporaneous methods.
- Quantitative evaluation of generation is primarily qualitative; no metrics such as NLL on held-out codes, Parzen estimates, or modern image metrics (acknowledging era constraints).
- Scalability: CMMD requires inverting K̃ per batch (O(b^3)); discussion on batch size, computational cost, and memory is brief. No kernel approximation (e.g., Nyström, RFF) or blockwise strategies are explored.
- Sensitivity to kernel choices: The paper does not report how kernels on X and Y (and λ) are chosen, or their effect on performance.
- Predictive use: For classification, prediction is based on a single sample’s argmax; averaging multiple samples or reporting calibration/uncertainty could better exploit the conditional distribution.
- Some theoretical assumptions (existence of CY|X in F ⊗ G, smoothness conditions) are standard but not empirically validated; finite-sample bias/variance trade-offs of the operator estimator are not analyzed.

Specific suggestions and questions
- Please include comparisons to conditional GANs and conditional VAEs on MNIST/Yale Faces (qualitative and, where possible, quantitative), using comparable architectures.
- Report runtime and memory per epoch, batch sizes, and how K̃−1 is computed/stored. Consider kernel approximations to improve scalability and provide ablations on batch size b vs. performance.
- Detail kernel choices for X and Y (Gaussian? bandwidth selection method? median heuristic?), sensitivity analyses, and the role of λ.
- For prediction, evaluate averaging over multiple y samples and report NLL and calibration (e.g., ECE or Brier score).
- Clarify the procedure to sample x for estimating CMMD during training in all settings (exactly reusing training x vs. perturbations; effect on generalization to unseen x).
- In the discrete-X case (labels), please expand on direct estimation of CYX and CXX in the main text, including numerical stability considerations.
- Provide additional distillation baselines (e.g., KL distillation to a deterministic network) and results on more datasets to assess generality.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, simple, and well-motivated objective for conditional generative modeling.
- Broad applicability with competitive results and a compelling distillation use case.
- Clear connection to RKHS conditional embedding theory; easy to implement and train.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong conditional generative baselines in experiments.
- Limited quantitative evaluation of generation and limited analysis of scalability/hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Modest improvements in prediction vs. state-of-the-art discriminative methods.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-motivated contribution that extends GMMNs to conditional modeling with a principled objective and demonstrates breadth across tasks. With stronger empirical comparisons to cGAN/cVAE and deeper analysis of computational aspects, this work would be even more compelling. I lean positive, contingent on addressing the experimental baselines and clarifying kernel/hyperparameter choices.
