This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional distributions p(y|x) using deep neural networks. This work is a natural and significant extension of the recently proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which focused on modeling joint distributions. The core technical contribution is a novel training objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). CMMD measures the dissimilarity between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective kernel mean embedding operators. The generative model itself is straightforward: a deep network maps a conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` to an output sample `y`. The network's parameters are optimized by minimizing the CMMD objective between the empirical data distribution and the model's generated distribution via back-propagation. The paper effectively demonstrates the model's versatility across a wide range of applications, including predictive modeling, contextual image generation, and knowledge distillation from complex Bayesian models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work is the novel and principled extension of the MMD framework to the conditional setting. While conditional versions of other deep generative models like GANs and VAEs exist, this paper fills an important gap by developing a moment-matching-based approach. The CMMD objective is an elegant generalization of MMD, and its theoretical underpinning in the context of kernel embeddings of conditional distributions is sound. This provides a valuable new tool for conditional density estimation.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear motivation for conditional modeling, a concise review of the necessary background on RKHS embeddings, and a logical step-by-step development of the CMMD objective and the CGMMN model. The connection established in Remark 1, which intuitively explains CMMD as a weighted version of MMD, is particularly insightful.

3.  **Broad Applicability:** A major strength is the comprehensive experimental evaluation across three distinct and important problem domains. Demonstrating competitive performance on prediction (MNIST, SVHN classification), generation (label-conditioned image synthesis), and model compression ("Bayesian dark knowledge") convincingly showcases the flexibility and general utility of the proposed framework.

4.  **Training Stability:** By formulating the learning problem as a direct minimization of a discrepancy measure, CGMMN potentially offers a more stable training procedure than competing approaches like conditional GANs, which rely on solving a difficult min-max optimization problem.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The empirical CMMD objective in Equation (2) requires the computation and inversion of kernel Gram matrices of size B x B, where B is the mini-batch size. This results in a computational cost of O(B³), which can be prohibitive for the large mini-batch sizes often required for stable training of deep networks. While a mini-batch algorithm is presented, the paper does not discuss the scalability of this approach or potential methods to mitigate this cost (e.g., using low-rank approximations like the Nystrom method or random Fourier features). Could the authors comment on the practical limitations imposed by this complexity and whether they explored any approximations?

2.  **Quantitative Comparison for Generative Tasks:** For the contextual generation experiments (Section 4.2), the evaluation is purely qualitative. While the generated images are compelling, the claims of "competitive performance" would be much stronger if supported by quantitative metrics. At the time of this work, comparisons against models like conditional GANs [21] and conditional VAEs [28] using metrics like log-likelihood estimates or other quantitative scores would be highly informative.

3.  **Details on Hyperparameter Selection:** The performance of kernel-based methods like MMD and CMMD can be sensitive to the choice of kernels and their associated hyperparameters (e.g., kernel bandwidth). The paper lacks detail on how the kernels (for both input space `x` and output space `y`) and their parameters were selected for the different experiments. Providing this information, along with a brief analysis of the model's sensitivity to these choices, would greatly improve the paper's reproducibility and practical value.

4.  **Autoencoder Pre-processing:** In Section 4.2.1, an autoencoder is used to learn a code space for generating smoother images. While this is a reasonable practical choice, it makes the generative model a two-stage process. It would be beneficial to discuss why the base CGMMN model produces noisy samples and whether this could be addressed within the CMMD framework itself, for instance, through different choices of kernels or network architectures, to enable truly end-to-end learning of high-quality samples.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of deep generative models. The proposed CMMD objective is a well-founded and elegant method for training conditional models. Despite the concerns regarding computational cost and the lack of quantitative comparisons in the generation experiments, the core ideas are strong and the breadth of successful applications is impressive. This work is a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
