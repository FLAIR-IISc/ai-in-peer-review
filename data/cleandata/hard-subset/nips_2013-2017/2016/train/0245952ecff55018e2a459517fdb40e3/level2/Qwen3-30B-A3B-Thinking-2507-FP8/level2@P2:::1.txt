

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extends Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to conditional distributions using a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The authors propose a principled approach to learning conditional distributions that builds upon kernel mean embedding techniques and maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) while maintaining the simplicity of training via backpropagation. The paper demonstrates CGMMN across three application domains: predictive modeling (MNIST/SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST/Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing).

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution by extending MMD to conditional distributions (CMMD), with Theorem 3 establishing a sufficient condition for conditional distribution equality. The generative process is well-motivated and aligns with prior work on deep generative models. The experimental evaluation covers multiple tasks with reasonable comparisons to relevant baselines, including state-of-the-art methods like CMMVA for predictive modeling and various deep learning architectures.

Strengths include the clear theoretical development of CMMD, the versatility demonstrated across multiple application domains, and the interesting application to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The paper provides a well-structured comparison with prior work on conditional generation (e.g., Conditional GANs, Conditional VAEs), though it could have engaged more deeply with these approaches.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution. First, the experimental section lacks critical details for reproducibility: kernel choices, regularization parameters, and hyperparameter tuning procedures are not specified, making it difficult to reproduce the results. Second, the comparison with Conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) is superficial - the paper doesn't discuss key advantages of CMMD over GANs (e.g., training stability, mode collapse avoidance), which is particularly surprising given the paper's claim that GANs are "hard to solve" in the introduction. Third, the theoretical justification for why CMMD is preferable to other conditional moment matching approaches is weak. The paper claims CGMMN "fills a research void" but doesn't clearly articulate why previous approaches (like Conditional VAEs or Conditional GANs) don't address the same problem space.

The most serious weakness is the lack of meaningful comparison with the most relevant prior work: Conditional GANs. While the paper mentions Conditional GANs, it doesn't provide a thorough comparison of CMMD against the standard conditional generative approach, which is the most direct alternative. The authors claim CGMMN is "competitive" with CMMVA (a max-margin DGM), but don't demonstrate how it compares to the dominant conditional generative approach (Conditional GANs) which has been highly successful in image generation tasks.

The Bayesian dark knowledge application is promising but lacks sufficient detail about how CGMMN compares to alternative distillation approaches. The paper claims CGMMN "does not harm" prediction performance compared to the teacher model, but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for this claim.

The paper's significance is somewhat diminished by the lack of clear advantages over existing conditional generative approaches. While the CMMD criterion is theoretically sound, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this approach is superior to other methods in practice, particularly given the widespread success of Conditional GANs. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison with Conditional GANs, including visual comparisons of generated samples and analysis of training dynamics.

The writing is clear and well-organized, though some sections (e.g., the kernel embedding of conditional distributions) could benefit from more intuitive explanations for readers unfamiliar with RKHS theory. The figures are helpful but could be improved with more detailed captions explaining the key findings.

Overall, the paper presents a theoretically sound extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with promising empirical results across multiple tasks. However, the lack of detailed experimental procedures, superficial comparison with the most relevant prior work (Conditional GANs), and insufficient justification for why CMMD is preferable to other approaches limit its contribution. The paper would benefit significantly from additional experiments comparing CGMMN directly with Conditional GANs and providing more comprehensive details about the experimental setup.

The paper's main contribution is a natural extension of GMMN to conditional settings, but it doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this extension is necessary or advantageous over existing approaches. While the results are competitive, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that CGMMN offers meaningful advantages over established conditional generative models. For NIPS, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison with Conditional GANs and a clearer articulation of the specific problem space that CGMMN uniquely addresses.
