**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional probability distributions `p(y|x)`. The work extends the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) [19, 3], which models joint distributions, to the conditional setting. The core contribution is the formulation of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective function. This objective measures the distance between the true conditional distribution and the model-generated distribution by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between their respective conditional kernel mean embedding operators, building on prior work in conditional embeddings [29]. The generative model is a deep neural network that maps a conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` to an output sample `y`. The model is trained via straightforward back-propagation to minimize the CMMD loss, avoiding the challenging min-max optimization often found in adversarial training [5]. The paper validates the proposed method on a diverse set of tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, contextual image generation on MNIST and the Yale Face dataset, and knowledge distillation from a Bayesian model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Significant Contribution:** The extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting is a logical and important step forward. Conditional models have much broader applicability than unconditional ones, and this work fills a clear gap in the literature. The proposed CMMD objective is technically sound, well-motivated, and grounded in the theory of kernel mean embeddings.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very clearly written and well-structured. It begins with a solid foundation of the prerequisite concepts (MMD, kernel embeddings) before carefully developing the CMMD objective and the CGMMN architecture. The connection between MMD and CMMD (Remark 1) is particularly insightful, explaining CMMD as a form of weighted MMD.

3.  **Versatile and Comprehensive Experiments:** A major strength is the demonstration of the framework's versatility across three distinct problem domains:
    *   **Prediction:** The model achieves competitive results against other deep generative models on standard classification benchmarks.
    *   **Generation:** It successfully generates images conditioned on class labels, with plausible results. The use of an autoencoder to produce codes for generation is a clever practical technique for improving sample quality.
    *   **Bayesian Dark Knowledge:** The application of CGMMN for distilling knowledge from a complex Bayesian model into a simpler student network is novel and compelling, showcasing a unique use case for this type of conditional density estimator.

4.  **Stable Training Objective:** By framing the learning problem as a direct minimization of a distance metric, the method avoids the instability and difficult convergence properties associated with the adversarial min-max training of GANs, which represents a significant practical advantage.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The work is solid, but could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The CMMD objective in Eq. (2) involves the inversion of kernel matrices, an operation with cubic complexity `O(B^3)` in the mini-batch size `B`. This could limit the model's scalability to the large batch sizes that are often beneficial for training deep networks. The paper could be improved by including a discussion of this computational cost. It would be valuable to comment on the practical limits of batch size or suggest potential avenues for mitigating this cost, such as using linear-time MMD approximations or randomized methods for matrix inversion.

2.  **Quantitative Comparison with other Conditional DGMs:** The experiments compare well against discriminative models and joint DGMs. However, for the contextual generation tasks, a direct quantitative comparison to other prominent conditional generative models from the same era, such as Conditional GANs [21] or Conditional VAEs [28], is missing. Including such a comparison, even on one dataset like MNIST, would provide a clearer context for CGMMN's performance and better situate its contributions within the broader landscape of deep conditional models.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The CMMD formulation and kernel methods in general introduce several key hyperparameters, including the kernel choices and their parameters (e.g., RBF bandwidth) and the regularization constant `λ`. The paper does not discuss the process for selecting these hyperparameters or the model's sensitivity to them. Adding a brief section or appendix note on this topic would enhance the work's reproducibility and practical value for other researchers.

4.  **Generative Sample Quality:** While the generated samples are recognizable, the raw images from the base CGMMN (Fig. 2) appear somewhat noisy compared to state-of-the-art results from other generative frameworks. The paper commendably addresses this by using an autoencoder, but it would be interesting to know if the base model's quality could be improved directly, for instance, through different network architectures (e.g., deconvolutional layers) or kernel functions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and well-founded extension of moment-matching networks for the important task of conditional density estimation.
*   The proposed CMMD objective is principled and leads to a more stable training procedure than adversarial methods.
*   The paper is supported by a strong and diverse set of experiments that convincingly demonstrate the model's utility and flexibility.
*   The work is clearly presented and makes a solid contribution to the field of deep generative models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational cost of the CMMD objective may limit its practical scalability, a point that is not discussed.
*   The paper lacks a direct quantitative comparison with contemporary conditional generative models like cGANs on generation tasks.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The proposed CGMMN framework is a valuable contribution to the literature on deep generative models. The suggestions above are intended to further strengthen what is already a high-quality submission.
