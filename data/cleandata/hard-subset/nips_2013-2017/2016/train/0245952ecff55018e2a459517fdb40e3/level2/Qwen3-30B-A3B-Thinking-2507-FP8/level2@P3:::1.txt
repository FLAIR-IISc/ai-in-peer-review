

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for training, measuring the difference between kernel mean embeddings of empirical conditional distributions and the generative model. The framework builds on kernel mean embedding techniques and enables efficient training via backpropagation, offering a simpler alternative to conditional GANs. The paper evaluates CGMMN across predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing), demonstrating competitive performance in all tasks.

The theoretical contribution is well-motivated and grounded in established kernel methods. The paper provides clear definitions of CMMD and establishes its theoretical properties through relevant theorems, extending the MMD framework to conditional distributions. The connection to previous work, particularly GMMN [19] and conditional GANs [21], is appropriately contextualized, highlighting how CGMMN fills a gap in moment-matching approaches for conditional modeling. The authors properly cite related work on conditional variational autoencoders [28] and conditional sum-product networks [4], demonstrating awareness of the broader literature.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, addressing multiple application domains. In predictive modeling, CGMMN-CNN achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art methods on MNIST and SVHN, particularly notable given the absence of data augmentation or model ensembling. The contextual generation experiments demonstrate the model's ability to produce diverse samples conditioned on class labels, with visual evidence showing samples are not mere copies of training data. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is particularly compelling, showing CGMMN can effectively distill knowledge from Bayesian models without significant performance degradation on the Boston housing regression task.

The paper is clearly written with good organization, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with kernel methods and deep learning. The algorithmic description (Algorithm 1) and experimental details provide sufficient information for reproducibility. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that generative samples can contain noise and suggesting potential improvements through integration with autoencoders.

Several minor weaknesses warrant attention. The computational complexity of the CMMD objective is not thoroughly analyzed, particularly the cubic cost of kernel matrix inversion, though the authors do provide a mini-batch version. The paper could more explicitly address the trade-offs between computational efficiency and the quality of the kernel approximation. Additionally, while the generative results on MNIST show promise, the visual quality of generated samples could be improved, as noted by the authors' own suggestion to combine with autoencoders for smoother images.

The significance of the work is strong, as conditional generative models are widely applicable across machine learning domains. The simplicity of the training procedure via backpropagation represents a practical advantage over more complex approaches like conditional GANs. The Bayesian dark knowledge application demonstrates a novel use case that could benefit from further exploration. The paper advances the state of the art in moment-matching networks by providing a principled framework for conditional modeling that maintains the simplicity of the GMMN approach.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field, with solid theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results across multiple domains. The work addresses a meaningful gap in the literature, extending the applicability of moment-matching networks while preserving the simplicity of the training procedure. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed, providing convincing evidence of the approach's effectiveness.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, original in its extension of moment-matching networks to conditional distributions, and significant in its potential applications. The contribution is distinct from previous NIPS work, particularly in the conditional setting for moment-matching networks, and the results advance the state of the art in generative modeling. The paper should be accepted as it makes a clear, valuable contribution to the field of generative models.
