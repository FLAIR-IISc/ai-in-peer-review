## Review of Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) as an extension of existing Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to model conditional distributions. The core idea is to leverage the conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to train a deep network that generates samples conditioned on input variables. The authors demonstrate the applicability of CGMMN to predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, presenting competitive results on various datasets.

The paper builds upon a solid foundation of recent work in generative modeling, particularly MMD-based approaches like GMMN and GANs. The motivation for extending MMD to the conditional setting is well-articulated, highlighting the practical benefits of modeling conditional distributions over joint distributions in several application scenarios. The theoretical development of CMMD is clear and logically presented, building on existing kernel embedding theory. The authors correctly identify and address the challenges of estimating the conditional embedding operator, providing a practical implementation using kernel tricks and backpropagation.

A key strength of this work is its versatility. The experiments demonstrate that CGMMN can be effectively applied to diverse tasks, showcasing its adaptability. The results are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in predictive modeling and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The inclusion of both MLP and CNN architectures allows for a broader evaluation of the approach.

However, the paper lacks some depth in analysis. While the experimental results are promising, a more thorough ablation study would be beneficial to understand the contribution of different components of the CGMMN architecture and training procedure. The discussion of limitations is brief. For instance, the computational cost of kernel methods, especially with large datasets, is not fully addressed beyond mentioning mini-batch optimization. Further, the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of kernel and its parameters could be explored. The connection to other conditional generative models, such as conditional GANs and variational autoencoders, could be more thoroughly discussed, highlighting the specific advantages of the proposed approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of MMD to conditional distributions.
*   Clear theoretical foundation and practical implementation.
*   Versatile applicability demonstrated through diverse experiments.
*   Competitive performance on multiple tasks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited ablation study and analysis of model components.
*   Brief discussion of limitations and computational cost.
*   Insufficient comparison and discussion with related conditional generative models.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling. While some aspects could be strengthened through more detailed analysis and discussion, the novelty, clarity, and promising experimental results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. The authors should be encouraged to further explore the theoretical and practical implications of CGMMN in future work.
