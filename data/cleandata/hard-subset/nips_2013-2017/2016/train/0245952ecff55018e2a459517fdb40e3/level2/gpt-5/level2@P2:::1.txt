Summary
The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep conditional generative modeling framework trained by minimizing Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) between the empirical conditional distribution and the model. CMMD is defined as the squared Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators estimated from samples, yielding a kernelized, distribution-matching objective that is optimized via back-propagation. The model conditions on inputs x and uses auxiliary noise h concatenated with x, passed through a deep network to generate outputs y ~ pθ(y|x). Experiments cover: (i) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN (classification), (ii) conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and (iii) “Bayesian dark knowledge” distillation by matching a Bayesian teacher’s predictive conditional distribution (Boston Housing).

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on kernel mean embeddings and MMD-based generative modeling (GMMN; Li et al. 2015; Dziugaite et al. 2015), and on conditional mean embeddings (Song et al. 2009; Grunewälder et al. 2012). It is analogous in ambition to conditional GANs and conditional VAEs, but uses a single-sided, stable objective. The main conceptual novelty is leveraging the conditional mean embedding operator as a learning target for deep conditional generators. The paper mentions cGAN and CVAE but does not provide empirical comparisons to them on conditional generation.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMNs from joint to conditional modeling with a principled operator-matching objective (CMMD).
- Practical training via minibatch CMMD with back-propagation; the inverse Gram matrices with respect to x are independent of θ, simplifying gradients.
- Versatile applications spanning prediction, conditional image generation, and Bayesian model distillation, demonstrating the generality of the framework.
- Competitive classification performance for a generative method (close to MMVA/CMMVA) and visually plausible conditional samples on MNIST and Yale Faces.
- The distillation experiment is compelling: matches PBP’s RMSE on Boston while offering a compact student.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Missing comparisons to strong conditional generative baselines (cGAN, CVAE) on generation tasks. Visuals are encouraging, but quantitative evaluation is limited.
- Kernel choices and hyperparameters (on X and Y) are underspecified. It is unclear which kernels were used for L and K, how bandwidths were set (median heuristic?), and how sensitive results are to these choices.
- Computational/memory complexity: CMMD involves inverting K+λI per minibatch (O(m^3)). Batch sizes of 300–500 could be expensive; empirical wall-clock and scaling vs GAN/CVAE are not reported.
- Theoretical justification is sufficient (if CY|X = CZ|X in a universal RKHS then PY|X = PZ|X), but finite-sample behavior and estimator bias/variance are not discussed. No analysis of the effect of λ or minibatch estimation error on optimization stability.
- Predictive modeling setup: for classification, the model samples a y vector and uses argmax. How stochasticity affects predictions, calibration, or robustness is not analyzed. A discriminative cross-entropy baseline with the same architecture would contextualize the performance gap.
- Distillation: only one small UCI dataset is shown. No assessment of predictive uncertainty (e.g., calibration, NLL), which is central to Bayesian distillation.

Quality
The method is technically sound and well-motivated by conditional mean embeddings. The CMMD derivation (trace form in Gram matrices) is consistent with prior embedding literature. The algorithm is simple to implement. However, several empirical choices (kernels, λ, bandwidths, noise dimension) lack detail, and the experimental evaluation misses key baselines and quantitative metrics for generation. Scalability and sensitivity analyses are absent.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with helpful background on MMD and conditional embeddings. The CMMD objective is presented cleanly. Some notational inconsistencies (s vs d superscripts) and typos exist. The training details for kernels and hyperparameters should be made explicit. A brief gradient expression would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Using CMMD to train deep conditional generators appears novel and is a natural, non-adversarial alternative to cGANs. The application to Bayesian dark knowledge is interesting, though related in spirit to prior student-teacher distillation works. The operator-matching perspective is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
A stable, general conditional generative training objective could be useful to practitioners who prefer to avoid adversarial training. The approach could be impactful if shown competitive with cGAN/CVAE at scale. As presented, the significance is moderate: promising but undervalidated on strong benchmarks and metrics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative generative evaluations for conditional generation (e.g., class-conditional FID/IS on CIFAR-10 or MNIST; retrieval metrics; precision/recall for generative models). Include comparisons to cGAN and CVAE under matched architectures.
- Specify kernel choices on X and Y, bandwidth selection strategy, λ, and conduct sensitivity analyses. Consider multiple kernels or learned kernels.
- Report computational costs (per-epoch time, memory), and discuss techniques to reduce O(m^3) inversions (e.g., low-rank approximations, random features, conjugate gradients).
- Analyze the effect of minibatch size on CMMD estimates and training stability. Consider unbiased/linear-time variants if applicable.
- For classification, compare against the same CNN trained with cross-entropy and report calibration (ECE/NLL). Discuss how sampling multiple y’s affects performance.
- Expand distillation experiments (additional UCI datasets; calibration and uncertainty metrics; inference speedup). Clarify whether CGMMN captures multimodal predictive posteriors.

Minor comments
- Fix typos and notational slips (e.g., “MINIST”, “convoluational”). Clarify R10+ notation.
- Clarify how x is perturbed when generating teacher/student pairs.
- Provide more nearest-neighbor analyses and diversity measures.

Recommendation
Leaning borderline accept. The paper presents a clear and potentially useful extension of MMD-based generative modeling to conditional settings, with a simple and general algorithm. To strengthen the case, add rigorous empirical comparisons to contemporaneous conditional generative models, specify kernel choices, and include quantitative generation and scalability analyses.

Pros
- Simple, principled objective; stable training.
- Broad applicability (prediction, generation, distillation).
- Competitive results for a non-adversarial generative method.

Cons
- Missing strong baselines and quantitative generative metrics.
- Limited scalability and sensitivity analysis.
- Kernel/hyperparameter details under-specified.
