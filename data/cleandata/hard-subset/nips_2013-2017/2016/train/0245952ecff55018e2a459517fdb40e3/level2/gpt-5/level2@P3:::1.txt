Summary
The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative framework trained by a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The key idea is to match conditional distributions by minimizing the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators estimated from (x, y) pairs generated by the model and those from data. This extends Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN; Dziugaite et al., UAI 2015) from joint to conditional modeling using the kernel conditional mean embedding framework (Song et al., ICML 2009; Grunewälder et al., ICML 2012). The model architecture is a deep network that takes as input both the conditioning variables and auxiliary noise and outputs samples from the target conditional. Training proceeds via backpropagation with mini-batch CMMD estimates.

Empirical evaluations cover: (a) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN (treating p(y|x) as the target and predicting by sampling then taking argmax), (b) conditional generation of images from labels on MNIST and Yale Faces (with qualitative results and a code-space variant via an autoencoder), and (c) distillation of Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge”; Korattikara et al., NIPS 2015) by training a CGMMN student to approximate the predictive distribution of a Bayesian teacher (PBP; Hernández-Lobato & Adams, ICML 2015). Results are competitive with several strong baselines in prediction (especially against max-margin deep generative models), produce reasonable conditional samples, and match PBP performance on Boston Housing.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of MMD-based training to conditionals via operator matching; the CMMD objective is natural in the conditional mean embedding framework and leads to a simple, stable training procedure without adversarial min–max.
- Practical training recipe with mini-batch CMMD, backpropagation through the generator, and the observation that kernels on x need not be differentiable since inverses do not depend on model parameters.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation, showing the versatility of the approach.
- Soundness: the objective follows directly from established RKHS theory; the sufficient condition (operator equality implies equality of conditionals) is clearly stated.
- Compatibility with different architectures (MLP, CNN) and with learned code spaces (autoencoders), which partially mitigates kernel limitations in high-dimensional pixel space.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation for conditional generation is largely qualitative; no quantitative metrics (e.g., log-likelihood proxies, Parzen estimates as used in GMMN, or classifier-based scores) are reported, nor comparisons to conditional GAN (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) or conditional VAE (Sohn et al., NIPS 2015) on the same tasks.
- Predictive modeling performance trails state-of-the-art discriminative models (e.g., DSN) and is only on par with prior DGMs (max-margin DGM). Uncertainty quality is not evaluated (e.g., NLL, Brier score), despite modeling p(y|x).
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analysis: results depend on kernel choices and regularization (bandwidths, λ), but selection procedures and robustness are not detailed. For image outputs, Gaussian kernels in pixel space can be problematic; the autoencoder workaround is promising but underexplored.
- Computational aspects are under-characterized. CMMD requires O(B^3) operations per mini-batch due to matrix inversions; runtime and memory cost relative to GAN/VAE training are not reported.
- Bayesian distillation is demonstrated on a single small dataset with only RMSE; calibration (e.g., NLL, coverage of predictive intervals) is not assessed.

Quality
The method is technically sound and well-grounded in RKHS theory. Derivations of CMMD and its empirical estimator are correct and clearly connected to conditional mean embeddings. The mini-batch stochastic optimization is reasonable, though the computational trade-offs deserve more empirical characterization. Claims are consistent with presented evidence, but broader quantitative evaluation would strengthen the case.

Clarity
Overall clearly written and organized. The explanation of CMMD and its relation to MMD is helpful. Some details would benefit from clarification: precise kernel choices and bandwidth selection on x and y in each experiment; treatment of discrete y in classification (kernel and normalization); guidance on λ and batch sizes. Minor typos exist but do not impede comprehension.

Originality
The combination of deep conditional generators with operator-matching via CMMD appears novel and a natural extension of GMMN to conditionals, leveraging existing conditional mean embedding theory. While conceptually straightforward, formalizing and implementing CMMD in a deep conditional generator fills a gap between cGAN/cVAE and MMD-based models.

Significance
Moderate. The work offers a stable, non-adversarial alternative for training conditional generators and a unifying view for conditional modeling and Bayesian distillation. Broader impact would be increased by stronger quantitative results and comparisons to contemporary conditional generative methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, elegant objective (CMMD) with clear theoretical grounding.
- Simple and stable training; broad scope of applications demonstrated.
- Competitive predictive performance among DGMs and successful Bayesian distillation.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited quantitative evaluation of generative quality and uncertainty.
- Missing empirical comparisons to cGAN and cVAE on conditional generation.
- Insufficient analysis of kernel hyperparameters and computational cost.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Include quantitative evaluation for conditional generation (e.g., Parzen log-likelihood as in GMMN, or classifier-based metrics on MNIST/Yale) and add comparisons to cGAN and CVAE under matched architectures.
- Report uncertainty metrics for p(y|x) (NLL, Brier) and for Bayesian distillation (NLL, calibration/coverage), to reflect the probabilistic nature of the model.
- Provide details and ablations on kernel choices (types, bandwidth selection strategies such as median heuristic), λ sensitivity, and their effect on performance.

Optional:
- Profile training time/memory and batch size impact; discuss strategies to scale CMMD (e.g., low-rank approximations, random features, Nyström).
- Explore additional conditional tasks where modeling p(y|x) is crucial (e.g., attribute-to-image generation) with quantitative measures.
- Analyze the effect of using learned representation spaces (autoencoders) more systematically for high-dimensional outputs.

Overall evaluation
A solid and well-motivated contribution that extends MMD-based training to conditional generative modeling with a clean operator-matching objective. With stronger quantitative comparisons and analysis, this work would have clear impact. As it stands, the paper is technically sound and promising, but the empirical section is not yet fully convincing against contemporary conditional baselines.
