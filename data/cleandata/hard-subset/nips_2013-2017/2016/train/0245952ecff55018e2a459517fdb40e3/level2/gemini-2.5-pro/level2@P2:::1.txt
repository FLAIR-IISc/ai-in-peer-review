This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional distributions `p(y|x)` using a deep generative model. The work is a direct extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which model joint distributions `p(x)` by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model-generated samples and real data samples. The key contribution of this paper is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective function. CMMD measures the distance between the kernel mean embeddings of two conditional distributions, `P(Y|X)` and `Q(Y|X)`, by comparing their conditional embedding operators in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The proposed generative model takes a conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to a deep neural network, which outputs a sample `y`. The network parameters are trained using stochastic gradient descent and backpropagation to minimize the CMMD loss. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of CGMMN on a variety of tasks, including classification, conditional image generation, and a novel application called "Bayesian dark knowledge," where a simple CGMMN is trained to mimic a more complex Bayesian model.

This work is well-positioned within the context of deep generative models of its time. It builds directly upon the theoretical foundations of kernel mean embeddings [29] and their use in generative modeling via MMD [19, 3]. It provides a compelling alternative to other prominent conditional generative models like conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (cGANs) [21] and conditional Variational Autoencoders (cVAEs) [28], offering a potentially more stable training objective than the min-max game of GANs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Technical Soundness:** The primary contribution, the CMMD objective for training conditional DGMs, is both novel and theoretically well-grounded. The paper provides a clear theoretical motivation, building upon established work on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. This provides an elegant, non-adversarial framework for conditional density estimation, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2.  **Significance and Broad Applicability:** The paper successfully demonstrates that a single, unified framework can be applied to a wide range of important problems. The experiments cover discriminative tasks (classification), generative tasks (contextual image generation), and model compression/distillation (Bayesian dark knowledge). This versatility highlights the potential impact and usefulness of the proposed method for both researchers and practitioners.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for conditional modeling is clearly laid out, the preliminary concepts are explained concisely, and the derivation of the model and learning algorithm is easy to follow. The connection between MMD and CMMD is explained intuitively in Remark 1.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Cost of CMMD:** The CMMD objective in Equation (2) requires the inversion of two kernel matrices of size `N x N`, where `N` is the minibatch size. This is an O(N^3) operation, which can be computationally prohibitive for large minibatches. The authors should discuss the scalability of their method with respect to minibatch size more explicitly. How does the performance trade-off with smaller, computationally cheaper minibatches? This seems to be a significant practical disadvantage compared to GANs or VAEs, whose per-batch costs are typically linear in batch size.

2.  **Lack of Comparison with Conditional GANs:** While cGANs are mentioned as related work, a direct experimental comparison is notably absent, especially for the conditional generation task. Given that cGANs were a state-of-the-art competitor for this task, a quantitative and qualitative comparison would be essential to substantiate the claim of "competitive performance." A key motivation for MMD-based training is often improved stability over the adversarial min-max objective; demonstrating this advantage empirically would significantly strengthen the paper.

3.  **Missing Experimental Details:** The paper omits crucial details needed for reproducibility.
    *   What specific kernel functions (e.g., RBF) and what hyperparameter settings (e.g., bandwidth) were used for the inputs `x` and outputs `y` in the various experiments? The performance of MMD-based methods is known to be highly sensitive to kernel choice.
    *   In the classification experiments (Sec 4.1.1), the output `y` is a 10-dimensional vector. How is the kernel for the CMMD objective defined over this output space? Clarifying these details is essential.

4.  **Quality of Generated Samples:** The generated MNIST samples in Figure 2 appear significantly noisier and less sharp than those produced by contemporary models like DCGAN. While the authors propose a two-stage approach using an autoencoder to improve sample quality (Figure 4), this adds complexity and relies on a separate model. It would be beneficial to discuss why the direct CMMD objective might lead to this perceptual characteristic and how it compares to the properties of an adversarial loss.

5.  **Clarification on Discrete Conditioning:** In Section 4.2.1, for MNIST generation, the conditioning variable (the class label) is discrete. The authors state they estimate `C_YX` and `C_XX^-1` directly. This seems to imply estimating statistics for each of the 10 classes separately, which appears to contradict the earlier motivation in Section 3.1 that CMMD is beneficial because it "shares statistics" across different values of `x`. A brief clarification on how this specific implementation relates to the general CMMD formulation would be helpful.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and theoretically sound method for conditional generative modeling. The CMMD objective is an elegant extension of MMD, and the paper demonstrates its utility across a diverse and impressive set of applications. The main weaknesses lie in the lack of a direct comparison to key competitors like cGANs, some missing experimental details that hinder reproducibility, and a discussion of the computational trade-offs. Despite these points, the originality and significance of the core contribution are high. The paper presents a valuable new tool for the deep learning community. I recommend acceptance.
