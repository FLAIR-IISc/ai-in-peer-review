This paper presents Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) to the conditional setting. The authors aim to learn a conditional distribution given some input variables based on a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The proposed method is evaluated on various tasks, including predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the related work in the field. The introduction effectively sets the stage for the proposed method, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches and the potential benefits of extending GMMN to the conditional setting.

The technical contribution of the paper is the development of the CMMD criterion, which measures the difference between two conditional distributions. The authors provide a thorough derivation of the CMMD objective and discuss its relationship to the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion. The proposed method is also well-supported by theoretical analysis, including a proof that the CMMD objective is a sufficient condition for two conditional distributions to be equal.

The experimental evaluation of the paper is comprehensive, covering various tasks and datasets. The results demonstrate the competitiveness of CGMMN with state-of-the-art methods, including Variational Autoencoders (VA), Max-Margin Deep Generative Models (MMVA), and Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (CGAN). The authors also provide visualizations of the generated samples, which are of good quality and demonstrate the ability of the model to capture the underlying structure of the data.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors rely heavily on formal mathematics to derive the CMMD objective, which may make it difficult for non-experts to follow. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the CMMD criterion and its relationship to MMD. The authors also do not provide a clear explanation of the actual computations performed by the algorithm and their computational efficiency.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from more detailed discussions on the choice of hyperparameters, such as the regularization parameter λ and the minibatch size. The authors also do not provide a thorough analysis of the robustness of the method to different types of noise or outliers in the data.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel extension of GMMN to the conditional setting, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors also provide a thorough review of the related work, highlighting the differences between their approach and existing methods.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to impact various applications, including predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge. The proposed method is also likely to be of interest to researchers in the field of deep learning and generative models.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field. However, it could benefit from more intuitive explanations, detailed discussions on hyperparameter choices, and a thorough analysis of the robustness of the method.
