This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMNs to learn conditional distributions p(y|x) by matching conditional mean embeddings in RKHS. Building on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012), the authors define a conditional MMD (CMMD) objective as the squared Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean operators CY|X from data and from the model, and derive an empirical trace form involving Gram matrices over inputs and outputs. The generative model is a deep network that takes both x and auxiliary noise h and outputs y; the CMMD loss is optimized by backpropagation. Applications include (i) predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), (ii) contextual generation (label-conditioned MNIST, Yale Faces), and (iii) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge,” Korattikara et al., 2015) by training a CGMMN student to match a teacher’s p(y|x, D).

Relation to prior work: CGMMN is a natural conditional analogue to GMMN (Dziugaite et al., 2015; Li et al., 2015) and leverages conditional kernel mean embeddings (Song et al., 2009). It contrasts with GANs and conditional GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Mirza & Osindero, 2014), VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2014), conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), and max-margin DGMs (Li et al., 2016). The Bayesian distillation application connects to PBP (Hernandez-Lobato & Adams, 2015) and Bayesian dark knowledge (Korattikara et al., 2015).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean objective: CMMD provides a direct, kernel-based criterion for matching conditionals, avoiding adversarial min-max and amortized variational bounds.
- Practical training: mini-batch CMMD with precomputed (K + λI)−1 per batch; gradients flow only through outputs y via the kernel on Y, so kernels on X need not be differentiable.
- Versatility: same framework applied to classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- The trace-form CMMD derivation is useful and, to my knowledge, new in deep generative modeling.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Conditional operator estimator and centering: The paper states ĈY|X = Φ(K + λI)−1Υ⊤ but does not discuss centering (i.e., using centered feature maps or the centering matrix H) despite defining CXY as a cross-covariance operator. This omission raises questions about bias, the exact relationship to ridge regression estimators of conditional mean embeddings, and whether Gram centering was used in experiments. It also impacts gradient stability and scale.
- Empirical competitiveness: On MNIST/SVHN classification, CGMMN-CNN lags behind state-of-the-art discriminative models (e.g., DSN) and is slightly worse than the conditional max-margin DGM (CMMVA), which is a closer generative baseline. This weakens the case that CMMD offers empirical advantages over strong alternatives.
- Generative evaluation: Conditional generation is shown qualitatively, but there are no quantitative metrics (e.g., conditional log-likelihood proxies, precision/recall for generative models) and no comparisons to conditional GANs or conditional VAEs on the same tasks.
- Scalability and kernel choices: The cost per batch scales cubically with batch size due to (K + λI)−1. The paper should discuss batch sizes used, wall-clock training time, and whether approximations (e.g., random Fourier features, low-rank kernel approximations) could scale CMMD further. Kernel/bandwidth selection and sensitivity are not discussed.
- Limited breadth in distillation: Only one real dataset is reported for Bayesian distillation, with no runtime gains vs. the teacher or calibration/uncertainty metrics.
- Clarity/typos: Several typos (e.g., “Frobeniu norm”, “an universal”, “which the”), and some notational overload would benefit from cleanup.

Quality
- The CMMD objective is mathematically motivated; Theorem 3 provides a sufficient condition for matching conditionals. Assumptions about operator existence and regularization are noted, but the empirical estimator’s centering and consistency should be discussed explicitly. The trace-form loss and optimization are sound. The experiments are carefully implemented but do not decisively establish superiority.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized with helpful algorithmic description. Please clarify the centering issue, specify kernels (on X and Y) and their hyperparameters, how λ is chosen, and add a brief complexity analysis. Fix typos and tighten notation.

Originality
- The combination of conditional mean embeddings with deep conditional generators trained by a CMMD loss is novel within the DGM literature. It extends GMMN in a principled way and connects kernel conditional regression with neural generators.

Significance
- Potentially impactful as a stable, non-adversarial alternative for conditional generative modeling and knowledge distillation. However, significance is limited by the current empirical evidence; stronger baselines and quantitative generative comparisons are needed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Document whether Gram matrices were centered in practice and, if not, justify why (e.g., regression view) and analyze the impact on gradients and bias; include the exact matrices used (H-centering, regularization).
- Add quantitative conditional generation metrics and baselines (conditional GAN, conditional VAE) on MNIST and Yale Faces; even simple conditional Parzen estimates would help.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for kernel bandwidth and λ; discuss approximations to speed up (K + λI)−1 (e.g., Nyström, RFF).
- Expand Bayesian distillation results: multiple datasets, runtime vs. teacher, calibration/NLL, and uncertainty quality.
- Minor: correct typos and improve proofreading.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, principled extension of GMMN to conditionals with a simple training pipeline.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across tasks, including an interesting distillation use case.
- Avoids adversarial training instabilities; objective is well-defined and differentiable.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Empirical performance on prediction not SOTA and slightly behind a strong generative baseline (CMMVA).
- Generative evaluation lacks quantitative comparisons to standard conditional generative baselines.
- Missing clarity on key estimator details (centering), scalability, and hyperparameter sensitivity.

Post-rebuttal update
- The authors clarified the centering issue, stating that Gram matrices are centered per minibatch (or, equivalently, the regression-based estimator is used), and gradients are computed accordingly; they will revise the text to make this explicit and include ablations. They also committed to adding quantitative conditional generation results and stronger baselines. With these clarifications, my concerns about soundness and clarity are largely addressed, and I raise my overall evaluation.
