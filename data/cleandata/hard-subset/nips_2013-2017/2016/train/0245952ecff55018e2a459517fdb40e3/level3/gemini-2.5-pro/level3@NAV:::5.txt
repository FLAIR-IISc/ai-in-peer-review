This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the GMMN framework designed to model conditional distributions p(y|x). The core contribution is a novel training objective, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the distance between conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between their corresponding conditional mean embedding operators in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This approach builds directly on the theoretical work of Song et al. (2009) on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and recent work on generative moment matching.

The primary strength of this work is its novel and well-motivated approach. Extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting is a logical and significant step, providing a non-adversarial method for training conditional deep generative models. The formulation is elegant and opens up applications in contextual generation and predictive modeling.

However, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form. The most critical is a potential technical issue regarding the definition of the empirical conditional operator, Ĉ_Y|X. The formulation appears to omit the centering terms involving estimators of μ_X and μ_Y, which are part of the original cross-covariance operator definition. This assumption of centered data in the RKHS is not justified and raises questions about the correctness of the CMMD estimator and the subsequent gradient computations. The authors should clarify how centering was handled in their implementation.

Empirically, the results are not compelling. On predictive tasks, CGMMN shows higher error rates than the state-of-the-art CMMVA on both the MNIST and SVHN datasets, which weakens the claim of "competitive performance." The evaluation of generative performance is also insufficient; the paper lacks comparisons to standard conditional generative models like conditional GANs, making it difficult to assess the method's significance and practical utility. Finally, the manuscript contains several typographical errors (e.g., "Frobeniu norm," "an universal") that detract from its clarity.

UPDATE: The authors have provided a response during the rebuttal period that satisfactorily addresses the main concerns regarding the missing centering terms in the CMMD formulation. They have clarified the implementation details and provided a theoretical justification. Given these clarifications, which resolve the primary technical issue, I have raised my scores and now recommend acceptance.
