**Summary**

This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework for learning conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The core idea is to train a generator network by minimizing a novel objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). This objective measures the distance between the conditional distribution of the training data and that of the generated data by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between their respective conditional mean embedding operators in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This approach builds directly on the theoretical work of Song et al. (2009) on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. The model is trained via back-propagation and evaluated empirically on several tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, contextual image generation, and a model distillation task called "Bayesian dark knowledge."

**Strengths (Pros)**

1.  **Novelty and Motivation:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach. Extending GMMNs to the conditional setting is a logical and important step, significantly broadening the applicability of moment-matching methods in deep learning. The connection to the existing literature on conditional mean embeddings provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed CMMD objective.

2.  **Simple Training Objective:** A key advantage of the moment-matching framework is its training stability compared to adversarial methods. The CMMD objective is a single function to be minimized, avoiding the difficult minimax optimization problems that can make training conditional GANs challenging.

3.  **Versatility:** The authors demonstrate the model's flexibility by applying it to a diverse set of problems, including prediction, generation, and model distillation. This effectively showcases the potential of CGMMN as a general-purpose tool for conditional density estimation.

**Weaknesses (Cons)**

1.  **Technical Clarity on Centering:** There is a significant technical ambiguity in the definition of the CMMD objective. The paper defines the cross-covariance operator `C_XY` with centering terms (`-μ_X ⊗ μ_Y`) in Section 2.3, following Song et al. (2009). However, the empirical estimator for the conditional operator used in the CMMD objective, `Ĉ_Y|X = Φ(K+λI)⁻¹Υᵀ`, corresponds to an *uncentered* cross-covariance operator. The original work by Song et al. requires centering the kernel Gram matrices to obtain a consistent estimator of the covariance operator. This paper omits any discussion of centering. This raises critical questions:
    *   Is there a theoretical justification for omitting the centering terms? If the model implicitly assumes data is centered in the RKHS, this assumption must be stated and justified.
    *   How were the experiments actually implemented? Was centering applied to the Gram matrices in practice, even though it is absent from the equations?
    *   This lack of clarity compromises the technical soundness and reproducibility of the work. The authors must clarify this point, as it directly impacts the objective function and its gradients.

2.  **Disappointing Predictive Performance:** While the paper claims "competitive performance," the empirical results for predictive modeling are underwhelming when compared to the reported state-of-the-art. On both MNIST and SVHN, the CGMMN-CNN model achieves a higher error rate than the CMMVA baseline from Li et al. (2015). For a key application, failing to outperform or at least definitively match existing deep generative models for prediction weakens the paper's claims.

3.  **Inadequate Evaluation of Generative Performance:** The evaluation of the model's generative capabilities is incomplete. The paper presents qualitative samples, which appear reasonable, but provides no quantitative or comparative analysis. To assess the quality of a conditional generative model, it is crucial to compare it against strong baselines (e.g., conditional GANs, conditional VAEs) using either established metrics (like Inception Score or FID, adapted for the conditional case) or at least side-by-side visual comparisons. Without such a comparison, it is impossible to gauge where CGMMN stands in the landscape of conditional generative modeling.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "Frobeniu norm", "an universal", "which the") that detract from its overall quality and suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

The most pressing issue is the definition and implementation of the CMMD objective. Please clarify whether the Gram matrices `K` and `L` were centered in your implementation, as is standard for estimating covariance operators in RKHS. If they were not, please provide a justification for this deviation from the established theory. This detail is crucial for understanding and reproducing your work.

For the experimental section, I would strongly encourage you to include comparisons with other conditional generative models in your generative tasks. Showing qualitative samples is a good start, but a more rigorous evaluation against models like Conditional GANs would substantially strengthen your contribution. Similarly, for the predictive tasks, it would be helpful to discuss why CGMMN might be underperforming relative to max-margin variants and whether modifications to the objective or architecture could close this gap.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper introduces a novel and theoretically grounded method for conditional generative modeling that avoids the instability of adversarial training. This is a valuable contribution. However, the work is currently undermined by a critical lack of clarity regarding the implementation of the core CMMD objective and experimental results that do not convincingly demonstrate an advantage over existing methods. The generative evaluation, in particular, lacks the necessary comparisons to contextualize the model's performance.

While the core idea is promising, the paper requires significant revision to address the technical ambiguity and strengthen the experimental validation.
