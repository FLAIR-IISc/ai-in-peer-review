Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep conditional generative model trained by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective derived from the RKHS embedding of conditional distributions. Concretely, a generator produces y = f(x, h; w) given conditioning variables x and auxiliary noise h; learning minimizes the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between the conditional mean operators of the data and model, estimated from mini-batches using Gram matrices over x and y. This extends GMMN (Li et al., 2015; Dziugaite et al., 2015), which matches joint distributions via MMD, to the conditional setting using conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012). The approach is positioned as a simpler alternative to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and complementary to CVAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), with applications in predictive modeling, conditional generation, and distillation of Bayesian “dark knowledge” (Korattikara et al., 2015).

Empirically, the paper reports: (1) predictive performance on MNIST and SVHN using CNN/MLP backbones; (2) conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces (with an autoencoder variant for cleaner images); and (3) distillation of a Bayesian neural network (PBP; Hernández-Lobato & Adams, 2015) on Boston housing.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean, principled extension of MMD training to conditional distributions via CMMD, grounded in the conditional mean embedding literature. The connection and operator-based objective are well-motivated.
- Simplicity and stability: Training reduces to backprop through the generator with a closed-form CMMD objective; no adversarial min–max, and the inverses over K̃ depend only on x, simplifying gradients.
- Breadth of applications: Demonstrates predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian model distillation within one framework.
- Practical details: Mini-batch training and the choice to share x across data/model batches make the method implementable.

Weaknesses
- Empirical competitiveness: Predictive errors on MNIST/SVHN are higher than state-of-the-art deep generative baselines (e.g., CMMVA) and substantially below strong discriminative CNNs. The paper does not convincingly show an advantage of CGMMN in predictive tasks.
- Generative evaluation: Conditional generation results are qualitative and lack comparisons to strong baselines such as conditional GANs or CVAEs on the same setup. No quantitative metrics (e.g., conditional log-likelihood proxies, classifier-based conditional consistency, human preference studies).
- Operator estimation and centering: The empirical conditional operator ĈY|X is presented without explicit centering terms (μX, μY). It is unclear whether centered or uncentered covariance operators are used, whether Gram matrices are centered in practice, and how this interacts with gradients and regularization. This ambiguity affects theoretical consistency and reproducibility.
- Sensitivity and scalability: The method’s performance may hinge on choices of kernels on X and Y (especially for high-dimensional x), kernel bandwidth selection, and the regularizer λ. These are not systematically ablated. Although mini-batching helps, per-batch inversions still scale cubically in batch size; scalability and memory footprint are not discussed in depth, nor are approximations (Nyström, random features).
- Presentation and polish: Several typos and minor grammatical issues, and some missing implementation details (e.g., precise conditioning protocol for generated batches, whether the same x’s are used in both terms, and how perturbations are applied).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The CMMD objective and gradient-based training are sound given standard assumptions from conditional mean embeddings. However, the omission/ambiguity regarding centering and operator assumptions (existence of CY|X in F⊗G, smoothness) should be clarified; otherwise, it is difficult to assess consistency and practical estimator bias.
- Empirical support: Results show the approach works, but do not establish state-of-the-art performance or clear advantages over conditional GANs/CVAEs. The Bayesian distillation experiment is small-scale but encouraging (no degradation vs. PBP on Boston).

Clarity
- Generally readable with helpful context, but the operator estimation section needs a precise statement of whether centering is used, how K̃ is formed, and the exact objects over which gradients are taken. Some typos detract from polish.

Originality
- Extending MMD-based training to conditional distributions via CMMD and integrating it with deep generators is a natural but novel contribution relative to GMMN, cGAN, and CVAE. The operator-based formulation and mini-batch training recipe are of interest.

Significance
- Potentially useful as a simple, stable alternative to adversarial training for conditional generation and as a general-purpose conditional density model. Current experiments suggest moderate significance; stronger comparisons and metrics would increase impact.

Specific comments and questions
- Please clarify the estimator: Are ĈYX and ĈXX centered covariance operators? Are Gram matrices centered in practice (and if so, do the centering matrices depend on model parameters via y)? If uncentered, please justify consistency (e.g., via kernel ridge regression view) and discuss the effect on CMMD.
- Gradients: Since K̃−1 depends only on x, is L (over y) the only term that carries gradients? If centering is applied to L, how is the gradient handled?
- Kernel choices: Specify kernels used for X and Y in each experiment, bandwidth selection procedure, and λ. Provide a sensitivity analysis.
- Complexity: Report wall-clock times vs. cGAN/CVAE, and discuss approximate methods (e.g., Nyström for K̃−1) for larger batches.
- Generation metrics: Add quantitative evaluation of conditional consistency (e.g., train a classifier and report classification accuracy of generated samples conditioned on labels), and comparisons to cGAN/CVAE on MNIST/Yale under matched architectures and data budgets.

Typos and minor issues
- “Frobeniu norm” → “Frobenius norm”; “an universal” → “a universal”; “which the” → “which”.
- Several grammar/spacing issues; suggest a careful proofreading pass.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, simple extension of MMD training to conditional distributions.
- Broad applicability; stable training without adversarial games.
- Bayesian distillation application is interesting and shows no performance loss.

Cons for acceptance
- Predictive performance lags strong baselines; limited evidence of superiority.
- Generative evaluation lacks quantitative metrics and comparisons to cGAN/CVAE.
- Ambiguity in operator estimation (centering) and missing ablations on kernels/λ.

Post-rebuttal update
The authors clarified how centering is handled and how gradients are computed in practice, which addresses my main methodological concern. With these clarifications, I raise my score. However, I still encourage adding quantitative generative comparisons and kernel sensitivity analyses to strengthen the empirical case.

Recommendation
Borderline reject, upgraded after rebuttal due to clarified methodology. The paper is well-motivated and technically interesting; with stronger empirical comparisons and clearer operator estimation details, it could be a solid contribution.
