

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions using conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). The authors build on the kernel mean embedding approach pioneered by Gretton et al. (2012) and Li et al. (2015), generalizing it to conditional settings to model distributions p(y|x) rather than joint distributions p(x,y). The approach is theoretically grounded in Hilbert space embeddings of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009) and develops a CMMD criterion to match the kernel mean embedding of the empirical conditional distribution with that of the generative model. The training procedure leverages backpropagation, making it computationally efficient compared to GANs' min-max optimization.

The paper makes several contributions that advance the state of the art in generative modeling. First, it provides a principled extension of GMMN to conditional settings, addressing a gap in the literature. Second, it demonstrates the versatility of the approach across three application domains: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (digit and face generation conditioned on labels), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The empirical results are generally strong, with CGMMN-CNN achieving competitive error rates on MNIST (1.18%) and SVHN (3.13%) compared to state-of-the-art discriminative methods like DSN (1.92% on SVHN). For generative tasks, the model produces coherent samples conditioned on class labels, and the Bayesian distillation experiment shows that CGMMN can effectively approximate the predictive distribution of a Bayesian model (PBP) on the Boston housing dataset with minimal performance degradation.

The paper's strengths include a clear theoretical foundation, a simple training procedure that avoids the instability issues of GAN training, and a demonstration of practical applicability across multiple tasks. The authors successfully address previous reviewer concerns from the GMMN paper (Li et al., 2015) regarding low-dimensional latent spaces and scalability, as evidenced by the model's performance on challenging tasks. The conditional framework is particularly valuable for applications where modeling p(y|x) directly is more natural than modeling the joint distribution.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that warrant attention. The most significant is Theorem 3, which claims that if CY|X = CZ|X then PY|X = PZ|X, but provides neither a reference nor a proof. This is a critical theoretical gap that should be addressed. The paper also overloads the symbol C (used for conditional embedding operators and elsewhere), which creates confusion. The derivation of Equation (2) is unclear, particularly regarding the dot notation (matrix vs. Hadamard product) and the mapping to the weighted MMD form. While these may be minor notational issues, they undermine the paper's technical precision.

The empirical evaluation, while adequate for demonstrating the approach's viability, has limitations. The Bayesian distillation application is evaluated on a single dataset (Boston housing) with a single teacher model (PBP), making it difficult to assess generality. The supervised learning application, while demonstrating versatility, isn't the strongest use case for the approach—conditional generative modeling is more naturally suited to tasks like image generation conditioned on labels rather than classification. The experiments on MNIST and SVHN, while standard, don't push the method to its limits; stronger results on more challenging datasets like CIFAR-10 would better demonstrate the approach's potential.

In terms of significance, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the generative modeling literature by extending MMD-based methods to conditional settings. The approach provides a simple alternative to conditional GANs that avoids their training instability. While the core idea isn't radically novel, the extension to conditional distributions is a meaningful contribution that fills an important gap in the literature. The paper's most compelling applications are in contextual generation tasks, where the ability to condition on input variables is essential.

The paper is well-written overall, with clear explanations of the theoretical foundations and sufficient detail to reproduce the experiments. However, the theoretical gaps and notational issues mentioned above should be addressed before publication. The authors should clarify Theorem 3, resolve the symbol overload, and provide a clearer derivation of Equation (2). With these improvements, the paper would make a solid contribution to the field.

In summary, this paper presents a technically sound extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with promising empirical results across multiple tasks. The approach is well-motivated and addresses a useful gap in the literature. While the theoretical presentation has some gaps and the empirical evaluation could be strengthened with more challenging datasets, the paper makes a valuable contribution to generative modeling that would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical and notational issues.
