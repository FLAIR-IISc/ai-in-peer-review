### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMNs), a deep generative framework for learning conditional distributions. The work extends the recently proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMNs), which model joint distributions by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model and data distributions. The core contribution of this paper is a novel objective function, termed Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions. This is achieved by calculating the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between the kernel mean embedding operators of the conditional distributions. The CGMMN model itself is a deep neural network that takes a conditional variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to generate a sample `y`. The network's parameters are learned via backpropagation by minimizing the CMMD objective. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach on a range of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (generating labeled MNIST digits and faces from the Yale Face dataset), and distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper presents a technically sound and logical extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. The theoretical motivation for using the CMMD objective is well-founded, building on established work in kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. The experimental evaluation covers a diverse set of applications, demonstrating the flexibility of the proposed framework. The results are competitive with other deep generative models, particularly in the prediction tasks where CGMMN performs on par with max-margin DGMs (CMMVA).

While the paper is generally well-written, the clarity could be improved in a few areas:

1.  **Mathematical Exposition:** The presentation of the CMMD objective in Section 3.1 is quite dense. For readers not deeply familiar with kernel conditional embeddings, the jump from the operator norm `||C_Y|X - C_Z|X||` to the final empirical estimator in Equation (2) may be difficult to follow. A more detailed derivation or an intuitive explanation of the components in Eq. (2) would greatly improve accessibility.

2.  **Reproducibility:** The paper lacks sufficient detail for practitioners to exactly reproduce the reported results. Key hyperparameters, such as the choice of kernels and their parameters (e.g., bandwidth), the regularization parameter λ in the CMMD objective, learning rates, and specific architectural details for all experiments are not fully specified. For a contribution that is largely empirical, these details are crucial. Providing source code would be the most effective way to address this and would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

3.  **Computational Cost:** A potential weakness is the computational complexity of the training procedure. The CMMD objective (Eq. 2) requires inverting kernel matrices for each mini-batch, an operation that scales cubically with the batch size. This could limit the model's scalability. The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational trade-offs, including training runtimes, and a comparison with the costs of alternative methods like conditional GANs or DSNs.

### Originality and Significance

The primary contribution—a deep conditional generative model trained with a CMMD objective—is novel and original. It thoughtfully combines recent advances in deep generative modeling (GMMNs) with more established theory on kernel methods. This work fills a clear gap, providing a moment-matching-based alternative to other prominent conditional frameworks like conditional GANs [21] and conditional VAEs [28].

The significance of this work is substantial. Conditional modeling is central to a vast number of machine learning problems. By extending moment-matching networks to this setting, the authors introduce a method that retains the benefits of GMMNs, such as a stable, non-adversarial training objective, while greatly expanding their applicability. The demonstration on knowledge distillation is particularly interesting and highlights the potential for this framework to be used in novel ways beyond standard prediction and generation tasks. The work is likely to be of interest to researchers in generative modeling, kernel methods, and deep learning, and could inspire further development in non-adversarial training techniques.

### Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Proposes a novel and well-motivated CMMD objective for training deep conditional generative models.
*   **Significance:** Addresses the important problem of conditional density estimation and provides a valuable alternative to adversarial or variational approaches, with a potentially more stable training dynamic.
*   **Versatility:** The model's effectiveness is demonstrated across a diverse and interesting set of tasks (prediction, generation, and knowledge distillation).
*   **Relevance:** The work is a timely and logical next step for moment-matching networks, significantly increasing their practical utility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Computational Concerns:** The scalability of the method is a potential issue due to the matrix inversions in the objective function, and this aspect is not discussed in the paper.
*   **Reproducibility:** The lack of implementation details and source code makes it difficult to reproduce the results and build upon the work.
*   **Limited Scale of Experiments:** While diverse, the experiments are conducted on relatively small-scale datasets. Demonstrating the approach on more challenging, larger-scale benchmarks would make the empirical case more compelling.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting an interesting, novel, and significant contribution to the field of deep generative models. The proposed CGMMN framework is elegant and its utility is well-demonstrated. The primary weaknesses relate to the lack of discussion on computational complexity and insufficient details for reproducibility. Despite these points, the strengths of the paper in terms of originality and significance are compelling. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the concerns regarding computational cost and reproducibility in the final version, ideally by releasing their code.
