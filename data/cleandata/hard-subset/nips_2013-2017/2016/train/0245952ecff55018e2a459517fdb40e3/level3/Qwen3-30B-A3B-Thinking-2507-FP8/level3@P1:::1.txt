

The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions via conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). This addresses a key limitation of prior work (Li et al., ICML 2015) by enabling flexible conditional modeling without adversarial training, while retaining the simplicity of gradient-based optimization. CGMMN leverages kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions (Theorem 2) to define CMMD, which is optimized via backpropagation. The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling (MNIST/SVHN classification), contextual generation (MNIST digits, Yale faces), and Bayesian knowledge distillation (Boston housing regression), demonstrating competitive performance across tasks.

**Strengths:**  
*Originality & Significance:* The work makes a clear and valuable extension to conditional deep generative modeling. While conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., 2015) exist, CGMMN is the first to apply MMD-based training to conditional distributions, avoiding GANs’ instability. This fills a notable gap in the NIPS literature (e.g., GMMN was presented at ICML 2015, but not extended to conditionals at NIPS). The Bayesian dark knowledge application is particularly novel, showing CGMMN can distill complex Bayesian models without performance loss (Table 3), with broader implications for efficient probabilistic inference.  
*Quality:* The theoretical foundation (Theorems 1–3) is sound, and the experimental design aligns well with the problem scope. Results are well-supported: CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error on SVHN (comparable to state-of-the-art CMMVA at 3.09%), and generation tasks (Fig. 2–5) show meaningful class-conditioned samples without data copying (Fig. 3). The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations (e.g., CMMD’s dependence on kernel smoothness).  
*Clarity:* The core idea is clearly articulated, though the mathematical exposition (e.g., Section 3.1) becomes dense. Key contributions are well-motivated by the three application scenarios (predictive modeling, contextual generation, Bayesian distillation), which are standard in NIPS generative modeling work (e.g., Sohn et al., NIPS 2015; Li et al., NIPS 2016).

**Weaknesses:**  
*Reproducibility:* Critical details for replication are missing. The paper lacks specific hyperparameters (e.g., exact CNN layer configurations for SVHN, kernel choices, regularization λ), and implementation details for the Bayesian distillation setup (e.g., "perturbing inputs slightly" in Section 4.3). The claim that "the total number of parameters is comparable" (Section 4.1.1) is unsubstantiated, hindering fair comparison. Reference code should be provided to meet NIPS reproducibility standards.  
*Computational Cost:* Training involves expensive matrix inversions (K̃⁻¹ in Eq. 2), yet runtime comparisons with baselines like DSN (1.92% error on SVHN) are absent. The authors note "the computation cost for kernel gram matrix grows cubically" but do not quantify this impact (e.g., "CGMMN training time: X hours vs. DSN: Y hours"). This is a significant omission for a practical contribution.  
*Scope:* While the tasks (MNIST, SVHN, Yale faces) are standard, they are relatively small-scale compared to recent NIPS generative work (e.g., BigGAN on ImageNet). However, the authors justify this by citing comparable baselines (e.g., DSN, CMMVA), so this is acceptable but warrants a brief acknowledgment.

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a solid theoretical and empirical contribution to conditional generative modeling, with strong relevance to NIPS. The originality is clear, the significance is demonstrated across multiple applications, and the technical quality is high. The primary concerns—reproducibility and computational cost—are fixable with minor revisions. The weaknesses do not undermine the core contribution but require addressing for publication.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel conditional MMD framework; competitive results on predictive/generative tasks; compelling Bayesian distillation application; sound theoretical grounding.  
*Con:* Insufficient implementation details for reproducibility; missing runtime comparisons; small-scale experiments (justified but acknowledged).  

Overall, this is a high-quality NIPS contribution that advances the state of the art in generative modeling. With minor revisions to address reproducibility and computational analysis, it merits acceptance. The work is well-positioned to influence future research on conditional generative models, particularly where stability (vs. GANs) and interpretability (vs. implicit generative models) are valued.
