

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to learn conditional distributions using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (cMMD) criterion. The authors build upon Li et al.'s GMMN (ICML 2015), which uses MMD to learn joint distributions, and extend it to handle conditional distributions p(y|x) by developing a conditional version of MMD. The paper positions CGMMN as a simpler alternative to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) that avoids the min-max optimization challenges of GANs while retaining the advantages of deep learning.

The main contribution is the theoretical development of cMMD as a criterion for learning conditional distributions, along with a practical implementation that uses back-propagation for training. The authors demonstrate the approach on three application areas: predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST and Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge (Boston housing regression). The results show competitive performance across these tasks, particularly in the contextual generation experiments where the model generates coherent samples conditioned on labels.

The paper is reasonably clear overall, though the mathematical exposition can be challenging to follow at times due to the technical nature of kernel embedding theory. The authors provide sufficient theoretical justification for their approach, building upon established work in Hilbert space embeddings (e.g., Smola et al., 2007; Song et al., 2009). The connection between MMD and cMMD is well-articulated in Remark 1, showing how the conditional version adapts the original MMD objective.

The experimental evaluation covers a relevant range of tasks that demonstrate the versatility of the approach. The predictive modeling results on MNIST and SVHN show competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods like CMMVA and DSN. The contextual generation results are particularly compelling, with the model producing coherent samples conditioned on class labels. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is an interesting extension that demonstrates practical utility.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. The experimental tasks, while standard, are somewhat limited to small benchmark datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face), which may not fully demonstrate the method's scalability to more complex generative tasks. The paper lacks a runtime comparison with methods like DSN, which is a strong baseline in the predictive modeling experiments. The training process involves computationally expensive steps (matrix inverses), but the paper does not provide adequate discussion of the computational complexity or scalability concerns.

Reproducibility is a significant concern. The paper lacks sufficient detail for practitioners to exactly reproduce the evaluated models, particularly regarding the implementation of the conditional embedding operator and the specific hyperparameter choices. The authors should provide reference code to strengthen the paper and address this important concern for the machine learning community.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to deep generative modeling by extending MMD-based methods to conditional distributions, which is a relevant and important direction. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the approach is conceptually simple and elegant compared to conditional GANs. The application to Bayesian dark knowledge is particularly interesting as it demonstrates practical utility beyond standard generative tasks.

Strengths: Novel extension of GMMN to conditional distributions, solid theoretical foundation, competitive performance across multiple tasks, simple training process using back-propagation, interesting application to Bayesian dark knowledge.

Weaknesses: Mathematically dense exposition, limited experimental scope to small datasets, insufficient detail for reproduction, no runtime comparison with strong baselines like DSN, computational cost of matrix inverses not adequately addressed.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling by providing a theoretically sound and practically implementable method for learning conditional distributions. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons and better reproducibility details, the core idea is novel and well-executed. The method represents an important step toward making moment-matching networks more applicable to real-world conditional learning tasks.

I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the reproducibility concerns in the camera-ready version by providing reference code and more detailed implementation specifications. The paper advances the state of the art in generative modeling by extending MMD-based methods to handle conditional distributions in a principled and effective manner.
