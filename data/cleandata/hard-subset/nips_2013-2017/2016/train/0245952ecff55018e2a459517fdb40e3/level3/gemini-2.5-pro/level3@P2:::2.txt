This paper presents Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The work is a direct and significant extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which learn joint distributions by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model and data distributions. The key technical contribution of this paper is the formulation of a Conditional MMD (CMMD) objective. Unlike MMD, which compares mean embeddings of distributions, CMMD compares the Hilbert space embedding *operators* of conditional distributions. This allows the model to match the entire family of distributions `p(y|x)` indexed by the conditioning variable `x`. The proposed model is trained via back-propagation and stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness on a diverse set of tasks, including predictive classification on MNIST and SVHN, conditional image generation, and knowledge distillation from Bayesian models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is based on a sound theoretical foundation, leveraging established work on kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions. The experimental results are competitive and demonstrate the model's viability across multiple domains. However, the work feels incomplete due to a lack of crucial details regarding the implementation and computational aspects of the proposed CMMD objective. The claims are generally well-supported by the experiments, but the path from the theory to the reported results is not clear enough for the work to be considered fully reproducible.

**Clarity:** The primary weakness of this paper is its lack of clarity. While the preliminary sections provide a reasonable overview of RKHS and MMD, the presentation of the core contribution—the CMMD objective—is overly formal and lacks intuition.
1.  **Missing Intuition:** The paper fails to provide a high-level, intuitive explanation of the CMMD objective function. The non-conditional MMD objective has a simple interpretation as minimizing the distance between the mean feature representations of two datasets. The conditional version in Equation (2) involves weighting the output Gram matrices (`Ld`, `Ls`, `Lds`) by complex terms involving the inverse of the input Gram matrices (`K̃⁻¹`). The paper does not explain what this re-weighting achieves intuitively. A conceptual explanation of how conditioning on `x` translates to this specific mathematical form would greatly aid understanding.
2.  **Opaque Algorithm:** The description of the practical algorithm is insufficient. Equation (2) is presented as the final objective without derivation or justification. It is not clear how this equation is derived from the definition of the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between empirical conditional embedding operators. Furthermore, the paper does not explain how the gradient of `L̂_CMMD` with respect to the network parameters `w` is computed for the mini-batch algorithm. This level of detail is essential for a reader to understand the method and reproduce the experiments. The reference to the appendix does not sufficiently resolve this, as the provided details can be difficult to follow.
3.  **Computational Concerns:** The objective in Equation (2) requires inverting `(batch_size x batch_size)` kernel matrices, an operation with cubic complexity. The paper reports using mini-batch sizes up to 500, which would make this inversion computationally expensive. There is no discussion of this potential bottleneck or how it was addressed.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. Extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. While conditional versions of other generative models like GANs exist, this paper is the first to propose a principled framework for conditional density estimation based on moment-matching of conditional distribution operators. The formulation of the CMMD objective is novel.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important and highly relevant problem. Conditional models are fundamental to a vast range of machine learning applications, including structured prediction, contextual generation, and reinforcement learning. By providing a stable, non-adversarial training objective for deep conditional generative models, this work has the potential to be widely used by both researchers and practitioners. The demonstrated application to distilling knowledge from complex Bayesian models is particularly interesting and highlights the model's versatility.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and principled method (CGMMN) for an important problem (conditional density modeling), extending the popular moment-matching framework.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The model demonstrates competitive performance across a diverse and challenging set of tasks, highlighting its flexibility and effectiveness.
*   **Non-Adversarial Training:** Like GMMN, this method avoids the instability issues often associated with training GANs, offering a potentially more robust alternative.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Lack of Clarity:** The paper's core contribution—the CMMD objective and its implementation—is poorly explained, lacking both intuition and sufficient technical detail for reproducibility.
*   **Unclear Computational Feasibility:** The paper fails to address the significant computational cost implied by the objective function, raising questions about the scalability and practicality of the method as presented.

### Request for Clarification

To improve the paper and address the major concerns, I would ask the authors to clarify the following two points in their rebuttal and a revised manuscript:
1.  Please provide a brief, intuitive derivation of Equation (2). Specifically, explain how the terms involving `K̃⁻¹` act as a form of conditional weighting on the output space distances.
2.  Please provide a clear, step-by-step description of how the gradient of the mini-batch CMMD objective is computed with respect to the generator's parameters, and discuss the computational complexity of this procedure.
