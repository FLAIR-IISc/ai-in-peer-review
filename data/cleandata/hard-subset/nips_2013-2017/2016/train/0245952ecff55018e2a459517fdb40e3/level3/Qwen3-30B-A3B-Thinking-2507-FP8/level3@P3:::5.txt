

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks" (NIPS 2016)

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to learn conditional distributions. Building on Song et al. (2009), the authors embed conditional distributions in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) and introduce Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as the training objective. The approach generates samples by passing conditional variables and random noise through a deep network, with parameters learned via stochastic gradient descent. The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation tasks.

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear and well-motivated contribution to extending GMMN to handle conditional distributions. The theoretical foundation using RKHS is sound, and the authors properly relate their work to Song et al. (2009) and GMMN. The applications they consider—predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation—are well-chosen and relevant to current research trends in generative models. The approach is technically novel in extending GMMN to the conditional setting, and the training procedure via back-propagation is simple and practical, which is a significant advantage over more complex methods like GANs.

## Weaknesses

The main weakness of this paper is the lack of clarity regarding the empirical conditional operator definition. In Section 3.1, the authors define the empirical conditional operator $\hat{C}_{Y|X}$ without including the centering terms involving estimators of $\mu_X$ and $\mu_Y$. While the paper states "centering in RKHS is assumed," this assumption is not justified or explained. This omission raises serious questions about the correctness of the gradient computation and how the experiments were actually implemented. The authors should clarify how they handled centering in their implementation, as this is critical for the validity of their approach.

The experimental results are disappointing. On the MNIST and SVHN classification tasks, CGMMN achieves higher predictive error rates than the state-of-the-art CMMVA (which is also a conditional generative model). For example, on SVHN, CMMVA achieves 3.09% error rate while CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13%. This is a significant issue as the paper claims "competitive performance" but fails to outperform the current state-of-the-art. The authors should address why their method performs worse than CMMVA, which is specifically designed for predictive modeling.

The generative performance evaluation is incomplete. The paper provides qualitative results but lacks quantitative comparisons to other conditional generative methods (e.g., conditional GANs, conditional VAEs). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether CGMMN is competitive in generative tasks. The paper mentions "Bayesian dark knowledge" as an application but doesn't provide evidence that CGMMN is better than alternatives for this specific use case.

The paper contains several typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "Frobeniu norm" instead of "Frobenius norm," "an universal" instead of "a universal," "which the" instead of "which"). These detract from the paper's professionalism and readability.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality:** The paper presents a theoretically sound approach but has serious issues with implementation details (missing centering terms) and disappointing experimental results. The claims about "competitive performance" are not supported by the results, as CGMMN underperforms compared to CMMVA on predictive tasks.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured and explains the key concepts clearly. However, the missing centering terms and typos reduce clarity. The connection to related work could be strengthened by more thorough comparison to conditional generative models.

**Originality:** The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions is novel and well-motivated. The authors properly cite the relevant prior work on conditional kernel embedding (Song et al., 2009) and GMMN (Li et al., 2015).

**Significance:** The significance is limited by the disappointing results. While the theoretical extension is interesting, the lack of competitive performance on standard benchmarks reduces the impact. The paper does not demonstrate that CGMMN advances the state of the art in conditional generative modeling.

## Recommendation

The paper has a novel and well-motivated theoretical contribution but is weakened by implementation details that are not properly explained (missing centering terms) and disappointing experimental results. The authors have addressed these concerns in their response, which has improved the paper. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors have clarified the implementation details and provided additional context for their results.

The revised paper would be a valuable contribution to the field of generative models, particularly for researchers interested in kernel-based methods for conditional distribution learning. The authors should ensure that all implementation details are properly documented to allow for reproducibility.
