## Review of Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

This paper presents Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) to the conditional setting. The authors aim to learn conditional distributions given input variables using a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The approach leverages kernel mean embeddings to avoid distributional assumptions and is trained via back-propagation. The method is evaluated on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation tasks.

The core idea of extending GMMNs to the conditional setting is well-motivated and represents a natural progression of the work. The theoretical foundation, building upon kernel methods and the work of Song et al. (2009) on embedding conditional distributions, is sound. The proposed CMMD criterion provides a theoretically grounded approach to learning these conditional models. The application to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation is also interesting and demonstrates a potential use case beyond standard generative modeling.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. A significant concern arises from the definition of the empirical conditional operator  $\hat{C}_{Y|X}$. The authors omit terms involving estimators of $\mu_X$ and $\mu_Y$ when computing this operator, seemingly assuming centering in the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This assumption is not adequately justified. The lack of explicit centering raises questions about the correctness of the gradient computation and how the experimental setup handled potential biases resulting from this omission. Clarification on this point is crucial. While the authors state in the related work that a similar observation has been made in Gretton et al. (2007) regarding asymptotic behavior, it doesn’t address the immediate impact on the practical implementation.

Furthermore, the experimental results are somewhat disappointing. On the MNIST and SVHN datasets for predictive modeling, CGMMN exhibits higher error rates compared to state-of-the-art methods like CMMVA (Li et al., 2016). While the authors demonstrate that the method is competitive, it doesn’t surpass existing techniques.  The generative performance evaluation lacks strong baselines.  While images are shown, there is no comparison to other conditional generative models such as conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) or conditional variational autoencoders (Sohn et al., 2015), making it difficult to assess the novelty and effectiveness of the generated samples.  A qualitative comparison would be valuable. The claim that generated samples aren't merely copies of the training data is supported by nearest neighbor visualization, but this metric is not commonly used to evaluate generative models and lacks robustness.

The paper also contains several minor typographical errors (e.g., "Frobeniu norm," "an universal," "which the") that detract from its polish.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The method is built on a solid theoretical foundation using kernel methods and RKHS embeddings.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The approach is applicable to a range of tasks, including predictive modeling, generation, and knowledge distillation.
*   **Simplicity:**  The training process via backpropagation maintains the relative simplicity of GMMNs.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Missing Centering:** The omission of centering terms in the conditional operator definition is a significant concern and requires clarification.
*   **Disappointing Experimental Results:** The predictive performance is not competitive with state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Weak Generative Evaluation:**  Lack of comparison to relevant conditional generative models.
*   **Typos:** Presence of minor typographical errors.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in generative modeling – learning conditional distributions.
*   The theoretical framework is sound and builds on established kernel methods.
*   The approach demonstrates potential for various applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of clarity regarding the centering terms raises concerns about the correctness and potential biases of the implementation.
*   The experimental results do not demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The generative evaluation lacks sufficient comparisons to establish the quality of the generated samples.



**Overall Assessment:**

I initially rated this paper as a weak reject due to the concerns regarding the omitted centering terms and the limited empirical performance. However, following a response from the authors addressing the centering issue with a justification and providing additional experimental comparisons, I am revising my assessment. The authors convincingly argue that the practical impact of omitting centering is minimal in their setup and provide evidence supporting this claim. Furthermore, they have added a comparison to conditional GANs, albeit limited, showing more competitive results.

Despite the remaining minor weaknesses, the core contribution of extending GMMNs to the conditional setting remains valuable, and the authors have adequately addressed the major concerns.

**Rating:**  Accept (Weak Accept)
