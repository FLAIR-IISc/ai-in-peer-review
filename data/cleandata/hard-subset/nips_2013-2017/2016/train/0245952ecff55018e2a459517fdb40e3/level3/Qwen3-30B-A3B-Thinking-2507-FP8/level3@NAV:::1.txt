

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to learn conditional distributions using conditional maximum mean discrepancy (cMMD). The authors propose a simple generative process that draws samples from a simple distribution and passes them through a deep network with conditional inputs, then optimizes the cMMD objective via back-propagation. The method is evaluated across three tasks: predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN classification), contextual generation (MNIST and Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

The paper makes a relevant contribution to deep generative modeling by extending MMD-based approaches to conditional settings, addressing an important gap in the literature. The work builds logically on GMMN [19] and conditional kernel methods [29], while offering a simpler training procedure compared to conditional GANs [21].

Strengths include the clear theoretical foundation for the cMMD criterion, the demonstration of competitive performance across diverse tasks, and the practical applicability to Bayesian knowledge distillation. The paper effectively connects to relevant prior work in the introduction and related work sections.

However, several weaknesses warrant attention. The mathematical exposition is sometimes difficult to follow, particularly in the conditional embedding sections (Section 2.3). The experimental scope is limited to small benchmark datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face), though this is comparable to recent work. The computational cost of training is not adequately addressed; the authors should provide runtime comparisons with methods like DSN [17], which they reference but do not compare against. Reproducibility is a significant concern - the model description lacks sufficient detail for exact replication, and reference code is not provided.

The paper demonstrates technical soundness with well-supported claims, but the lack of code and insufficient detail about the implementation make it difficult to fully assess the quality of the experimental results. While the method is original and addresses an important problem, the significance is somewhat limited by the modest performance gains over strong baselines and the small-scale experiments.

Overall, this is a promising extension to generative modeling with clear theoretical motivation, but the paper requires substantial revisions to address reproducibility concerns and provide more comprehensive experimental comparisons before it can be accepted.
