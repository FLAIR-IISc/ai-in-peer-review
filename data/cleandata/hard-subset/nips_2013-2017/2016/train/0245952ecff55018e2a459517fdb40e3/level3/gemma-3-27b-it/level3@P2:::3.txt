This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional distributions using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The work extends the Generative Moment Matching Network (GMMN) approach, previously applied to joint distributions, to scenarios where generation is conditioned on input variables. The authors demonstrate CGMMN's applicability across predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, providing empirical results on MNIST, SVHN, and Yale Face datasets.

The core idea of leveraging kernel mean embeddings to avoid distributional assumptions, as in GMMN, is a significant strength. Extending this to the conditional setting through CMMD is a natural and valuable contribution. The ability to train the model via backpropagation simplifies implementation and enables scaling to larger datasets. The paper effectively positions CGMMN within the landscape of deep generative models, drawing connections to GANs, VAEs, and other related approaches. The diverse set of experiments, while not groundbreaking in performance on each individual task, collectively demonstrate the model's versatility. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is particularly interesting, showing a potential for efficient knowledge transfer from complex Bayesian models.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The derivation and explanation of the CMMD objective, particularly the transition from Equation (2) to the described weighted MMD form, is unclear and requires greater detail. The notation uses dot notation, which could represent either matrix multiplication or a Hadamard product; clarification is crucial. The symbol ‘C’ is significantly overloaded, representing both the conditional embedding operator and other quantities, causing confusion. Theorem 3 presents a crucial statement regarding the equivalence of conditional distributions based on operator equality but lacks a citation or a proof. Providing either a reference to existing work establishing this result or a concise proof would significantly strengthen the theoretical foundation of the work.

While the empirical results are competitive, they don’t dramatically outperform state-of-the-art methods. The predictive modeling results on MNIST and SVHN are solid, but the comparisons often involve models with different architectural choices, making direct comparison difficult. The generation experiments, while visually appealing, lack quantitative evaluation metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) that are standard in the generative modeling literature. The Bayesian distillation experiment is limited to a single dataset and a single teacher model (PBP), hindering conclusions about the generalizability of this application. The authors acknowledge these limitations to some extent but addressing them would bolster the paper’s impact.

In terms of originality, the work builds upon established concepts like kernel methods and MMD, but the application to conditional generative modeling and the development of CMMD represent a novel contribution. The paper adequately references relevant prior work, although a more thorough discussion of existing conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs, conditional VAEs) would be beneficial. The significance of the work lies in providing a potentially simpler and more stable alternative to GAN-based conditional generative models. The theoretical underpinnings and the backpropagation-friendly training process are appealing features that could attract researchers interested in exploring moment matching approaches.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of GMMN to the conditional setting.
*   Simple and efficient training via backpropagation.
*   Demonstrated applicability to diverse tasks.
*   Interesting application to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.
*   Solid experimental validation on standard datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in the derivation of the CMMD objective.
*   Overloaded notation and missing proof for Theorem 3.
*   Limited quantitative evaluation of generative performance.
*   Narrow scope of the Bayesian distillation experiment.
*   Performance improvements are not substantial compared to state-of-the-art.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling. While the paper suffers from some clarity issues and lacks rigorous quantitative evaluation in certain areas, the core idea of CGMMN and the development of CMMD are significant. I believe the paper, with revisions addressing the aforementioned weaknesses, is worthy of acceptance. Specifically, clarifying the CMMD derivation, providing a citation or proof for Theorem 3, and adding quantitative evaluation metrics for the generation tasks would significantly strengthen the paper. A more thorough discussion of related conditional generative models would also be helpful.
