

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to learn conditional distributions. The authors propose a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion that measures the difference between kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, enabling training via standard backpropagation. The approach is evaluated on predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (conditional image generation), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

The core contribution is the CMMD formulation, which allows matching conditional distributions without requiring per-condition data samples. This addresses a key limitation of naive approaches (training separate GMMNs for each conditional value), which suffer from data sparsity, especially with continuous conditioning variables. The paper demonstrates that CGMMN can directly match the conditional embedding operator, avoiding the need for per-condition model training. The formulation is mathematically clean, with the CMMD objective computable using kernel matrices, and the K matrices in Eq. (2) are independent of model parameters, enabling standard backpropagation.

The paper's strengths include a theoretically sound approach that extends GMMN to conditional settings, a clear formulation of CMMD that avoids the pitfalls of per-condition training, and strong empirical results across multiple tasks. The classification results on MNIST and SVHN demonstrate competitive performance with state-of-the-art discriminative models, while the conditional generation results on MNIST and Yale Face show meaningful samples conditioned on labels. The Bayesian dark knowledge application shows CGMMN can effectively distill predictive distributions from Bayesian models without performance degradation.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The presentation of the conditional embedding operator in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is difficult to follow without consulting the referenced papers [29, 28], which is problematic for readers unfamiliar with this specialized topic. There is a minor but important error in stating that the MMD estimator is unbiased; it is actually asymptotically unbiased. Additionally, the paper's definition of the conditional embedding operator differs from [29], implying a different cross-covariance operator definition is needed, which creates confusion.

A significant concern is the practical implementation of CMMD. The CMMD objective requires computing matrix inverses of kernel matrices, which scales cubically with sample size. This creates a tension between using sufficiently large minibatches to reduce stochastic noise and avoiding computational infeasibility. The paper acknowledges this tradeoff but doesn't adequately address how to resolve it in practice, especially for large-scale applications.

The experimental design has one notable omission: the authors don't compare against the naive baseline of training separate GMMNs for each conditional value. While the paper correctly identifies that this approach suffers from data sparsity, especially with continuous conditioning, a comparison would strengthen the argument for CGMMN's advantages. The paper mentions this baseline in the key points but doesn't include it in the experiments.

In terms of quality, the theoretical foundation is sound, though the presentation could be clearer. The experimental results are well-conducted and demonstrate the approach's versatility across different tasks. The significance is strong, as conditional generative models are increasingly important in applications like image generation with class labels, predictive modeling, and knowledge distillation. The approach directly addresses limitations of previous methods and provides a unified framework for conditional generation.

Originality is solid, as the paper successfully extends the MMD-based generative modeling framework to conditional settings in a novel way. While conditional GANs exist [21], this work provides a simpler alternative using moment matching that avoids min-max optimization. The paper appropriately references related work, though the presentation of the conditional embedding operator could be better aligned with [29].

Clarity is the paper's weakest aspect. The mathematical presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is dense and requires consulting external references for full understanding. The paper would benefit from a clearer exposition of the conditional embedding operator and a more intuitive explanation of how CMMD differs from MMD.

Pro: Clear extension of GMMN to conditional settings; theoretically sound; strong empirical results across multiple tasks; avoids per-condition data sparsity issues; simple training via backpropagation.

Con: Difficult presentation of key mathematical concepts; minor error about MMD estimator being unbiased; no comparison to naive baseline of per-condition GMMNs; practical implementation concerns regarding matrix inverses and minibatch size.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with practical potential. The paper makes a meaningful advancement in conditional generative modeling by extending the MMD framework. While the presentation could be improved, the core idea is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated across multiple domains. The minor issues with presentation and the missing baseline comparison don't undermine the fundamental contribution. I recommend acceptance.
