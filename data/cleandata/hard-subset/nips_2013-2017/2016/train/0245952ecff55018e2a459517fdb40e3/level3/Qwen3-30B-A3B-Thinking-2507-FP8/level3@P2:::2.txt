

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to the conditional setting. The authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to learn conditional distributions, building on the work of Li et al. (ICML 2015) on GMMN and the kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML 2009; Gretton et al., JMLR 2012). The core contribution is extending moment matching to conditional distributions, enabling applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper addresses an important gap in generative modeling literature by extending moment matching to conditional distributions, which is non-trivial compared to simpler single-example conditioning approaches.

2. The experimental evaluation covers multiple relevant applications (predictive modeling on MNIST/SVHN, contextual generation on MNIST and Yale Face, and Bayesian dark knowledge on Boston housing), demonstrating competitive performance across diverse tasks.

3. The authors effectively demonstrate that CGMMN can achieve strong results without requiring complex training procedures, as the method can be trained via standard backpropagation.

4. The connection to Bayesian dark knowledge (Korattikara et al., NIPS 2015) provides a compelling application case where the conditional modeling capability is essential.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Clarity issues:** The paper begins with heavy formal math without providing an intuitive explanation of the conditional objective. The core difficulty of matching distributions over large mini-batches of outputs to many different conditional inputs (where distributions differ across conditionals) is not adequately explained. The transition from theoretical formulation to practical implementation is unclear.

2. **Lack of explanation for Equation (2):** The key optimization objective (Equation 2) is presented without sufficient derivation, assumptions, or practical implications. The paper fails to explain how this expression relates to the conditional embedding operators introduced earlier, making it difficult to understand the practical meaning of the optimization target.

3. **Insufficient algorithmic details:** The path from Equation (2) to the implemented algorithm is insufficiently detailed. The paper claims the approach is simple to implement via backpropagation, but provides minimal guidance on how this actually works in practice. The supplemental material is hard to follow and doesn't adequately address this gap.

4. **Comparison with conditional GANs:** The paper mentions conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) but fails to clearly articulate how CGMMN differs from or improves upon these approaches. The novelty relative to existing conditional generative models isn't sufficiently established.

5. **Computational efficiency claims:** While the paper states the method is computationally efficient, it doesn't provide concrete analysis of computational complexity or practical runtime comparisons with other conditional generative models.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The conditional extension of moment matching networks is a valuable contribution to the generative modeling literature.
- The empirical results across multiple tasks demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.
- The method is conceptually simple and can be implemented with standard deep learning frameworks.

*Con:*
- The paper lacks sufficient clarity in explaining the core technical contribution, making it difficult to evaluate the novelty and correctness of the approach.
- The mathematical presentation is overly formal without sufficient intuitive explanation, hindering understanding of the conditional mechanism.
- The connection to existing work on conditional generative models (particularly conditional GANs) is not adequately established.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting extension of moment matching networks to the conditional setting with promising empirical results. However, the lack of clarity in explaining the core technical contribution significantly undermines the paper's impact. The mathematical formulation is presented without sufficient intuition, and the path from theory to implementation is unclear. For a conference like NIPS, which values both theoretical rigor and clear exposition, these weaknesses are particularly problematic.

The authors should provide a more intuitive explanation of the CMMD objective, including a step-by-step derivation of Equation (2) and a clear mapping to the practical algorithm. A comparison with conditional GANs would also help establish the novelty of their approach. Without these clarifications, it's difficult to assess whether the conditional moment matching approach offers meaningful advantages over existing methods.

While the experimental results are promising, the paper's contribution would be stronger if it were better positioned within the literature and if the technical details were presented with greater clarity. Given the current state of the manuscript, I would recommend rejection due to the clarity issues that prevent readers from fully understanding and evaluating the contribution.
