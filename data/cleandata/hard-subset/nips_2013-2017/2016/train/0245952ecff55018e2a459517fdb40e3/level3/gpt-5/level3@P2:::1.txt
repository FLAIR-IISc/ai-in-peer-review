Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative modeling framework trained by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective. Building on kernel mean embeddings and previous MMD-based generative models (GMMN; Dziugaite et al., 2015; Li et al., 2015), the paper extends the moment-matching approach from joint to conditional distributions using the conditional mean embedding operator (Song et al., 2009/2013). The model concatenates conditioning variables x with auxiliary noise h and maps them through a deep network to produce y ~ p(y|x). The CMMD objective measures the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between estimated conditional embedding operators from data and the model, and can be optimized by backpropagation.

The method is positioned among deep conditional generative models such as conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; LAPGAN, Denton et al., 2015) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), while avoiding min–max optimization and amortized variational bounds. Empirically, CGMMN is evaluated on classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and “Bayesian dark knowledge” (distilling predictions from probabilistic backpropagation; Korattikara et al., 2015; Hernandez-Lobato & Adams, 2015). Results are competitive with prior MMD-based models and close to max-margin DGMs (Li et al., 2016) on classification, and qualitatively reasonable for conditional image generation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The extension from MMD to CMMD is well-motivated via conditional mean embeddings. The empirical CMMD objective (Eq. 2) follows from standard operator estimators with Tikhonov regularization. The observation that K̃−1 for x does not depend on model parameters is correct and simplifies gradients.
- Assumptions: Theoretical guarantees require smoothness conditions so that CY|X ∈ F ⊗ G; the paper rightly notes these may not strictly hold and uses regularized empirical operators. It would help to state the precise kernels used on x and y and the bandwidth/regularization selection strategy.
- Experiments: Classification on MNIST/SVHN is standard and comparisons are fair (no augmentation/multi-crop), though the tasks are small by current standards. Conditional generation is shown qualitatively; combining CGMMN with an autoencoder improves samples, consistent with prior GMMN work. The Bayesian distillation experiment on Boston Housing shows no degradation vs PBP in RMSE, but lacks uncertainty or speed comparisons.
- Efficiency: CMMD requires Gram matrices and inverses per minibatch. Even if K for x can be reused within a batch (and potentially across iterations if the same x are reused), the O(b^3) inversion cost can be material. A runtime/throughput comparison to strong discriminative baselines (e.g., DSN) and to cGAN/CVAE would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, but several derivations are terse. Eq. (2) would benefit from a clearer derivation, dimension annotations, and explicit definitions of kernels for x and y (e.g., Gaussian kernel on images? linear kernel on one-hot labels? kernel on logits vs probabilities for classification).
- Algorithm 1 is helpful; please specify Adam settings, learning rate schedule, number of epochs, early stopping criteria, and kernel hyperparameter selection (median heuristic? cross-validation?).
- The model section should detail network architectures sufficiently for reproduction (filter sizes/strides/padding, normalization layers, activation functions per layer, noise dimensionality and distribution, concatenation vs corruption variants).

Originality
- Conceptually, extending GMMN to conditional distributions via conditional mean embeddings is natural but nontrivial; the CMMD formulation and its practical backprop-compatible estimator are a meaningful contribution. Relative to cGAN/CVAE, CGMMN provides a new training criterion that avoids adversarial dynamics and variational bounds. To my knowledge, a conditional MMD objective in this precise form has not been explored in deep conditional generation prior to this work.

Significance
- The paper broadens the applicability of MMD-based DGMs to conditional tasks, which are central in modern ML (prediction, conditional generation, knowledge distillation). The results are competitive but do not set new SOTA; significance hinges on the methodological simplicity and stability benefits. Wider impact would be clearer with stronger quantitative generation metrics (e.g., class-conditional log-likelihood proxies, Parzen score used in GMMN, or a kernel two-sample test on held-out conditionals), and runtime/scaling evidence.

Pros
- Clean, principled extension of MMD training to conditional models using conditional mean embeddings.
- Simple sampling/training pipeline; avoids adversarial min–max.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Competitive classification results relative to prior DGMs; reasonable conditional samples.

Cons
- Computational cost of CMMD (Gram matrix inversions) not characterized; no runtime comparison to strong baselines.
- Experimental scope is modest (small datasets; limited quantitative evaluation of conditional generation).
- Reproducibility: missing architecture hyperparameters, kernel choices, and training details; no code link.
- Limited analysis/ablation: sensitivity to kernel/bandwidth/λ, minibatch size, noise dimension, and choice of combining x and h.

Questions for the authors
1. What kernels were used for x and y in each task (Gaussian with which bandwidths; linear for labels vs logits)? How were bandwidths and λ selected?
2. What is the per-iteration and total training time for MNIST/SVHN compared to DSN, CMMVA, cGAN, and CVAE on the same hardware? What minibatch sizes were used in all experiments, and did you cache K̃−1 across iterations?
3. For classification, do you train with the same x for both “data” and “model” sets (so Kd = Ks), and if so, can you reuse inverses and reduce cost?
4. For Bayesian distillation, how many y samples per x from the teacher were used? What was the input perturbation distribution? Can you report predictive log-likelihood and calibration (e.g., NLL, reliability diagrams), and speedups at test time?
5. For generation, can you provide quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen window log-likelihood on codes or images, CMMD on a held-out condition set) and comparisons to cGAN/CVAE on conditional MNIST?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add precise implementation details and release reference code to ensure reproducibility.
- Provide runtime and memory profiling of CMMD training; explore low-rank/Random Fourier features or blockwise solvers to reduce O(b^3) cost.
- Include ablations on kernel choice, λ, minibatch size, noise dim, and x–h combination (concatenation vs corruption).
- Expand quantitative evaluation for conditional generation and uncertainty quality in distillation.
- Clarify theoretical assumptions behind Theorem 3 and discuss necessity vs sufficiency; include a brief proof sketch in the appendix.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The method is a clear, relevant extension of MMD-based DGMs to the conditional setting, with a principled objective and competitive results. Addressing efficiency, reporting runtimes, and improving reproducibility would substantially strengthen the contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
