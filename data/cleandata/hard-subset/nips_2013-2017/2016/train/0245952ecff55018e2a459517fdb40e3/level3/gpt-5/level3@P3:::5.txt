Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to conditional generative modeling by defining Conditional GMMNs (CGMMNs) trained with a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The key idea is to embed conditional distributions P(Y|X) in an RKHS and to learn a generator y = f(x, h; w) that, conditioned on x and noise h, matches the empirical conditional mean embedding operator CY|X estimated from data. The CMMD criterion is the Hilbert–Schmidt norm of the difference between conditional embedding operators, building on the conditional mean embedding framework of Song et al. (ICML 2009) and subsequent work (e.g., Grunewälder et al., ICML 2012). Empirically, the paper evaluates CGMMN on classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional image generation (MNIST digits, Extended Yale Face B), and “Bayesian dark knowledge” by distilling predictions from a Bayesian network (PBP) on Boston Housing. The proposed objective is optimized by backpropagation with minibatches.

Quality
- Technical correctness and rigor: The derivation of CMMD as a distance between conditional mean embedding operators is well-grounded in the RKHS literature. However, the paper’s definition of the empirical conditional operator ĈY|X omits explicit centering terms (estimators of μX and μY). The theory defines CXY as a cross-covariance operator requiring centered features, while the paper uses Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ without clarifying whether Φ and Υ (or the Gram matrices) are centered in practice. This omission matters: (i) without centering, the estimator aligns cross-moments rather than cross-covariances and may implicitly assume zero-mean features or a bias term; (ii) the gradients through the CMMD objective can change under centering since L and K become H-centered (H = I − 11⊤/n). Please explicitly state whether you center in feature space (i.e., use HΦ and HΥ or the centered Gram matrices HKH and HLH), how this is implemented per minibatch, and whether K̃−1 and the centering matrices are treated as constants during backprop. If you do not center, please justify theoretically (e.g., equivalence to kernel ridge regression with bias) and discuss the impact on consistency of ĈY|X.
- Practical details: The objective in Eq. (2) depends critically on kernel choices and hyperparameters (kernel for X and Y, bandwidth selection, and λ). The paper should report how these were chosen (e.g., median heuristic, cross-validation), sensitivity analyses, and whether kernels were normalized. For discrete labels, specify the kernel (e.g., delta kernel) and how C−1
XX is computed/regularized.
- Computational aspects: Since CMMD requires inverting K̃ per batch, discuss numerical stability, λ selection, and complexity. Consider reporting alternatives like Nyström or random Fourier features to scale beyond minibatches.
- Experiments: While CGMMN is competitive, predictive results are not clearly superior to existing conditional deep generative models. On MNIST and SVHN, CGMMN-CNN yields slightly higher error than the max-margin DGM CMMVA; there is also a notable gap to strong discriminative baselines (e.g., DSN). For conditional generation, results are visually plausible, and the autoencoder+CGMMN variant yields cleaner samples, but the evaluation lacks quantitative or comparative baselines. In particular, comparisons to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015) are absent. Please include quantitative metrics (e.g., conditional classifier accuracy on generated samples, diversity measures, or log-likelihood proxies used in prior work) and side-by-side comparisons to CGAN/CVAE on the same conditional tasks.
- Bayesian distillation: The Boston Housing experiment is promising: the distilled CGMMN matches PBP’s RMSE. It would be stronger to show additional datasets, calibration metrics (NLL/CRPS), and wall-clock/throughput improvements at inference.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and gives helpful intuition (Remark 1). However:
  - Clarify the centering issue and provide explicit empirical formulas for ĈXY, ĈXX, and ĈY|X (e.g., with H).
  - Specify kernels and hyperparameters for each experiment.
  - Explain the stochastic gradient computation under CMMD (which terms are differentiated; which are precomputed per batch; any moving-average estimate of Gram matrices).
- Typos and minor issues: “Frobeniu norm” → “Frobenius norm”; “an universal” → “a universal”; “which the” appears in several places; “convoluational” → “convolutional”; consistent notation for superscripts s/d.

Originality
- The combination of conditional mean embeddings with deep generators and training via CMMD is, to my knowledge, novel and well-motivated. It extends GMMN from joint to conditional modeling in a principled way and connects elegantly to Song et al. (2009). While conditional deep generative modeling has strong contemporaries (CGAN, CVAE), using RKHS operator distances as the training objective for conditional generation appears new.

Significance
- If made robust and well-evaluated, CGMMN could be a useful alternative to adversarial or variational training for conditional generation, with potential advantages in stability and simplicity. However, the current empirical evidence is mixed: predictive performance does not surpass the strongest conditional DGMs; the generative evaluation lacks comparisons to established conditional methods; and scalability beyond minibatches is not demonstrated. Addressing these will increase impact.

Pros
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMN to conditional modeling via operator distances.
- Simple training by backprop; no adversarial min-max.
- Broad applicability (prediction, conditional synthesis, Bayesian distillation).
- Promising result on Bayesian distillation without loss in RMSE.

Cons
- Ambiguity around centering of conditional operators and its implications for gradients and consistency.
- Predictive accuracy trails state-of-the-art CMMVA and discriminative CNNs.
- Generative evaluation lacks quantitative metrics and comparisons to CGAN/CVAE.
- Limited ablations on kernel choices, λ, batch size; limited scalability discussion.
- Several typos.

Questions for the authors
1) Do you center Φ, Υ (or the Gram matrices) when estimating ĈY|X? If yes, provide formulas and clarify gradient handling; if no, please justify and discuss bias.
2) What kernels and bandwidth-selection strategies were used for X and Y across tasks? How sensitive are results?
3) How does CMMD compare to MMD-on-joint baselines for conditional tasks? Please add an ablation.
4) Can you add comparisons to CGAN and CVAE for conditional generation (with quantitative metrics)?
5) Can you report calibration (NLL) and inference-time speed for the distillation task?

Recommendation
Before rebuttal: borderline reject due to missing clarifications on centering/gradients and insufficient empirical comparisons for conditional generation. After rebuttal: the authors addressed the centering/gradient issues and clarified experimental details; I will raise my scores accordingly. With added comparisons and clarifications, I view the paper as a solid, novel contribution.
