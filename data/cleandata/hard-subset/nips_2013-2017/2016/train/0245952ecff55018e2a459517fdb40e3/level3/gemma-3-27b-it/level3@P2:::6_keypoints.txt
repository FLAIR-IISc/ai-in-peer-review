- Proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) that extend GMMN to learn p(y|x) using a conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion.  
- Empirically evaluated on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation, showing competitive performance.  
- Motivation is well-articulated: direct conditional modeling is preferable to joint modeling in many applications.  
- Positions the work relative to conditional generative models (Conditional GANs, CVAEs) and offers CMMD as a non-adversarial, potentially more stable alternative.  
- Clear mathematical formulation of the CMMD objective and practical implementation via backpropagation.  
- Efficient CMMD computation leverages kernel tricks and avoids per-condition training required by naive GMMN application.  
- Strong experimental results on MNIST, SVHN, and Bayesian teacher-student distillation demonstrating practical utility.  
- Presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is dense and needs more intuitive explanations for accessibility.  
- Incorrect claim that the MMD estimator is unbiased; it is asymptotically unbiased and this should be clarified.  
- Slight inconsistency in the conditional embedding operator definition compared to Song et al. (2013); requires justification.  
- Concern about computational cost of CMMD (matrix inversions) for large mini-batches; mitigation strategies (approximate inverses, low-rank methods) are not sufficiently discussed.  
- Lacks empirical comparison to a naive baseline of training separate GMMNs per condition; even simple experiments (e.g., MNIST classes) would strengthen the claim.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated extension; clear CMMD formulation; efficient implementation; competitive results and addresses data-sparsity issues.  
- Arguments against acceptance: readability issues, minor technical inaccuracies, missing naive-baseline comparison, and limited discussion of computational optimizations.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity, correct technical inaccuracies, justify the operator definition, add (or discuss) baseline comparisons, and briefly address computational optimizations.
