Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) from modeling joint distributions to conditional generation. The core idea is to replace the unconditional MMD objective with a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) that measures the Hilbert–Schmidt distance between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X from the data and from the model. The conditional model is a neural generator y = f(x, h; w), where x is the context and h is exogenous noise; the loss compares empirical conditional embeddings via Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ and can be written purely in terms of Gram matrices over both inputs (X) and outputs (Y). Optimization proceeds via backpropagation through f, as in GMMN, with the practical benefit that the matrix inverses in CMMD do not depend on w. The paper demonstrates CGMMN on three tasks: predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual image generation (MNIST, Yale), and Bayesian prediction distillation (“Bayesian dark knowledge”) from PBP to a compact student.

This work builds directly on kernel mean embeddings (Gretton et al., Song et al.) and GMMN (Li et al., Dziugaite et al.), and is complementary to conditional deep generative modeling via cGANs, CVAE/conditional VAEs, and discriminative max-margin DGMs. The conditional extension is natural conceptually, but the specific empirical CMMD estimator and its efficient trace–Gram formulation are non-obvious and practically useful.

Strengths
- A clear, principled conditional extension of MMD-based deep generative modeling via conditional mean embeddings.
- Elegant derivation of a practical CMMD estimator that enables training with the same machinery as standard GMMN (SGD/backprop), with mini-batch updates (Algorithm 1).
- Broad and relevant applications: predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian prediction distillation; competitive empirical results.
- Simple to implement and likely attractive to practitioners who already use MMD/GMMN or need teacher–student distillation without adversarial training.
- Well-written and organized; the appendix provides helpful methodological and experimental details.

Weaknesses
- Computational scalability: CMMD requires matrix inversions per minibatch; cubic cost in batch size can be limiting. The paper uses minibatches but does not analyze the bias/variance trade-offs or provide run-time profiles.
- Limited theoretical guarantees: Theorem 3 gives a sufficient condition; assumptions for CY|X ∈ F⊗G and the effect of regularization λ are discussed but not analyzed for finite-sample consistency or sample complexity in the conditional setting.
- Generative evaluation is mostly qualitative; comparisons to strong conditional baselines (cGAN, CVAE) on standard quantitative metrics (e.g., classification FID on generated digits, attribute-conditional fidelity) are missing.
- Predictive experiments are solid but not SoTA; ablations (kernels, bandwidth selection, noise dimension, using perturbed vs. unperturbed x) would strengthen the story.
- Bayesian distillation is demonstrated on a small dataset; no uncertainty calibration metrics (e.g., NLL, predictive intervals) are reported.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound, grounded in established kernel embedding theory. The CMMD objective and its empirical estimator are derived cleanly; the computational trick that the inverses on X do not depend on w is important and well-exploited. Equations appear correct, though I did not exhaustively verify all derivations. Experiments are carefully described and demonstrate that CGMMN is competitive in classification, can conditionally generate plausible images, and can distill a Bayesian predictor without degrading RMSE. However, quantitative evaluation of conditional generation and uncertainty calibration would improve the evidential support.

Clarity
The paper is clear and well organized. Background on MMD and conditional embeddings is concise and sufficient. The presentation of CMMD, its trace form with Gram matrices, and the training algorithm are easy to follow. Algorithm 1 is straightforward and actionable; the appendix provides additional implementation details. Figures are informative. Minor nit: a brief complexity discussion (per-step time/memory vs. batch size, kernel choice) in the main text would help practitioners.

Originality
While the step from MMD to conditional MMD using conditional mean embeddings is conceptually natural, the empirical estimator, its practical trace form, and demonstrating that this leads to a simple, backprop-trainable conditional generator are novel and useful. To my knowledge, a direct conditional GMMN trained via CMMD had not appeared in prior NeurIPS work. The application to Bayesian dark knowledge via CGMMN as student is a nice twist.

Significance
Conditional generation is central to modern deep generative modeling. This work expands the MMD/GMMN toolkit into that space with a method that avoids adversarial training, is easy to implement, and is broadly applicable. Practitioners who value stability and simplicity may prefer CMMD-based training. The technique is likely to spur follow-ups on scaling (e.g., random features/Nyström approximations), richer kernels on X, and stronger conditional benchmarks. The current empirical gains are competitive rather than state-of-the-art, but the contribution is methodologically meaningful.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Report computational cost and memory vs. batch size and input/output kernel choices; discuss practical upper bounds on batch size and how you chose λ.
- Describe kernel choices and bandwidth selection strategies for both X and Y (e.g., Gaussian with median heuristic?). Provide sensitivity analyses.
- Compare to conditional GAN and CVAE on at least one conditional generation benchmark with quantitative metrics.
- For Bayesian distillation, add negative log-likelihood, calibration (e.g., expected calibration error), and predictive interval coverage; include a larger regression dataset.
- Analyze or empirically probe the effect of using perturbed vs. exact training inputs as contexts for the generated set in CMMD.
- Consider scalable approximations (random Fourier features, Nyström) and discuss their impact on training stability and accuracy.

Minor comments
- Proof sketches or pointers for the assumptions ensuring CY|X ∈ F⊗G would be helpful in the appendix.
- Ensure consistent notation for s/d superscripts and clarify when N=M in Eq. (2).
- Some references have missing years/pages.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled, and practically useful conditional extension of GMMN via CMMD.
- Simple training procedure, easy to adopt (Algorithm 1), with solid empirical breadth.
- Well-presented and likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited quantitative evaluation for conditional generation and uncertainty calibration.
- Scalability and complexity not deeply analyzed; results do not surpass SoTA discriminative baselines.
- Theoretical guarantees are mostly inherited and do not address finite-sample properties in depth.

Overall assessment: Weak accept. Confidence: High.
