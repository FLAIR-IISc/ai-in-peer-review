Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative modeling framework trained by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (cMMD) objective between true and model conditional distributions. The method leverages kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009) and extends Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN; Dziugaite et al., 2015) from unconditional to conditional settings. Unlike GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), cMMD yields a single-sided, non-adversarial loss, avoiding min–max optimization while retaining flexibility of deep neural generators. The paper positions CGMMN among conditional DGMs such as CVAE (Sohn et al., 2015), conditional GANs, and discriminative deep generative models (MMVA; Li et al., 2015), and connects to the kernel two-sample test (MMD; Gretton et al., 2012) and conditional mean embedding theory.

Empirically, CGMMN is evaluated on: (1) discriminative prediction on MNIST and SVHN (p(y|x)); (2) class-conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces (p(x|y)), including an autoencoder-code variant; and (3) “Bayesian dark knowledge” distillation, approximating p(y|x, D) from a Bayesian teacher (PBP) on Boston Housing. Results are competitive with prior GMMN-style models and in the vicinity of other DGMs, though below strong discriminative baselines such as DSN.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of MMD-based training to conditional distributions via an operator norm between conditional mean embeddings; technically relevant and timely.
- Avoids adversarial training pathologies while enabling conditional generation and prediction within a single framework.
- Empirical scope spans classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation; qualitative samples are reasonable, and classification results on MNIST/SVHN are competitive with MMVA.
- The cMMD objective is clearly defined and grounded in established RKHS theory; backprop-based learning is straightforward once Gram matrices are computed.
- Practical touches: mini-batch training, discrete-conditional handling for labels, and a simple sampling protocol.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Mathematical exposition, while mostly correct, is dense: assumptions for operator existence, regularization role, and connections to consistency are not always easy to follow for non-experts.
- Scalability: computing and inverting K+λI per mini-batch is O(B^3) and potentially expensive, especially with batch sizes of 300–500 reported; no runtime or memory comparisons are provided.
- Experimental scope is limited to relatively small-scale, standard benchmarks; quantitative generative metrics are absent (relying mainly on visual inspection and NN analyses).
- Reproducibility: key implementation details are missing (exact kernels on x and y, bandwidth selection, λ, normalization, number of samples per x, noise distribution/dimensionality sensitivity). No reference code is provided.
- For prediction, baselines include strong discriminative models (DSN) but there is no wall-clock comparison, and the computational overhead of cMMD vs cross-entropy training is unclear.
- The Bayesian distillation experiment is small (Boston Housing) and only reports RMSE; calibration (NLL), uncertainty quality, and speed-up over the teacher are not reported.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The cMMD formulation is well-motivated; using empirical conditional embedding operators with Tikhonov regularization is standard. The sufficient-condition nature of the operator equality and the reliance on universal kernels are acknowledged. However, finite-sample behavior, bias/variance of the mini-batch estimator, and sensitivity to kernel hyperparameters are not analyzed.
- Empirical support: Classification results are solid on MNIST and reasonable on SVHN; conditional generation looks plausible. The distillation preserves RMSE relative to PBP on one dataset. Stronger, more diverse benchmarks and quantitative generative metrics would strengthen claims.
- Computational aspects: Training likely incurs significant cubic costs per batch; no profiling is provided.

Clarity
- The paper is reasonably well organized. Sections 2–3 give the necessary background and the objective derivation. However, derivations could be streamlined with clearer statements of assumptions, and more guidance on kernel choices and hyperparameters is needed for practitioners. The algorithm section would benefit from explicit complexity analysis.

Originality
- The contribution—extending MMD-based training to conditional models via conditional mean embedding operators—is novel and non-trivial. It fills a gap between unconditional GMMN and conditional DGMs (cGAN/CVAE), with a distinct objective and training dynamics.

Significance
- Conditional generative modeling is important; providing a stable, non-adversarial alternative is valuable. The work is likely to spur follow-ups (e.g., scalable cMMD approximations via random features/Nyström, structured outputs, richer conditionals). Current experimental scope limits immediate impact claims, but the methodological idea is promising.

Detailed comments and questions for the authors
- Kernels: What kernels are used for x and y in each experiment? How are bandwidths chosen (median heuristic, cross-validation)? For discrete labels, do you use a delta kernel or linear kernel on one-hot vectors?
- Regularization: How is λ selected? Sensitivity?
- Complexity: Please report wall-clock time per epoch and total training time, memory usage, and hardware. Compare to DSN, MMVA, CVAE, and cGAN where possible.
- Mini-batching: Are K̃−1 recomputed every mini-batch? Any numerical stabilization tricks? What is the effective batch size vs stability/speed trade-off?
- Sampling: How many noise samples h per x during training? At test time for prediction, do you average or sample once? Impact on accuracy?
- Quantitative generation: Consider reporting a classifier-based conditional log-likelihood proxy or inception-based metrics (when applicable) and precision/recall trade-offs for generative models.
- Distillation: Please add NLL and calibration metrics, plus speed-ups vs the Bayesian teacher at inference time.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide reference code and detailed hyperparameter settings to ensure reproducibility.
- Add runtime and scalability analysis, and consider approximations (random Fourier features, Nyström) to reduce O(B^3) costs.
- Expand experiments to larger or more diverse conditional generation tasks; include quantitative generative evaluation.
- Clarify model specification details (architectures, kernels, λ, bandwidths, noise dimension) sufficient for exact reproduction.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled extension of MMD training to conditional models with a simple, stable objective.
- Interesting and relevant to deep generative modeling; fills a gap between GMMN and cGAN/CVAE.
- Competitive results across classification and generation; useful application to Bayesian distillation.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical scale and lack of quantitative generative metrics.
- Missing computational/runtime analysis; potential scalability concerns.
- Reproducibility gaps and insufficient implementation details; no code.

Overall assessment
A timely and interesting contribution with solid theoretical grounding and promising empirical indications, but with notable gaps in scalability analysis, quantitative generative evaluation, and reproducibility. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can add runtime comparisons, clarify kernels/hyperparameters, and commit to releasing code; otherwise, this is borderline.
