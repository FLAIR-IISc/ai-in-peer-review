The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a novel framework for learning conditional distributions using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The CMMD is an extension of the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) criterion, which is widely used in generative moment-matching networks (GMMN). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN on various tasks, including predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge.

The main idea of CGMMN is to learn a conditional distribution given some input variables based on the CMMD criterion. The CMMD measures the difference between the kernel mean embedding of an empirical conditional distribution and that of the generative model. The authors provide a theoretical foundation for CGMMN by introducing Theorem 3, which states that if the embedding of conditional distributions CY|X = CZ|X, then PY|X = PZ|X in the sense that for every fixed x, we have PY|x = PZ|x.

The authors evaluate CGMMN on several datasets, including MNIST, SVHN, and Yale Face. The experimental results show that CGMMN is competitive with state-of-the-art models in generative tasks. The authors also demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN by applying it to supervised learning tasks, where it achieves decent results. Furthermore, the authors show that CGMMN can be used for Bayesian distillation, which is an interesting application of the proposed framework.

The strengths of the paper are:

* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the background and related work, making it easy for readers to understand the context and significance of the proposed framework.
* The theoretical foundation of CGMMN is well-established, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the CMMD criterion and its relationship to MMD.
* The experimental results are decent and demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN on various tasks.
* The authors address previous reviewer concerns and provide clarifications on the revised text.

The weaknesses of the paper are:

* The mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form is unclear, and the dot notation's meaning (matrix multiplication vs. Hadamard product) must be clarified.
* The symbol C is overloaded (used for conditional embedding and elsewhere), which is confusing and unfortunate.
* Theorem 3 lacks a reference or provided proof and needs one.
* The Bayesian distillation application is only evaluated on one dataset/model, so generality is unclear.
* The approach may not be the strongest for supervised learning tasks, and the results are not significantly better than state-of-the-art models.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, some clarifications and improvements are needed to make the paper more polished.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed framework and its applications. However, some notations and equations could be clarified, and the symbol C should be used consistently throughout the paper.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel framework for learning conditional distributions using a CMMD criterion, which is an extension of the MMD criterion. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN on various tasks, including Bayesian distillation, which is an interesting application of the proposed framework.

In terms of significance, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of generative modeling and deep learning. The proposed framework has the potential to be widely adopted in various applications, including image and video generation, data augmentation, and Bayesian distillation.
