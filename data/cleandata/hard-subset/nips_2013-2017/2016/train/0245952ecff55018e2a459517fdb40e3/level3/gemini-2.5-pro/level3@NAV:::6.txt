This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMNs), a framework for learning conditional distributions. The central technical contribution is a novel Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective based on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. This work extends the generative moment-matching network (GMMN) framework to the conditional setting, analogous to how Conditional GANs and VAEs extended their respective unconditional counterparts. The authors demonstrate the model's capabilities on a diverse set of tasks, including classification, conditional generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

The paper's originality and significance lie in its principled and elegant approach. By treating all conditional distributions as a single family and matching the conditional embedding operator directly, the proposed CMMD formulation cleverly avoids the data sparsity issues that would plague a naive approach, especially with continuous conditioning variables. This is a key conceptual advantage. The technical quality is also strong; the CMMD objective has a clean formulation, and the fact that key matrices in the objective are independent of model parameters makes it amenable to standard backpropagation. The experimental results, particularly for classification and Bayesian model distillation, are promising and highlight the model's versatility and potential impact.

However, the paper has weaknesses, primarily in clarity and experimental evaluation. The technical exposition in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is difficult to follow without consulting external references, hindering the paper's accessibility and reproducibility. These sections should be revised to be more self-contained. Experimentally, the paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing CGMMN to a naive baseline (e.g., a GMMN trained per condition with shared parameters), which could perform reasonably in discrete settings like MNIST. This would empirically quantify the proposed method's advantage. A potential quality concern is the trade-off between minibatch size and computational cost; CMMD may require large batches to reduce estimator variance, but the matrix inversions are computationally expensive. An analysis of this sensitivity is warranted.

Finally, there are minor technical points to address: the claim that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect for the formulation used, and an apparent inconsistency in the definition of the conditional embedding operator relative to reference [29] needs clarification. Despite these points, the core contribution is solid and addresses an important problem in generative modeling.
