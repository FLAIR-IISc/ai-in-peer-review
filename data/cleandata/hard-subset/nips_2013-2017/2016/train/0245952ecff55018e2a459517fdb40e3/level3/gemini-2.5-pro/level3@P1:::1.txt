This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional probability distributions using deep neural networks. The work extends the recently proposed Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN), which learn a joint distribution by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model-generated samples and data samples. The key contribution of this paper is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective function. CMMD measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective conditional kernel mean embedding operators. This allows the model to learn a mapping from a conditional variable `x` and a random noise variable `h` to a target variable `y`, i.e., `p(y|x)`. The parameters of the deep network that defines this mapping are learned via stochastic gradient descent with back-propagation.

This work is well-situated within the landscape of deep generative models. It provides a natural and necessary conditional counterpart to GMMN, much like Conditional GANs extended GANs and Conditional VAEs extended VAEs. By doing so, it significantly expands the applicability of moment-matching methods to a wider range of supervised and semi-supervised tasks, moving beyond simple joint density estimation. The proposed CMMD objective offers an alternative to the often unstable min-max optimization of GANs, providing a single, well-defined loss function to minimize.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of the paper is the novel formulation of the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) and its application to train deep conditional generative models. This is a theoretically grounded and elegant extension of the MMD framework. It fills an important gap in the literature and makes moment-matching networks a much more versatile tool for tasks requiring conditional modeling, such as prediction, contextual generation, and structured output prediction.

2.  **Training Stability:** The proposed training objective avoids the adversarial, min-max formulation of GANs, which is a significant practical advantage. The CMMD objective is a single loss function that can be directly minimized using standard gradient-based optimization methods, potentially leading to more stable and straightforward training.

3.  **Broad Applicability:** The authors do an excellent job of demonstrating the model's versatility. The experiments cover a diverse set of applications:
    *   **Predictive Modeling:** The model achieves competitive classification performance on MNIST and SVHN, demonstrating its viability for discriminative tasks.
    *   **Contextual Generation:** The ability to generate images conditioned on class labels for MNIST and Yale Faces showcases its core generative capability.
    *   **Bayesian Dark Knowledge:** This is a particularly interesting application, showing how CGMMN can be used to distill a complex Bayesian model into a faster student network without significant performance loss.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   While the paper is generally well-written, the mathematical exposition in Section 3.1 is quite dense. For readers not already expert in kernel embeddings of conditional operators, the derivation and intuition behind the CMMD objective may be difficult to follow. A more intuitive explanation or a simplified graphical illustration could greatly improve the accessibility of this core concept.
    *   The paper lacks sufficient implementation detail to ensure reproducibility. Key details about the network architectures (e.g., initialization schemes, exact optimizer parameters for AdaM), the choice of kernels for CMMD, and the setting of the regularization parameter λ are omitted. For a contribution to be widely adopted, it is crucial that other researchers can easily replicate the results. Providing source code would be the most effective way to address this.

2.  **Computational Cost:** The CMMD objective (Eq. 2) requires the computation and inversion of kernel Gram matrices within each mini-batch. This operation is typically O(B^3), where B is the batch size. This could make training CGMMN significantly more computationally expensive and slower than competing methods like VAEs or GANs, whose per-batch complexity is often linear in B. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this computational complexity and an empirical comparison of training runtimes against the reported baselines (e.g., CMMVA, DSN).

3.  **Scale of Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small-scale datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Faces). While these are standard benchmarks and the comparisons are fair within the context of the cited literature, the field of generative modeling was rapidly advancing to more complex datasets like CIFAR-10, CelebA, and LSUN by 2016. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness and scalability on at least one of these more challenging datasets would make a much stronger case for its practical significance. The quality of the generated MNIST samples in Figure 2, while recognizable, appears noisy compared to contemporary GAN results.

**Detailed Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the practical choice of kernels (for both x and y) and the regularization parameter λ in your experiments? How sensitive is the model's performance to these hyperparameters?
*   In Algorithm 1, for each `x` in a minibatch `B`, a new `y` is generated to form batch `B'`. Does this mean the batch size for computing the CMMD loss is `2 * |B|`? Please clarify the exact procedure.
*   The use of an autoencoder to pre-process images for generation (Figure 4) significantly improves sample quality. Does this suggest that CGMMN struggles with modeling raw pixel distributions directly, and is it more effective at modeling distributions in a lower-dimensional latent space?

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and novel contribution to the field of deep generative models. The development of a conditional moment-matching framework is a significant step forward. The primary weaknesses relate to the potential computational cost and the lack of implementation details needed for full reproducibility. These concerns, however, are largely addressable. The strengths of the work—its originality, training stability, and demonstrated versatility—are compelling.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel and theoretically sound CMMD objective for conditional generative modeling.
*   Provides a non-adversarial alternative for training deep conditional models, which is an important research direction.
*   Demonstrates strong performance across a diverse and interesting set of tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Computational cost of the CMMD objective may limit its scalability, and this is not discussed.
*   Experiments are limited to small-scale datasets.
*   Lack of key implementation details and source code raises reproducibility concerns.
