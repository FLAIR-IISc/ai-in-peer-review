Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative model trained with a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) loss derived from conditional kernel mean embeddings. The model samples noise h and conditions on x, passes [x, h] through a neural network, and learns to match the conditional distribution P(y|x) via the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between empirical conditional embedding operators. This extends GMMN/MMD from matching marginals to matching conditionals, connecting to prior kernel work on conditional mean embeddings and to deep generative modeling alternatives such as cGANs and CVAEs. The authors evaluate on supervised prediction (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST digits, Yale faces), and Bayesian distillation (PBP teacher on Boston housing). The revision appears to address earlier concerns regarding latent dimensionality, scalability (mini-batch CMMD), and utility across tasks.

Quality
- Theory: The CMMD objective is natural given operator-valued conditional embeddings. Theorems 1 and 2 correctly cite prior results for unconditional MMD and conditional embeddings. However, Theorem 3 (CY|X = CZ|X ⇒ PY|X = PZ|X with universal kernels) lacks a citation or proof. This result is plausible by combining universality with the fact that equality of conditional mean embeddings at all x implies equality of conditionals, but a formal reference (e.g., Fukumizu et al. 2011; Song et al. 2009/2013) or a provided proof is needed.
- Objective and derivations: Equation (2) gives a computable CMMD in terms of Gram matrices. The remark relating CMMD to a “weighted MMD” is insightful, but the mapping is not transparent: please show explicitly how Tr(Kd K̃d^{-1} Ld K̃d^{-1}) etc. expand into pairwise kernel sums with weights and define the weight matrices C1, C2, C3. Also clarify whether “·” denotes standard matrix multiplication (it appears so) or Hadamard product. In Remark 1, 1 denotes the all-ones matrix; with standard matrix multiplication Tr(Ld 1) equals the sum of all entries in Ld—please state this explicitly.
- Notation: The symbol C is heavily overloaded (cross-covariances CXY, CXX, conditional operator CY|X, and weight matrices C1–C3). This is confusing; consider renaming the weight matrices to W1–W3 or using different glyphs for operators (e.g., calligraphic).
- Practicality and scalability: The mini-batch CMMD keeps the cubic cost O(b^3) in the batch size for K̃−1, which is acceptable for b≈200–500 but limits scaling. Suggestions: Nyström or random feature approximations to K, blockwise solves, or preconditioned conjugate gradients; discuss complexity and memory and include timing. It is good that K̃−1 does not depend on model parameters, allowing non-differentiable kernels on x; gradients still require smooth kernels on y—please state the kernel choices and bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic) for each experiment.
- Experiments: Results are solid but modest in scope. Classification: MNIST and SVHN performance is competitive with max-margin DGM baselines (CMMVA), as expected for a conditional generative approach; SOTA discriminative CNNs still outperform. Generative: conditional MNIST and Yale face samples are reasonable and comparable to GMMN; AE-code space generation improves perceptual quality. Bayesian distillation: the Boston housing experiment shows no degradation versus PBP, but a single dataset/model leaves generality unclear.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. To further improve:
- Provide the derivation steps from operator norm to Eq. (2), define all symbols inline (Φ, Υ, Ksd, Lds), and explicitly connect Remark 1 to the weighted pairwise form.
- Clarify the “dot” notation and the all-ones matrix. Avoid overloading C.
- State the kernels used for x and y in each task, bandwidth selection, and sensitivity.
- Briefly discuss how x is chosen for generated pairs during training (same x as in data vs. perturbed x) and the effect on bias/variance.

Originality
Extending GMMN to conditional distributions via conditional mean embeddings is a natural but nontrivial advance that leverages established kernel operator theory in a deep generative setting. Relative to cGANs and CVAEs, the objective is simpler (no adversarial training, no variational bound) and offers a different bias via kernels. The combination and its empirical validation are original enough for NIPS 2016.

Significance
The main impact is likely in conditional generative modeling where stable training and distribution matching are desirable, and in scenarios where kernels over complex outputs are beneficial. The supervised learning application is not the strongest use case but shows versatility. The Bayesian distillation idea is interesting; broader validation (more teachers/datasets) would strengthen significance. Wider adoption will require stronger results on more challenging conditional generation (e.g., CIFAR-10 class-conditional, LSUN attributes) and scalability demonstrations.

Pros
- Clear, principled extension of GMMN to conditional modeling with a simple, stable objective.
- Decent empirical results across classification, generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Mini-batch training and ability to use non-differentiable kernels on x.
- Good discussion of connections to MMD and embedding literature.

Cons
- Theorem 3 lacks citation/proof; theoretical guarantees are incomplete.
- Equation (2) to “weighted MMD” mapping and notation (dot, C overload) need clarification.
- Scalability beyond moderate batch sizes not demonstrated; cubic kernel costs remain.
- Experiments limited to MNIST/SVHN/Yale and one distillation case; no results on more challenging datasets.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a formal reference or proof for Theorem 3.
- Expand the derivation of Eq. (2), define the operations, and rename overloaded symbols.
- Report kernel choices, bandwidth selection, and sensitivity analyses.
- Discuss and/or implement scalability strategies (Nyström, random Fourier features) and report training time/memory.
- Add experiments on more challenging conditional generation (CIFAR-10 class-conditional; LSUN attributes) and more distillation settings (e.g., different Bayesian teachers, classification tasks).
- Include qualitative analyses (nearest neighbors, diversity metrics) and quantitative generation metrics if feasible.

Overall evaluation
A technically sound and well-motivated extension of MMD-based DGMs to conditional modeling, with competitive results and clean training. Some theoretical and clarity issues (Theorem 3, notation) and limited experimental scope reduce the impact. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can add a reference/proof for Theorem 3 and clarify the CMMD derivation and notation; otherwise, this is a strong borderline paper.
