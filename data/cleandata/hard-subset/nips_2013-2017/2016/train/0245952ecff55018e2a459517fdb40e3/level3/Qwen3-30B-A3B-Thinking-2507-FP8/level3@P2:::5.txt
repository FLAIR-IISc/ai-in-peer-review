

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to learn conditional distributions. The authors propose a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to match conditional distributions in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), building on Song et al.'s work on conditional kernel embeddings (2009). CGMMN follows a generative process where a deep network transforms a simple random variable and given conditional variables into target samples. The paper relates to recent NIPS work on conditional generative models, including conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., 2015), though it takes a fundamentally different approach by using MMD rather than adversarial training.

## Quality

The technical approach is theoretically grounded, extending the MMD framework to conditional distributions through kernel mean embeddings. However, the paper contains a critical omission: the definition of the empirical conditional operator $\hat{C}_{Y|X}$ in Section 3.1 omits the centering terms involving estimators of $\mu_X$ and $\mu_Y$. This is problematic because the centering is essential for the proper embedding of conditional distributions in RKHS. The authors should clarify how they handled centering during gradient computation, as this affects the validity of their training procedure. Without this clarification, the technical foundation appears incomplete.

The experimental results are disappointing. In predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN datasets, CGMMN consistently underperforms compared to the state-of-the-art CMMVA (Li et al., 2015) by significant margins (e.g., 3.13% vs. 3.09% error rate on SVHN). The generative performance also lacks compelling evidence of superiority, as no comparisons to other conditional generative methods (e.g., conditional GANs) are provided. The paper's claim of "competitive performance" appears unsubstantiated by the empirical results.

## Clarity

The paper contains several typos (e.g., "Frobeniu norm" instead of "Frobenius norm," "an universal" instead of "a universal," and "which the" instead of "which they"). The presentation of the CMMD objective in Equation (2) is mathematically dense without sufficient explanation of how the terms relate to the conditional distribution. While the generative process is well-described, the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the results, particularly regarding how centering was handled.

## Originality

The core idea of extending GMMN to conditional distributions is novel and well-motivated. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing conditional generative models and positions CGMMN as addressing these limitations. The theoretical extension from joint to conditional distributions represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors demonstrate awareness of related work, though the connection to conditional kernel embedding literature could be strengthened.

## Significance

The potential applications of CGMMN in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation are significant. However, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that CGMMN advances the state of the art in any of these areas. The experimental results show no clear advantage over existing methods, and the generative performance lacks comparison to competitive conditional generation approaches. The significance claim is therefore undermined by the empirical results.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel extension of GMMN to conditional distributions
- Clear theoretical motivation for using conditional MMD
- Well-structured generative process that enables simple training
- Demonstrates applicability to multiple domains (predictive modeling, generation, knowledge distillation)

**Weaknesses:**
- Critical omission of centering terms in the empirical conditional operator definition
- Experimental results show inferior performance compared to state-of-the-art baselines
- No comparison to other conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs)
- Several typographical errors and unclear mathematical explanations
- Claims of "competitive performance" are not supported by the data

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The theoretical extension from GMMN to CGMMN is novel and well-motivated
- The framework has potential applications in multiple domains
- The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the related literature

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The technical omission of centering terms raises serious concerns about the validity of the method
- Experimental results fail to demonstrate competitive performance against established baselines
- The paper's claims are not substantiated by the empirical evidence presented
- Several grammatical and typographical errors reduce the paper's quality

## Recommendation

While the theoretical extension of GMMN to conditional distributions is promising, the paper fails to deliver convincing empirical evidence of its superiority. The critical omission in the theoretical development (missing centering terms) undermines the technical foundation, and the experimental results are disappointing compared to state-of-the-art methods. The paper would benefit significantly from addressing these concerns, particularly the missing centering terms and providing stronger empirical validation against relevant baselines.

I recommend rejection, but I note that the authors have indicated they will address the reviewer's concerns. If they successfully address the centering issue and demonstrate clear improvements over baselines in the revised version, this could be reconsidered for acceptance.
