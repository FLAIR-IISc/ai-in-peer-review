This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep generative framework that learns conditional distributions p(y|x) by matching conditional kernel mean embeddings using a conditional MMD (CMMD) objective. The model draws noise h from a simple prior, concatenates with x, and generates y through a neural network; parameters are learned by minimizing the CMMD between real and generated (x, y) pairs via backpropagation. The key idea is to treat the family {p(y|x)}x as a single object—the conditional embedding operator CY|X—and to match this operator rather than training separate models for each x or each discrete class. Experiments cover: (i) classification on MNIST and SVHN by generating predictive distributions y given x; (ii) conditional generation of images given labels on MNIST and Yale Faces; and (iii) Bayesian “dark knowledge” distillation by fitting CGMMN to samples from a Bayesian teacher’s predictive distribution.

Relation to prior work: CGMMN extends GMMN [19, 3] from unconditional to conditional modeling by importing conditional embeddings [29] and testing via CMMD, analogous to the MMD two-sample test [7]. It is complementary to conditional GANs [21] and conditional VAEs [28], offering a simpler objective without adversarial training or variational bounds. Compared to max-margin DGMs [18], it targets conditional distributions directly without an explicit likelihood.

Quality:
- Theory: The CMMD criterion is well-motivated. The result that CY|X equality implies equality of conditionals for all x is consistent with conditional embedding theory (under universality and regularity). It would help to state exactly the assumptions (e.g., characteristic kernels, conditions for CXX^{-1} existence) and discuss practical implications when they fail.
- Algorithm: The objective in Eq. (2) is clean, with the useful observation that K̃^{-1} depends only on x and λ, hence backprop need not pass through these inverses, allowing non-differentiable kernels on x. However, computing inverses per minibatch can be expensive and unstable for large batches; the paper acknowledges cubic costs and adopts minibatching but does not quantify trade-offs.
- Empirics: Results are broadly competitive. CGMMN-CNN performs reasonably on MNIST/SVHN, conditional samples on MNIST/Yale look plausible (improved when trained in an autoencoder code space), and the distillation experiment on Boston housing matches the Bayesian teacher’s RMSE.

Clarity:
- Sections 2.3 and 3.1 are hard to follow without consulting [29]. The paper alternates between centered and uncentered cross-covariance definitions; the conditional operator CY|X = CYX CXX^{-1} depends on this choice. Please align the operator and covariance definitions with [29] (or clearly state the variant used) and ensure consistency throughout.
- Minor error: the statement “Asymptotically, L̂^2_MMD is an unbiased estimator” is incorrect as written. The commonly used V-statistic estimator is biased but consistent; the U-statistic provides an unbiased finite-sample estimator of MMD^2 [7]. Please correct and specify which estimator is used in practice.
- Important practical details are missing: kernel choices and bandwidth selection for x and y, λ setting, noise dimension sensitivity, and how x is sampled for synthetic pairs during training in predictive tasks.

Originality:
- Extending GMMNs to conditional modeling via CMMD is a natural but nontrivial step that leverages operator-valued kernel embeddings. Matching the conditional operator across all x, instead of per-condition training, is the key novelty and addresses data sparsity for continuous x.
- The use of CMMD for Bayesian distillation is novel and interesting.

Significance:
- Methodologically, CGMMN adds a simple, stable alternative to cGANs/cVAEs for conditional generation and predictive modeling, with flexible kernels over outputs and inputs.
- Practically, the approach is promising but not yet state of the art in classification; nevertheless, results are competitive for a generative conditional method. The Bayesian distillation result suggests wider applicability.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean extension of MMD-based training to conditional models via operator matching.
- Simple training (no adversarial min-max), compatible with standard backprop.
- Demonstrated across diverse tasks, including a compelling Bayesian distillation application.
- Insightful observation about K̃^{-1} independence from parameters enabling non-differentiable kernels on x.

Cons and concerns:
- Computational scalability of CMMD requires large batches for stable estimates but faces cubic-time inverses; limited discussion of efficiency vs. performance.
- Missing comparisons to strong conditional baselines (e.g., cGAN, CVAE) and to a naive per-condition GMMN baseline, especially for low-cardinality discrete x where the gap may be small.
- Clarity issues in conditional operator definitions and a minor statistical error about MMD estimators.
- Limited ablations (kernel/bandwidth choices, λ, minibatch size), calibration metrics, and runtime/stability analysis.

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- Add a naive baseline: train separate GMMNs per class (shared architecture/hyperparameters). This will highlight CGMMN’s advantage in data efficiency and continuous x settings.
- Compare to cGAN [21] and conditional VAE [28] on conditional generation (FID/IS where appropriate) and to discriminative baselines on predictive tasks (also report NLL/Brier/calibration).
- Detail kernel choices and selection (e.g., Gaussian kernels with median heuristic) for both x and y; report sensitivity to bandwidth and λ. Consider random-feature or Nyström approximations to avoid explicit inverses and enable larger effective batch sizes; linear-time CMMD variants could further scale.
- Clarify Sections 2.3/3.1: explicitly define centered vs. uncentered cross-covariance, conditions for CXX^{-1} (regularization), and restate Theorem 3 with precise assumptions (characteristic kernels, measurability).
- Correct the MMD estimator statement and specify whether U- or V-statistics are used; consider the unbiased linear-time estimator for efficiency.
- Provide training-time and memory costs, and discuss stability vs. minibatch size. If large batches are needed, report how often kernel parameters are re-estimated and whether moving-average estimates of K̃^{-1} help.

Overall: A solid, well-motivated contribution that extends MMD-based generative training to conditional modeling with a principled operator-matching objective. With improved clarity, stronger baselines, and more thorough empirical analysis of kernel/efficiency trade-offs, this paper would make a valuable addition.
