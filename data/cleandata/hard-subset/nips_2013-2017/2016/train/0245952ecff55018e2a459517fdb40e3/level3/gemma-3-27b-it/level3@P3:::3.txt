This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the GMMN framework to learn conditional distributions using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The approach leverages kernel mean embeddings and offers a potentially simpler training process compared to GANs. The authors demonstrate CGMMN on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian distillation. While the empirical results are decent, and previous reviewer concerns seem addressed, some clarity and theoretical justifications are required.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound extension of GMMN. The core idea of applying MMD to conditional distributions is well-motivated and theoretically grounded in prior work on kernel embeddings (Theorems 1 & 2). The empirical evaluation, while not extensive, demonstrates the method’s viability across diverse tasks. The authors acknowledge limitations and provide comparisons to existing methods. The revised text appears to address previous concerns regarding the low-dimensional latent space and scalability. However, the lack of a proof or citation for Theorem 3 is a notable weakness. The use of back-propagation for training is a significant strength, enabling practical implementation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although improvements could be made. The mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form is unclear and requires further explanation. Specifically, the meaning of the dot notation (matrix multiplication vs. Hadamard product) should be explicitly defined. The symbol ‘C’ is overloaded, representing both the conditional embedding and other quantities, which is confusing. While the explanation of CMMD is reasonably clear, a more intuitive explanation connecting it to the standard MMD formulation would be beneficial. The explanations regarding the mini-batch algorithm (Alg. 1) and its connection to the full CMMD objective could be improved.

**Originality:** The work builds upon existing techniques (GMMN, MMD, kernel embeddings) but presents a novel combination to address the problem of conditional generative modeling. Extending MMD to the conditional setting is a valuable contribution, offering an alternative to GANs and variational approaches. While conditional GANs (CGANs) exist, the MMD-based approach provides a different optimization landscape and potentially avoids the training instability often associated with GANs. The application to Bayesian distillation, while not entirely novel, is a good demonstration of the method’s versatility.

**Significance:** The results, while not groundbreaking, are competitive with existing approaches, suggesting that CGMMN is a viable method for conditional generative modeling. The greatest potential impact is likely within the generative modeling domain itself, offering a potentially more stable and easier-to-train alternative to GANs. The Bayesian distillation application is interesting but limited by its evaluation on a single dataset/model. Wider adoption will likely hinge on demonstrating stronger performance on more challenging datasets, such as CIFAR-10 or LSUN.  The approach offers a principled way to learn conditional distributions without making strong assumptions about their form, which is valuable in many applications.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Extends GMMN to the conditional setting in a theoretically sound manner.
*   Addresses previous reviewer concerns.
*   Demonstrates viability across multiple tasks (classification, generation, distillation).
*   Potentially offers a more stable training process compared to GANs.
*   Provides a flexible framework for conditional distribution learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of a proof or citation for Theorem 3.
*   Unclear mapping from Equation (2) and overloaded symbol 'C'.
*   Empirical results are decent but not significantly superior to existing methods.
*   Bayesian distillation results are limited in scope.
*   Evaluation on more challenging datasets is needed to establish broader applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of generative modeling. The proposed CGMMN framework is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and demonstrates promising empirical performance. Addressing the minor clarity issues (Equation 2, symbol 'C') and providing a justification for Theorem 3 would significantly strengthen the paper. While the current results are not state-of-the-art, the approach has the potential to become a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners working with conditional generative models, particularly if it can demonstrate its effectiveness on more challenging datasets. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
