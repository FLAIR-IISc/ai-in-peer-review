This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a novel approach to learning conditional distributions based on the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) criterion. The authors extend the capabilities of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) to scenarios requiring conditional modeling, addressing a common need in tasks like predictive modeling, contextual generation, and knowledge distillation. The core idea revolves around utilizing kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and defining a conditional MMD (CMMD) to facilitate learning via stochastic gradient descent and back-propagation. The method is evaluated across a range of tasks, demonstrating competitive performance.

The motivation behind CGMMN is well-defined. Existing GMMNs are designed for joint distribution modeling, while many practical applications necessitate understanding and generating data conditioned on specific inputs.  A naive approach to tackle this would be to train a separate GMMN for each conditional distribution, sharing network parameters. However, this suffers from data sparsity, particularly when dealing with continuous conditioning variables. CGMMN addresses this limitation by treating the collection of conditional distributions as a family and directly matching the conditional embedding operator, effectively sidestepping the per-condition data sparsity issue. This is a significant contribution, offering a more efficient and scalable solution to conditional generative modeling.

The strengths of this work lie in its clear mathematical formulation and its compatibility with standard deep learning training procedures. The CMMD objective is elegantly derived and, critically, can be computed similarly to the unconditional MMD, enabling efficient gradient-based optimization via back-propagation. The independence of the kernel matrices in Eq. 2 from the model parameters further streamlines the training process. The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN through a diverse set of experiments, including predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The experimental results are promising, showing that CGMMN achieves competitive performance against existing state-of-the-art methods.

However, the paper also exhibits some weaknesses. The presentation of the theoretical background in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is somewhat dense and difficult to follow. The reviewer found it necessary to consult the referenced papers ([29], [7]) to fully grasp the concepts.  Improving the clarity and flow of these sections would greatly enhance the paper's accessibility.  Furthermore, there’s a minor inaccuracy – the claim that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect; it is consistent, but not unbiased.  A more nuanced statement is required.  Additionally, there’s a minor inconsistency concerning the conditional embedding operator definition, which appears to diverge from the definition presented in reference [29], suggesting a potentially different cross-covariance operator definition is being employed. This discrepancy should be clarified.

A potential concern is the computational cost associated with CMMD, particularly with respect to minibatch size. The authors acknowledge that CMMD may require large minibatches to mitigate stochastic noise, but large minibatches can significantly increase the cost of operations such as matrix inversions.  A discussion of strategies for mitigating this issue, such as employing low-rank approximations or alternative kernel methods, would be valuable.  While the authors mention a mini-batch version of the algorithm, a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between minibatch size, computational cost, and convergence rate is warranted.

A useful addition would be a comparison to the aforementioned naive baseline of training separate GMMNs for each condition, particularly on datasets with low-cardinality discrete conditions like MNIST. While the authors rightly point out the limitations of this approach in more complex scenarios, demonstrating its performance on a simpler case would strengthen their argument for the advantages of CGMMN.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to conditional generative modeling using kernel embeddings and CMMD.
*   Addresses data sparsity issues inherent in naive conditional GMMN approaches.
*   Competitive performance across diverse tasks, including predictive modeling and knowledge distillation.
*   Clear mathematical formulation and compatibility with standard deep learning training procedures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Theoretical sections (2.3, 3.1) could benefit from improved clarity and flow.
*   Minor inaccuracies and inconsistencies in the presentation of theoretical concepts.
*   Potential computational challenges related to minibatch size and matrix inversions.
*   Lack of comparison to a direct baseline (separate GMMNs per condition).



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses in presentation and potential computational concerns, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling. The proposed CGMMN framework offers a compelling solution to the challenge of learning conditional distributions, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness.  I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the clarity issues in the theoretical sections and acknowledging/correcting the minor inaccuracies. The authors should also consider including a comparison to the baseline of training independent GMMNs.
