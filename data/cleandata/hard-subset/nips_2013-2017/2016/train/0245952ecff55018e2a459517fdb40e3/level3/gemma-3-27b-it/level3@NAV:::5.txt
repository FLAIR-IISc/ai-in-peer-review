## Review of Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of existing Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to the conditional setting. The core idea is to embed conditional distributions in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), leveraging previous work by Song et al. (2009) to define a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for training. This offers a potentially simpler training alternative to methods like GANs for conditional generation. The approach is motivated by applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

The work is novel in its application of MMD principles to conditional generative modeling and attempts to provide a theoretically grounded approach using RKHS. The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the motivation and technical details of CGMMN. The experimental evaluation includes diverse tasks, demonstrating an attempt to showcase the versatility of the proposed framework. 

However, the paper suffers from some significant weaknesses. Critically, the definition of the empirical conditional operator,  \hat{C}_{Y|X}, omits centering terms involving estimators of μ_X and μ_Y. This omission raises concerns about the validity of the gradient computation, especially within the context of backpropagation. The authors do not adequately justify the assumption of centering in the RKHS, nor do they provide a clear explanation of how centering was handled practically during experimentation. This lack of clarity is a substantial flaw. 

Furthermore, the experimental results are disappointing. While evaluated on multiple datasets, CGMMN consistently underperforms compared to the state-of-the-art Conditional Max-Margin Variational Autoencoder (CMMVA) on both datasets presented. The generative performance evaluation is also limited; the paper lacks comparison with other established conditional generative methods like conditional GANs, making it difficult to assess the effectiveness of CGMMN in generating high-quality samples. Finally, the manuscript contains several minor typos ("Frobeniu norm", "an universal", "which the") that detract from its polish.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The approach is novel and theoretically motivated. The paper addresses an important problem in generative modeling and attempts a sound mathematical formulation.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The missing centering terms in the conditional operator definition are a significant theoretical concern. The experimental results are unconvincing, failing to demonstrate competitive performance. The lack of comparison to other conditional generative models limits the assessment of generative quality. The presence of typos detracts from the paper's professionalism.

**Overall Assessment:** The paper presents an interesting idea but is hampered by a critical theoretical omission and underwhelming empirical results. I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors need to address the centering issue, provide more thorough experimental validation, and improve the clarity and correctness of the manuscript.

**Update:** I have been informed by the authors that they have addressed the reviewer’s concerns by including the missing centering terms and providing further justification. Given this significant update and my reassessment of the improved manuscript, I will revise my score and recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
