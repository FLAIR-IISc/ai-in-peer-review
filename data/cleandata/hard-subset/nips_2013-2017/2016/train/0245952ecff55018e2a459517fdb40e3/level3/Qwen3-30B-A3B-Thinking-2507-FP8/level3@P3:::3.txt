

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for learning conditional distributions, leveraging kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions. CGMMN builds upon the GMMN work of Li et al. (2015) and extends it to conditional modeling, enabling applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian distillation. The paper relates to prior work on conditional kernel embeddings (Song et al., 2009; Grunewald et al., 2012) and conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), but provides a novel MMD-based approach to conditional generative modeling.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound extension of GMMN to conditional settings. The theoretical foundation using kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions is well-grounded in the literature, though Theorem 3 lacks a reference or proof as noted in the key points. The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance in predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN) and generation (MNIST and Yale Face), with a novel application to Bayesian distillation. The authors have addressed previous reviewer concerns regarding low-dimensional latent space (they clarify that the dimension is sufficient for their tasks), scalability (through mini-batch implementation), and utility (by demonstrating diverse applications). However, the paper would benefit from stronger results on more challenging datasets like CIFAR-10 to demonstrate broader applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts. However, several points require clarification: (1) The mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form is ambiguous, particularly regarding the dot notation (matrix multiplication vs. Hadamard product); (2) The symbol C is overloaded (used for conditional embedding and elsewhere), which creates confusion; (3) The connection between the CMMD objective and standard MMD is not sufficiently explained for readers unfamiliar with conditional kernel embeddings. The authors have made some revisions addressing prior reviewer concerns, but these remaining ambiguities reduce clarity.

**Originality:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution by extending GMMN to conditional settings, which is a significant gap in the literature. While conditional GANs and conditional VAEs exist, this MMD-based approach offers a different training objective with theoretical guarantees. The extension to Bayesian distillation is particularly novel, though the evaluation is limited to a single regression task. The work is sufficiently distinct from prior NIPS papers, including the authors' own GMMN work, and from conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Kingma et al., 2014).

**Significance:** The significance of this work is moderate. The conditional generative modeling framework is useful for several applications, and the paper demonstrates its viability across multiple tasks. However, the empirical results are largely comparable to existing methods (e.g., CMMVA for prediction), without substantial improvements. The Bayesian distillation application is interesting but limited to one dataset, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why CGMMN is superior to other distillation approaches. While the approach shows promise for pure generative modeling (as evidenced by the digit/face generation results), it's less compelling for supervised learning applications, which the authors acknowledge. The work would be more significant with stronger results on more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) and a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art conditional generative models.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical extension of GMMN to conditional distributions
- Demonstrates versatility across multiple tasks (prediction, generation, distillation)
- Addresses previous reviewer concerns in the revised manuscript
- Provides a theoretically grounded approach to conditional generative modeling
- Empirical results are generally competitive with baselines

**Weaknesses:**
- Ambiguous notation (dot product, overloaded symbol C)
- Theorem 3 lacks reference or proof
- Limited evaluation on challenging datasets (only MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face)
- Bayesian distillation evaluated on only one dataset
- Supervised learning application is not the strongest use case
- No clear advantage over existing conditional generative models in terms of performance

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper presents a technically sound extension of GMMN to conditional settings with a clear theoretical foundation. While the empirical results are not groundbreaking, they are competitive and demonstrate the framework's versatility. The authors have addressed most previous reviewer concerns, and the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of generative modeling. With the noted clarifications (particularly regarding the notation and Theorem 3), the paper would be suitable for publication at NIPS. The work is most significant for pure generative modeling applications, and the authors should consider expanding evaluation to more challenging datasets in future work.
