Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMNs to conditional distributions by using kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and a conditional MMD (CMMD) objective. The key idea is to match conditional mean embedding operators CY|X across data and model, rather than matching samples for each fixed x. This avoids per-condition data sparsity and yields a single objective that ties all conditionals together. The optimization uses backpropagation through a conditional generator f(x, h; w), with the CMMD computed from kernel Gram matrices over inputs and outputs; crucially, the inverses (K + λI)−1 in Eq. 2 depend only on the conditioning variables, not model parameters, allowing standard SGD.

The method builds on GMMN (MMD-based deep generators) and on kernel conditional mean embeddings (Song et al.; Grunewälder et al.). It complements conditional GANs and conditional VAEs by offering a stable, min-max–free objective with a clean kernel-based formulation. The paper evaluates CGMMN on three fronts: (i) classification (learning p(y|x)) on MNIST and SVHN; (ii) conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces; and (iii) distillation of Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge”) by training a student CGMMN to mimic a teacher’s p(y|x, D).

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: CMMD is a natural conditional analogue of MMD—defined as the squared Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional embedding operators—and it admits a compact trace form (Eq. 2) computed similarly to unconditional MMD. The observation that K matrices in Eq. 2 are independent of model parameters is useful for implementation and allows standard backpropagation.
- Addresses an important limitation of naive conditional training: treating each P(y|x = x0) separately with a GMMN wastes data and fails for continuous x; the proposed operator-matching approach explicitly couples all conditionals and avoids per-condition sparsity.
- Broad and interesting applications: competitive classification results, convincing conditional generation (including with an autoencoder code space), and a promising Bayesian distillation experiment showing no loss in accuracy on Boston Housing relative to a PBP teacher.
- Generally simple training procedure (no min–max game), and compatible with common deep architectures.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Computational–statistical trade-off of CMMD: The estimator involves inverses of minibatch Gram matrices K + λI, which scale cubically in the batch size. Because CMMD aggregates information across conditions, it may require relatively large minibatches to suppress stochastic noise; the paper does not quantify sensitivity to batch size or report training time/memory. Practical mitigations (e.g., Nyström/inducing point approximations for k(x, x′), random Fourier features with ridge regression in feature space, block-diagonal approximations, conjugate-gradient solves with preconditioning, or Woodbury updates for amortized inverses) should be discussed and, ideally, evaluated.
- Baselines: The clear advantage over a naive conditional GMMN trained per-condition (with shared parameters) is well-argued for continuous x, but for low-cardinality discrete x (e.g., MNIST labels) that baseline could perform reasonably. A direct comparison on such settings would strengthen the empirical claim. Comparisons to cGAN and CVAE on conditional generation would also contextualize CGMMN’s trade-offs.
- Clarity: Sections 2.3 and 3.1 are difficult to follow without consulting the conditional embedding literature. The mapping between spaces (F, G), adjoints, and operator domains/codomains (e.g., CXY vs CYX) should be made explicit, and assumptions for Theorem 3 (e.g., C−3/2
XX CXY Hilbert–Schmidt) should be stated plainly with intuition.
- Minor technical issues:
  - The text states that L̂^2_MMD is an “unbiased” estimator “asymptotically.” The standard V-statistic is biased; the U-statistic is unbiased in finite samples; both are consistent. Please correct the wording.
  - The definition of the conditional embedding operator appears to differ from [29]. If CY|X := CYX CXX^{-1} is intended, please align notation with the reference, define cross-covariance operators carefully (including adjoints and tensor space memberships), and ensure consistency in the domains of operators.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a clean derivation of CMMD and a correct, differentiable objective. The main claims are supported by experiments across multiple tasks. However, the paper lacks an analysis of CMMD’s computational cost vs. batch size and omits some relevant baselines, which slightly weakens the empirical case.

Clarity
Moderately clear. The high-level story is compelling, and Eq. 2 is useful. Theoretical sections could be more self-contained: define all operators precisely, sketch the derivation from operator norms to the trace form, and add a brief appendix on computational approximations. Fix the minor estimator and notation issues noted above.

Originality
A meaningful and nontrivial extension of GMMN to conditional distributions using conditional mean embeddings. While conditional generation is well-studied via GANs/VAEs, matching conditional embedding operators within a deep generator is novel and interesting.

Significance
Potentially significant as a stable, simple alternative for conditional generative modeling and for distilling Bayesian predictive distributions. If the computational concerns are addressed and stronger baselines added, this could be a method others adopt.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add the naive per-condition GMMN baseline (with shared parameters) on low-cardinality discrete conditions, and include cGAN/CVAE comparisons on conditional generation.
- Report training time/memory and sensitivity to minibatch size; evaluate one or two scalable CMMD variants (Nyström, random features with ridge regression).
- Clarify Sections 2.3 and 3.1 with explicit operator domains, adjoints, and a short derivation of Eq. 2; fix the MMD estimator statement and align the operator definition with [29].
- Explore a continuous-conditioning benchmark (e.g., regression with real-valued x) to better showcase the advantage over naive baselines.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, principled extension of MMD training to conditional models.
- Simple training; objective compatible with backprop; no adversarial instability.
- Diverse and promising empirical results, including Bayesian distillation.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing key baselines and limited analysis of computational scalability.
- Some notational inconsistencies and clarity issues in theoretical sections.

Overall
A solid and timely contribution. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can address clarity, correct the minor technical points, and strengthen the experimental comparisons; otherwise, it is borderline but still promising.
