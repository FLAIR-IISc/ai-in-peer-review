This paper extends Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) to the conditional setting by introducing a conditional MMD (CMMD) objective and training deep conditional generators via backpropagation. The main idea is to compare conditional mean embeddings CY|X induced by the data and the model, measuring their Hilbert–Schmidt distance, and to optimize a generator f(x, h; w) that maps inputs and noise to outputs. The approach relates to prior kernel mean embedding work (Gretton et al.; Song et al.) and to deep generative modeling at NIPS and elsewhere, including GANs and conditional GANs (Goodfellow; Mirza & Osindero), VAEs/CVAEs (Kingma & Welling; Sohn et al.), and max-margin DGMs. Experiments cover predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Korattikara et al.), with broadly competitive results.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and potentially significant extension of MMD training to conditional models, avoiding min–max optimization.
- Broad applicability and simple sampling/training loop compatible with CNN/MLP architectures.
- Empirical results are competitive with strong baselines in multiple tasks; the Bayesian distillation use case is interesting.

Weaknesses and clarity concerns
- The presentation starts with heavy formalism and lacks intuition for the conditional objective. In contrast to the non-conditional MMD case (which admits a simple sample-matching view), the conditional case needs an equally accessible explanation.
- Equation (2) is central but under-explained: no derivation, no explicit assumptions, and limited discussion of its practical implications.
- The path from Eq. (2) to the implemented algorithm is insufficiently detailed; the supplement is difficult to follow.
- Computational aspects are opaque: kernel choices on X and Y, setting λ, batchwise construction of Gram matrices, complexity and memory (O(b^3)/O(b^2)), and any approximations (e.g., low-rank, random features) are not clearly specified.

Requested clarifications
1) Provide an intuitive derivation of Eq. (2): start from CY|X ≈ Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤, expand ∥Ĉd−Ĉs∥2F⊗G, and show how this yields the trace form. Use the finite-X case to build intuition, connect CMMD to a weighted MMD where weights depend on similarities in X, and include a toy example illustrating how different conditionals induce non-uniform weights.
2) Provide a clear, step-by-step mapping from Eq. (2) to the practical algorithm: how mini-batches are sampled; how Υ, Φ, Ks, Kd, Ls, Ld, cross-Grams, and K̃−1 are computed; which quantities depend on w; how gradients flow only through Φ of generated Y; kernel choices and differentiability requirements; complexity per batch; and any stabilization tricks (bandwidth selection, λ, centering).

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and timely conditional extension of MMD training
- Simple, general framework with competitive results
- Bridges kernel embedding theory and deep conditional generation

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficiently clear objective intuition and algorithmic details
- Limited analysis of computational efficiency and scalability
- Derivation and assumptions around Eq. (2) are under-documented

With the above clarifications and stronger exposition, this could be a solid contribution.
