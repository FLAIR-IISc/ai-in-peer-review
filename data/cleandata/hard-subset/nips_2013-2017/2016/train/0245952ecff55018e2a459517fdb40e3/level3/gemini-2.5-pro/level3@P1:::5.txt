### Summary

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the recently proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN). While GMMNs learn a joint distribution by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model and data distributions, CGMMNs are designed to learn conditional distributions p(y|x). The core technical contribution is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. This objective measures the distance between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective conditional mean embedding operators, an idea building on prior work by Song et al. (2009). The generative model is a deep neural network that takes the conditional variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to produce a sample `y`. The model parameters are learned via stochastic gradient descent with backpropagation. The authors demonstrate the utility of CGMMN on a variety of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification), contextual image generation, and a "Bayesian dark knowledge" distillation task.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper presents a complete piece of work, with a clear model definition, a theoretically grounded training objective, and an empirical evaluation across multiple tasks. However, there is a significant concern regarding the technical soundness of the proposed CMMD estimator.

-   The paper defines the conditional embedding operator `C_{Y|X}` based on the cross-covariance operator `C_{XY}` (Theorem 2), which is correctly defined as a centered quantity: `C_{XY} := E_{XY}[φ(X)⊗φ(Y)] - μ_X⊗μ_Y`. However, the empirical estimator presented in Section 2.3, `Ĉ_{Y|X} = Φ(K+λI)⁻¹Υᵀ`, and the resulting CMMD objective in Equation (2) appear to be derived from uncentered moments. This formulation omits the terms corresponding to the empirical estimates of `μ_X` and `μ_Y`. This is a critical point that needs clarification. Is this an intentional simplification, and if so, what is the justification? Or is it an error in the derivation? This ambiguity raises questions about the correctness of the gradients used for training and the validity of the empirical results.

-   The experimental evaluation is another weak point. In the predictive tasks on MNIST and SVHN, the proposed CGMMN does not outperform the state-of-the-art baseline, CMMVA. For the generative tasks, the evaluation is purely qualitative. While the generated samples appear reasonable, the lack of quantitative comparisons to other strong conditional generative models (e.g., Conditional GANs) makes it difficult to assess the significance of the results. The paper would be much stronger with a more thorough and comparative empirical study.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for extending GMMNs to the conditional setting is clear and compelling. The preliminary sections provide a helpful overview of kernel mean embeddings and MMD. However, the technical concern mentioned above about the CMMD estimator also constitutes a clarity issue, as the derivation is not transparent. Additionally, the manuscript contains several minor typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "an universal RKHS", "data augumentation") that should be corrected in a final version.

**Originality:**
The paper's core idea is novel and significant. Extending generative moment matching to the conditional setting is a natural and important research direction. The formulation of a CMMD loss based on conditional mean embedding operators is an elegant and principled approach to this problem. While it builds on existing work in kernel methods, its application to training deep generative models is new and constitutes a valuable contribution to the field.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is high. A robust and effective method for training conditional generative models without the training instabilities of adversarial methods would be of great interest to the community. CGMMNs could be applied to a wide range of problems, as demonstrated by the diverse experiments. However, the current manuscript does not fully realize this potential due to the technical ambiguity and the somewhat disappointing experimental results. If the technical concerns can be resolved and the performance can be shown to be competitive with or superior to other methods, the impact of this work could be substantial.

### Detailed Comments

1.  **CMMD Estimator:** The most pressing issue is the apparent lack of centering in the empirical CMMD objective. The authors must clarify whether `Φ(K+λI)⁻¹Υᵀ` is the intended estimator and, if so, why the centering terms from the theoretical definition of the cross-covariance operator are omitted. Clarifying this point is essential for verifying the technical correctness of the method.

2.  **Generative Evaluation:** To demonstrate the value of CGMMN as a generative model, it is crucial to include comparisons against relevant baselines. The most obvious competitor is the Conditional GAN (Mirza & Osindero, 2014). A quantitative comparison (e.g., using Inception Score or Fréchet Inception Distance, if applicable, or other metrics for conditional generation) would greatly strengthen the paper's claims about its generative performance.

3.  **Predictive Performance:** While the predictive results are competitive, they lag behind the state-of-the-art CMMVA. It would be useful for the authors to discuss potential reasons for this performance gap. Is it a limitation of the moment-matching objective compared to a max-margin objective for classification, or could it be addressed with different network architectures or hyperparameter tuning?

### Conclusion

The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated framework, CGMMN, for learning conditional distributions using a moment-matching objective in RKHS. The core idea is elegant and represents a significant conceptual advance. However, the current manuscript is weakened by a potentially serious technical issue regarding the formulation of the CMMD estimator and an empirical evaluation that does not convincingly demonstrate an advantage over existing methods.

I believe the core contribution is valuable, but the paper is not ready for publication in its current form. I would be willing to reconsider my assessment if the authors can provide a satisfactory clarification regarding the CMMD estimator and strengthen their experimental comparisons.
