This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to the conditional setting. The core idea is to learn a conditional distribution *p(y|x)* by embedding it in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) and minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). The approach builds on previous work by Song et al. (2009) regarding kernel embedding of conditional distributions and recent advances in moment matching with GMMNs (Li et al., 2016). The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge transfer tasks using several datasets.

The approach is novel and well-motivated. Extending GMMNs to the conditional setting is a natural progression, and the use of RKHS provides a theoretically grounded framework. The potential applications highlighted – predictive modeling, contextual generation, and knowledge distillation – are all relevant and demonstrate the versatility of the proposed method. The authors correctly identify existing limitations of GANs and highlight the potential advantages of a moment-matching approach, namely a simpler optimization objective.

However, despite the appealing theoretical foundation and clear presentation, the paper suffers from a critical flaw in its empirical implementation and reports disappointing experimental results. A significant concern is the omission of centering terms in the definition of the empirical conditional operator \hat{C}_{Y|X}. The authors acknowledge that centering in the RKHS is assumed but provide no justification for this simplification. This omission raises serious questions about the correctness of the gradient computation and how the experiments were handled. Without clarification, it's difficult to assess the validity of the reported results. Furthermore, the experimental results themselves are underwhelming. CGMMN consistently underperforms the state-of-the-art CMMVA on both the MNIST and SVHN datasets for predictive modeling. The generative performance evaluation is also limited; there's no comparison to other conditional generative models like conditional GANs, hindering a proper assessment of the approach's strengths and weaknesses.

The paper also contains several minor issues, including typos ("Frobeniu norm", "an universal", "which the") that detract from its overall polish.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting.
*   Sound theoretical grounding based on RKHS and MMD.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Omission of centering terms in the CMMD calculation raises concerns about the correctness and validity of the results.
*   Disappointing experimental performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Limited evaluation of generative performance.
*   Minor typos and grammatical errors.

**Quality:** The technical soundness is questionable due to the missing centering terms. The claims are not fully supported by the experimental results, which are consistently worse than existing methods. The paper feels incomplete because of the unaddressed concerns regarding the centering issue.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the method easy to understand. However, the lack of justification for the centering omission detracts from the overall clarity.

**Originality:** The approach is original in extending GMMNs to the conditional setting using RKHS. However, it builds heavily on existing work by Song et al. (2009) and Li et al. (2016).

**Significance:** The results, as presented, do not significantly advance the state of the art. The potential significance is hampered by the lack of strong empirical validation and the unresolved concerns regarding the implementation.
