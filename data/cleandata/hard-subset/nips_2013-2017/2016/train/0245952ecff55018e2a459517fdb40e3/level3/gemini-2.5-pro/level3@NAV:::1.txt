This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep generative model for learning conditional distributions. The core contribution is a novel training objective based on Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (cMMD), which extends the MMD criterion used in prior work like Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN). By framing the learning problem as minimizing the discrepancy between the kernel mean embeddings of two conditional distributions, the model avoids the complex min-max optimization of adversarial methods. The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN on a range of tasks, including discriminative classification on MNIST and SVHN, conditional image generation, and distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model.

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength is its originality and significance. Extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting is a logical and valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling, analogous to the progression from GANs to conditional GANs. This work successfully fills a research void and broadens the applicability of MMD-based generative models to a wider class of problems. The proposed cMMD objective is an interesting theoretical development. The empirical evaluation is another strong point; the authors demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines on standard, albeit small, benchmarks for both discriminative and generative tasks, which validates the effectiveness of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite its contributions, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its impact. First, while the paper is reasonably clear overall, the mathematical exposition of the cMMD objective can be difficult to follow, which may hinder a full understanding of the underlying mechanics.

A more significant concern is reproducibility. The paper lacks sufficient architectural details and hyperparameter settings for an expert to exactly reproduce the reported results. For a contribution to be impactful, it must be verifiable. The authors are strongly encouraged to provide reference code and more detailed model descriptions, perhaps in an appendix, to substantially strengthen the paper.

Finally, the experimental evaluation could be improved. The training process for CGMMN appears computationally expensive, particularly the step involving kernel matrix inversions in the CMMD objective. A discussion of the computational complexity and a runtime comparison against other methods, such as Deeply-Supervised Nets (DSN) or conditional GANs, would provide valuable context on the practical trade-offs of this method. While the chosen tasks are appropriate, demonstrating the model's scalability on larger and more complex datasets would further bolster the paper's claims.
