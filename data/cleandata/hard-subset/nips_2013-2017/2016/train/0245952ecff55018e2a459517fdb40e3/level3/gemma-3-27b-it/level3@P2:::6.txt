This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a novel approach to learning conditional distributions by extending the framework of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN). The core idea is to utilize a conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to match the embedding of the empirical conditional distribution with that of a deep generative model. The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, demonstrating competitive performance across these tasks.

The paper builds upon recent advances in deep generative modeling, particularly the success of MMD as a criterion for training such models, avoiding the complexities of GANs.  The motivation for extending GMMN to the conditional setting is well-articulated, highlighting applications where modeling the joint distribution is less desirable than directly learning the conditional distribution p(y|x). This work connects to a broader line of research focused on conditional generative models, including Conditional GANs [Mirza et al., 2014] and Conditional Variational Autoencoders [Sohn et al., 2015].  The proposed CMMD criterion provides an alternative to adversarial training or variational inference, offering a potentially more stable and efficient learning process.

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear formulation of the conditional MMD objective and its practical implementation via backpropagation. The authors demonstrate that the CMMD objective can be efficiently computed, leveraging kernel tricks and avoiding the need for per-condition training as would be required by naively applying GMMN to each conditional distribution. The experimental results are encouraging, showing that CGMMN achieves competitive performance on diverse tasks, including challenging prediction problems on MNIST and SVHN datasets and successful distillation of knowledge from a Bayesian model. The authors also correctly identify the potential for applying CGMMN in a wider range of applications, such as contextual generation.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The presentation in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is dense and can be difficult to follow without consulting the referenced papers. While the mathematical notation is generally correct, a more intuitive explanation of the conditional embedding operator and the CMMD criterion would improve readability.  The statement that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect; it is asymptotically unbiased, and this distinction should be clarified. Furthermore, there’s a slight inconsistency in the definition of the conditional embedding operator compared to [Song et al., 2013], implying a deviation in the cross-covariance operator definition. A detailed justification for this divergence is warranted.

A key concern is the potential computational cost associated with the CMMD calculation, especially as the size of the mini-batch increases. While the authors acknowledge the cost of matrix inversions, a more in-depth discussion of strategies for mitigating this cost – such as using approximate inverse methods or low-rank approximations – would be beneficial.  The comparison to a naive baseline – training a separate GMMN for each conditional value – is mentioned as being hampered by data sparsity. Including empirical results for this baseline, even on a simplified task like MNIST with its low cardinality of classes, would further bolster the argument for the proposed approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated extension of GMMN to the conditional setting.
*   Clear mathematical formulation of the CMMD criterion and efficient implementation via backpropagation.
*   Competitive performance on a diverse set of tasks, demonstrating the practicality of the approach.
*   Addresses a key limitation of applying GMMN directly to conditional distributions by avoiding data sparsity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Sections 2.3 and 3.1 are difficult to follow and lack intuitive explanations.
*   Minor technical inaccuracies regarding the unbiasedness of MMD and the consistency of the conditional embedding operator definition.
*   Lack of empirical comparison to a naive baseline (separate GMMN per condition).
*   Limited discussion of computational considerations related to CMMD calculation and potential mitigation strategies.

**Overall assessment:**

Despite the aforementioned weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling. The proposed CGMMN framework offers a promising alternative to existing conditional generative models, and the experimental results demonstrate its potential for a wide range of applications. The paper addresses a significant challenge in conditional modeling and offers a technically sound and practical solution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity and technical issues raised above. Specifically, the authors should focus on improving the presentation of the mathematical derivations, correcting the inaccurate statement about MMD, justifying the deviation in the conditional embedding operator definition, and potentially including a comparison to a naive baseline. A brief discussion of computational optimization strategies for the CMMD calculation would also strengthen the paper.
