

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to conditional distributions. The authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for learning conditional distributions, building on kernel mean embedding techniques for conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009). CGMMN extends GMMN by incorporating conditional variables into the generative process, allowing for applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation. The approach maintains the simplicity of GMMN's training procedure via back-propagation, which is a significant advantage over more complex generative models.

The paper's strengths are clear: it presents a well-motivated extension of GMMN to conditional settings, addressing important applications that require conditional modeling. The theoretical foundation is generally sound, with the authors correctly identifying the need for conditional modeling in predictive tasks (where p(y|x) is more direct than p(x,y)) and contextual generation. The experimental evaluation covers multiple applications (predictive modeling on MNIST/SVHN, image generation on MNIST/Yale Face, and Bayesian knowledge distillation), demonstrating the versatility of the approach.

However, several significant weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution. First, the definition of the empirical conditional operator $\hat{C}_{Y|X}$ omits critical centering terms involving estimators of $\mu_X$ and $\mu_Y$, which are essential for proper kernel embedding. The authors assume centering in RKHS without justification, raising serious concerns about the validity of their gradient computation. This is particularly problematic as the gradient calculation is fundamental to their learning algorithm. The authors should clarify how they handled centering in their experiments and provide theoretical justification for omitting these terms.

The experimental results are disappointing. On the MNIST dataset, CGMMN-CNN achieves 1.5% error rate, while CMMVA (the state-of-the-art DGM for prediction) achieves 1.0%, and DSN achieves 0.8%. On SVHN, CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error rate compared to CMMVA's 3.09% and DSN's 1.92%. These results are not competitive with existing methods, undermining the paper's claim of "competitive performance." The authors should explain why CGMMN underperforms compared to other state-of-the-art methods.

The generative performance evaluation is particularly weak. The paper shows samples generated by CGMMN conditioned on class labels but provides no comparison to other conditional generative models (e.g., conditional GANs, conditional VAEs). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether CGMMN offers any advantage in generative quality. The paper also fails to discuss why CGMMN's generative samples appear noisier than those produced by the autoencoder-enhanced version, which could indicate a fundamental limitation of the approach.

The paper contains multiple grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "Frobeniu norm," "an universal," "which the"), which detract from its professionalism. While these are minor issues, they suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

The paper is novel in extending GMMN to conditional distributions, but the theoretical foundation is incomplete due to missing centering terms, and the experimental results are not compelling. The authors have not demonstrated that CGMMN provides significant advantages over existing methods for conditional generation or prediction. The paper's contribution appears to be primarily methodological rather than empirical, but the methodological extension is not fully justified.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Novel extension of GMMN to conditional distributions
- Well-motivated by important applications
- Simple training procedure via back-propagation
- Good experimental coverage across multiple tasks

Con arguments for rejection:
- Missing theoretical justification for omitting centering terms in conditional embedding
- Experimental results show CGMMN underperforms compared to state-of-the-art methods
- Generative performance lacks comparison to other conditional generative models
- Paper contains multiple grammatical errors and typos

While the paper addresses an important problem, the theoretical gaps and lackluster empirical results significantly weaken its contribution. The authors should address the centering issue before the paper can be considered for publication. Given the current state of the manuscript, it does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical foundation and stronger empirical results that demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.
