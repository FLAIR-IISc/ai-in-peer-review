This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN [19] to learn conditional distributions p(y|x) via a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective grounded in kernel embeddings of conditional distributions [29]. The key idea is to represent conditionals as operators CY|X in RKHS and to minimize the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between empirical operators from data and from a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w), where h is noise. The resulting objective admits back-propagation with a simple stochastic gradient procedure (Algorithm 1), avoiding the adversarial min–max of GANs [5] while generalizing MMD-based learning [7, 19] to the conditional case. Experiments span predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST digits and Yale Faces, including a variant that models codes from an autoencoder), and distilling Bayesian predictive posteriors (Bayesian “dark knowledge” [15]) where CGMMN serves as a student approximator to PBP [10]. Results are competitive with prior DGMs for prediction (e.g., MMVA [18]) and yield plausible conditional samples.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on kernel mean embeddings [7, 29] and GMMN [19], and relates to conditional GANs [21] and conditional VAEs [28] as alternative conditional DGMs. It also connects to work on distillation from Bayesian models [15]. Compared to cGANs and cVAEs, CGMMN provides a non-adversarial, likelihood-free conditional training criterion with straightforward SGD.

Quality: The methodological contribution is sound and well-motivated. The CMMD objective is derived by measuring differences between empirical conditional embedding operators, leading to a computable trace form in terms of Gram matrices on X (inputs) and Y (outputs) with Tikhonov regularization. Assumptions and caveats (e.g., operator conditions, practical regularized estimator) are acknowledged, and the dependence of inverses on data rather than parameters is noted (allowing non-differentiable kernels on x). The learning algorithm is simple and clearly described. Empirical validation covers classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation; while not state-of-the-art across the board, the results are credible and support the claims of flexibility and competitiveness. Some technical aspects would benefit from deeper analysis (see weaknesses), but overall the paper appears technically careful.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. Background on MMD and conditional embeddings is concise and sufficient for NIPS readers. The CMMD derivation is explicit, with helpful remarks connecting CMMD and MMD weighting. Algorithmic details (minibatch training, choice of noise, architectures) are presented with additional material in the appendix. Figures of generated samples and nearest-neighbor analyses aid interpretation.

Originality: The conditional extension is a natural but non-trivial development: moving from unconditional MMD to operator-based CMMD using conditional embeddings [29] and making this estimable with generated samples and back-propagation is a useful and, to my knowledge, novel combination. The application to Bayesian dark knowledge provides an interesting angle beyond standard generation/prediction benchmarks.

Significance: CGMMN offers a practical alternative to adversarial training for conditional generation and a general-purpose tool for conditional modeling and distillation. The simplicity of Algorithm 1, compatibility with arbitrary differentiable networks, and the ability to plug in various kernels on x make it attractive to practitioners. While the empirical gains are modest, the method broadens the toolbox for conditional generative learning and may spur further work on operator-matching objectives.

Strengths
- Clean, principled extension of GMMN to conditional distributions via CMMD; derivation and estimator are useful contributions [29, 7].
- Simple, stable training with back-propagation; avoids adversarial optimization.
- Broad applicability demonstrated: prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation [15].
- Compatible with CNN/MLP backbones and code-space modeling with autoencoders.
- Clear writing and thorough appendices.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental conceptually; the main step is importing conditional embeddings into the GMMN framework.
- Computational concerns: CMMD involves inverses of Gram matrices; minibatching alleviates cubic scaling but the statistical impact of minibatch CMMD is not analyzed.
- Kernel choices and bandwidth selection for x and y are not systematically studied; sensitivity and guidelines would help.
- Limited baselines for conditional generation (no direct comparisons to cGAN [21] or cVAE [28]); evaluation is mostly qualitative.
- Classification setup uses a single sampled y with argmax; comparisons to standard discriminative losses and calibration are limited.
- Bayesian distillation is shown only on Boston Housing; lacking broader UCI tests or comparisons to the original dark knowledge method [15].

Suggestions for improvement
- Include quantitative comparisons for conditional generation (e.g., Parzen estimates, classification accuracy of generated samples, or human study) and add cGAN/cVAE baselines.
- Analyze sensitivity to kernel type and bandwidth; describe selection protocol (e.g., median heuristic vs. cross-validation).
- Provide computational profiling (time/memory) and discuss scalability; consider Nyström/Random Features to reduce CMMD cost.
- Explore multiple samples at test time for classification and report calibration metrics (ECE/NLL).
- Expand Bayesian distillation experiments to more datasets and compare with [15].
- Release code to facilitate adoption.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, well-executed extension with clear practical value.
- Simple training objective that is likely more stable than adversarial methods.
- Diverse applications including an interesting Bayesian distillation use case.
- Clear exposition and reproducible methodology.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty; missing strong empirical comparisons to leading conditional DGMs.
- Limited quantitative evaluation of generation quality and scalability analysis.
- Bayesian distillation evidence is thin.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper provides a solid and useful conditional generalization of MMD-based generative modeling with clear methodological contributions and broadly applicable experiments. With stronger baselines and analysis, this could be a clear accept. Confidence: medium.
