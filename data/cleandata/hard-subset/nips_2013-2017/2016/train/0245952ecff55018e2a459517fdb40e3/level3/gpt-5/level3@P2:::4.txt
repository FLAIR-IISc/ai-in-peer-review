Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMNs to model conditional distributions P(Y|X) using conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). Building on kernel mean embeddings and the conditional embedding operator CY|X, the authors define a discrepancy between conditional distributions as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm of the difference between conditional embedding operators. They derive a practical empirical estimator that relies on Gram matrices, regularization, and simple sampling from a conditional deep generator y = f(x, h | w), where h is noise. Training proceeds by back-propagation through the generator to minimize CMMD, with a mini-batch algorithm to mitigate cubic kernel costs.

The approach is situated at the intersection of kernel methods for distributions (Gretton et al., Song et al.) and deep generative modeling. It is closely related to GMMN (Li et al., ICML’15), extending it to conditional settings in a manner analogous to cGAN (Mirza & Osindero) and conditional VAE (Sohn et al., NIPS’15). The paper also connects to Bayesian model distillation/dark knowledge (Korattikara et al., NIPS’15), offering CGMMN as a flexible student model.

Experiments cover: (i) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN by sampling class probabilities; (ii) contextual generation conditioned on labels for MNIST and Yale Faces, including an autoencoder code-space variant; and (iii) distillation of Bayesian neural networks (PBP) on Boston housing, showing no degradation in RMSE.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The CMMD objective is well motivated from conditional kernel embedding theory, with a clear empirical estimator and practical mini-batch training. Theorems link equality of conditional embedding operators to equality of conditional distributions under standard assumptions. While some smoothness assumptions may not strictly hold, the finite-sample estimator is well defined; this mirrors standard practice in kernel mean embedding literature.
- Optimization: The stochastic gradient procedure is straightforward, leveraging back-propagation; importantly, inverses depend only on X-kernels, so the kernel on X need not be differentiable.
- Empirics: Results are competitive for generative and predictive tasks, and the distillation experiment is convincing for preserving predictive accuracy. However, the empirical scope is modest (MNIST, SVHN, Yale, Boston), and comparisons to strong conditional generative baselines (cGAN, CVAE) are limited. Image generation evaluation is primarily qualitative and nearest-neighbor checks; more rigorous metrics (for the era, e.g., Parzen estimates or label-conditional MMD) would strengthen claims.
- Efficiency/scalability: Kernel Gram matrices introduce O(B^3) per-batch costs for inverses, which is acceptable for moderate batch sizes but may bottleneck larger-scale settings. Discussion on kernel choices and bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic) is limited; sensitivity analyses would be useful.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a helpful appendix. The derivation from conditional embeddings to the CMMD estimator is one of the strongest parts. Minor suggestions:
- Summarize computational complexity per mini-batch (time and memory), and note any tricks for stable inverses and λ selection.
- Clarify kernel choices and hyperparameter selection strategies for both X and Y, and report sensitivity.
- Explicitly compare different ways to combine x and h (concatenate vs corruption) in ablations.

Originality
The extension from MMD/GMMN to CMMD/CGMMN is natural but non-trivial. The operator-based CMMD loss and its empirical estimator are useful contributions that make conditional moment matching practical in deep models. Applying the framework to Bayesian dark knowledge is a nice demonstration of flexibility. Relative to prior conditional deep generators (cGAN, CVAE), the main novelty is a simple, divergence-free objective with kernel-based conditional matching.

Significance
Conditional generative modeling is central in many NeurIPS topics. CGMMN’s simplicity (no min–max, no variational bound) and generality make it attractive to practitioners. The method should be broadly useful for conditional synthesis, structured prediction, and distillation, and it fills a gap for conditional moment-matching approaches. The current experiments indicate competitiveness, though broader benchmarks and stronger baselines would increase impact.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, principled derivation of a conditional MMD objective from kernel embedding theory.
- Simple, stable training via back-propagation without adversarial min–max.
- Versatile applications demonstrated: prediction, conditional generation, Bayesian distillation.
- Competitive results with strong DGMs; straightforward algorithm likely to be adopted.
- Well written; appendix contains helpful details.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Experimental scope is limited; lacks thorough comparisons to cGAN/CVAE across tasks and datasets.
- Generative evaluation is mostly qualitative; limited quantitative metrics at scale.
- Computational overhead from kernel inverses may limit scalability; no empirical runtime analysis.
- Limited ablations on kernel choices, λ, batch size, and conditioning strategies.
- Theoretical assumptions are standard but practical guidance on when CMMD provides best gains is sparse.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please include comparisons to cGAN and CVAE on the same conditional generation tasks (both visual quality and conditional fidelity).
- Provide runtime/memory benchmarks and discuss how batch size and kernel bandwidth affect performance and stability.
- Add ablations on kernel types for X and Y, and bandwidth selection strategies; report sensitivity of test performance to λ.
- For prediction, can you exploit the stochastic nature to improve uncertainty calibration (e.g., ECE/NLL) beyond top-1 error?
- In the distillation setting, include calibration metrics and additional datasets to demonstrate broader utility.

Recommendation
Leaning accept. The paper offers a clean, well-motivated conditional extension of GMMN with a practical estimator and demonstrates utility across several tasks. With stronger empirical comparisons and analysis, it would be a solid addition to the NeurIPS program.
