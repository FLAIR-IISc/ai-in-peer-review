## Review of Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the framework of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to model conditional distributions. The core idea is to embed conditional distributions within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), building upon the work of Song et al. (2009). The authors present a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion and demonstrate its application in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

The motivation for CGMMN is well-articulated. Existing generative models often focus on joint distributions, while many applications require conditional modeling. The approach of leveraging RKHS and MMD offers a potentially attractive alternative to GANs and variational methods, promising simpler training dynamics. The paper clearly outlines the theoretical foundation, connecting it to established concepts like kernel embeddings and Hilbert-Schmidt norms. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. A significant concern lies in the definition of the empirical conditional operator, \hat{C}_{Y|X}. The omission of centering terms involving estimators of μ_X and μ_Y, despite assuming centering in the RKHS, is a critical flaw. This omission raises serious questions about the correctness of the gradient computation and how the experiments accounted for this centering issue. The authors need to provide a detailed justification for this simplification or demonstrate its impact on the results. 

Furthermore, while the experimental evaluation covers a range of tasks, the results are disappointing. CGMMN consistently underperforms compared to the state-of-the-art CMMVA on both MNIST and SVHN datasets. The generative performance evaluation is also weak, lacking comparisons to other conditional generative models like conditional GANs. This makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of CGMMN. The paper also contains several minor typos ("Frobeniu norm", "an universal", "which the") that detract from its polish.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The approach is novel and well-motivated, offering a potentially valuable extension to existing generative modeling techniques.
**Arguments against acceptance:** The theoretical justification is incomplete, the empirical results are uncompetitive, and the evaluation of generative performance is insufficient.

**Overall Assessment:** While the idea behind CGMMN is promising, the current implementation and evaluation are not strong enough for acceptance at a competitive conference like NIPS. The authors need to address the theoretical concerns regarding centering, improve the empirical performance, and provide a more thorough evaluation of the generative capabilities.
