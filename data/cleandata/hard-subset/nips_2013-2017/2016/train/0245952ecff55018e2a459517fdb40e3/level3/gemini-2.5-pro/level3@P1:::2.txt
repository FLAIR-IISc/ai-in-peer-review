This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional distributions p(y|x) within the framework of deep generative models. The work is a direct and significant extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which model a joint distribution by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model samples and data. The core technical contribution of this paper is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective, which measures the distance between conditional distributions by comparing their respective kernel mean embedding operators in a tensor product Hilbert space. The authors propose a simple generative process where samples are produced by a deep neural network that takes both the conditional variable x and a random noise vector h as input. The model is trained end-to-end using back-propagation. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness and versatility of CGMMN on a range of tasks, including classification, conditional image generation, and "Bayesian dark knowledge" distillation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the important problem of conditional density estimation using moment-matching techniques. Extending GMMN from the joint to the conditional setting is a natural and valuable contribution that significantly broadens the applicability of this class of models. Conditional models are fundamental to most supervised learning tasks, and this work provides a new, non-adversarial approach for training deep conditional generators. This extension is non-trivial; the challenge lies in matching a whole family of output distributions corresponding to different conditioning inputs, rather than matching a single joint distribution.

2.  **Broad Applicability:** The authors do an excellent job of demonstrating the model's versatility. The experiments convincingly cover three distinct and relevant problem domains: standard predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (generating images from labels), and model compression (distilling a Bayesian model into a faster student network).

3.  **Competitive Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are strong across the board. The model achieves competitive error rates on MNIST and SVHN classification, generates plausible and diverse conditional samples on image datasets, and successfully distills a Bayesian model with negligible performance loss. This suggests that the proposed CMMD objective is practically effective.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of the paper is its lack of clarity in explaining the core technical contribution—the CMMD objective and its practical implementation. This significantly hinders the reader's ability to understand the method, assess its technical soundness, and reproduce the results.

1.  **Lack of Intuition:** The paper begins with a heavy mathematical formalism involving operators on tensor product spaces (Theorem 2 & 3). While formally correct, it fails to provide an intuitive explanation for the CMMD objective. The standard MMD has a simple and powerful intuition: two distributions are identical if and only if their mean embeddings in a sufficiently rich RKHS are the same. The paper does not build a similar intuitive bridge for the conditional case. It is not made clear why comparing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of conditional embedding *operators* is the natural and correct extension for measuring the distance between `p(y|x)` and `q(y|x)`.

2.  **Opacity of the Objective Function and Algorithm:** The leap from the theoretical definition of CMMD to the final objective in Equation (2) is abrupt and insufficiently explained. This equation is the heart of the algorithm, yet its derivation is omitted. The reader is left to wonder how the trace of various Gram matrix products relates to the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between conditional embedding operators. The terms in Equation (2) are defined abstractly, but it is not explicitly stated how they are constructed from a mini-batch of real data `{(xi, yi)}` and a corresponding mini-batch of generated data. This opacity makes it very difficult to understand what computations are actually being performed.

3.  **Missing Computational Details:** The algorithm description is high-level. Equation (2) involves matrix inversions (e.g., `(Kd + λI)⁻¹`), which are `O(N³)` in the batch size `N`. The paper does not discuss the computational cost or how this is managed with the specified mini-batch sizes (e.g., 500 for MNIST). Are these inversions performed explicitly on every iteration? Are there approximations used? This is a critical practical detail that is entirely absent.

**Detailed Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper's potential is high, but its impact is currently limited by the clarity issues mentioned above. I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the manuscript to address these points. Specifically:

*   **Provide an intuitive derivation of the CMMD objective.** It would be immensely helpful if the authors could walk the reader from the goal of minimizing an averaged distance `E_x[MMD²(p(y|x), q(y|x))]` to the final kernelized objective in Equation (2). This would ground the complex formalism in a more accessible concept.

*   **Clarify the concrete algorithm for computing the objective and its gradient.** Please provide a clear, step-by-step description or pseudo-code for a single mini-batch update. Given a mini-batch of real pairs `B = {(x_i, y_i)}` for `i=1...N`:
    1.  How are the generated samples `B' = {(x_i, y'_i)}` created? Are the same `x_i` from the real batch used? The paper seems to imply this.
    2.  How, precisely, are the Gram matrices `Kd`, `Ks`, `Ld`, `Ls`, `Ksd`, `Lds` constructed from `B` and `B'`? For example, is `Kd` the Gram matrix of `x`'s from `B'` and `Ks` the Gram matrix of `x`'s from `B`? A concrete definition is needed.
    3.  How is the gradient with respect to the network parameters `w` (which only influence `y'_i`) computed through the trace operations and matrix inversions in Equation (2)?

Addressing these questions would transform the paper from a promising but opaque work into a clear, understandable, and reproducible scientific contribution.
