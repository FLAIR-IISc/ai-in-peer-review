

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the unconditional GMMN framework [Li et al., 2015] to conditional distributions via a novel conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The authors derive a practical estimator for CMMD using kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, enabling gradient-based training through backpropagation. CGMMN generates samples conditioned on input variables by combining them with latent noise in a deep network, then optimizes the CMMD objective between empirical and generated conditional distributions. The approach is evaluated across predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston Housing).

**Strengths**  
*Quality & Technical Soundness*: The derivation of CMMD (Section 3.1) is rigorous, building on kernel embedding theory [Song et al., 2009] and providing a clear empirical estimator (Eq. 2). The training algorithm (Algorithm 1) is straightforward and leverages standard deep learning machinery. Experiments are comprehensive, with competitive results across diverse tasks: CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error on SVHN (comparable to state-of-the-art CMMVA [Li et al., 2015]), generates class-conditioned MNIST samples (Fig. 2), and successfully distills Bayesian predictive distributions without performance degradation (Table 3). Theoretical claims are well-supported by experiments.  
*Clarity & Presentation*: The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem setup (Section 1), technical preliminaries (Section 2), and method (Section 3) flow logically. Algorithm 1 and the appendix provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The writing avoids excessive jargon, making the contribution accessible.  
*Originality*: While conditional generative models exist (e.g., conditional GANs [Mirza & Osindero, 2014]), CGMMN’s CMMD-based approach is novel. The key insight—extending MMD to conditionals via kernel embedding operators (Theorem 3)—is non-trivial and fills a clear gap in moment-matching literature. The paper adequately distinguishes itself from related work (e.g., conditional VAEs [Soň et al., 2015]) in Section 5.  
*Significance*: CGMMN offers a practical, plug-and-play framework for conditional generation with minimal implementation overhead. Its success in Bayesian distillation (Section 4.3) demonstrates direct applicability to real-world challenges like model compression, where efficiency is critical. The simplicity of training (via backprop) makes it highly relevant for practitioners building conditional generative models.

**Weaknesses**  
*Limited Comparison to Conditional GANs*: The paper compares CGMMN primarily to non-generative baselines (e.g., DSN, Maxout) and unconditional GMMN. A direct comparison to conditional GAN variants (e.g., [Mirza & Osindero, 2014], [Zhang et al., 2016]) would strengthen the significance claim, as GANs dominate conditional generation.  
*Theoretical Constraints*: The CMMD derivation assumes smoothness conditions (e.g., \(C^{-3/2}_{XX} C_{XY}\) Hilbert-Schmidt [Grunewalder et al., 2012]), which may not hold in all cases. While the empirical estimator is well-justified, the paper could more explicitly discuss limitations under weak assumptions.  
*Experimental Depth*: The Bayesian distillation experiment (Section 4.3) is promising but limited to a single regression task (Boston Housing). Including results on classification or higher-dimensional data would better demonstrate generalizability.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments**  
*Pro*:  
- Fills a clear gap in conditional moment-matching literature with a technically sound, implementable framework.  
- Demonstrates broad applicability (prediction, generation, distillation) with competitive results.  
- Presentation is exemplary—readers can reproduce the method from the paper.  
- Directly addresses a practical need: efficient conditional generation without min-max optimization.  

*Con*:  
- The core idea (conditional extension) is conceptually straightforward; the novelty lies in the CMMD derivation, which may not be sufficiently disruptive for a top-tier venue.  
- Comparison to state-of-the-art conditional generative models (beyond GANs) is insufficient.  

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a clear contribution to conditional generative modeling. The CMMD derivation is novel and practically valuable, and the empirical results are compelling across multiple domains. While the theoretical novelty is moderate, the work’s significance lies in its simplicity, broad applicability, and immediate utility for practitioners. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), distinct from prior work (Originality), and impactful for real-world applications (Significance). The weaknesses are minor and addressable in revision; they do not undermine the core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
