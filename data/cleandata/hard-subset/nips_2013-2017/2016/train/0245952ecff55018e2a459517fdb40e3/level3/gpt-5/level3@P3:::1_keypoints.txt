- Proposal: Introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) trained by minimizing conditional MMD between true and model conditionals using conditional mean embeddings.
- Relation to prior work: Extends GMMN to conditional settings and positions CGMMN among CVAE, cGAN, MMVA, and MMD-based two-sample tests.
- Key advantage: Provides a single-sided, non-adversarial loss avoiding min–max optimization while keeping flexible neural generators.
- Empirical evaluation: Tested on discriminative prediction (MNIST, SVHN), class-conditional generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and Bayesian distillation (Boston Housing).
- Performance summary: Results are competitive with GMMN-style models and near other DGMs but below strong discriminative baselines like DSN.
- Strength — conceptual: Clean, technically relevant extension of MMD to conditional distributions via operator norm between conditional embeddings.
- Strength — stability: Avoids adversarial training pathologies while enabling both conditional generation and prediction.
- Strength — empirical scope: Covers classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation with reasonable qualitative samples.
- Strength — theory/practice: cMMD objective grounded in RKHS theory and straightforward to optimize with backprop once Gram matrices are computed.
- Strength — practical details: Addresses mini-batching, discrete-condition handling, and sampling protocol.
- Weakness — exposition: Mathematical exposition is dense and assumptions/regularization/consistency are not easy for non-experts to follow.
- Weakness — scalability: Computing and inverting K+λI per mini-batch is O(B^3), potentially expensive with large batch sizes and no runtime/memory comparisons provided.
- Weakness — experiments: Limited to small, standard benchmarks with no quantitative generative metrics beyond visual inspection and NN analyses.
- Weakness — reproducibility: Missing key implementation details (kernels, bandwidths, λ, normalization, noise settings) and no reference code.
- Weakness — computational comparison: No wall-clock or computational overhead comparison versus strong discriminative baselines (e.g., DSN) or cross-entropy training.
- Weakness — distillation experiment: Small-scale (Boston Housing) and reports only RMSE without NLL, calibration, uncertainty quality, or speed-up metrics.
- Quality — technical soundness: Formulation is well-motivated and standard regularization used, but finite-sample behavior and estimator bias/variance are unanalyzed.
- Quality — empirical support: Classification and generation results are promising but would benefit from stronger, more diverse benchmarks and quantitative metrics.
- Clarity: Paper is organized but needs clearer derivations, explicit kernel/hyperparameter guidance, and complexity analysis for practitioners.
- Originality: Novel and non-trivial extension of MMD training to conditional models that fills a gap between unconditional GMMN and conditional DGMs.
- Significance: Provides a stable, non-adversarial alternative for conditional generative modeling with potential for follow-up work on scalability and structured outputs.
- Major questions: Requests clarification on kernels, bandwidth selection, λ sensitivity, mini-batch recomputation/stability, number of noise samples, and test-time sampling/averaging.
- Suggested improvements: Release code, provide runtime and scalability analysis, consider random features/Nyström approximations, expand experiments, and include quantitative generative evaluation.
- Pros for acceptance: Principled extension of MMD to conditional models, relevant to deep generative modeling, and competitive empirical results across tasks.
- Cons for acceptance: Limited empirical scale, lack of quantitative generative metrics, missing runtime analysis, and reproducibility gaps.
- Overall assessment: Promising and timely contribution with solid grounding; leans toward acceptance if authors add runtime comparisons, clarify hyperparameters/kernels, and commit to releasing code, otherwise borderline.
