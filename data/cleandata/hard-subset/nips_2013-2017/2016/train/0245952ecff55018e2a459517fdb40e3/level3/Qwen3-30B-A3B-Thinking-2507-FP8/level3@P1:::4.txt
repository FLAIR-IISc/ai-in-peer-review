

The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to conditional generation via a novel Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. Building on GMMN (Li et al., ICML 2015), which uses MMD for unconditional generative modeling, CGMMN addresses a key gap: learning conditional distributions \(p(y|x)\) without the min-max instability of conditional GANs (e.g., Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Gulrajani et al., NIPS 2017). The core innovation lies in deriving CMMD as the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, enabling gradient-based optimization via backpropagation—retaining GMMN’s simplicity while generalizing to conditional settings. This directly advances NIPS 2015–2016 work on kernel-based generative models (e.g., Gretton et al., JMLR 2012; Song et al., ICML 2013) and addresses the need for scalable conditional generative tools highlighted in recent NIPS papers (e.g., conditional VAEs by Sohn et al., NIPS 2015).

**Strengths**  
*Technical Soundness & Novelty*: The derivation of CMMD (Theorems 2–3) is rigorous and non-trivial, resolving the challenge of conditional distribution comparison under continuous \(x\). By leveraging kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML 2013), the authors avoid ad-hoc assumptions and provide a theoretically grounded estimator (Eq. 2), which is a significant step beyond prior conditional GANs. The empirical estimator’s implementation (Algorithm 1) is elegant, using mini-batches and tensor products without requiring differentiable kernels on \(x\), making it practical. The framework’s simplicity—reusing GMMN’s training machinery—highlights its novelty: while conditional GANs (e.g., Mirza & Osindero, 2014) add complexity via min-max, CGMMN retains the stable, single-objective training of GMMN.  

*Clarity & Presentation*: The paper is exceptionally clear, with well-structured sections (Preliminaries, CMMD derivation, CGMMN architecture) and a helpful appendix. The motivation for conditional modeling (Section 1) effectively contextualizes the work against predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian distillation—three high-impact applications. The experimental setup is transparent, with ablation studies (e.g., MLP vs. CNN on MNIST/SVHN) and ablation of hidden dimension (Section 4.1.1) demonstrating careful design.  

*Significance & Impact*: CGMMN achieves competitive results across diverse tasks:  
- **Predictive modeling**: CGMMN-CNN matches CMMVA (a state-of-the-art discriminative DGM; Li et al., NIPS 2015) on MNIST (1.14% error vs. 1.17%) and SVHN (3.13% vs. 3.09% error), outperforming unsupervised baselines like VAE.  
- **Contextual generation**: On MNIST and Yale Face datasets, CGMMN generates class-conditioned samples that are diverse and not mere copies (Fig. 3), with autoencoder-augmented results (Fig. 4) showing improved smoothness.  
- **Bayesian dark knowledge**: CGMMN distills PBP models (Hernández-Lobato & Adams, ICML 2015) without performance degradation (RMSE 2.580 ± 0.093 vs. 2.574 ± 0.089), offering a faster alternative to Bayesian inference.  
These results demonstrate broad applicability, directly addressing practitioners’ needs for scalable conditional generative models in prediction, generation, and knowledge distillation—where prior work (e.g., conditional GANs) often suffers from training instability or complexity.

**Weaknesses**  
While technically robust, the paper could strengthen its comparison to *conditional GAN variants*. Though CGMMN outperforms CMMVA on SVHN (Table 2), a direct comparison with conditional GANs (e.g., InfoGAN, Chen et al., ICML 2016) would better contextualize its advantages. The authors note that CGMMN’s simplicity avoids min-max instability, but this claim would be more persuasive with a brief qualitative analysis (e.g., training stability curves). Additionally, the conditional variable dimension (e.g., 20 for MNIST) is chosen empirically without ablation; a brief exploration of how dimensionality affects performance would add depth.  

**Overall Assessment**  
CGMMN makes a strong, well-executed contribution to the generative modeling literature. It solves a clear problem (conditional generation) with a novel yet practical extension of GMMN, validated across three significant applications. The technical derivation is sound, the implementation is straightforward (Algorithm 1), and the empirical results are competitive with state-of-the-art methods. The paper’s clarity, thoroughness, and relevance to NIPS’ focus on scalable, principled deep learning make it a compelling candidate for acceptance. While minor improvements in comparative analysis would elevate it further, the work’s significance—enabling simpler, stable conditional generation for practitioners—far outweighs these limitations.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*: Novel framework (CMMD) with rigorous derivation; practical implementation matching GMMN’s simplicity; competitive results across diverse tasks (prediction, generation, distillation); clear, well-organized presentation.  
*Con*: Limited comparison to conditional GAN variants; no ablation on conditional variable dimension.  

This paper advances the state of the art in generative modeling by providing a theoretically grounded, easy-to-use conditional generative framework—directly addressing a key gap in NIPS 2015–2017 work on kernel-based methods. It is technically sound, highly relevant, and poised to impact both research and practice.
