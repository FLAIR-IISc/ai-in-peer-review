**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning deep generative models of conditional distributions p(y|x). This work is a direct and significant extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which model joint distributions by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between generated samples and real data. The core contribution of this paper is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective, which measures the distance between two conditional distributions in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space by comparing their conditional mean embedding operators. This CMMD objective allows the GMMN framework to be extended to the conditional setting, enabling training via standard backpropagation. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach on a wide range of tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, contextual image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and distilling knowledge from a complex Bayesian model into a simpler student network.

This work fits well within the landscape of deep generative models. It provides a moment-matching analogue to other conditional generative models like Conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and Conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015). While GANs rely on a difficult-to-train min-max adversarial objective, CGMMN offers a simpler single objective function, which can lead to more stable training. The theoretical underpinnings are based on established work in kernel mean embeddings, particularly the embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the CMMD and its empirical estimator (Eq. 2) is the central technical contribution and appears to be sound, building logically upon prior work on conditional mean embeddings. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that convincingly demonstrate the model's capabilities across three distinct and relevant domains. The work is presented as a complete contribution, including theoretical motivation, model architecture, a clear learning algorithm, and thorough empirical validation. The authors are also careful in their evaluation, for instance by checking whether the model simply copies training data and being transparent about performance relative to state-of-the-art discriminative models in the prediction tasks.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a concise but sufficient background on kernel mean embeddings and MMD before introducing their CMMD criterion. The motivation for conditional modeling is well-articulated, and the connection between the proposed CMMD and the original MMD objective is thoughtfully explained in Remark 1. The model architecture and training procedure (Algorithm 1) are simple to understand. The inclusion of an appendix with further details on the algorithm and experiments adds to the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

**Originality:** The main novelty lies in the formulation of the CMMD objective and its application to train a deep conditional generative model. While extending GMMNs to the conditional setting is a natural conceptual step, the derivation of a practical, kernel-based empirical estimator for the discrepancy between conditional operators is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. This provides a new, principled way to train conditional deep generative models without resorting to adversarial training or variational inference. The application of this framework to distill knowledge from Bayesian models is also a novel and interesting use case that demonstrates the model's flexibility.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant as it greatly broadens the applicability of the moment-matching approach to deep generative modeling. Conditional models are fundamental to a vast number of problems in machine learning, and this paper provides a powerful and practical tool for this setting. The relative simplicity and stability of the training procedure compared to adversarial methods make CGMMN an attractive option for both researchers and practitioners. The strong performance across diverse tasks suggests that this framework is not just a theoretical curiosity but a versatile and effective method. It is highly likely that other researchers will use and build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Strong theoretical contribution:** The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated CMMD objective for comparing conditional distributions, which is a significant extension of the popular MMD criterion.
*   **Practical and versatile framework:** The proposed CGMMN is straightforward to implement and train using standard backpropagation, and its effectiveness is demonstrated on prediction, generation, and model distillation tasks.
*   **Clear alternative to existing methods:** It provides a compelling alternative to Conditional GANs, potentially offering more stable training by avoiding the min-max game.
*   **High clarity and completeness:** The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and presents a complete piece of work from theory to extensive experimentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The generated image quality, while good, may not match the state-of-the-art from contemporary GAN-based methods. However, the authors acknowledge this and show that quality can be improved by combining CGMMN with an autoencoder, which is a reasonable and practical solution.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a solid and useful contribution to the field of deep generative models. The extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting is an important step, and the authors execute it elegantly both in theory and in practice. The paper is technically sound, clearly presented, and the results demonstrate significant impact and versatility.
