This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment Matching Network (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions via a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The approach learns a conditional distribution p(y|x) by sampling from a simple distribution and passing it, along with x, through a deep network. The method is evaluated on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation tasks with competitive results.

The work builds upon recent advancements in deep generative modeling, particularly GMMN [19] and GANs [5], offering an alternative to the often-difficult-to-optimize min-max formulation of GANs. The motivation for focusing on conditional distributions is well articulated, highlighting their importance in various applications like predictive modeling and building larger generative models [1, 21]. The use of MMD as a moment matching criterion, as introduced by Gretton et al. [7], provides a theoretically sound basis for the generative process. Extending this to the conditional setting via CMMD is a natural and potentially impactful direction.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Extension:** The paper successfully extends the GMMN framework to conditional distributions, a valuable addition to the toolkit of deep generative modeling techniques. This broadens the applicability of moment-matching approaches beyond joint distribution estimation.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The approach is grounded in solid theoretical principles, leveraging kernel methods and MMD to define and optimize the conditional generative model. The connection to Hilbert space embeddings provides a principled way to handle distributions without making strong parametric assumptions.
* **Competitive Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that CGMMN achieves competitive performance on a variety of tasks, indicating its practical effectiveness. The results on MNIST and SVHN for predictive modeling are particularly encouraging.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for exploring conditional generative models and highlights their advantages over joint models in specific scenarios.
* **Well-Structured:** The paper is generally well-organized, with a logical flow from introducing the problem to presenting the proposed method, experiments, and conclusions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Mathematical Clarity:** While the underlying theory is sound, the mathematical exposition can be challenging to follow at times.  The derivation of the CMMD objective (Equation 2) and the associated notation could benefit from more detailed explanation and potentially breaking it down into smaller steps.
* **Limited Scale of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation, while thorough for the chosen tasks, involves relatively small datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face). While comparable to other work in the area, scaling up to larger and more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would strengthen the claim of broader applicability.
* **Computational Cost:** The training process, involving kernel computations and potentially inverse matrix operations, appears computationally expensive. A runtime comparison with other generative models like DSN [17] or GANs would be highly valuable to assess the practical feasibility of the approach.  The mini-batch optimization strategy mentioned in Algorithm 1 is a good step, but a more detailed discussion of its efficiency and scalability is needed.
* **Reproducibility:**  A significant concern is the lack of publicly available code.  Providing reference code is crucial for verifying the results, fostering further research, and ensuring reproducibility – a key tenet of scientific contributions.
* **Model Specificity:** The description of the evaluated models (e.g., the MLP and CNN architectures) lacks sufficient detail.  Precise specifications (e.g., number of layers, kernel sizes, activation functions) are needed to allow practitioners to accurately reproduce the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound extension of GMMNs.
* Demonstrates competitive performance on a variety of tasks.
* Addresses an important problem in deep generative modeling.
* The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Mathematical exposition can be difficult to follow.
* Limited scale of experiments.
* Lack of runtime comparison with other methods.
* Absence of publicly available code hinders reproducibility.
* Insufficient detail in model descriptions for reproduction.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling. The extension of GMMNs to conditional distributions via CMMD is a novel and theoretically well-motivated approach. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating competitive performance on several tasks. However, the concerns regarding mathematical clarity, scalability, reproducibility, and model specificity need to be addressed.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should prioritize providing reference code, improving the clarity of the mathematical exposition, and including a runtime comparison with other relevant methods. Furthermore, they should provide more detailed specifications of the evaluated models to facilitate reproducibility. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
