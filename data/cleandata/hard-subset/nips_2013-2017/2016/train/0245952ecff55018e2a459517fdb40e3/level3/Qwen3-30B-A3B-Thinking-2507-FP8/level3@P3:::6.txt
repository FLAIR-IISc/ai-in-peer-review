

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper extends Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMNs) to the conditional setting, introducing Conditional GMMNs (CGMMNs) that learn conditional distributions via a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The authors build upon the GMMN framework (Li et al., ICML 2015) which used MMD to learn joint distributions, extending it to handle conditional distributions where some input variables are given. This is a natural extension addressing important applications including predictive modeling (where conditional distributions p(y|x) are more direct than joint distributions p(x,y)), contextual generation (e.g., generating images conditioned on class labels), and Bayesian model distillation.

The key contribution is the development of a method to treat all conditional distributions as a family and match the conditional embedding operator directly, avoiding the data sparsity problem that plagues the naive approach of training a separate GMMN per conditional value. The authors provide a clean formulation of the conditional MMD that can be computed similarly to the unconditional MMD, with K matrices in Eq. 2 independent of model parameters, allowing standard backpropagation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear technical contribution:** The paper successfully extends GMMNs to the conditional setting in a theoretically sound manner, addressing a real limitation of the naive approach. The conditional MMD formulation is elegant and well-motivated.

2. **Strong empirical results:** The method demonstrates competitive performance across diverse tasks including classification (MNIST and SVHN datasets), conditional generation (MNIST and Yale Face datasets), and Bayesian model distillation (Boston housing regression). The results are convincing and show the approach is practical.

3. **Practical implementation:** The CGMMN architecture is straightforward to implement using standard neural network components, and the training procedure follows naturally from the MMD framework.

4. **Significant applications:** The paper demonstrates the approach on meaningful tasks that benefit from conditional modeling, showing real-world applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Minor technical errors:** The paper incorrectly claims the MMD estimator is unbiased (it's asymptotically unbiased but not unbiased), and the definition of the conditional embedding operator differs from reference [29], implying a different cross-covariance operator definition is needed. These should be corrected.

2. **Presentation issues:** Sections 2.3 (Kernel Embedding of Conditional Distributions) and 3.1 (Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy) are hard to follow without consulting referenced papers. A more self-contained explanation would greatly improve clarity.

3. **Practical concern about minibatch size:** The paper notes that CMMD may require large minibatches to avoid stochastic noise, but large minibatches make computation expensive due to matrix inverses. The authors should discuss how they addressed this in practice or propose potential solutions.

4. **Lack of comparison to naive baseline:** While the paper mentions the naive baseline (training a GMMN per conditional distribution), it doesn't provide a fair comparison. The naive approach may perform reasonably well with low-cardinality discrete conditions (e.g., MNIST), which should be explicitly compared.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundation and well-supported experimental results. The main weaknesses are minor technical errors and presentation issues rather than fundamental flaws.

**Clarity:** The paper would benefit from clearer explanations of the conditional embedding operator and better organization of Sections 2.3 and 3.1. The core ideas are sound but not as accessible as they could be.

**Originality:** The extension of GMMNs to conditional distributions is novel and represents a meaningful advancement in generative modeling. The approach of directly matching the conditional embedding operator is a clean and original solution to the data sparsity problem.

**Significance:** The work addresses a significant limitation in conditional generative modeling and provides a simple, effective solution applicable to multiple important tasks. The method can be readily integrated with existing deep learning frameworks and has clear potential for broader application.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper makes a valuable contribution to generative modeling by extending GMMNs to the conditional setting in a theoretically sound and practically effective manner. The empirical results are strong across diverse applications, and the approach has clear potential for adoption by the community.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The minor technical errors and presentation issues, while not fatal, do reduce the paper's quality. The lack of comparison to the naive baseline and insufficient discussion of the minibatch size concern are notable omissions.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to generative modeling. The minor technical errors and presentation issues are easily fixable, and the core ideas are compelling. The paper would benefit from careful revision to address the clarity concerns and technical inaccuracies before acceptance. The work is sufficiently novel, technically sound, and significant to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
