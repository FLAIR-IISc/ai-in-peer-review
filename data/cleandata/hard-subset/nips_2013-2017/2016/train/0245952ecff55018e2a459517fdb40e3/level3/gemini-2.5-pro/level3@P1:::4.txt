This paper presents Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the previously proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to model conditional distributions. The core idea is to train a deep generative model, which maps a conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` to an output `y`, by minimizing a novel objective function called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). CMMD is a non-parametric measure of divergence between two conditional distributions, based on the distance between their respective kernel mean embedding operators in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors derive an empirical estimator for the CMMD objective that can be calculated from samples and, crucially, is differentiable with respect to the generative model's parameters. This allows the network to be trained end-to-end using standard stochastic gradient descent and backpropagation.

This work builds directly on GMMN [Li et al., 2015; Dziugaite et al., 2015], which uses Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to learn joint distributions. The proposed CGMMN framework provides the conditional counterpart, analogous to how Conditional GANs [Mirza & Osindero, 2014] and Conditional VAEs [Sohn et al., 2015] extend their respective unconditional frameworks. The key technical contribution lies in leveraging the theory of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions [Song et al., 2009] to derive a practical and trainable loss function. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach on three distinct tasks: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation of images, and "Bayesian dark knowledge," where the CGMMN is used to distill a complex Bayesian model into a faster student network.

### Strengths

1.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation and Novel Objective:** The main contribution of the paper is the development of the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The extension from MMD to CMMD is a natural and important step for moment-matching networks, but the derivation of a practical, empirical estimator based on kernel embedding operators is non-trivial. The work is technically sound and provides a principled, non-adversarial objective for training conditional generative models.

2.  **Practicality and Simplicity:** Despite the theoretical complexity of RKHS operators, the final training algorithm is remarkably simple and practical (Algorithm 1). The CMMD loss can be computed using kernel Gram matrices, and its gradient can be obtained via backpropagation. This makes the framework easy to implement within standard deep learning libraries and offers a stable training alternative to the often challenging min-max optimization of Conditional GANs.

3.  **Broad Applicability and Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper effectively showcases the flexibility of the CGMMN framework by applying it to a diverse set of problems. The experiments on predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and model distillation are convincing. The model achieves competitive performance across these tasks, demonstrating its utility as a general-purpose tool for conditional density estimation. The "Bayesian dark knowledge" application is particularly interesting and highlights a unique use case for this type of generative model.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides a clear introduction to the necessary background on MMD and conditional kernel embeddings before smoothly transitioning to the proposed method. The motivation for conditional modeling is well-articulated, and the experimental setup and results are presented clearly. The helpful appendix provides further details that aid in understanding and reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The empirical CMMD estimator (Eq. 2) requires the inversion of kernel matrices whose size is determined by the mini-batch size (B). This operation has a computational complexity of O(B^3), which can become a bottleneck for large batch sizes commonly used in deep learning. This contrasts with GANs or VAEs, whose per-batch cost is typically linear in B. While the paper uses a mini-batch approach, a discussion on the computational trade-offs and potential avenues for scaling up the CMMD computation (e.g., via random Fourier features or other approximations) would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Generative Quality:** The quality of the generated MNIST samples shown in Figure 2 appears somewhat noisy and less sharp compared to results from state-of-the-art GANs from the same period. The authors acknowledge this and demonstrate that combining CGMMN with an autoencoder yields much cleaner samples (Figure 4). However, this suggests that the base CGMMN model may not be sufficient on its own for generating high-fidelity images and might benefit from architectural improvements or a different choice of kernel. A qualitative comparison with samples from a Conditional GAN would have been useful for context.

### Review Criteria Assessment

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed CMMD objective is well-founded in statistical learning theory, and the claims are rigorously supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The work is presented as a complete and polished contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is extremely clear and well-written. The structure is logical, and the authors have taken care to explain complex concepts accessibly. It provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.
*   **Originality:** The paper makes a significant original contribution. While the idea of conditional generative modeling is not new, this is the first work to propose a practical and effective training objective for deep conditional models based on moment matching in an RKHS. The derivation of the empirical CMMD estimator is a novel and key insight.
*   **Significance:** This work is significant because it broadens the applicability of MMD-based deep generative models, providing a valuable and stable alternative to adversarial training. The demonstrated successes in diverse areas, especially model distillation, suggest that the CGMMN framework is likely to be used and built upon by other researchers and practitioners in the field.

### Summary

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, theoretically principled, and practical framework for conditional generative modeling. It successfully extends moment-matching networks to the conditional setting and demonstrates competitive performance on a wide range of important tasks. The clarity of the presentation and the significance of the contribution are high. While there are minor concerns about computational scaling and the visual quality of generated samples, the strengths of the paper far outweigh these weaknesses. This paper is a clear contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS.
