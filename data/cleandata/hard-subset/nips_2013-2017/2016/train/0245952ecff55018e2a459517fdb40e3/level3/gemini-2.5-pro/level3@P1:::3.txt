This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions. The core idea is to replace the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) objective used in GMMN with a novel Conditional MMD (CMMD) loss. This CMMD objective is derived from the theory of Hilbert space embeddings of conditional distributions, measuring the discrepancy between conditional operators rather than unconditional mean embeddings. The generative model itself is a deep neural network that takes a conditional variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to produce a sample `y`. The authors demonstrate the versatility of this framework on three distinct tasks: predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (generating MNIST digits and Yale faces conditioned on class labels), and "Bayesian dark knowledge," where CGMMN is used to distill a complex Bayesian model into a faster student network. This work builds directly upon GMMN (Li et al., 2015; Dziugaite et al., 2015) and the theoretical work on conditional kernel embeddings (Song et al., 2009), positioning itself as a conditional counterpart in the moment-matching family of generative models, analogous to how conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) extend the original GAN framework (Goodfellow et al., 2014).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound for the most part, providing a principled extension of MMD to the conditional setting. The formulation of the CMMD objective based on the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between conditional embedding operators is well-founded. The experimental results are solid and demonstrate that the proposed method is functional and achieves competitive performance, particularly when compared to other deep generative models.

However, there is a significant concern regarding theoretical rigor. Theorem 3 is a central theoretical justification for using the proposed CMMD objective, as it states that the objective is zero if and only if the conditional distributions are identical. Unlike Theorems 1 and 2, which are correctly attributed to prior work, Theorem 3 is presented without a proof or a citation. This is a critical omission that weakens the paper's theoretical foundation. The authors should either provide a proof in the appendix or cite the relevant literature where this result is established.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for conditional modeling is clear, and the progression from MMD to CMMD is logical. The description of the CGMMN architecture and training procedure is understandable.

There are, however, a few points that could be clarified to improve the manuscript:
1.  In Remark 1, the connection between CMMD and a weighted MMD is an interesting intuition, but the notation is ambiguous. The expression `Tr(Ld · C1)` is unclear. Does the dot represent a standard matrix product or an element-wise (Hadamard) product? This should be explicitly defined to make the insight clear to the reader.
2.  The symbol `C` is overloaded. It is used to denote the conditional embedding operator (`C_Y|X`), covariance operators (`C_XY`), and later as weighting matrices (`C1`, `C2`, `C3`) in Remark 1. While context helps, using distinct notation would improve readability and prevent confusion.

**Originality:**
The paper's main contribution—the formulation of CMMD and its application to train conditional deep generative models—is novel. While the idea of conditional generation is not new (e.g., conditional GANs, conditional VAEs), this is the first work to our knowledge that extends the moment-matching framework in this way. The application to distilling Bayesian models is also an interesting and original use case for this type of generative model, providing a new perspective on the "dark knowledge" concept. The work is well-situated within the literature and clearly distinguishes itself from prior contributions.

**Significance:**
The work is significant as it broadens the applicability of moment-matching networks, a class of models that offers a simpler and more stable training objective compared to the adversarial training of GANs. The proposed CGMMN framework is versatile and can be applied to a range of important problems that require conditional density estimation.

The significance of the empirical results is mixed.
- The generative modeling results on MNIST and Yale Faces are competent and successfully demonstrate the core idea. However, to establish CGMMN as a strong competitor in the rapidly advancing field of generative models, evaluation on more challenging and diverse datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, LSUN) is needed. The visual quality of the generated samples is decent but does not appear to surpass the state of the art.
- The use of CGMMN for supervised classification is a good demonstration of its flexibility, but its performance is comparable, not superior, to state-of-the-art discriminative models. This is expected and acceptable, but it suggests the primary strength of the model lies elsewhere.
- The Bayesian distillation experiment is compelling but limited to a single dataset and teacher model. Its generality is therefore hard to assess.

Overall, the approach is most promising for pure conditional generative modeling tasks, and its wider adoption will likely depend on scaling it to more complex data distributions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** Proposes a novel and theoretically grounded method (CGMMN) for conditional generative modeling based on a new CMMD objective.
2.  **Significance:** Addresses an important gap by extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting, increasing their applicability.
3.  **Versatility:** The model's effectiveness is demonstrated across three different domains: prediction, generation, and model distillation.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core concepts are presented clearly.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Theoretical Gap:** The key theoretical justification, Theorem 3, is stated without proof or citation, which is a significant flaw in a paper making a theoretical contribution.
2.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** The generative experiments are confined to relatively simple datasets (MNIST, Faces), which makes it difficult to assess the model's performance and scalability against state-of-the-art methods on more complex data.
3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** Ambiguous notation in Remark 1 and overloaded symbols slightly detract from the paper's overall quality.
