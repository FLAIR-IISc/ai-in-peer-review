This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) for learning conditional distributions, extending the GMMN framework to scenarios where conditioning variables are present. The core idea is to utilize a conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to match moments between the true conditional distribution and the model’s generated conditional distribution. The approach is evaluated on classification, contextual generation, and Bayesian model distillation tasks.

The work builds upon recent advances in deep generative modeling, particularly GMMNs [19, 3] and GANs [5]. While GANs have shown success, they suffer from training instability. GMMNs provide a more stable alternative based on moment matching using MMD. This paper logically extends this moment matching approach to the conditional setting, addressing a common need in many applications. The problem of conditional generation is well-motivated, and the application to Bayesian model distillation is interesting. Compared to directly modeling p(x,y), focusing on p(y|x) can be more efficient and accurate, a point well noted by the authors referencing prior work [23, 16].

**Strengths:**

* **Technically Sound:** The paper presents a well-defined approach based on solid theoretical foundations in kernel methods and MMD. The extension to the conditional setting through CMMD is logically sound. The formulation of CMMD, while potentially computationally demanding, is clean and clearly presented.
* **Clear Advantages:** The authors convincingly argue that treating all conditionals as a family and matching the conditional embedding operator directly avoids data sparsity issues encountered in a naive per-condition GMMN approach, especially with continuous conditioning variables.
* **Promising Results:** The experimental results on classification and Bayesian model distillation are encouraging, demonstrating competitive performance with existing methods. The MNIST and SVHN results are solid, and the distillation experiment suggests a practical application.
* **Backpropagation Friendly:** The formulation allows for efficient training via backpropagation, a significant practical advantage. The independence of the K matrices in Eq. 2 from model parameters further streamlines the gradient calculation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity Issues:** Sections 2.3 and 3.1 are difficult to follow. The explanation of the conditional embedding operator relies heavily on referencing [29] without providing sufficient context within the paper. A more detailed derivation or explanation would improve understanding. The connection between the cross-covariance operator and the presented conditional embedding definition isn't immediately clear.
* **Computational Cost:** While acknowledged, the potential computational burden of CMMD due to large minibatch requirements for stable estimation is a significant concern. The trade-off between minibatch size, matrix inversion cost, and stochastic gradient noise is not fully explored. The paper could benefit from discussing strategies to mitigate this issue (e.g., approximation techniques for the matrix inverse).
* **Minor Technical Errors:** The statement that the MMD estimator is unbiased is incorrect. It is asymptotically consistent, but not unbiased.
* **Inconsistency with Reference:** The definition of the conditional embedding operator differs from that in reference [29], implying a different cross-covariance operator definition is needed. This discrepancy should be clarified and justified.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** The experiments lack comprehensive ablation studies to assess the importance of different components of the CGMMN architecture (e.g., the dimensionality of the hidden variables, the choice of kernel).

**Originality:**

The paper introduces a novel application of MMD to the conditional generation setting. While the core concepts of MMD and GMMNs are not new, the extension to CMMD and the associated theoretical analysis and practical implementation represent a significant contribution. The application to Bayesian model distillation is also a valuable addition.

**Significance:**

CGMMN offers a promising approach to conditional generation with potential for broader applications in areas such as image generation, machine translation, and time series modeling. The ability to distill knowledge from complex Bayesian models using a simpler CGMMN student network could be particularly impactful. The work advances the state-of-the-art in moment matching-based generative models.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to conditional generation.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on a variety of tasks.
* The CMMD criterion offers a clean and theoretically grounded alternative to existing methods.
* The backpropagation-friendly formulation makes the approach practical for large-scale applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The clarity of certain sections (2.3, 3.1) needs improvement.
* The potential computational cost of CMMD is a significant concern.
* Minor technical errors and inconsistencies need to be addressed.
* The lack of comprehensive ablation studies limits the insights gained from the experiments.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of deep generative modeling. While there are some weaknesses regarding clarity and computational complexity, the strengths of the approach and the promising experimental results outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity issues, correct the technical error, and clarify the inconsistency with reference [29]. Further discussion on computational mitigation strategies would also be beneficial.
