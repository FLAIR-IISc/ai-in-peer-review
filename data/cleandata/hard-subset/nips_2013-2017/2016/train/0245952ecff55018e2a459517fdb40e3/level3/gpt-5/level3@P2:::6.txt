Summary
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative modeling framework trained by minimizing conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). Building on kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009/2013), the authors define CMMD as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between two conditional embedding operators, and instantiate a conditional generator y = f(x, h | w), where h is noise and x is the condition. Training uses backpropagation through the y-kernel; the x-kernel Gram matrices and inverses are independent of network parameters, simplifying differentiation. The method is evaluated on (i) predictive modeling by directly learning p(y|x) for MNIST and SVHN; (ii) contextual generation on MNIST and Yale Faces; and (iii) “Bayesian dark knowledge” by distilling a Bayesian predictor into a CGMMN on Boston Housing. Results are competitive with strong generative baselines and reasonable compared with discriminative models.

Relative to prior work, CGMMN extends GMMN (Li et al., ICML’15; Dziugaite et al., UAI’15) from unconditional to conditional generation, paralleling the transition from GAN to cGAN (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and from VAE to conditional VAE (Sohn et al., NIPS’15). It leverages the conditional mean embedding literature (e.g., Song et al., ICML’09; Grunewalder et al., ICML’12).

Quality
- Soundness: The CMMD objective—‖ĈY|X − ĈZ|X‖2HS—follows standard conditional mean embedding theory. The observation that K̃−1 depends only on x and λ (not on w) is correct and helpful for backprop.
- Theory: Theorem 3 (equality of operators implies equality of conditional distributions) is a standard sufficient condition under universal kernels and regularity assumptions; a brief discussion of necessity/characteristic kernels would strengthen it. Practical reliance on the regularized estimator Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ is appropriate.
- Algorithms/complexity: Mini-batch training introduces a cubic cost in batch size for K̃−1 per batch. This raises practical concerns about batch sizes needed for low-variance CMMD estimates versus compute/memory constraints (see Weaknesses).
- Empirics: The classification and distillation experiments are interesting; generative results are sensible and improved by combining with an autoencoder. However, comparisons against conditional baselines (cGAN, CVAE) are mostly qualitative, and stronger quantitative evaluation would help.

Clarity
- The exposition of conditional embeddings (Sec. 2.3) and CMMD (Sec. 3.1) is terse. While definitions are standard, the precise operator domains/codomains, adjoints, and the relationship to cross-covariance operators could be clarified to avoid confusion without consulting [29]. The derivation of Eq. (2) is otherwise clear.
- Minor error: the statement “Asymptotically, L̂2MMD is an unbiased estimator.” is misleading. The commonly used V-statistic estimator of MMD2 is biased but consistent; a U-statistic form is unbiased in finite samples (Gretton et al., JMLR’12). Please correct this.
- Minor inconsistency: the paper defines CXY: G→F and uses ĈY|X = Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤. This matches Song et al. if notation is consistent, but the text could better align the operator mapping directions and transpose conventions with [29] to preclude ambiguity.

Originality
- Using CMMD to train conditional deep generators appears novel and is a natural but nontrivial extension of GMMN, with a clean, kernel-based formulation distinct from adversarial or variational objectives.
- The “family-level” matching of all conditionals via a single operator avoids the data fragmentation that would arise from training separate GMMNs per condition.

Significance
- The contribution fills a gap among non-adversarial kernel-based generative methods by enabling conditional generation and predictive modeling without likelihoods or adversarial games.
- The approach is general and could be adopted by practitioners who prefer stability/simplicity over adversarial training. The distillation application is promising.
- Empirical gains are competitive but not yet state of the art; broader benchmarks and stronger baselines would increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear, principled CMMD objective grounded in kernel conditional embeddings.
- Simple training via backprop; x-kernel inverses are parameter-independent.
- Versatile applications: classification, conditional generation, Bayesian model distillation.
- Qualitative results demonstrate conditional control; nearest-neighbor checks for non-triviality; combination with autoencoders improves sample quality.

Weaknesses
- Computational scalability: CMMD requires inverting K̃ per mini-batch (O(B3)), pushing toward smaller batches, but small batches increase CMMD estimator noise. No ablations on batch size, λ, or kernel bandwidths.
- Baselines: Missing a “naive” conditional baseline (e.g., training per-class GMMNs sharing architecture) for low-cardinality discrete x (MNIST labels) to highlight the advantage of operator-level sharing. For continuous x, comparisons to local/nearest-neighbor conditional GMMN or kernel regression-style baselines would be informative.
- Limited quantitative evaluation of generative quality and uncertainty (e.g., no NLL/Brier for classification; no predictive NLL on Boston; no quantitative conditional generation metrics).
- Clarity issues in Sections 2.3/3.1; minor technical inaccuracies as noted.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a naive baseline: per-class GMMN (shared architecture) on MNIST conditional generation and classification, and discuss data efficiency versus CGMMN. For continuous x, include a locally-weighted unconditional GMMN baseline to demonstrate failure modes.
- Report sensitivity to batch size, λ, and kernel bandwidths on x and y (median heuristic vs tuning), and provide practical guidance. Consider scalable approximations: Nyström/inducing points for K, random Fourier features on x, or precomputing K̃−1 for fixed training x (if you always condition on training x) with warm starts/Cholesky updates.
- Provide quantitative metrics: classification NLL/Brier and calibration; for generation, conditional inception/classifier accuracy or class-conditional MMD to a held-out set. For distillation, add predictive NLL/aleatoric-epistemic analysis, not just RMSE.
- Clarify Section 2.3: explicitly state domains/codomains of operators, adjoints, and align notation with [29]; move some algebra to the appendix but retain an intuitive exposition in the main text.
- Correct the MMD estimator statement (V- vs U-statistic).
- Discuss multi-modality: include a toy multi-modal conditional regression to demonstrate stochastic outputs (beyond label-conditioned images).

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, clean extension of GMMN to conditional settings with broad applicability.
- Simple, stable training objective; avoids adversarial pitfalls.
- Solid results across diverse tasks; interesting distillation use case.

Cons against acceptance
- Computational and variance trade-offs of CMMD with mini-batches are underexplored.
- Missing key baselines and quantitative evaluations limit the strength of the empirical case.
- Clarity issues and minor technical inaccuracies.

Overall evaluation
A solid, principled contribution that extends kernel-based deep generative modeling to conditional settings. With improved clarity, stronger baselines (including the naive per-condition GMMN), and more thorough quantitative evaluation and scalability analysis, this paper would be a strong addition. As it stands, it is promising and likely of interest to the NIPS community, but could benefit from the suggested additions.

Questions for the authors
- What kernels and bandwidths were used for x and y in each experiment, and how were they selected? How sensitive is performance to these choices and to λ?
- How large were your effective batch sizes for stable training, and what were the wall-clock and memory costs of computing K̃−1 per batch?
- Can you precompute K̃−1 for fixed training x’s to amortize cost (when conditioning only on training x), or use Nyström/inducing approximations?
- Does CGMMN capture multi-modality in p(y|x) beyond label-conditioned generation? Could you include a demonstrative regression with multi-modal outputs?
