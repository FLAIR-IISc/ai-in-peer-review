

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to the conditional setting. The authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to learn conditional distributions, enabling applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation. The work builds on Li et al.'s GMMN (ICML 2015) and the kernel embedding literature (Gretton et al., JMLR 2012; Song et al., ICML 2009), which established MMD as a powerful alternative to GANs for generative modeling.

The core contribution is the extension of MMD to conditional distributions, which is non-trivial compared to the unconditional case. The authors derive CMMD as a Hilbert-Schmidt norm between conditional kernel mean embeddings, theoretically justifying its use for conditional distribution matching. The paper demonstrates competitive performance on MNIST and SVHN classification tasks, conditional image generation (including MNIST and Yale Face datasets), and Bayesian knowledge distillation on the Boston housing dataset.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical foundation and empirical validation across multiple domains. The conditional extension addresses a meaningful gap in the literature, as many applications (predictive modeling, contextual generation) inherently require conditioning. The results on classification tasks are particularly compelling, showing CGMMN-CNN achieves error rates competitive with state-of-the-art discriminative models like CMMVA (3.09% vs. 3.13% on SVHN), without requiring data augmentation or ensemble methods.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that undermine its scientific contribution. The authors begin with heavy formalism (Section 2) but fail to provide an intuitive explanation of the conditional objective. For instance, Equation (2) - the key optimization objective - is presented without derivation, assumptions, or practical interpretation. The path from Equation (2) to the implemented algorithm is insufficiently detailed, making it difficult for readers to understand what computations are actually performed. The supplemental material does not adequately address these gaps.

The conditional case requires a different intuition than the unconditional GMMN, yet the paper fails to provide this. In the unconditional setting, the MMD objective is intuitive: match the kernel mean embeddings of generated and real data. For the conditional case, the authors must explain how they handle the fact that distributions differ across conditionals (i.e., matching distributions over large mini-batches of outputs to many different conditional inputs). The paper lacks this crucial intuitive explanation, making the core contribution hard to grasp.

The paper also fails to clearly articulate computational efficiency. While the authors mention a minibatch algorithm (Algorithm 1), they don't discuss how the computational complexity scales with the number of conditional values or the dimensionality of the conditionals. The use of matrix inverses (K̃⁻¹) in Equation (2) suggests cubic complexity in sample size, but the paper doesn't address how this is managed in practice.

Regarding significance, CGMMN has potential applications in domains requiring conditional generation, but the unclear presentation limits its immediate impact. The Bayesian knowledge distillation results are interesting but not particularly novel, as similar approaches have been explored in the context of distilling knowledge from Bayesian neural networks (Korattikara et al., NIPS 2015).

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting, with competitive empirical results. However, the lack of clarity in explaining the conditional objective (Equation 2) and the connection to implementation significantly weakens its contribution. The authors should provide a more intuitive explanation of the conditional matching problem, derive Equation (2) with clear assumptions, and detail the computational steps from the theory to the algorithm.

Pro: Theoretical extension of GMMN to conditional setting with competitive empirical results across multiple domains.
Con: Poor clarity in explaining the core conditional objective (Equation 2) and its implementation, making the contribution difficult to evaluate and reproduce.
Con: Insufficient discussion of computational complexity and practical implementation details.
Con: The conditional extension lacks intuitive explanation compared to the straightforward unconditional case.

The paper is technically sound but would benefit from significant revision to improve clarity, particularly regarding the conditional objective and its implementation. As currently written, the theoretical contribution is obscured by inadequate explanation, which limits its value to the community.
