Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) by learning conditional distributions p(y|x) using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective derived from kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. The generator concatenates the conditioning variables x with noise h and maps them through a deep network to produce y, while training minimizes the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between estimated conditional mean operators for data and model. The approach avoids adversarial min–max optimization and variational bounds, and is trained end-to-end with backpropagation.

The work builds on: (i) MMD two-sample testing and kernel mean embeddings; (ii) kernel embeddings of conditional distributions (Song et al.; Grunewalder et al.); and (iii) GMMN (Li et al.; Dziugaite et al.). It is positioned relative to conditional generative adversarial nets (cGAN) and conditional variational auto-encoders (CVAE), offering a simpler, moment-matching alternative. Applications are shown in classification (treating the network as a conditional generator over labels), conditional image generation (digits and faces), and distilling Bayesian predictors into a fast student.

Strengths
- Clear and principled extension of MMD/GMMN to the conditional setting via operator-valued embeddings; the CMMD objective is simple and differentiable.
- Avoids adversarial training instability; optimization reduces to backprop through a trace-based loss.
- Versatile demonstrations: classification on MNIST/SVHN, conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation from PBP on Boston Housing.
- Practical training recipe with minibatches; simple sampling strategy; shows combining CGMMN with autoencoders to improve sample quality.

Weaknesses
- Theory/notation clarity: Theorem 3 is stated without proof or citation; the mapping from Eq. (2) to a “weighted MMD” form is not fully justified; overloaded symbols and ambiguous dot notation cause confusion.
- Empirics are limited in scope: conditional generation is only on digits/faces; no comparisons to strong contemporaries (cGAN/CVAE) on more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, LSUN). Metrics beyond visuals (e.g., Inception score, FID or conditional likelihood proxies) are absent.
- Supervised learning via CGMMN underperforms strong discriminative baselines; its value there is mainly to illustrate generality rather than state-of-the-art accuracy.
- Bayesian distillation is promising but only tested on one small regression dataset and one teacher model; calibration and NLL are not reported.

Quality
- The CMMD objective is well motivated from conditional mean embeddings; the empirical operator estimator is standard. However:
  - Please provide a proof of Theorem 3 or cite prior results establishing that equality of conditional mean operators in a universal RKHS implies equality of conditional distributions almost surely. Clarify required assumptions (measurability, universality on Y, and conditions ensuring CY|X ∈ F ⊗ G).
  - Eq. (2): spell out the derivation from operator norms to traces over Gram matrices; state clearly what “·” denotes (matrix multiplication vs. Hadamard), and ensure shapes and symmetry conditions are explicit. The “weighted MMD” remark should be formalized (i.e., show L̂2_CMMD = Tr(LdW1) + Tr(LsW2) − 2 Tr(LdsW3) with W’s defined).
  - Discuss the bias/variance of the minibatch CMMD estimator and when the stochastic gradient is unbiased.
  - Complexity: computing K̃−1 per minibatch is O(b^3). Provide batch sizes used, wall-clock times, and memory; discuss scalability strategies (Nyström, random Fourier features, blockwise solves).
  - Kernels: specify kernels for x and y, bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic), and sensitivity.
- Empirical methodology is mostly sound; architectural and optimization details are given, and nearest-neighbor checks reduce copying concerns. For distillation, please also report predictive NLL and calibration.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but improve notation:
  - Avoid overloading C (used for operators and other matrices). Consider CY|X for operators, Kx, Ky for Gram matrices, and reserve C1–C3 for weighting matrices only.
  - Define all matrices in Eq. (2) with dimensions, and replace ambiguous “·” with explicit multiplication or “∘” for Hadamard.
  - Fix minor typos (e.g., “MINIST”, “adverisarial”, “Mathmetical”).
  - Summarize the gradient w.r.t. y and network parameters, noting which terms depend on w (L’s) and which are treated constant (K̃−1).

Originality
- Using conditional mean embeddings to derive a tractable training loss for deep conditional generators appears novel relative to GMMN, CVAE, and cGAN at the time. The operator-based objective and its practical minibatch implementation are original contributions.

Significance
- Moderate. CGMMN offers a clean alternative to adversarial/variational training for conditional generation, which could appeal to practitioners seeking stability and simplicity. Broader impact depends on demonstrating competitive results on more complex conditional generation tasks and stronger baselines; the framework is likely most impactful for conditional generative modeling rather than supervised prediction.

Pro/Con arguments for acceptance
Pros:
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMN to conditional settings.
- Simple, stable objective; easy to implement and differentiate.
- Versatile applications; promising direction for non-adversarial conditional generation.

Cons:
- Limited empirical breadth; lacks strong comparisons on challenging datasets and metrics.
- Theoretical statement (Theorem 3) lacks proof/citation; some derivations/notation unclear.
- Classification/dark knowledge evaluations are modest and do not establish clear advantages.

Questions for the authors
- Provide a proof or reference for Theorem 3 and clarify assumptions (universality on Y, conditions under which CY|X exists).
- Which kernels and bandwidths were used for x and y in each task? How sensitive are results?
- Is the minibatch CMMD gradient unbiased? If not, is there evidence it still converges well?
- Can you report runtime/memory vs. batch size, and compare to cGAN/CVAE training cost?
- For distillation, can you add NLL and calibration metrics and a second dataset/teacher (e.g., SGHMC BNN) to assess generality?

Suggestions
- Add experiments on CIFAR-10 or LSUN with conditional labels or attributes, and compare to cGAN/CVAE; report quantitative metrics.
- Explore kernel approximations (Nyström/RFF) to enable larger batches.
- Provide an ablation on noise dimensionality and on the choice of conditioning strategy (concatenate vs. additive noise).
- Release code to aid reproducibility.

Overall evaluation
Weak accept. The paper presents a principled and useful conditional extension of GMMN with a clear objective and competitive preliminary results. Addressing the noted theoretical clarifications and expanding empirical comparisons would strengthen the contribution.
