This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions P(Y|X). The core contribution is a novel training objective, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the distance between the conditional kernel mean embeddings of the true data distribution and the generative model's distribution. This allows for training a deep generative model that can produce samples of Y conditioned on an input X by backpropagating through the CMMD loss. The approach is a natural and important extension of prior work on moment-matching networks, analogous to how Conditional GANs extended the original GAN framework. The authors demonstrate the model's versatility on several tasks, including supervised classification, conditional image generation (digits and faces), and a novel application in distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a well-motivated and elegant extension of the MMD-based training paradigm for deep generative models. By leveraging kernel embeddings of conditional distributions, it provides a principled way to train conditional generators without the instability often associated with the adversarial min-max training of GANs. This fills an important gap in the literature on moment-matching networks and broadens their applicability to a wider range of problems.

2.  **Versatility of Applications:** A key strength is the demonstration of the framework's flexibility across diverse tasks. While the application to conditional image generation is expected, its use for standard supervised classification (predicting labels from images) shows its utility as a general-purpose conditional model. The application to "Bayesian dark knowledge" is particularly interesting and novel, showcasing a creative use case for distilling a complex Bayesian predictive distribution into a single, efficient feed-forward network.

3.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are sound and effectively demonstrate that the proposed method works. On generative tasks (MNIST, Yale Faces), the quality is reasonable and comparable to what one might expect from the original GMMN, showing that the conditioning mechanism is effective. In the predictive setting, the model achieves competitive results against other deep generative models on MNIST and SVHN, even if it does not surpass state-of-the-art discriminative classifiers. The experiments on standard datasets are sufficient to validate the core ideas.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The manuscript could be improved in clarity in a few key areas.
    *   **Remark 1 and Equation (2):** The connection made in Remark 1 between the CMMD objective in Equation (2) and a "weighted MMD" is confusing. The notation `Tr(Ld · C1)` is non-standard, and it is unclear whether the dot `·` represents matrix multiplication or an element-wise (Hadamard) product. This makes the intuition difficult to follow and should be clarified or rephrased using standard linear algebra notation.
    *   **Overloaded Notation:** The symbol `C` is used to represent the conditional embedding operator (`C_Y|X`), the cross-covariance operator (`C_XY`), and the weight matrices in Remark 1 (`C1`, `C2`, `C3`). This overloading of notation is confusing and should be avoided to improve readability.

2.  **Missing Theoretical Justification:** A notable weakness is the lack of a proof or citation for Theorem 3. This theorem provides the theoretical justification for using the CMMD metric, stating that equality of the conditional embedding operators implies equality of the conditional distributions. While Theorems 1 and 2 are correctly attributed to prior work, this new result is central to the paper's contribution and requires formal justification. Providing a proof (e.g., in an appendix) would significantly strengthen the paper's technical soundness.

3.  **Scope of Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments are sufficient as a proof-of-concept, their scope is somewhat limited.
    *   **Datasets:** The generative performance is only shown on relatively simple datasets (MNIST, Yale Faces). For the model to gain wider traction and demonstrate its scalability, evaluation on more challenging and higher-resolution datasets like CIFAR-10 or LSUN would be necessary.
    *   **Bayesian Distillation:** The Bayesian distillation experiment is compelling but is only demonstrated on a single low-dimensional regression dataset (Boston housing) and one "teacher" model (PBP). The generality and effectiveness of this approach for more complex models and tasks remain unclear. More extensive experiments would be needed to establish this as a broadly useful technique.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
*   Novel and principled extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting.
*   Avoids the unstable min-max optimization of adversarial models.
*   Demonstrates strong versatility across generative, predictive, and knowledge distillation tasks.
*   Empirical results are solid and validate the effectiveness of the approach on standard benchmarks.

**Cons:**
*   Clarity issues with notation in Remark 1 and the overloaded symbol `C`.
*   A key theoretical result (Theorem 3) is presented without proof or citation.
*   Experiments are limited to simpler datasets, leaving scalability to more challenging problems an open question.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and logical extension to the family of moment-matching generative models. The core idea is sound, and the diverse applications are compelling. The primary weaknesses relate to clarity and the need for stronger theoretical and empirical support in specific areas. These weaknesses are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution of the paper.
