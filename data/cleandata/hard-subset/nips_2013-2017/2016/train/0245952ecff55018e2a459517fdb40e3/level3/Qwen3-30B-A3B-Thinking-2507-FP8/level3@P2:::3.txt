

**Review for "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"**

**Summary**  
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to model conditional distributions. The authors propose Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a training criterion, building upon kernel mean embedding of conditional distributions (Gretton et al., JMLR 2012; Song et al., ICML 2009). CGMMN learns conditional distributions by drawing samples from a simple distribution and passing them through a deep network with conditional variables, using CMMD to match the kernel mean embedding of the empirical conditional distribution. The paper demonstrates CGMMN on predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (digits/faces), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, showing competitive performance across these tasks.

**Quality**  
The paper is technically sound, with a solid theoretical foundation supported by Theorems 1-3. The derivation of CMMD (Equation 2) is generally correct, though the dot notation's meaning (matrix multiplication vs. Hadamard product) is unclear and needs clarification for reproducibility. The symbol "C" is overloaded (used for conditional embedding operators and elsewhere), which creates confusion. Theorem 3 lacks a reference or proof, though Theorems 1-2 cite prior work. The empirical evaluation is thorough but limited: Bayesian distillation is tested on only one dataset (Boston housing), and the generative tasks use relatively simple datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face). While the results are convincing for the presented tasks, the lack of evaluation on more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) limits claims about broader applicability.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from preliminary concepts to the proposed method and experiments. The connection between the conditional generative process and CMMD is well-explained. However, the mapping from Equation (2) to the weighted MMD form is confusing, and the notation could be improved for greater clarity. The authors have addressed previous reviewer concerns about low-dimensional latent spaces and scalability in the revised text, which is commendable.

**Originality**  
CGMMN represents a meaningful extension of GMMN to conditional settings, filling a gap in the literature. The work clearly differentiates itself from prior conditional generative models like conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., NIPS 2015). The use of CMMD for conditional generation, rather than adversarial training, offers a simpler alternative to GANs while retaining the benefits of deep learning. The paper is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS work, particularly in its theoretical grounding using kernel embedding of conditional distributions.

**Significance**  
The results demonstrate that CGMMN can achieve competitive performance across multiple tasks, offering a viable alternative to GANs for conditional generation. The Bayesian distillation application is particularly interesting, though its limited evaluation on a single dataset weakens the claim of broader utility. The approach has potential for wider adoption in conditional generative modeling due to its simplicity and compatibility with standard deep learning architectures. However, the significance is somewhat constrained by the relatively simple datasets used and the lack of comparison with more recent conditional generative models.

**Arguments For Acceptance**  
- Clear and meaningful extension of GMMN to conditional distributions, addressing a natural gap in the literature.  
- Solid theoretical foundation with well-supported theorems (despite minor notation issues).  
- Demonstrates versatility across multiple tasks (classification, generation, distillation).  
- Simpler training procedure compared to GANs, which could appeal to practitioners.

**Arguments Against Acceptance**  
- Technical details in Equation (2) require clarification to ensure reproducibility.  
- Limited evaluation on challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) weakens claims about broader applicability.  
- Bayesian distillation application is too narrow to make strong generalization claims.  
- The supervised learning application, while demonstrating versatility, is not the strongest use case.

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to conditional generative modeling. The extension of GMMN to conditional settings is novel and well-justified, with solid empirical results across multiple tasks. While the evaluation could be strengthened with more challenging datasets and broader comparisons, the core ideas are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the technical details in Equation (2) and address the overloaded notation. The paper advances the field of generative modeling by providing a simpler, theoretically grounded alternative to conditional GANs for conditional generation tasks.
