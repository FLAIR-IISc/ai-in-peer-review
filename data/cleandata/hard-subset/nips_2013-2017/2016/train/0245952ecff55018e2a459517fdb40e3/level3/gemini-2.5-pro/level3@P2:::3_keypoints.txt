- Proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) that model p(y|x) using a new Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) training objective.  
- CMMD is derived from kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, representing conditionals as operators between RKHSs.  
- CGMMN uses a deep network mapping (x, noise h) → y and trains by minimizing CMMD between model and empirical conditionals via SGD.  
- Builds directly on GMMN, extending moment-matching from joint to conditional distributions and offering a non-adversarial alternative to conditional GANs/VAEs.  
- Demonstrates versatility on three tasks: predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual image generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and Bayesian model distillation.  
- Strength — Novelty/significance: principled extension of moment-matching to conditional modeling with well-motivated CMMD objective.  
- Strength — Versatility: effective application across generation, prediction, and an interesting Bayesian “dark knowledge” distillation use case.  
- Strength — Generative quality: produces clear, diverse, class-conditional samples with nearest-neighbor checks against memorization.  
- Weakness — Missing theoretical justification: Theorem 3 (equality of conditional embedding operators ⇒ equality of conditionals) lacks proof or citation and must be provided.  
- Weakness — Mathematical clarity: ambiguous notation in Remark 1 (dot product vs. Hadamard) and overloaded use of symbol C create confusion.  
- Weakness — Empirical scope limited: experiments restricted to simple datasets (MNIST, Yale Faces), predictive results not SOTA, and Bayesian distillation only on small Boston dataset.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically-grounded conditional extension; stable non-adversarial training objective; demonstrated multi-task versatility.  
- Arguments against acceptance: absence of proof/citation for critical theorem undermines rigor; limited empirical evaluation; minor notational clarity issues.  
- Recommendation: overall positive — recommend acceptance provided the authors add the missing proof/citation for Theorem 3 and clarify notation, and ideally expand empirical validation.
