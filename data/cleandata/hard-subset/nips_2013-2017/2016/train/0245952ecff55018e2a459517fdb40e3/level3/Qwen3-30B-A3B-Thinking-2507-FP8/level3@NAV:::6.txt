

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the GMMN framework to learn conditional distributions using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The authors propose matching the conditional embedding operator directly rather than training separate GMMNs for each condition, addressing data sparsity issues in continuous conditioning scenarios. The approach is evaluated on classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation tasks.

The paper demonstrates several strengths: (1) The theoretical foundation of CMMD is well-motivated and builds logically on kernel embedding techniques; (2) The conditional MMD formulation has a clean mathematical structure that can be efficiently computed using kernel matrices; (3) Experimental results on MNIST, SVHN, and Bayesian distillation tasks show competitive performance compared to strong baselines. The authors convincingly demonstrate the advantage of their approach over naive per-condition training, particularly for continuous conditioning.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The presentation of key concepts in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is difficult to follow without consulting external references, which undermines clarity. A minor but important error states that the MMD estimator is unbiased, when it is actually asymptotically unbiased. There's also an inconsistency between the paper's definition of the conditional embedding operator and reference [29], which requires clarification. The concern about requiring large minibatches for stable CMMD computation is valid but not adequately addressed, as large minibatches significantly increase computational costs due to matrix inversion requirements.

The work shows solid technical quality with well-supported experimental results, though the theoretical presentation could be strengthened. The originality is moderate, as it extends existing MMD-based generative models rather than introducing fundamentally new concepts. The significance is demonstrated through compelling applications across multiple domains, though the method's computational demands may limit practical adoption.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with promising applications, but the presentation issues and computational concerns warrant careful consideration. The authors should address the clarity issues, correct the theoretical errors, and provide more detailed discussion of the computational trade-offs to strengthen the contribution.
