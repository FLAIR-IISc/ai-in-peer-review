

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to the conditional setting. GMMN, introduced by Li et al. (ICML 2015), uses maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) as a training criterion for generative models, avoiding the min-max optimization challenges of GANs. CGMMN addresses a significant limitation of GMMN by learning conditional distributions p(y|x) rather than joint distributions, which is crucial for predictive modeling, contextual generation, and building complex generative models. The paper builds on kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML 2009) and extends the MMD criterion to the conditional case, proposing a new metric called conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD).

## Strengths
The paper identifies an important practical limitation of GMMN and makes a theoretically grounded extension to the conditional setting. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The results demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines, particularly in the conditional generation task where the model successfully generates class-specific digits. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is particularly novel and shows practical utility.

## Weaknesses
The paper's primary weakness lies in its lack of clarity regarding the core technical contribution. The authors begin with heavy formal math without sufficient intuitive explanation, making the conditional objective difficult to grasp. Specifically:

1. The core difficulty of matching distributions over large mini-batches of outputs to many different conditional inputs (where distributions differ across conditionals) is not clearly articulated.

2. Equation (2), the key optimization objective, lacks derivation, assumptions, and practical implications. The paper does not explain why this particular formulation is appropriate or how it differs from simpler approaches.

3. The connection between the theoretical formulation and the implemented algorithm is insufficiently detailed. Algorithm 1 states that the model is trained via stochastic gradient descent, but the paper fails to provide a clear step-by-step mapping from Equation (2) to the actual computations performed in the algorithm.

4. The paper does not adequately address computational efficiency, which is critical for the claimed practical applicability. The cubic complexity of kernel gram matrix computation is mentioned but not properly contextualized for the claimed mini-batch approach.

5. The non-conditional GMMN has a simple, intuitive explanation (matching empirical distributions via kernel mean embeddings), but the conditional case lacks this accessibility. The paper does not provide an equally intuitive explanation for why CMMD works for conditional distributions.

## Quality and Clarity
The theoretical foundation of the paper is sound, but the presentation is poor. The paper suffers from the classic "math-first" approach without sufficient conceptual explanation. For readers to understand why CMMD is the right objective for conditional generation, the paper should have provided a simple intuitive explanation (e.g., "We need to match the distribution of outputs for each condition, rather than matching the overall joint distribution"). The supplemental material is hard to follow, suggesting the authors themselves may not have a clear intuitive grasp of their own method.

## Originality and Significance
The extension to conditional generation is conceptually valuable, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related work. Conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., 2015) address similar problems, and the paper should have more clearly articulated how CGMMN differs in approach and advantages. The significance is limited by the paper's lack of clarity regarding why this particular approach is better than alternatives for conditional generation.

## Recommendation
The paper presents an interesting extension to GMMN but fails to adequately communicate its core contribution due to poor exposition. The reviewers' two specific requests for clarification—intuitive derivation of Equation (2) and clear step-by-step mapping from Equation (2) to the practical algorithm—are not addressed in the current manuscript. Without these clarifications, it's difficult to assess the paper's technical merits or understand how to implement the method.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite focusing on intuitive explanations before presenting the mathematical formalism. A simple diagram showing how the conditional distribution is matched across different conditions would help readers grasp the core idea. The authors should explicitly contrast their approach with conditional GANs and conditional VAEs to better establish their contribution.

Given the current state of the manuscript, I recommend rejection. The technical contribution is valuable but the paper fails to communicate it effectively, which is a critical requirement for publication at NIPS. The authors should address the clarity issues before resubmitting.
