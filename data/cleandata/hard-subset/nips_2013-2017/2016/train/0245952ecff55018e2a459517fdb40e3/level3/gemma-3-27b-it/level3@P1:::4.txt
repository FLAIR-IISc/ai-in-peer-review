This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the capabilities of existing Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to model conditional distributions. The authors address the common need to learn p(y|x) rather than the joint distribution p(x,y) in many applications, including predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The core contribution lies in the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion, which allows for efficient training via back-propagation, mirroring the simplicity of the original GMMN approach. The paper demonstrates competitive performance across a range of tasks, highlighting the practical utility of the proposed framework.

The work builds upon recent advances in deep generative modeling, specifically referencing Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and GMMNs. GANs, while successful, often suffer from training instability due to their min-max formulation. GMMNs offered a simpler alternative based on moment matching via Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), and this work extends that approach to the conditional setting. The paper appropriately positions itself within this landscape, acknowledging prior work while presenting a novel contribution that addresses a significant need for conditional generative models. Related work on conditional GANs and variational autoencoders are also discussed, establishing the relevance and potential impact of this research.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. Technically, the derivation of the CMMD criterion and the associated empirical estimator appear sound, though a rigorous verification of all equations was beyond the scope of this review. The paper provides sufficient detail to allow for replication, and the inclusion of an algorithm detailing the stochastic gradient descent procedure is particularly helpful. The experimental validation is also a significant strength. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN on diverse tasks – predictive modeling with MNIST and SVHN datasets, contextual image generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation – showcasing the versatility of the framework. Competitive results compared to state-of-the-art methods indicate that this approach is a viable alternative to existing techniques. The clarity of the writing is commendable. The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with the specific details of MMD or GMMNs. The inclusion of a comprehensive appendix further enhances readability and allows for a deeper understanding of the methodological choices.

However, the paper is not without minor weaknesses. While the experimental results are promising, the ablation studies are limited. Exploring the sensitivity of the model to different kernel choices or network architectures could provide further insights. Additionally, the discussion of the theoretical assumptions underlying the CMMD criterion (specifically regarding smoothness and the Hilbert-Schmidt norm) could be expanded to provide a more complete understanding of the limitations of the approach. A discussion of computational costs and scalability might also be beneficial, particularly given the cubic complexity of kernel matrix calculations, which is partially mitigated by the mini-batch approach outlined in Algorithm 1. Finally, while the paper mentions the possibility of combining CGMMN with autoencoders for improved generative performance, the results presented are limited; a more thorough investigation of this synergy could be worthwhile.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and valuable extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting.
*   Technically sound and well-presented, with clear derivations and a practical algorithm.
*   Competitive performance on a diverse set of tasks, demonstrating versatility.
*   Clear and concise writing, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.
*   Potential impact on practitioners working with deep generative models and knowledge distillation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited ablation studies could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis.
*   Theoretical discussion could be expanded to address limitations and assumptions.
*   Scalability concerns related to kernel computations are only briefly addressed.
*   The synergistic potential of combining CGMMN with autoencoders requires further investigation.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling. While minor improvements could be made, the novelty, technical soundness, and experimental validation demonstrate the significance of this work. The paper meets the criteria for acceptance to NIPS and is likely to be of interest to a broad audience of researchers and practitioners. I recommend acceptance.
