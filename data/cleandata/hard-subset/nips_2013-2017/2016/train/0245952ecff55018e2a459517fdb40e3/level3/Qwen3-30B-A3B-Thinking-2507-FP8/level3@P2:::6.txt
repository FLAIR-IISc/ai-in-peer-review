

**Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"**

**Summary**  
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending Li et al.'s (ICML 2015) Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to conditional distributions. The authors propose a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to match kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, enabling flexible conditional modeling through standard backpropagation. CGMMN is evaluated on three tasks: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation, and distilling Bayesian models (Bayesian dark knowledge). The work builds on NIPS 2015's GMMN and extends the kernel embedding framework for conditional distributions from Song et al. (ICML 2009) and Grunewalder et al. (ICML 2012).

**Strengths**  
*Originality*: The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions addresses a clear gap in the literature. The authors convincingly argue that conditional modeling is essential for predictive tasks, contextual generation, and building large-scale generative models, distinguishing their work from unconditional GMMN and conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014). The CMMD formulation provides a theoretically sound and elegant approach to conditional moment matching.

*Significance*: The results across multiple tasks demonstrate CGMMN's practical utility. In classification (MNIST/SVHN), CGMMN-CNN achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods (CMMVA, DSN) without data augmentation. For contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), it produces diverse samples conditioned on labels. The Bayesian dark knowledge application shows CGMMN can distill predictive distributions from Bayesian models without performance degradation (Boston housing).

*Quality*: The technical development of CMMD is rigorous, with clear connections to kernel embedding theory. The training procedure via backpropagation is well-motivated, and the empirical results are thorough across diverse datasets. The authors correctly note that the K matrices in Eq. 2 are independent of model parameters, enabling efficient gradient computation.

**Weaknesses**  
*Clarity*: The presentation of conditional embedding operators (Sections 2.3, 3.1) is difficult to follow without consulting referenced papers (Song et al., Grunewalder et al.). The connection between the theoretical framework and the proposed CMMD is not sufficiently explained in the main text, forcing readers to consult external references for comprehension.

*Technical Inaccuracies*: The claim that "MMD is an unbiased estimator" is incorrect; it's asymptotically unbiased but not unbiased for finite samples. This minor but important error needs correction.

*Baseline Comparison*: The paper omits a critical baseline: training a GMMN per conditional value with shared network parameters (e.g., one GMMN per MNIST class). For discrete conditioning (like MNIST labels), this naive approach may perform reasonably well, making CGMMN's advantages less clear. A comparison would strengthen the claims about CGMMN's superiority.

*Computational Concerns*: The cubic scaling of matrix inversion (K̃⁻¹) for large minibatches is acknowledged but not adequately addressed. The authors note that large minibatches reduce stochastic noise but increase computational cost, yet this trade-off lacks discussion of practical implementation strategies.

**Recommendation**  
This is a strong submission that makes a valuable contribution to generative modeling. The theoretical extension is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates practical utility across multiple applications. With minor revisions to address clarity and technical concerns, it would be an excellent NIPS paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance**  
- Novel extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with clear theoretical foundation  
- Competitive performance across diverse tasks (classification, generation, Bayesian distillation)  
- Practical utility demonstrated in real-world applications  
- Builds coherently on prior NIPS work (GMMN, kernel embeddings)

**Arguments against Acceptance**  
- Technical presentation requires consulting external references for full comprehension  
- Minor technical error about MMD unbiasedness  
- Missing comparison to a natural baseline for discrete conditioning  
- Insufficient discussion of computational trade-offs

The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The conditional modeling framework is a meaningful advance that will be useful to the broader machine learning community. I recommend acceptance with the following revisions: 1) clarify the connection between conditional embedding theory and CMMD in the main text, 2) correct the MMD unbiasedness claim, 3) add a comparison to the per-condition GMMN baseline, and 4) expand discussion of computational considerations.
