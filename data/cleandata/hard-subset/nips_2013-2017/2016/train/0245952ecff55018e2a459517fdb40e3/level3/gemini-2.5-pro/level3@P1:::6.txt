This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions P(Y|X). The core contribution is the development of a novel training objective, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the distance between the kernel mean embeddings of conditional distribution operators. This allows the model to learn a generator that maps a conditioning variable X and a random noise vector Z to a sample Y, such that the learned conditional distribution matches the true data distribution. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their framework on three distinct tasks: predictive modeling (image classification), contextual generation (generating images conditioned on labels), and Bayesian dark knowledge (distilling a complex Bayesian model into a faster CGMMN).

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides a principled and elegant extension of the MMD-based generative modeling framework to the conditional setting. Modeling conditional distributions is a fundamental problem, and this work significantly expands the applicability of GMMNs beyond just modeling joint distributions. The proposed CMMD metric, based on matching conditional embedding operators, is a novel and well-suited objective for this task.

2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The proposed method is well-grounded in the theory of kernel embeddings. The core idea of matching the entire family of conditional distributions via a single operator-norm distance is statistically more powerful than a naive approach of training separate models per condition. The latter would suffer from data sparsity, especially with continuous or high-cardinality conditioning variables. The formulation of the CMMD objective (Eq. 2) cleverly isolates the model parameters from the components depending on the conditioning variable, allowing for straightforward optimization via standard backpropagation.

3.  **Strong and Diverse Experimental Results:** The paper provides compelling empirical validation across a diverse set of applications. The classification results on MNIST and SVHN are competitive with other state-of-the-art deep generative models. The conditional generation results on MNIST and Yale Faces are qualitatively good. Furthermore, the application to distilling Bayesian models is particularly interesting and showcases a novel use-case for this type of generative model, with results showing minimal performance degradation compared to the original Bayesian "teacher" model.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The theoretical exposition in Sections 2.3 and 3.1 is quite dense and may be difficult for readers not already expert in kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. The concepts of cross-covariance operators and their Hilbert-Schmidt norms are introduced with minimal intuition, making it challenging to grasp the core mechanism of CMMD without consulting the original references (e.g., Song et al., 2009). A more self-contained and intuitive explanation would significantly improve the paper's accessibility and impact.

2.  **Computational Trade-offs:** The CMMD objective requires computing and inverting kernel matrices, an operation that scales cubically with the minibatch size. This creates a challenging trade-off: MMD-style objectives are known to require reasonably large batch sizes to obtain stable, low-variance gradient estimates, but large batches are computationally prohibitive. The paper uses minibatches of up to 500, which is already computationally demanding. A discussion on the sensitivity of training stability and performance to this minibatch size hyperparameter would be a valuable addition.

3.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** While the proposed approach is conceptually superior to a naive baseline, an empirical comparison would be beneficial. For the discrete conditioning case (e.g., MNIST generation conditioned on class label), one could train a single GMMN for all classes (sharing network parameters) and simply treat the conditioning variable as an additional input. Comparing CGMMN against such a baseline would more concretely demonstrate the practical benefits of matching the conditional operator directly.

4.  **Minor Technical Issues:**
    *   In Section 2.2, the paper states that the empirical squared MMD estimator is unbiased. This is incorrect; it is a biased estimator. The unbiased estimator is a U-statistic with a different form. While this is a minor point, it should be corrected for technical accuracy.
    *   There appears to be a slight notational inconsistency in the definition of the conditional embedding operator `CY|X := CYX C^{-1}_{XX}` compared to the formulation in the cited work [29], which may imply a different definition of the cross-covariance operator C_XY. Clarifying this would be helpful.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and significant contribution to the field of deep generative models. The extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting is an important step forward, and the paper demonstrates its utility with impressive results across multiple challenging tasks. The primary weaknesses relate to the clarity of the theoretical sections and a lack of discussion on computational trade-offs, which are addressable in a revision. The strengths in originality, technical soundness, and empirical results make a compelling case for acceptance.
