

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions via a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The authors formalize a conditional kernel mean embedding operator and derive a CMMD objective to train deep networks that generate samples conditioned on input variables. The method is evaluated on predictive modeling (MNIST/SVHN classification), contextual generation (MNIST digits conditioned on labels, Yale face images), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. Results show competitive performance against strong baselines like CMMVA and DSN on prediction tasks, with visually plausible conditional generations.

**Quality**  
The technical foundation is sound, with rigorous theoretical development (Theorems 1–3) and a clear derivation of the CMMD objective. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive for the tasks considered, using standard benchmarks and comparisons to relevant baselines (e.g., CMMVA, DSN). However, the computational cost of CMMD (O(n³) for kernel matrices) is not adequately addressed. The authors omit runtime comparisons with discriminative baselines like DSN (Table 1), which is critical for assessing practical viability. The generative results (Fig. 2) show some noise compared to state-of-the-art GANs, and the lack of ablation studies on kernel choices or conditional variable encodings weakens the analysis. The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment (Sec. 4.3) is a compelling application but limited to a single regression dataset.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-organized and logically structured, with clear motivation in the introduction. The mathematical exposition, while dense in Sections 2–3, is generally precise. However, key implementation details for reproducibility are missing: the exact hyperparameters for CNN architectures (e.g., learning rates, kernel choices), sampling strategies for conditional generation (beyond "with/without perturbation"), and the autoencoder integration (Sec. 4.2.1) are insufficiently specified. Algorithm 1 omits regularization details, and the reference to "Appendix A.2.1" for mini-batch handling is inadequate. The figures (Figs. 1–5) effectively illustrate concepts but could better clarify the generative process.

**Originality**  
This work makes a significant contribution by extending GMMN (Li et al., ICML 2015) to conditional distributions, addressing a notable gap in moment-matching generative models. Unlike conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), CGMMN avoids adversarial training instability while enabling flexible conditional generation. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work (e.g., conditional VAEs [Sohn et al., NIPS 2015]), though it could better contextualize the novelty against recent conditional moment-matching methods like those in Song et al. (ICML 2013). The Bayesian dark knowledge application is a novel use case for conditional generative models.

**Significance**  
CGMMN advances the applicability of moment-matching networks to key tasks where conditioning is essential (e.g., predictive modeling, contextual generation). The competitive results on classification (Table 1) and the Bayesian distillation framework demonstrate tangible utility. However, the scope is limited to small-scale benchmarks (MNIST/SVHN), and the generative performance does not match GAN-based methods. The method’s simplicity (training via backpropagation) is a strength, but the computational overhead may hinder adoption. The broader significance lies in providing a theoretically grounded alternative to conditional GANs for tasks requiring stable training.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- Novel conditional extension of moment-matching networks with strong theoretical grounding.  
- Competitive predictive performance on standard benchmarks.  
- Interesting application to Bayesian model distillation.  
- Clear generative process enabling diverse conditional samples.  

*Con*:  
- Reproducibility concerns: missing hyperparameters, unclear sampling details, no code release.  
- Computational cost not benchmarked against discriminative baselines (e.g., DSN).  
- Generative results lack state-of-the-art quality (e.g., noisy MNIST samples in Fig. 2).  
- Limited to small datasets; scalability untested.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a valuable theoretical and methodological contribution to conditional generative modeling. While the experimental scope is modest, the core idea is well-motivated and the results are compelling within the evaluated tasks. The main limitations—reproducibility gaps and computational overhead—are addressable with minor revisions. I recommend **weak acceptance** (3/5), as the work advances the field meaningfully but requires stronger empirical validation and implementation details to fully realize its potential. The authors should provide code, hyperparameters, and runtime comparisons in the camera-ready version.
