This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend GMMN by learning conditional distributions using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The key idea is to embed conditional distributions via the conditional mean operator CY|X in an RKHS and minimize the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between the empirical operator from data and that induced by a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w). The generator takes as input the conditioning variables x and noise h, and is trained end-to-end by back-propagation through a differentiable kernel on outputs. The CMMD objective admits a closed-form in terms of Gram matrices, enabling minibatch SGD. Experiments cover (i) discriminative prediction on MNIST/SVHN by conditioning on images and generating class-score vectors, (ii) conditional generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and (iii) distillation of Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge” [15]) where CGMMN mimics the teacher’s predictive p(y|x, D). Results are competitive with prior MMD-based generators [19], conditional GANs [21] conceptually (not always empirically compared), and max-margin DGMs [18], with simple training that avoids adversarial min–max optimization.

Relation to prior work: CGMMN builds on MMD [7], kernel conditional mean embeddings [29], and GMMNs [19, 3] by moving from joint to conditional matching. It is conceptually parallel to conditional GANs [21] and conditional VAEs [28], but uses kernel-based moment matching rather than likelihood bounds or adversarial losses, offering a stable, single-objective training criterion.

Quality
- The CMMD objective is well-motivated: if CY|X matches, then PY|x matches for all x (under standard universality assumptions). The derivation to a trace form using Gram matrices is sound, and the stochastic training procedure is straightforward.
- Empirical results show competitive classification error on MNIST/SVHN (close to CMMVA [18], behind strong discriminative CNNs such as DSN [17]), plausible class-conditional samples on MNIST/Yale, and a regression distillation example where RMSE matches PBP [10].
- However, several important details needed to assess/replicate the method are missing or buried in the appendix: exact kernels and bandwidth selection for y, choice of kernel and regularization λ for x, number of generated samples per x, handling of continuous x in sampling, and stability tricks for minibatch CMMD (e.g., Cholesky solves vs explicit inverses).
- Computational complexity is a concern: per-minibatch inversions of K + λI are O(B^3). A clear wall-clock/runtime comparison against strong baselines (e.g., DSN [17], CMMVA [18]) and an ablation on batch size vs performance would substantiate practicality. Discussion of scalable approximations (Nyström, random features, block-diagonal K, caching) would be helpful.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but the CMMD derivation is dense. Moving the key steps (operator embedding assumptions, mini-batch estimator, stability/regularization) from the appendix into the main text would improve accessibility.
- Please specify kernels and hyperparameters explicitly for each task: kernel on outputs (Gaussian? bandwidth? median heuristic?), kernel on inputs, λ, noise distribution for h and its dimension, learning rate schedule, and data perturbation scheme used when sampling “near” training inputs for distillation.
- A concise algorithmic box with the exact minibatch CMMD computation (including how Ksd, Lds are formed and inverted per-batch) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- Extending MMD training from joint distributions (GMMN) to conditional distributions via operator matching is a natural but nontrivial step; the formulation and empirical validation are novel to my knowledge within deep generative modeling circa NIPS 2016.
- The use of CMMD for Bayesian dark knowledge is interesting and distinct from [15], which distills via minimizing KL to a soft target; here, the student matches the teacher’s conditional distribution in RKHS.

Significance
- Conditional modeling is widely useful (prediction, controllable generation, modular probabilistic systems). A stable, backprop-trainable alternative to adversarial training is valuable.
- The empirical scope is limited to small/medium benchmarks. Demonstrations on larger-scale conditional generation (e.g., CIFAR-10 class-conditional) or structured prediction (e.g., image-to-attribute) would strengthen impact. Including comparisons to cVAE [28] and cGAN [21] baselines on the same tasks would clarify the trade-offs.

Strengths
- Simple, principled objective; avoids adversarial instability.
- Flexible architecture; compatible with CNN/MLP and autoencoder-code matching.
- Competitive results on standard benchmarks.
- Clear link to kernel conditional mean embeddings, with a closed-form minibatch objective.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scalability: cubic cost in batch size; no runtime/memory comparison reported.
- Sensitivity: no studies on kernel choices, bandwidth, λ, batch size, or noise dimension h.
- Limited baselines for conditional generation (no cVAE/cGAN comparisons), and limited quantitative evaluation of samples beyond nearest neighbors.
- Reproducibility: insufficient architectural and training details; code not provided.
- Theory: the equality of operators is a sufficient condition; practical settings may violate assumptions (e.g., non-characteristic kernels on x). Some discussion of robustness to these violations would be useful.

Suggestions
- Report wall-clock training times and throughput vs baselines; include ablations on batch size and λ.
- Add sensitivity analyses for kernel bandwidth (median heuristic vs cross-validation), number/dimension of h, and number of generated samples per conditioning x.
- Compare against conditional VAEs [28] and conditional GANs [21] on conditional generation tasks; include quantitative sample quality (e.g., Parzen estimates on codes as in [19] or task-specific metrics).
- For Bayesian dark knowledge, report predictive NLL and calibration metrics (e.g., reliability plots), not only RMSE.
- Release reference code and provide full hyperparameter/architecture specifications.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, general extension of MMD-based training to conditional settings.
- Stable and easy-to-implement objective with competitive performance.
- Broad applicability (prediction, conditional generation, model distillation).

Arguments against acceptance
- Computational scalability and runtime not convincingly addressed.
- Empirical evaluation limited in scope and missing key baselines for conditional generation.
- Reproducibility concerns due to missing details and lack of code.

Overall: a solid and relevant contribution that advances MMD-based generative modeling to conditional distributions. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can clarify computational cost, add missing details/baselines, and strengthen reproducibility.
