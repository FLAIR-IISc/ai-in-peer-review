This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a novel deep generative modeling technique based on extending the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) criterion to the conditional setting. The core idea is to learn a conditional distribution p(y|x) by minimizing the conditional MMD between the empirical conditional distribution and the model’s generated distribution. The approach leverages back-propagation for efficient training, making it a practical extension to existing GMMN frameworks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN across a diverse set of tasks: predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, showcasing competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of recent advancements in generative modeling, notably the success of GANs [Goodfellow et al., 2014] and GMMNs [Li et al., 2016]. However, the authors rightly point out the challenges associated with the min-max optimization of GANs and the appeal of MMD as a simpler, yet effective alternative. Extending this MMD approach to the conditional setting addresses a key limitation of GMMNs, which primarily focus on modeling joint distributions. Related work in conditional generative modeling includes conditional GANs [Mirza & Osindero, 2014] and conditional variational autoencoders [Sohn et al., 2015].  The paper effectively positions CGMMN within this landscape, highlighting its unique contribution of a moment-matching approach to conditional generation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** CGMMN presents a novel and significant extension to the GMMN framework. The application of MMD to conditional distributions is a valuable contribution, providing an alternative to adversarial and variational approaches. The demonstrated applicability to tasks like Bayesian dark knowledge distillation is particularly interesting, potentially offering a more efficient way to transfer knowledge from complex Bayesian models.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a sound theoretical foundation for the proposed method, drawing upon concepts from kernel methods and reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS).  The derivation of the conditional MMD criterion is well-motivated and provides a clear objective function for training.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The authors perform a comprehensive empirical evaluation on a range of tasks, demonstrating competitive performance with existing state-of-the-art methods. The results on MNIST, SVHN, and the Boston Housing dataset provide evidence of the method’s effectiveness across different data modalities and problem settings.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the core concepts and methodology. The figures and tables are informative and aid in understanding the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The training process, involving kernel computations and matrix inversions, can be computationally expensive. While the authors mention using mini-batches, a detailed comparison of runtime performance against methods like Deeply-Supervised Networks (DSN) [Lee et al., 2015] (which can be trained efficiently) would be beneficial. A discussion of scalability to larger datasets would strengthen the paper.
*   **Reproducibility:** A critical weakness is the lack of publicly available code.  Reproducibility is paramount in machine learning research, and providing code is essential for allowing others to verify the results and build upon the work.
*   **Model Details:** The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the specific network architectures used in the experiments.  While the general architecture (e.g., MLP, CNN) is mentioned, important hyperparameters such as the number of layers, the size of hidden units, and the choice of optimization algorithm (beyond AdaM) are not always clearly specified. This makes it difficult for practitioners to reproduce the results exactly.
*   **Mathematical Exposition:** While theoretically sound, some sections, particularly those involving kernel embeddings, can be challenging to follow for readers not deeply familiar with the underlying mathematics. Providing more intuitive explanations and examples could improve clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to conditional generative modeling.
*   The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance on a range of tasks.
*   The work addresses an important gap in the existing literature, extending MMD-based generative models to the conditional setting.
*   The potential for applying CGMMN to tasks like Bayesian dark knowledge distillation is promising.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of publicly available code hinders reproducibility.
*   The computational cost of training is a potential concern, especially for large datasets.
*   The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the specific network architectures used in the experiments.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling.  While the lack of code and the computational considerations are concerns, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical validation of the proposed method outweigh these weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with the strong encouragement to the authors to release their code to the research community. Addressing the details of the network architectures in a revised version would also be beneficial.
