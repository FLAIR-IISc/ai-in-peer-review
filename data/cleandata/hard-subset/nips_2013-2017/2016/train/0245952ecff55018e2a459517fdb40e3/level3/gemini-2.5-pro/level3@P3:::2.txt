This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The work extends Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which train generative models by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between generated and real data distributions. The core contribution is the development of a Conditional MMD (CMMD) objective function. This objective is based on matching the kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, which are represented as operators in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors propose training a deep neural network that takes a conditional input `x` and a random noise vector `h` to produce a sample `y`. The network's parameters are learned by minimizing the CMMD objective using mini-batch stochastic gradient descent and backpropagation. The method is evaluated on a diverse set of tasks, including predictive modeling, contextual image generation, and knowledge distillation from Bayesian models, demonstrating competitive performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper tackles the fundamental and widely applicable problem of conditional generation. Extending moment-matching networks to this setting is a logical and significant next step for this line of research.

2.  **Principled Approach:** The proposed CMMD objective is a principled extension of MMD. The authors correctly identify that naively applying MMD for each condition `x` is infeasible and instead propose a global objective based on matching conditional mean operators. This is a non-trivial and technically interesting contribution.

3.  **Broad Experimental Validation:** The paper demonstrates the versatility and effectiveness of CGMMN across a range of different tasks and datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Faces, Boston housing). This comprehensive evaluation strengthens the paper's claims and shows the potential impact of the method.

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of this paper is its lack of clarity, which severely hinders the understanding and reproducibility of the core technical contribution. While the high-level idea is appealing, the connection between the theory and the practical implementation is opaque.

1.  **Lack of Intuitive Explanation:** The paper begins with a dense, formal introduction to RKHS operators and tensor products but fails to provide an intuitive motivation for the CMMD objective. The unconditional MMD objective has a clear intuition: it minimizes the distance between the mean feature representations of two distributions. The paper would be much stronger if it could provide a similarly accessible explanation for CMMD. For instance, Remark 1 hints at CMMD being a weighted version of MMD, where weights are determined by the conditional variables. This is a promising idea that is unfortunately not developed into a clear, intuitive picture for the reader.

2.  **Opaque Derivation and Explanation of Equation (2):** Equation (2) is the central formula for the entire method—the empirical CMMD estimator that is optimized. However, its presentation is highly problematic. It appears without a clear derivation from the theoretical definition `||C_d - C_s||^2_F⊗G`. The reader is left to guess how the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference of operators translates into a formula involving traces and inverted Gram matrices. This gap makes it impossible to understand the assumptions made or the practical implications of each term in the objective.

3.  **Insufficient Algorithmic Details:** The path from Equation (2) to the practical implementation in Algorithm 1 is not detailed enough for reproducibility. The paper does not state the precise computations performed in a mini-batch update. For example:
    *   How are the kernel matrices (`K_d`, `K_s`, `L_d`, `L_s`, etc.) and their inverses computed efficiently for each mini-batch?
    *   What are the computational and memory complexities of a single gradient step with respect to the batch size? Matrix inversion is `O(n^3)`, which is a major concern. The paper mentions this cost but does not explain how it is managed in the algorithm.
    *   How is the gradient of the complex trace expression in Equation (2) computed with respect to the generator's parameters `w`? The parameters `w` only influence the terms related to the generated samples (`d`), but the exact backpropagation path is unclear.

The reliance on supplemental material that is itself difficult to follow does not excuse these omissions from the main paper. A paper should be self-contained in its core technical details.

### Requested Clarifications

To address the major weaknesses, I would ask the authors to incorporate two concrete clarifications in a revised version:

1.  **An Intuitive Derivation of Equation (2):** Please provide a clear, step-by-step derivation of the empirical CMMD objective in Equation (2) starting from the squared Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference of the empirical conditional operators `||Φ_d(K_d + λI)⁻¹Υ_dᵀ - Φ_s(K_s + λI)⁻¹Υ_sᵀ||²`. This derivation should explain the role of each component, particularly the `(K + λI)⁻¹` term, in an intuitive way. This would bridge the gap between the high-level theory and the concrete objective function.

2.  **A Clear Mapping from Equation (2) to the Algorithm:** Please provide a detailed, step-by-step description of the computations for a single mini-batch gradient update. This should include:
    *   The precise dimensions of all matrices involved (e.g., `K_s`, `K_d`, `L_ds`) for a mini-batch of size `B`.
    *   The explicit formula for the gradient `∂L̂_CMMD / ∂w`.
    *   A discussion of the computational complexity of one update step and how the `O(B^3)` matrix inversion cost is handled in practice.

### Summary and Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting and potentially significant method for conditional generative modeling. The idea of extending MMD to the conditional setting is novel, and the experimental results are promising. However, the paper is severely hampered by a lack of clarity in its core technical sections. The key objective function is presented without adequate derivation or intuition, and the description of the algorithm is insufficient for readers to understand or reproduce the work.

While the contribution has high potential, the execution of the paper in its current form is not up to the standard for NIPS. I am therefore on the borderline but lean towards rejection. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can substantially revise the paper to address the clarity issues detailed above, as the underlying ideas are valuable to the community.
