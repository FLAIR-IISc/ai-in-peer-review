This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN/MMD to conditional settings via conditional kernel mean embeddings and a new CMMD loss. The model concatenates conditioning variables with noise, trains by backprop, and is evaluated on supervised classification (MNIST, SVHN), contextual image generation (MNIST digits, Yale faces), and Bayesian “dark knowledge” distillation. Relative to prior work, it connects kernel conditional mean operators (Song et al.) to deep generative modeling, complements adversarial approaches (GAN/cGAN; Laplacian pyramid GAN at NIPS) and conditional VAEs, and relates to max-margin DGMs for prediction. The revision appears to have addressed earlier concerns about low-dimensional latent spaces, scalability, and utility vs. alternatives, and clarifies several minor points.

Quality: The approach is technically grounded, with CMMD derived from conditional embeddings and trained efficiently via minibatches. However, rigor has a key gap: Theorem 3 is stated without a proof or citation; a proof sketch (e.g., via characteristic kernels and operator equality implying equality of conditional mean embeddings for all x) is necessary. Equation (2) is central but the mapping to the “weighted MMD” view remains opaque; the manuscript must clarify the “·” notation (matrix multiplication vs. Hadamard product) and show the algebraic steps. Notationally, the symbol C is overloaded for both conditional operators and other quantities, which is confusing.

Clarity: Generally well organized, but the above notation issues impede reproducibility. A brief complexity discussion of CMMD with minibatches (costs of K̃−1, memory) and practical kernel choices would improve clarity.

Originality: The conditional extension of GMMN via CMMD is a natural but nontrivial contribution that differs from cGAN/CVAE by avoiding adversarial/variational objectives.

Significance: Empirical results are decent—on par with GMMN for generation and competitive with MM-DGMs for classification—showing versatility. The strongest traction is likely in pure conditional generation. The Bayesian distillation application is intriguing but only shown on a single dataset/model, limiting claims of generality. Wider impact requires results on more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, LSUN) and broader distillation studies.

Pros:
- Clean conditional extension of MMD training; simple, stable optimization
- Versatile applications; competitive results on digits/faces
- Addresses several prior reviewer concerns

Cons:
- Missing proof/reference for Theorem 3
- Unclear derivation/notation in Eq. (2); overloaded C
- Limited experimental breadth (no CIFAR/LSUN; single distillation setting)

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on fixing theory/notation and strengthening experiments.
