This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The core contribution is the development of a novel training objective, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This objective is derived from the theory of kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, where conditional distributions are represented as operators mapping from the input RKHS to the output RKHS. The proposed CGMMN model uses a deep neural network to map a conditional variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` to an output sample `y`. The network's parameters are learned by minimizing the CMMD between the model's conditional distribution and the empirical data distribution using stochastic gradient descent.

This work builds directly on GMMN [19, 3], which used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to learn a joint distribution. By extending this to the conditional case, the authors significantly broaden the applicability of the moment-matching framework, placing it as an alternative to other conditional deep generative models like Conditional GANs [21] and Conditional VAEs [28]. The paper demonstrates the model's versatility on three distinct tasks: predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual image generation (generating handwritten digits and faces conditioned on labels), and distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and principled extension of the moment-matching framework to conditional generative modeling. This addresses a significant gap, providing a stable, non-adversarial training alternative to Conditional GANs. The CMMD objective is a well-motivated application of established theory on conditional kernel embeddings [29]. This contribution significantly increases the utility and scope of GMMN-style models.

2.  **Versatility of the Framework:** The authors effectively showcase the model's flexibility by applying it to a diverse set of problems. The predictive modeling and contextual generation tasks are natural applications. The application to "Bayesian dark knowledge" is particularly interesting and novel, suggesting a new way to create fast, deterministic "student" networks that approximate the predictive posterior of complex Bayesian "teacher" models.

3.  **Solid Generative Results:** For the contextual generation task, the model produces clear and diverse samples on both the MNIST and Yale Faces datasets. The qualitative results demonstrate that the model successfully learns to generate samples from the correct conditional distribution (e.g., generating digits corresponding to a given class label). The nearest neighbor analysis provides evidence that the model is not simply memorizing the training data.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Missing Theoretical Justification:** The paper's theoretical foundation rests on Theorem 3, which states that equality of the conditional embedding operators implies equality of the conditional distributions. This is a critical result that justifies the use of CMMD as a loss function. However, the paper provides neither a proof for this theorem nor a citation to prior work where it may have been established. This is a significant omission that must be addressed to ensure the technical soundness of the paper. A proof, even if straightforward, should be included in the appendix.

2.  **Clarity of Mathematical Exposition:**
    *   In Remark 1, the authors draw an analogy between MMD and CMMD, rewriting them in a compact form: `Tr(L · C)`. The notation is ambiguous. It is not specified whether the dot `(·)` represents standard matrix multiplication or an element-wise (Hadamard) product. Clarifying this is essential for the reader to understand the intended connection.
    *   The symbol `C` is overloaded. It is used to denote the conditional embedding operator (`CY|X`), the cross-covariance operator (`CXY`), and then reused for generic matrices `C1, C2, C3` in Remark 1. This overloading creates unnecessary confusion and should be avoided.

3.  **Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments are varied, they are conducted on datasets that are relatively simple by modern standards for generative models.
    *   The generative modeling results are limited to MNIST and Yale Faces. Demonstrating the method's performance on more challenging and higher-resolution datasets, such as CIFAR-10, CelebA, or LSUN, would be necessary to establish its scalability and significance in comparison to contemporary generative models.
    *   The predictive modeling results, while competitive with other generative approaches, are not state-of-the-art compared to purely discriminative deep networks. This is expected, but it frames this application more as a proof-of-concept of the model's versatility rather than a primary contribution.
    *   The Bayesian distillation experiment is a compelling idea, but its evaluation on only the small Boston housing dataset makes it difficult to judge its general applicability and effectiveness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel, theoretically-grounded, and important extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting.
*   The proposed CMMD objective provides a stable, non-adversarial training method for conditional generative models, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The model's versatility is well-demonstrated across generation, prediction, and a novel model distillation task.
*   The paper is well-written and the core idea is clearly communicated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The lack of a proof or citation for Theorem 3 is a serious flaw that undermines the paper's theoretical rigor.
*   The empirical evaluation is performed on simple datasets, which limits the demonstrated impact and scalability of the method.
*   Minor clarity issues in the mathematical notation could be improved.

In summary, this paper presents a strong and valuable contribution by developing a conditional version of generative moment-matching networks. The core idea is elegant and the potential applications are broad. The main weaknesses are the omission of a key proof for Theorem 3 and an empirical evaluation that could be more ambitious. However, the strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of the conditional framework, are substantial. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the theoretical gap concerning Theorem 3 and clarify the notational ambiguities in the final version.
