This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMNs), a method for learning conditional distributions. The work extends the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework, which uses Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to match a model's joint distribution to a data distribution. The key contribution here is the development of a Conditional MMD (CMMD) objective. This objective is derived from the theory of kernel embeddings of conditional distributions, where a conditional distribution P(Y|X) is represented by an operator that maps the embedding of a condition x to the embedding of the corresponding distribution over Y. By minimizing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between the estimated operators for the true data and the generated data, the model learns the conditional mapping. This work is well-situated within the growing literature on deep generative models, providing a moment-matching alternative to conditional variational autoencoders and conditional GANs.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of the paper lies in its novel formulation for conditional generative modeling within the moment-matching framework. Extending GMMNs to the conditional setting is a natural and important step, significantly broadening their applicability. The proposed CMMD objective, based on matching conditional embedding operators, is an elegant and theoretically principled approach. It correctly treats the family of conditional distributions P(Y|x) as a single object to be learned, rather than a collection of independent distributions. This provides a distinct advantage over a naive baseline that would train a separate GMMN for each value of the conditioning variable, a strategy that is inefficient and fails for continuous or high-cardinality conditioning variables due to data sparsity. The application to distilling knowledge from Bayesian models is also a novel and compelling use case.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed CMMD loss has a clean analytical form (Eq. 2) that can be computed using kernel matrices, much like the standard MMD. A crucial practical detail is that the computationally intensive matrix inverses ((K+λI)⁻¹) depend only on the conditioning variables (x) and not on the network parameters (w). This allows for straightforward gradient computation via standard backpropagation through the output variables (y), making the training procedure relatively simple and efficient. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating competitive performance across a diverse set of tasks, including classification, conditional image generation, and the aforementioned Bayesian model distillation. These results effectively validate the utility of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The main weakness of the paper is its clarity, particularly in the presentation of the theoretical background. Sections 2.3 and 3.1, which introduce the kernel embedding of conditional distributions and the CMMD objective, are very dense and difficult to follow without consulting the referenced papers (e.g., [29]). The paper would be significantly improved and made more self-contained if the authors provided more intuition and explanation behind the concepts of cross-covariance operators and conditional embedding operators. A clearer link between the operator view and the final objective in Eq. 2 would be beneficial for readers not already expert in this specific area of kernel methods.

2.  **Practical Concerns about Minibatch Size:** The CMMD objective relies on empirical estimates of kernel matrices from minibatches of data. The quality of these estimates, and thus the stability of the gradient, is highly dependent on the minibatch size. There seems to be a difficult trade-off: a small minibatch may lead to a very noisy estimate of the CMMD, while a large minibatch makes the O(N³) computation of the kernel matrix inverses prohibitively expensive. The authors use minibatch sizes of 300-500, which are already computationally demanding. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss this trade-off, comment on the sensitivity of the training to the minibatch size, and perhaps report on the computational cost of their approach.

3.  **Experimental Comparisons:** While the experiments are interesting, the paper could be strengthened by including a comparison to the naive baseline mentioned earlier, at least for a simple case. For the MNIST conditional generation task (conditioning on a discrete class label), it would be straightforward to train a separate GMMN for each of the 10 digits, perhaps with shared lower-level network layers. While the proposed CGMMN is theoretically more elegant and general, demonstrating its empirical superiority over this strong, simpler baseline would make its advantages more concrete.

**Minor Issues:**

*   In Section 2.2, the paper states that the MMD estimator is unbiased. This is incorrect for the V-statistic formulation presented. The unbiased estimator is the U-statistic, which involves a different summation that omits diagonal terms. This should be corrected for technical accuracy.
*   There appears to be a minor inconsistency in the definition of the conditional embedding operator. The paper states µY|x = C_Y|X φ(x), whereas reference [29] (Song et al., 2009) defines it as µY|x = C_Y|X k(x, ·). This may imply a different definition of the cross-covariance operator is being used, which should be clarified.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and significant extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. The proposed CMMD objective is elegant, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness on a variety of tasks. The primary weaknesses are the lack of clarity in the theoretical exposition and unaddressed practical concerns regarding computational trade-offs. Addressing these issues and including a comparison to a strong baseline would substantially improve the manuscript. Nevertheless, the contribution is solid and of clear interest to the NIPS community.
