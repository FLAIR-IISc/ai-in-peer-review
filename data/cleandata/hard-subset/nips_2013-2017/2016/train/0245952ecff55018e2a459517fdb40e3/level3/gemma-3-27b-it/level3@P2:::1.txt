This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a novel approach to learning conditional distributions using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The authors extend the successful framework of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) to scenarios where conditioning on input variables is crucial, such as predictive modeling, contextual generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models. This work builds upon the growing body of research leveraging kernel methods for generative modeling, notably the earlier work on GMMN [19] and the related field of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [5]. The core idea – utilizing a moment matching approach via CMMD – provides an alternative to the often unstable min-max game of GANs.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and contributions. The mathematical formulation of CMMD, while dense, is presented logically and builds upon established concepts like Hilbert space embeddings and maximum mean discrepancy [7]. The authors effectively connect CGMMN to several relevant application areas, demonstrating the versatility of the proposed method. The experimental evaluation is thorough, encompassing predictive tasks on MNIST and SVHN datasets, generative modeling experiments, and a demonstration of Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. Competitive performance is reported across all tasks.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** CGMMN presents a valuable extension to existing generative modeling techniques, specifically addressing the important problem of conditional generation within the MMD framework. This offers an alternative to GAN-based approaches, potentially offering more stable training dynamics.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear mathematical foundation for CMMD, leveraging established results from kernel methods and embedding theory. The connection to existing work on MMD and conditional kernel embeddings is well-established.
* **Versatility:** The applicability of CGMMN to diverse tasks—predictive modeling, contextual generation, and knowledge distillation—highlights its potential for broad impact.
* **Competitive Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that CGMMN achieves competitive performance on benchmark datasets compared to established methods like DSN [17], CMMVA [18], and others.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and logically organized, facilitating understanding of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Mathematical Accessibility:** While the mathematical formulation is sound, it can be challenging for readers not intimately familiar with kernel methods and Hilbert space theory. More intuitive explanations or examples could improve accessibility.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper does not provide sufficient details regarding the computational cost of training CGMMN, particularly concerning the inversion of kernel matrices. A runtime comparison with competitive methods, such as DSN, would be highly valuable. While the mini-batch algorithm is mentioned, its practical effectiveness in reducing computational burden isn’t quantified.
* **Reproducibility Concerns:** The lack of publicly available code hinders reproducibility.  Providing source code is crucial for verifying the reported results and facilitating further research.
* **Model Details:** The descriptions of the network architectures used in the experiments (e.g., number of layers, specific activation functions) are somewhat lacking. More precise details are needed for practitioners seeking to replicate the experiments. The statement about parameter count being "comparable" is insufficient.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the results are competitive, a more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different kernel choices, network architectures, and regularization parameters would strengthen the analysis.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension to existing generative modeling techniques.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across a range of tasks.
* The approach offers a potentially more stable alternative to GANs for conditional generation.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of publicly available code hinders reproducibility.
* The computational cost and scalability of the method are not adequately addressed.
* The model descriptions lack sufficient detail for precise replication.
* The mathematical exposition might be inaccessible to some readers.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling. The proposed CGMMN framework offers a promising approach to conditional generation with a strong theoretical foundation and competitive performance. The primary concern is reproducibility, which can be easily addressed by releasing the code. Addressing the computational cost aspect with a more detailed runtime analysis would further strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with the condition that the authors publicly release the code used for the experiments. Minor revisions addressing the clarity of the mathematical exposition and providing more detailed model descriptions would also be beneficial.
