## Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to the conditional setting (CGMMN). The core idea of using a conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) is well-motivated and the theoretical foundations are sound. The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating the effectiveness of CGMMN across various tasks. While the work doesn’t present a radical departure from existing generative modeling approaches, it offers a compelling and relatively simple alternative, particularly for conditional generation. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The authors address the limitations of standard GMMNs – their inability to model conditional distributions directly – which is critical for tasks like predictive modeling, contextual generation, and building hierarchical generative models. They propose CGMMN, which leverages a simple generative process (sampling from a distribution and passing through a DNN) and a novel CMMD objective for training. The paper effectively situates itself within the landscape of deep generative models, referencing GANs, VAEs, and prior MMD-based approaches. The connection to related work is generally strong, covering key papers in the field.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the CMMD objective is clear and builds logically upon existing MMD theory. Theorem 3 provides a solid justification for the approach.
* **Generality & Flexibility:** CGMMN provides a flexible framework applicable to a range of conditional generation tasks. The authors demonstrate this with experiments spanning prediction, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.
* **Simplicity:** Compared to GANs, the training objective (CMMD) is relatively straightforward and avoids the instability often associated with min-max games. This ease of training is a significant advantage.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors evaluate CGMMN on several datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face) and tasks, providing strong empirical evidence of its effectiveness. The inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., using different network architectures) and comparisons to strong baselines strengthens the conclusions.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the core concepts and mathematical derivations.  The inclusion of Algorithm 1 is helpful.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** While CGMMN is a valuable extension, it doesn't introduce a fundamentally new breakthrough in generative modeling. The core idea of extending MMD to a conditional setting is a natural progression, and the overall approach shares similarities with existing conditional GANs and VAEs.  The paper could benefit from a more critical discussion of how CGMMN *specifically* differentiates itself beyond being a “MMD-based alternative” to GANs/VAEs.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance likely depends on the choice of kernel and the regularization parameter λ. The paper doesn’t offer sufficient guidance on selecting these parameters.  A sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** Calculating and inverting the kernel matrices (even with mini-batch approximations) can be computationally expensive, particularly for high-dimensional data. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t offer a detailed discussion of scalability concerns.  Could techniques like Nyström approximation or other kernel approximation methods be applied?
* **Image Quality (MNIST & Yale):** While the generated images demonstrate the model's ability to capture the conditional distribution, the generated samples (Figures 2 & 5) appear somewhat blurry or noisy.  Comparing results with state-of-the-art conditional GANs or VAEs in terms of visual fidelity would be insightful.  The discussion of using an autoencoder as a pre-processor (Figure 4) points towards this limitation.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.1 (CMMD):** The discussion of the Hilbert-Schmidt assumptions feels a bit hand-wavy. While acknowledging they may not always hold, a more detailed explanation of the practical implications and potential workarounds would be appreciated.
* **Section 3.2 (CGMMN Nets):**  The choice of a uniform prior for the hidden variables *h* is somewhat arbitrary. Did the authors experiment with other distributions (e.g., Gaussian)?  Could the performance be sensitive to the choice of this prior?
* **Section 4.1.1 (MNIST):** It's unclear why the validation set is not used during training.  Are early stopping criteria applied based on the validation set?
* **Section 4.2 (Generative Performance):**  The comparison to the nearest neighbors in the training set (Figure 3) is useful, but it doesn't fully quantify the diversity of the generated samples.  Metrics like Inception Score or Frechet Inception Distance (FID) would provide a more objective assessment of the generation quality.
* **Section 4.3 (Bayesian Dark Knowledge):** The results seem promising, but more detailed information about the PBP model (e.g., its architecture, hyperparameters) would be helpful for reproducibility.

**5. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and clear presentation.
* Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple tasks.
* Addresses a practical need for conditional generative modeling.
* Provides a relatively simple and stable training procedure.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Incremental novelty compared to existing generative modeling approaches.
* Potential computational scalability issues.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.



**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

* Elaborate on the novelty of CGMMN beyond being a simple extension of GMMNs.
* Provide guidance on selecting appropriate kernel functions and regularization parameters.
* Discuss potential scalability issues and explore possible mitigation strategies.
* Include more quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) to assess the quality of generated samples.
* Add more details about the PBP model used in the Bayesian dark knowledge experiment.
