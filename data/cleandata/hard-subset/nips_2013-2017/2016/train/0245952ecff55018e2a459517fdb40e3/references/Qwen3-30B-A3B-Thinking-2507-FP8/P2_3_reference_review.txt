

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to conditional distributions. GMMN, introduced by Li et al. (2015), uses maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for training generative models without adversarial optimization. CGMMN extends this framework to handle conditional distributions by introducing Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the difference between kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions. The paper provides theoretical foundations for CMMD, presents a conditional generative architecture, and demonstrates CGMMN on predictive modeling (MNIST/SVHN classification), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on labels), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by extending MMD to conditional distributions. The authors establish a sufficient condition (Theorem 3) that if the conditional embedding operators are equal, then the conditional distributions are equal. This provides a solid foundation for their approach, building upon the kernel embedding framework for conditional distributions (Theorem 2). The theoretical development is rigorous and well-motivated.

**Methodological Simplicity and Practicality:** CGMMN avoids the instability issues of adversarial training (common in GANs) while retaining the flexibility of deep generative models. The approach is straightforward to implement using standard backpropagation, making it accessible to practitioners. The paper demonstrates how to estimate CMMD using empirical samples, which is computationally feasible with mini-batch optimization (Algorithm 1).

**Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate CGMMN across three distinct application domains, providing strong evidence of its versatility. The predictive modeling results on MNIST and SVHN demonstrate competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods, particularly noting that CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error on SVHN (comparable to CMMVA's 3.09%). The contextual generation results (Fig. 2-5) show that the model can generate meaningful samples conditioned on labels. The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment (Table 3) demonstrates that CGMMN can effectively distill knowledge from Bayesian models without degrading performance.

**Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The preliminary section provides a clear foundation for the technical contributions, and the experimental results are presented in a straightforward manner with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Comparison with Conditional GANs:** While the paper contrasts CGMMN with conditional VAEs and other discriminative models, it provides limited comparison with conditional GANs (e.g., [21]), which are the most common alternative for conditional generative modeling. The authors claim CGMMN "largely extends the ability of previous DGM based on maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) while keeping the training process simple," but they don't adequately demonstrate how CGMMN compares to conditional GANs in terms of sample quality, training stability, or computational efficiency.

**Insufficient Discussion of Limitations:** The paper mentions that "CGMMN is compatible with various network architectures and we are expected to get better results with more sophisticated structures," but doesn't discuss specific limitations of the approach. For instance, it doesn't address how CMMD scales with conditional variable dimensionality or how the method handles complex conditional dependencies that might require more sophisticated modeling.

**Theoretical Gaps:** While the paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, it doesn't fully explore the statistical properties of the CMMD estimator. The authors note that "the assumptions may not hold, however, the empirical estimator is always an element in the tensor product space which gives as a well-justified approximation," but don't provide theoretical guarantees about the estimator's convergence or bias.

**Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't include ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of specific components of CGMMN, such as the role of the conditional embedding operator or the impact of different kernel choices. This makes it difficult to assess which aspects of the framework contribute most to the performance gains.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling. The extension of moment-matching networks to conditional distributions is both theoretically sound and practically useful, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The paper is well-written, provides a comprehensive evaluation across multiple tasks, and demonstrates competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

The main weaknesses are the limited comparison with conditional GANs (the most common alternative for conditional generative modeling) and insufficient discussion of the method's limitations. However, these are not critical flaws that undermine the paper's overall contribution.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of generative modeling by extending moment-matching networks to conditional distributions in a theoretically sound and practically useful way. The approach is simple, avoids the instability of adversarial training, and demonstrates strong performance across multiple application domains. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a clear contribution that advances the state of the art in generative modeling.

The paper's main limitation is the insufficient comparison with conditional GANs, but this doesn't significantly detract from the paper's overall quality. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field that will be of interest to researchers working on generative models, particularly those seeking non-adversarial alternatives to GANs.

The paper meets all the NIPS review criteria: it's technically sound with strong theoretical foundations and empirical validation (Quality), clearly written and well-organized (Clarity), extends GMMN to a new conditional setting with novel CMMD (Originality), and provides a versatile framework for conditional generative modeling with demonstrated utility across multiple tasks (Significance).
