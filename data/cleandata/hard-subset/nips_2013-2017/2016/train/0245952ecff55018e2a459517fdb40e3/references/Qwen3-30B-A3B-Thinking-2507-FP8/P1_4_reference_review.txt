

# Peer Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extends the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors develop Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a criterion for measuring the difference between conditional distributions, and present a simple generative process that draws samples from a simple distribution and passes them through a deep network along with conditional variables. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of CGMMN across three application domains: predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

The work builds on Li et al.'s GMMN (ICML 2015), which extended the moment-matching approach to generative modeling. It also relates to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., 2015), but distinguishes itself by avoiding the min-max optimization challenges of GANs and providing a theoretically grounded moment-matching approach for conditional distributions.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for CMMD, establishing clear connections to MMD and demonstrating how the conditional embedding operators can be used to measure differences between conditional distributions. Theorems 1-3 are well-motivated and properly proven.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The authors evaluate CGMMN across multiple domains and datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face), demonstrating competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods in predictive modeling (outperforming VA and matching CMMVA on MNIST, with competitive results on SVHN) and generating diverse, high-quality samples in contextual generation tasks.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The paper provides clear implementation details, including the mini-batch training algorithm (Algorithm 1), making the approach easily reproducible. The authors also address practical concerns like the use of autoencoders to improve sample quality.

4. **Novel Application**: The Bayesian dark knowledge distillation application is particularly compelling, demonstrating that CGMMN can effectively distill knowledge from Bayesian models without degrading predictive performance (RMSE: 2.574±0.089 vs. 2.580±0.093).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Conditional GANs**: While the paper acknowledges conditional GANs as related work, it doesn't provide a direct comparison between CGMMN and conditional GANs in generation tasks. Given that conditional GANs are the dominant approach for conditional generation, this is a significant omission that weakens the paper's claim of being competitive.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The paper mentions that kernel gram matrix computation grows cubically with sample size but doesn't provide concrete analysis of the computational cost or scalability of CGMMN for large datasets. This is a critical consideration for practical adoption.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze how the choice of kernel, regularization parameter λ, or hidden variable dimension affects performance. This limits the practical utility for researchers who might want to apply the method to new domains.

4. **Theoretical Justification for Kernel Choice**: While the paper mentions that the kernel should be smooth for backpropagation, it doesn't provide guidance on choosing appropriate kernels for different conditional distributions or datasets.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., "CGMMN is compatible with various network architectures and we are expected to get better results with more sophisticated structures"). However, the lack of comparison with conditional GANs is a notable weakness in the evaluation.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The mathematical formulations are clear, though some sections (particularly in the preliminary) may be challenging for non-experts. The figures effectively illustrate the model and results. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality**: The extension from GMMN to conditional distributions is a natural but valuable progression. The development of CMMD as a criterion for conditional distributions is novel, and the application to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation is particularly innovative. The paper adequately references prior work and clearly distinguishes its contributions.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a simple, effective method for conditional generative modeling that avoids the optimization challenges of GANs. The results across multiple domains demonstrate the versatility of the approach, and the Bayesian dark knowledge application has practical relevance for model compression. The method could be particularly valuable for practitioners who want to avoid the instability of GAN training.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The work makes a meaningful contribution to the field of generative modeling by extending moment-matching networks to handle conditional distributions in a theoretically sound and practically effective manner. The comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple domains demonstrates the method's versatility and competitive performance. While the paper has some weaknesses (particularly the limited comparison with conditional GANs), these do not significantly undermine the overall contribution.

The paper is well-suited to NIPS as it advances the state of the art in generative modeling with a novel, theoretically grounded approach that has practical applications. The method's simplicity and effectiveness make it likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison with conditional GANs in the generation experiments to better establish the relative strengths of CGMMN.

2. Include a section discussing computational complexity and scalability for larger datasets.

3. Provide more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (kernel choice, λ, hidden dimension).

4. Clarify the practical considerations for choosing kernel functions in different application domains.

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling, and I believe it would be of interest to the NIPS community.
