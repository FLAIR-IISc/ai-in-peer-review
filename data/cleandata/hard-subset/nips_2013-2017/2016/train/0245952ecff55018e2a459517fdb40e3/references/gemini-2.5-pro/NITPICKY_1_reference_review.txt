This paper proposes the Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Network (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) to model conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The core contribution is the development of a novel training objective, the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). Whereas the standard Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) measures the distance between the kernel mean embeddings of two distributions, CMMD measures the distance between the kernel mean embedding *operators* of two conditional distributions. The proposed CGMMN model takes a conditional variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to a deep neural network to generate a sample `y`. The network's parameters are learned by minimizing the CMMD between the model's conditional distribution and the empirical conditional distribution from the data. This work positions itself as an alternative to Conditional GANs, retaining the benefit of a stable, single objective function from GMMNs. The authors demonstrate the model's versatility on tasks including predictive modeling, contextual image generation, and knowledge distillation from Bayesian models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of CMMD:** The formulation of the CMMD objective as the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators is a principled and logical extension of MMD to the conditional setting. It provides a non-parametric way to measure the discrepancy between two conditional distributions, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

2.  **Stable Training Objective:** By extending the moment-matching framework, the proposed method avoids the difficult min-max optimization and potential mode-collapse issues associated with training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). This focus on a single, well-defined (though complex) objective function is a clear advantage.

3.  **Demonstrated Versatility:** The paper successfully applies the CGMMN framework to a diverse set of problems, from standard classification and image generation to the more unique application of distilling "dark knowledge" from a Bayesian model. The distillation experiment, in particular, showcases an interesting and non-obvious use case for a conditional generative model.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The CMMD objective in Equation (2) requires the computation and inversion of kernel Gram matrices (`K_d`, `K_s`) for each mini-batch. This incurs a computational cost of `O(B^3)` for a mini-batch of size `B`. This is substantially more expensive than the objective functions for VAEs or GANs and presents a significant barrier to scaling to large batch sizes or datasets. The paper does not adequately discuss this major practical limitation.

2.  **Questionable Evaluation Protocol for Prediction:** In the predictive modeling experiments (Section 4.1), the authors state that a prediction is made by drawing a single sample from the model and choosing the index of the maximum element. This is a highly unorthodox and potentially unstable evaluation method for a classifier. A standard approach would be to estimate the expected value of the output `y` given `x`, or to have the network output a probability distribution directly (e.g., via a softmax layer). Using a single stochastic sample introduces unnecessary variance into the prediction, making the reported error rates difficult to interpret and compare reliably with other methods.

3.  **Unconvincing Generative Quality:** The generated MNIST samples in Figure 2 appear noisy and lack the sharpness of contemporary (c. 2016) models like DCGAN. The authors themselves implicitly acknowledge this by introducing a second, more complex pipeline where CGMMN operates in the latent space of a pre-trained autoencoder (Figure 4). While this improves the results, it also suggests that the core CGMMN model struggles to generate high-quality images directly in pixel space. This two-stage process feels like a workaround for a weakness in the primary model, rather than a strength.

4.  **Lack of Technical and Methodological Clarity:**
    *   The theoretical justification in Section 3.1 relies on smoothness assumptions for the conditional mean embedding operator to be well-defined. These are mentioned only in passing, with the paper quickly pivoting to the empirical estimator being a "well-justified approximation." This glosses over important theoretical subtleties.
    *   The description of the Bayesian dark knowledge experiment is sparse. The process of generating the teacher dataset by "perturbing the inputs in the training set slightly" lacks crucial details about the nature and magnitude of these perturbations, which could significantly affect the outcome.
    *   Remark 1 provides some intuition for CMMD as a weighted version of MMD, but this connection could be explored more formally and clearly.

5.  **Numerous Minor Presentation Flaws:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of typographical and formatting errors, which detracts from its overall quality. These include:
    *   A template remnant in the footer of the first page: "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...".
    *   "MINIST dataset" instead of "MNIST dataset" in the title of Section 4.1.1.
    *   "out network" instead of "our network" in Section 4.1.2.
    *   "data augumentation" instead of "data augmentation" in the discussion of Table 1.
    *   The notation `Φ = (φ(y1), ...,φ(yN ))` is ambiguous without specifying how these feature vectors are arranged into an operator.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    1.  The CMMD loss is a novel and theoretically grounded extension of MMD for conditional models.
    2.  The model offers a stable alternative to the adversarial training of CGANs.
    3.  The application to Bayesian model distillation is creative and shows promise.

*   **Arguments for rejection:**
    1.  The high computational cost of the CMMD objective is a major practical drawback that is not addressed.
    2.  The evaluation protocol for the classification task is non-standard and unconvincing.
    3.  The generative quality is mediocre without an auxiliary autoencoder, suggesting limitations in the core method.
    4.  The paper lacks clarity on key technical and experimental details.
    5.  The manuscript is marred by numerous typographical and formatting errors, indicating a lack of careful preparation.

**Decision**

While the paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting idea in CMMD, its significant practical drawbacks (computational cost), questionable experimental choices (classification protocol), and unpolished presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The accumulation of minor flaws, combined with the more substantial weaknesses in evaluation and scalability, suggests that the work is not yet ready for publication at NIPS. I would encourage the authors to address the computational costs, adopt standard evaluation protocols, and thoroughly revise the manuscript for clarity and correctness.
