### Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

#### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the previously proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) [19, 3] to model conditional distributions p(y|x). While GMMN learns a joint distribution by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between generated samples and real data, CGMMN learns a conditional distribution by minimizing a novel objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD).

The core technical contribution is the CMMD criterion, which measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective kernel mean embedding operators. This approach is derived from the theory of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions [29]. The paper demonstrates that equality of these operators implies equality of the conditional distributions, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the objective function.

The generative architecture is straightforward: to generate a sample `y` conditioned on an input `x`, the network takes both `x` and a sample `h` from a simple prior distribution (e.g., uniform) as input and maps them through a deep neural network, i.e., `y = f(x, h | w)`. The CMMD objective, which is expressed in terms of kernel Gram matrices, is differentiable with respect to the network parameters `w`, allowing for end-to-end training via back-propagation.

The authors evaluate CGMMN on a diverse set of tasks: 1) predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), 2) contextual generation (generating MNIST digits conditioned on class labels and Yale faces conditioned on person identity), and 3) distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model. The experimental results demonstrate that CGMMN achieves performance competitive with state-of-the-art deep generative models.

#### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality: (Technical Soundness)**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed CMMD objective is a principled extension of MMD to the conditional setting, grounded in established theory on kernel embeddings of distributions [29]. The connection between the CMMD objective and the equality of conditional distributions (Theorem 3) provides strong theoretical justification. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments on multiple datasets and tasks, demonstrating the model's versatility. The results on predictive tasks are competitive with strong baselines like max-margin deep generative models [18].

*   **Weaknesses:** The practical computation of the CMMD objective involves inverting kernel matrices, an operation that scales as O(N^3) with the mini-batch size N. This could pose a scalability challenge compared to methods like conditional GANs, whose discriminators typically have a lower computational cost per batch. The paper mentions this scaling issue but does not analyze its practical implications in detail or discuss potential large-scale approximations beyond mini-batching. Additionally, the paper notes that the theoretical assumptions for CMMD may not hold in practice, relying on the empirical estimator as a well-justified approximation. While pragmatic, this represents a small gap between the theory and its practical implementation.

**Clarity: (Writing and Organization)**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically structured. The introduction provides excellent motivation for conditional generative modeling and situates the work within the existing literature. The "Preliminary" section effectively explains the necessary background on MMD and conditional kernel embeddings, making the paper accessible. The description of the model, the CMMD objective, and the training algorithm is easy to follow.

*   **Weaknesses:** While the final form of the empirical CMMD objective (Equation 2) is presented, the derivation is omitted. Including this derivation, perhaps in an appendix, would improve the paper's completeness and reproducibility. The details of the mini-batch algorithm are also somewhat brief; for instance, a more explicit discussion of how the two sets of samples (real and generated) are handled within each batch would be beneficial.

**Originality: (Novelty)**

*   **Strengths:** The paper's main contribution—a moment-matching framework for conditional generative modeling—is novel. While other conditional generative models like conditional GANs [21] and conditional VAEs [28] exist, this work is the first to propose a method based on conditional kernel mean embeddings. The formulation of the CMMD objective is a non-trivial and elegant extension of MMD. Furthermore, the application of this framework to distill knowledge from Bayesian models is an interesting and novel use case that highlights the model's flexibility.

*   **Weaknesses:** The work can be seen as a novel combination of existing components: the generative architecture is similar to that used in conditional GANs, and the theoretical tools are built upon prior work in kernel methods [29] and GMMNs [19]. However, the synthesis of these ideas into a coherent and effective framework is a significant contribution in its own right.

**Significance: (Impact)**

*   **Strengths:** This work significantly enhances the applicability of moment-matching networks, which are an important class of generative models that offer a more stable training alternative to the adversarial training of GANs. By providing a robust method for conditional density estimation, CGMMN addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with broad applications. The strong empirical results suggest that CGMMN is a valuable and practical tool for researchers and practitioners. The model's success across diverse tasks indicates its potential for wide adoption and for inspiring future research in non-adversarial generative modeling.

*   **Weaknesses:** The quality of the generated images (e.g., Figure 2) appears somewhat noisy compared to state-of-the-art results from contemporary GANs, although the authors demonstrate that this can be mitigated by using an autoencoder. The potential computational bottleneck from kernel matrix inversion might limit its application in scenarios requiring very large batch sizes.

#### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Principled and Novel Method:** Introduces CMMD, a theoretically-grounded and novel objective for training conditional generative models.
2.  **Stable Training:** Offers an alternative to adversarial training, inheriting the simpler and potentially more stable optimization of moment-matching methods.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The model's effectiveness is demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on diverse tasks, achieving competitive performance.
4.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is extremely well-written, making the contributions easy to understand and appreciate.
5.  **Broadened Applicability:** Significantly extends the scope of GMMNs, a promising direction in deep generative modeling.

#### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The cubic scaling of the CMMD objective with respect to batch size is a potential limitation that is not fully addressed.
2.  **Sample Quality:** The visual quality of generated samples, without additional components like an autoencoder, may not match the highest standards set by top-performing GANs.
