- Introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN) and Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) to model p(y|x).
- CMMD measures discrepancy via the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean operators in RKHS.
- Uses a deep generator y = f(x, h; w) trained by minimizing a minibatch CMMD objective with kernel Gram matrices.
- Provides a sufficiency theorem: equal conditional mean operators imply equal conditional distributions under strong assumptions.
- Builds on GMMNs and kernel embeddings of conditional distributions, offering an alternative to cGANs and CVAEs with a single-objective training.
- Connects to Bayesian dark knowledge by using CGMMN for distilling predictive distributions.
- Theory is well-motivated but lacks statistical analysis of consistency or sample complexity for training the deep generator.
- Strong theoretical assumptions (universality, EY|X[g|X] ∈ F, operator class) are not discussed for practical plausibility.
- Regularized operator estimator is implementable, but guidance on choosing λ and kernels is missing.
- Minibatch algorithm is straightforward, and inverse matrices do not depend on model weights, but per-batch cubic cost and memory are concerns.
- No timing, scaling analysis, or exploration of kernel approximations (Nyström/RFF) to address computational cost.
- Classification results are reasonable but lag behind strong discriminative baselines and lack comparison to identical architectures trained with cross-entropy.
- Conditional generation is shown qualitatively with nearest-neighbor checks, but lacks quantitative generative metrics and comparisons to cGAN/CVAE.
- Distillation experiment shows no degradation on a small regression dataset but is minimal and lacks calibration or broader comparisons.
- Reproducibility details (kernel types, bandwidths, λ, exact architectures/hyperparameters) are incomplete.
- Paper is generally well written with clear CMMD derivations, but main-text guidance on kernel choices and discrete vs. continuous x is weak.
- Figures show plausible but sometimes noisy samples; captions and minor typos/referencing inconsistencies need fixes.
- Extending MMD to conditional models via conditional mean embeddings is novel and nontrivial, offering a stable kernel-based alternative to adversarial/variational methods.
- CMMD could be a practical tool for conditional generators without adversarial training, appealing for stable training and kernel control.
- Empirical evidence is limited to toy/medium-scale datasets and does not yet demonstrate state-of-the-art or broad applicability.
- Pros: conceptually clean extension, single-objective stable training, architectural flexibility, and clear theoretical grounding.
- Cons: limited experimental evaluation, unaddressed computational scalability, sensitivity to kernel/hyperparameters not analyzed, and strong theoretical assumptions without guarantees.
- Key author questions: which kernels/bandwidths/λ were used, sensitivity to these choices, computational cost/memory vs. baselines, quantitative generative metrics, and feasibility of Nyström/RFF scalability.
- Suggestions: add quantitative generation metrics and cGAN/CVAE comparisons, include same-architecture discriminative baselines, ablate kernel/bandwidth/λ/minibatch, report runtime/memory and kernel approximations, and expand distillation experiments.
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — novel and technically sound but needs stronger empirical comparisons and quantitative evaluations.
