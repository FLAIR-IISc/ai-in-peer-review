Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to conditional distributions p(y|x). The key idea is to train a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w), with noise h, by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) between the conditional mean embeddings of the empirical data and of the model. Using the conditional mean operator CY|X and its empirical estimator via kernel Gram matrices, the authors derive a practical loss (Eq. 2) that can be optimized by back-propagation. They present applications to (i) predictive modeling (classification on MNIST/SVHN), (ii) conditional generation (digits conditioned on labels; Yale Faces), and (iii) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge”) on Boston Housing, showing competitive performance to recent DGMs (e.g., max-margin DGM) and reasonable sample quality.

This work builds on MMD and kernel mean embeddings [Gretton et al., JMLR 2012; Song et al., ICML 2009/2013], and on GMMN [Li et al., ICML 2015], extending the latter to the conditional case. It is related to conditional GANs [Mirza & Osindero, 2014], conditional VAEs [Sohn et al., NIPS 2015], and Laplacian pyramid GANs [Denton et al., NIPS 2015]. The paper’s novelty is to define and use CMMD as a principled, kernel-based conditional discrepancy to train conditional generators, avoiding adversarial min–max optimization.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The use of conditional mean embeddings to define CMMD is natural and principled. The connection to MMD is well articulated.
- Simplicity and stability: Training reduces to backprop through a deterministic network with a closed-form, single-objective loss; no adversarial game or variational bound.
- Generality: Applicable to discrete or continuous x and y; does not require differentiable kernels on x since the relevant inverses do not depend on w.
- Empirical breadth: Demonstrates classification, conditional image generation, and Bayesian distillation. Results on SVHN are close to strong DGM baselines (e.g., CMMVA).
- Practical implementation: Mini-batch training and straightforward sampling procedure; clear algorithmic description.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
Quality/technical soundness
- Theory is sufficient but not comprehensive: Theorem 3 gives a sufficiency statement (equality of conditional operators implies equality of conditionals) under strong assumptions. There is no finite-sample analysis, convergence rate, or generalization bound relating empirical CMMD minimization to conditional distribution consistency.
- Kernel/hyperparameter sensitivity: The success of MMD-style objectives hinges on kernel choices and bandwidths. The paper does not specify kernels for x and y nor describe selection/tuning procedures or sensitivity analyses.
- Computational complexity: Each mini-batch requires inverting K+λI. While done per batch, the cubic cost and numerical stability as batch size grows are not analyzed. Alternatives (e.g., low-rank approximations, random features) are not explored.
- Classification setup: For MNIST/SVHN, the empirical conditional p(y|x) is degenerate (one label per x). It would be helpful to justify why CMMD between a delta-conditional and the model’s output is preferable to standard cross-entropy, and to report calibration metrics or probabilistic accuracy.
- Evaluation depth: For generation, quantitative measures (e.g., Parzen log-likelihood used in contemporaneous work, or classifier-based scores) are missing; comparisons to CGAN/CVAE are absent. The Bayesian distillation experiment is very limited (single small dataset) and lacks runtime comparisons.

Clarity
- The derivation of Eq. (2) is compact; a short appendix with the full steps and shapes of matrices would help reproducibility. Practical details like kernel types, bandwidth selection, λ, and how x is perturbed for teacher sampling are missing or deferred.
- Notational overload between “s”/“d” datasets and “data”/“generated” could be clarified early.

Originality
- Extending GMMN to conditional settings via conditional mean embeddings is a logical and useful step. While conditional kernels and operator embeddings are known, using the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between empirical conditional operators as a training loss for deep conditional generators appears novel and well-motivated.

Significance
- CGMMN provides a stable, general-purpose alternative to CGAN/CVAE for conditional modeling, with promising results and potentially easier training. If scaled and better benchmarked, it could be a practical tool. However, the current empirical evidence, while positive, is not yet compelling across tasks, and ablations/comparisons are limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with a coherent loss.
- Simple, broadly applicable training procedure.
- Competitive classification accuracy vs. strong DGM baselines; reasonable conditional generation; viable Bayesian distillation.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical rigor: no comparison to CGAN/CVAE on conditional generation; no quantitative generation metrics; minimal Bayesian distillation evaluation.
- Missing details about kernels/hyperparameters and their effect; no ablation or sensitivity analysis.
- Lack of scalability strategies and computational analysis; theoretical guarantees are high-level only.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide kernel choices for x and y, bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic, cross-validation), and sensitivity studies. Report how these affect stability and accuracy.
- Compare against CGAN and CVAE on the same conditional generation tasks, with quantitative metrics common at the time (e.g., Parzen-window log-likelihood; classifier-based accuracy of generated samples).
- Add ablations: (i) kernel on x vs. the simpler MMD on the joint (x,y); (ii) batch size and λ impact; (iii) dimension of noise h; (iv) use of random Fourier features or Nyström to scale CMMD.
- Include runtime and memory benchmarks versus CGAN/CVAE and against a cross-entropy classifier for classification tasks.
- Strengthen theory with a finite-sample generalization bound for empirical CMMD minimizing conditional risk, or at least discuss sample complexity and regularization effects.
- Expand Bayesian distillation to more datasets and report predictive uncertainty calibration, test log-likelihood, and inference speedups over the teacher.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is well-motivated, technically sound at a high level, and proposes a simple, promising objective for conditional generative modeling. However, the empirical evaluation is not yet strong or comprehensive enough relative to contemporary conditional generative baselines, and important practical details (kernel choices/sensitivity, scalability) are missing. With stronger experiments, clearer implementation details, and some ablations, this work would be significantly strengthened.
