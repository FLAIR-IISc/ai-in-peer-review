

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions. The authors develop a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to train these models, which can learn conditional distributions p(y|x) efficiently through backpropagation. The paper demonstrates CGMMN on predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and distilling Bayesian models (Boston housing).

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for CMMD, building on kernel embedding of conditional distributions and extending the MMD framework to conditional settings. The derivation of Theorem 3 and the CMMD objective (Equation 2) is mathematically sound.

2. **Practical Implementation:** The authors present a clear generative process for CGMMN (Section 3.2) that enables efficient sampling for training. The implementation details and algorithm (Algorithm 1) are well-described, making it easy for readers to understand and potentially reproduce.

3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The paper evaluates CGMMN across multiple relevant tasks (predictive modeling, contextual generation, Bayesian knowledge distillation), demonstrating its versatility. The results on MNIST and SVHN for predictive tasks are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, and the contextual generation results are visually convincing.

4. **Clear Motivation:** The paper effectively motivates the need for conditional generative models, particularly for predictive modeling (where p(y|x) is more direct than p(x,y)), contextual generation, and building larger generative models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the CMMD objective is well-defined, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of convergence properties or sample complexity. It's unclear how the CMMD objective compares to alternative conditional generative criteria in terms of statistical efficiency.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Related Work:** The paper claims CGMMN "fills the research void" but doesn't adequately contrast with existing conditional generative models. The comparison with conditional GANs (e.g., Mirza & Osindero, 2014) is minimal, despite conditional GANs being the dominant approach for conditional generation. The paper should clarify how CGMMN differs from and improves upon these methods.

3. **Experimental Limitations:** The predictive modeling results on SVHN (Table 2) show CGMMN-CNN achieving 3.13% error, while DSN achieves 1.92%. The paper doesn't adequately explain why CGMMN underperforms here or how it might be improved. The paper also lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components (e.g., the conditional aspect vs. the MMD objective).

4. **Implementation Details:** Some critical implementation details are missing. For example, the paper mentions using a uniform prior for hidden variables (Section 3.2), but doesn't justify the choice of dimension (e.g., 5 for MNIST MLP, 20 for generation). The regularization parameters, learning rates, and training duration are also not specified.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. **Strengthen Theoretical Comparison:** Add a section comparing CMMD with alternative conditional generative criteria, including theoretical analysis of sample complexity or convergence rates. This would help establish CGMMN's theoretical advantages.

2. **Expand Related Work Discussion:** Include a more thorough comparison with conditional GANs and conditional VAEs, highlighting specific advantages of CGMMN (e.g., simpler training, no need for adversarial training). Cite recent works on conditional generative models (e.g., conditional VAEs, conditional GANs) to better position CGMMN in the literature.

3. **Improve Experimental Analysis:** Add ablation studies to show the contribution of the conditional aspect versus the MMD objective. For the SVHN results, provide a more detailed analysis of where CGMMN falls short compared to discriminative models and potential remedies.

4. **Provide More Implementation Details:** Specify hyperparameters, training times, and computational resources used. For the hidden variable dimension, provide justification or ablation results showing how dimension affects performance.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a well-motivated extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with solid theoretical foundations and promising empirical results across multiple tasks. However, the paper's contribution appears incremental rather than transformative, as it extends an existing framework to conditional settings without clearly demonstrating significant advantages over established conditional generative approaches. The lack of thorough comparison with conditional GANs and the limited theoretical analysis of the CMMD objective weaken the paper's claim of "significantly improving the applicability of moment-matching networks."

**Recommendation:**

The paper has merit but requires significant revisions to strengthen its theoretical foundation, comparative analysis, and experimental evaluation. The core idea is sound and useful for the community, but the current presentation doesn't sufficiently establish its novelty or advantages over existing conditional generative models. With the suggested revisions, this paper could make a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, I recommend **weak rejection** with the understanding that with substantial revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, the paper could be a strong candidate for future consideration.
