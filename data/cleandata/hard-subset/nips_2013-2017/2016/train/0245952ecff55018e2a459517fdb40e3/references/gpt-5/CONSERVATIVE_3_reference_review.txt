Summary
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of GMMN that models conditional distributions p(y|x). The key idea is to train a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w), where h is a simple noise source, by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) between the empirical conditional embedding ĈY|X from data and that induced by the generator. CMMD is defined as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators in RKHS, and can be computed using Gram matrices over x and y with regularization. Training uses backpropagation through the generator; unlike GANs, there is no min–max optimization. Experiments span: (i) predictive modeling on MNIST/SVHN (classification), (ii) conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and (iii) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (Boston Housing) by training the student CGMMN to match samples from a teacher Bayesian model.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on GMMN (Li et al., Dziugaite et al.) and kernel mean embeddings (Gretton et al.; Song et al.; Grunewälder et al.). The use of conditional mean embeddings to represent p(y|x) is standard; CMMD is essentially the HS-norm distance between conditional embedding operators estimated from finite samples.
- Addresses similar goals as conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero; Denton et al.) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al.), but replaces adversarial/variational training with moment matching.
- For prediction, comparisons to max-margin deep generative models (Li et al.) and discriminative CNNs are provided.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean formulation: training a conditional generator by matching conditional mean embeddings is elegant and avoids adversarial training instabilities.
- Derivation of a computable CMMD objective using Gram matrices is straightforward, and independence of K̃−1 from network parameters allows non-differentiable kernels on x.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across three tasks; implementation is simple (SGD with mini-batches, backprop only through y-kernels).
- Qualitative conditional generation results are reasonable; nearest-neighbor checks help alleviate (though do not rule out) copying concerns.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty/significance: The main mathematical machinery (conditional mean embeddings, operator estimation, HS norms) is well-established. The contribution is largely an application of known conditional embedding operators to the GMMN training regime. The paper does not clearly differentiate CMMD from prior uses of conditional embeddings as regression operators nor establish theoretical advantages over existing conditional generative models (cGAN, CVAE).
- Theoretical support: Theorem 3 is a sufficient condition and essentially restates known properties of universal RKHS embeddings. There is no analysis of the statistical consistency of minimizing empirical CMMD with samples from a parameterized generator, no generalization bounds, and no discussion of bias introduced by minibatch CMMD and regularization. The required smoothness assumptions are acknowledged but then set aside.
- Experimental rigor:
  - Classification: Reporting only error rates without negative log-likelihood or calibration (important for conditional generative models) is limiting. Comparisons omit a strong plain discriminative CNN trained with cross-entropy under the same architecture and training protocol. Hyperparameter search, number of runs, and variance are not reported. The method returns a single y-sample and takes argmax; the effect of sampling variance or averaging multiple samples is not examined.
  - SVHN: Performance is behind strong discriminative baselines by a meaningful margin; the paper claims competitiveness but does not analyze why/when the generative approach is preferable.
  - Generation: Only qualitative images and nearest neighbors are shown. No quantitative evaluation (e.g., Parzen estimated log-likelihood as used by GMMN/GAN in that era, or structured similarity metrics conditioned on labels). For MNIST, the need to add an autoencoder stage to get “clearer” images suggests kernel choice in pixel space is suboptimal; yet kernel selection is not systematically studied.
  - Distillation: A single small dataset (Boston Housing) with nearly identical RMSE to teacher, no uncertainty calibration, no runtime/computation comparison at test time, and no ablation against simpler students (e.g., MSE teacher targets or temperature-scaled soft targets). This is insufficient to support the distillation claim.
- Missing details hinder reproducibility: The exact kernels for x and y (type, bandwidth selection strategy, multiple-kernel choices), regularization λ, mini-batch size sensitivity, and computational cost per iteration are not clearly specified in the main text. Performance can be highly sensitive to kernel bandwidth; no ablation or sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Scalability: CMMD requires O(b^3) per minibatch due to K̃−1; the paper mentions mini-batching but does not provide empirical scaling, memory footprint, or practical upper bounds on batch size/dimensionality. Using pixel-space kernels for images is known to be problematic.
- Related work coverage: Conditional VAEs and cGANs are discussed but not compared quantitatively on conditional generation tasks. There is no comparison to GMMN+AE in the conditional setting beyond a qualitative figure.

Questions for the authors
1. What kernels and bandwidths were used for x and y in each experiment? How were bandwidths selected (e.g., median heuristic per batch, cross-validation)? How sensitive are results to these choices?
2. What is the computational cost (time and memory) per training step for typical batch sizes on MNIST/SVHN? How does training time compare to cGAN/CVAE?
3. For classification, why is cross-entropy not used as an auxiliary loss? What is test NLL or ECE? How many y-samples are drawn per x at test time, and does averaging improve accuracy?
4. Can you provide quantitative conditional generation metrics (even Parzen log-likelihood in code space) and side-by-side comparisons with cGAN/CVAE?
5. In distillation, what is the predictive uncertainty quality (e.g., NLL, calibration curves)? Any larger-scale datasets and test-time latency measurements?

Pros
- Simple, stable alternative to adversarial/variational training for conditional models.
- Solid grounding in kernel embeddings; implementation is straightforward.
- Broad set of tasks; qualitative results are reasonable.

Cons
- Incremental novelty; limited theoretical and empirical evidence that CMMD offers clear benefits over established conditional generative methods.
- Missing key experimental baselines/metrics; limited reproducibility details and sensitivity analyses.
- Scalability and kernel selection issues are not addressed.

Recommendation: Weak reject
While the idea of training conditional generators via operator-valued MMD is clean and potentially useful, the contribution feels incremental relative to known conditional embeddings and GMMN, and the experimental validation does not meet the level of rigor needed to endorse a new training objective over established cGAN/CVAE baselines. Strengthening the empirical comparisons (including calibration and likelihood metrics), providing ablations and kernel sensitivity analysis, and clarifying scalability would significantly improve the paper.

Confidence: High (familiar with kernel embeddings and generative modeling literature).
