Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep conditional generative model trained by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The model generates y given x by concatenating x with latent noise h sampled from a simple distribution and passing through a deep network. The learning objective is the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between empirical conditional mean embedding operators ĈY|X from data and from model-generated samples, estimated via kernel mean embeddings with regularization. This extends Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN; Dziugaite et al., 2015; Li et al., 2015) from unconditional to conditional settings by leveraging the kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009; Grunewälder et al., 2012). The paper contrasts its approach with conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), and max-margin deep generative models (Li et al., 2015).

Empirically, the paper evaluates: (a) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, using CGMMN to model p(y|x) and predicting via argmax of a sampled output; (b) conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces given class labels; and (c) distillation of Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge”) by training a CGMMN student to mimic a PBP teacher on Boston Housing.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: CMMD provides a direct, well-defined objective to match conditional distributions without adversarial min-max optimization. Backpropagation is straightforward since kernel matrices on x do not depend on model parameters.
- Sound use of kernel conditional mean embeddings: The construction of ĈY|X = Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ and the CMMD loss as a Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional operators are standard, principled tools, applied here to train deep conditional generators.
- Practical training details: Minibatch algorithm; observation that inverses of K̃ on x are parameter-independent; and flexibility in network architectures (MLP/CNN).
- Broad applicability shown across tasks: classification, label-conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: The extension from MMD to “conditional MMD” by comparing conditional embedding operators is a fairly direct application of known kernel embedding theory. Theorem 3 is a straightforward sufficiency statement under standard universality assumptions. The main novelty lies in using this objective to train conditional deep generators, which is a natural extension of GMMN.
- Empirical evaluation is limited relative to claims:
  - Predictive modeling: Results are competitive but do not clearly improve over strong discriminative or conditional generative baselines. For MNIST/SVHN, models like DSN and other CNNs outperform; comparisons to CVAE/cGAN for conditional prediction are absent.
  - Conditional generation: Only qualitative samples are shown. No quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen/IS/FID, human evaluation) or comparisons to conditional GAN/CVAE are provided. The autoencoder+CGMMN variant is plausible but again only qualitative.
  - Distillation: Only a single small regression dataset is reported, with no runtime or efficiency gains quantified, and no uncertainty calibration metrics.
- Missing implementation details and ablations: Kernel choices for x and y (type, bandwidth selection, regularization λ), sensitivity analyses, and computational complexity per minibatch are not reported. Given the O(b^3) cost in batch size b for inverting K̃, scalability requires careful discussion. In the presented algorithm, x is reused between data and generated pairs, which simplifies Eq. (2); clarifying this special case and its computational benefits would help.
- Theoretical depth: No finite-sample generalization analysis or consistency rates for the CMMD estimator are provided; the paper relies on known asymptotic properties. The sufficiency of operator equality for conditional equality is standard; necessity is not discussed.
- Related work coverage: Work on conditional two-sample testing and operator-based discrepancies beyond what is cited could be acknowledged; comparisons to CVAE/cGAN on conditional generation are particularly relevant.

Quality
- The derivation of CMMD from conditional mean embeddings is technically correct and consistent with prior RKHS literature. The empirical estimator and its expression via Gram matrices are standard. Using non-differentiable kernels on x is valid since K̃ is parameter-independent.
- Experimental methodology is generally reasonable, but lacks quantitative evaluation for generation, comprehensive baselines for conditional generation, detailed hyperparameter reporting, runtime/scalability analysis, and robustness/sensitivity studies.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well-structured, with a helpful background section and algorithm outline. However, key practical details are missing: kernel families and hyperparameters, bandwidth selection procedure, λ, and exact architectures for all tasks. Some typographical issues exist but do not hinder understanding.

Originality
- Moderate. The approach is a natural and direct extension of GMMN to conditional settings via known conditional embedding machinery. Using CMMD to train deep conditional generators appears not widely explored, but the conceptual step is incremental.

Significance
- Potentially useful as a simple, stable alternative to cGAN/cVAE for conditional modeling, with a principled objective. However, given current experiments, the improvement over existing methods is not demonstrated, and the computational costs of kernel operations may limit scalability. The work may be more impactful with stronger empirical evidence and clearer advantages.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative metrics and stronger baselines for conditional generation (e.g., CVAE, cGAN) and report FID/IS or task-specific metrics.
- Detail kernel choices, bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic or cross-validation), regularization λ, and sensitivity.
- Analyze computational complexity and wall-clock training time, especially the cost of K̃ inversions; discuss strategies like low-rank approximations or inducing points for larger batches.
- Include ablations on latent noise dimensionality, effect of reusing x versus sampling new x, and choice of kernel on y (especially for classification where y is one-hot).
- For Bayesian distillation, report predictive uncertainty calibration (e.g., NLL, calibration curves) and speedups at inference.

Pros
- Simple, principled objective avoiding adversarial training
- Solid grounding in kernel conditional embeddings
- Applicable across prediction, generation, and distillation
- Empirically competitive on classification

Cons
- Modest novelty; straightforward extension of GMMN
- Limited and largely qualitative generative evaluation; missing key baselines
- Incomplete details on kernels/hyperparameters; no sensitivity or scalability analysis
- Minimal demonstration of benefits for Bayesian distillation

Recommendation
- Borderline reject. The idea is technically sound and clearly presented, but empirical support and demonstrated advantages over existing conditional generative methods are insufficient at present. Strengthening experiments and providing more thorough analyses could raise the impact.
