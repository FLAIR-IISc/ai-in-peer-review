

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to conditional distributions. The authors introduce a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to train a conditional generative model that draws samples from a simple distribution and passes them through a deep network with conditional inputs. The paper claims CGMMN is competitive in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

**Quality:**
The theoretical foundation of CGMMN appears sound, with proper mathematical formulation and theorems supporting the CMMD criterion. The extension from GMMN to conditional distributions is theoretically plausible. However, the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims of competitiveness with state-of-the-art methods. The predictive modeling experiments on MNIST and SVHN lack comprehensive comparisons with recent strong baselines (e.g., recent conditional GAN variants or advanced VAE architectures) and don't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of the CMMD criterion. The generative results on MNIST (Fig. 2) show reasonable samples but lack quantitative metrics (FID, Inception Score) expected for generative model papers. The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment is limited to a single regression task with minimal analysis of the distillation quality. The paper also doesn't address computational complexity or scalability of the CMMD objective, which could be significant given the cubic complexity of kernel gram matrices.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good mathematical formulations. However, key implementation details are missing for reproducibility. The description of how conditional variables are handled (Section 3.2) is vague, and the experimental setup lacks sufficient hyperparameter details (e.g., kernel choices, regularization parameters, exact network architectures for baselines). The appendix references but doesn't adequately explain the practical implementation of the conditional embedding operator (Section 2.3), which is crucial for understanding the method.

**Originality:**
The core idea of extending GMMN to conditional distributions is incremental rather than novel. Prior work has established conditional generative models, including conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015). The paper fails to clearly articulate how CGMMN differs meaningfully from these approaches. While the CMMD criterion is presented as novel, it's a straightforward extension of MMD to conditional settings, and the paper doesn't demonstrate significant advantages over existing conditional generative approaches. The comparison with related work is superficial, and the paper doesn't adequately distinguish itself from conditional VAEs, which have been extensively studied.

**Significance:**
The paper addresses a relevant problem (conditional generation), but the proposed solution doesn't offer clear advantages over existing methods. The claimed "competitive performance" is not convincingly demonstrated. The results on MNIST and SVHN are comparable to state-of-the-art, but without showing significant improvements or unique advantages, the contribution appears incremental. The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment is interesting but too limited to demonstrate broad significance. The paper doesn't provide evidence that CGMMN solves a problem that existing conditional generative models cannot address more efficiently.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical development of CMMD is sound and well-motivated.
- The paper presents a coherent extension of GMMN to conditional distributions.
- The experimental results on predictive modeling are reasonable and show the approach works.
- The architecture is simple and amenable to backpropagation, as claimed.

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient experimental validation with limited baselines and metrics.
- Failure to distinguish CGMMN meaningfully from existing conditional generative models.
- Lack of computational complexity analysis and scalability discussion.
- Inadequate comparison with recent conditional generative approaches (e.g., conditional GANs with recent advances).
- Limited scope of experiments (only MNIST, SVHN, and one regression task).
- No ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of the CMMD criterion.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a theoretically sound extension of GMMN to conditional distributions, the lack of comprehensive experimental validation and failure to demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods significantly weakens its contribution. The authors present an incremental approach without sufficient evidence that it offers meaningful improvements over established techniques. For NIPS, which prioritizes significant advances, this paper falls short of the expected standard. The results are promising but not sufficiently validated to claim competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

I recommend rejection, as the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and significance. The authors would need to substantially expand their experimental validation with more comprehensive comparisons to recent conditional generative models, provide additional quantitative metrics for generative performance, and more clearly articulate the advantages of their approach over existing methods before it could be considered for acceptance.
