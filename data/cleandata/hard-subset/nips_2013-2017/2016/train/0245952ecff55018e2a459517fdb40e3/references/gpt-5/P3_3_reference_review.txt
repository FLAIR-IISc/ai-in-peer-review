Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of GMMN that learns conditional distributions p(y|x) by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The key idea is to measure the distance between conditional distributions via the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X estimated from data and from the generator. The generator takes both x and a noise vector h as inputs, passes them through a DNN, and outputs y. Training is performed by backpropagation through the CMMD loss, with practical mini-batch approximations.

This builds directly on kernel mean embeddings for conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012) and on GMMN (Li et al., UAI’15; Dziugaite et al., UAI’15), while offering a conditional alternative to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero) and CVAE (Sohn et al.). Empirically, the paper demonstrates classification on MNIST and SVHN, conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and a distillation example for Bayesian prediction (PBP).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension: Using conditional mean embeddings to define a CMMD loss for conditional generators is natural, elegant, and avoids min–max optimization.
- Practical training: The objective has a closed-form trace expression in terms of Gram matrices; backprop only needs gradients wrt generated outputs. The observation that kernels on x need not be differentiable (since their inverses are parameter-independent) is useful.
- Stability: Unlike GANs, the optimization is single-objective and tends to be stable in practice.
- Breadth of applications: The paper covers prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation in a single framework.
- Results: Competitive classification results without data augmentation; reasonable conditional samples; distillation preserves RMSE on Boston housing.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality/technical soundness
- Theory stops at sufficiency: Theorem 3 states that CY|X equality implies equality of conditional distributions under strong assumptions (universality, smoothness). However, there is no analysis of (i) consistency of the empirical CMMD estimator under regularization, (ii) whether minimizing empirical CMMD yields a consistent estimator within the parametric generator class, or (iii) statistical/sample complexity.
- Bias and regularization: The estimator ĈY|X = Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ is biased due to ridge regularization and inversion on finite samples. How sensitive is CMMD to λ? Is there guidance beyond cross-validation?
- Kernel choice and bandwidth: Performance hinges on kernels for both x and y (and bandwidths/mixtures). These choices are under-specified. In high-dimensional x (images), RBF kernels often suffer concentration; what kernels were used, with what bandwidth selection? Any MK-MMD as in GMMN?
- Computational cost: Each mini-batch requires O(b^3) inversions and O(b^2) Gram computations. With b=300–500 this is nontrivial; training time and memory are not reported. Are there approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features) or preconditioning to reduce cost/instability?
- Gradient variance and stability: The paper asserts easy backprop but does not analyze gradient variance of CMMD vs MMD nor provide training curves or failure modes.
- Predictive modeling objective: For classification, the empirical “conditional” is essentially a one-hot label per x; CMMD reduces to pushing y toward a one-hot target weighted by a kernel on x. This resembles kernel regression/classification with learned deep features; a discussion contrasting CMMD training to cross-entropy would help. Why/when does CMMD confer an advantage?
- Distillation evaluation: Only RMSE on a single dataset is shown. A key motivation is to approximate predictive distributions; calibration (NLL, CRPS, PIT) and uncertainty quality are not evaluated, nor is speed-up at test time quantified.

Clarity
- Some notational overload between F and G and feature maps; a diagram of tensor shapes would aid reproducibility.
- Kernel and hyperparameter details are missing or relegated to the appendix (e.g., kernel types for X and Y, bandwidth selection, λ, noise dimension, mixture-of-kernels).
- Minor typos and reference formatting (e.g., MINIST, augumentation, missing years) detract from polish.

Originality
- The conditional mean embedding operator is well known; the main novelty is using the HS distance between empirical operators as a training loss for conditional generators. This is a fairly direct and reasonable extension of GMMN, but still incremental. To the best of my knowledge, training deep conditional generators with CMMD is new and merits publication if empirically convincing.

Significance
- Potentially impactful as a simpler, more stable alternative to cGAN/CVAE when good kernels on x and y are available. However, the current empirical evaluation is modest: classification is competitive but not SOTA; conditional generation is shown qualitatively without quantitative metrics or comparisons to cGAN/CVAE; distillation is demonstrated on one small regression dataset.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations on kernel choices and bandwidth selection; compare single RBF vs MK-MMD; analyze sensitivity to λ and batch size.
- Report training/test-time complexity and wall-clock comparisons to cGAN/CVAE and to cross-entropy-trained classifiers.
- For generation: add quantitative metrics (e.g., FID/Inception on CIFAR-10 or Parzen MMD to held-out codes), and direct comparisons to cGAN/CVAE on the same architectures.
- For distillation: evaluate predictive uncertainty (NLL, calibration), and provide speed-up over teacher.
- Discuss the connection to kernel ridge regression/conditional mean embedding regression, clarifying what CMMD optimizes relative to standard discriminative losses.
- Consider scalable approximations (Nyström, random Fourier features) for the Gram inverses and report their effect.

Pros
- Clean, principled objective; avoids adversarial training.
- General and easy to implement; supports CNN/MLP backbones.
- Broad applicability demonstrated; competitive classification results; promising distillation.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to known conditional embeddings and GMMN.
- Missing kernel/hyperparameter details and ablations; computational aspects underreported.
- Generative evaluation is qualitative and lacks comparisons; distillation evaluation limited.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is sound and practically useful, but the empirical section needs stronger and more quantitative evidence, and the paper would benefit from clearer reporting of kernel choices and computational costs. My confidence is medium-high.
