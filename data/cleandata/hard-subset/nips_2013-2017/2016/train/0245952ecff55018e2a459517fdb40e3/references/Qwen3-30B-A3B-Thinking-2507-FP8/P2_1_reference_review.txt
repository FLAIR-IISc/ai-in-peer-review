

# NIPS Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions. The authors develop a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to measure the difference between conditional distributions, enabling the training of conditional generative models through backpropagation. The approach builds on kernel embedding techniques for conditional distributions and provides a simple generative process that takes both conditional variables and random noise as inputs. The paper evaluates CGMMN across three application areas: predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN datasets), contextual generation (MNIST and Yale Face datasets), and distilling Bayesian models (Boston housing dataset). The authors demonstrate that CGMMN achieves competitive performance across these diverse tasks.

The work extends the GMMN framework introduced by Li et al. (2015) by adding conditional capabilities, which fills an important gap since previous GMMN work only handled unconditional distributions. It also relates to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) but uses a different learning criterion (MMD vs. min-max optimization) that avoids the training difficulties of GANs.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for CMMD, extending the kernel embedding of conditional distributions to a practical learning criterion. The mathematical development is rigorous, with careful attention to the conditions required for the CMMD objective to be well-defined (Theorem 3).

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors successfully implement a simple and efficient training algorithm that leverages backpropagation. The approach is straightforward to implement and avoids the complex training dynamics of GANs.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper evaluates CGMMN across three distinct application domains (predictive modeling, generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation), demonstrating the versatility of the framework. The experimental results are well-documented with appropriate baselines.

4. **Clear Writing**: The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the technical concepts. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the figures (e.g., Fig. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5) effectively illustrate the key ideas and results.

5. **Addressing a Real Need**: The paper correctly identifies that conditional distributions are crucial for many applications (predictive modeling, contextual generation, building large networks) but have been underexplored in the moment-matching framework.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Contributions**: While the paper successfully extends GMMN to conditional distributions, it doesn't make novel theoretical contributions to kernel embedding or moment matching. The CMMD criterion builds directly on existing work (Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012), and the paper doesn't provide new theoretical insights about the convergence properties or sample complexity of CGMMN.

2. **Incremental Improvement Over Baselines**: The results on predictive modeling (Tables 1 and 2) show CGMMN-CNN is competitive with state-of-the-art methods but doesn't significantly outperform them. For instance, on SVHN, CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error rate compared to DSN's 1.92%, which is a meaningful gap. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how much better CGMMN is compared to alternatives.

3. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper acknowledges that the approach requires the conditional variables to be finite for direct estimation (Sec. 2.3) but doesn't thoroughly discuss how to handle continuous conditional variables, which is a major limitation for many real-world applications. The computational complexity of kernel methods (O(N^3)) isn't sufficiently addressed beyond mentioning mini-batch training.

4. **Superficial Comparison to Related Work**: While the paper cites conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), it doesn't adequately differentiate CGMMN from this work. The paper states "CGMMN largely extends the capability of GMMN" but doesn't explain why the moment-matching approach is preferable to conditional GANs for the tasks they address.

5. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of specific components (e.g., the conditional embedding operator vs. the unconditional approach, the effect of different kernel functions, or the dimensionality of the noise variable).

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The theoretical development is solid, and the experimental evaluation is thorough across multiple tasks. However, the claims are not strongly supported by theoretical analysis beyond what's needed for the CMMD criterion. The paper is a complete piece of work rather than a position paper. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for finite conditional variables, computational complexity), but they could have been more critical of the gaps between CGMMN and state-of-the-art methods.

**Clarity**: The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and clearly explains the technical concepts. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the figures effectively illustrate the approach. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results (with some minor exceptions like the exact implementation of the conditional embedding operator in Appendix A.2.2).

**Originality**: The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions is a reasonable and useful contribution, but it's not highly novel. The CMMD criterion builds directly on existing work in kernel embedding of conditional distributions, and the conditional generative architecture is similar to other conditional generative models. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from related work like conditional GANs or conditional VAEs. The originality is moderate - it fills a gap in the literature but doesn't introduce a fundamentally new approach.

**Significance**: The paper addresses an important practical need for conditional generative modeling across multiple applications. However, the significance is limited because the results don't demonstrate a dramatic improvement over existing methods. The approach is simple and avoids the training difficulties of GANs, which is valuable, but the paper doesn't show that CGMMN is significantly better than other conditional generative models for most tasks. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is interesting but not sufficiently developed to show major advantages.

## Recommendation

The paper presents a reasonable extension of the GMMN framework to conditional distributions with practical implementation and evaluation across multiple tasks. While the work is technically sound and well-executed, it doesn't make a sufficiently novel contribution to the field to be considered highly significant. The results are competitive but not groundbreaking, and the theoretical contributions are modest.

I recommend **weak acceptance** (accept with minor revisions), as the paper fills an important gap in the literature and provides a useful alternative to conditional GANs. However, the authors should address the limitations mentioned above, particularly the discussion of continuous conditional variables and the comparison to conditional GANs.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a more thorough discussion of how to handle continuous conditional variables (beyond the finite case mentioned in Sec. 2.3).

2. Conduct a more detailed comparison with conditional GANs, explaining why CGMMN might be preferable in certain scenarios.

3. Include ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of key components of the CGMMN framework.

4. Discuss the computational complexity of the approach more thoroughly and provide guidance on scaling to larger datasets.

5. Clarify the advantages of CGMMN over other conditional generative models in the introduction and related work sections.

## Final Assessment

This is a solid, well-executed paper that extends an existing framework to a more general case. While the contributions are not revolutionary, they are practical and address a meaningful need in generative modeling. The paper would be stronger with more rigorous theoretical analysis, a more critical evaluation of the approach's limitations, and a clearer articulation of how CGMMN differs from and improves upon existing methods. With these improvements, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
