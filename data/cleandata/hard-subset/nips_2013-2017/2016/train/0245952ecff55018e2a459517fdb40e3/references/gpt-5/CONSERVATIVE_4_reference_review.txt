Summary
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to conditional distributions. The model defines a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w) taking as input the conditioning variable x and auxiliary noise h, and trains it by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) between the empirical conditional distribution and the model’s conditional distribution. CMMD is formulated as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators estimated from samples via kernel tricks. The authors provide an SGD training procedure using minibatches and apply CGMMN to: (i) predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), (ii) conditional generation (MNIST digits given labels, Yale Faces given identity), and (iii) distilling Bayesian predictors (PBP on Boston Housing).

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The extension from MMD to conditional MMD via conditional mean embeddings is natural and clearly described. The operator-based CMMD formulation is technically sound under standard RKHS assumptions.
- Simplicity and implementability: Training reduces to backpropagation through a feed-forward generator with fixed Gram-matrix weights from the conditioning variables. The inverses do not depend on model parameters, simplifying gradients.
- Breadth of applications: The paper shows the same objective can be used for classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Qualitative samples: Conditional generation on MNIST and Yale appears plausible, and nearest-neighbor checks suggest non-trivial diversity. The AE-code variant is a sensible practical enhancement.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Theoretical guarantees are limited. Theorem 3 (equality of conditional embeddings implies equality of conditionals) is known in the kernel embedding literature and is only a sufficient condition. There is no new convergence analysis for the empirical CMMD objective in the finite-sample, minibatch setting, no consistency or generalization guarantees, and no discussion of the bias induced by minibatch inverses and regularization.
- Experimental validation is not sufficiently rigorous. For classification, the procedure outputs a real-valued vector y and predicts argmax, but there is no assessment of calibration or probabilistic quality (e.g., NLL, Brier score). It is unclear whether outputs are normalized probabilities. Comparisons on MNIST/SVHN are limited and do not include strong conditional generative baselines (e.g., Conditional VAE, Conditional GAN), despite citing them. The MNIST results are described but the actual table/values are missing here, and on SVHN the method is “comparable” to CMMVA but below strong discriminative baselines.
- Generative evaluation is qualitative only. There are no quantitative scores (e.g., Parzen estimates as in prior GMMN work; or classifier-based scores), no human studies, and no comparisons to cGAN/CVAE. Nearest-neighbor in pixel space is a weak test.
- Distillation experiment is minimal: one small regression dataset, evaluated only by RMSE; no negative log-likelihood or calibration, no runtime/latency comparison, and no uncertainty assessment—despite the stated goal of distilling predictive distributions.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity: No study of kernel choices for x and y, bandwidth/λ selection, minibatch size effects, or noise dimensionality h. The method’s performance likely hinges on these choices.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but some notational choices are confusing (e.g., switching between superscripts s and d for “data” vs “generated,” and the roles of Ds vs Dd). The paper would benefit from a clearer, consistent naming scheme.
- Important implementation details are missing: exact kernels for x and y, bandwidth selection procedure, regularization λ, perturbation strategy for “near” samples in distillation, and computational cost per iteration. Without these, reproduction is difficult.

Originality
- The main idea—training a conditional generator by matching conditional mean embeddings—is a relatively direct extension of prior GMMN (Li et al., 2015/ICML) using established conditional embedding operators (Song et al., 2009/2010; Fukumizu et al.). The theoretical statement (Theorem 3) is essentially known in the embedding literature. The novelty lies primarily in integrating CMMD with deep generators and applying it across tasks. Given existing conditional generative approaches (cGAN, CVAE) and prior uses of conditional MMD/embedding in other contexts, the contribution feels incremental.

Significance
- Practical impact is unclear. Empirically, results are not SOTA on classification; generative results are only qualitative; and distillation evaluation is too limited to draw strong conclusions. The computational burden of kernel matrices (even with minibatches) and hyperparameter sensitivity may hinder adoption. Without stronger comparisons to cGAN/CVAE and more thorough metrics, it is hard to conclude that CMMD offers a compelling advantage over established conditional generative training objectives.

Questions for the authors
1. What kernels were used for x and y in each experiment, and how were bandwidths and λ chosen? How sensitive are results to these choices?
2. For classification, are outputs normalized to a simplex (softmax)? If not, why is CMMD preferable to standard cross-entropy? Please report NLL and calibration.
3. Can you provide quantitative generative evaluations and comparisons to cGAN/CVAE on MNIST/Yale (e.g., Parzen log-likelihood used in GMMN, or proxy classifier accuracy)?
4. What is the runtime and memory cost per minibatch (e.g., batch size 500) due to Gram matrix inverses? How does performance scale?
5. In distillation, can you report NLL/calibration and speedups vs the Bayesian teacher, and include larger datasets?

Pros
- Clear formulation connecting conditional embeddings and deep generators
- Simple training and inference; no adversarial min-max
- Broad applicability; decent classification performance without heavy tricks
- Qualitative conditional samples look reasonable

Cons
- Incremental contribution relative to GMMN and conditional embeddings literature
- Limited empirical rigor; weak or missing comparisons to key baselines (cGAN, CVAE)
- No quantitative generative metrics; minimal distillation evidence
- Missing theoretical and practical analyses (consistency, sensitivity, scalability)

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is reasonable and execution is competent, but the contribution over prior work is modest and the empirical validation falls short of NIPS standards for new training objectives. Strengthening the paper with rigorous quantitative evaluations against established conditional generative baselines, clearer implementation details, ablations, and a more thorough theory/analysis section could change this assessment.

Confidence: High.
