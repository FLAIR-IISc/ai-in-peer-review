## Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective for training, enabling the generation of samples conditioned on input variables. The work demonstrates the application of CGMMN to predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, showcasing competitive performance on various datasets. The core idea builds upon the MMD framework, offering a potentially simpler and more stable alternative to GANs for conditional generative modeling.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of deep generative models. It acknowledges the advancements in GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and GMMNs (Li et al., 2016), framing CGMMN as a logical extension to address conditional generation, a crucial aspect for many practical applications. The comparison to other conditional generative models like Conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and Conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015) is implicitly made through the experimental results. The connection to kernel methods and MMD (Gretton et al., 2007) is clearly established, emphasizing the theoretical foundation. The paper also correctly acknowledges related work on Bayesian model distillation (Korattikara et al., 2015).

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper builds on a solid theoretical foundation rooted in kernel methods and MMD, providing a well-defined objective function (CMMD) for conditional generative modeling. The theorems regarding the Hilbert space embedding and conditional distributions are clearly stated and contribute to the understanding of the method.
* **Simplicity and Stability:** Compared to GANs, the MMD-based approach (and therefore CGMMN) potentially offers a more stable training process due to the absence of a min-max game. This is a significant advantage.
* **Generality:** CGMMN is a flexible framework applicable to a wide range of tasks, demonstrated through experiments on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors present comprehensive experimental results on several datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face) demonstrating the competitive performance of CGMMN against various baseline methods. The results are well-presented and analyzed.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and the technical details are explained adequately, allowing for reproduction of the results (though implementation details could be more extensive - see weaknesses).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** While the extension of GMMN to the conditional setting is valuable, the core idea of applying MMD to conditional distributions isn’t entirely novel. Previous work (e.g., Song et al., 2013) has explored kernel embedding of conditional distributions. The contribution lies more in the specific implementation and application within the GMMN framework.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to hyperparameters (e.g., regularization parameter λ in CMMD).  The paper doesn’t provide sufficient guidance on how to choose these parameters effectively.  A more thorough ablation study varying these parameters would be beneficial.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks detailed implementation specifics. While the authors mention using AdaM, the learning rate, batch size, and network architectures are briefly discussed but could benefit from more precision.  Code release would significantly increase the reproducibility of the results.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons are made to several baselines, they don’t always include the *most* state-of-the-art methods, particularly in the generative modeling space.  Comparing to more recent and powerful GAN variants (e.g., StyleGAN) would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** Although the authors address the computational cost of inverse matrix calculations in CMMD, a discussion of the overall computational complexity compared to GANs or VAEs, especially for large datasets, is missing.
* **Limited Analysis of Generated Samples:** While visual examples of generated samples are provided, a more quantitative evaluation of the generated samples (e.g., using Inception Score or FID) would strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting.
* The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance on a variety of tasks.
* The use of MMD provides a potentially more stable training alternative to GANs.
* The paper addresses an important problem in deep generative modeling - conditional generation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the core idea is somewhat limited.
* The lack of detailed implementation details and code release hinders reproducibility.
* The comparison to the absolute state-of-the-art is incomplete.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is **above average** and represents a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative modeling. While the core idea isn't groundbreaking, the extension to conditional distributions, coupled with the theoretical grounding and empirical validation, makes it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS program.  However, addressing the weaknesses regarding implementation details, hyperparameter sensitivity, and comparison to stronger baselines would significantly strengthen the paper.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, with a strong encouragement to release the code.

**Confidence:** 8/10
