Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMNs to model conditional distributions p(y|x). The key idea is to replace the unconditional MMD objective with a Conditional MMD (CMMD): the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X and CZ|X in RKHS. The generator takes both x and a noise vector h as input and outputs y = f(x, h; w). Training draws (x, y) pairs from the data and from the generator, computes an empirical CMMD based on kernel Gram matrices for x and y (with Tikhonov regularization), and updates w via backpropagation. The authors emphasize that, unlike GANs, the objective is a simple minimum (no min–max), and the kernel on x need not be differentiable w.r.t. model parameters. They provide a sufficient condition (Theorem 3) for equality of conditional distributions when conditional embedding operators match.

Empirically, they evaluate: (1) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN (images → labels) using CNN/MLP backbones; (2) conditional generation on MNIST (labels → images) and Extended Yale Faces; and (3) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions on Boston Housing (from PBP) into a CGMMN. Classification results are competitive with prior deep generative predictors (MMVA/CMMVA), though behind strong discriminative baselines (e.g., DSN). Generated samples on MNIST/Yale look reasonable; nearest-neighbor checks suggest they are not simple copies; AE+CGMMN improves visual quality. The distillation result shows similar RMSE to the teacher.

The work builds directly on GMMN (Dziugaite et al., 2015; Li et al., 2015) and conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., 2009; Fukumizu et al., 2013). Compared to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), CGMMN uses a kernel-based objective that avoids adversarial training or variational bounds.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual extension of GMMN to the conditional setting via well-established conditional mean embedding operators; the CMMD objective is natural and easy to optimize by backprop.
- Simplicity and stability: no adversarial min–max; kernel on x can be non-differentiable; minibatch training is straightforward.
- Versatility: applied to prediction, class-conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation with a single framework.
- Empirical results show competitive classification performance among generative methods and reasonably diverse conditional samples.
- Theoretical piece (Theorem 3) clarifies a sufficient condition for equality of conditional distributions; operator-view yields a principled metric.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical depth for generation: only MNIST and Yale Faces; no quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen estimates used historically for GMMN, or any classifier-based metrics), and no comparisons to conditional GANs or conditional VAEs despite citing them.
- Scalability and sensitivity not adequately addressed: CMMD requires inverses of Gram matrices per minibatch (O(b^3)); there is little analysis of kernel choices (bandwidths, kernels for x vs. y), regularization λ, noise dimension, or minibatch size. No ablations.
- Theoretical guarantees are thin: while CMMD is a principled surrogate, there are no generalization bounds, consistency results for the empirical operator estimator in this deep setting, or analysis of the bias introduced by minibatch CMMD vs. full CMMD.
- Predictive modeling setup is unconventional: training a generator to emit y given x and then using argmax for classification. There is no calibration or likelihood-based evaluation of the predicted distributions; it’s unclear how the CMMD objective aligns with classification error, and how one-hot vs. soft labels are handled.
- Bayesian distillation is minimal (one small regression dataset), with no analysis of calibration, uncertainty, or runtime/throughput improvements vs. the teacher.
- Some clarity issues: notation occasionally confusing (s/d indices, Ksd/Lds definitions), missing details on kernel hyperparameters/selection, and computational cost/runtime reporting.

Quality
- The method is technically sound as an operator-matching approach using conditional embeddings, and the empirical CMMD is correctly derived from kernel Gram matrices with regularization. However, the paper lacks deeper theoretical or empirical analysis of approximation error, variance, and scalability that would firmly establish reliability in realistic settings.

Clarity
- Generally readable and well-structured. The training algorithm is clear. More precise descriptions of kernel choices, hyperparameter selection, and computational complexity would improve reproducibility. Minor typos and notational inconsistencies should be cleaned up.

Originality
- The idea of using conditional mean embeddings is known, and GMMN is known; the contribution is the combination—using the HS-norm of conditional operators as a training objective for conditional deep generators. This is a sensible and somewhat novel adaptation, though incremental relative to the cited building blocks.

Significance
- Potentially useful as a stable, simple alternative to conditional GAN/conditional VAE in some regimes, but current experiments do not convincingly establish superiority or unique advantages on challenging benchmarks. The broader impact is limited by sparse comparisons and analyses.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong generative baselines (conditional GANs, CVAEs) and quantitative metrics for conditional generation; consider including more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10 conditioned on class).
- Provide ablations on kernel types, bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic vs. tuning), λ, minibatch size, and noise dimensionality. Report training time and memory vs. GAN/CVAE.
- Discuss and, if possible, mitigate O(b^3) scaling: approximate inverses, low-rank approximations, random Fourier features, or Nyström methods.
- Clarify how y is represented in classification (one-hot vs. smoothed labels), and consider adding a classification-aware term or evaluating calibration (ECE/Brier).
- Expand Bayesian distillation to multiple datasets, report predictive uncertainty calibration and runtime gains.
- Improve notation and detail for Ksd/Lds, and describe kernel choices explicitly for each task.

Pros
- Simple, stable objective; principled conditional operator view.
- Versatile framework across tasks; competitive generative classification results.
- Clear algorithm; avoids adversarial training pitfalls.

Cons
- Limited empirical evaluation and missing strong baselines for conditional generation.
- No quantitative generative metrics; minimal theory beyond the sufficient condition.
- Scalability and sensitivity to kernel choices not analyzed.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper presents a clean and potentially useful extension of GMMNs to conditional modeling, but the empirical evaluation and analysis are not yet strong enough for NeurIPS. Addressing the above suggestions would substantially strengthen the case.
