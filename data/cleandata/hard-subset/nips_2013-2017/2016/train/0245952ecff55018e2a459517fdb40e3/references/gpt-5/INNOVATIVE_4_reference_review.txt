Summary
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative model trained by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective derived from kernel conditional mean embeddings. The model maps a pair (x, h) composed of conditioning variables x and auxiliary noise h through a deep net to generate y ~ pθ(y | x). Training minimizes the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between empirical conditional mean embedding operators of data and model, which yields a closed-form objective in terms of Gram matrices and (K + λI)−1. The approach yields a simple backprop-trainable objective (no min–max game), supports arbitrary network architectures (MLP/CNN), and is demonstrated on: (i) predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), (ii) conditional generation (MNIST digits, Yale Faces), and (iii) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge”) with results competitive with max-margin DGM baselines.

Relation to prior work
CGMMN extends GMMN (Li et al., ICML’15) from unconditional to conditional modeling by leveraging the conditional mean embedding operator (Song et al., ICML’09; Grunewalder et al., ICML’12). Compared to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., NIPS’15), CGMMN replaces adversarial/variational objectives with a kernel-based CMMD objective. The derivation and practical estimator of CMMD for training deep conditional generators appears novel and bridges kernel embeddings and modern DGMs.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean, principled way to train conditional generators via CMMD, avoiding adversarial min–max and variational bounds. This is a nontrivial and elegant extension of MMD-based learning to the conditional setting.
- Theoretical grounding: The paper builds on conditional mean embeddings and provides a sufficient condition (operator equality implies equality of conditionals) to justify the objective. The trace-form objective and its relation to MMD are clearly articulated.
- Simplicity and stability: Training reduces to backprop through a single loss; the inverse Gram matrices are independent of network parameters, simplifying differentiation and allowing non-differentiable kernels on x.
- Versatility: The same training recipe is used for classification, conditional image generation, and Bayesian predictive distillation.
- Empirics: Competitive classification vs strong DGM baselines (CMMVA) on MNIST/SVHN; plausible class-conditional samples on MNIST and Yale Faces; and successful Bayesian distillation without performance degradation on Boston Housing.

Weaknesses and questions
- Kernel choice and hyperparameters: Results can be sensitive to kernel on y, bandwidth selection, and λ. The paper should specify how kernels were chosen/tuned (median heuristic? cross-validation?) and report sensitivity/ablation. This is crucial for reproducibility and fairness.
- Computational scalability: CMMD requires (per minibatch) inverses of K + λI and multiple Gram matrices; complexity is O(b^3) in batch size. The paper uses minibatching, but lacks wall-clock comparisons, memory profile, and guidance on feasible batch sizes for large-scale settings.
- Limited quantitative evaluation of generation: The generative results are mostly qualitative. Comparisons against conditional GAN/CVAE with standard metrics (e.g., classification accuracy of generated digits, FID/IS where appropriate, or downstream performance from generated data) would strengthen claims.
- Predictive modeling protocol: Using a stochastic conditional generator to produce y and taking argmax is unconventional compared to cross-entropy training; the gains over discriminative methods are limited (lagging DSN, Maxout, NiN). An ablation contrasting CMMD training vs standard discriminative losses (and hybrids) would clarify when CMMD is preferred.
- Distillation evaluation is too small: Only Boston Housing is reported; there are no latency/model-size measurements or broader regression tasks. One of the key promises of distillation is inference speed—please include timing and size comparisons vs the Bayesian teacher and vs other student baselines.
- Theory scope: The sufficiency statement (operator equality ⇒ equality of conditionals) relies on universality and smoothness assumptions; finite-sample bias of the regularized estimator is not discussed. A brief statement on consistency rates or known guarantees for conditional embedding regression would be useful.

Clarity
Overall clearly written, well organized, and places the work in context. Some edits would help:
- Provide explicit kernel choices, bandwidth selection, λ, and batch sizes per experiment in the main text.
- Include complexity discussion of Eq. (2) and how mini-batching approximates the full objective (bias/variance trade-off).
- Minor typos/grammar throughout (e.g., “MINIST”, “adverisarial”, “the convoluational layers”).

Significance
I view this as a meaningful and timely contribution. It proposes a simple, stable alternative to adversarial/variational training for conditional generative modeling, backed by solid kernel embedding theory, and demonstrates applicability across tasks. Even if classification accuracy is not SOTA against purely discriminative models, the broader idea—training conditional generators with CMMD—opens a novel direction that others can build on (e.g., structured prediction, controllable generation, likelihood-free inference). Given the community interest in robust training objectives for generative models, this seems impactful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare against conditional GAN and conditional VAE baselines on generation.
- Report ablations on kernels and λ, and sensitivity to noise dimensionality and conditioning corruption.
- Provide computational benchmarks (time per epoch vs GAN/CVAE; memory).
- Evaluate distillation on more datasets and report inference speed and model size.
- Consider hybrid losses (CMMD + cross-entropy) for classification.

Overall recommendation
Score: 7 (weak accept)
Confidence: 0.7

Rationale: The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated objective (CMMD) for training conditional generators, with a clean derivation and broad applicability. While some experiments are limited and certain baselines are missing, the conceptual advance is clear and the method is pragmatic and stable. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing kernel/complexity details and strengthening empirical comparisons.
