Summary
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN from modeling joint distributions to modeling conditional distributions p(y|x). The key technical idea is Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), defined as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X from data and from the generator. The resulting objective has a closed form in terms of kernel Gram matrices and can be optimized by backpropagation through a neural network generator that takes both x and noise h as input and outputs y. The paper connects to kernel mean embeddings and conditional embedding operators (Song et al. 2009), and to deep generative modeling without adversarial min–max (GMMN), offering a simple, stable alternative to conditional GANs. Experiments cover: (a) predictive modeling (y are class scores given x) on MNIST and SVHN with MLP/CNN backbones; (b) label-conditioned generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, with and without an AE code space; and (c) distilling Bayesian predictors (PBP) on Boston Housing.

Relation to prior work: Compared to GAN/conditional GAN, CGMMN uses CMMD rather than an adversarial discriminator, avoiding min–max optimization. Compared to GMMN/GMMN+AE, CGMMN introduces a conditional discrepancy and conditional generator. Compared to conditional VAE, CGMMN is likelihood-free and kernel-based. The formulation leverages the established conditional mean embeddings literature but, to my knowledge, is the first to turn the operator norm between conditional embeddings into a practical deep conditional generative training loss.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and simplicity: CMMD is a clean, principled extension of MMD to conditionals via conditional operators; plugging this into a deep generator yields a tractable, stable training scheme without adversarial games.
- Practical appeal: The inverse Gram matrices for x are independent of network parameters, enabling non-differentiable kernels on x and straightforward minibatch training. The method is flexible across architectures (MLP/CNN) and tasks.
- Breadth of applications: Demonstrations across classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge indicate generality. The “student” CMMD approach for distilling a Bayesian predictor is particularly interesting.
- Empirical competitiveness: Classification performance is comparable to state-of-the-art deep generative models for prediction (MMVA/CMMVA) and not far from strong discriminative CNNs; label-conditioned samples look reasonable; AE+CGMMN improves sample quality as in GMMN+AE.

Weaknesses
- Theory: The main theoretical statement (operator equality implies equality of conditionals) is a sufficient condition and largely follows from known conditional embedding properties; finite-sample behavior, consistency of the CMMD estimator in the conditional setting, and guidance on λ are not analyzed.
- Missing baselines for conditional generation: No direct comparisons to conditional GANs or conditional VAEs on generation quality; evaluation is qualitative, with limited metrics.
- Scalability/complexity: The per-batch computation involves K̃−1 and traces over Gram matrices; while amortized over minibatches, the cubic scaling with batch size and kernel bandwidth selection are not discussed in depth. No exploration of random feature approximations or kernel choices’ impact.
- Experimental depth: The Bayesian distillation section is promising but minimal (only Boston Housing). Predictive experiments lack ablations (kernel types/bandwidth, λ, minibatch size) and training stability analyses.
- Clarity/details: Some implementation details (kernel choices for x and y in each task, bandwidth selection strategy, regularization λ, computational cost) are deferred or insufficiently specified, which could hinder reproducibility.

Quality
Technically sound overall: CMMD objective derivation is correct given the conditional embedding estimator; gradients are straightforward. Claims are supported by experiments, though comparisons on conditional generation are incomplete, and theory is mostly a re-packaging of known operator facts. The empirical scope is adequate but could be strengthened with more thorough baselines and analyses.

Clarity
Generally well-organized with a clear motivation and method. The operator/kernels notation is dense; more explicit dimension checks and a brief derivation of Eq. (2) would help. Provide concrete kernel choices, bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic), and λ values per experiment. Minor typos exist, but overall clarity is acceptable.

Originality
The central contribution—training deep conditional generators by minimizing an operator-norm CMMD—is, to my knowledge, new in deep learning. While built on established conditional embedding operators, turning this into a practical, minibatch-trainable loss and demonstrating broad applicability is a meaningful and novel step beyond GMMN.

Significance
Moderate to high. This is a simple, stable, and general alternative to conditional GANs and conditional VAEs, likely to interest practitioners who prefer non-adversarial training. The Bayesian distillation angle could spur follow-up work. Even if some empirical results are not SOTA, the conceptual advance is valuable.

Questions and suggestions
- Please report kernel choices and bandwidth selection strategies for x and y across tasks, and sensitivity to λ.
- Compare to conditional GAN and conditional VAE on at least one conditional generation benchmark with quantitative metrics (e.g., reconstruction errors in code space, nearest-neighbor distances, or human study; for classification, calibration metrics).
- Discuss computational cost per iteration and wall-clock vs GAN/CVAE; consider random Fourier features or Nyström to scale CMMD.
- Provide ablations on minibatch size and the effect of using non-differentiable kernels on x.
- For Bayesian distillation, add more datasets and compare predictive uncertainty calibration (e.g., NLL, calibration curves) vs the teacher.

Pros
- Novel, principled, and simple conditional training objective.
- Broad applicability, stable training, and competitive results.
- Bridges kernel conditional embeddings with deep generative modeling.

Cons
- Limited theory beyond sufficiency; limited scalability discussion.
- Missing strong conditional generation baselines and quantitative metrics.
- Experimental analysis could be deeper; distillation evaluation is thin.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). The paper introduces a clear and novel direction—CMMD-trained conditional generators—that is likely to influence subsequent work. Despite some empirical and theoretical gaps, the idea is bold, well-motivated, and practically useful.

Confidence
4/5.
