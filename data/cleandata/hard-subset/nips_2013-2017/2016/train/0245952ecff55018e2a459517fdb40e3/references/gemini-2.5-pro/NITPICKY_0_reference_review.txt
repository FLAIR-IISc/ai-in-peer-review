### **Summary**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) for learning conditional distributions. The core contribution is the development of a novel training objective, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective conditional kernel mean embedding operators. The proposed generative model produces a sample `y` conditioned on an input `x` by passing `x` along with a random noise variable `h` through a deep neural network. The authors demonstrate the utility of CGMMN across three distinct tasks: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (class-conditional image synthesis), and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models. The work positions itself as an alternative to Conditional GANs, offering a simpler and potentially more stable objective function.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper is built on a solid theoretical foundation, extending the well-established MMD framework to the conditional setting. The derivation of the empirical CMMD estimator in Equation (2) appears sound, providing a concrete objective for optimization. However, the quality of the work is diminished by a lack of technical precision and several omissions that hinder reproducibility and a full assessment of the method's limitations.

**Strengths:**
*   The proposed CMMD objective is a principled and logical extension of MMD for conditional distributions, relying on established theory of kernel embeddings of operators.
*   The generative architecture is simple and flexible, compatible with standard deep learning components like MLPs and CNNs.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Computational Scalability:** The CMMD objective involves the inversion of `N x N` kernel matrices, where `N` is the mini-batch size. This results in an O(N³) computational complexity, which is a significant practical limitation for training with large mini-batches. This issue is a well-known drawback of kernel methods and is not adequately addressed or analyzed in the paper.
*   **Technical Ambiguity:** The mini-batch training algorithm (Alg. 1) implies that the set of conditional variables `x` is identical for both the real and generated samples within a batch. If so, `Ks = Kd = Ksd`, which would significantly simplify the CMMD expression in Eq. (2). This is a critical implementation detail that is not explicitly stated or justified, leaving ambiguity about the exact formula being optimized.
*   **Missing Details:** For the classification experiments (Sec 4.1.1), the output `y` is a vector in R¹⁰⁺. The paper fails to specify the kernel function used on this output space, which is a crucial detail for calculating the `L` matrices in the CMMD objective and for reproducing the results.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is generally well-structured, but the clarity is hampered by imprecise language, typos, and awkward formatting.

**Strengths:**
*   The introduction clearly motivates the need for conditional generative models and situates the work relative to GMMNs and GANs.
*   The high-level concept is easy to grasp.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Imprecise Explanations:** Remark 1 attempts to provide an intuition for CMMD as a weighted version of MMD, but the explanation is vague. It states CMMD uses weighting matrices `C1, C2, C3` "based on the conditional variables" without defining them, making the comparison unenlightening.
*   **Inconsistent Method Description:** The paper introduces a general CMMD estimator in Section 3.1 but then mentions in Section 4.2.1 that for the finite-domain case (class labels as conditions), they "estimate CYX and C⁻¹XX directly," with details deferred to the appendix. This switch in methodology is not well-motivated in the main text and fragments the technical presentation.
*   **Formatting and Typos:** The manuscript contains multiple errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading. These include: "MINIST dataset" (Sec 4.1.1), "out network" (Sec 4.1.2), inconsistent numbering in the property list preceding Eq. (1) (the "1." is missing), and an unusual footnote on the first page specifying the conference venue, which is typically not part of a submission.

#### **Originality**

The main contribution—the CMMD objective for training conditional generative models—is novel. While building upon prior work on GMMNs [19] and conditional kernel embeddings [29], the synthesis of these ideas into a trainable deep learning framework is original. The model architecture itself is a straightforward adaptation of approaches seen in models like conditional GANs [21], but the novelty lies in the loss function.

#### **Significance**

The paper addresses the important problem of learning conditional distributions with deep models. By providing an alternative to the adversarial min-max objective, it could potentially offer a more stable training paradigm. The applications explored, particularly the novel idea of using this framework for "Bayesian dark knowledge," are interesting. However, the significance of the contribution is tempered by the experimental results and the unaddressed practical limitations. The performance, while competitive with some generative models, does not advance the state of the art on the presented tasks, and the scalability concerns may limit its adoption for large-scale problems.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and theoretically grounded loss function (CMMD) for training conditional deep generative models.
*   The training objective is simpler than the min-max game of GANs, potentially offering more stable optimization.
*   Demonstrates versatility across several interesting and diverse applications, including a novel use case in knowledge distillation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The paper suffers from a lack of technical clarity, with crucial implementation details (e.g., the exact form of the mini-batch objective, kernel choices) missing.
*   Serious computational scalability issues (O(N³) in batch size) are not adequately discussed or mitigated, questioning the method's practicality.
*   The empirical results are not strong enough to be considered a new state of the art; performance is modest, and image quality is poor without a supplementary autoencoder.
*   The manuscript is marred by numerous minor typos and formatting errors, reflecting a lack of polish and attention to detail.

### **Recommendation**

I recommend **rejection**. While the core idea of a conditional MMD objective is elegant and promising, the paper's execution is flawed. The accumulation of unclear technical descriptions, missing experimental details, unaddressed scalability concerns, and a general lack of polish prevents me from recommending acceptance. The work has potential, but the manuscript in its current form does not meet the high standards of NIPS. A revised version that clarifies the technical ambiguities, provides all necessary implementation details, includes a frank discussion of computational costs, and demonstrates more compelling empirical results would be much stronger.
