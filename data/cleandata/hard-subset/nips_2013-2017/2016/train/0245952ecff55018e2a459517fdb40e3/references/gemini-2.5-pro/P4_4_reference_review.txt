**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions p(y|x). The original GMMN [Li et al., ICML 2015] provided a simpler training alternative to Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between the data distribution and the model distribution. However, GMMN was limited to modeling joint distributions. This work addresses that limitation.

The core contribution is two-fold. First, it proposes a generative architecture where samples `y` are produced by a deep neural network that takes both the conditional variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input. This creates a parametric model for p(y|x). Second, and more critically, the paper derives a new training objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). Unlike MMD, which measures the distance between the mean embeddings of two marginal distributions in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), CMMD measures the distance between the *kernel mean embedding operators* of two conditional distributions. This distance is defined as the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between the operators, providing a principled way to test if two conditional distributions are identical.

The resulting CGMMN model is trained via backpropagation to minimize the CMMD between the empirical conditional distribution from the training data and the one defined by the generator network. The paper demonstrates the versatility of this approach through experiments on three distinct tasks: (1) predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), (2) contextual generation (generating MNIST digits and Yale faces conditioned on class labels), and (3) distilling knowledge from a complex Bayesian model into a faster CGMMN. This work positions itself as a natural and significant extension of the moment-matching line of research for deep generative models, analogous to how Conditional GANs [Mirza & Osindero, 2014] extended the original GAN framework.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the Conditional MMD objective is well-grounded in the existing literature on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions [Song et al., 2009]. Theorem 3 correctly establishes the connection between the equality of conditional embedding operators and the equality of the conditional distributions themselves, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed training objective. The final form of the empirical CMMD estimator (Eq. 2) is correctly derived using standard kernel methods. The experiments are conducted on standard datasets and provide support for the authors' claims. The authors are also forthright about the potential for smoothness assumptions not holding in practice, but justify their empirical estimator as a reasonable approximation.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the need for conditional models and situates the work within the broader context of deep generative models. The preliminary section provides a concise and necessary background on MMD and kernel embeddings. The proposed CGMMN model and the CMMD objective are explained logically. Remark 1, which draws an intuitive connection between MMD as a uniformly weighted objective and CMMD as a non-uniformly weighted objective, is particularly insightful for the reader. The experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward manner.

**Originality:**
The main contribution, the CMMD objective, is novel and represents a significant conceptual advance over the MMD objective used in the original GMMN. While the idea of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions is not new, its application as a training objective for a deep conditional generative model is original. This work successfully fills a logical gap in the literature by providing a conditional counterpart to GMMN, making the moment-matching approach competitive with GANs and VAEs in terms of modeling flexibility. The application to Bayesian dark knowledge is also a creative use case for the proposed framework.

**Significance:**
The work is significant as it substantially increases the applicability of moment-matching networks. Unconditional generative models have limited direct use, whereas conditional models are central to a vast array of machine learning tasks, including classification, regression, structured prediction, and data imputation. By providing a stable and theoretically-grounded framework for conditional generation that avoids the difficult min-max optimization of GANs, CGMMN offers a valuable tool for both researchers and practitioners. The diverse set of experiments effectively showcases this broad utility.

---
**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** A major practical limitation of the proposed CMMD objective is the need to compute and invert kernel matrices (`K_d + λI` and `K_s + λI`), an operation that scales cubically with the batch size. The paper proposes a mini-batch algorithm (Alg. 1), but provides little discussion on the practical implications. How does the mini-batch size affect the stability and quality of the CMMD gradient? The quality of the conditional operator estimate is highly dependent on having a sufficient number of samples. A small batch size may lead to a poor estimate and high-variance gradients, potentially hindering training. A more thorough analysis of the trade-offs between computational cost and estimation quality would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Experimental Comparison:** While the results are competitive with other generative models, the generative quality on MNIST (Fig. 2) appears noisy and is not visually on par with state-of-the-art conditional GANs from the same period. The paper improves results by using an autoencoder (Fig. 4), but this adds an extra, separate training stage and moves away from the end-to-end simplicity that is presented as an advantage. A direct quantitative and qualitative comparison against Conditional GANs on the generation tasks would have provided a clearer picture of the model's standing.

3.  **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks some details that would be crucial for reproducibility. The choice of kernel functions and their parameters (e.g., bandwidth for an RBF kernel) is critical to the performance of any MMD-based method. Similarly, the selection of the regularization parameter `λ` in the matrix inversion is important. The paper does not discuss how these hyperparameters were chosen or provide a sensitivity analysis, leaving the reader to guess at these important implementation details.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**Arguments for:**
*   Proposes a novel, theoretically sound, and well-motivated objective function (CMMD) for training conditional generative models.
*   Significantly extends the GMMN framework, broadening its applicability to a wide range of important problems.
*   Provides a valuable alternative to Conditional GANs that avoids unstable min-max training dynamics.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance across a diverse set of tasks, highlighting the framework's versatility.

**Arguments against:**
*   The computational cost associated with kernel matrix inversion raises scalability concerns that are not fully addressed.
*   The quality of generated samples is not state-of-the-art compared to contemporary GANs, and the best results require a separate autoencoder pre-training step.
*   The paper omits key implementation details regarding hyperparameter selection, which could hinder reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of deep generative models. The development of Conditional MMD is a significant theoretical and practical step forward for moment-matching networks. Despite some weaknesses related to computational scalability and the visual quality of generated samples, the strengths in originality, technical soundness, and significance are compelling. The paper opens up a promising research direction and provides a useful new tool for the community. I recommend acceptance.
