### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional probability distributions `p(y|x)`. It extends the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework, which learns joint distributions by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model and data distributions. The core contribution is a new objective function called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). CMMD is defined as the squared Hilbert-Schmidt norm between the kernel mean embedding operators of the true conditional distribution and the model's conditional distribution. The generative model is a deep neural network that takes the conditional variable `x` and an auxiliary random noise vector `h` as input to produce a sample `y`. The network is trained end-to-end by minimizing the CMMD objective using backpropagation. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on a wide range of tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, contextual image generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

### **Review**

This paper presents a novel and significant extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting, providing a valuable non-adversarial alternative for training deep conditional generative models. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and supported by a good range of experiments.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed CMMD objective is a principled and well-motivated criterion derived from the theory of kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. The mathematical formulation is sound, building logically upon prior work on MMD and conditional mean embeddings. The claims are well-supported by the experimental results. The experiments cover three distinct application domains (prediction, generation, and model distillation), effectively demonstrating the versatility of the proposed framework. In the prediction tasks, CGMMN achieves performance competitive with state-of-the-art deep generative models like CMMVA, validating its utility as a discriminative model.

However, there are a few points that could be strengthened:
1.  **Computational Cost:** The CMMD objective requires the inversion of kernel matrices for each mini-batch, which scales as `O(B^3)` where `B` is the batch size. This can be a significant computational bottleneck, especially for large batch sizes that are often beneficial for training deep networks. The paper does not address this limitation or discuss potential scalable approximations (e.g., using random Fourier features or Nyström approximations), which would be important for application to larger-scale problems.
2.  **Generative Quality:** While the generated samples successfully demonstrate conditional control, their visual quality (e.g., Figure 2) is modest compared to contemporary results from Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The images appear somewhat noisy and lack sharpness. The authors mitigate this by using a pre-trained autoencoder, which indeed improves the results (Figure 4), but this adds an extra layer of complexity and makes it a two-stage process, diverging from a purely end-to-end generative model.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The introduction provides excellent motivation for conditional generative modeling. The background section gives a concise yet sufficient overview of the required concepts from kernel methods. The core proposal—the CMMD objective and the CGMMN architecture—is presented logically and is easy to follow. The connection drawn in Remark 1 between MMD and CMMD, highlighting CMMD as a weighted version of MMD, is particularly insightful. The experimental setup is described clearly, and the results are presented in an organized manner. For reproducibility, it would have been helpful to include more specific details on the choice of kernels and the setting of their hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidths), as the performance of MMD-based methods is known to be sensitive to these choices.

**Originality**

The work is highly original. At the time of writing, extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting was a novel and non-trivial step. While conditional versions of other deep generative models like GANs and VAEs existed, this paper was the first to propose a principled moment-matching objective for this task. The formulation of CMMD based on the difference between conditional mean operators in a tensor product Hilbert space is an elegant and novel contribution. The paper appropriately cites and builds upon related work in kernel methods and deep generative models.

**Significance**

The significance of this work is substantial. Conditional models are fundamental to a vast array of machine learning tasks. This paper provides a stable, non-adversarial training paradigm for deep conditional generative models, offering a valuable alternative to GANs, which are often difficult to train due to their min-max optimization. The CMMD objective is a general-purpose tool that could be adopted in other contexts where matching conditional distributions is necessary. Furthermore, the creative application to "Bayesian dark knowledge" showcases the model's flexibility and opens up a new avenue for model compression and distillation. By significantly expanding the capabilities of GMMNs, this work provides a solid contribution that others in the field can build upon.

### **Final Assessment**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel, principled, and technically sound method (CMMD) for training conditional generative models.
*   Significantly extends the applicability of the GMMN framework, filling an important gap in non-adversarial generative modeling.
*   The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to understand.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance across a diverse and interesting set of applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational cost of the CMMD objective may limit scalability, a point not discussed in the paper.
*   The visual quality of the generated samples is not state-of-the-art compared to GAN-based approaches from the same period.

**Recommendation:** The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a foundational and elegant technique for an important problem. The contributions are original, significant, and of high quality. I strongly recommend **acceptance**.
