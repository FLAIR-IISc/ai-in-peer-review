

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors introduce Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as the training objective and demonstrate the approach on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge tasks.

## Strengths
The paper makes a reasonable extension of the GMMN framework to conditional settings, which is a natural progression given the importance of conditional modeling in many applications. The theoretical foundation of CMMD is well-motivated, building upon the kernel embedding framework. The experimental results across multiple tasks demonstrate that CGMMN can achieve competitive performance without requiring complex architectures.

## Weaknesses

**1. Theoretical inconsistencies and ambiguities:**
- Theorem 3 states "If the embedding of conditional distributions CY|X = CZ|X, then PY|X = PZ|X," but fails to establish the injectivity condition required for this implication to hold. The paper cites [29] as the source for this result, but [29] does not provide this exact theorem.
- In Section 3.1, the notation "K̃ = K+λI" is used, but then "K̃−1d" appears in the CMMD objective (Eq. 2), which is inconsistent. It should be "K̃d−1" or "K̃−1d".
- The claim that "the inverse matrices K̃−1s and K̃−1d in the CMMD objective are independent of the model parameters" is misleading. K̃ depends on the input data, which is affected by the model parameters during training.
- The paper incorrectly references [19, 3] as presenting GMMN, when [19] is the GMMN paper itself, and [3] appears to be a typo.

**2. Experimental evaluation concerns:**
- The MNIST results (Table 1) show CGMMN-CNN with 3.13% error rate, while DSN achieves 1.92% - a substantial gap that the paper does not adequately address. The claim that "CGMMN-CNN is competitive" is questionable given the significant performance difference.
- The SVHN results (Table 2) show CGMMN-CNN at 3.13% error rate, while CMMVA (the state-of-the-art DGM for prediction) achieves 3.09%. The paper claims "our results are comparable," but the difference is minimal and potentially within statistical noise.
- The paper does not report standard deviations for error rates, making it impossible to assess statistical significance of the results.
- The paper claims "CGMMN is compatible with various network architectures" but only evaluates MLP and CNN, without exploring other architectures like RNNs or Transformers.

**3. Methodological and technical flaws:**
- The paper fails to explain how the sampling procedure for the CMMD objective is implemented in practice, particularly how the conditional variables are handled during sampling.
- The computational complexity of the CMMD objective (O(N^3) due to matrix inversions) is not discussed, which is a critical limitation for large-scale applications.
- The choice of kernel function is not addressed, despite being crucial for kernel-based methods. The paper mentions "we use a Gaussian kernel" in the appendix, but this is not reflected in the main text.
- The paper incorrectly states "the empirical estimator Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ is always an element in the tensor product space" without providing justification for this claim.

**4. Related work and positioning:**
- The paper does not adequately differentiate CGMMN from existing conditional generative models, particularly conditional GANs (e.g., [21]) and conditional VAEs (e.g., [28]).
- The claim that "our work fills the research void to significantly improve the applicability of moment-matching networks" is overstated. The paper does not provide a clear comparison to other conditional moment-matching approaches.

**5. Formatting and presentation issues:**
- Inconsistent notation throughout (e.g., CMMD vs. CMMD, MMD vs. MMD).
- The reference list contains formatting inconsistencies (e.g., some entries include "NIPS" while others use "ICML").
- The abstract claims "CGMMN largely extends the capability of GMMN," but the paper doesn't clearly explain how it differs from other conditional extensions of GMMN.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a theoretically grounded extension of GMMN to conditional settings, the paper suffers from significant technical flaws, inconsistent notation, and insufficient experimental evaluation. The claimed competitive performance is questionable given the substantial performance gaps with state-of-the-art methods in the reported results. The theoretical foundation is not sufficiently rigorous, and the paper fails to adequately address the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding computational complexity and kernel choice.

The paper is not sufficiently novel compared to existing conditional generative models, and the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate that CGMMN provides significant advantages over simpler approaches. The methodological and theoretical shortcomings outweigh the paper's contribution, making it difficult to recommend acceptance.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and theoretical gaps that undermine its claims. The experimental results are not sufficiently compelling to justify the theoretical contribution, and the paper fails to properly position itself against existing work. The minor improvements claimed over prior work are not supported by adequate evidence, and the paper would benefit from substantial revision to address these issues before consideration for publication.
