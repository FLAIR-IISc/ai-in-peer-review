

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors introduce Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a training objective, develop a conditional generative architecture, and evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge tasks. The work builds on prior work in MMD-based generative models and conditional distribution learning.

## Strengths
The paper presents a clear extension of GMMN to conditional settings with a well-motivated objective function. The technical development of CMMD is logically derived from the kernel embedding of conditional distributions. The experimental evaluation covers multiple relevant tasks with reasonable baselines, and the results demonstrate competitive performance on MNIST and SVHN classification tasks. The paper provides a clear explanation of the generative process and training algorithm.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies
The paper contains several critical formatting issues that undermine its technical credibility. In Section 2.3, Theorem 2 cites [29], but this reference is missing from the bibliography. Similarly, Section 1 references [23, 16] without including these in the references. The notation for kernel matrices is inconsistent: "K = Υ⊤Υ" is defined, but later "K̃ = K+λI" appears without clarifying that K here is different from the earlier definition. In equation (2), the notation "K̃ = K+λI" conflicts with the prior definition of K, creating significant confusion.

### Theoretical and Technical Flaws
Theorem 3 makes an overgeneralization: "If the embedding of conditional distributions CY|X = CZ|X, then PY|X = PZ|X." This is incorrect because the conditional embedding is not a single element but a family of points indexed by x (as correctly stated in Section 2.3). The paper fails to clarify this fundamental point. 

The paper claims "the empirical estimator Φ(K + λI)−1Υ⊤ is always an element in the tensor product space which gives as a well-justified approximation (i.e., the Hilbert-Schmidt norm exists)" but this is not accurate without additional assumptions about the regularity of the kernel and distributions. The Hilbert-Schmidt norm may not exist without specific smoothness conditions.

### Experimental Details
The MNIST dataset split (50k training, 10k validation, 10k test) differs from the standard 60k/10k split, which creates a significant methodological inconsistency that should be justified. The paper claims CGMMN-CNN "is competitive with various state-of-the-art competitors that do not use data augmentation or multiple model voting" but fails to specify which competitors use data augmentation (CMMVA, DSN, etc.), making this claim misleading.

In Table 2, CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error rate on SVHN compared to CMMVA's 3.09%, yet the paper claims "our results are comparable with those of CMMVA" - this is a very minor difference that doesn't warrant the stated claim. The paper also states "we are expected to get better results with more sophisticated structures" but provides no evidence for this claim.

### Missing Information
The paper references "Appendix A.2.2" for details on estimating C−1XX and CYX when the domain of X is finite, but no appendix is provided. Similarly, the paper mentions "Appendix A.4.1" and "Appendix A.4.2" without including these sections. The experimental details for the Bayesian dark knowledge task are insufficiently explained, particularly regarding how the "near" training data is generated for the teacher network.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a reasonable extension of GMMN to conditional settings, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and theoretical oversights undermine its credibility. The paper fails to properly clarify fundamental concepts (like the conditional embedding being a family of points rather than a single element), contains critical notational errors, and makes unsupported claims about performance comparisons. These issues are not minor - they represent substantial flaws in the paper's technical presentation and reasoning.

The paper has some merit in its application of MMD to conditional distributions, but the execution is marred by these technical shortcomings. The reviewers should be concerned about the paper's ability to be reproduced due to the missing details and inconsistent notation. The claims about performance are not sufficiently supported by the presented results.

Given the stringent review criteria at NIPS, particularly the requirement for technical soundness and clarity, I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit significantly from a complete revision to address these technical inconsistencies before it can be considered for publication. The authors should carefully re-examine their mathematical formulations, clarify their notation, provide complete experimental details, and properly reference all cited works.
