Summary
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to model conditional distributions p(y|x). The key technical contribution is Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X estimated from (i) empirical data and (ii) samples generated by a neural network that conditions on x and noise h. Training proceeds by sampling y = f(x, h; w) and minimizing the empirical CMMD objective via backprop. The authors evaluate CGMMN on three tasks: predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST and Yale Faces, conditioned on labels), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston Housing), showing competitive predictive performance vs. prior DGMs and plausible conditional samples.

Relation to prior work
- GMMN uses MMD between empirical and generated unconditional distributions. This work generalizes to conditionals via operator-valued embeddings (Song et al., 2009) and uses CMMD for training.
- Compared to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al.), CGMMN avoids min-max optimization and variational bounds, offering a simple squared-loss objective.
- The use of conditional mean embeddings is well-established in kernel literature; the novelty lies in marrying these with deep generators for conditional modeling.

Quality
- Theory: The definition of CMMD and its empirical estimator follow established conditional embedding theory. Theorem 3 (equality of operators implies equality of conditionals) is reasonable, though the assumptions could be specified more carefully (e.g., characteristic kernel on Y, function class assumptions for EY|X[g(Y)|X]).
- Algorithm: The minibatch CMMD objective is straightforward; the observation that K̃−1 on X is independent of w is useful (allowing non-differentiable kernels on X). However, computational costs are cubic in minibatch size due to Gram inversions; practical minibatch sizes mitigate this but may induce bias/variance trade-offs not analyzed.
- Experiments: Predictive results on MNIST/SVHN are competitive with DGM baselines (MMVA/CMMVA), but do not reach the strongest discriminative methods; conditional generation is qualitatively reasonable; the Bayesian distillation case is promising but limited (single small dataset). Missing ablations (kernel bandwidths, λ, noise dimension, fusion choices), sensitivity analyses, and quantitative generation metrics.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized. The CMMD estimator expression is clear, with useful remarks (relation to MMD via non-uniform weights).
- Some important details are missing for reproducibility: the exact kernels and hyperparameters for X and Y in each experiment, bandwidth selection, λ, training schedules, and number of training epochs.
- Minor typos/grammar issues, but not obstructive.

Originality
- Conceptually, extending GMMN to conditional settings via conditional mean embeddings is a natural but non-trivial step; to my knowledge, a conditional MMD-based deep generator had not been developed prior to this.
- The operator-based CMMD objective is not new per se in kernel methods, but its use as a practical deep learning training objective for conditional generation/prediction is novel.

Significance
- Provides a simple alternative to CGAN/CVAE for conditional modeling without adversarial training or variational inference.
- Potentially useful in scenarios where stable training is preferred over adversarial setups; the Bayesian distillation application is interesting and could spur follow-up work.
- The empirical scope is somewhat limited (small/medium-scale datasets and qualitative generation) and lacks strong quantitative benchmarks against CGAN/CVAE on conditional generation.

Strengths
- Clear, simple training objective; backprop-compatible and stable.
- Solid theoretical grounding via conditional mean embeddings.
- Versatile framework applied to prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Competitive predictive performance vs. DGM baselines; qualitative conditional samples are plausible.

Weaknesses
- Limited experimental rigor: missing kernel/hyperparameter details; no ablation or sensitivity analyses; no quantitative generation metrics.
- Computational scalability: O(b^3) per minibatch due to Gram matrix inversions; no exploration of random features / Nyström approximations.
- Theoretical assumptions in Theorem 3 could be made more precise (conditions on Y-kernel being characteristic/universal).
- Comparisons: lacks systematic comparison with CGAN and CVAE on conditional generation; classification comparisons rely on argmax over generated y rather than standard cross-entropy training, which may underperform.
- The Bayesian distillation evaluation is too small to draw strong conclusions.

Questions and suggestions
- Specify kernels used for X and Y in each experiment (e.g., RBF with bandwidth selection via median heuristic or cross-validation) and regularization λ; discuss sensitivity.
- Analyze minibatch bias/variance for CMMD and how it impacts training stability.
- Explore computational speedups (e.g., random Fourier features for Y, Nyström for X) to reduce cubic complexity.
- Compare against CGAN/CVAE on conditional generation with quantitative metrics (e.g., class-conditional accuracy of a pretrained classifier, reconstruction error in code space, or more modern scores if applicable).
- Consider alternative conditioning mechanisms beyond concatenation (e.g., conditional batch norm, FiLM), and study the role of noise dimensionality.
- Clarify assumptions in Theorem 3; ensure the kernel on Y is characteristic to guarantee injectivity of mean embeddings.
- For classification, compare against standard discriminative training (cross-entropy) and hybrid losses (e.g., CMMD + CE), and report calibration metrics if modeling uncertainty is a goal.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean extension of GMMN to conditional modeling with solid kernel-theoretic grounding.
- Simple, stable objective that may be attractive in practice.
- Demonstrates breadth across multiple tasks, including Bayesian distillation.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental evaluation is not sufficiently thorough; missing key comparisons, metrics, and ablations.
- Scalability concerns not addressed; limited analysis of computational and statistical properties.
- Some theoretical assumptions and practical details are underspecified, impacting reproducibility.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is interesting and the approach is elegant, but the empirical evaluation and analysis are not yet strong enough for NeurIPS. Strengthening the experiments (comparisons to CGAN/CVAE, quantitative generation metrics, ablations/sensitivity), clarifying theory and implementation details, and addressing scalability would make this a compelling submission.
