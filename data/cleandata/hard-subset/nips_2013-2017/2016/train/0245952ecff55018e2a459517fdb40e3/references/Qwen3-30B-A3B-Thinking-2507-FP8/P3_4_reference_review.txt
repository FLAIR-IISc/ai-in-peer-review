

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

## Summary and Context

This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to handle conditional distributions. The authors introduce Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a training objective for conditional generative models, building on the kernel mean embedding framework. The paper demonstrates CGMMN's effectiveness across three applications: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on labels), and distilling Bayesian models. The work builds upon prior GMMN (Li et al., ICML 2015) and kernel embedding techniques (Gretton et al., JMLR 2012), extending them to conditional settings.

## Strengths

The paper makes a technically sound contribution by formally extending GMMN to conditional settings with a well-motivated theoretical foundation. The CMMD objective is rigorously derived from kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, providing a principled approach to conditional generative modeling. The training procedure remains simple and amenable to backpropagation, preserving the key advantage of GMMN. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive across three distinct applications, showing competitive results on MNIST and SVHN for classification, compelling generation quality on MNIST and Yale Face datasets, and successful distillation of Bayesian knowledge on the Boston housing dataset. The paper's clarity in presenting the mathematical framework and experimental results is commendable.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

Despite its strengths, the paper has several significant limitations that impact its contribution to the field:

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison with relevant prior work**: The paper fails to adequately compare CGMMN with established conditional generative models like Conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and Conditional VAEs (Soň et al., NIPS 2015). While the authors mention these works in the introduction, they do not provide a thorough comparison in the experiments. For instance, the classification results on MNIST and SVHN are compared to discriminative methods like DSN and CMMVA, but not to conditional GANs or CVAEs, which are the dominant approaches for conditional generation. This omission weakens the claim of CGMMN's competitiveness.

2. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: While the CMMD objective is theoretically grounded, the paper lacks analysis of key practical concerns. There's no discussion of how the choice of kernel affects performance, or how the method scales with conditional variable dimensionality. The authors assume the conditional mean embedding operator CY|X ∈ F⊗G, but don't address how this assumption holds in practice, especially when the domain of X is continuous (as they acknowledge in Section 3.1).

3. **Modest empirical improvements**: The results show CGMMN achieves competitive performance but often with small gains over existing methods. For example, on SVHN, CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error compared to DSN's 1.92%, but the paper doesn't demonstrate why these marginal improvements justify the new framework. The generation results (Fig. 2) show artifacts that require additional post-processing (autoencoder combination), suggesting the method isn't yet ready for production use.

4. **Limited ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of CMMD versus other aspects of the architecture. For instance, it's unclear whether the performance gains come from the conditional objective or simply the network architecture.

## Originality and Significance

While the extension of GMMN to conditional settings is a logical step, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from or improves upon existing conditional generative approaches. The authors reference conditional GANs and CVAEs but don't sufficiently distinguish CGMMN's advantages. The significance is somewhat limited because conditional GANs have already demonstrated strong performance in all three application domains the paper addresses. The paper would be significantly stronger if it could demonstrate a clear advantage of CMMD over the standard conditional GAN training objective.

## Recommendation

I recommend **rejection** of this paper. While the work is technically sound and the experiments are well-executed, the paper fails to sufficiently differentiate its approach from established methods and doesn't demonstrate compelling advantages. The marginal improvements over existing methods don't justify the new framework, especially when compared to the substantial body of work on conditional generative models. The paper would need to address the lack of comparison with relevant prior work, provide stronger theoretical justification for the CMMD objective, and demonstrate more significant empirical advantages to be considered for publication at NIPS.

The authors should consider submitting this work to a venue with a more specialized focus on generative modeling, where the incremental nature of the contribution might be more appropriately evaluated. For NIPS, the paper would need to substantially strengthen its theoretical and empirical claims to demonstrate a meaningful advance in the field.
