This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the GMMN framework to handle conditional distributions. The core idea – leveraging the simplicity of moment matching with deep networks for conditional generation – is a compelling and potentially impactful contribution. The authors correctly identify a need for flexible conditional generative models and propose a clean approach using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective. This builds nicely on recent work in moment matching and GANs, offering a potentially more stable training alternative. The application to predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation further demonstrates the versatility of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** While building on GMMNs, extending them to the conditional setting with CMMD is a genuinely novel contribution. The paper tackles a practical need in generative modeling – controlling generation based on input conditions – with a fresh perspective. This isn't just a minor tweak; it opens up new application possibilities.
* **Simplicity and Elegance:** The approach is conceptually simple and avoids the complex game-theoretic dynamics of GANs. The direct optimization via backpropagation is a significant advantage.
* **Versatility:** The demonstrated applicability to diverse tasks (prediction, generation, distillation) strengthens the case for CGMMN as a general-purpose framework.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding in kernel methods and MMD provides a solid theoretical basis.  The derivations and explanations are generally clear.
* **Competitive Results:** Achieving competitive performance on MNIST, SVHN and demonstrating effective knowledge distillation is encouraging, particularly given the relative simplicity of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks thorough ablation studies to dissect the contributions of different components (e.g., network architecture choices, kernel parameters). While results are reported with MLP and CNN architectures, the systematic impact of these choices isn't clearly analyzed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity to hyperparameters (especially the regularization parameter λ in CMMD) is not extensively explored. The optimal value appears dataset-dependent, and guidance for setting this parameter would be helpful.
* **Scalability Concerns:** While the paper mentions mini-batch optimization, the computational cost of computing kernel matrices, even with approximations, could become a bottleneck for high-dimensional data or very large datasets. This needs further discussion and potentially exploration of more efficient kernel approximations.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons are made to relevant methods, some stronger baselines (e.g., more recent and sophisticated GAN variants like StyleGAN, or more advanced VAE architectures) are missing. A more comprehensive comparison would better position the contribution.
* **Limited Qualitative Assessment:**  For the generative tasks, the paper relies mostly on quantitative results. Providing more visual examples (particularly failure cases) and a more detailed qualitative assessment of the generated samples would be beneficial. The generated samples in the paper are relatively small and don't give a strong sense of the model's capabilities.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to conditional generative modeling.
* The method is conceptually simple, theoretically sound, and exhibits competitive performance.
* The versatility of CGMMN across different tasks is a strong point in its favor.
* The paper addresses a practical need and offers a potentially more stable alternative to GANs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of extensive ablation studies and hyperparameter analysis weakens the empirical validation.
* Scalability concerns could limit the applicability to certain datasets.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is not exhaustive.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some limitations, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution. The novelty and elegance of the approach outweigh the minor weaknesses.  The paper introduces a promising new direction in generative modeling, and I anticipate that it will stimulate further research in this area.  I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the scalability concerns and provide more detailed ablation studies in a future version or response to reviewers. The paper leans towards bold ideas over methodological perfection, which is exactly what NIPS should value.
