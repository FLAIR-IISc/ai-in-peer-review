**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The core contribution is the development of a novel training objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). CMMD is derived from the theory of kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their conditional embedding operators. The generative model itself is a deep neural network that takes the conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to produce a sample `y`. The parameters of this network are learned by minimizing the CMMD between the model's conditional distribution and the empirical data distribution, using backpropagation. The authors demonstrate the utility of CGMMN across three distinct tasks: predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on class labels for MNIST and Yale Faces), and "Bayesian dark knowledge" (distilling a Bayesian neural network into a faster CGMMN).

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Novel Objective Function:** The proposed Conditional MMD (CMMD) is an elegant and theoretically grounded extension of the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) criterion to the conditional setting. By leveraging the representation of conditional distributions as operators in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), the authors derive a loss function that is a natural counterpart to MMD for conditional modeling. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the work, distinguishing it from more heuristic approaches.

2.  **Significant Extension of GMMN:** This work addresses a critical limitation of the original GMMN, which could only model joint distributions. By enabling conditional modeling, CGMMN significantly broadens the applicability of moment-matching-based generative models to a wide range of important problems, including prediction, structured output, and contextual generation. This is an important and logical step forward for this class of models, analogous to the development of conditional GANs and conditional VAEs.

3.  **Comprehensive and Diverse Experimental Validation:** The paper provides strong empirical support for the proposed model across a varied set of tasks.
    *   The predictive modeling experiments show that CGMMN is competitive with state-of-the-art deep generative models for classification (e.g., CMMVA).
    *   The contextual generation experiments successfully demonstrate the model's ability to generate plausible samples conditioned on class labels.
    *   The "Bayesian dark knowledge" experiment is a particularly novel and interesting application, showcasing the model's flexibility in approximating complex predictive distributions from a "teacher" model without significant performance degradation. This diversity of applications makes a strong case for the general utility of the CGMMN framework.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of first reviewing the necessary background on MMD and kernel embeddings of conditional distributions before introducing their own contribution. The motivation is clearly laid out, and the connection between the CMMD objective and the standard MMD is nicely explained in Remark 1. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The primary weakness of the proposed CMMD objective lies in its computational complexity. The objective function in Equation (2) requires the computation and inversion of kernel Gram matrices `(K + λI)^-1`, which scales as `O(B^3)` where `B` is the mini-batch size. While the authors use a mini-batch algorithm, this cubic scaling still imposes a severe practical limit on the batch size that can be used, potentially hindering training stability and performance on very large-scale problems. This is a significant disadvantage compared to GANs or VAEs, whose per-iteration cost scales linearly with batch size. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this limitation, including its practical impact on training time and memory, and potential directions for mitigation (e.g., low-rank approximations to the kernel matrix).

2.  **Quality of Generated Samples:** While the generative results are promising, the samples generated directly by CGMMN (Fig. 2) appear somewhat noisy. The authors show much cleaner results by first training an autoencoder and then applying CGMMN in the latent "code" space. While this is a clever and practical solution, it introduces an extra training step and makes the overall model more complex. It would be valuable to discuss why the direct pixel-space generation struggles. Is this an inherent limitation of the CMMD objective in high-dimensional spaces, or could it be addressed with different network architectures or kernel choices? Furthermore, the evaluation of generative quality is purely qualitative. The inclusion of quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score or FID, though perhaps less standard at the time of writing) would provide a more objective assessment.

3.  **Novelty of the Generative Architecture:** While the CMMD objective is novel, the architecture of the conditional generator—concatenating the conditioning variable `x` with a noise vector `h` and feeding it through a DNN—is a standard and widely used technique in other conditional generative models like conditional GANs. The novelty of the paper lies almost entirely in the objective function. This is not a major flaw, but it is worth noting that the architectural contribution is limited.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative models. The introduction of the CMMD objective is a principled and important step that successfully extends moment-matching networks to the conditional setting, greatly expanding their utility. The authors support their proposal with a thorough theoretical motivation and a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate impressive results on diverse tasks.

The main weakness is the computational cost of the CMMD objective, which may limit its scalability. However, the conceptual contribution is significant and is likely to inspire follow-up work on more scalable approximations. The strengths in terms of originality of the objective, clarity of the presentation, and the breadth of the experimental validation substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The paper clearly advances the state of the art for this class of generative models and would be a solid addition to the NIPS program.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
