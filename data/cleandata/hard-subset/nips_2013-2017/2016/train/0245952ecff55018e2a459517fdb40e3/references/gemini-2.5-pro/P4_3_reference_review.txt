### **Summary**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional probability distributions `p(y|x)`. The work is a direct extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which model joint distributions `p(z)` by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model-generated samples and true data samples. The key contribution of this paper is the formulation of a new training objective, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions.

Theoretically, CMMD is defined as the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between the conditional mean embedding operators of the two distributions. This extends the MMD, which measures the distance between mean embeddings in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors provide an empirical estimator for the CMMD objective based on kernel Gram matrices of both the conditioning variables `x` and the target variables `y`. The proposed generative model is a deep neural network that takes the conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to produce a sample `y`. The network parameters are learned via backpropagation by minimizing the CMMD objective.

This work is well-situated within the context of deep generative models. It provides a non-adversarial alternative to Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (Conditional GANs) [21], much like how GMMN [19] provides an alternative to GANs [5]. The theoretical underpinnings for the CMMD objective build directly upon prior work on the Hilbert space embedding of conditional distributions [29]. The authors demonstrate the model's versatility on a range of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual image generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and distilling knowledge from complex Bayesian models.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed CMMD objective is a logical and well-motivated extension of MMD to the conditional setting, grounded in established theory on kernel embeddings. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that cover three distinct application domains. The results demonstrate that CGMMN is competitive with other state-of-the-art deep generative models, such as max-margin DGMs [18], especially in prediction tasks. The authors are reasonably careful in their evaluation, for instance by checking whether generated samples are mere copies of the training data and by exploring an autoencoder-based approach to improve sample quality.

However, a potential weakness lies in the discussion of the computational complexity and theoretical assumptions. The CMMD objective in Equation (2) requires the inversion of kernel matrices of size `B x B` (where `B` is the mini-batch size), leading to a computational cost of `O(B^3)`. This can be a significant bottleneck for large batch sizes and may limit the model's scalability compared to competitors like Conditional GANs or VAEs, which often have a lower per-batch cost. The paper mentions this scaling issue only briefly. Furthermore, the paper notes that the theoretical well-posedness of CMMD relies on certain smoothness assumptions (specifically, that `C_{XX}^{-3/2} C_{XY}` is Hilbert-Schmidt) which may not hold in practice. While the empirical estimator is presented as a valid approximation, a more detailed discussion of the potential consequences of violating these assumptions would strengthen the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The introduction effectively motivates the need for conditional generative models and positions the work in relation to GMMN and GANs. The preliminary section provides the necessary background for readers not deeply familiar with kernel mean embeddings. The model architecture and learning algorithm are described logically.

There are minor areas where clarity could be improved. The derivation of the empirical CMMD objective in Equation (2) from the Hilbert-Schmidt norm is presented without intermediate steps. While this may be standard for experts, including a more detailed derivation in the appendix would enhance the paper's self-containedness and reproducibility. The description of how the two sample sets (`s` for source/true data and `d` for generated/destination data) are constructed for the mini-batch algorithm could be more explicit. For instance, it should be clarified whether the same set of `x_i` from the mini-batch is used for both the true pairs `(x_i, y_i)` and to generate the model samples `(x_i, f(x_i, h_i))`.

**Originality:** The primary novel contribution is the formulation and application of the CMMD objective for training a deep conditional generative model. While the components are based on existing work—the generator architecture is similar to GMMN, and the theory of conditional mean embeddings is from [29]—their combination to create a non-adversarial, moment-matching framework for conditional generation is new. This fills an important gap, providing a direct counterpart to Conditional GANs in the moment-matching paradigm. The application to distilling Bayesian "dark knowledge" is also a novel and interesting demonstration of the model's capability. The conceptual step from GMMN to CGMMN is a natural one, but the technical execution and empirical validation are solid contributions.

**Significance:** This work is significant as it provides a practical and stable alternative for learning conditional distributions. The training of GANs is notoriously unstable due to the min-max optimization. By offering a simpler objective function that can be directly minimized, CGMMN presents a valuable tool for practitioners and researchers. The demonstrated competitiveness on prediction and generation tasks suggests it is a viable approach. The framework is flexible and can be combined with various network architectures (MLP, CNN), which increases its potential for broad application. It is likely that this work will be used by others and will inspire further research into non-adversarial methods for conditional generation, possibly including efforts to improve the computational efficiency of the CMMD estimator.

### **Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Arguments for:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** Proposes CMMD, a novel and well-founded objective for training conditional generative models, providing a much-needed, stable, non-adversarial alternative to Conditional GANs.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-grounded in the theory of kernel mean embeddings.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The model's effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated across a diverse set of tasks (prediction, generation, model distillation) and datasets, with performance competitive with relevant state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against:**
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The `O(B^3)` computational complexity of the CMMD objective within each mini-batch may limit its application to problems requiring very large batch sizes, a point that is not fully addressed.
*   **Incremental Nature:** The work can be viewed as a natural, albeit non-trivial, extension of GMMN to the conditional setting, using established theoretical tools for conditional embeddings.
*   **Theoretical Gaps:** The practical implications of violating the smoothness assumptions required for the CMMD objective are not deeply explored.
