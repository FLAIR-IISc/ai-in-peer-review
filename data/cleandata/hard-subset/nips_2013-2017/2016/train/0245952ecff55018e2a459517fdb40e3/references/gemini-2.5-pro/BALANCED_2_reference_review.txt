This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional distributions `p(y|x)` within a deep generative model framework. The work is a direct extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which model joint distributions by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model-generated samples and real data. The core technical contribution is the development of a novel training objective, termed Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). CMMD is derived from the theory of kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, defining the discrepancy as the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between the conditional mean embedding operators of the true and model distributions. The proposed generative architecture is simple and elegant: it conditions the output `y` on an input `x` by feeding both `x` and a random noise vector `h` through a deep neural network. The authors demonstrate the utility of CGMMN on a diverse set of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (generating MNIST digits and Yale faces conditioned on labels), and distilling knowledge from complex Bayesian models. This work is situated alongside other efforts to build conditional deep generative models, such as Conditional GANs and Conditional VAEs, offering a non-adversarial, moment-matching alternative.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Novel Objective:** The primary strength of the paper lies in its formulation of the Conditional MMD (CMMD) objective. This is a natural and theoretically-grounded extension of MMD to the conditional setting, building upon established work in kernel methods on conditional mean embeddings [Song et al., 2009]. It provides a principled way to train conditional generative models without the instability often associated with the min-max optimization of adversarial training. The insight in Remark 1, which frames CMMD as a non-uniformly weighted version of MMD, is particularly clarifying.

2.  **Model Versatility:** The paper successfully demonstrates the flexibility of the CGMMN framework across three distinct and important problem domains: prediction, generation, and model distillation. The application to "Bayesian dark knowledge" is particularly novel and compelling, suggesting a practical use case for CGMMN in compressing complex probabilistic models into efficient predictors. This breadth of evaluation strengthens the paper's claim of general applicability.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The preliminary section provides the necessary background on MMD and kernel embeddings, making the subsequent development of CMMD accessible. The generative process and learning algorithm are explained straightforwardly.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Generative Quality and Evaluation:** While the contextual generation results are promising, the visual quality of the generated samples (Fig. 2) is noticeably poor, exhibiting significant noise and lack of sharpness compared to contemporary generative models from that period (e.g., DCGAN). The use of a pre-trained autoencoder to improve sample quality (Fig. 4) feels like a workaround that obscures the direct generative capability of the CMMD objective. For a more convincing demonstration, a direct comparison against a baseline like a Conditional GAN on the same generation tasks would be necessary.

2.  **Computational Cost:** The CMMD objective, as formulated in Eq. (2), requires the computation and inversion of kernel Gram matrices of size `B x B`, where `B` is the mini-batch size. This results in a computational complexity of O(B³), which can be prohibitively expensive and limits the practical batch sizes. While the authors propose a mini-batch algorithm, the scalability of the method remains a significant concern that is not adequately addressed. This contrasts with GANs and VAEs, whose per-iteration costs are typically linear in the batch size.

3.  **Missing Experimental Details and Baselines:** Key details required for reproducibility are missing. The choice of kernel functions (e.g., RBF) and the method for selecting their hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidth) for both `x` and `y` variables are not specified. These choices are critical to the performance of any MMD-based method. Furthermore, the prediction experiments lack a direct comparison with other conditional generative models like Conditional VAEs or Conditional GANs adapted for classification, which would provide a more direct assessment of CGMMN's standing within its specific model class.

4.  **Positioning with Respect to Prior Art:** The paper presents CMMD as a new criterion. While its application to training deep generative models is novel, the idea of using the norm difference between conditional operators for two-sample testing has been explored in the kernel methods literature (e.g., in work on conditional independence testing). A more thorough discussion of this prior work would help to more precisely situate the paper's theoretical novelty, which lies primarily in the integration of these ideas into an end-to-end deep learning framework.

### Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Computational Scalability:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of the computational complexity of the CMMD objective. The authors should consider and discuss potential linear-time approximations for CMMD, analogous to those that exist for MMD, which would make the method more practical for larger batch sizes.
*   **Generative Comparisons:** To better evaluate generative performance, I strongly recommend including qualitative and quantitative comparisons with a cGAN baseline on the MNIST and Yale Faces generation tasks. This would provide a much-needed context for the visual quality of the generated samples.
*   **Reproducibility:** Please provide complete details on the kernel functions used (type, hyperparameter selection strategy) for all experiments. This is crucial for other researchers to be able to build upon this work.
*   **Predictive Task Framing:** In the predictive modeling experiments, the results trail state-of-the-art discriminative methods. To better highlight the significance, consider showcasing a task where a generative conditional model has a clear advantage, such as classification with missing input features or providing well-calibrated uncertainty estimates, and evaluate the model on these aspects.

In summary, this paper presents a solid and valuable contribution by extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. The proposed CMMD objective is theoretically elegant, and the demonstrated versatility of the model is a significant strength. However, the work is constrained by concerns about the practical scalability of the CMMD estimator and the somewhat underwhelming quality of the generated samples compared to competing approaches. Addressing these weaknesses, particularly the computational aspects and the comparative evaluation, would make this a much stronger paper.
