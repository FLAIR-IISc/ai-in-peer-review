Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN by learning conditional distributions p(y|x). The key idea is to define a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD), the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X estimated from data and from a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w), where h is noise. Training minimizes CMMD via backprop using kernel Gram matrices, with minibatching to control cubic costs in batch size. Applications include: (i) predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), (ii) conditional generation (MNIST digits, Yale faces), and (iii) distilling Bayesian models (Boston Housing regression), where the student CGMMN is trained to match samples from a Bayesian teacher’s predictive distribution.

The work builds on kernel mean embeddings (Fukumizu, Song et al.), MMD/GMMN (Gretton; Dziugaite et al.), and conditional generation with deep nets (CGAN, CVAE). The CMMD objective connects to the conditional mean operator learned from data via ridge regression in RKHS.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean: replacing adversarial min–max with a single CMMD objective is appealing and potentially more stable.
- The use of conditional mean embeddings to define a training criterion is well-motivated and leverages established kernel machinery.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation.
- The CMMD objective has a clear interpretation and reduces to weighted MMD, integrating influence of conditioning variables.
- Empirical results are competitive in some settings (e.g., classification with CNN backbones without augmentation/voting; distilled model not degrading RMSE).

Weaknesses
Originality and novelty
- The main technical step—matching conditional mean embedding operators—is a relatively direct extension of GMMN to the conditional case and follows known kernel embedding theory. Similar ideas appear in the conditional embedding literature and in domain adaptation (e.g., conditional MMD variants), but the paper does not position CMMD against these closely related approaches.
- Theorem 3 (CY|X equal implies equality in conditional distributions) is a standard corollary of universality; it provides sufficiency only and offers limited new theoretical insight. No generalization or consistency guarantees are provided for the finite-sample estimator when combined with deep generators.

Methodological clarity and rigor
- Critical training details are missing: kernel choices for X and Y, bandwidth selection (median heuristic? learned?), regularization λ, output kernel differentiability, and sensitivity analyses. MMD/CMMD performance is notoriously sensitive to kernel hyperparameters.
- Computational aspects are underdeveloped. Each minibatch requires inversions of (K + λI); complexity is cubic in batch size with nontrivial constants. No runtime or memory analysis, and no practical accelerations (e.g., random features / Nyström) are explored or evaluated.

Empirical evaluation
- Predictive modeling: While MNIST/SVHN numbers are reasonable, CGMMN does not outperform strong discriminative baselines and lags behind state of the art (e.g., DSN). The paper argues “competitive,” but the benefit of CMMD over standard discriminative training remains unclear. Using CMMD to train a classifier is unconventional; a direct cross-entropy baseline with identical architectures is needed.
- Conditional generation: Evaluation is almost exclusively qualitative, with nearest-neighbor sanity checks. No quantitative metrics (at the time, Parzen-based log-likelihood in input/code space) or human studies; no comparisons to CGAN or CVAE, which are natural baselines for conditional generation.
- Distillation: Only a single small dataset with RMSE is shown; no evaluation of uncertainty (NLL, calibration), no runtime/latency gains, and no comparison to the KD approach in Korattikara et al. (teacher–student matching). This leaves the value of CMMD for distillation unsubstantiated.

Reproducibility and presentation
- Important experimental details are deferred or omitted (e.g., precise perturbation scheme for “near” training data, code release, hyperparameters).
- Several typos and inconsistencies (e.g., “MINIST”), and figures/tables lack detailed captions. It is not clear whether reported CNNs use identical capacity/training schedules as baselines.

Pros
- Simple, general objective; avoids adversarial min–max optimization.
- Coherent use of conditional mean embeddings; theoretically grounded criterion.
- Versatility across tasks; initial results suggest viability.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond a straightforward conditionalization of GMMN via known operator embeddings.
- Missing key baselines (CGAN, CVAE) and quantitative generative evaluations.
- Lack of analysis on kernel choices, sensitivity, scalability, and runtime.
- Predictive results do not surpass strong discriminative methods; unclear advantages over simpler training objectives.
- Distillation experiment is too small-scale and evaluation is incomplete.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add thorough comparisons to CGAN and CVAE for conditional generation, with quantitative metrics appropriate for the era (e.g., Parzen window log-likelihood in code space) and qualitative user studies.
- Include strong discriminative baselines with identical architectures (cross-entropy) and report statistical significance.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for kernels (type, bandwidth), λ, batch size, and noise dimension; document selection strategies.
- Analyze and report training time/memory versus GAN/CVAE; explore scalable approximations (random Fourier features/Nyström) to reduce cubic costs.
- For distillation, evaluate on multiple datasets and include NLL, calibration (ECE), and latency comparisons; compare directly to the method of Korattikara et al. and other KD baselines.
- Clarify and standardize experimental details; fix typos.

Recommendation
Given the limited novelty, lack of rigorous empirical validation against the most relevant baselines, and insufficient analysis of the method’s sensitivity and scalability, I lean to reject. The idea is interesting and could be impactful with a more thorough theoretical and empirical treatment.
