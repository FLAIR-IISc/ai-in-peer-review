Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to learn conditional distributions p(y|x). The core idea is to (i) define a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w) where h is noise and f is a deep network; and (ii) train w by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) between the empirical conditional distribution of (X, Y) and the model-induced conditional distribution. CMMD is formulated via kernel embeddings of conditional distributions: the conditional mean operator CY|X ≈ Φ(K + λI)^{-1}Υ^T, and the objective is the squared Hilbert–Schmidt distance between operators estimated from real and generated samples. Gradients backpropagate through the output-kernel Gram matrices; kernels on x need not be differentiable since their inverses are independent of w. A minibatch algorithm is used to avoid O(n^3) scaling.

Empirically, CGMMN is applied to:
- Predictive modeling: MNIST and SVHN classification (MLP/CNN backbones), predicting labels given images by sampling y and taking argmax.
- Conditional generation: class-conditional MNIST and identity-conditional Yale Faces; also combined with an autoencoder to generate in code space for smoother images.
- Bayesian dark knowledge: distilling predictive distributions from a Bayesian teacher (PBP) on Boston housing, showing similar RMSE.

The work relates to: GMMN (Li et al., Dziugaite et al.), kernel two-sample tests and conditional mean embeddings (Gretton et al., Song et al., Grünewälder et al.), and conditional deep generative models such as cGAN (Mirza & Osindero), Laplacian pyramid GANs, CVAE (Sohn et al.). Compared to GAN-based approaches, CGMMN optimizes a single, non-adversarial objective; compared to (C)VAE, it does not require an explicit likelihood nor variational bound.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMN to the conditional setting using conditional mean embeddings. The CMMD objective is well-motivated and avoids adversarial training instabilities.
- Training is simple: draw conditional inputs from data, sample noise, forward through a network, compute Gram matrices, backpropagate. The observation that K̃^{-1} on X is constant w.r.t. w is practical.
- Broad applicability demonstrated: supervised prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation, using both MLP and CNN backbones.
- Theoretical grounding: CMMD is tied to operator equality CY|X = CZ|X in universal RKHSs, which suffices for equality of conditional distributions (under stated assumptions).
- Practical touches: minibatch training, discussion on different ways to inject noise h, and combining with autoencoders to improve sample quality.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited quantitative evaluation of generative quality. Results rely on qualitative figures and nearest-neighbor checks; there are no standard metrics (e.g., Parzen-window estimates used in contemporaneous work, or class-conditional likelihood proxies). No comparisons against cGAN/CVAE on the same tasks.
- Predictive modeling results are competitive but not state-of-the-art; gaps to strong discriminative baselines (e.g., DSN) remain, and it is unclear whether CGMMN offers consistent advantages over max-margin DGMs beyond simplicity.
- Scalability and bias of the minibatch CMMD are not analyzed. The operator-norm objective is computed on minibatches, but the statistical implications (bias/variance) and alternatives (e.g., random features, block approximations, Nyström) are not explored.
- Kernel choices are under-specified. The paper omits kernel types, bandwidth selection, and centering. In high-dimensional output spaces (images), RBF bandwidth matters greatly for gradients and stability; some guidance or ablations are needed.
- Theoretical guarantees stop short of consistency of the empirical CMMD training for the parametric generator, or sample complexity. Assumptions for CY|X ∈ F ⊗ G, and the practical effect of using the regularized estimator Φ(K + λI)^{-1}Υ^T, are not discussed beyond citing prior work.
- Bayesian distillation is demonstrated only on a small regression dataset, with no uncertainty calibration or predictive distribution assessment; procedure at test time (number of samples from y|x for prediction) is not fully specified.

Quality
- The CMMD derivation and estimator follow standard kernel embedding literature, and the use within a deep generator appears technically sound. However, important implementation details are missing (kernels, bandwidths, centering), and empirical analysis is relatively light, which weakens claims of broad competitiveness.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, but there are typos and minor inconsistencies (e.g., “MINIST”, “augumentation”, “convoluational”, “Through” instead of “Though”). The notation with superscripts s and d is clear once defined, but Sections 3.2/4 occasionally swap s/d labels. Providing explicit kernel choices and hyperparameters would improve reproducibility. Algorithm 1 is helpful.

Originality
- The main novelty is combining the conditional mean embedding operator with deep generators and training via CMMD. While conditional embeddings are known, and GMMN exists, this conditional extension with a practical training recipe is a meaningful, if incremental, contribution.

Significance
- The approach provides a non-adversarial alternative to cGAN and a non-likelihood alternative to CVAE for conditional modeling. If shown to scale and to match their performance, it could be impactful. As presented, empirical gains are modest but the method is simple and potentially useful.

Questions for the authors
- What kernels were used for Y and X in each experiment, and how were bandwidths selected (e.g., median heuristic)? Were features centered?
- How sensitive is training to kernel choices and λ? Any failure modes?
- What is the computational/memory cost per iteration for typical batch sizes? Any tricks to reduce O(B^3) matrix inversions (e.g., caching, low-rank, random features)?
- For classification and regression at test time, do you average over multiple samples of h? How many? Any results on calibration (e.g., ECE/NLL)?
- In conditional generation, how are x’s chosen for the generated set? Always reuse minibatch x’s, or do you perturb them?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative generative evaluations and direct comparisons against cGAN/CVAE on the same conditional tasks.
- Provide ablations on kernel types/bandwidths and batch sizes; consider random Fourier features to scale CMMD and enable larger batches.
- Report standard deviations over multiple runs for predictive tasks; detail all hyperparameters.
- Explore richer conditioning mechanisms (e.g., learned feature extractors for x, conditional normalization) and analyze the effect of latent dimensionality.
- Expand Bayesian distillation experiments (larger datasets; uncertainty metrics).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled extension of GMMN to conditional modeling with simple training.
- Broad applicability shown across tasks; competitive classification results with a generative training objective.
- The CMMD objective may interest the community as a stable, alternative conditional training criterion.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is limited; lacks strong comparisons and quantitative generative metrics.
- Missing implementation details about kernels; scalability analysis is shallow.
- Incremental novelty relative to known conditional embeddings and GMMN.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is clean and potentially useful, but the paper would benefit from stronger empirical validation and more implementation detail. If space allows, addressing kernel choices and adding head-to-head comparisons with cGAN/CVAE would substantially strengthen the case.
