Summary
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to model conditional distributions p(y|x). The key technical ingredient is a conditional MMD (CMMD) objective: the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between empirical conditional mean embedding operators ĈY|X of two conditional distributions (data vs. model). The generator takes concatenated inputs (x,h) with h sampled from a simple prior and is trained end-to-end by backpropagation using the CMMD loss computed from kernel Gram matrices. The paper presents: (i) the CMMD formulation and empirical estimator; (ii) a simple conditional deep generator; and (iii) experiments on classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and Bayesian model distillation (Boston Housing).

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on kernel mean embeddings and conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., 2009/2013), MMD-based generative models (GMMN), and conditional DGMs such as cGAN and cVAE. The novelty is to use the operator norm between conditional mean embeddings as a training objective for conditional deep generators.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMN to the conditional case via established kernel machinery (conditional mean embeddings).
- Simple training via backprop; avoids min–max optimization of GANs.
- The CMMD objective provides a principled way to match conditional distributions without assuming parametric likelihoods.
- The idea is broadly applicable (prediction, conditional generation, distillation), and the approach is architecture-agnostic (MLPs/CNNs).
- Empirically, results are competitive with previous MMD-based DGMs and in the vicinity of other conditional DGMs in some settings.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality/technical soundness
- Theorem 3 is only a sufficiency statement; finite-sample properties of the empirical CMMD estimator are not analyzed. No consistency/variance analysis, no discussion of the effect of λ → 0 with growing N, and no guidance on kernel/bandwidth selection. By contrast, for MMD there are known unbiased/linear-time estimators and asymptotics; here these are missing.
- Section 2.2 states “Asymptotically, L̂^2_MMD is an unbiased estimator.” The estimator given (squared norm of difference of empirical means) is the biased estimator (consistent but biased for finite N); the unbiased U-statistic form is different. This is a technical inaccuracy.
- Computational complexity: CMMD requires inverting K̃ per dataset/batch, which is O(b^3) per minibatch. The paper uses batch sizes up to 500, which is heavy unless K is shared (e.g., if x are identical across the two sets) and/or efficient Cholesky/warm-starts are used. The main text hand-waves to an appendix but provides no quantitative runtime or memory analysis.
- Gradient flow requires the output kernel on y to be differentiable w.r.t. y. The paper asserts that the x-kernel need not be differentiable (true), but does not specify the y-kernel used, its bandwidth selection, or whether gradient stability is an issue. For classification, what kernel over y (probability/simplex vectors) is used?
- Hyperparameters are under-specified: λ for K̃; choice of kernels on x and y; bandwidths; whether median heuristic is used; sensitivity analysis is absent. Reproducibility is therefore limited.
- In Sec. 3.2 sampling: one y per x per update is used. For conditional matching, variance can be high if only one sample per x is drawn. No ablations on number of samples per x, nor on noise dimension h.
- The “finite X” case claims direct estimation of C−1_XX and C_YX, but details are in the appendix; no main-text guidance or complexity analysis is given.
- The distillation experiment is minimal (Boston Housing only), with no runtime/latency/throughput comparison to the teacher, thus not demonstrating the claimed efficiency benefit.

Clarity/presentation
- Many typos and grammatical issues: “MINIST,” “HilbertSchmidt,” “convoluational,” “augumentation,” “Through there is a gap,” etc. References contain multiple misspellings (e.g., Kallenberg) and are often incomplete (years/venues missing, arXiv-only without proper formatting). This undermines professionalism.
- Notation inconsistencies: s/d superscripts seem to flip between “sampled/generated” vs. “data,” and Eq. (2) uses Ksd/Lds with minimal explanation; it’s hard to track which Gram matrices correspond to which dataset.
- Missing experimental details: kernel types and bandwidths, λ values, optimization settings beyond “Adam,” number of training epochs, early stopping, data preprocessing beyond LCN on SVHN.
- Some tables/figures referred to in text are missing or incomplete: Table 1 for MNIST classification is not included; Table 2 labels “CVA+Pegasos” (likely VAE) and reports puzzling 25.3% error; generated samples and nearest neighbors are discussed but not shown here.
- Claims like “total number of parameters is comparable” are not substantiated with counts.

Originality
- The core idea—match conditional distributions by minimizing the HS norm between conditional mean embedding operators—is a natural extension of GMMN using known conditional embedding estimators (Song et al.). The contribution is more an application/engineering of known theory to deep conditional generative modeling than a fundamentally new statistical construct. There appears to be no comparison to existing conditional kernel two-sample testing frameworks or other operator-discrepancy measures.
- Relative to cGAN/cVAE, the conceptual difference (moment matching vs. likelihood/adversarial) is known; the conditional extension here is incremental.

Significance
- The method is potentially useful as a simpler alternative to cGANs (no adversary) and cVAEs (no variational bound) for some tasks. However, experimental evidence is limited:
  - Classification: MNIST table is missing; SVHN results are behind strong discriminative baselines and comparable to prior DGM (CMMVA).
  - Generation: only qualitative results on MNIST/Yale; no quantitative likelihood proxies or sample quality metrics; AE+CGMMN is an intuitive tweak but remains qualitative.
  - Distillation: single small dataset; no efficiency gains demonstrated.
- Without stronger, broader, and more rigorous evaluation (including ablations and hyperparameter transparency), it is hard to assess practical impact.

Questions for the authors
1. What kernels and bandwidths were used for x and y across all experiments? How were they selected?
2. What λ values were used, and how sensitive are results to λ?
3. What is the per-iteration runtime and memory for different batch sizes? Are K̃ inverses shared between real/generated batches (i.e., same x), or recomputed?
4. How many samples per x were used during training? Does increasing samples per x improve stability/accuracy?
5. Can you provide a consistency result for the CMMD estimator (with λ → 0 as N grows), or at least an empirical study of its bias/variance?
6. Please clarify the anomalies in Table 2 (CVA vs. VAE; unusually high error).

Minor issues/typos (non-exhaustive)
- “MINIST,” “HilbertSchmidt,” “entites,” “convoluational,” “augumentation,” “Through there is a gap,” “Kallenbery,” “Mathmetical,” “adverisarial,” “dark knowledge, an interesting case of distilling dark knowledge” (repetitive), misplaced footnote about NIPS.
- Reference formatting is inconsistent and often incomplete.

Pros
- Clean, general, and easy-to-implement objective for conditional generation.
- Avoids min–max training; gradient flows are straightforward.
- Demonstrates applicability across prediction, generation, and distillation.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond straightforward application of conditional embeddings.
- Insufficient theoretical and empirical depth (no finite-sample analysis, sparse ablations).
- Under-specified experimental details; missing/unclear tables; numerous typos.
- Computational complexity and scalability not convincingly addressed.

Recommendation: Reject
While the idea is sensible and potentially useful, the paper in its current form is not up to NeurIPS standards due to presentation issues, incomplete experimental validation, under-specified methodology, and modest novelty. A revised version with cleaner theory, complete and rigorous experiments (including quantitative generation metrics, broader comparisons to cGAN/cVAE, ablations, and runtime), and substantial proofreading could be much stronger.

Confidence: High
