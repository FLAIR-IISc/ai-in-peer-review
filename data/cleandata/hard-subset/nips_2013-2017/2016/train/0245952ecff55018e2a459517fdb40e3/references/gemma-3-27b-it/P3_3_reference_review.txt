## Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a logical extension of Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMNs) to the conditional setting (CGMMN). The authors identify a clear need for conditional generative models and propose a sound approach leveraging conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). While the core idea is solid and the experimental validation is reasonably broad, the paper lacks significant novelty and the presentation, while clear, could benefit from more in-depth discussion of limitations and connections to existing conditional generative modeling techniques like C-GANs and C-VAEs. I recommend a **weak accept** with a request for revisions addressing the points below.

**1. Summary & Related Work (Quality/Clarity):**

The paper effectively introduces the motivation for conditional generative modeling, outlining use cases in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and building larger models. The connection to GMMNs and the rationale for extending them are well-explained. The review of related work is adequate but could be more nuanced. While GANs and GMMNs are mentioned, the paper doesn't deeply engage with the existing landscape of *conditional* generative models. A more thorough discussion of Conditional GANs (C-GANs) [Mirza et al., 2014] and Conditional Variational Autoencoders (C-VAEs) [Sohn et al., 2015] would be valuable.  How does CMMD compare to the adversarial training or variational inference objectives used in these models? What are the specific advantages and disadvantages of CGMMN in those contexts?  The paper states CMMD offers a simpler objective than GANs, but doesn’t quantify or deeply analyze that simplicity (e.g., computational cost, hyperparameter sensitivity).

**2. Technical Contributions & Soundness (Quality/Originality):**

The core contribution – formulating a CMMD criterion for training conditional generative models – is a natural and reasonable extension of MMD. The theoretical grounding, referencing kernel methods and Hilbert space embeddings, is solid.  The derivation of the CMMD objective and its practical estimation are clearly presented.  However, the theoretical novelty is limited. The theorem stating equivalence under the CMMD criterion (Theorem 3) is a straightforward application of existing kernel embedding theory.  

The use of readily available backpropagation for training is a practical strength, simplifying implementation.  The mini-batch algorithm (Algorithm 1) is a good addition, addressing the computational cost of kernel methods. However, the paper doesn't discuss the practical challenges of selecting a good kernel and regularization parameter (λ) and their impact on performance.

**3. Experimental Evaluation (Quality/Significance):**

The experiments are a strength, demonstrating the applicability of CGMMN across different tasks: predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), generative modeling (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. The results are competitive with existing methods, although rarely state-of-the-art.  

However, the experimental setup could be improved.  

*   **Hyperparameter tuning:** The paper lacks detail regarding hyperparameter optimization. What grid search or optimization strategies were employed for selecting network architectures, learning rates, and regularization parameters?
*   **Ablation studies:**  Performing ablation studies to assess the impact of different components (e.g., hidden layer size, kernel choice) would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Comparison to C-GANs/C-VAEs:** Crucially, the experiments would benefit from a direct comparison to C-GANs and C-VAEs on the same datasets. This would allow a more informed assessment of the trade-offs between CGMMN and these widely used approaches.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** For the generative tasks, more comprehensive visual evaluations (e.g., using metrics like Inception Score or FID) and a more detailed qualitative discussion of the generated samples are needed. The current examples are limited.

**4. Clarity & Presentation:**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow. However, the writing is somewhat dry and could benefit from more intuitive explanations and examples. The figures are clear and helpful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Logically sound extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting.
*   Solid theoretical foundation based on kernel methods.
*   Reasonably broad experimental validation across different tasks.
*   Relatively simple training procedure using backpropagation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty in the theoretical contributions.
*   Lack of a thorough comparison to existing conditional generative models (C-GANs, C-VAEs).
*   Insufficient details regarding hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies.
*   Limited qualitative analysis of generated samples.
*   The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.



**Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

*   **Expand the related work section:**  Provide a more in-depth discussion of C-GANs and C-VAEs, contrasting them with CGMMN.
*   **Add a comparative experiment:** Include a direct comparison of CGMMN with C-GANs and C-VAEs on at least one of the datasets used in the paper.
*   **Provide more details on hyperparameter tuning:** Describe the strategies used to optimize hyperparameters.
*   **Conduct ablation studies:**  Investigate the impact of different network architectures, kernel choices, and regularization parameters.
*   **Enhance the qualitative analysis:** Include more generated samples and consider using quantitative metrics like Inception Score or FID.
*   **Discuss the limitations:** Explicitly address the limitations of the approach, such as sensitivity to kernel choice and computational cost.

*   Sohn, K., Yan, X., Lee, H., & Kim, J. (2015). Learning structured output representations using deep conditional generative models. *arXiv preprint arXiv:1512.00570*.
