

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to measure the difference between conditional distributions, and present a conditional generative architecture that takes both conditional variables and random noise as inputs. The method is evaluated across predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for CMMD, with clear theorems establishing the relationship between CMMD and conditional distribution equality. The derivation of CMMD from the kernel embedding of conditional distributions is well-motivated and technically sound.

2. **Versatility of Approach:** The authors successfully demonstrate the applicability of CGMMN across three distinct tasks: predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation. This breadth of application showcases the framework's flexibility and practical utility.

3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from theoretical foundations to experimental validation. The figures (particularly Fig. 2-5) effectively illustrate the generative capabilities of the model.

4. **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide a simple and efficient implementation of CGMMN using back-propagation, which is a significant advantage over more complex conditional generative approaches like conditional GANs that require solving min-max optimization problems.

5. **Novel Application:** The application to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Section 4.3) is particularly novel and demonstrates the method's potential for practical applications beyond standard generative modeling.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares CGMMN to several baselines, it lacks comprehensive comparison with more recent conditional generative models, particularly conditional GANs and conditional VAEs. For instance, the paper should compare against conditional GANs (e.g., [21, 32]) and conditional VAEs (e.g., [28]), which have become standard approaches for conditional generation.

2. **Computational Complexity Concerns:** The paper does not adequately address the computational limitations of kernel-based methods. The cubic complexity of kernel matrix inversion (K̃⁻¹) could be a significant bottleneck for large-scale applications, and the paper lacks discussion of how this limitation might be mitigated in practice.

3. **Theoretical Limitations:** The theoretical analysis is limited to the conditions under which CMMD is well-defined. The paper could benefit from more comprehensive theoretical analysis, such as convergence guarantees or sample complexity bounds for CMMD optimization.

4. **Experimental Details:** Some experimental details are missing that would aid reproducibility. For example, the paper doesn't specify the exact kernel functions used in the experiments, the regularization parameters (λ), or the specific noise perturbations used for the Bayesian dark knowledge experiments.

5. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While the paper shows results for different network architectures (MLP vs. CNN), it lacks ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of specific components of the CGMMN framework (e.g., the impact of different kernel choices, the effect of the dimensionality of the noise variables).

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

1. Include a more comprehensive comparison with recent conditional generative models, particularly conditional GANs and conditional VAEs, to better establish the relative strengths and weaknesses of CGMMN.

2. Address the computational complexity concerns by discussing potential scalability solutions (e.g., using random Fourier features or approximations to the kernel matrix) or by providing runtime comparisons with other methods.

3. Expand the theoretical analysis to include convergence properties or sample complexity bounds for the CMMD objective.

4. Provide more detailed experimental setup information to enable reproducibility, including kernel choices, regularization parameters, and noise perturbation schemes.

5. Add ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of key components of the CGMMN framework.

**Final Assessment:**

This paper presents a well-motivated extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with a sound theoretical foundation and demonstrated utility across multiple tasks. The method is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature. However, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with recent state-of-the-art conditional generative models, better addressing of computational limitations, and more detailed experimental reporting.

The significance of the work is strong, as conditional generative models are crucial for many applications in machine learning. While the approach has some limitations (particularly computational complexity), it offers a simpler alternative to more complex conditional generative frameworks. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by extending moment-matching networks to handle conditional distributions in a principled way.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a clear, novel contribution to the field of generative modeling with strong theoretical foundations and diverse experimental validation. With the suggested improvements, it would be an even stronger contribution to the NIPS community.
