

# Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extends the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions. The authors develop Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a training objective for learning conditional generative models, building on the kernel embedding framework for conditional distributions. CGMMN generates samples by passing conditional variables and random noise through a deep network, with training performed via back-propagation using the CMMD objective. The paper evaluates CGMMN across three application areas: predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on labels), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

The paper relates to previous NIPS work through its connection to GMMN (Li et al., ICML 2015), which extended generative models using maximum mean discrepancy. It also builds on kernel embedding techniques for conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML 2009; Grunewalder et al., ICML 2012) and addresses the need for conditional generative models that was previously addressed by conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2014).

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development of CMMD is well-motivated and supported by Theorem 3, which establishes the connection between equal conditional embedding operators and identical conditional distributions. The derivation of the CMMD objective (equation 2) and its practical implementation are mathematically sound.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear sections that logically build the argument. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility, and figures effectively illustrate key concepts and results.

3. **Novelty:** The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions represents a significant contribution to the field. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work, particularly conditional GANs, which require solving a difficult min-max optimization problem. The authors demonstrate that CGMMN can handle the same range of applications as conditional GANs but with a simpler training procedure.

4. **Significance:** The paper demonstrates competitive performance across multiple tasks (classification, image generation, Bayesian knowledge distillation) using the same framework. The framework's flexibility to work with different network architectures (MLP, CNN) and its ability to address practical challenges like Bayesian model distillation show significant potential for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the paper compares CGMMN to several baselines (VAE, MMVA, CNNs), it lacks direct comparison to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) on the same tasks. A thorough comparison would better establish the relative advantages of CGMMN over the most relevant alternative.

2. **Theoretical Justification for Practical Implementation:** The paper notes that "the assumptions may not hold" in practice but the empirical estimator remains valid. However, it doesn't provide sufficient theoretical justification for why the empirical approximation works well in practice, particularly given the cubic complexity of kernel matrix inversion.

3. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment is limited to a single regression dataset (Boston housing). A more comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets or tasks would strengthen the claims about the method's applicability to Bayesian model distillation.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't include ablation studies to examine the contribution of specific components (e.g., the dimensionality of the noise input, the choice of kernel function) to the overall performance.

**Final Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and novel extension of GMMN to conditional distributions. The theoretical development is rigorous, the implementation is clear, and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across multiple application domains. The work fills an important gap in the literature by providing a simpler alternative to conditional GANs for conditional generative modeling.

While the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with conditional GANs and additional ablation studies, these are minor concerns given the paper's overall contribution. The framework is both theoretically grounded and practically useful, with clear potential for adoption in various machine learning applications.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro Acceptance:*
- Novel extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with a well-motivated theoretical foundation
- Clear and well-written presentation with sufficient experimental validation
- Demonstrates competitive performance across multiple challenging tasks
- Provides a simpler alternative to conditional GANs with the same application scope

*Con Acceptance:*
- Limited comparison to the most relevant alternative (conditional GANs)
- Limited experimental scope for the Bayesian knowledge distillation task
- Insufficient theoretical justification for practical implementation choices

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of generative modeling by providing a theoretically sound and practically effective framework for conditional generative modeling. It addresses an important gap in the literature and demonstrates clear utility across multiple application domains. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and represents a valuable contribution to the field.
