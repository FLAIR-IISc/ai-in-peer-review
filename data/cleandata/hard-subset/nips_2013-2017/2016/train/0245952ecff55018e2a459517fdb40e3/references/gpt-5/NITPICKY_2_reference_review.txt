Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN [19,3] to model conditional distributions p(y|x). The method defines a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w) with noise h drawn from a simple prior, and learns w by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) loss. CMMD measures the Hilbert–Schmidt distance between empirical conditional mean embeddings ĈY|X from data and from the model, leading to a closed-form objective in terms of Gram matrices with Tikhonov regularization. Training proceeds by sampling y from the generator for minibatches of x and backpropagating through the CMMD objective. Experiments cover conditional classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and Bayesian model distillation (Boston Housing), where CGMMN is claimed to be competitive with conditional GANs [21], conditional VAEs [28], and max-margin DGMs [18].

Strengths
- Problem setup is timely: flexible conditional generation and student networks for Bayesian dark knowledge are important topics.
- CMMD objective is conceptually clean and leverages the conditional mean embedding literature [29], providing a non-adversarial alternative to conditional GANs.
- Training is straightforward with backprop; the dependence on x only via Gram matrices permits non-differentiable kernels on x.
- The empirical scope spans prediction, generation, and distillation, showing the generality of the framework.
- Visual generation results improve when combined with an autoencoder, consistent with prior observations in GMMN [19].

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/theoretical:
- Theorem 3’s conditions appear incorrect/misaligned with the literature: to conclude PY|x = PZ|x from equality of conditional mean embeddings µY|x, the kernel on Y must be characteristic (i.e., G universal/characteristic), not the kernel on X (F). The theorem assumes “F is a universal RKHS,” which is not the discriminativity needed for distributions over Y. Please clarify assumptions (characteristic kernel on Y, measurability/regularity on X) and provide a rigorous proof or a precise citation.
- The CMMD estimator in Eq. (2) uses Gram matrices L = Φ⊤Φ without centering. Conditional mean embeddings typically rely on covariance (and cross-covariance) operators; if means are not zero, centering matters. The paper references cross-covariance CXY but doesn’t reflect centering in the empirical objective. Please justify the omission or include centered versions.
- MMD estimator in Sec. 2.2: the presented V-statistic is biased; stating “Asymptotically ... unbiased” is misleading without specifying the U-statistic form. This suggests carelessness with statistical details.
- Assumptions for existence of CY|X and for the Hilbert–Schmidt norm to be finite are acknowledged but not verified for the kernels/datasets used. A brief discussion of kernel choices and regularization λ relative to these requirements would strengthen the claims.

Algorithmic/computational:
- The method requires inverting K̃ = K + λI per minibatch (O(B^3)). No wall-clock comparisons or scaling analysis are provided; practical limits for batch sizes/kernels on large datasets are unclear.
- Kernel choices and hyperparameters (for both X and Y) are critical but unspecified. There is no description of bandwidth selection, normalization, or sensitivity analysis. Reproducibility is compromised.
- For classification, Y is 10-dimensional continuous output used with a kernel; the paper does not specify whether labels are one-hot or logits, nor the kernel on Y. At test time, a single sample and argmax is used; no analysis of variance across samples or calibration.

Empirical evaluation:
- Predictive results: For MNIST and SVHN, only single numbers are reported for CGMMN; no standard deviations across runs/splits. The fairness regarding data augmentation differs across baselines (e.g., DSN uses heavy augmentation). A clearer controlled comparison (same architecture, with/without augmentation) is needed.
- Generative results: Only qualitative samples and nearest-neighbor analyses are provided; no quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen-window estimates as in [19], Inception/FID scores). The images are somewhat noisy without AE; the AE+CGMMN variant improves visuals but again without quantitative evidence.
- Bayesian distillation: Only Boston Housing is reported, with essentially matched RMSE to PBP [10]. No negative log-likelihood, no multiple datasets, no architectural details for the student, and the training procedure for sampling from the teacher is underspecified. Evidence is too limited to substantiate the claim that CGMMN is broadly effective for distillation.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous typos and grammatical issues hinder clarity: “MINIST,” “Varitional,” “convoluational,” “augumentation,” “Through there is a gap,” “Kallenbery,” “Mathmetical.” The paper also includes “∗Corresponding author” and a stray line “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)...,” breaking anonymity guidelines.
- Notation inconsistencies: datasets Dd vs Ds are used with swapped semantics across Sec. 3.1/3.2; please standardize. Acronyms “VA,” “CVA+Pegasos” appear without prior definition (likely VAE).
- Critical implementation details are missing: kernel types and bandwidths on both X and Y, λ selection, minibatch sizes per experiment, number of training epochs, optimizer hyperparameters beyond “Adam,” and preprocessing specifics.

Related work:
- The connection to conditional mean embedding regression (e.g., Song et al. 2009; Grunewalder et al. 2012 [8]) could be deepened, clarifying how CMMD differs from standard CME regression and prior conditional density estimators.
- While conditional GAN [21] and conditional VAE [28] are cited, there is no direct empirical comparison to conditional GANs on the same tasks/architectures.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, non-adversarial conditional generative training objective with broad applicability.
- Solid grounding in kernel mean embeddings; potentially useful in practice when adversarial training is unstable.
- Promising initial results across several tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical assumptions appear misstated, and the estimator may be missing centering; overall rigor is insufficient.
- Key experimental details are omitted; reproducibility and fairness are weak.
- Limited and largely qualitative evaluation for generation and distillation; predictive gains are modest.
- Numerous typos, formatting, and anonymity violations.

Questions for the authors
- Specify kernels and bandwidth selection procedures for X and Y in all experiments. Did you use Gaussian kernels? How was λ chosen?
- Correct or clarify Theorem 3 assumptions (characteristic kernel on Y) and whether centering is used in Eq. (2).
- Provide quantitative generative metrics and more comprehensive distillation experiments (including NLL).
- Clarify computational costs vs. GAN/VAE baselines and discuss scalability.

Typos/formatting (non-exhaustive)
- “MINIST” → MNIST; “Varitional” → Variational; “convoluational” → convolutional; “augumentation” → augmentation; “Through there is a gap” → Although; “Kallenbery” → Kallenberg; “Mathmetical” → Mathematical.
- Incomplete/incorrect references (missing years/venues, e.g., many arXiv entries, [1]–[33] inconsistencies). Remove “∗Corresponding author” and NIPS header from the main text.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the current submission has theoretical inaccuracies, insufficient experimental rigor, missing implementation details, and significant presentation issues that collectively undermine confidence in the contribution. With corrected theory, complete details, stronger and fair comparisons, and improved writing, this work could be competitive in a future submission.
