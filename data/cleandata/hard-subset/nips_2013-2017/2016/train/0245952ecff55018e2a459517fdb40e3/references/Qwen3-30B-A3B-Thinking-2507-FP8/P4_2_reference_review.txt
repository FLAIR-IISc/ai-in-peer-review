

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. While GMMN learns joint distributions p(x,y) using Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), CGMMN learns conditional distributions p(y|x) using a novel Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion. The authors theoretically derive CMMD as a Hilbert-Schmidt norm between kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions, and demonstrate the approach across three applications: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on class labels), and Bayesian knowledge distillation. The work builds on the GMMN framework (Li et al., ICML 2015) and positions itself as filling a research void in extending moment-matching networks to conditional settings, contrasting with conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., NIPS 2015).

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a clear and rigorous theoretical foundation for CMMD, with well-constructed theorems (Theorems 1-3) establishing the mathematical properties of the proposed criterion. The derivation of CMMD as a natural extension of MMD is logically sound.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental section demonstrates the versatility of CGMMN across multiple challenging applications. The evaluation includes three distinct tasks (classification, image generation, Bayesian knowledge distillation) on multiple datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face, Boston Housing), with appropriate baselines.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a clear and implementable training algorithm (Algorithm 1) that uses standard backpropagation. The paper effectively demonstrates that the conditional extension maintains the simplicity of the original GMMN framework.

4. **Clear Differentiation from Prior Work**: The paper properly contextualizes its contribution relative to existing work on conditional generative models, particularly highlighting how CGMMN differs from conditional GANs and conditional VAEs.

## Weaknesses
1. **Kernel Selection Ambiguity**: The paper does not adequately address the critical issue of kernel selection for MMD-based methods. While the authors use Gaussian kernels implicitly, they do not discuss how kernel choice affects performance or provide analysis on optimal kernel selection for different tasks.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The paper does not thoroughly analyze the computational complexity of the CMMD calculation, particularly the cost of matrix inversions in Eq. (2). This is a significant omission given the cubic scaling with sample size mentioned in Section 3.2.

3. **Performance Gap with Discriminative Models**: The results in Table 1 and 2 show CGMMN-CNN underperforms compared to discriminative models like DSN (1.92% vs. 3.13% error on SVHN). The paper does not adequately explain this gap or discuss when CGMMN would be preferable to discriminative approaches.

4. **Limited Discussion of Assumptions**: The paper mentions assumptions required for the CMMD objective to be well-defined (e.g., smoothness assumptions for C_{XX}^{-3/2}C_{XY} being Hilbert-Schmidt), but does not discuss how frequently these assumptions fail in practice or how to handle violations.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- The paper makes a meaningful extension to the GMMN framework that addresses a practical need for conditional generative models.
- The theoretical contribution (CMMD) is novel and well-justified.
- The experimental validation across multiple domains demonstrates the versatility of the approach.
- The method maintains the simplicity of the original GMMN framework while significantly expanding its applicability.
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- The performance gains over state-of-the-art methods are not substantial in classification tasks (CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error on SVHN vs. DSN's 1.92%).
- The paper does not sufficiently address critical implementation details (kernel selection, computational complexity) that would be necessary for others to reproduce and build upon the work.
- The theoretical contribution, while sound, is somewhat incremental given the direct extension of MMD to conditional distributions.
- The paper does not provide sufficient analysis of the limitations of the approach in complex conditional settings.

## Overall Assessment
This is a solid contribution that extends the GMMN framework to conditional distributions in a theoretically sound manner. The paper demonstrates the practical utility of the approach across multiple domains with competitive results. While the performance gains over discriminative methods in classification tasks are modest, the paper makes a clear theoretical contribution that fills a research void in the literature on moment-matching networks.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful extension to the field. The weaknesses identified are important but do not undermine the overall contribution. The work is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing fundamental problems in generative modeling with a theoretically grounded approach.

I recommend acceptance, as the paper advances the state of the art in conditional generative modeling with a clean, theoretically sound approach that has demonstrated utility across multiple applications. The work provides a valuable framework for researchers working on conditional generative models.
