Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a non-adversarial approach to learning conditional generative models p(y|x) by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD). The core idea is to embed conditional distributions into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) via the conditional mean operator CY|X, and to train a neural generator y = f(x, h; w) (with h sampled from a simple prior) so that its conditional embedding matches that of the data in Hilbert-Schmidt norm. The objective admits a closed-form estimator involving Gram matrices on x and y (with Tikhonov regularization), and gradients w.r.t. network parameters are computed by back-propagating through the y-kernel; the inverses on the x-side do not depend on model parameters. The method extends GMMN (Dziugaite et al., UAI 2015) from joint to conditional distributions using the kernel conditional embedding framework (Song et al., ICML 2009; Grunewalder et al., ICML 2012). Compared to GANs (Goodfellow et al., NIPS 2014) and conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), the objective avoids min-max optimization. Related conditional deep generative approaches include conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., NIPS 2015). The paper also positions CGMMN as a student network to distill Bayesian predictive distributions (cf. Bayesian Dark Knowledge, Korattikara et al., NIPS 2015).

Theoretical contribution is mostly a sufficient condition: if the conditional embedding operators coincide (CY|X = CZ|X in a universal RKHS), then PY|X = PZ|X pointwise. The practical CMMD loss is an empirical Hilbert-Schmidt norm between estimated operators, computable via traces of products of Gram matrices.

Experiments cover: classification (MNIST and SVHN) by predicting y given x, conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces (optionally via an autoencoder code space), and distillation of a Bayesian neural net (PBP; Hernández-Lobato & Adams, ICML 2015) on Boston Housing.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean extension of GMMN to the conditional setting using well-established kernel conditional embeddings. The CMMD objective is simple, stable, and differentiable.
- Practicality: training reduces to standard back-prop; no adversarial game. The formulation allows non-differentiable kernels on inputs x, since K̃−1 depends only on data.
- Flexibility: works with MLPs or CNNs; can operate in pixel space or an autoencoder code space to improve sample quality.
- Empirical evidence across tasks: competitive classification accuracy (especially with CNNs) and reasonable label-conditioned samples; proof-of-concept for Bayesian distillation without loss in RMSE on Boston Housing.

Weaknesses
- Originality is moderate. The use of conditional mean embeddings and operator norms to match conditionals is a natural—and arguably straightforward—extension of GMMN using known tools (Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012). The theoretical statement (Theorem 3) follows directly from properties of universal RKHS conditional embeddings; novelty lies primarily in applying this to train deep conditional generators.
- Limited empirical rigor for generation. The paper provides qualitative samples and nearest-neighbor checks, but no quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen-window log-likelihood then in vogue, or diversity metrics), nor comparisons against cGAN or cVAE on conditional generation. The Yale Faces results are small-scale.
- Scalability concerns. Each CMMD term requires inverting K̃ per minibatch, with O(b^3) cost (b = batch size), and O(b^2) memory for Gram matrices. There is no analysis of runtime, memory, or approximation strategies (e.g., Nyström, random features, block/kernel online updates).
- Hyperparameter/kernel choices under-specified. Kernel types/bandwidth selection on y (particularly for high-dimensional images) critically affect MMD-style objectives; details are sparse. The need to move to AE code space to obtain clearer MNIST samples suggests sensitivity to output-space kernels.
- Limited breadth and depth of evaluation. Classification numbers are not state-of-the-art and sometimes below strong discriminative baselines (e.g., DSN). The distillation experiment is only on a small UCI dataset; no ablations (noise dimension, λ, kernel bandwidths), no uncertainty calibration, and no speedup measurements vis-à-vis the teacher.
- Clarity and presentation issues. Some typos (e.g., “CVA+Pegasos”), incomplete references, and notational overload with s/d datasets. Algorithmic details and practical tips (e.g., kernel selection, regularization) are mostly deferred.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The method is technically sound and correctly leverages conditional mean embeddings. However, empirical evaluation lacks quantitative generative metrics, thorough comparisons (especially to cGAN/cVAE), ablations, and scalability analysis.
- Clarity: Generally understandable, with a clear derivation of the CMMD estimator. Some notation and editorial issues remain; more details on kernels and training heuristics would improve reproducibility.
- Originality: Incremental relative to GMMN and conditional embedding literature; novelty is in marrying these to train deep conditional generators.
- Significance: Moderate. A simple, stable, non-adversarial conditional generator is potentially useful, and the distillation application is appealing. The current evidence, however, falls short of demonstrating clear advantages over established alternatives.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general framework that avoids adversarial training.
- Sound use of conditional kernel embeddings; objective has a closed-form estimator.
- Works across tasks (prediction, generation, distillation) with one unified training principle.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond a straightforward conditionalization of GMMN.
- Experimental evaluation is not sufficiently comprehensive or competitive, especially for conditional generation.
- Scalability and kernel/hyperparameter sensitivity are not adequately addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative generative evaluations and direct comparisons to cGAN and cVAE on the same conditioned tasks (e.g., class-conditional MNIST/SVHN), including metrics prevalent at submission time.
- Provide ablations on kernel choices (RBF vs. IMQ, bandwidth selection), noise dimensionality, and regularization λ; report sensitivity and training stability.
- Address scalability with approximate kernel methods (Nyström, random Fourier features) and analyze time/memory complexity; include wall-clock and throughput comparisons to GAN/cVAE.
- Evaluate on more challenging conditional generation or structured prediction tasks where p(y|x) is multi-modal, demonstrating that the model captures diverse outputs.
- For distillation, include larger datasets, uncertainty calibration (e.g., NLL, calibration curves), and speedups at test time relative to the Bayesian teacher.
- Improve clarity: fix typos/references; detail training heuristics and kernel hyperparameter selection; clarify the mini-batch CMMD estimator’s bias/variance.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a coherent and practically appealing extension of GMMN to conditional modeling, but the novelty is limited and the empirical evidence is not yet strong enough for NeurIPS. Strengthening the experimental section and addressing scalability and kernel choices would significantly improve the paper.
