Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative modeling framework that extends GMMN by matching conditional distributions via kernel mean embeddings. The key technical contribution is a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective that measures the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between empirical conditional mean embedding operators ĈY|X estimated from data and from the generator. The generator takes both the conditioning variable x and injected noise h through a deep network y = f(x, h | w), enabling flexible conditional sampling. Training is done by backprop through the CMMD objective, where the inverse Gram matrices on x, (K + λI)−1, are independent of network parameters. The authors present applications in (i) predictive modeling p(y|x), (ii) contextual generation (e.g., class-conditional image generation), and (iii) distillation of Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge”).

This work builds on: (1) MMD and kernel mean embeddings [Gretton et al. 2007; Song et al. 2009], (2) GMMN [Li et al. 2015; Dziugaite et al. 2015], and (3) conditional deep generative models such as cGAN [Mirza & Osindero, 2014] and CVAE [Sohn et al., 2015]. The proposed CMMD objective is a natural and useful extension of MMD to conditional distributions, leveraging conditional mean embedding estimators ĈY|X = Φ(K + λI)−1Υ⊤. The paper provides a sufficient-identifiability statement (Theorem 3) under universal kernels and regularity assumptions, and demonstrates competitive empirical results on MNIST/SVHN classification, class-conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and regression distillation on Boston housing.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Extends the elegant GMMN/MMD recipe to conditional distributions in a principled way using kernel conditional mean operators. The resulting CMMD loss has a closed-form in terms of Gram matrices (Eq. 2), enabling straightforward implementation via backprop.
- Practicality: The inverse Gram matrices depend only on x, simplifying differentiation; the minibatch algorithm is simple and uses standard optimizers (Adam).
- Versatility: Demonstrates three distinct use cases (conditional prediction, conditional generation, Bayesian distillation) with consistent, competitive performance. On SVHN classification, results are close to a strong DGM baseline (CMMVA).
- Useful insights: The remark connecting CMMD to MMD with non-uniform weights induced by x is helpful for intuition; the AE+CGMMN trick for smoother samples mirrors GMMN+AE practices.
- Stability: Avoids adversarial min–max instability while allowing conditional generation, which is appealing relative to cGAN at training time.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Theoretical guarantees: While Theorem 3 provides a sufficient condition, there is no analysis of the empirical CMMD estimator (bias/variance), nor consistency of minimizing empirical CMMD over network parameters. In particular, conditions under which L̂CMMD → 0 implies convergence of the learned conditional to the true conditional are not discussed.
- Computational complexity: Each minibatch requires inverting (K + λI) for x (O(m^3)), which can be heavy for large minibatches (m up to 500 in experiments). There is no runtime or memory report, nor discussion of using low-rank/approximate kernels (e.g., Nyström or random features) to scale further.
- Experimental depth for generation: Conditional generation results are limited to MNIST and Yale Faces, with qualitative samples and nearest-neighbor sanity checks, but no quantitative metrics or comparisons against cGAN/CVAE. The field already has strong cGAN/CVAE baselines for class-conditional generation; omitting these makes it hard to assess generative quality and diversity. An ablation against a simpler baseline (joint GMMN on (x, y) with an RBF kernel on the concatenated pair) would also be informative to isolate the benefit of CMMD over unconditional MMD on the joint.
- Kernel choices and hyperparameters: The paper does not specify kernels on y and x (Gaussian? bandwidth selection? median heuristic?), λ values, or sensitivity analyses. Since the weighting induced by K is central, the performance dependence on kernel parameters should be reported.
- Training protocol when x is reused: In Algorithm 1, the generated pairs (x, ŷ) share the same x as the data minibatch, implying Ksd = Ks = Kd. Then CMMD reduces to a reweighted MMD on outputs (as noted in the remark). This is reasonable, but then the method may not exploit generalization over a broader support of X unless x-perturbations are used. Please clarify the extent of perturbations and their impact on performance.
- Predictive modeling evaluation: While MNIST/SVHN numbers are competitive with DGMs, they lag behind purely discriminative methods. There is no calibration or uncertainty evaluation despite modeling p(y|x); using proper scoring rules (e.g., NLL/Brier) and calibration plots would better demonstrate benefits over standard discriminative training.
- Bayesian distillation: The regression experiment is minimal (Boston housing only) with RMSE parity to PBP but no uncertainty evaluation (e.g., predictive log-likelihood, calibration, interval coverage), which is crucial for Bayesian dark knowledge.

Quality
- The method is technically sound and well-grounded in kernel embedding theory. However, the paper would benefit from empirical ablations and more thorough analysis of kernel choices, computational cost, and estimator behavior. Generative comparisons are limited.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized; the CMMD derivation and Eq. (2) are helpful. Some typos/formatting: “MINIST”, “augumentation”, “convoluational”, stray “th Conference…”, and table placement. Please specify kernels, bandwidth selection, λ, and exact architectures/hyperparameters in the main text or a clear appendix sufficient for reproduction.

Originality
- The extension from MMD to CMMD for deep conditional generators is a natural but novel combination. To my knowledge (circa the cited works), conditional moment-matching with kernel conditional operators in deep nets is new.

Significance
- Conditional generative modeling without adversarial training is important. If strengthened experimentally (especially against cGAN/CVAE, and with scalable approximations), this approach could be adopted by practitioners seeking stable conditional generation and probabilistic prediction.

Questions for the authors
1. What kernels and bandwidth selection strategies were used for x and y in each experiment? How sensitive are results to these choices and to λ?
2. What are the computational costs per iteration (wall-clock, memory) for typical minibatch sizes? Have you tried low-rank kernel approximations to reduce O(m^3)?
3. Did you compare to a GMMN trained on the joint (x, y) with an RBF kernel on concatenated inputs, then using the generator conditionally? How does CMMD compare?
4. In Algorithm 1, are x’s exactly shared between data and model minibatches? If so, how much x-perturbation did you use, and does it improve generalization?
5. For Bayesian distillation, can you report predictive log-likelihoods, calibration (e.g., PIT, coverage), and perhaps a larger dataset?

Pros for acceptance
- Principled and simple conditional extension of GMMN; avoids adversarial training.
- Clear formulation and implementable objective with backprop.
- Versatile demonstrations across tasks; competitive DGM classification results.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited generative evaluation and lack of comparison to cGAN/CVAE.
- Missing details on kernels/hyperparameters and computational cost.
- No theoretical consistency guarantees for empirical CMMD minimization.
- Minimal Bayesian distillation evidence and no uncertainty evaluation.

Overall: A solid, well-motivated contribution that extends MMD-based training to conditional generative modeling. With stronger empirical comparisons and more details on kernels/complexity, this could be a valuable addition. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can address the experimental and clarity gaps; otherwise borderline.
