### Summary

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional probability distributions `p(y|x)`. It extends the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework, which uses Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to train an unconditional generative model, to the conditional setting. The core technical contribution is the development of a novel objective function, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). This objective measures the distance between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective conditional mean embedding operators. The generative model itself is a deep neural network that takes the conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to produce a sample `y`. The model is trained end-to-end using backpropagation on the CMMD objective. The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN on three distinct tasks: predictive modeling (classification on MNIST/SVHN), contextual generation (generating images conditioned on labels), and model distillation (learning a compact network from a Bayesian model).

### Review

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting, a crucial step for making these models widely applicable. The proposed CMMD objective is a principled approach grounded in the theory of kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. The work is a valuable contribution, analogous to the development of conditional GANs and conditional VAEs for their respective frameworks.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the CMMD objective from the theory of conditional mean embedding operators is clear and correct. The proposed generative architecture is standard but appropriate for the task. The empirical evaluation is extensive, covering a diverse set of applications that convincingly demonstrate the model's flexibility. The results in predictive modeling and Bayesian model distillation are competitive, showing that the framework is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practical tool.

However, there are a few points that could be clarified to further improve the quality:

1.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The CMMD objective in Eq. (2) requires computing and inverting kernel matrices of size `B x B`, where `B` is the mini-batch size. This has a computational complexity of O(B³), which can be a significant bottleneck for large batch sizes commonly used in deep learning. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this limitation and how it compares to alternatives like conditional GANs. Mentioning potential approximation techniques (e.g., Nystrom, Random Fourier Features) for the kernel inverse could also be valuable for future work.

2.  **Kernel Selection:** The performance of any MMD-based method is highly dependent on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., the bandwidth of an RBF kernel). The paper does not provide details on how these were selected for the various experiments. A discussion on the sensitivity of CGMMN to these choices and the methodology used for their selection would be very helpful for reproducibility and for practitioners looking to apply this model. Similarly, the choice of the regularization parameter `λ` is not discussed.

3.  **Quality of Generated Samples:** The generated MNIST samples (Fig. 2) appear quite noisy compared to what contemporary (c. 2016) generative adversarial models could produce. While the authors' use of an autoencoder pre-training step improves the visual quality (Fig. 4), this adds complexity to the training pipeline. A direct quantitative and qualitative comparison against a relevant baseline like Conditional GAN would provide a more calibrated assessment of the model's generative capabilities.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the need for conditional models. The preliminary section provides a concise and sufficient background on kernel embeddings and MMD, making the paper accessible. The transition from the theory of conditional operators to the empirical CMMD objective is logical and easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly. The connection between MMD and CMMD highlighted in Remark 1, which frames CMMD as a weighted version of MMD, is particularly insightful.

**Originality:** The paper's main contribution—the formulation of the CMMD objective and its application to training a conditional deep generative model—is novel. While building upon the established theory of conditional kernel embeddings [29] and the GMMN framework [19], the synthesis of these ideas into a practical training objective for deep networks is a significant and original step. It successfully fills a void in the landscape of deep generative models, providing a non-adversarial, moment-matching alternative for conditional generation. The related work is adequately cited.

**Significance:** The work is significant as it substantially broadens the applicability of MMD-based generative models. Unconditional models have limited use, whereas conditional models are central to a vast range of machine learning tasks, as the authors rightly point out. By providing a stable, non-adversarial training scheme for conditional generation, CGMMN offers a valuable alternative to cGANs, which can be difficult to train. The demonstration on Bayesian dark knowledge is particularly interesting, showcasing a novel application for this type of generative model that could inspire further research in model compression and distillation. Researchers working on generative models, kernel methods, and applications requiring tractable conditional density models are likely to build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Principled and Novel Objective:** Proposes a theoretically-grounded and novel CMMD objective for training conditional generative models.
*   **Stable Training:** Offers a stable, non-adversarial alternative to cGANs, avoiding the difficulties of min-max optimization.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates effectiveness across a diverse and relevant set of tasks (prediction, generation, distillation).
*   **Clarity and High Quality:** The paper is well-written, methodologically sound, and easy to understand.
*   **Significant Contribution:** Greatly expands the scope of moment-matching networks, making them relevant to a much wider range of problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The O(B³) complexity of the CMMD objective for a mini-batch of size B is a potential limitation that is not discussed.
*   **Missing Practical Details:** Lack of detail on kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning makes reproduction difficult and obscures a critical aspect of the method's practical implementation.
*   **Generative Quality:** The visual quality of the generated samples is not on par with state-of-the-art GANs from the same period, suggesting a potential trade-off between training stability and sample fidelity.

**Recommendation:** Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful framework for conditional generative modeling. The work is a significant contribution to the field of deep generative models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
