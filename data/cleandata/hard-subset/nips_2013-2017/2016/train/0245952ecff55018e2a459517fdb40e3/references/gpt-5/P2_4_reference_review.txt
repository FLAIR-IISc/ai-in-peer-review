Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative model trained by minimizing a conditional variant of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The key technical contribution is a Conditional MMD (CMMD) objective defined as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X of the data and the model, building on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009/2013). A conditional generator maps (x, h) → y via a deep network f with noise h ∼ simple prior, and parameters are trained by backpropagating through a minibatch estimate of the CMMD objective expressed via Gram matrices. The method avoids GAN-style min–max optimization and does not require likelihoods as in VAEs.

This extends GMMN (Li et al., 2015; Dziugaite et al., 2015) from unconditional to conditional settings, akin to how cGAN extends GAN and CVAE extends VAE. The paper applies CGMMN to three settings: (1) supervised prediction (MNIST, SVHN), (2) conditional generation (MNIST digits and Yale faces given labels), and (3) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (PBP teacher on Boston housing). It compares to VA/CVAE-style pipelines and max-margin DGMs for prediction, and provides qualitative samples for generation.

Quality
- Theory: The CMMD objective is well-motivated from conditional mean embeddings. Theorem 3 gives a sufficient condition (equality of operators implies equality of conditionals) under standard RKHS assumptions. The empirical objective (Eq. 2) is derived clearly, with regularization λ and minibatch training to control cubic costs in batch size. However, the theoretical section stops short of error bounds or sample complexity for the empirical operator, and heavily relies on assumptions (e.g., universality, smoothness so C−3/2XX CXY is Hilbert–Schmidt) that are acknowledged but not examined empirically.
- Algorithms: The training loop is straightforward. An advantage is that kernels on X need not be differentiable since K̃−1 depends only on x; gradients flow through y’s kernel. This is a practical benefit.
- Empirical evaluation: The supervised prediction results on MNIST/SVHN are competitive with generative baselines (MMVA/CMMVA) but below the strongest discriminative models (DSN, Maxout, NiN). Conditional generation is shown qualitatively, with a nearest-neighbor sanity check; improved samples are obtained when CGMMN is trained in an autoencoder code space. For Bayesian distillation, the student matches teacher RMSE on Boston housing.
- Missing details and analyses: Important practical aspects are not reported in the main text: kernel choices and bandwidth selection for X and Y (especially for continuous y in classification), sensitivity to λ and batch size, and computational costs. There is no quantitative evaluation of generation quality (e.g., Parzen log-likelihood, inception/classifier scores for the era), and no comparison to cGAN or CVAE on conditional generation tasks. In prediction, the objective implicitly matches conditional distributions using single-label observations per x (thus relying on kernel smoothing over X), but there is no ablation against standard cross-entropy or regression losses with the same architectures. The output layer is sometimes sigmoid (not simplex-normalized), yet classification is evaluated via argmax; calibration or NLL is not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a solid background on MMD and conditional embeddings, and a compact derivation of the CMMD loss. The algorithm is easy to follow. There are some typos and formatting issues (references missing years; “augumentation”), and many crucial implementation details are deferred to the appendix (kernel families/bandwidths, minibatch CMMD variant), which may hinder reproducibility from the main text alone. It would help to explicitly state the kernels used for X and Y in each experiment and how hyperparameters were selected.

Originality
The idea of training a conditional generator by matching conditional mean embeddings appears to be a natural but nontrivial extension of GMMN, leveraging existing conditional embedding theory. While conditional kernel embeddings are known, using the Hilbert–Schmidt distance between empirical conditional operators as a deep learning objective seems novel at the time, and offers a simple alternative to cGAN/CVAE without adversarial or variational machinery. The Bayesian distillation angle is interesting, though shown on a small example.

Significance
The work is potentially useful: it provides a simple, stable, and general objective for conditional generative modeling that avoids adversarial training instabilities and likelihood design. However, the empirical section, while suggestive, does not convincingly demonstrate superiority or parity with strong conditional baselines on generation, nor clear advantages over standard discriminative training on prediction. The method may inspire follow-up work, but the current evidence for impact is moderate.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of MMD training to conditional models using conditional embeddings.
- Simple training with backprop; no adversarial min–max.
- General-purpose conditional generator applicable to prediction, conditional synthesis, and Bayesian distillation.
- Competitive supervised results against generative baselines; reasonable qualitative samples.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation of generation; no comparison to cGAN/CVAE on conditional generation.
- Key hyperparameters (kernel type/bandwidth, λ) and sensitivity analyses are missing; unclear robustness.
- In prediction, no ablations vs cross-entropy with the same architectures; unclear when CMMD offers a practical advantage.
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to sufficiency; no finite-sample analysis of the empirical operator objective.
- Reproducibility details are sparse in the main text; some references/formatting issues.

Questions for the authors
1. What kernels and bandwidth selection procedures were used for X and Y in each experiment? How sensitive are results to these choices and to λ?
2. In supervised prediction, do you use the same x in the s and d sets within a minibatch? If so, does the CMMD reduce to an MMD on Y with non-uniform weights? Any empirical observations from perturbing x?
3. How does CGMMN perform against cross-entropy training with the same CNN/MLP architectures on MNIST/SVHN?
4. Can you provide quantitative metrics for conditional generation (e.g., classifier-based scores) and comparisons to cGAN/CVAE?
5. What are typical batch sizes and computational costs per iteration relative to CE or GAN training?

Recommendation
Borderline reject (leaning weak reject). The idea is sound and the objective is attractive in its simplicity, but the empirical validation is not yet strong enough, especially on conditional generation, and important implementation details are missing. Strengthening comparisons to cGAN/CVAE, adding quantitative generation metrics, providing ablations and sensitivity analyses, and clarifying kernel choices would significantly improve the paper.
