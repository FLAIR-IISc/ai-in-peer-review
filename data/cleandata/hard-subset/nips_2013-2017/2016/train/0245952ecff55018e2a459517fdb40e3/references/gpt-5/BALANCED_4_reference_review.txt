Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to conditional settings. The key idea is to define a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w), where h is noise, and to learn w by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) between the empirical conditional distribution P(Y|X) and the model’s conditional P̂(Y|X). CMMD is defined as the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X estimated from data and from generated samples; this yields a training objective expressible in terms of kernel Gram matrices over inputs and outputs. Optimization is done by backprop, with minibatch estimates and regularization. Applications include: (i) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN; (ii) conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces; and (iii) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (Bayesian “dark knowledge”) on Boston Housing.

The work builds on: kernel mean embeddings and MMD (Gretton et al.), conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., Fukumizu et al., Grunewälder et al.), deep generative moment matching (GMMN; Li et al.), and conditional deep generators such as cGAN (Mirza & Osindero) and CVAE (Sohn et al.). The contribution is to replace MMD with a conditional operator distance and to couple it with a deep conditional generator trained via SGD.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMN to conditional modeling using well-established conditional mean embedding theory; the CMMD objective is simple and differentiable for the generator.
- Avoids adversarial min–max optimization; training reduces to backprop through a single objective with stable gradients.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Theoretical grounding: the operator equality CY|X = CZ|X is a sufficient condition for equality of conditional distributions under standard RKHS assumptions.
- Practical algorithm with minibatch training and flexibility in choosing kernels for x and y.

Weaknesses:
- Originality is modest: extending MMD to conditional settings via conditional embeddings is a fairly direct application of known theory; Theorem 3 is essentially a corollary of existing results.
- Empirical evaluation lacks critical baselines and rigor:
  - For classification, there is no comparison to the same architectures trained with standard cross-entropy; hence the value of CMMD over straightforward discriminative training is unclear.
  - Generation results are qualitative; no quantitative metrics (e.g., FID/Inception for images) or user studies; nearest-neighbor visual checks are weak.
  - Bayesian distillation is demonstrated on a single small dataset with marginal gains; no calibration or uncertainty evaluation.
- Important details are missing/under-specified: kernel choices for x and y, bandwidth/regularization selection, normalization, and sensitivity analyses. Since CMMD critically depends on kernels, this limits reproducibility and understanding.
- The training objective, when using the same x’s for real and generated pairs (as suggested), largely enforces matching of the conditional at training x’s; generalization to unseen x depends entirely on the network and is not analyzed. Perturbation is briefly mentioned but not systematically studied.
- Computational aspects: each minibatch requires inverting (K + λI); while done per minibatch, complexity and scaling behavior (batch sizes vs speed/accuracy) are not discussed or compared to adversarial/likelihood-based methods.
- Some claims (e.g., “competitive” prediction) lag behind strong discriminative baselines and are not clearly attributable to the proposed method versus architecture/capacity.

Quality:
- Technically sound in leveraging conditional mean embedding theory; assumptions are acknowledged. However, proofs are deferred/standard and the novelty is primarily in combining CMMD with a deep conditional generator.
- Experimental methodology is incomplete: missing key baselines (cross-entropy), ablations (kernel, λ, noise dimension), and quantitative measures for generation and uncertainty.

Clarity:
- Generally readable and well-organized. The CMMD derivation and operator-based view are clear.
- Missing implementation details on kernel choices, hyperparameter selection, and computational costs hinder reproducibility.
- Some notation frictions (e.g., reuse of d/s superscripts) and small typos, but nothing fatal.

Originality:
- Incremental over GMMN and conditional mean embeddings; comparable in spirit to cGAN and CVAE but with an MMD-style objective conditioned via operator embeddings. Novelty is moderate.

Significance:
- Potentially useful as a non-adversarial conditional generator; could be attractive for tasks sensitive to stability.
- Current experiments do not convincingly show a clear advantage over established alternatives (cGAN/CVAE for generation; cross-entropy for classification; temperature scaling/ensembles for uncertainty).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clean, theoretically justified extension of GMMN to conditional settings.
- Simple training procedure; practical and flexible.
- Diverse applications demonstrating generality.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty beyond established kernel embedding machinery.
- Insufficient empirical rigor and missing baselines; unclear benefits over standard training.
- Weak quantitative evaluation of generation and uncertainty.
- Scalability and kernel/hyperparameter sensitivity not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add baselines with identical architectures trained by cross-entropy (classification) and by cGAN/CVAE (generation), including quantitative metrics (FID/Inception for images; calibration/Brier score/NLL for uncertainty).
- Provide detailed kernel choices and selection procedures; include ablations on bandwidth, regularization λ, and noise dimension h.
- Analyze the impact of using the same vs perturbed x’s in CMMD; study generalization to unseen x and sample complexity.
- Report computational cost per epoch and compare to GAN/CVAE training.
- Include larger-scale datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) and more robust Bayesian distillation experiments (e.g., heteroscedastic regression, OOD calibration).
- Discuss consistency/identifiability conditions and practical heuristics for kernel selection.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper presents a neat and potentially useful idea, but the incremental nature of the contribution and the gaps in experimental rigor and baselines make it below the NeurIPS bar in its current form. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying implementation details could make this a solid contribution.
