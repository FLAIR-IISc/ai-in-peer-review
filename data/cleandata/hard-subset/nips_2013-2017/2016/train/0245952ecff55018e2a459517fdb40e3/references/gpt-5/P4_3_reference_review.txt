Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework to learn conditional distributions p(y|x) by training a deep conditional generator with a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective derived from kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. The generator takes both the conditioning variables x and a noise vector h, and outputs y = f(x, h; w). Training minimizes the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between empirical conditional embedding operators estimated from data and from model-generated samples, yielding a closed-form objective in terms of kernel Gram matrices and their regularized inverses. The model is trained via backpropagation, with mini-batch estimates to manage the cubic cost in batch size.

This work extends Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN; Li et al., ICML’15) from unconditional to conditional settings by leveraging conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., ICML’09; Grunewalder et al., ICML’12). It positions CGMMN as a simpler alternative to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional variational autoencoders (Sohn et al., NIPS’15), avoiding adversarial min–max difficulties and variational bounds. Applications demonstrated include predictive modeling (classification), conditional generation (MNIST digits and Yale faces), and distillation of Bayesian predictive distributions (Bayesian dark knowledge; Korattikara et al., NIPS’15) from a PBP teacher (Hernández-Lobato & Adams, ICML’15).

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A principled conditional analogue of MMD based on conditional mean embeddings, with a clear operator-level objective and a practical empirical estimator using Gram matrices.
- Simplicity and implementability: The CMMD objective is differentiable w.r.t. model outputs, enabling end-to-end backprop; inverses depend only on x-kernels, not on model parameters.
- Generality: The same training principle applies to classification, regression, and conditional image synthesis, and to distilling Bayesian predictive distributions.
- Technical correctness (mostly): The expansion of the empirical CMMD objective and its mini-batch application are consistent with standard RKHS operator norms and conditional embedding estimators (Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤).
- Empirical breadth: Results on MNIST and SVHN classification, MNIST/Yale conditional generation (with a GMMN+AE variant), and a Bayesian distillation experiment (Boston Housing) show the method is competitive and viable.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Theorem 3 assumptions: As stated, it assumes universality of the RKHS on X (F), but the claim that µY|x equality implies PY|x equality requires a characteristic/universal kernel on Y (G), not on X. The sufficient condition should require G to be characteristic on Y for each fixed x, along with CY|X = CZ|X. Please correct the statement and assumptions.
- Missing implementation details crucial for reproducibility:
  - Choice of output-space kernels (G) across tasks: classification (discrete one-hot labels), regression (real-valued y), and high-dimensional images. Specify kernel types (e.g., Gaussian/RBF), bandwidth selection (median heuristic or cross-validation), and any normalization.
  - Input-space kernels (F) on x, especially for images; whether linear kernels were used (as implied in some prior GMMN work) or RBFs; how bandwidths were set; and the effect on training stability.
  - Regularization λ and batch sizes’ effect on the stability and bias of the CMMD estimator; ablation/sensitivity is absent.
- Evaluation gaps:
  - Conditional generation is evaluated qualitatively; there are no quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen window log-likelihood on codes, FID/Inception-like metrics, or conditional classifier scores) nor direct comparisons to conditional GANs/CVAEs.
  - For classification, CGMMN is compared to strong baselines but does not compare to the same architectures trained with standard cross-entropy, making it hard to separate the contribution of CMMD from the backbone architecture.
  - Bayesian distillation is limited to a single small dataset (Boston Housing) and reports only RMSE, not predictive log-likelihood or calibration (which are central for Bayesian models).
- Computational considerations: CMMD involves O(B^3) inversion per mini-batch; no timing or memory benchmarks are provided, nor strategies like low-rank approximations or random feature approximations.
- Data usage and generalization: The generator is trained with x taken from the training set (possibly with small perturbations), which can implicitly regularize the conditional operator but may limit coverage; discussion of generalization to out-of-support x is minimal.
- Clarity and presentation: Several typos, inconsistent notation (e.g., Cs vs Cd superscripts), and incomplete references; key choices relegated to appendices hinder self-contained understanding.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please correct Theorem 3 to require a characteristic/universal kernel on Y (G) and clarify all assumptions (e.g., domains, measurability, boundedness).
- Provide full details on kernels for x and y in each experiment, bandwidth selection, and λ. Include sensitivity analyses for these hyperparameters and batch size.
- Add quantitative metrics for conditional generation and direct comparisons to conditional GANs and CVAEs on MNIST (and ideally Yale or another dataset).
- For classification, compare to the same CNN/MLP trained with cross-entropy; report calibration metrics or negative log-likelihood if possible.
- For Bayesian distillation, evaluate on additional datasets and report NLL and calibration (e.g., reliability diagrams) to assess preservation of uncertainty.
- Discuss computational cost per iteration, scaling with batch size, and potential accelerations (e.g., Nyström, random Fourier features, or shared inverse across steps if x-batches repeat).

Arguments for acceptance
- Introduces a principled and broadly applicable CMMD objective extending GMMN to conditional settings.
- Simple, backprop-friendly training without adversarial optimization or variational inference.
- Demonstrates versatility across prediction, generation, and Bayesian distillation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key theoretical assumption in Theorem 3 is misstated; needs correction.
- Empirical evaluation lacks quantitative generation metrics and direct comparisons to established conditional generative models.
- Missing implementation details and ablations limit reproducibility and understanding of when CMMD works best.
- Improvements over strong baselines are modest; significance would be stronger with clearer advantages.

Ratings
- Quality: 6/10 (sound construction overall, but theorem assumption issue and limited evaluation depth)
- Clarity: 6/10 (good high-level exposition; needs corrections and more implementation detail)
- Originality: 6.5/10 (natural but nontrivial extension of GMMN via conditional embeddings; distinguishes from cGAN/CVAE)
- Significance: 6/10 (potentially useful general tool; impact would be higher with stronger empirical evidence and comparisons)

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is clean and potentially impactful, but the paper should correct Theorem 3, strengthen empirical comparisons (especially to conditional GANs/CVAEs), and add essential implementation details and ablations.
