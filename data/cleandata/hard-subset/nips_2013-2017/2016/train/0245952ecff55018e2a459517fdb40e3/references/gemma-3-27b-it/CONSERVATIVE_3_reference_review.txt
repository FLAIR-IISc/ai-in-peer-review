This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the GMMN framework to handle conditional distributions. The core idea is to leverage the simplicity of moment matching (via MMD) for conditional modeling, offering an alternative to GANs and variational methods. While the motivation is reasonable – conditional generation is a common and important task – and the theoretical groundwork presented is sound, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant advancement over established methods.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical progression from background to method to experiments. The mathematical derivations, particularly regarding the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), are presented with sufficient detail and clarity. The authors adequately reference related work, acknowledging the existing landscape of generative models and moment matching techniques. However, the experimental results, while demonstrating competitive performance, do not showcase a clear advantage over existing, well-established approaches. The improvements, where observed, are often marginal and do not justify the introduction of a new framework. 

The primary weakness lies in the lack of a truly compelling demonstration of the benefits of CGMMN. The experiments cover a range of tasks (predictive modeling, contextual generation, Bayesian dark knowledge distillation), which is commendable, but the performance gains are not substantial enough to warrant a strong recommendation. For instance, in the MNIST and SVHN predictive tasks, CGMMN-CNN performs comparably to other deep learning models, but falls short of state-of-the-art results achieved by methods like DSN, which have been thoroughly vetted. The generative experiments, while visually appealing, lack a quantitative comparison to other generative models (e.g., GANs, VAEs) using standard metrics like Inception Score or FID. Similarly, the dark knowledge distillation experiment provides only a limited comparison, lacking statistical significance assessment or ablation studies to isolate the contribution of CGMMN. 

Furthermore, the paper doesn't fully address potential limitations. The choice of kernel function and its impact on performance is briefly mentioned but not thoroughly explored. The sensitivity of CMMD to the regularization parameter λ isn't comprehensively investigated. The authors claim the simplicity of the training process, but don’t provide detailed computational costs for the proposed method compared to GANs or variational approaches. The mini-batch algorithm described in the appendix is a practical consideration, but a more detailed analysis of its convergence properties would be beneficial. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretically sound approach extending GMMN to conditional distributions.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on several tasks.
*   Potentially simpler training compared to GANs (though not definitively proven).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of significant performance improvement over established methods.
*   Limited quantitative evaluation of generative performance.
*   Insufficient analysis of the method's limitations and sensitivity.
*   The contributions feel incremental, rather than groundbreaking.
*   The experiments do not provide strong evidence of a clear advantage over existing techniques in any of the tested scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution but currently falls short of the bar for acceptance at NIPS. While the theoretical foundation is strong and the presentation is clear, the lack of compelling experimental results and thorough analysis weakens the overall impact. The paper reads more like a proof-of-concept than a breakthrough. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above – including more rigorous comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, a more in-depth analysis of the method’s limitations, and a clearer demonstration of its benefits – is needed before this work could be considered for publication. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit.
