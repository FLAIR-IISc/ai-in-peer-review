Summary
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to learn conditional distributions p(y|x). The core idea is to train a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w), with h drawn from a simple prior, by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) between the empirical conditional mean embedding of the data and that of the model. The conditional mean embedding operator is estimated as Φ (K + λI)^{-1} Υ^T, and the CMMD objective reduces to Hilbert–Schmidt norms that can be computed via Gram matrices on X and Y. Training uses backpropagation with mini-batches, no adversarial min-max. Experiments include (i) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN (y are 10D label vectors given images), (ii) conditional image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces (y are images given labels), and (iii) distilling a Bayesian model’s predictive distribution (Boston Housing), showing roughly comparable performance to baselines.

Relation to prior work
- Extends GMMN (Li et al., ICML’15; Dziugaite et al., UAI’15) to the conditional setting using kernel conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., 2009; Grunewalder et al., 2012). The objective is a natural operator-norm analogue of MMD.
- Related to CGAN (Mirza & Osindero) and CVAE (Sohn et al.), which are strong baselines for conditional generation; these are cited but not compared experimentally.
- Related to Bayesian dark knowledge (Korattikara et al.), but uses CMMD in place of KL/temperature matching.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of MMD/GMMN to conditional distributions via conditional mean embeddings.
- Simple, unified objective amenable to backprop; avoids adversarial training instabilities.
- Architecture-agnostic; applicable with MLPs or CNNs.
- The observation that K-inverses depend only on X (and are independent of generator parameters) is useful; when using the same X for both data and model samples per batch, K_s = K_d, simplifying computation.
- Reasonable predictive results (MNIST, SVHN) relative to other DGMs; the Bayesian distillation result indicates feasibility without significant loss.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical rigor and clarity
- Assumptions for Theorem 3 are incomplete/possibly misstated. To infer equality of conditional distributions from equal operators, a characteristic kernel on Y (ensuring injective mean embeddings) is needed; the paper emphasizes universality of F (on X) but not characteristicness on G (Y).
- Notation is confusing and inconsistent: φ is used for both X and Y; F and G are swapped in places; Eq. (1) formatting is broken; the operator mapping spaces are not always clear (C_YX : G → F vs. tensor product statements).
- The matrix form of the CMMD objective (Eq. 2) is plausible, but dimensions and symmetry assumptions should be stated explicitly. The “Remark 1” rewriting of MMD using Tr(L·1) is imprecise (it should be 1^T L 1 or Tr(L 11^T)); please correct this to avoid ambiguity.
- Requirements for the existence of the CMMD objective (e.g., C^{-3/2}_{XX} C_{XY} Hilbert–Schmidt) are mentioned but not discussed in practical terms.

Missing essential details (reproducibility)
- Choice of kernels for X and Y, bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic, multi-kernel MMD), and regularization λ are not specified for any experiment. This is critical for MMD-based methods.
- For classification, the label representation and kernel on Y are unclear. If using one-hot labels with Gaussian kernels, behavior depends strongly on bandwidth; if using soft labels, how are they obtained from training data?
- Optimization details (Adam settings, learning rate schedules, weight decay, early stopping) and architectural minutiae (initialization, normalization) are lacking.
- For conditional generation with finite X (labels), the paper states using C_YX and C_XX^{-1} “directly,” but provides no concrete procedure or regularization choices (especially important when classes are imbalanced).

Experimental evaluation
- No comparison to CVAE/CGAN (or GMMN+AE baselines) for conditional generation; results are only qualitative with no quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen NLL, Inception score, FID, or classifier-based accuracy on generated images). The nearest-neighbor check in pixel space is weak and known to be unreliable.
- The predictive experiments lack ablations on kernels, batch size, λ, hidden noise dimensionality, and whether using sampling vs. deterministic outputs matters (why sample y and then take argmax rather than use expected y?).
- Scalability is not addressed: per-step costs are O(B^2) memory, O(B^3) time for K^{-1} per batch. If K_s = K_d (same X), you could precompute inverses per batch; this simplification should be exploited and analyzed. No wall-clock or memory comparisons are given.
- The SVHN VA baseline (25.3% error) seems implausible; please verify. MNIST classification Table 1 is missing in the provided text; ensure numbers are correct and clearly attributed.
- The Bayesian distillation experiment (Boston Housing) lacks details on splits, number of trials, student architecture, input perturbation scale, and kernel choices. A single small dataset is insufficient to substantiate the claim.

Writing, formatting, and references
- Numerous typos and formatting issues: “adverisarial,” “convoluational,” “Mathmetical,” “Kallenbery,” dangling “∗Corresponding author,” stray line “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)”. Reference entries are incomplete (years/pages missing), inconsistent capitalization, and several arXiv citations are unformatted.
- Some figure/table references are not self-contained; heavy reliance on appendices for core methodology details further hinders clarity.

Pros for acceptance
- Natural, principled extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with a clear objective.
- Avoids adversarial training; simple to implement in modern autodiff frameworks.
- Demonstrates applicability across tasks (classification, generation, distillation).

Cons for acceptance
- Key experimental and methodological details (kernels, λ, bandwidths) are missing, limiting reproducibility and interpretability.
- No direct experimental comparison to strong conditional generative baselines (CVAE/CGAN), and no quantitative evaluation of generation quality.
- Theoretical assumptions are not fully or correctly specified; notation and equation clarity need significant revision.
- Scalability and computational complexity are insufficiently analyzed.
- Numerous typos, formatting, and citation issues indicative of a rushed submission.

Questions for the authors
1. What kernel families and bandwidth selection strategies were used for X and Y in each experiment? Did you use multi-kernel MMD?
2. For classification, what is the training target on Y (one-hot vs. soft labels)? Why sample y rather than use a deterministic output (e.g., mean prediction)?
3. Do you exploit that K_s = K_d per mini-batch to reuse inverses? What are typical batch sizes, and what is the per-epoch runtime vs. baselines?
4. Can you provide quantitative generation metrics and comparisons to CVAE/CGAN?
5. Please clarify the exact assumptions in Theorem 3 (characteristic kernels on Y) and fix notation inconsistencies.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the paper, in its current form, lacks essential methodological details, theoretical precision, and rigorous experimental comparisons/metrics. A thorough revision addressing kernel choices and assumptions, adding strong conditional generation baselines with quantitative metrics, analyzing scalability, and fixing clarity/formatting would substantially strengthen the submission.
