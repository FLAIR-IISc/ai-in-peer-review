

# Peer Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extends the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions. GMMN, as proposed by Li et al. (2015), uses Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to match the moments of a generative model with the data distribution, avoiding the challenging min-max optimization of GANs. The authors extend this to the conditional case by developing Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), a criterion to measure the difference between conditional distributions. CGMMN generates samples by passing both the conditional variables and random noise through a deep network, and trains using CMMD as the objective function. The paper evaluates CGMMN on three tasks: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on labels), and distilling Bayesian models (Bayesian dark knowledge). The results demonstrate competitive performance across these diverse applications.

The paper builds on the strong theoretical foundation of kernel embedding and MMD, extending it to the conditional setting. It relates to prior work on conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), and Bayesian dark knowledge (Korattikara et al., 2015), but provides a novel moment-matching approach to conditional generative modeling.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the CMMD criterion, building logically on the kernel embedding of conditional distributions. The derivation of Theorem 3 is clear and well-supported.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper evaluates CGMMN across three distinct application domains (predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation), demonstrating the framework's versatility. This breadth of evaluation is impressive and shows the method's practical utility.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide clear implementation details (network architectures, hyperparameters, training procedures) that would allow for reproduction of their results. The simplicity of the training process (backpropagation-based optimization) is a significant advantage over GANs.

4. **Clear Motivation**: The introduction effectively justifies the need for conditional generative models across multiple application domains, making a strong case for the paper's contribution.

5. **Good Experimental Design**: The experiments include appropriate baselines and address potential concerns (e.g., showing generated samples aren't just copies of training data).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty Compared to Conditional GANs**: While the paper correctly cites conditional GANs as related work, it doesn't sufficiently differentiate CGMMN from these approaches. Conditional GANs have been widely adopted and have many successful variants (AC-GAN, InfoGAN, etc.). The paper should better articulate why CMMD is preferable to these methods in the conditional setting.

2. **Competitive Results, Not Superior**: In the classification experiments (Tables 1 and 2), CGMMN-CNN achieves competitive but not superior results compared to the state-of-the-art (DSN achieves the best results on both MNIST and SVHN). The paper claims "competitive" performance but doesn't clearly demonstrate when CGMMN outperforms alternatives.

3. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't thoroughly address the computational complexity of CMMD compared to other methods. The matrix inversion operations required for CMMD (K̃⁻¹s and K̃⁻¹d) can be expensive for large datasets, which might limit the method's applicability to very large-scale problems.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper could better discuss the limitations of CGMMN. For instance:
   - How does the method scale with the number of conditional classes?
   - What are the limitations when dealing with high-dimensional conditional variables?
   - How does the dimensionality of the noise input affect performance?

5. **Comparison to More Recent Methods**: The paper doesn't compare to more recent conditional generative models beyond the standard baselines. For example, it doesn't compare to conditional diffusion models or more advanced GAN variants that have emerged since the cited works.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound, with well-justified claims supported by experimental results. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the implementation details are thorough. The work is complete rather than a position paper, with a clear method, implementation, and evaluation. The authors are generally honest about the strengths and limitations of their approach.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly states the motivation, and the preliminary section provides necessary background without being overly verbose. The experimental results are presented clearly with appropriate visualizations. However, some sections (particularly the CMMD derivation in Section 3.1) could benefit from additional intuitive explanations to make the content more accessible to readers less familiar with kernel methods.

## Originality

The paper's main contribution is the extension of GMMN to the conditional case via CMMD. While this is a natural extension, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate this from existing conditional generative models. The novelty is somewhat incremental, as the core idea is applying an existing technique (MMD) to a new setting (conditional distributions). The paper could better highlight how CGMMN differs from and improves upon existing conditional generative models.

## Significance

The significance of this work lies in providing a simple, backpropagation-friendly approach to conditional generative modeling that avoids the min-max optimization challenges of GANs. The broad applicability across multiple tasks (classification, generation, Bayesian distillation) demonstrates the framework's utility. However, the paper doesn't convincingly show that CGMMN advances the state-of-the-art in any single task beyond what's already achievable with existing methods.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a well-motivated extension of an existing framework with solid theoretical grounding and practical evaluation across multiple domains. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the work is technically sound and contributes to the growing literature on moment-matching generative models.

## Specific Suggestions for Revision

1. **Strengthen the comparison to conditional GANs**: Provide a more detailed discussion of why CMMD might be preferable to conditional GANs in specific scenarios (e.g., when training stability is a concern).

2. **Clarify the performance advantage**: In the classification experiments, provide more detailed analysis of where CGMMN excels compared to the best baselines, rather than just stating it's "competitive."

3. **Address computational complexity**: Discuss the computational cost of CMMD, particularly for larger datasets, and compare it to the computational cost of training conditional GANs.

4. **Expand the discussion of limitations**: Add a subsection discussing the limitations of CGMMN, particularly regarding scalability to large numbers of conditional classes and high-dimensional conditional variables.

5. **Update related work**: Include a brief comparison to more recent conditional generative models (e.g., conditional diffusion models) to better situate the work in the current literature.

## Final Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on generative models by extending moment-matching to the conditional case. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the work is technically sound, well-evaluated, and has broad applicability. With the suggested revisions to strengthen the comparison to related work and clarify the advantages of the approach, this would be a strong NIPS paper. The paper is well-suited for the conference as it addresses a practical problem with a clear, theoretically-grounded solution that advances the field of generative modeling.

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions)
