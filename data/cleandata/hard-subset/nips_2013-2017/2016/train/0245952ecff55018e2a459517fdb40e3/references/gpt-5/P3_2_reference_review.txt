Summary
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to conditional distributions p(y|x) by introducing Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). The key idea is to embed conditional distributions as linear operators CY|X in a tensor RKHS via kernel conditional mean embeddings, and to measure discrepancy between conditional distributions as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between empirical operators estimated from samples. A conditional generator y = f(x, h; w) maps input x and exogenous noise h through a deep net; parameters are learned by minimizing CMMD between empirical conditionals from data and the generator. The authors present CNN/MLP instantiations and evaluate on classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST digits, Yale Faces), and “Bayesian dark knowledge” distillation from PBP on Boston Housing.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on GMMN (Li et al., Dziugaite et al.) and kernel mean embeddings (Song, Gretton, Fukumizu). The conditional embedding operator CY|X is standard; leveraging its empirical estimator Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ is classical.
- Compared to GANs and cGANs, CGMMN replaces min–max with a single CMMD objective, similar in spirit to GMMN’s stability benefits.
- Related to Conditional VAE and conditional SPNs; the paper positions itself appropriately but lacks direct empirical comparisons to cGAN/CVAE on generation tasks.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a natural, principled extension of MMD training to conditional distributions via operator embeddings.
- Training simplicity and stability: backprop on a single objective; inverse Gram matrices are independent of w, so kernel choice on x need not be differentiable.
- Versatility: applied to classification, conditional image generation, and Bayesian model distillation with reasonable results.
- Insightful connection between MMD and CMMD as uniform vs non-uniform weighting induced by x, clarifying when conditionalization matters.
- Practical recipe for conditional generation with exogenous noise and variants (concatenation vs corruption), plus minibatch training.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: CMMD as operator distance follows directly from the conditional mean embedding framework; the main contribution is its use as a training loss for deep conditional generators. Prior work in kernel two-sample testing for conditional distributions and conditional MMD in domain adaptation contexts should be cited and contrasted.
- Limited theoretical guarantees: While Theorem 3 is correct as a sufficiency statement under standard assumptions, there is no generalization analysis linking minimization of empirical CMMD (with regularization) to convergence of pθ(y|x) to p(y|x), nor discussion of estimator bias due to λ and finite samples.
- Scalability concerns: CMMD requires inverting K+λI per batch (O(B^3)). The paper notes minibatching and that K inverses do not depend on w, but when x is perturbed during training, K changes across batches. The empirical cost and memory footprint, and any caching strategy (e.g., using the same x for s and d to reuse K) should be reported.
- Sensitivity to kernels and bandwidths: Performance of (C)MMD is notoriously sensitive to kernel choice, especially in high-dimensional outputs (images). The paper does not detail kernel choices, bandwidth selection procedures, or ablations.
- Empirical scope: 
  - Classification: CGMMN-CNN is competitive but does not outperform strong discriminative models; the benefit of modeling p(y|x) via CMMD over cross-entropy remains unclear.
  - Generation: Results are visually plausible on MNIST and Yale; however, there is no quantitative evaluation or comparison to cGAN/CVAE. The GMMN+AE variant is acknowledged (and used) to improve sample smoothness, but comparative baselines are missing.
  - Distillation: Only a small UCI dataset is shown; more tasks (including classification) and runtime comparisons versus teacher would strengthen claims.

Quality
Technically sound overall; CMMD derivations follow standard kernel machinery, and the operator estimator is well-known. Some details are omitted or deferred to the appendix (e.g., finite X case, gradient expressions), and the estimator’s statistical properties in the conditional setting aren’t analyzed. Experimental methodology is generally clear, but lacks ablations and critical comparisons (cGAN/CVAE).

Clarity
The paper is mostly well-written and organized. Suggestions:
- Clarify when Kd = Ks (i.e., using the same x for data and generated pairs) to avoid recomputing inverses; state computational complexity and wall-clock times.
- Specify kernel types and hyperparameter selection for both x and y across tasks.
- Be precise about the MMD estimator used (biased vs unbiased) and its properties.
- Provide proof sketches or citations for Theorem 3; note the assumptions (e.g., universality, regularity) more explicitly in the main text.

Originality
Moderate. The step from MMD to CMMD via conditional mean embeddings is natural; the contribution is to instantiate this as a general-purpose conditional deep generator training objective. The application to Bayesian distillation is interesting but incremental.

Significance
Moderate. For practitioners seeking a stable alternative to adversarial training in conditional settings, CGMMN is appealing. However, without stronger empirical evidence (particularly against cGAN/CVAE) or theoretical guarantees, impact may be limited. The method could spur follow-up on scalable CMMD (e.g., Nyström/RFF approximations, inducing points) and on principled kernel selection.

Questions for authors
- How are kernel bandwidths chosen for x and y? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Do you reuse the same x for s and d, making Kd = Ks and enabling precomputation of K̃−1? If you perturb x, how do you manage computational cost?
- Can you report training time vs cGAN/CVAE baselines?
- Do you observe mode collapse or mode dropping in conditional generation? Any diagnostics beyond nearest neighbors?
- Could you compare against cGAN/CVAE on MNIST/Yale and include a quantitative metric?

Pros
- Simple, stable training objective for conditional generative modeling.
- Solid grounding in kernel conditional embeddings.
- Versatile applications; competitive classification and plausible generation.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond applying known operator embeddings to deep conditional generators.
- Missing critical empirical comparisons and ablations; unclear scalability and kernel selection.
- No generalization/consistency analysis for empirical CMMD training.
