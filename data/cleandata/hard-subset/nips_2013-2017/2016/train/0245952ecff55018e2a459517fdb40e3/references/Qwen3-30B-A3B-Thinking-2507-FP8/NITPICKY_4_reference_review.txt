

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions. The authors propose a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to train a deep generative model that can produce samples conditioned on input variables. The paper demonstrates CGMMN on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation tasks, showing competitive results.

## Strengths
The paper presents a natural extension of GMMN to conditional settings, which is an important direction for generative modeling. The experimental results on MNIST, SVHN, and face generation are generally promising, and the Bayesian dark knowledge distillation application is novel. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for CMMD based on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Theoretical Issues
1. The paper claims that "CY|X = CZ|X implies PY|X = PZ|X" (Theorem 3), but provides no proof or citation for this critical result. The authors reference [29] for Theorem 2, but Theorem 3 appears to be a new contribution without sufficient justification. Without a proof, it's impossible to verify this fundamental claim.

2. The paper states "the empirical estimator Φ(K+λI)⁻¹Υ⊤ is always an element in the tensor product space which gives as a well-justified approximation" (Section 3.1), but provides no reference to the specific part of [29] that supports this claim. This is a significant theoretical gap.

3. In Section 4.1.1, the paper claims "CGMMN-CNN is competitive with various state-of-the-art competitors" but provides no statistical significance testing for the reported error rates. Without this, it's impossible to determine if the performance differences are meaningful.

4. The paper states "we did not observe much difference with a higher dimension (e.g., 20 or 50)" (Section 4.1.1) but doesn't provide any results or analysis for these higher-dimensional cases, making this claim unsubstantiated.

### Experimental and Methodological Issues
1. The paper refers to "Appendix A.2.2" for details on estimating C⁻¹XX and CYX, but the appendix is not included in the submission. This makes it impossible to verify the practical implementation details.

2. In Table 1, the paper claims "DSN benefits from using more supervision signal in every hidden layer and outperforms the other competitors," but doesn't provide sufficient explanation for why this is the case or how it relates to CGMMN.

3. The paper states "we are not restricted to use differentiable kernels on the conditional variables x" (Section 3.2) but fails to explain why this is an advantage or how it affects model performance.

4. The paper mentions "AdaM" (Section 4.1.1) which is a typo for "Adam" (Adaptive Moment Estimation), a commonly used optimizer.

5. The paper claims "the total number of parameters in the network is comparable with the competitors" (Section 4.1.1) but provides no actual parameter counts for comparison.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
1. The reference "Generative adverisarial nets" (Reference 5) contains a typo ("adverisarial" instead of "adversarial").

2. The reference "Pegasos: Primal estimated sub-gradient solver for svm" (Reference 26) has inconsistent capitalization ("svm" should be "SVM").

3. The paper uses inconsistent capitalization of "NIPS" (sometimes as "NIPS", sometimes as "Neural Information Processing Systems").

4. The paper claims "we can easily draw a set of samples to estimate the kernel mean embedding" (Section 3.1) but doesn't specify how many samples are needed for accurate estimation.

5. The paper states "we are expected to get better results with more sophisticated structures" (Section 4.1.1) but provides no evidence for this claim.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a novel extension of GMMN to conditional settings and demonstrates promising results on several tasks, it suffers from multiple substantial technical and methodological weaknesses. The lack of theoretical justification for the key Theorem 3, the missing details in the appendices, and the absence of statistical significance testing for experimental results are particularly concerning. The paper also contains several formatting errors and typos that undermine its professionalism.

The authors make several claims without providing sufficient evidence or justification, including the theoretical foundation for CMMD, the parameter sensitivity analysis, and the comparison with other methods. These issues collectively prevent the paper from meeting the high standards required for publication at NIPS.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem and shows some promising results, the fundamental theoretical gaps and methodological shortcomings outweigh the potential contributions. The authors need to provide a rigorous proof for Theorem 3, include the missing appendices with implementation details, conduct statistical significance testing for all experimental results, and address the numerous formatting and technical errors before the paper can be considered for publication.
