**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional probability distributions `p(y|x)`. This work is a direct and significant extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which model joint distributions `p(x)`. The core contributions are twofold: 1) The proposal of a novel training objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). CMMD is defined as the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between the conditional mean embedding operators of the true and generated distributions. 2) A simple yet effective generator architecture that takes a conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to a deep neural network to produce a sample `y`.

The paper situates this work as an alternative to Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (cGANs), offering a simpler and more stable objective function that avoids the difficulties of min-max optimization, while retaining the modeling power of deep networks. The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN through a diverse set of experiments, including predictive modeling (image classification), contextual generation (generating images from labels), and a novel application in distilling knowledge from complex Bayesian models ("Bayesian dark knowledge").

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Novel Objective Function:** The introduction of the Conditional MMD (CMMD) is the paper's main strength. It is a theoretically well-grounded extension of the MMD criterion to the conditional setting, building upon established work in kernel embeddings of conditional distributions [29]. This provides a solid foundation for the proposed method and represents a significant contribution to the family of moment-matching generative models.

2.  **Simplicity and Stability:** Compared to the adversarial training of GANs, the proposed CMMD objective is a single, direct optimization problem. This avoids the challenges of finding a stable Nash equilibrium in a min-max game, which often plagues GAN training. The entire model can be trained end-to-end with standard back-propagation, making it easy to implement and tune.

3.  **Broad Applicability and Strong Experimental Validation:** The authors showcase the model's flexibility on an impressive range of tasks.
    *   **Predictive Modeling:** The classification results on MNIST and SVHN are competitive with other state-of-the-art deep generative models (e.g., CMMVA), demonstrating that the framework can be used effectively for discriminative tasks.
    *   **Contextual Generation:** The ability to generate MNIST digits and Yale faces conditioned on labels is clearly demonstrated. The results are compelling and show that the model learns the conditional distributions correctly.
    *   **Bayesian Dark Knowledge:** This is a particularly novel and interesting application. The idea of using CGMMN to learn a fast "student" network that mimics the predictive distribution of a complex Bayesian "teacher" model is clever and highlights a unique strength of being able to match arbitrary conditional distributions.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. Section 2 provides an excellent and necessary background on kernel embeddings, making the subsequent introduction of CMMD in Section 3 clear and logical. The motivations are well-articulated, and the experimental setup is clearly described.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The CMMD objective in Equation (2) requires the inversion of kernel Gram matrices (`K+λI`). This operation has a computational complexity of O(B^3) and a memory complexity of O(B^2), where B is the mini-batch size. While the paper proposes a mini-batch algorithm, this cubic complexity is a significant practical limitation that restricts the use of large batch sizes, which are often beneficial for training deep networks. The paper should discuss this limitation more explicitly and perhaps compare the training time/cost against alternatives like cGANs or conditional VAEs.

2.  **Generated Sample Quality:** While the generated samples are recognizable, they exhibit noticeable noise and blurriness (e.g., Figure 2), a common characteristic of MMD-based generative models. The authors acknowledge this and propose using an autoencoder as a feature extractor to generate cleaner images (Figure 4). While this is a good practical solution, it complicates the framework and relies on a separate, pre-trained model. A discussion on why the base CMMD objective might struggle with generating high-fidelity samples compared to adversarial objectives would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Lack of Direct Comparison with cGANs:** Given that cGANs [21] are the most direct and popular competitor for this task, the lack of a direct experimental comparison is a notable omission. It would be highly valuable to see a side-by-side comparison on a generation task (e.g., MNIST generation) to empirically validate the claims of competitive performance and simpler training.

4.  **Details on Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of any kernel-based method, including CMMD, depends critically on the choice of kernels and their parameters (e.g., bandwidth), as well as the regularization term `λ`. The paper provides limited details on how these were chosen or how sensitive the model is to these choices. A brief sensitivity analysis or more detailed discussion in the appendix would improve the paper's reproducibility and practical value.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **Remark 1:** The connection drawn between MMD and CMMD in Remark 1 is insightful. It clearly explains that CMMD re-weights the sample differences based on the conditional variables, providing a nice intuition for how the conditioning is incorporated.
*   **Finite Domain of X:** The paper mentions that for finite `x` (like class labels), `C_{Y|X}` can be estimated differently. It would be beneficial to elaborate on this in the main text, as it's a common and important special case. (The appendix is mentioned, but a sentence of intuition in the main text would help).

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear and significant contribution. The CMMD objective is novel, theoretically sound, and offers a compelling alternative to adversarial training for conditional generative modeling. The broad experimental validation demonstrates its practical utility. While there are weaknesses concerning computational scalability and a lack of direct comparison to cGANs, the strengths of the core contribution are substantial. This work fills an important gap in the literature by extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting, and it is likely to be influential and built upon by future research. I recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and well-motivated CMMD loss function.
*   Significantly extends the applicability of GMMNs to a wide range of conditional modeling tasks.
*   Offers a simpler and potentially more stable training paradigm than cGANs.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance across diverse and interesting applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Computational complexity (cubic in batch size) is a major practical drawback that is not sufficiently discussed.
*   Generated sample quality is modest without an auxiliary autoencoder.
*   Lacks a direct experimental comparison to the primary baseline, Conditional GANs.
