Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to model conditional distributions p(y|x). The key technical contribution is Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X estimated from data and from the generator. The generator concatenates x with noise h and passes them through a DNN to produce y; parameters are learned by minimizing CMMD via backpropagation. Theoretical grounding relies on kernel mean embeddings and the existence of a conditional embedding operator (Song et al., 2009), and on MMD-based training for generators (Dziugaite et al., 2015). The paper situates CGMMN relative to conditional GANs, conditional VAEs, and max-margin DGMs, and evaluates on classification (MNIST, SVHN), conditional image generation (MNIST, Yale Faces), and distilling Bayesian models (Boston Housing).

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity: natural extension of GMMN to conditional settings via operator-valued embeddings; training reduces to differentiating a closed-form CMMD objective.
- Solid foundations: builds on established theory of kernel mean embeddings and conditional operators; CMMD is a principled surrogate matching conditional distributions.
- Flexible architecture: compatible with MLP/CNN and alternative ways to combine x and noise; non-differentiable kernels allowed on x because K inverses do not depend on network parameters.
- Empirical coverage: demonstrates applicability to prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge; classification performance is competitive with prior DGM baselines (MMVA/CMMVA) and reasonable compared to strong discriminative CNNs.
- Practical training recipe: minibatch CMMD and straightforward sampling loop; the observation that inverses in CMMD do not depend on model parameters simplifies implementation.

Weaknesses

Quality (technical soundness and empirical support)
- CMMD objective: While Theorem 3 gives a sufficient condition (equality of operators implies equality of conditionals), there is no consistency result that minimizing empirical CMMD over a rich generator family recovers the true conditional under standard assumptions (e.g., with universal kernels and increasing data/capacity). Discussion of estimator bias/variance, regularization λ, and batch-based approximations is limited.
- Computational complexity: Each CMMD evaluation involves inverting (K+λI) on minibatches (O(b^3)). The paper mentions minibatching but does not quantify runtime, memory, or strategies to reuse inverses (e.g., when x in generated and data batches coincide, Kd=Ks). Scalability to large datasets or high-dimensional x is not analyzed.
- Kernel choices: The performance of CMMD critically depends on kernel selection and bandwidths on x and y. The main text does not specify kernels, bandwidth selection, or sensitivity analyses; details are deferred to the appendix. For discrete y (classification), the “finite domain” shortcut is mentioned but not fully specified in the main text.
- Empirical evaluation: 
  - Classification uses single-sample argmax from the generator; no evaluation of probabilistic quality (e.g., log-likelihood, calibration, Brier score), which would be natural for a conditional generative model.
  - Conditional generation is assessed qualitatively; no quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen estimates, Inception/FID where applicable) and no comparisons to CVAE/cGAN under similar architectures.
  - Distillation experiment is limited to one small regression dataset, without latency/throughput measurements to substantiate efficiency benefits, and without comparison to alternative student objectives.

Clarity
- Overall presentation is clear and well-organized with useful background on RKHS embeddings and conditional operators. However, there are typos and minor inconsistencies (e.g., “MINIST”, grammar). Important practical details (kernels, λ, bandwidth search, data perturbation schemes) are largely in the appendix; including concise summaries in the main text would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The idea to match conditional distributions via the RKHS conditional embedding operator is a natural extension of MMD-based training and of conditional mean embedding literature (Song et al., 2009; Grunewälder et al., 2012). Using the operator norm as a training loss for deep conditional generators appears novel, but the conceptual increment over GMMN is moderate.

Significance
- A simple, stable alternative to adversarial training for conditional generation and supervised tasks is valuable. However, the empirical evidence, while positive, does not demonstrate clear state-of-the-art results or distinctive advantages over CVAE/cGAN across tasks, and quantitative generative evaluation is limited. The potential impact is moderate unless stronger empirical results or scalability evidence are added.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled extension of GMMN to conditional settings using a well-motivated CMMD loss.
- Simple training procedure with broad applicability (prediction, conditional generation, Bayesian distillation).
- Competitive predictive accuracy with prior DGMs and reasonable qualitative generation.
- Theoretical grounding connects to established RKHS literature; derivations are sound at a high level.

Arguments against acceptance
- Novelty is incremental given existing conditional mean embedding theory and prior GMMN work.
- Limited quantitative evaluation for generation and lack of comparisons to cGAN/CVAE on conditional generation benchmarks.
- Missing analysis of kernel choices, sensitivity, and computational scalability; key implementation details are not sufficiently documented in the main text.
- The probabilistic quality of predictions is not evaluated; the classifier usage is ad-hoc (single-sample argmax).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative generative metrics and direct comparisons to CVAE/cGAN with matched architectures on MNIST/Yale or other conditional generation datasets.
- Report kernel choices, bandwidth selection procedures, λ, and sensitivity studies; analyze the effect of batch size and reusing K inverses.
- Provide runtime/memory benchmarks and discuss scalability; clarify when Kd=Ks can be exploited.
- Evaluate predictive uncertainty (e.g., calibration, NLL) and consider averaging multiple y samples at test time.
- Include more substantial distillation experiments, with efficiency metrics and comparisons to alternative student losses.

Overall, the paper presents a clear and useful extension of MMD-based generative modeling to conditional distributions with a simple training objective. The current empirical evidence and novelty level suggest a borderline case; strengthening quantitative evaluations and scalability analysis would significantly improve the contribution.
