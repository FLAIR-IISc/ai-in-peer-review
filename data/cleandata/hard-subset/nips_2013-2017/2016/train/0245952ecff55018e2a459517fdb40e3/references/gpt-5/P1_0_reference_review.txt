Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to model conditional distributions p(y|x) by minimizing a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) between the data conditional and a deep conditional generator. The generator takes the conditioning variable x and auxiliary noise h, passes them through a DNN, and outputs y = f(x,h; w). The key technical contribution is to define CMMD as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X, estimated from samples via kernel Gram matrices, with a regularized inverse (K+λI)−1. The loss (Eq. 2) depends only on kernels on X and Y; crucially, the inverses for X depend only on X and are independent of the generator parameters, enabling backpropagation through the L matrices for Y only.

This builds directly on GMMN (Dziugaite et al. 2015; Li et al. 2015) which uses MMD to match marginal distributions, and on kernel conditional mean embeddings (Song et al. 2009; Grunewälder et al. 2012). It is a kernel-based alternative to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al. 2015). The paper also draws a connection between CMMD and MMD: CMMD can be seen as a non-uniformly weighted MMD where the weights depend on x.

Experiments evaluate (i) predictive modeling by treating y as predicted label vector, (ii) contextual generation conditioned on class labels (MNIST, Yale Faces), and (iii) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (PBP teacher on Boston Housing). Results are competitive with max-margin DGMs (Li et al. 2015) on MNIST/SVHN classification, and generated samples are plausible; distillation preserves RMSE on Boston.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and simplicity: CMMD is a principled, natural extension of MMD to conditionals via operator embeddings, avoiding adversarial training instabilities.
- Generality: Works with arbitrary differentiable generators and kernels, and with discrete or continuous X (finite-X case handled via direct operator estimation).
- Practical training: Inverses depend on X only, simplifying gradients; minibatch SGD is straightforward. The approach is plug-and-play for many architectures (MLP/CNN).
- Breadth of applications: Demonstrates use for prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.
- Useful perspective: The “weighted MMD” view provides intuition for how conditioning reshapes the discrepancy.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical scope and metrics: Generation quality is assessed qualitatively and by nearest neighbors; no quantitative measures (e.g., Inception score; at least Parzen log-likelihood in 2016) or user studies. Comparisons to cGAN/CVAE on conditional generation are missing.
- Predictive performance: While competitive, classification results lag behind strong discriminative baselines (e.g., DSN) and do not clearly improve over CMMVA; MNIST table is incomplete in the manuscript.
- Scalability and complexity: CMMD requires Gram matrices and inverses per minibatch (O(m^3) time), and O(m^2) memory. There is no discussion of scaling to large batches or datasets beyond simple mini-batching; no exploration of low-rank or random Fourier feature approximations to avoid matrix inversions.
- Theoretical guarantees: Theorem 3 gives a sufficient condition (operator equality implies equality of conditionals) under strong assumptions. There is no analysis of estimator bias/consistency of the regularized empirical operator nor generalization bounds, and no guidance on λ or kernel hyperparameter selection beyond standard practice.
- Details missing or unclear: Exact kernels on X and Y, bandwidth selection, and sensitivity are not reported; gradient derivations are deferred; training stability versus GANs is not discussed; the effect of noise dimension and conditioning strategies (concatenate vs corruption) is not ablated.
- Presentation/formatting: Several typos and formatting issues (missing Table 1; inconsistent notation; incomplete references) hinder reproducibility. Some implementation choices (e.g., per-batch resampling strategy, how x is perturbed) should be made explicit.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the CMMD objective and its empirical estimator are correct and implementable. The operator-based derivation is appropriate, and the connection to MMD is insightful. However, the lack of quantitative generation metrics, incomplete baselines (cGAN/CVAE), and limited analysis of scalability and hyperparameters weaken the empirical foundation.

Clarity
Generally readable with a clear high-level story. Nonetheless, the paper would benefit from:
- Precisely specifying kernels on X and Y, bandwidth selection, and λ.
- Including full experimental tables (MNIST) and quantitative generation metrics.
- Adding gradient expressions wrt y and w in the appendix and a complexity discussion.
- Fixing typos and completing references.

Originality
Moderate. The work combines known ingredients—GMMN and conditional mean embeddings—to produce a conditional training criterion and deep conditional generator. To my knowledge, this particular instantiation (CMMD for training deep conditional generators) was not standard in prior work at the time and represents a meaningful methodological extension.

Significance
Moderate. A simple, stable alternative to cGAN/CVAE for conditional modeling is valuable. If scaled and better evaluated, CMMD-trained conditional generators could be widely useful for structured prediction and conditional generation. Current experiments show promise but stop short of establishing state-of-the-art performance or broad applicability.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- What kernels were used on X and Y in each experiment? How were bandwidths and λ chosen? Please report sensitivity.
- Can you compare against cGAN and CVAE on conditional generation (MNIST, Yale) with quantitative metrics?
- How does performance and training time scale with minibatch size? Could you use random Fourier features or Nyström approximations to avoid O(m^3) inversions?
- For prediction, why not average multiple y samples instead of single-sample argmax? Is the model calibrated?
- In the distillation experiment, please report inference speedup and calibration compared to the teacher, and consider larger datasets.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, principled extension of MMD training to conditional models.
- Avoids adversarial min–max; easy to implement and differentiate.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across tasks.
- Solid theoretical grounding via operator embeddings.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is limited; missing key baselines and quantitative generation metrics.
- Scalability and hyperparameter selection not deeply addressed.
- Writing and presentation issues; missing details hinder reproducibility.
- Gains over strong baselines are modest.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is elegant and potentially impactful, but the current empirical evidence and clarity are not yet at the level I would expect for NeurIPS. With stronger quantitative evaluations (including cGAN/CVAE baselines), scalability analysis, and clearer experimental details, I would be inclined to recommend acceptance.

Confidence
High: familiar with MMD/mean embeddings and deep generative modeling.
