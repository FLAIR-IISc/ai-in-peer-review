

# Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a training objective for learning conditional generative models, which they argue provides a simpler alternative to conditional GANs while retaining the benefits of deep learning. The paper demonstrates the approach across three application areas: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation, and distillation of Bayesian models.

## Strengths

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature by extending moment-matching networks to conditional settings, addressing an important gap in the literature. The theoretical development of CMMD is sound, building logically on the kernel embedding framework and MMD. The paper provides a clear derivation of the empirical CMMD objective and its implementation details. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face) and application domains (classification, image generation, Bayesian distillation). The results demonstrate competitive performance across all tasks, with particularly strong generation quality on MNIST and Yale Face datasets. The paper also makes thoughtful connections to related work, including conditional GANs and conditional VAEs, and appropriately cites relevant prior work.

## Weaknesses

While the paper presents a technically sound extension, it has several weaknesses that limit its impact. First, the comparison with state-of-the-art conditional generative models is insufficient. The paper primarily compares with CMMVA (a max-margin DGM) but fails to include recent conditional GAN variants (e.g., conditional WGAN, conditional InfoGAN) that have become the standard for conditional generation tasks. This omission makes it difficult to assess the true competitiveness of CGMMN relative to current methods.

Second, the paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to understand the sensitivity of the approach to key hyperparameters (e.g., kernel choice, hidden dimension, perturbation magnitude for conditional variables). The authors mention "small perturbation" but provide no details on how this was implemented or why it was necessary.

Third, the paper makes a strong claim about simplicity compared to GANs, but the computational complexity of the CMMD objective (with cubic scaling in sample size) is not adequately discussed. The paper mentions this limitation but doesn't provide concrete guidance on when CGMMN would be preferable to other methods based on dataset size.

Fourth, the theoretical assumptions underlying the CMMD objective (e.g., CY|X ∈ F ⊗ G) are acknowledged but not thoroughly explored in practical terms. The paper doesn't discuss how violations of these assumptions might affect performance in real-world applications.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the experimental comparison to include recent conditional GAN variants (e.g., conditional WGAN, conditional InfoGAN) to better position CGMMN in the current landscape of conditional generative models.

2. Conduct ablation studies on key hyperparameters (kernel choice, hidden dimension, perturbation magnitude) to provide readers with practical guidance on implementing CGMMN.

3. Provide more concrete discussion about the computational trade-offs of CGMMN versus other methods, including a scalability analysis on larger datasets.

4. Include a more detailed discussion of the theoretical assumptions and their practical implications, perhaps with a small-scale experiment demonstrating the effects of violating these assumptions.

5. Clarify the "small perturbation" technique used for conditional variables in the experimental section, including the magnitude and distribution of the perturbation.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound extension of GMMN to conditional settings with promising results across multiple tasks. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the experimental evaluation is thorough for the applications presented. However, the lack of comparison with more recent conditional generative models and insufficient ablation studies limit the paper's impact and make it difficult to assess the true novelty and competitiveness of CGMMN. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these issues.

The work addresses an important gap in the literature and has potential for practical applications in conditional generation tasks. While the current version is not ready for acceptance at NIPS due to the limitations noted above, the core idea is promising and could be substantially improved with additional experiments and analysis.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject (with strong potential for improvement). The paper has a solid technical foundation but needs significant revisions to better position its contribution and demonstrate its competitiveness with state-of-the-art methods.
