Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative modeling framework trained by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The key idea is to use the RKHS embedding of conditional distributions: represent P(Y|X) via a conditional mean embedding operator CY|X, estimate it from data using kernel methods, and train a conditional generator y = f(x, h; w) (with noise h and conditioning x) so that its induced operator matches that of the data by minimizing the Hilbert–Schmidt norm ∥ĈY|X(data) − ĈY|X(model)∥^2. This yields a simple, backprop-friendly loss (no min–max game), with a closed-form expression in terms of Gram matrices and regularized inverses. The model supports arbitrary deep architectures for f, and the authors demonstrate applications in predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), conditional generation (MNIST, Yale), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing).

Relative to prior work: GMMN (Dziugaite et al., UAI’15) uses MMD to train unconditional deep generators. This work extends that paradigm to conditional distributions via conditional mean embeddings (Song et al.), which is conceptually different from simply applying MMD on the joint (X,Y): CMMD compares conditional operators, yielding non-uniform weights that reflect the conditioning variables. Compared to GANs and conditional GANs (Goodfellow et al.; Mirza & Osindero), CGMMN avoids adversarial min–max optimization. Compared to (conditional) VAEs (Kingma & Welling; Sohn et al.), CGMMN uses a non-likelihood, kernel-based objective. The paper also connects to max-margin DGMs (Li et al.) and to Bayesian distillation (Korattikara et al.).

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Turning conditional mean embeddings into a training objective for deep conditional generators is a neat and principled extension of GMMN. The CMMD formulation is elegant, with a clear theoretical sufficient condition (equality of operators implies equality of conditionals).
- Simplicity and stability: No adversarial game; the loss is a sum of trace terms in Gram matrices and can be optimized by vanilla backprop. The observation that K^{-1} terms do not depend on network parameters allows non-differentiable kernels on x.
- Versatility: The same framework covers discriminative prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian model distillation. The distillation angle—learning a student conditional generator that mimics a Bayesian predictive distribution—is interesting and practically appealing.
- Clear connection to MMD: The “non-uniform weights” perspective helps interpret how CMMD differs from MMD on the joint, and why it is better aligned with comparing conditionals.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Scalability and efficiency: Each CMMD evaluation requires inverting regularized Gram matrices on the minibatch, which is O(b^3) and can be a bottleneck as batch sizes grow. There is no analysis of computational/memory cost, nor approximations (Nyström, random features, conjugate gradients with caching) to mitigate it.
- Experimental scope and rigor: Results are competitive but not state-of-the-art for classification; generative results are largely qualitative. There are no quantitative generation metrics (acknowledging the 2016 context, but still some simple kernel MMD-to-test-set or Parzen log-likelihood surrogates could help). The distillation experiment is limited (single small regression dataset), making it hard to judge generality.
- Missing ablations/baselines: The most direct baseline is joint MMD on concatenated (x,y) pairs using the same architecture; it is unclear whether CMMD materially improves over that simpler alternative. No study of kernel choices, bandwidth selection, or the effect of λ regularization and minibatch size. Multi-kernel MMD (MK-MMD) could alleviate bandwidth sensitivity but is not discussed.
- Practical details are sparse: For prediction, what kernel is used on y? For image generation, what kernel on y (pixels) and how were bandwidths chosen? How are x perturbed for the “near data” sampling in distillation? Some of this is relegated to the appendix, but minimally summarizing choices in the main text would improve reproducibility.
- Limitations of MMD-style training: The paper notes using autoencoder codes to improve sample quality, but does not analyze the tendency of MMD with Gaussian kernels to average modes/blurriness or discuss alternative kernels that may help.

Quality:
Technically sound overall: the CMMD loss is well-defined, the empirical estimator is standard for conditional mean embeddings with Tikhonov regularization, and the backpropagation is straightforward. The theoretical statement is a sufficient condition, appropriately caveated. However, the empirical evaluation, while reasonably broad, lacks ablations and quantitative diagnostics that would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity:
Generally clear and well organized, with a good preliminaries section. Some typos and small notational glitches remain. Important practical details (kernel choices, hyperparameters, runtime) should be surfaced in the main text. Algorithm 1 is helpful; a brief complexity discussion would be valuable.

Originality:
Moderately high. While conditional mean embeddings are known, using the operator distance as a training objective for deep conditional generators is, to my knowledge, new at the time, and differs from simply applying MMD on the joint. The distillation application is also a nice twist.

Significance:
Medium. The idea is simple and general, and could be useful to practitioners who prefer stable training without adversarial games. If scaled and benchmarked more extensively, CMMD-trained conditional generators could become a viable alternative in some settings. As presented, the empirical gains are modest, but the concept opens a new line of work.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Please compare against a baseline that minimizes MMD on the joint (x,y) with the same architecture. Does CMMD bring consistent improvements?
- Describe kernel choices and bandwidth selection strategies for all experiments. Did you explore MK-MMD to reduce bandwidth sensitivity?
- Provide a brief runtime/memory analysis per batch and discuss possible approximations (Nyström, random Fourier features).
- Report sensitivity to λ and minibatch size. How stable is training across random seeds?
- For distillation, add at least one more dataset and compare to standard distillation with temperature scaling.

Minor:
- Fix typos (e.g., “adverisarial”, “th Conference…”).
- Clarify the handling of finite X (labels) in Section 4.2.1 in the main text.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper introduces a clean and potentially impactful idea—CMMD as a loss for conditional deep generative modeling—that is novel and broadly applicable. Despite experimental limitations and some missing analyses, the conceptual contribution justifies acceptance, and I expect others to build on this direction. Confidence: medium.
