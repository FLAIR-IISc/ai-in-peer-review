This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the prior GMMN framework to conditional distributions using a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The core idea – leveraging moment matching for generative modeling – is not entirely novel, building upon recent work in GANs and GMMNs. However, the authors position CGMMN as a simpler, more stable alternative, particularly for conditional generation tasks. While the motivation is reasonable and the technical presentation is generally clear, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant advance over established methods.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its formalization of CMMD and its theoretical connection to kernel embeddings. The derivations appear sound, and the extension of MMD to the conditional setting is logically presented. The authors also correctly identify relevant applications where conditional generative models are valuable, such as predictive modeling and contextual generation. 

However, numerous weaknesses hinder the overall impact of the work. First, the experimental evaluation feels somewhat underwhelming. While experiments cover a range of tasks (predictive modeling on MNIST/SVHN, generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation), the results often demonstrate *competitiveness* rather than *superiority* to existing methods.  The gains, where present, are modest and not consistently observed across datasets. This is particularly problematic given the simplicity claim; a simpler method should ideally outperform more complex ones, or at least offer a significant trade-off in computational cost. The comparison to strong baselines like DSN and recent GAN-based methods is limited. The architecture choices, while standard, are not particularly innovative, and the sensitivity of performance to hyperparameter tuning is not adequately discussed. 

Second, the authors heavily rely on established network architectures (MLP, CNN) *within* the CGMMN framework. This begs the question: is the contribution truly stemming from the CMMD objective and the moment-matching approach, or is it primarily attributable to the underlying network capacity? The paper doesn't convincingly disentangle these factors. A more compelling demonstration would involve exploring less-conventional network structures or demonstrating improved sample quality compared to GANs with comparable architectures.

Third, the connection to Bayesian dark knowledge is briefly mentioned but lacks sufficient detail. The benefit of using CGMMN for distillation over other methods isn’t thoroughly explained. The results presented are limited and don't provide strong evidence of a clear advantage.

Finally, while the authors acknowledge assumptions related to the CMMD objective (specifically, the smoothness requirements for Hilbert-Schmidt norms), the discussion feels cursory. A more in-depth analysis of the practical implications of these assumptions and potential mitigation strategies would strengthen the technical rigor of the work. The lack of ablation studies examining the impact of the regularization parameter λ is also a noticeable omission.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Formalization of CMMD provides a theoretically sound extension of MMD for conditional distributions.
*   The paper demonstrates the feasibility of using moment matching for conditional generation.
*   Clear and generally well-written presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental results show competitiveness, but not clear superiority, to existing methods.
*   The contribution of CMMD vs. underlying network architecture is not sufficiently disentangled.
*   Limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity and ablation studies.
*   Insufficiently detailed discussion of the practical implications of theoretical assumptions.
*   The advantages of using CGMMN for Bayesian dark knowledge are not convincingly demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper, in its current form, is not strong enough for acceptance to NIPS. While the technical aspects are reasonably sound, the lack of compelling empirical results and a clear demonstration of novelty prevent it from making a significant contribution to the field. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a definitive advancement. I recommend rejection, with encouragement to the authors to conduct more extensive experiments, explore novel network architectures, and provide a more thorough analysis of the theoretical limitations of their approach.
