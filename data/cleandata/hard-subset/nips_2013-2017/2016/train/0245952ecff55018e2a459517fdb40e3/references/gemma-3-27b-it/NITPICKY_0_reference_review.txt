This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the GMMN framework to handle conditional distributions. The core idea is to leverage conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) for training, offering a potentially simpler alternative to GANs for conditional generative modeling. While the concept is logically sound and builds upon existing MMD-based generative models, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of thoroughness in presentation that, cumulatively, undermine its contribution.

The introduction establishes the motivation reasonably well, highlighting the benefits of conditional generative modeling. However, the writing is often verbose and lacks conciseness. For example, the explanation of why conditional models are preferable to joint models could be significantly streamlined. The citations also feel somewhat haphazard; while many are relevant, the density feels arbitrary at times.

Section 2 provides a necessary review of background material, including Hilbert space embedding and MMD. The mathematical derivations are generally correct, but notation is occasionally inconsistent. The explanation of Theorem 2 feels particularly rushed and lacks intuitive explanation of the cross-covariance operator. A crucial detail is missing: the regularity assumptions necessary for the theorems to hold need more explicit discussion. The paper states assumptions are needed, but doesn’t fully delineate them which compromises the theoretical foundations.

Section 3, detailing CGMMN, is where the majority of concerns arise. The definition of CMMD (Theorem 3) lacks sufficient justification for its direct application. While the theorem *states* that CY|X = CZ|X implies distributional equivalence, the practical implications of *estimating* these operators and the potential for approximation errors are glossed over. The equation for L̂2CMMD (Equation 2) is presented without sufficient context; it's difficult to follow the derivation and understand why certain terms are included. The claim that the inverse matrices are independent of model parameters is misleading, as the kernel parameters *are* part of the model and influence the kernel matrix. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is overly simplistic and doesn't address practical considerations like hyperparameter tuning or optimization challenges. Specifically, the choice of lambda, and its effect on the performance, is not discussed.

The experimental section (Section 4) is the weakest aspect of the paper. The results are presented without sufficient detail. The model architectures are vaguely described (e.g., "3 hidden layers with hidden unit number (500, 200, 100)"), making it difficult to reproduce the experiments. The comparison to baselines is incomplete; it’s unclear why certain methods were chosen and others were omitted. The discussion of results is superficial, often lacking statistical significance testing and error analysis. For instance, the MNIST and SVHN results are presented as single numbers without standard deviations or confidence intervals. The generation examples are visually underwhelming, and there's no quantitative evaluation of the generated samples (e.g., Inception Score or FID). The claim that generated samples are “not merely the copy” based on nearest neighbor search is a weak argument, as this metric is sensitive to the choice of distance function and doesn’t necessarily indicate high-quality generation. The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment lacks detail on the PBP model's specifics and the rationale for selecting that particular problem.

Minor issues throughout include inconsistent use of subscripts and superscripts, typographical errors, and a lack of figures illustrating the CGMMN architecture. The formatting of equations is occasionally awkward. The appendix is not appropriately referenced within the main text.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The core idea of extending GMMN to the conditional setting is conceptually valid.
*   The paper builds upon established techniques (MMD, kernel methods).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper is riddled with minor flaws and inconsistencies.
*   The theoretical justification for CMMD is incomplete.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient and lacks rigor.
*   The writing is often verbose and unclear.
*   The paper does not demonstrate a significant advancement over existing methods.
*   The lack of detail makes reproduction challenging.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the concept has merit, the paper's numerous shortcomings and lack of polish outweigh its potential contributions. The authors need to address the issues outlined above, including providing a more rigorous theoretical analysis, conducting a more thorough experimental evaluation, and improving the clarity and presentation of the work. A major revision addressing these points would be necessary for reconsideration.
