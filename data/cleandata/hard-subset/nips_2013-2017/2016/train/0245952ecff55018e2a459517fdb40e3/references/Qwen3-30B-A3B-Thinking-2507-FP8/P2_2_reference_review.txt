

# Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to handle conditional distributions. GMMN uses Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) as a training objective instead of the adversarial min-max formulation used in GANs. The authors propose a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to measure the difference between conditional distributions, and design a conditional generative architecture that takes both conditional variables and random noise as inputs to generate target samples. The paper evaluates CGMMN on three main tasks: predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN datasets), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on labels), and distilling Bayesian models (Bayesian dark knowledge). The results demonstrate competitive performance across these tasks.

The work builds on previous research in kernel embedding techniques [7, 29] and GMMN [19], extending these to conditional settings. The authors position their work as filling a void in applying moment-matching networks to conditional generative modeling, contrasting with previous conditional generative approaches like Conditional GANs [21] and Conditional VAEs [28].

## Quality Assessment

**Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to conditional generative modeling using kernel mean embeddings. The theoretical foundation (Theorems 1-3) is sound, with clear mathematical definitions of CMMD and its relationship to conditional distributions. The paper provides a clear derivation of the CMMD objective and its practical implementation.

**Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are generally well-designed, covering multiple tasks and datasets. The comparison with strong baselines (CMMVA, DSN, CNN, etc.) is appropriate for the predictive modeling task. For image generation, the paper demonstrates reasonable results with qualitative examples. The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment shows promise, though limited to a single regression task.

**Weaknesses in Quality:** The paper lacks sufficient detail about implementation details (e.g., specific kernel choices, regularization parameters, computational complexity). The comparison with Conditional GANs [21] is notably absent, which is a major omission since this is the most direct competitor to CGMMN. The paper mentions "CMMVA" as a competitor but doesn't clearly explain how this differs from the proposed CGMMN. Additionally, the paper doesn't provide training curves or convergence analysis, which would help assess the stability of the method.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from background to method to experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The introduction effectively motivates the need for conditional generative models.

**Areas for Improvement:** Some technical sections (particularly Section 2.3 on kernel embedding of conditional distributions) could benefit from more intuitive explanations or examples. The paper would be strengthened by including a diagram of the CGMMN architecture. The experimental section could better explain the specific implementation choices (e.g., why they chose uniform noise with dimension 20 for MNIST generation, why certain network architectures were selected).

## Originality Assessment

The core idea of extending GMMN to conditional settings is a natural extension, but the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate this from prior work. The paper mentions Conditional GANs [21] and Conditional VAEs [28] but doesn't clearly articulate how CGMMN differs from these approaches or why it might be preferable. The theoretical contribution of CMMD is sound, but the novelty is somewhat incremental given the existing literature on conditional generative models.

**Comparison to Prior Work:** The paper cites relevant work but could better highlight the specific technical differences between CGMMN and existing methods. For example, it doesn't address how CGMMN compares to Conditional GANs in terms of training stability (a known issue with GANs), computational efficiency, or sample quality. The paper states CGMMN "retains the advantages of deep learning" but doesn't explicitly state what advantages these are compared to conditional GANs.

## Significance Assessment

The paper addresses a useful problem with many applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and knowledge distillation. The results show that CGMMN can achieve competitive performance across multiple tasks, suggesting it could be a valuable addition to the generative modeling toolkit.

**Limitations in Significance:** The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why CGMMN is superior to existing methods. The experimental results show it's competitive but don't provide strong evidence that it's significantly better. The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment is interesting but limited in scope. The paper doesn't provide compelling evidence that CGMMN advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way compared to existing conditional generative models.

## Overall Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical foundation using kernel mean embeddings
- Well-organized paper with logical flow
- Demonstrates competitive performance across multiple tasks
- Addresses a useful problem with practical applications
- Provides a theoretically grounded alternative to conditional GANs

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison with Conditional GANs (the most direct competitor)
- Insufficient detail about implementation and hyperparameters
- Doesn't clearly articulate the advantages of CGMMN over existing methods
- Limited scope of experiments (only regression for Bayesian dark knowledge)
- Missing training dynamics analysis (convergence, stability)

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The work presents a technically sound extension of GMMN to conditional settings with reasonable experimental results. However, the paper falls short in demonstrating clear advantages over existing methods, particularly Conditional GANs, which is a major omission for a paper claiming to extend generative modeling techniques. The significance of the contribution is somewhat limited by the lack of strong comparative evidence and the incremental nature of the extension.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more thorough comparison with Conditional GANs (not just CMMVA)
2. Additional details about implementation choices and hyperparameters
3. A clearer articulation of the advantages of CGMMN over existing conditional generative models
4. More comprehensive experiments (e.g., showing training curves, ablation studies)

While the paper makes a valid contribution to the field of generative modeling, the current version doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how it advances the state of the art beyond the natural extension of GMMN to conditional settings. With the suggested revisions, this could be a strong contribution to NIPS.

**Overall Score: 6 (Weak Accept)**
