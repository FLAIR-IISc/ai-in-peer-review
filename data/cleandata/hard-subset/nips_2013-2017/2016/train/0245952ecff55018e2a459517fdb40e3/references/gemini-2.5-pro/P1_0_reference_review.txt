### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMNs), a method for learning conditional probability distributions p(y|x). This work extends the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework, which models joint distributions, to the more versatile conditional setting. The core contributions are twofold: 1) A new training objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions by comparing their kernel mean embedding operators in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). 2) A simple and effective deep generative architecture where samples are generated by passing the conditioning variable `x` along with a random noise vector `h` through a deep neural network.

The CMMD objective is a principled extension of the MMD criterion used in GMMNs, avoiding the unstable min-max optimization of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors demonstrate the versatility and effectiveness of their approach on a wide range of tasks: predictive modeling (image classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (generating MNIST digits and Yale faces conditioned on labels), and a novel application of distilling knowledge from a complex Bayesian model ("Bayesian dark knowledge"). The experimental results show that CGMMN achieves performance competitive with other state-of-the-art deep generative models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled and Novel Objective Function:** The introduction of Conditional MMD (CMMD) is the paper's main strength. It is a theoretically sound and elegant extension of MMD to conditional distributions, grounded in the established literature on kernel embeddings of conditional operators. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed model.

2.  **Stable and Simple Training:** A significant practical advantage of CGMMN over its main competitor, the conditional GAN, is the training stability. The CMMD objective is a single, well-defined loss function that can be minimized directly via backpropagation. This avoids the difficult and often unstable min-max game inherent in adversarial training, making the model easier to train and tune.

3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The paper presents a strong empirical evaluation across three distinct and important domains.
    *   **Prediction:** The model achieves competitive error rates on MNIST and SVHN when compared to other deep generative models designed for prediction (e.g., CMMVA), demonstrating its discriminative capabilities.
    *   **Generation:** The contextual generation results on MNIST and Yale Faces are convincing, showing that the model can produce diverse and class-consistent samples. The use of an autoencoder to generate in a latent space is a clever practical technique to improve sample quality.
    *   **Model Distillation:** The "Bayesian dark knowledge" experiment is a novel and interesting application, showcasing the model's ability to approximate a complex predictive distribution from a Bayesian "teacher" model without sacrificing performance. This highlights the flexibility of the CGMMN framework.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and clearly structured. It starts with a strong motivation, provides the necessary background on kernel embeddings, logically develops the CMMD objective, and presents the experimental results in an easy-to-follow manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The CMMD objective (Eq. 2) requires the computation and inversion of kernel matrices for each mini-batch, which scales as O(B³) where B is the batch size. This can be computationally expensive for large batch sizes, potentially limiting the model's scalability compared to conditional VAEs or GANs, whose per-batch costs are typically linear in B. The paper mentions a mini-batch algorithm but does not discuss this computational trade-off or its practical implications (e.g., maximum feasible batch size, training time comparisons).

2.  **Quality of Raw Generated Samples:** The samples generated directly in pixel space (Fig. 2) appear quite noisy compared to state-of-the-art results from GANs of the same era (e.g., DCGAN). The authors themselves acknowledge this by introducing an autoencoder pre-training step to generate smoother images (Fig. 4). While this is a valid solution, it adds complexity and a separate training phase. A discussion on why the base CMMD objective might lead to noisier samples in high-dimensional spaces like images would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Lack of Details on Kernel Selection:** The performance of MMD-based methods is often sensitive to the choice of kernel function and its hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidth). The paper does not specify which kernels were used for the inputs `x` and outputs `y` in the experiments, nor does it describe the procedure for selecting their hyperparameters. This information is crucial for reproducibility. For example, were different kernels used for image data versus label data? How were the bandwidths tuned?

### Detailed Comments and Questions

-   **Regarding CMMD computation:** The formulation in Eq. (2) appears to require separate kernel matrices for the "data" and "generated" samples (`Ks`, `Kd`) and their cross-kernel matrices. In the proposed training algorithm, the generated samples `B'` use the same conditional variables `x` as the data samples `B`. Does this mean `Υd = Υs` and thus `Kd = Ks = Ksd` within a mini-batch? If so, this could simplify the expression and should be clarified.
-   **Comparison with Conditional VAEs:** The paper compares CGMMN to GANs and other DGMs but misses a direct comparison or discussion with Conditional Variational Autoencoders (CVAEs) [28], which is cited but not evaluated against. CVAEs also offer a stable training objective and are a highly relevant baseline for conditional generation.
-   **Predictive Task Setup:** For the prediction task on MNIST/SVHN, the output `y` is a 10-dimensional vector representing class confidences. How is the "ground truth" `y` for the training data constructed? Is it a one-hot vector? Using MMD/CMMD to match a generated continuous vector to a one-hot vector seems unusual. Some clarification on the target distribution being modeled would be helpful.

### Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of deep generative models. The proposed CMMD objective is theoretically well-founded and offers a practical, stable alternative to adversarial training for conditional generation. The extensive experiments convincingly demonstrate the model's effectiveness and versatility. While there are some minor weaknesses regarding computational trade-offs and missing implementation details, the strengths far outweigh them. This work clearly advances the state of the art for moment-matching networks and is likely to be influential.

**Decision: Accept**
