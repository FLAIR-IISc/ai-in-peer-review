Summary
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative model trained by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The key idea is to compare conditional distributions via the distance between their conditional mean embedding operators in RKHS, which yields a tractable objective expressible in terms of kernel Gram matrices. The generator concatenates observed covariates x with noise h and maps them through a deep network to produce y, enabling sampling for both training and conditional generation. Training is done with backpropagation and mini-batches; crucially, inverses of the input-kernel Gram matrices do not depend on network parameters, simplifying differentiation. Experiments cover: (i) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, (ii) conditional image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces (with optional autoencoder codes), and (iii) distillation of Bayesian predictive distributions (teacher: PBP) on Boston Housing. Results are competitive with prior deep generative models and reasonable against strong discriminative baselines.

Relation to prior work
CGMMN extends GMMN (Li et al., UAI’15; Dziugaite et al., UAI’15) by moving from unconditional MMD to a conditional counterpart, leveraging the theory of kernel conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., ICML’09; Grunewalder et al., ICML’12). Compared to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and CVAE (Sohn et al., NIPS’15), CGMMN offers a simple, non-adversarial, non-variational training criterion for conditional generation. The use of a Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional embedding operators as a learning objective appears to be new in deep generative modeling, although “conditional MMD” has been used in other contexts (e.g., domain adaptation) with a different formulation. The paper situates itself reasonably within these lines, though a more direct empirical comparison to cGAN/CVAE is missing.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and elegance: Ties deep conditional generation to a well-founded kernel operator view. The CMMD objective is clean, stable (no min-max), and differentiable end-to-end.
- Generality: One framework supports classification, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation; works with MLPs/CNNs and with raw data or autoencoder codes.
- Practical training insights: The inverses in CMMD depend only on input kernels, so non-differentiable kernels on x are allowed; mini-batch training mitigates cubic kernel costs.
- Theory-backed: Theorem 3 (sufficiency that equality of conditional embedding operators implies equality of conditionals) gives a principled justification; connections between MMD and CMMD are clearly articulated.
- Empirically competitive: On MNIST/SVHN, CGMMN-CNN is in the ballpark of state-of-the-art DGMs (e.g., CMMVA). Conditional samples look plausible on MNIST/Yale; distillation matches PBP on Boston.

Weaknesses
- Incremental feel relative to GMMN: The jump from MMD to CMMD is natural given the existing kernel-embedding literature; the novelty lies more in applying it to deep generators than in the CMMD itself.
- Scalability and complexity: CMMD requires O(b^3) per mini-batch for matrix inversion; the paper lacks runtime/memory analysis, and there is no exploration of approximations (Nyström, random Fourier features).
- Experimental scope and comparisons: No quantitative generative metrics (e.g., Parzen-window LL used at the time; or Inception score/FID later) and no direct comparison to cGAN/CVAE on conditional image generation. The Bayesian distillation evaluation is limited to a single small UCI dataset.
- Sensitivity to kernel choices: The kernels for x and y (types, bandwidth selection) are under-specified, and there is no ablation on kernel hyperparameters, which are known to significantly affect MMD-style objectives.
- Predictive performance: While respectable for a generative model, the classification results lag strong discriminative baselines; the paper could better argue when one should prefer CGMMN over cross-entropy-trained discriminative nets.

Quality
Technically sound, with correct use of conditional mean embeddings and a derivation of a computable CMMD objective. Theoretical claims are appropriate but stop at sufficiency; no generalization or consistency guarantees for the learned deep model are provided. Experimental evidence is suggestive but not exhaustive; some choices (kernels, bandwidths) need clearer description.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with helpful background on RKHS embeddings and MMD. Some implementation details are deferred to the appendix; a concise summary in the main text of kernel choices and hyperparameters would improve reproducibility. Figures illustrate conditional generation well.

Originality
Moderate. The operator-based CMMD objective for training conditional deep generators appears novel and is the main contribution. However, it builds directly on established tools (GMMN, conditional embeddings), and the high-level extension to conditioning is unsurprising.

Significance
The paper offers a simple, stable alternative to adversarial/variational training for conditional generation, which is valuable. If adopted, CMMD could become a useful tool whenever conditional sampling is needed without adversarial games. Broader impact would be clearer with stronger empirical comparisons and scaling demonstrations.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Specify the kernels used for x and y in all experiments (Gaussian? bandwidth selection via median heuristic or validation?) and report sensitivity.
- Provide computational costs per update and wall-clock comparisons to cGAN/CVAE; discuss using Nyström/RFF to scale CMMD.
- Add quantitative generation metrics and direct comparisons to cGAN/CVAE on MNIST/Yale (and perhaps CIFAR small-scale conditional tasks).
- Clarify for classification what kernel on outputs is used when y is a probability vector, and whether you backprop through L.
- Expand Bayesian distillation to more UCI datasets and include calibration metrics (NLL) in addition to RMSE.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, principled, and potentially impactful objective connecting kernel conditional embeddings and deep conditional generation.
- Training simplicity and stability relative to GANs; versatility across tasks.
- Competitive empirical results and appealing conditional samples.

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty is incremental relative to GMMN and conditional embeddings.
- Limited and partially qualitative experimental evaluation; missing strong baselines on conditional generation and scalability analysis.
- Kernel choices/hyperparameters under-documented; unclear robustness.

Recommendation: 7 (accept)
Confidence: 3/5
