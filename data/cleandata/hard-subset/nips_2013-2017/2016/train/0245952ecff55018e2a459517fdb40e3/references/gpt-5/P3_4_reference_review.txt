Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to model conditional distributions p(y|x). The key idea is to match conditional distributions via a new criterion, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), defined as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X estimated from data and from the generator. Practically, samples are produced by a deep deterministic network y = f(x, h; w) that takes the conditioning variable x and an i.i.d. noise vector h, and parameters are learned by backpropagating through a CMMD loss computed on mini-batches using kernel Gram matrices on x and y. The method is applied to: (i) predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), (ii) conditional image generation (MNIST digits conditioned on labels; Yale Faces conditioned on identity), and (iii) knowledge distillation from Bayesian models (PBP on Boston Housing).

This builds on: kernel mean embeddings and MMD for two-sample testing; conditional mean embeddings (Song et al., Fukumizu et al.) to represent conditional distributions as operators; GMMN (Li et al., Dziugaite et al.) for unconditional generative modeling with MMD; and conditional deep generative models such as CGAN, CVAE, and conditional SPNs.

Quality
- Theory: The sufficiency result (Theorem 3) that equality of conditional embedding operators implies equality of conditional distributions (under standard universality and regularity assumptions) is sound and aligns with the conditional mean embedding literature. The CMMD objective is a natural and principled extension of MMD to the conditional setting. The discussion on estimator existence via regularized empirical operators Φ(K+λI)^{-1}Υ⊤ is appropriate.
- However, the paper lacks statistical analysis beyond sufficiency: no consistency or finite-sample bounds for CMMD (contrast with MMD’s well-known concentration results), no discussion of the bias introduced by the regularization λ in the operator inversion, and no guidance on kernel and bandwidth selection, which critically affect CMMD in practice.
- Algorithmic concerns: Computing K̃^{-1} per mini-batch is O(b^3) and memory O(b^2). With b up to 500, this is nontrivial; the paper does not report runtime or explore kernel approximations (e.g., Nyström/random features) to reduce cost. The observation that K̃−1 is independent of w allows non-differentiable kernels on x, but this also implies that for the common training protocol where x in the “data” and “generated” sets are the same minibatch, Kd=Ks=Ksd and CMMD simplifies to a weighted discrepancy between label Gram matrices; this connection to conditional kernel ridge regression (conditional mean embedding regression) is not analyzed and would help interpret the loss.
- Empirics: Classification results are competitive with MMVA/CMMVA but behind strong discriminative baselines on SVHN. For generation, evaluation is qualitative; there is no quantitative comparison to CGAN/CVAE or to unconditional GMMN (e.g., Parzen window log-likelihood then-standard, or kernel MMD to held-out data). For MNIST, the need to add an autoencoder to improve sample quality suggests a standard pixel-space kernel may be ill-suited; this is reasonable but should be analyzed more systematically (e.g., demonstrate that code-space CMMD improves FID-like metrics, or at least MMD to held-out codes). The Bayesian distillation experiment is promising but limited (single small dataset, no comparison to the “Bayesian dark knowledge” student baselines).

Clarity
- Generally clear exposition of RKHS embeddings, MMD, and conditional embeddings; proofs are referenced appropriately. The CMMD expression via Gram matrices is helpful. The algorithm description is straightforward.
- Missing important implementation details: kernels used for x and y (Gaussian? linear for labels? bandwidth selection? normalization?), value and role of λ, number of noise samples per x, sensitivity to noise dimension, choice of mini-batch size vs stability, and whether K is recomputed per batch or cached when x repeats. Some tables/figures are referenced but not fully shown; make sure all quantitative results are presented clearly.
- It would be useful to explicitly relate CMMD to MMD on the joint (x,y) (with product kernels) and explain when/why CMMD is preferable; the remark partially addresses this but a more formal treatment would improve clarity.

Originality
- The main conceptual contribution—using the conditional mean embedding operator to define a conditional discrepancy for training deep conditional generators—appears novel at the time and is a natural extension of GMMN. Prior work on conditional GANs and CVAEs addressed conditional generation with different objectives; CMMD provides a stable, non-adversarial alternative with a principled statistical interpretation.
- The paper could better acknowledge the close relationship to conditional mean embedding regression and operator-valued kernel regression; drawing these connections would not diminish originality but would situate the work more precisely.

Significance
- Conditional generative modeling is important for prediction, conditional synthesis, and modular generative systems. Providing a stable conditional training objective without min-max games is valuable. The method is architecture-agnostic and easy to implement with backprop.
- That said, the empirical gains over strong baselines are modest for classification, and the generation evaluation is not compelling quantitatively. The scalability of CMMD is a practical concern. With stronger empirical validation (including comparisons to CGAN/CVAE and ablations on kernels/λ/batch size) the impact would be higher.

Questions and requests for clarification
- What kernels and bandwidths are used for x and y in each experiment? How sensitive are results to these choices and to λ?
- In classification, are x identical in Ds and Dd within a minibatch? If so, CMMD reduces to a weighted discrepancy between Ls and Ld; can you derive and discuss this explicitly and relate it to (conditional) kernel ridge regression?
- How many h samples per x are used during training? Does multiple samples per x improve stability/performance?
- What is the computational cost per iteration and overall training time compared to CGAN/CVAE and MMVA/CMMVA?
- For generation, can you provide quantitative metrics (then-standard) and a comparison to CGAN/CVAE on MNIST/Yale?
- Have you explored kernel approximations or sharing K̃−1 across batches to reduce O(b^3) cost?

Pros
- Principled conditional discrepancy (CMMD) grounded in RKHS conditional embeddings.
- Simple, stable training via backprop; no adversarial game.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Competitive classification performance with DGMs.

Cons
- Limited theoretical analysis (no consistency/concentration for CMMD estimator).
- Scalability concerns due to O(b^3) kernel inversions; no approximations explored.
- Insufficient implementation details and ablations on kernel choices and hyperparameters.
- Generation results lack quantitative evaluation and comparisons to CGAN/CVAE; visual quality relies on auxiliary autoencoder.
- Modest gains over baselines; unclear advantages over cross-entropy for classification.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The core idea is sound and timely, and the conditional operator-matching view is elegant. However, to strengthen the contribution, I encourage the authors to: provide implementation details and sensitivity analyses; add quantitative generative evaluations and comparisons to CGAN/CVAE; clarify the reduction of CMMD when x is shared; and discuss/mitigate computational cost (e.g., via kernel approximations).
