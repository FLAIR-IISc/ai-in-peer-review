This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the previously proposed GMMN framework to handle conditional distributions. The core idea is to leverage the simplicity of moment matching via Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) to learn flexible conditional distributions, offering an alternative to GANs and variational methods. The authors demonstrate the approach on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

While the extension of GMMN to the conditional setting is a logical step, and the theoretical justification using conditional MMD is sound, the paper feels incremental and lacks compelling novelty to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The core contribution—applying MMD to conditional distributions—feels like a relatively straightforward adaptation of existing techniques rather than a substantial breakthrough.  The authors correctly identify the advantages of conditional modeling, but the presented CGMMN doesn't seem to offer significant advantages over established methods in these areas. 

The paper’s strength lies in its methodological soundness. The derivation of the conditional MMD objective (CMMD) appears correct, and the use of kernel methods provides a theoretically grounded approach. The authors acknowledge potential assumptions required for the CMMD objective to be well-defined. The experiments are reasonably comprehensive, covering several tasks and datasets. However, the results, while competitive, aren’t significantly better than existing approaches. The MNIST and SVHN experiments show performance comparable to existing models, but often rely on relatively complex network architectures (CNNs) to achieve these results, diminishing the appeal of the supposed simplicity of the MMD approach.  The Bayesian dark knowledge distillation experiment, while interesting, is only briefly presented. 

Several weaknesses detract from the paper’s overall impact. Firstly, the experimental setup feels somewhat standard; the authors do not explore the sensitivity of the results to different kernel choices, which is a crucial aspect of MMD-based methods.  A more detailed ablation study analyzing the impact of different network architectures and hyperparameters would strengthen the evaluation. Secondly, the paper lacks a rigorous comparison to conditional GANs, which are a dominant approach in conditional generation. While GANs can be difficult to train, a fair comparison, perhaps with recently developed training stabilization techniques, is essential. Finally, the discussion of the limitations of the method (e.g., the Hilbert-Schmidt assumption) feels cursory.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretically sound extension of GMMN to conditional distributions.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation across several tasks.
*   Clear presentation of the CMMD objective and its derivation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution; lacks significant novelty.
*   Results are competitive but not state-of-the-art.
*   Insufficient comparison to conditional GANs.
*   Limited discussion of limitations and sensitivity analysis.
*   The justification for some design choices (e.g., dimensionality of the auxiliary random variables) is weak.



Overall, I find this paper to be a solid but unremarkable contribution. The work is technically correct and well-executed, but it doesn't offer a compelling enough advance to justify acceptance to NIPS. I recommend **rejection**. The authors would benefit from further exploring the advantages of CGMMN over existing methods, conducting a more thorough comparison to conditional GANs, and providing a more in-depth analysis of the method's limitations and sensitivity to hyperparameters.
