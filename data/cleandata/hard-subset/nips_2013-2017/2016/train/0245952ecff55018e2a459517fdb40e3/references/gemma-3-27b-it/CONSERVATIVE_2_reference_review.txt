This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the existing Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to handle conditional distributions. The core idea is to leverage Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in a conditional setting (CMMD) to train a deep network that maps a combination of random variables and conditional inputs to a target sample. The authors demonstrate the approach on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

While the paper presents a logically sound extension of GMMN, and the CMMD formulation is a reasonable adaptation of MMD, I find myself largely unpersuaded by its novelty and particularly, its demonstrated impact. The central weakness lies in a lack of compelling evidence that CGMMN significantly advances the state-of-the-art, especially considering the well-established and highly optimized baselines employed. The paper feels iterative rather than groundbreaking.

The motivation for conditional generative models is well-established. However, the presented work doesn’t sufficiently highlight *why* CGMMN is preferable to existing conditional generative models like Conditional GANs or conditional VAEs. The authors allude to the simpler training process compared to GANs, but do not convincingly demonstrate that this simplicity translates into tangible benefits like improved stability, faster convergence, or better sample quality. The discussion of related work is adequate, but doesn’t deeply engage with the trade-offs between CGMMN and established alternatives. 

Methodologically, the paper is sound. The mathematical formulation of CMMD is correct, and the use of kernel methods is appropriate. However, the practical implementation details, while described, lack critical discussion about potential challenges. For instance, the choice of kernel, the regularization parameter λ, and the mini-batch size are presented without detailed justification or ablation studies. The statement that the inverse matrices are independent of model parameters is true, but doesn’t address the potential computational burden of inverting them, especially for larger datasets.

The experimental results are mixed. The MNIST and SVHN predictive modeling results are competitive but not leading edge, with CGMMN-CNN performing similarly to other established methods. The generative examples on MNIST are visually plausible but lack quantitative evaluation. Simply *showing* generated images isn’t sufficient; metrics like Inception Score or FID are essential for assessing generative model quality. The Yale Face dataset example is promising, but the limited scale and lack of comparison to other generative face models weaken the conclusions. The Bayesian dark knowledge distillation experiment, while interesting, is limited to a single dataset and lacks a thorough comparison to other distillation techniques. 

The clarity of the paper is generally good, although the notation could be improved. For example, consistently using subscripts to denote datasets (e.g., *D<sub>sXY</sub>*) would enhance readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Logically sound extension of GMMN to conditional distributions.
*   CMMD is a reasonable adaptation of MMD for conditional modeling.
*   Demonstrates applicability across diverse tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of compelling novelty.
*   Limited empirical advantage over established baselines.
*   Insufficient quantitative evaluation of generative performance.
*   Missing ablation studies and sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters.
*   Doesn't convincingly demonstrate why CGMMN is superior to existing conditional generative models.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the technical work is correct and the ideas are reasonable, the lack of substantial novelty and the absence of strong empirical results do not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a significant contribution to the field. The authors would need to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods, provide more rigorous quantitative evaluation, and address the limitations outlined above to make this work competitive.
