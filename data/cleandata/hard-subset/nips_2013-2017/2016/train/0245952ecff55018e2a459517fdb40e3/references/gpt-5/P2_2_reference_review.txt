Summary
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to model conditional distributions p(y|x). The key idea is to define a deep conditional generator y = f(x, h; w), where h is noise, and learn w by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. CMMD is defined as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X estimated from data and from the generator. This provides a simple, backpropagation-friendly objective without a min–max game (unlike GANs). The authors derive a practical empirical CMMD estimator relying on kernel Gram matrices over inputs (X) and outputs (Y), with regularization. They evaluate CGMMN on: (i) classification (MNIST, SVHN), (ii) conditional generation (class-conditional MNIST and Yale Faces), including an autoencoder-latent variant, and (iii) Bayesian dark knowledge distillation on Boston Housing, showing competitive results relative to deep generative baselines.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on MMD and kernel mean embeddings (Gretton et al., 2012; Song et al., 2009) and the GMMN framework (Dziugaite et al., 2015; Li et al., 2015). It is analogous in spirit to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), but replaces adversarial or variational training with CMMD. The use of conditional operator embeddings to define a learning loss for deep generators appears novel and well-motivated relative to the kernel literature on conditional mean embeddings and regression.

Quality
- Theory: The definition of CMMD as ∥CY|X − CZ|X∥HS and the sufficiency result “CY|X equality ⇒ p(y|x) equality for all x” follow from known properties of universal RKHSs and conditional embeddings. The empirical estimator and its trace form are standard and sound. The paper is careful to note assumptions (e.g., smoothness for operator existence) and the practicality of the regularized empirical estimator.
- Optimization: The objective is differentiable w.r.t. generator parameters. The inverses in K̃−1 depend only on X, not on w, which simplifies gradients and permits non-differentiable kernels on X.
- Practicality: A mini-batch algorithm is given, but the computational complexity per batch is cubic in batch size due to kernel inversions; no scalability tricks (e.g., random features, Nyström, preconditioned conjugate gradients) are explored. Kernel choice and hyperparameters (bandwidths, λ) critically affect performance but are not detailed.
- Experiments: 
  - Classification: CGMMN-CNN is competitive with MMVA (a strong deep generative baseline) but behind purely discriminative SOTA (e.g., DSN), which is expected but limits impact. No ablation on kernel choices or stability.
  - Generation: Class-conditional MNIST and Yale faces are visually plausible; nearest-neighbor checks reduce the copying concern. The AE+CGMMN variant is sensible (as with GMMN+AE) and improves sample quality. However, quantitative generative metrics are absent, and datasets are relatively simple.
  - Bayesian distillation: The Boston Housing result shows no degradation vs. PBP in RMSE, but the evaluation is minimal (single dataset; no calibration metrics like NLL or sharpness/coverage).

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The CMMD derivation is clear, and the operator viewpoint is insightful. However:
- Kernel choices for X and Y, bandwidth selection (median heuristic? cross-validation?), and λ are not specified, hindering reproducibility.
- Computational costs (per-iteration runtime, batch sizes vs. stability), and sensitivity to hyperparameters are not discussed.
- Some notational switches between s/d superscripts and xd/xs cause friction; there are minor typos.

Originality
Extending GMMN to conditional distributions via an operator-level MMD is a natural but non-trivial step. To my knowledge, using the Hilbert–Schmidt distance between conditional mean operators as a learning objective for deep conditional generators is novel in the deep generative modeling literature circa 2016. The method contrasts with cGAN/cVAE by avoiding adversarial/variational optimization while retaining conditional modeling flexibility.

Significance
The contribution is a simple, general conditional generative learning principle with theoretical grounding and broad applicability. While classification performance is not SOTA, it is competitive for a generative approach. The conditional generation results demonstrate feasibility but lack rigorous quantitative evaluation. The Bayesian distillation example is promising but underdeveloped. Overall, the method could influence practitioners seeking stable, non-adversarial conditional generation, but the experimental evidence is moderate.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean, non-adversarial conditional generative training objective with a solid kernel embedding foundation.
- Easy to implement with backprop; compatible with various architectures; no need for discriminator or variational bounds.
- Demonstrates applicability across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Insightful interpretation: CMMD as non-uniformly weighted MMD modulated by conditional inputs.

Weaknesses
- Scalability: O(B^3) kernel operations per batch; no approximation strategies or runtime reporting.
- Missing details on kernel choices/hyperparameters; no ablations or sensitivity analyses.
- Limited and largely qualitative generative evaluation; no comparisons to cGAN/cVAE on standard conditional generation benchmarks.
- Classification gains are modest versus discriminative baselines; significance is therefore moderate.
- Bayesian distillation evaluation is too small to draw strong conclusions (no calibration metrics).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add kernel approximation techniques (random Fourier features, Nyström) and report speed/accuracy trade-offs.
- Specify kernel choices, bandwidth selection procedures, λ, and provide ablations.
- Include quantitative generative metrics (e.g., FID/KID where appropriate), and compare against cGAN/cVAE on MNIST and a harder dataset (e.g., CIFAR-10 class-conditional).
- Report calibration metrics (NLL, reliability diagrams) for Bayesian distillation on multiple datasets.
- Discuss stability wrt batch size and kernel parameters; provide code for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled extension of MMD-based training to conditional generative modeling.
- Simple, stable training objective; avoids adversarial difficulties.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across tasks.
- The operator-embedding perspective may inspire further research.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental validation is not comprehensive; limited quantitative evidence, small datasets for generation, minimal Bayesian evaluation.
- Scalability and kernel hyperparameter sensitivity left unaddressed.
- Empirical results do not advance SOTA; significance may be incremental.

Overall
A solid and well-motivated methodological contribution with clear theory and promising, though not definitive, experiments. I lean toward a positive evaluation if space allows for methods that advance principled training objectives, but stronger experimental validation and scalability considerations would significantly strengthen the case.
