**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the previously proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) [19, 3] to the conditional setting. The core idea is to learn a conditional distribution p(y|x) using a deep neural network that maps a conditional input `x` and a random noise vector `h` to a sample `y`. The key contribution is the formulation of a novel training objective, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which serves as a metric to measure the divergence between the true conditional distribution (represented by empirical data) and the one modeled by the network.

The CMMD criterion is theoretically grounded in the literature on kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions [29]. It measures the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between the conditional mean embedding operators of the two distributions. This allows for a principled, non-parametric comparison of conditional distributions without making explicit density assumptions. The resulting objective function can be calculated using kernel matrices and optimized via back-propagation, enabling end-to-end training of the deep generative network.

The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN through experiments on a diverse set of tasks: (1) predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), (2) contextual generation (generating class-conditional MNIST digits and identity-conditional Yale faces), and (3) distilling knowledge from a Bayesian model. The results are shown to be competitive with other deep generative models, particularly those based on adversarial training or variational inference.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The proposed CMMD objective is a well-founded extension of MMD, building directly on established theory of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions [29]. The derivation of the empirical estimator for the CMMD loss (Equation 2) is clear and correct. The claims are generally well-supported by the experiments, which cover a sensible and diverse range of applications for a conditional generative model. The authors are also transparent about certain limitations, for instance, by noting that using an autoencoder as a feature extractor can improve the perceptual quality of generated images, which implicitly acknowledges that the base model's pixel-level generation can be noisy.

A potential weakness lies in the computational complexity of the CMMD objective. The calculation requires inverting kernel matrices of size `B x B` (where `B` is the mini-batch size), which scales as `O(B^3)`. While feasible for the batch sizes used in the experiments (200-500), this could become a significant bottleneck for larger batch sizes often required for training very deep networks, and the authors do not discuss potential approximations or scaling strategies.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It starts with a good motivation for conditional generative models and a concise review of the necessary background on MMD and kernel embeddings. The transition from these concepts to the proposed CMMD criterion and the CGMMN architecture is logical and easy to follow. The description of the model architecture and training algorithm is sufficient for an expert to understand the implementation. The figures and tables effectively support the experimental results.

**Originality:**
The main contribution—the formulation of CMMD as a training objective for a deep conditional generative model—is novel. While GMMNs [19] had already proposed using MMD for generative modeling, extending this to the conditional setting is a non-trivial step that requires different theoretical machinery (i.e., conditional operators instead of mean embeddings). This work provides a direct alternative to Conditional GANs [21] and Conditional VAEs [28]. The paper does a good job of positioning its contribution with respect to prior work, especially GMMNs and the foundational work on kernel methods. The application to Bayesian dark knowledge is also an interesting and less common use case that highlights the model's flexibility.

**Significance:**
The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of deep generative models. The primary significance lies in providing a non-adversarial framework for conditional generation. The min-max optimization of GANs is notoriously unstable and difficult to tune. CGMMN, with its single, direct optimization objective, offers a potentially more stable and simpler training paradigm. This is a valuable alternative for practitioners and researchers. The demonstrated applicability across classification, generation, and knowledge distillation suggests that CGMMN is a flexible and powerful tool. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by researchers exploring alternatives to GANs or working on applications that require stable training of conditional generative models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Objective:** Proposes CMMD, a novel and theoretically sound objective function for training conditional generative models, based on established kernel methods.
2.  **Stable Training Alternative:** Provides a non-adversarial alternative to Conditional GANs, potentially avoiding the training instabilities associated with min-max optimization.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The model's effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated across a diverse range of tasks, including prediction, generation, and knowledge distillation.
4.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is well-written, technically sound, and clearly situates its contribution within the existing literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Computational Scalability:** The `O(B^3)` complexity of the CMMD loss per mini-batch may limit its scalability to very large batch sizes, a point that is not fully addressed.
2.  **Sample Quality:** The visual quality of the generated images, without the use of a pre-trained autoencoder, appears to be lower than that of state-of-the-art GAN-based models from the same era.
3.  **Predictive Performance:** While competitive with other DGMs, the classification results do not surpass state-of-the-art discriminative models, which slightly tempers the claims for the predictive modeling task.
