Summary
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN to conditional distributions p(y|x). The key idea is to match conditional kernel mean embeddings by minimizing the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional embedding operators, leading to a Conditional MMD (CMMD) objective. The generative model concatenates the conditioning variables x with exogenous noise h and maps through a DNN y = f(x,h; w). Training minimizes an empirical CMMD between the real pairs (x, y) and generated pairs (x, f(x, h; w)), using backprop; the inverses in the operator estimator depend only on x-grams and are independent of w. Experiments include: predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), conditional image generation (MNIST, Yale), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (PBP → student on Boston Housing). Results are competitive with MMVA on prediction; generated samples are plausible; the distilled model appears not to degrade RMSE.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of MMD to conditional distributions via operator-valued kernel mean embeddings. The CMMD objective is intuitive and fits naturally with deep conditional generators.
- Practical training: only output-side kernels need to be differentiable; the x-side kernel can be non-differentiable since inverses are treated as constants w.r.t. w.
- General, modular framework applicable to prediction, conditional generation, and distillation; compatible with MLPs and CNNs.
- Empirical results suggest viability across diverse tasks, with simple sampling-based training.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/theoretical:
- No analysis of statistical properties of the CMMD estimator: bias/variance, consistency, or generalization guarantees for conditional distribution learning. Theorem 3 is a sufficiency statement; there is no result that minimizing empirical CMMD recovers p(y|x) under standard assumptions (e.g., as sample size → ∞ with appropriate regularization). This is a gap compared to the (unconditional) MMD literature and to conditional embedding regression results.
- The CMMD formula (Eq. 2) is given without derivation. Dimensions/transposes and symmetry assumptions are glossed over; the cross-term Tr(Ksd K̃d^{-1} Lds K̃s^{-1}) needs a careful derivation to ensure it is the correct Hilbert–Schmidt inner product between empirical operators Φd(Kd+λI)−1Υd^T and Φs(Ks+λI)−1Υs^T. Please provide a step-by-step derivation and check the placement of inverses and transposes.
- Computational complexity is significant: per-minibatch O(b^3) kernel inversions for both source and model batches, plus O(b^2) kernel formation. There is no discussion of numerical stability, choice of λ, or use of low-rank/iterative solvers. How large can b be in practice?
- Kernel choices and hyperparameters are unspecified throughout: which kernels for x and y, bandwidth selection (median heuristic? multi-kernel MMD?), normalization, and sensitivity. For images, RBF kernels in pixel space are notoriously degenerate in high dimensions; no ablation or discussion is provided.
- The assumptions required for CY|X ∈ F ⊗ G and CXX invertibility (or regularized inverse) are stated but not justified for the used kernels/data. There is no discussion of the effect of λ on bias/variance.

Methodology:
- Classification setup is unclear/inconsistent. The model outputs y ∈ R^10+ with a sigmoid final layer in the CNN case, and prediction takes argmax of a single stochastic sample. This is not a normalized categorical distribution and introduces unnecessary sampling variance. Why not predict expected probabilities (e.g., average over multiple h) or use a softmax output? Calibration is not reported.
- The description of how x is sampled for the model batch is vague (“with/without small perturbation”). This is central: CMMD estimates an x-weighted discrepancy, so the choice of x distribution matters for generalization. Please precisely specify perturbation distributions and show sensitivity analyses.
- No quantitative generative evaluation (e.g., FID/Inception score for MNIST variants, Parzen estimates, or kernel scores). Nearest-neighbor inspection alone is inadequate.
- Limited and somewhat weak empirical comparisons for conditional generation: no comparisons against conditional GANs (cGAN) or conditional VAEs on the same tasks/architectures.
- Dark knowledge distillation evaluation is minimal: only RMSE on Boston Housing, no predictive log-likelihood or uncertainty calibration, and no speed/memory comparison between teacher and student, which is the motivation.
- No ablations: effect of noise dimension, kernel bandwidth, λ, batch size, and architecture on CMMD and downstream metrics.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous typos and formatting errors (e.g., “MINIST”, “augumentation”, “convoluational”, stray “th Conference…” line), missing or inconsistent figures/tables (Table 1 absent; Table 3 embedded in text; figures not shown), and many incomplete references (missing years, venues). This significantly hinders reproducibility and readability.
- Important implementation details are missing from the main text (delegated to appendices which are not present): gradient derivations, minibatch CMMD details, finite-domain conditional operator estimation. The paper as submitted is incomplete for reproduction.

Questions for the authors
1. Precisely specify the kernels for x and y in all experiments, kernel bandwidth selection, and λ values; include sensitivity plots.
2. Provide a derivation for Eq. (2) and confirm dimensional consistency of the cross-term.
3. Why use sigmoid (not softmax) for classification, and why argmax of a single sample? How do results change with averaging over multiple samples or using expected y?
4. What are the computational costs (per-epoch wall-clock) and memory footprint? Any use of low-rank approximations?
5. Add quantitative generative metrics and comparisons to cGAN/cVAE; provide ablations.
6. For distillation, report NLL/calibration and runtime speedups vs PBP.

Pros
- Conceptually appealing, simple conditional extension of GMMN with a clear operator-matching objective.
- Broad applicability; compatible with standard deep architectures.
- Some competitive predictive results; plausible conditional generations.

Cons
- Missing theoretical guarantees beyond sufficiency (no consistency/generalization).
- Many critical implementation details omitted; kernel/hyperparameter choices unspecified.
- Weak/limited empirical evaluation for conditional generation and distillation; no rigorous baselines or quantitative metrics.
- Clarity/formatting issues and incomplete references/figures.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The core idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the submission has too many methodological and presentation deficiencies to meet NeurIPS standards in its current form. A revision addressing the theoretical, empirical, and clarity gaps could be competitive. Confidence: high.
