## Review of Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the GMMN framework to handle conditional distributions. The core idea is to utilize a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to train a deep network that generates samples conditioned on input variables. The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation, demonstrating competitive performance.

While the paper presents a logical extension of GMMN and the CMMD formulation is mathematically sound, a number of shortcomings, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to significant concerns about experimental thoroughness, lead me to recommend rejection. My primary issue is the meticulousness with which these flaws accumulate, overshadowing the potentially valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Logical Extension:** Extending GMMN to the conditional case is a natural and sensible direction.
* **Mathematical Formulation:** The CMMD criterion is presented clearly and grounded in established kernel methods.
* **Broad Applicability:** The potential for application in areas like predictive modeling and contextual generation is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Inconsistency & Clarity:** The notation throughout the paper is frequently inconsistent. For example, the subscripts 's' and 'd' are introduced for datasets but their usage is not always consistent, creating confusion.  The definition of Φ and Υ varies depending on the equation, making it difficult to follow the derivations. The authors use both `L̂2CMMD` and `L2CMMD` interchangeably without clarifying if the hat signifies an empirical estimate.
* **Missing Details in CMMD Calculation:** Equation (2) for the practical CMMD calculation lacks crucial details. It assumes pre-computed kernel matrices, but doesn't specify the kernel function used (RBF, polynomial, etc.). This is a critical detail for reproducibility. Further, the regularization parameter `λ` is mentioned but its selection process is not discussed.
* **Insufficient Justification for Uniform Prior:** The choice of a uniform prior for the auxiliary variables `h` is not adequately justified.  Why is a uniform distribution preferred over, say, a Gaussian, and how does this choice impact the learned conditional distribution?
* **Experimental Setup Concerns:**  The experimental evaluation, while broad, lacks critical details.  For the MNIST experiments, the architecture is described vaguely (“3 hidden layers with hidden unit number (500, 200, 100)”). Were these layers fully connected? Dropout? Batch normalization? Similarly, for the CNN case, the architecture is referenced from [18], but a concise restatement here is necessary for self-containedness.  The optimization details are minimal. What learning rate schedule was used?
* **Limited Ablation Studies:**  The paper lacks ablation studies. How does performance change with different kernel functions, regularization parameters, network architectures, or dimensions of the hidden variable `h`? This absence limits understanding of the model's sensitivity and optimal configuration.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to baselines is weak in several cases. While including state-of-the-art models is commendable, more recent and powerful approaches (e.g., Variational Autoencoders with more advanced architectures) should have been considered.
* **Weak Discussion of Assumptions:**  The authors acknowledge the assumption that `EY |X [g(Y )|X] ∈ F`, but dismiss it quickly, stating that the empirical estimator still provides a “well-justified approximation.” This is insufficient. A more thorough discussion of the implications of violating this assumption is needed.
* **Formatting Errors:** Numerous minor formatting issues detract from the professionalism of the paper. Examples include inconsistent spacing, incorrect citation formatting (e.g. [23, 16] should likely be [16, 23] depending on publication date), and awkwardly worded sentences.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The idea of extending GMMN to conditional distributions is a logical and potentially valuable contribution.
* The theoretical framework is sound and well-motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The numerous flaws, both technical and presentational, significantly detract from the paper’s quality.
* The lack of experimental detail and ablation studies makes it difficult to assess the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach.
* The comparison to baselines is incomplete, and the paper fails to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* The issues with notation and clarity impede understanding and reproducibility.



**Overall:**

While CGMMN represents a reasonable extension to existing generative modeling techniques, the paper suffers from a pervasive lack of rigor and detail. The combination of notational inconsistencies, insufficient experimental description, missing ablation studies, and minor formatting errors leads me to believe that this work is not ready for publication at NIPS.  The authors need to address these issues comprehensively to improve the clarity, reproducibility, and overall impact of their work.
