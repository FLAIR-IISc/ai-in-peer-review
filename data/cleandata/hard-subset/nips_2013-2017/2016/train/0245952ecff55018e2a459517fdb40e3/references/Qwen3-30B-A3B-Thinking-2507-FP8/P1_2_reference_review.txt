

# Peer Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extends the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. Building upon the GMMN work by Li et al. (2015), the authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to measure the difference between conditional distributions, rather than the standard joint distributions handled by GMMN. The CGMMN architecture takes both conditional variables x and random variables h as inputs to generate target samples y. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of CGMMN across three application domains: predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on labels), and distilling Bayesian models (Bayesian dark knowledge).

The work sits in the intersection of generative modeling and conditional learning, extending the moment-matching approach pioneered by GMMN to a more general conditional setting. It builds upon related work on conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and kernel embeddings of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009), but addresses an important gap in the moment-matching literature.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and presents a solid theoretical foundation for CGMMN. The authors provide clear theorems establishing the connection between CMMD and conditional distribution equality, which is crucial for justifying their approach. The CMMD objective is well-motivated and properly derived from the kernel embedding framework.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and application domains. The authors compare CGMMN with relevant baselines including variational autoencoders, max-margin DGMs, and other deep learning architectures. The results on predictive modeling (Tables 1-2) demonstrate competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods, particularly on the SVHN dataset where CGMMN-CNN achieves an error rate of 3.13% compared to 1.92% for DSN. The generative results (Fig. 2-4) show that CGMMN can produce diverse, class-conditioned samples that are not mere copies of training data.

However, the paper has some technical limitations. The computational complexity of CMMD (cubic in sample size) is acknowledged but not thoroughly addressed. While the authors mention mini-batch techniques (Algorithm 1), they don't explore the trade-offs between computational efficiency and performance. The choice of kernel is also not discussed in depth, which is critical for MMD-based methods.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The theoretical background (Section 2) provides a solid foundation for the proposed method, and the explanations of kernel mean embeddings and conditional distributions are accessible to readers familiar with machine learning. The figures (particularly Fig. 1) effectively illustrate the CGMMN architecture.

The experimental details are sufficiently described, with network architectures, hyperparameters, and training procedures clearly specified. The results are presented in a logical manner, with appropriate tables and figures. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implementation details for the CMMD calculation, particularly regarding the regularization parameter λ.

## Originality

The paper makes a valuable extension to the GMMN framework by introducing conditional modeling. While conditional GANs (cGANs) are well-established, extending the moment-matching approach to conditional distributions is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The CMMD criterion is a meaningful contribution that builds upon existing kernel embedding techniques.

The authors correctly cite related work, including GMMN (Li et al., 2015), conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), and kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009). However, the paper could have more thoroughly compared with conditional GANs, which are the dominant approach for conditional generation. The authors acknowledge the connection to cGANs in the introduction but don't provide a direct comparison of CGMMN with cGANs in the experiments.

## Significance

The ability to learn conditional distributions is crucial for many applications in machine learning, including predictive modeling, contextual generation, and knowledge distillation. The paper demonstrates that CGMMN can achieve competitive performance across these diverse tasks, showing the framework's versatility.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the fact that conditional GANs have already established strong performance on these tasks. The paper doesn't clearly articulate why CGMMN would be preferable to cGANs beyond the theoretical advantage of avoiding adversarial training. The computational complexity of CMMD may also limit its practical utility for large-scale applications.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths:
1. Clear theoretical extension of GMMN to conditional distributions
2. Solid experimental validation across multiple application domains
3. Practical implementation details and algorithm description
4. Demonstrated ability to learn meaningful conditional distributions
5. Good comparison with relevant baselines in predictive modeling

### Weaknesses:
1. Inadequate comparison with conditional GANs (cGANs), the dominant approach for conditional generation
2. Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability
3. Insufficient exploration of kernel choices and their impact on performance
4. Knowledge distillation experiment limited to a single regression task
5. Doesn't clearly articulate advantages over existing conditional generative methods beyond theoretical simplicity

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a meaningful extension to the GMMN framework that addresses an important gap in the literature. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across multiple tasks. However, the authors should address the following concerns before acceptance:

1. Include a direct comparison with conditional GANs (cGANs) in the experiments to better establish the relative merits of the CGMMN approach.
2. Provide more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of CMMD and its practical implications.
3. Expand the discussion of kernel choices and their impact on performance.
4. Consider adding more challenging knowledge distillation experiments beyond the Boston housing regression task.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on generative modeling, particularly for those interested in moment-matching approaches. While the advantages over conditional GANs aren't fully articulated, the method offers a theoretically grounded alternative to adversarial training that deserves attention in the community. The work is technically sound, well-written, and demonstrates significant potential for practical applications.

## Specific Comments

1. In Section 4.1, the comparison with CMMVA (a max-margin DGM) is useful, but the paper should also compare with conditional GANs (cGANs), which are the most common approach for conditional generation. The authors mention cGANs in the introduction but don't provide a direct comparison in the experiments.

2. The computational complexity of CMMD (cubic in sample size) is a significant limitation that should be discussed more thoroughly. The paper mentions mini-batch techniques but doesn't explore the trade-offs between computational efficiency and performance.

3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of kernel choices. The choice of kernel has a major impact on MMD-based methods, but the paper doesn't explore this aspect.

4. In Section 4.3 (Bayesian dark knowledge), the experiment is limited to a single regression task. More challenging experiments on image data would better demonstrate the applicability of CGMMN for knowledge distillation.

5. The authors should clarify why CMMD would be preferable to other conditional generative approaches beyond the theoretical simplicity of not requiring adversarial training.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of generative modeling. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
