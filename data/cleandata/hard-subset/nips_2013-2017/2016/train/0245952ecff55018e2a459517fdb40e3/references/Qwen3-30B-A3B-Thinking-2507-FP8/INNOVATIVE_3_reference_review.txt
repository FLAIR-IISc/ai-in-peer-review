

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a novel extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) that enables learning of conditional distributions. The authors develop Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as the training objective, which extends the MMD framework to handle conditional distributions. The key insight is that CGMMN can model p(y|x) by drawing samples from a simple distribution and passing them through a deep network along with the conditional variables x. This approach allows CGMMN to address a range of applications including predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

The paper builds on the GMMN framework (Li et al., 2015) and extends it to conditional distributions, which is a significant gap in the literature. While conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., 2015) exist, this work provides the first conditional extension of moment-matching networks, which have advantages over GANs in training stability. The theoretical development of CMMD is sound and properly builds on the kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009).

The paper makes a strong case for the novelty of its contribution by clearly stating that "Conditional modeling has been practiced as a natural step towards improving the discriminative ability of a statistical model and/or relaxing unnecessary assumptions of the conditional variables" but that moment-matching networks have not been extended to handle conditional distributions. The authors convincingly argue that this extension "fills the research void to significantly improve the applicability of moment-matching networks."

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering three distinct application areas: predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN classification), contextual generation (MNIST and Yale Face datasets), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing regression). The results show that CGMMN achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods across these diverse tasks. For instance, on SVHN, CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error rate compared to CMMVA's 3.09% (the state-of-the-art for DGMs on this task), and on MNIST classification, CGMMN-CNN achieves competitive performance without data augmentation or model averaging.

The paper's main strength is its novel conceptual contribution: extending the moment-matching framework to conditional distributions. This is a significant step that opens up new applications for moment-matching networks while maintaining their training simplicity. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for CMMD and demonstrate its practical utility across multiple domains.

Potential weaknesses include:
1. The paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison with conditional GANs, which are the dominant conditional generative model approach. This would strengthen the position that CGMMN is a meaningful alternative.
2. The computational complexity of CMMD (involving kernel matrix inversions) isn't thoroughly analyzed for large-scale applications.
3. The paper mentions using low-dimensional hidden spaces for classification tasks but doesn't explore how this dimensionality affects performance.

However, as a reviewer prioritizing novelty over methodological perfection, I believe these limitations are outweighed by the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The extension of moment-matching networks to handle conditional distributions is a substantial advancement that addresses a clear gap in the literature. The authors successfully demonstrate the utility of their approach across multiple application domains, showing that CGMMN can be competitive with state-of-the-art methods without requiring complex architectures or extensive data augmentation.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and properly situates its contribution within the broader literature. The theoretical development is sound, and the experimental results are compelling. While not all details are fully explored (e.g., computational complexity analysis), the core contribution is novel, well-justified, and practically significant.

I believe this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of generative modeling. It introduces a significant new concept that extends the applicability of moment-matching networks to conditional distributions, which has been an important gap in the literature. The authors demonstrate the practical utility of their approach across multiple challenging tasks, showing competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

For these reasons, I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a bold and novel approach that advances the state of the art in generative modeling by extending moment-matching networks to handle conditional distributions. This work will likely inspire future research on conditional generative models and provide a valuable alternative to conditional GANs for certain applications.
