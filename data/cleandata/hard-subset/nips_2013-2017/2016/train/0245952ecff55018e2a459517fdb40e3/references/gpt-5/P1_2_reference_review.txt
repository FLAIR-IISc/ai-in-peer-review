Summary
The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMNs to conditional distributions p(y|x). The key contribution is Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the discrepancy between conditional distributions via the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between conditional mean operators CY|X in RKHS. A deep generator y = f(x, h; w) maps conditioning variables x and auxiliary noise h to outputs, and parameters are learned by minimizing a minibatch CMMD objective computed with kernel Gram matrices over both x and y. The paper provides a sufficient-condition theorem: if CY|X coincide (under universality and smoothness assumptions), then the conditional distributions coincide. Experiments cover three use cases: (i) predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), (ii) conditional generation (MNIST digits and Yale Faces), and (iii) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (PBP on Boston Housing).

Relation to prior work: The approach builds directly on GMMNs trained with MMD, replacing unconditional MMD with a conditional operator norm grounded in kernel embeddings of conditional distributions (Song et al.). It provides an alternative to cGANs and conditional VAEs for conditional modeling, with a simpler single-objective training (no min–max, no variational bound). The paper also connects to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation by using CGMMN as a student model.

Quality
- Theory: The CMMD criterion is well-motivated from kernel mean embedding theory. The theorem giving sufficiency of equal operators is standard and appropriate; the estimator uses regularization (K + λI)−1 and is implementable. However, there is no statistical analysis of consistency or sample complexity of training the deep generator under CMMD, and the strong assumptions (universality, EY|X[g|X] ∈ F, operator class) are not discussed in terms of their practical plausibility. The paper acknowledges the approximations but provides no guidance on λ, kernel choice, or sensitivity.
- Optimization/computation: The minibatch algorithm is straightforward, and the note that inverses in CMMD do not depend on w is useful. However, the cubic cost per minibatch and memory footprint can still be large; no timing, scaling analysis, or kernel approximation (Nyström/RFF) is explored.
- Experiments: The classification results are reasonable (competitive with MMVA; below strong discriminative baselines). Conditional generation is demonstrated qualitatively; nearest-neighbor checks are provided. The distillation experiment shows no degradation on a small regression dataset. However, generative results lack quantitative evaluation (e.g., Parzen log-likelihood at the time; inception-based metrics later) and comparisons to cGAN/CVAE. For classification, there is no comparison to the same architectures trained with cross-entropy, which would be a strong and relevant baseline. Details needed for reproducibility (kernel types, bandwidth selection, λ, exact architectures/hyperparameters for each task) are incomplete.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The derivations of CMMD and the operator estimator are clear. Some clarity gaps:
- Kernel choices for x and y, bandwidth/σ selection, and sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Practicalities of estimating CY|X for discrete vs. continuous x are deferred to the appendix; more main-text guidance would help.
- Figures show plausible samples but some are noisy; captions could better describe training settings.
- Minor typos and referencing inconsistencies exist.

Originality
Extending MMD-based training to conditional models via conditional mean embeddings is a natural and nontrivial idea. To my knowledge, no prior DGM used the conditional operator norm as a training objective at that time. While cGANs/CVAEs already address conditional generation, the CMMD-driven training offers a distinct, stable alternative with a principled kernel-based objective. The connection to Bayesian distillation is also interesting, though the experimental validation is limited.

Significance
If adopted, CMMD provides a general tool for training conditional generators without adversarial games or variational bounds, and can be plugged into arbitrary architectures. The approach may appeal to practitioners seeking stable training and kernel control over conditionals. However, the empirical evidence is not yet strong enough to show clear state-of-the-art improvements or broad applicability beyond toy/medium-scale datasets. Additional quantitative generation metrics and stronger baselines would enhance impact.

Pros
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMN to conditional distributions using well-founded conditional mean embeddings.
- Simple, stable training via a single objective; no adversarial min–max.
- Flexibility: any deep architecture; kernels over x need not be differentiable.
- Demonstrates utility across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Theoretical grounding and practical estimator clearly presented.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is limited: no quantitative generative metrics; no comparisons to cGAN/CVAE; classification not compared against identical architectures trained with cross-entropy.
- Computational aspects (cubic cost, scalability, kernel approximation) not addressed empirically.
- No analysis of sensitivity to kernel choice, bandwidth, λ, or minibatch size.
- Theoretical assumptions are strong; no consistency/estimation guarantees for the learned network.
- Distillation experiment is minimal; no uncertainty calibration or comparison to prior dark-knowledge methods.

Questions for authors
- What kernels were used for x and y in each experiment? How were bandwidths and λ chosen? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- What is the computational cost per minibatch and overall training time vs. cGAN/CVAE or cross-entropy baselines? Any memory issues?
- Can you report quantitative generative metrics and include cGAN/CVAE comparisons on MNIST/Yale?
- For classification, how does CGMMN perform vs. the same CNN trained with cross-entropy? Are the predicted y vectors well-calibrated?
- Could you incorporate Nyström or random Fourier features to improve scalability, and how does that affect performance?

Suggestions
- Add quantitative generation evaluations and comparisons to conditional baselines (cGAN, CVAE).
- Include stronger discriminative baselines using the same architectures.
- Report ablations on kernel/bandwidth/λ and minibatch size; discuss practical guidelines.
- Provide runtime/memory benchmarks and consider kernel approximations.
- Expand distillation to larger datasets and report calibration metrics.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a novel, principled, and simple objective for conditional generative modeling with promising results. With stronger experimental comparisons and quantitative evaluations, it would be a solid contribution. As is, it is interesting and technically sound, but the empirical section needs strengthening to maximize impact.
