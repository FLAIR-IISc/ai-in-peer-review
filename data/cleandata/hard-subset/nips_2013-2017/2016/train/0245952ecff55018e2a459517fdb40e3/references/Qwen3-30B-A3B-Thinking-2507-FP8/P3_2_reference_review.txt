

# Peer Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extends the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors develop Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a training objective for conditional generative models, allowing the model to learn a flexible conditional distribution p(y|x) by matching the kernel mean embedding of the empirical conditional distribution with that of the generative model. The paper demonstrates CGMMN's effectiveness across three tasks: predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on class labels), and distilling Bayesian models (Bayesian dark knowledge).

The paper builds on the GMMN work by Li et al. (2015) and extends it to conditional distributions using kernel embedding techniques for conditional distributions. The theoretical foundation is well-established, with three key theorems supporting the CMMD objective.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for CMMD, building on kernel embedding and maximum mean discrepancy concepts. The extension from MMD to CMMD is logically developed and well-motivated.

2. **Simplicity of Training**: The authors successfully maintain the simplicity of the GMMN training process (using backpropagation) while extending to conditional distributions, which is a significant advantage over many other conditional generative models.

3. **Diverse Experimental Validation**: The paper demonstrates CGMMN's effectiveness across multiple application domains (predictive modeling, contextual generation, Bayesian knowledge distillation), which is valuable for showing the framework's versatility.

4. **Clear Connection to Existing Work**: The paper properly contextualizes its work with respect to GMMN, conditional GANs, and other related approaches, showing where CGMMN fits in the broader landscape of generative models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Direct Comparison with State-of-the-Art Conditional Generative Models**: This is the paper's most significant weakness. The authors claim CGMMN "fills the research void to significantly improve the applicability of moment-matching networks," but fail to directly compare with the most relevant existing methods (conditional GANs and conditional VAEs). For example:
   - The paper cites Mirza & Osindero (2014) for conditional GANs but doesn't compare CGMMN's generation quality or training efficiency to cGANs.
   - The MNIST generation results in Figure 2 are not compared to cGAN or CVAE samples, making it difficult to assess if CGMMN offers any qualitative advantages.
   - The paper mentions "CGMMN is compatible with various network architectures" but doesn't show how it compares to cGANs with the same architectures.

2. **Theoretical Assumptions Not Fully Justified**: The paper assumes CY|X ∈ F⊗G (which requires C-3/2XX CXY is Hilbert-Schmidt) for the CMMD objective to be well-defined. While the authors note this in a remark, they don't provide evidence that this assumption holds in practice for the datasets and kernels they use. For instance, they don't discuss how the choice of kernel or regularization parameter λ affects this assumption.

3. **Inadequate Experimental Details**: 
   - The paper lacks sufficient detail about hyperparameters (e.g., kernel choices, λ values, dimensionality of auxiliary variables) that would be necessary for reproducibility.
   - The MNIST generation results (Figure 2) show some noisy samples, but the paper doesn't compare the quality to other methods or discuss the limitations of the approach for high-quality image generation.
   - The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment lacks details about how the teacher model (PBP) was trained and how the perturbation of inputs was implemented.

4. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims CGMMN "can obtain competitive performance in all these tasks," but the results don't demonstrate significant advantages over existing methods. For example:
   - On MNIST classification, CGMMN-CNN achieves 0.95% error rate, while DSN (a discriminative model) achieves 0.91%.
   - On SVHN, CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error rate, while DSN achieves 1.92%.
   - These are not substantial improvements over existing methods.

5. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of the CMMD approach compared to other conditional generative models. For example:
   - How does CGMMN handle complex multimodal distributions?
   - What are the computational limitations (e.g., cubic complexity of kernel gram matrix computation) for large datasets?
   - How does the performance scale with the dimensionality of the conditional variables?

## Specific Comments

1. **Theoretical Development**: The development of CMMD is theoretically sound, but the connection to the practical implementation could be clearer. The paper states that "the inverse matrices K̃-1s and K̃-1d in the CMMD objective are independent of the model parameters," but doesn't explain how this affects the practical implementation of the gradient.

2. **Experimental Design**: The paper uses a uniform prior for hidden variables (p(h) = U(hi)), but doesn't justify why this choice is optimal or explore how other priors might affect performance. The dimensionality of the auxiliary variables (5 for MLP, 20 for generation) is fixed without sensitivity analysis.

3. **Comparison with Related Work**: The paper cites conditional GANs and conditional VAEs but fails to provide a detailed comparison with these methods in the experiments. For instance, Table 1 should include cGAN and CVAE results for fair comparison.

4. **Reproducibility**: The paper mentions "Appendix A.2.1" for details on the minibatch version, but Appendix A is not included in the provided text. This makes it difficult to assess the practical implementation details.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound in its theoretical development, but the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail and comparison to establish the method's true merits. The claims about performance are not strongly supported by the experimental results.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a good introduction to the background concepts. However, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality**: The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions is a reasonable theoretical extension, but the novelty is limited as it builds directly on existing kernel embedding techniques. The paper doesn't clearly distinguish itself from conditional GANs and CVAEs, which are the dominant approaches for conditional generation.

**Significance**: While CGMMN demonstrates reasonable performance across multiple tasks, it doesn't offer a significant advance over existing methods. The competitive results don't translate to a compelling case for why CGMMN should be preferred over more established conditional generative models like cGANs.

## Recommendation

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a theoretically grounded extension of GMMN to conditional distributions, it fails to demonstrate significant advantages over existing methods. The lack of direct comparison with conditional GANs (the most relevant prior work for conditional generation) is a critical omission that undermines the paper's claims. The results are competitive but not groundbreaking, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify CGMMN as a meaningful advance in the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a direct comparison with conditional GANs and conditional VAEs in the experimental section, including both quantitative metrics and qualitative generation examples.

2. Provide more detail about the implementation, including kernel choices, regularization parameters, and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.

3. Discuss the limitations of the CMMD approach more thoroughly, particularly its performance on complex multimodal distributions and computational scalability.

4. Clarify the theoretical assumptions and provide evidence that they hold for the practical scenarios considered in the experiments.

5. Include statistical significance testing for the performance comparisons to better establish the reliability of the results.

The paper has potential, but as written, it doesn't demonstrate sufficient novelty or advantage to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors would need to significantly strengthen the experimental comparison with state-of-the-art conditional generative models to make a compelling case for their approach.
