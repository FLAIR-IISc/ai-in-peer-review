

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to conditional distributions. The authors define a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion for training conditional generative models and demonstrate applications in predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

**Strengths:**
The paper provides a clear theoretical extension of MMD to conditional distributions, with well-structured mathematical derivations. The experimental validation across multiple tasks (classification, generation, and knowledge distillation) demonstrates that CGMMN can achieve competitive performance. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient technical detail for reproduction.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty:** The core idea of extending moment-matching networks to conditional distributions is not novel. Conditional GANs (CGANs) were introduced in [21] and have since been widely adopted. The paper fails to convincingly argue why CMMD is superior to CGANs or other established conditional generative approaches. The comparison with CGANs is notably absent from the experimental section, which is a significant omission given that CGANs are the dominant approach for conditional generation.

2. **Insufficient experimental validation:** The authors claim CGMMN is "competitive" with state-of-the-art methods, but the results do not demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods. For instance, on MNIST, CGMMN-CNN achieves 1.28% error rate compared to DSN's 1.92% (Table 1), but the paper does not provide statistical significance testing for this difference. On SVHN, CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error rate compared to CMMVA's 3.09% (Table 2), suggesting marginal improvement at best.

3. **Lack of comparison with relevant baselines:** The paper omits crucial comparisons with conditional variational autoencoders (CVAEs) [28], which are a standard approach for conditional generation and have been extensively studied in the literature. CVAEs are particularly relevant since the authors cite them in the related work but fail to compare against them in experiments. This omission undermines the claim that CGMMN "fills the research void" in conditional moment-matching networks.

4. **Practical scalability concerns:** The paper notes that "the inverse matrices K̃−1s and K̃−1d in the CMMD objective are independent of the model parameters," which is a significant limitation. The cubic computational complexity of kernel matrix inversion (O(N^3)) makes this approach impractical for large-scale datasets, which are common in modern deep learning applications. The authors provide a mini-batch version (Algorithm 1), but the paper does not evaluate the impact of mini-batching on performance or demonstrate scalability on larger datasets.

5. **Overstated significance:** The paper claims that CGMMN "largely extends the capability of GMMN," but the extension to conditional distributions is straightforward and does not fundamentally advance the moment-matching approach. The paper fails to demonstrate how CGMMN addresses the known limitations of GMMN (e.g., mode collapse in generative models), which would be necessary to justify the extension.

**Specific concerns:**

- The paper states that CGMMN "can obtain competitive performance in all these tasks," but the results for generative tasks (Fig. 2-5) show images with noticeable noise and artifacts, particularly when compared to state-of-the-art GAN-based methods. The authors attempt to mitigate this by combining with autoencoders (Fig. 4), but this suggests the core CGMMN approach may not be sufficiently effective on its own.

- The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment (Section 4.3) is too limited, testing only on a single regression dataset (Boston housing) and comparing with one baseline (PBP). More comprehensive evaluation on classification tasks or with multiple Bayesian models would strengthen this contribution.

- The paper claims that "CGMMN is compatible with various network architectures," but the experimental results do not demonstrate substantial performance gains with more complex architectures, which would be necessary to support this claim.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents a technically sound extension of GMMN to conditional distributions, the novelty is limited, and the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims of competitiveness with state-of-the-art methods. The paper fails to provide a convincing comparison with the dominant conditional generative approach (CGANs) and omits relevant baselines (CVAEs). The scalability concerns and lack of significant performance improvements over existing methods undermine the paper's significance.

The paper does not make a compelling case for why the proposed CMMD criterion is preferable to existing approaches for conditional generation. The incremental nature of the contribution, combined with the lack of thorough experimental validation against established baselines, makes it difficult to justify publication at NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper presents an incremental extension of an existing method without sufficient evidence of significant advantages over established approaches. The experimental validation is incomplete and fails to address key concerns about scalability and comparison with relevant baselines.
