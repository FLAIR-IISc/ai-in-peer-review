### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes the Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Network (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The core idea is to train a deep neural network that maps a conditional input `x` and a random noise vector `h` to an output sample `y`. The network's parameters are learned by minimizing a novel objective function called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). CMMD measures the dissimilarity between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective kernel mean embedding operators. This approach avoids the unstable min-max optimization of Conditional GANs while providing a flexible framework for conditional density estimation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN on a diverse set of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), contextual generation (generating images of digits and faces conditioned on labels), and distilling knowledge from complex Bayesian models.

### Strengths and Positive Aspects

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a logical and important extension of the moment-matching network paradigm. While conditional versions of VAEs and GANs existed at the time of publication, this work fills a clear gap by providing a conditional formulation for MMD-based deep generative models. This offers a valuable alternative to the existing frameworks, particularly given the training stability challenges associated with GANs. The proposed CMMD objective is a principled method for comparing conditional distributions, grounded in the established theory of kernel embeddings.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical motivation for the CMMD objective is sound. It builds directly upon prior work on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions [29] and the properties of MMD [7]. The derivation of the empirical CMMD estimator (Eq. 2) is clear and directly translates the theoretical concept into a computable loss function. The proposed generative architecture `y = f(x, h|w)` is a standard and effective way to model conditional generation, making the overall framework easy to understand and implement.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** A major strength of this paper is the breadth of its experiments. The authors don't just show that the model can perform a single task well; they demonstrate its versatility across three distinct domains:
    *   **Predictive Modeling:** The competitive classification results on MNIST and SVHN show that the model can be used for discriminative tasks, directly modeling `p(label|image)`. This is a strong demonstration that CGMMN is not just a generative tool.
    *   **Contextual Generation:** The experiments on MNIST and Yale Faces show the model's ability in the canonical conditional generation task, `p(image|label)`. The generated samples are plausible and conditioned correctly on the input label.
    *   **Bayesian Dark Knowledge:** This is a particularly clever and insightful application. Using CGMMN to distill a complex Bayesian predictive distribution into a single, efficient deep network showcases the framework's flexibility to approximate any conditional data-generating process, not just one defined by a static dataset.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It starts by clearly motivating the need for conditional models, provides the necessary background on MMD and kernel embeddings, and then smoothly introduces the core contributions of CMMD and the CGMMN architecture. The connection between MMD and CMMD (Remark 1) is insightful, highlighting that CMMD is effectively a weighted version of MMD where weights are determined by the conditional variables.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The primary weakness of the proposed method is its computational cost. The CMMD objective in Eq. 2 requires computing and inverting two kernel Gram matrices (`Kd` and `Ks`) of size `B x B`, where `B` is the mini-batch size. This matrix inversion operation has a complexity of O(B³). This is a significant computational bottleneck compared to GANs and VAEs, whose per-batch complexity is typically linear in `B`. This scalability issue limits the batch sizes that can be used in practice and may slow down training considerably. The paper mentions a mini-batch algorithm but does not adequately discuss this limitation or potential workarounds (e.g., low-rank approximations).

2.  **Sensitivity to Kernel Choice and Hyperparameters:** The performance of all MMD-based methods is highly dependent on the choice of kernel and its associated hyperparameters (e.g., the bandwidth of an RBF kernel). This paper does not provide any discussion on how the kernels for the `x` and `y` spaces were chosen or how their parameters were tuned. This is a critical detail for reproducibility and for understanding the practical usability of the method. Similarly, the regularization parameter `λ` in the matrix inversion is another key hyperparameter whose effect is not discussed. A sensitivity analysis or at least a description of the tuning process would significantly strengthen the paper.

3.  **Qualitative Generative Performance:** While the generated samples are reasonable, their quality, particularly in Figure 2 for MNIST, is not state-of-the-art compared to contemporary GAN-based methods. The images are noticeably noisy and lack the sharpness seen in other generative models. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by including a second experiment where CGMMN is combined with an autoencoder to produce cleaner images (Figure 4). While this is a valid approach, it suggests that the base CGMMN model may struggle with generating high-fidelity samples directly in pixel space. A more direct comparison and discussion against conditional GANs on generative quality would be beneficial.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative models. It introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and versatile framework for conditional density estimation that serves as a compelling alternative to conditional GANs and VAEs. The key strengths are the principled CMMD objective, the simple and stable training procedure (compared to the min-max game of GANs), and the impressive breadth of successful applications. The main weaknesses are the O(B³) computational complexity of the objective and the lack of discussion regarding kernel selection and hyperparameter sensitivity. Despite these limitations, the paper's originality, technical quality, and demonstrated significance make it a clear contribution to the NIPS community. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by future research in moment-matching networks and generative modeling in general.
