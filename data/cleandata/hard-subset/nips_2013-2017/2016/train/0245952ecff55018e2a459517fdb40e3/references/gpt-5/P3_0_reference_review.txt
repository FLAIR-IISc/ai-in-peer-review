Summary and contributions
The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional extension of GMMNs that uses conditional mean embeddings in RKHS to define a training objective, conditional MMD (CMMD). A conditional generator y = f(x, h; w) is trained to match the conditional distribution P(Y|X) by minimizing the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between empirical conditional embedding operators estimated from real and generated (x, y) pairs. The CMMD objective can be computed from Gram matrices, enabling gradient-based training through the kernel on Y, while the kernel on X need not be differentiable. The paper provides a sufficient condition (Theorem 3) that equality of conditional embedding operators implies equality of conditionals, and shows applications to classification on MNIST/SVHN, conditional generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and distillation of a Bayesian regressor (PBP) on Boston Housing.

Relation to prior work
CGMMN builds directly on GMMN and kernel embeddings of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009/2013; Fukumizu et al.), extending the MMD-based training paradigm to conditional settings. It is closely related to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al.), but offers a simpler, non-adversarial training objective and avoids variational bounds. The use of conditional mean embeddings as an explicit CMMD criterion is, to my knowledge, novel in deep generative modeling, and the operator-based derivation connects nicely to kernelized regression interpretations.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The extension from MMD to CMMD via conditional mean operators is natural and well motivated. The derivation and the “weighted MMD” perspective are insightful.
- Simplicity and stability: Training is a single-objective minimization (no min–max), which in practice is often more stable than GANs.
- Flexibility: CGMMN applies to prediction, conditional generation, and teacher-student distillation. The observation that the kernel on X can be non-differentiable is practical.
- Empirical breadth: Results span classification, label-conditional image synthesis, and Bayesian dark knowledge, suggesting generality.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Theorem 3 assumptions and correctness: The theorem states that CY|X = CZ|X implies PY|X = PZ|X. Typically, equality of conditional mean embeddings implies equality of conditionals when the kernel on Y is characteristic (or universal) and equality holds for almost all x under P_X. The current statement assumes universality on F (X side) and conditions on EY|X[g|X]∈F, but does not require characteristic kernels on Y nor clarifies “for all x” vs “P_X-a.e.”. Please clarify the precise conditions (characteristic kernel on Y; support/measure-theoretic caveats) and whether equality holds almost surely or pointwise only when {φ(x)} spans F.
- Estimation bias and regularization: The empirical operator Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ introduces λ-regularization. CMMD with λ>0 is a biased proxy; the paper does not discuss the impact of λ on estimation consistency or the optimization landscape. Guidance for λ and kernel bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic, multiple kernels) is missing.
- Computational complexity: Each step requires inverting K̃∈R^{b×b} (b=batch size), so O(b^3) per batch. With batch sizes of 300–500, this is nontrivial. There is little reporting on runtime, memory, or scalability to larger datasets or higher-resolution images. Approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features) are not explored.
- Empirical comparisons for generation are weak: The conditional generation experiments are on MNIST and Yale Faces only, with qualitative figures and nearest-neighbor checks. There is no comparison to cGAN or CVAE on the same tasks, nor quantitative metrics (even simple Parzen estimates, NLL surrogates, or FID/IS where appropriate). The AE+CGMMN variant echoes GMMN+AE; again, no comparison to conditional counterparts (e.g., CVAE on codes).
- Predictive modeling setup: Treating classification as conditional generation and picking argmax from a single sample is unusual; accuracy trails strong discriminative baselines on SVHN and matches prior DGM (MMVA) on MNIST/SVHN without clear advantages. There is no analysis of calibration, uncertainty, or robustness—purported advantages of probabilistic modeling.
- Practical details missing: Choice of kernels on X and Y (types, bandwidths), sensitivity analyses, and the effect of the noise dimension for h are not reported. The claim that kernels on X need not be differentiable is useful, but the actual kernel used for images (RBF over pixels?) and its preprocessing are unspecified.
- Distillation: The Boston Housing result matches PBP, but the experiment is small-scale. There is no comparison to the original “Bayesian Dark Knowledge” training objective (e.g., KL on predictive distributions), nor an analysis of predictive uncertainty or runtime gains.

Quality
The method is technically sound at a high level, with a correct operator-based CMMD objective and a practical training algorithm. However, theoretical conditions in Theorem 3 need tightening, and empirical evaluation lacks stronger baselines and quantitative generative metrics. Practical choices (kernels, λ) and computational costs are under-specified.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with a clear derivation of CMMD and algorithm. Some typos (e.g., “MINIST”, “adopted as well”, “out network”), and certain practical details are omitted (kernel/bandwidth selection, runtime). Figures are helpful but low-res.

Originality
Moderate. The extension of GMMN to conditional modeling via conditional mean embeddings is novel in deep deep generative modeling, though the building blocks (GMMN, conditional embeddings) are established. The positioning relative to cGAN/CVAE could be strengthened.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners seeking non-adversarial conditional generative training. However, without stronger empirical evidence (especially versus cGAN/CVAE), the impact is limited. The distillation angle is interesting but preliminary.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please clarify Theorem 3: assumptions on characteristic kernels on Y, “for all x” vs “P_X-a.e.”, and references (e.g., Fukumizu et al., 2011; Grunewälder et al., 2012) supporting this equivalence.
- Detail kernel choices for X and Y in all experiments, bandwidth/λ selection methods, and provide sensitivity analyses.
- Report training time, per-iteration cost as a function of batch size, and memory use. Consider scalable approximations (Nyström, random features) and compare accuracy/speed trade-offs.
- Add quantitative generative comparisons to cGAN and CVAE on MNIST/Yale (and ideally a harder dataset), and include standard metrics or human Turing tests where appropriate.
- For classification, compare to cross-entropy-trained models with similar architectures and analyze calibration/uncertainty.
- In distillation, compare to the original dark knowledge loss and evaluate predictive uncertainty (e.g., NLL) and inference speedups.

Pros
- Clean, principled extension of MMD training to conditional models.
- Simple and stable training objective; works across tasks.
- Solid kernel-embedding grounding; operator view is elegant.

Cons
- Theory conditions need clarification; estimator bias unaddressed.
- Computational cost and scalability not convincingly handled.
- Empirical comparisons for generation are limited; no cGAN/CVAE baselines.
- Practical details (kernels, hyperparameters) and ablations are missing.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is neat and likely of interest to the community, but I would like to see stronger empirical comparisons and tighter theoretical statements. If space permits revisions addressing the most critical points (theoretical conditions, kernel/hyperparameter details, and at least one direct comparison to cGAN/CVAE), this would merit acceptance.

Confidence: high.
