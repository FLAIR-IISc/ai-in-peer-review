This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional distributions `p(y|x)` within the framework of deep generative models. The work is a direct extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which model joint distributions by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between generated samples and real data. The core contribution is the development of a novel training criterion, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions. CMMD is defined as the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between the kernel mean embedding operators of the conditional distributions. The proposed CGMMN architecture is a deep neural network that takes a conditional variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to generate a sample `y`. The network's parameters are learned via back-propagation to minimize the CMMD loss between the model's conditional distribution and the empirical conditional distribution from the training data. This work is analogous to the extension of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) to Conditional GANs and fills an important gap in the literature for moment-matching based generative models. The authors demonstrate the model's efficacy across a range of tasks, including predictive modeling, contextual image generation, and knowledge distillation from Bayesian models.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed CMMD criterion is a well-founded extension of MMD, grounded in the established theory of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009). The derivation of the empirical CMMD estimator (Equation 2) is a direct and valid application of this theory. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering three distinct application areas (prediction, generation, distillation) on multiple standard datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Faces, Boston Housing). The results are strong and competitive with relevant baselines, particularly other deep generative models like CMMVA, which substantiates the claims of the paper and demonstrates the viability of the proposed approach.

**Originality:** The main contribution—formulating the CMMD objective and applying it to train a deep conditional generative model—is novel. While the concept of conditional generation is well-established (e.g., Conditional GANs, Conditional VAEs), this paper is the first to develop a conditional framework for moment-matching networks. This represents a significant and original step, broadening the GMMN family of models. Furthermore, the application to distilling "Bayesian dark knowledge" is an interesting and less-explored use case that highlights the model's versatility.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for conditional modeling is clearly articulated in the introduction. The background section provides a concise yet sufficient overview of MMD and conditional kernel embeddings, making the subsequent technical development easy to follow. The relationship between MMD and CMMD is elucidated in Remark 1, providing useful intuition. The overall architecture and training algorithm are presented clearly.

**Significance:** This work significantly increases the scope and applicability of moment-matching networks, extending them from unconditional to conditional generation. This is an important contribution because GMMNs offer an alternative to GANs that replaces the often-unstable min-max optimization with a simpler objective function, which can lead to more stable training. By enabling conditional modeling, CGMMN makes this potentially more stable training paradigm available for a much wider range of tasks, such as structured prediction, image-to-image translation, and data imputation. The method's demonstrated flexibility across different tasks suggests it could become a valuable tool for both researchers and practitioners.

### Weaknesses

**Quality/Scalability:** The primary technical weakness lies in the computational cost of the CMMD objective. The loss calculation requires inverting two kernel matrices (`K_d + λI` and `K_s + λI`), an operation that scales as O(N^3) with the mini-batch size N. While the paper proposes a mini-batch algorithm, this cubic scaling remains a significant bottleneck that may limit the model's application to problems requiring very large batch sizes. A more detailed discussion of this limitation and a comparison of the computational complexity against alternatives like Conditional GANs would strengthen the paper.

**Clarity/Reproducibility:** Several key experimental details are underspecified, which could hinder reproducibility.
1.  **Kernel Choice and Hyperparameters:** The paper does not specify the type of kernels used for the input variables `x` and output variables `y` (e.g., RBF, linear) or the method for selecting kernel hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidth) and the regularization parameter `λ`. These choices are critical to the performance of any kernel-based method.
2.  **Sampling Strategy:** For estimating the CMMD objective, samples are drawn from the generator using conditional variables `x` from the training set. The paper mentions this can be done "with/without small perturbation," but does not clarify which strategy was used in each experiment or analyze the impact of this choice.
3.  **Derivations:** While the final form of the CMMD objective in Equation 2 is presented, the derivation from the Hilbert-Schmidt norm is omitted. Including a brief derivation in the appendix would improve the paper's self-containedness and clarity.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated extension of GMMNs to the conditional setting.
*   The proposed CMMD objective is theoretically sound and grounded in established kernel methods literature.
*   Provides an alternative to Conditional GANs that avoids unstable min-max optimization.
*   Demonstrates strong, competitive results across a diverse set of tasks, highlighting the method's versatility and significance.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-structured.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational complexity of the CMMD objective (O(N^3) in batch size) is a significant practical limitation that is not sufficiently discussed.
*   Key details required for reproducibility, such as kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning, are missing from the main text.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty, technical soundness, and potential significance, outweigh its weaknesses. The work introduces a valuable new tool to the deep generative modeling toolbox. The concerns regarding reproducibility can be addressed by adding the missing details to the appendix.
