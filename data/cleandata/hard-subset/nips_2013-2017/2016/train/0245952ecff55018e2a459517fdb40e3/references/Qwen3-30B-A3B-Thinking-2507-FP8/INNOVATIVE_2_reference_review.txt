

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a training objective, which measures the difference between kernel mean embeddings of empirical conditional distributions and generative models. The approach provides a simpler training procedure than conditional GANs by avoiding min-max optimization, while maintaining the ability to learn flexible conditional distributions.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it generalizes the MMD framework to conditional distributions, establishing a theoretical foundation with Theorem 3 showing that equal conditional embedding operators imply identical conditional distributions. Second, it develops a practical training algorithm (Algorithm 1) that uses backpropagation to optimize the CMMD objective. Third, it demonstrates the versatility of CGMMN across multiple application domains: predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While conditional GANs have been explored (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), CGMMN provides a fundamentally different training approach based on moment matching rather than adversarial learning. The conditional extension to GMMN fills a clear gap in the literature, as the authors rightly note that conditional distributions are essential building blocks for many generative tasks. The theoretical development of CMMD (Equation 2) is elegant and represents a meaningful advancement over previous moment-matching approaches.

The experimental results are compelling across multiple domains. For predictive modeling, CGMMN-CNN achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art discriminative models on MNIST and SVHN. For generation, the model produces class-conditioned samples that are not merely copies of training data (Fig. 3), and combining with autoencoders yields higher-quality images (Fig. 4). The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment (Table 3) demonstrates practical utility for distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

The paper's strengths are its conceptual novelty, clear theoretical foundation, and demonstrated versatility. The authors have successfully extended GMMN to handle conditional distributions in a principled way, avoiding the training instability of conditional GANs. The simplicity of the approach (training via standard backpropagation) is particularly appealing.

While the paper is strong, there are minor weaknesses that could be addressed. The comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods on MNIST/SVHN could be more comprehensive (e.g., including results from recent ResNet-based approaches). The theoretical assumptions (e.g., universality of RKHS) could be more thoroughly discussed in relation to practical implementation. The scalability of CMMD to larger datasets or higher-dimensional conditionals isn't fully explored.

However, these are minor concerns that don't detract from the paper's core contribution. The conditional extension to GMMN represents a significant conceptual advance that opens new possibilities for generative modeling. The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation, a practical algorithm, and demonstrates utility across multiple application domains.

Given my emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, I believe this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It extends an existing framework in a non-obvious way to address a common need in generative modeling (conditional distributions), and does so with a theoretically sound approach that avoids the pitfalls of adversarial training. The versatility demonstrated across multiple tasks further strengthens the contribution.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. While not all aspects of the approach are thoroughly explored, the core idea is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept** 

**Pro Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical contribution (CMMD) extending GMMN to conditional distributions
- Simpler training procedure than conditional GANs (no min-max optimization)
- Demonstrated versatility across multiple application domains
- Clear theoretical foundation with well-explained theorems
- Competitive results on multiple benchmarks

**Con Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with the very latest discriminative models
- Some theoretical assumptions could be more thoroughly discussed
- Scalability to very large datasets could be better addressed

The paper makes a significant contribution to generative modeling by extending the moment-matching framework to handle conditional distributions in a principled way. This represents a bold advancement that addresses a clear gap in the literature and has demonstrated utility across multiple application domains. I recommend acceptance.
