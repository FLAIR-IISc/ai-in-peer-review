

# Peer Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extends the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to measure the difference between conditional distributions and present a conditional generative model that can sample from conditional distributions given input variables. The framework is evaluated across three application domains: predictive modeling (MNIST and SVHN classification), contextual generation (MNIST and Yale Face datasets), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing regression). The paper builds on previous work by Li et al. (2015) on GMMN and relates to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., 2015), but makes the key contribution of extending the MMD-based generative framework to conditional settings.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a sound theoretical foundation with Theorem 3 establishing the sufficient condition for conditional distribution matching using the proposed CMMD criterion.
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple applications and datasets with appropriate baselines (CMMVA, DSN, CNN, etc.).
- Implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, including network architectures, hyperparameters, and training procedures.
- The authors acknowledge limitations and provide appropriate comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with conditional VAEs and other conditional generative models beyond GANs. For instance, it doesn't compare sample quality or training stability with conditional VAEs.
- The theoretical analysis of the CMMD estimator is limited. While the paper states "asymptotically, L̂2MMD is an unbiased estimator," it doesn't provide similar guarantees for CMMD, such as convergence rates or sample complexity.
- The results on SVHN (3.13% error rate for CGMMN-CNN vs. 1.92% for DSN) show a significant gap that the authors acknowledge but don't adequately address. The claim that "CGMMN is compatible with various network architectures and we are expected to get better results with more sophisticated structures" lacks empirical support.
- The computational complexity of CMMD compared to alternative methods (e.g., conditional GANs) is not discussed, making it difficult to assess practical advantages.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized with clear sections on preliminary knowledge, proposed method, and experimental results.
- Notation is consistent and well-defined throughout.
- Figures (particularly Fig. 2-5) effectively illustrate the model architecture and generated samples.

**Weaknesses:**
- Some technical details in Section 3.1 (CMMD) are dense without sufficient intuition or examples to clarify the mathematical formulation.
- The connection between the theoretical CMMD objective and the practical implementation in Algorithm 1 could be clearer. It's not immediately obvious how the theoretical CMMD objective translates to the stochastic gradient descent procedure.
- The paper refers to appendices for details (e.g., "See Appendix A.2.1 for more details") without providing those details in the main text, which slightly hinders readability for readers without access to the appendix.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions is a natural and useful contribution to the field.
- The CMMD criterion represents a novel adaptation of MMD to the conditional setting, with the key insight about using the conditional embedding operator.
- The diverse applications (predictive modeling, contextual generation, Bayesian distillation) demonstrate the versatility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The core idea of extending GMMN to conditional settings is not entirely novel. Conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., 2015) have already been proposed, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from these.
- The paper doesn't clearly articulate how CMMD differs from or improves upon conditional distribution matching approaches used in conditional GANs (which typically use adversarial training rather than moment matching).
- The related work section references existing methods but doesn't thoroughly discuss how CGMMN differs from or improves upon previous conditional generative models.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a practical need for conditional generative models in many applications (predictive modeling, contextual generation, Bayesian distillation).
- The simplicity of the CGMMN training procedure (using backpropagation on a simple objective) is a significant advantage over more complex generative models like GANs.
- The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across multiple tasks, suggesting practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The performance gains over state-of-the-art methods are not substantial enough to justify a major contribution. On SVHN, CGMMN-CNN is significantly outperformed by DSN (3.13% vs. 1.92% error rate).
- The paper doesn't provide evidence that CGMMN can handle more complex conditional distributions than existing methods (e.g., high-dimensional conditionals or complex conditional dependencies).
- The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment is limited to a single regression task on the Boston housing dataset, which doesn't fully demonstrate the broader applicability of the approach.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a reasonable extension of the GMMN framework to conditional distributions. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across multiple tasks. However, the novelty is somewhat incremental, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from existing conditional generative models (conditional GANs, conditional VAEs) or demonstrate clear advantages over them.

The paper is technically sound and well-written, but the performance gains over state-of-the-art methods are not substantial enough to justify a major contribution to the field at NIPS. Given the high quality of NIPS submissions, I would lean toward rejection unless the authors can provide stronger evidence of advantages over existing methods.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a more detailed comparison with conditional GANs and conditional VAEs in terms of both performance and training dynamics.
2. Provide theoretical analysis of the CMMD estimator's convergence rates or sample complexity bounds.
3. Conduct additional experiments to demonstrate clear advantages of CGMMN over existing methods on more challenging datasets.
4. Clarify the connection between the theoretical CMMD and the practical implementation in Algorithm 1.
5. Discuss computational complexity of CMMD compared to alternative methods, including training time and memory requirements.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- Clear extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with sound theoretical foundation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple application domains
- Simple training procedure avoiding GAN-style training instability
- Demonstrates competitive performance on multiple tasks

**Against Acceptance:**
- Limited novelty compared to existing conditional generative models (conditional GANs, VAEs)
- Performance gains over state-of-the-art are not substantial (particularly on SVHN)
- Theoretical analysis of CMMD is limited
- Doesn't provide sufficient evidence of computational advantages over existing methods

In conclusion, this paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field but falls short of the novelty and impact expected for NIPS. The work is technically sound but lacks sufficient differentiation from existing methods and fails to demonstrate clear advantages that would justify acceptance at this venue.
