

# Peer Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a framework for learning conditional distributions by extending the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) approach. The authors address a significant limitation in existing moment-matching networks (which are limited to unconditional distributions) by developing a conditional version using Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). The paper builds upon the GMMN work by Li et al. (ICML 2015) and extends the theoretical framework of kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML 2009) to create a simple yet effective conditional generative model.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature, as it extends the active line of research on generative models beyond GANs (e.g., GMMN, VAEs, and their variants). It connects with recent work on conditional generative models like conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., 2015), though it approaches the problem from a fundamentally different perspective based on moment matching rather than adversarial training.

## Strengths

1. **Clear motivation and problem formulation**: The paper effectively identifies the gap between unconditional generative models (like GMMN) and the practical need for conditional modeling in many applications (predictive modeling, contextual generation, building large networks).

2. **Theoretical foundation**: The development of CMMD is well-motivated and grounded in the literature on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions. Theorems 1-3 provide a solid theoretical basis for the approach.

3. **Simple and efficient training**: The paper successfully demonstrates that CGMMN can be trained using standard backpropagation, avoiding the complex min-max optimization of GANs. The simplicity of the approach is a significant advantage.

4. **Diverse experimental evaluation**: The authors evaluate CGMMN across three distinct application domains: predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and distilling Bayesian models (Boston housing). This comprehensive evaluation demonstrates the versatility of the approach.

5. **Practical implementation details**: The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including network architectures, hyperparameters, and training procedures.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical gaps in the main contribution**: The key theoretical claim (Theorem 3) that "CY|X = CZ|X implies PY|X = PZ|X" is not sufficiently justified. The reference to [29] is not adequately explained, and the paper lacks a clear proof or discussion of the necessary conditions for this implication. This is a critical gap in the theoretical foundation of the paper.

2. **Inadequate comparison to relevant prior work**: The paper mentions conditional GANs (cGANs) in the introduction but fails to provide a thorough comparison with this state-of-the-art approach for conditional generation. Given that cGANs have been highly successful for image generation (e.g., in the conditional image generation tasks demonstrated in the paper), this omission is significant. The paper should directly compare CGMMN with cGANs on the same tasks to establish its relative advantages and limitations.

3. **Computational efficiency concerns**: The CMMD objective requires computing kernel matrices and their inverses, which has O(N^3) complexity. While the paper mentions a mini-batch version (Algorithm 1), it doesn't discuss the trade-offs between mini-batch size and approximation quality, nor does it provide computational complexity comparisons with other methods like cGANs or VAEs.

4. **Limited empirical evaluation**: The results on MNIST and SVHN are competitive but not groundbreaking, and the paper doesn't clearly establish how much better CGMMN is than the baselines. The comparison to cGANs is particularly lacking, which is a major omission given the success of conditional GANs in similar tasks.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't include ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components (e.g., the impact of noise dimensionality, choice of kernel, or network architecture).

6. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of CGMMN, such as its performance on high-dimensional data (beyond the MNIST and SVHN experiments) or its sensitivity to the choice of kernel function.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Strengthen the theoretical foundation**: Provide a complete proof or detailed explanation of Theorem 3, including the necessary conditions under which the implication holds. Reference [29] more thoroughly to establish the theoretical basis.

2. **Add comparison with conditional GANs**: Include a direct comparison with cGANs (or similar conditional GAN variants) on the image generation tasks. This would be critical for establishing the value proposition of CGMMN.

3. **Discuss computational trade-offs**: Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of CMMD and how the mini-batch approach affects performance. Include a comparison of training times with cGANs or other conditional generative models.

4. **Expand the empirical evaluation**: Add results on more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10 or ImageNet) and include a comparison with state-of-the-art conditional generative models.

5. **Include ablation studies**: Add experiments to analyze the impact of different hyperparameters (noise dimension, kernel choice, network depth) on performance.

6. **Address limitations more thoroughly**: Discuss the limitations of the approach in a dedicated section, including potential failure cases and scenarios where CGMMN might not be the best choice.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a reasonable extension to GMMN to handle conditional distributions, and the CMMD formulation is theoretically grounded. The simplicity of the training procedure is a notable advantage over GAN-based approaches. However, the paper's main contribution is significantly weakened by the lack of comparison to the most relevant prior work (conditional GANs) and the insufficient theoretical justification for the key claim.

While the experimental results are competitive, they don't demonstrate a clear advantage over existing conditional generative models, which is a requirement for a strong NIPS contribution. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation against the current state-of-the-art in conditional generation.

## Recommendation

**Weak Reject**

The paper presents a technically sound extension to GMMN for conditional generation, but its contribution is limited by the lack of comparison to the most relevant prior work (conditional GANs) and the insufficient theoretical justification for the key claim. The paper would need substantial revisions to address these weaknesses before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.

## Arguments Pro and Con

**Pro (Strengths):**
- Clear motivation for extending GMMN to conditional distributions
- Well-justified theoretical foundation for CMMD (despite gaps)
- Simple training procedure using backpropagation
- Comprehensive evaluation across multiple application domains
- Good writing and organization

**Con (Weaknesses):**
- Key theoretical claim (Theorem 3) lacks sufficient justification
- No meaningful comparison to conditional GANs, which are the state-of-the-art for conditional generation
- Computational complexity of CMMD is not adequately addressed
- Empirical results are competitive but not groundbreaking
- Lack of ablation studies and discussion of limitations

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field of generative modeling, but it doesn't significantly advance the state of the art or provide compelling evidence that CGMMN is superior to existing approaches for conditional generation. The omission of the most relevant comparison (to conditional GANs) is particularly problematic for a paper claiming to "significantly improve the applicability of moment-matching networks."
