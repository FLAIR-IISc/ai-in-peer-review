Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional extension of GMMN trained via a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The key idea is to represent conditional distributions via the kernel mean embedding operator CY|X and to learn a deep generator y = f(x, h; w) such that the empirical conditional embedding induced by generated samples matches that of the data. The CMMD objective is the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between two empirical conditional embedding operators, which is computable using Gram matrices on X and Y. Training is done by backpropagation, drawing samples from the model conditioned on minibatch inputs. The method is demonstrated on three tasks: supervised prediction (MNIST, SVHN), conditional image generation (MNIST, Yale faces), and distilling Bayesian predictors (Boston Housing).

This builds on: (i) MMD-based training for generative models (GMMN) and GMMN+AE, (ii) conditional mean embeddings (Song et al.), and (iii) conditional generative models like cGAN and CVAE. The technical novelty is to bring conditional mean embeddings into deep conditional generation, yielding a simple, stable, non-adversarial training objective; and to show applications beyond generation (prediction, distillation).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The CMMD formulation is well-grounded in conditional mean embedding theory. The derivation of the empirical objective using Gram matrices and its gradient is consistent. Theorem 3 (equality of conditional embedding operators implies equality of conditionals for all x) is a straightforward but correct sufficient condition under standard assumptions (universality, regularity, invertibility/regularization). The paper acknowledges practical regularization via (K + λI)^{-1}.
- Practicality and complexity: Training requires inverting batch Gram matrices on X per minibatch (O(b^3)). This is acceptable for moderate b, but scalability is a concern for large-batch or large-scale problems; the paper mentions a minibatch approximation but provides limited analysis of its bias/stability. The choice of kernels (on X and Y) and bandwidth selection is crucial for CMMD performance; these details and sensitivity analyses are largely absent. For high-dimensional Y (images), Gaussian kernels can be brittle; using autoencoder codes alleviates this, but quantitative evaluation is not provided.
- Experiments: The prediction results are competitive with prior deep generative models (MMVA/CMMVA) but trail state-of-the-art discriminative CNNs. For conditional generation, results are qualitative; no comparison to cGAN/CVAE or quantitative metrics are offered. The distillation experiment is small-scale (Boston Housing) and reports only RMSE; calibration or predictive likelihood and runtime benefits are not reported. Overall, the experiments support feasibility but are not comprehensive.
- Claims vs evidence: Claims about wide applicability are supported by diverse tasks, but claims of superiority are not; the paper is careful to position CGMMN as competitive rather than SOTA.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The review of MMD and conditional embeddings is concise and accurate. The CMMD objective and its computation are clearly spelled out. However:
- Kernel choices (types, bandwidths), preprocessing, and sensitivity are under-specified.
- Training details for each task (noise dimension, concatenation vs corruption, kernel on Y for classification vs images) are scattered or missing.
- Discussion of computational costs and potential approximations (Nyström, random features, multi-kernel MMD) would help reproducibility and adoption.

Originality
Moderate. The work is an incremental but meaningful extension: it combines known conditional mean embedding machinery with deep generators and MMD-based training to obtain a conditional, non-adversarial generative learning objective. The CMMD objective itself and its use for deep conditional generation appear novel. The theoretical components are adaptations of known results rather than new theory.

Significance
Moderate. A stable, simple alternative to adversarial training for conditional generation is valuable. The framework’s breadth (prediction, generation, distillation) is attractive. However, the empirical section does not convincingly advance the state of the art or establish clear advantages over cGAN/CVAE in quality or efficiency. The need to select kernels and the cubic cost per batch may limit large-scale impact unless addressed.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMN to conditional settings via CMMD.
- Simple training (no min-max), backprop-ready, and applicable across tasks.
- Solid grounding in kernel conditional embedding theory.
- Competitive results vs prior DGM baselines; plausible conditional samples.

Weaknesses
- Missing strong baselines for conditional generation (cGAN, CVAE) and quantitative metrics.
- Limited analysis of kernel choices/bandwidths and sensitivity; no multi-kernel approach.
- Scalability concerns (O(b^3) inversions) not addressed with approximations.
- Sparse evaluation for distillation (small dataset, only RMSE; no calibration/runtime).
- Theoretical contribution is modest (sufficient condition and empirical operator matching).

Questions for authors
- What kernels and bandwidth selection strategies were used on X and Y across tasks? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Can you report quantitative generation metrics and comparisons to cGAN/CVAE?
- How does performance and runtime scale with minibatch size, and can Nyström or random feature approximations preserve performance?
- For classification, does the model produce calibrated probabilities? How is y encoded and what output kernel is used?
- For distillation, can you show NLL/calibration and speed/memory gains vs the teacher?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled objective enabling conditional generation without adversarial training.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across tasks.
- Methodologically interesting to the NeurIPS community working on kernel embeddings and generative modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is not comprehensive and lacks key baselines/metrics.
- Limited discussion of kernel hyperparameters and scalability.
- Incremental theoretical novelty.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is appealing and technically sound, but the current empirical evidence and comparisons are insufficient to establish significance relative to existing conditional generative methods. Strengthening baselines, metrics, and scalability analysis would improve the case for acceptance.
