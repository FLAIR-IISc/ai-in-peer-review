Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep conditional generative model trained by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The core idea is to represent conditional distributions via RKHS conditional mean embeddings CY|X, and to measure the distance between the data conditional and the model conditional as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm of the difference of their empirical conditional operators. A deep network f(x, h; w) takes both the conditioning variable x and a noise vector h as input to generate y; training minimizes the CMMD between generated pairs and real pairs. The resulting objective is simple, differentiable, and requires no adversarial min–max.

This work extends GMMN (Li et al., ICML’15; Dziugaite et al., UAI’15) to the conditional setting using the conditional embedding machinery (Song et al., Fukumizu et al.), and offers a counterpart to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al.). The proposed CMMD has an intuitive connection to MMD: it can be seen as reweighting the output Gram matrices by functions of the inputs. Empirically, CGMMN is applied to predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST digits conditioned on labels; Yale faces conditioned on identity), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation (Boston housing).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and novel extension: Using conditional mean embeddings to define a tractable, backprop-able discrepancy for conditional distributions is elegant and, to my knowledge, new in the context of training deep conditional generators. It preserves the simplicity that made GMMN attractive while addressing an important gap (learning p(y|x)).
- Theoretical grounding: The objective is motivated by standard RKHS results; the sufficiency statement (operator equality implies equality of conditionals) is clear, and the empirical operator estimator is well-established. The connection to MMD clarifies intuition.
- Practicality: The training loop is straightforward (no min–max), requires only sampling from a base noise and computing Gram matrices. The inverse Gram matrices do not depend on model parameters, allowing flexible kernels on x.
- Versatility: The same framework is demonstrated on three diverse tasks. Classification performance is competitive with prior deep generative models (max-margin DGM), conditional samples look reasonable, and Bayesian distillation shows no loss in accuracy relative to the teacher.
- Architectural flexibility: Works with MLPs and CNNs; the CGMMN+AE idea mirrors GMMN+AE and improves visual quality.

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical scope and comparisons: The generative evaluation is largely qualitative, with no quantitative measures (e.g., Inception/FID or human studies), and lacks direct comparisons against conditional GANs or conditional VAEs on the same tasks. The classification results, while competitive for DGMs, lag behind strong discriminative baselines; the paper does not analyze when a conditional generator is preferable for prediction beyond conceptual arguments.
- Kernel choices and sensitivity: The paper does not specify which kernels are used on x and y across tasks, nor how bandwidth/regularization are selected. This is crucial, especially when x is high-dimensional (e.g., images) where naïve RBF kernels can be problematic. An ablation on kernel types, median heuristic vs. tuning, and λ sensitivity would strengthen the story.
- Scalability and efficiency: CMMD requires O(b^3) per minibatch for inverting K+λI and O(b^2) storage; this is not discussed quantitatively (batch sizes, wall-clock, memory), nor are approximations (Nyström, random features) explored. How does this compare empirically to cGAN/cVAE training times?
- Finite-sample/statistical behavior: Theorem 3 provides a sufficiency statement under standard assumptions, but there is no analysis of finite-sample bias/variance of the CMMD estimator, or of the minibatch approximation error introduced by using per-batch K inverses. Any guidance on batch size vs. stability would be useful.
- Implementation details: For classification/regression, it’s unclear how the output layer is parameterized (e.g., is y constrained to the simplex, what activation is used, how is argmax justified vs. proper likelihood?). The Yale face setup and data splits also merit more detail.
- Minor clarity/typos: Some typographical errors (e.g., “MINIST”, “adverisarial”), and a few dense sections could benefit from additional intuition (e.g., the operator view vs. per-x MMD).

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a simple, general, and well-motivated objective (CMMD) for conditional generation, likely to inspire follow-up work.
- Avoids adversarial training while providing flexibility comparable to cGANs, which is appealing for robustness and ease of use.
- Demonstrates applicability across distinct problem classes; results are reasonable and sometimes competitive.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited head-to-head comparisons with the most relevant baselines (cGAN/CVAE) on conditional generation.
- Missing analysis on kernel choice, scalability, and sensitivity that are central to kernel-based methods.
- Empirical evaluation is solid but not exhaustive; quantitative generative metrics are absent.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report quantitative generative metrics and add cGAN/CVAE comparisons on MNIST (and, if feasible, CIFAR-10/LSUN subsets).
- Detail kernel choices and selection strategy; include ablations on kernels/bandwidths/λ. Consider learned or deep kernels.
- Discuss and/or implement scalable CMMD approximations (Nyström, random features) and compare training/runtime to cGAN/CVAE.
- Provide more implementation details (output parameterization, normalization) and release code.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper introduces a clear and novel conditional training criterion for deep generators with broad potential impact. Despite some empirical and clarity gaps, the conceptual contribution is strong and the approach is simple and practical. With minor revisions, this would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS.

Confidence: 0.6
