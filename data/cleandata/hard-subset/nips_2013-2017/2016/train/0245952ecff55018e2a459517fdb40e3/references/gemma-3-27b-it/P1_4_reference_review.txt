## Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of the recent Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to the conditional setting. The core idea is to learn a conditional distribution *p(y|x)* by training a deep network that maps a combination of input *x* and a random variable *h* to *y*, and then minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) between the generated conditional distribution and the empirical conditional distribution of a dataset.  The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN across various tasks – predictive modeling, contextual generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models – showing competitive performance on standard datasets.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions CGMMN within the landscape of deep generative models. It acknowledges the limitations of early DGMs in predictive performance and highlights the success of GANs and, more recently, GMMNs.  The work builds directly upon the MMD-based learning approach of GMMN and extends it to the conditional case. It also acknowledges related work in conditional GANs and conditional VAEs.  The citation of relevant work (e.g., Gretton et al. on MMD, Huszar et al. on conditional GANs) is generally good.

**Strengths:**

*   **Sound Technical Foundation:** The paper builds on a solid theoretical foundation with clear connections to kernel methods and MMD. The CMMD criterion is well-defined and the derivation seems correct.
*   **Simplicity and Ease of Implementation:**  The approach is relatively simple to implement and train, leveraging backpropagation.  The authors highlight this as a key advantage over GANs, which can be notoriously difficult to optimize.
*   **Versatility:** The framework is flexible and applicable to various tasks involving conditional generation. The empirical evaluation demonstrates this with results on predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian knowledge distillation.
*   **Competitive Performance:** The results on MNIST and SVHN for predictive modeling are competitive with state-of-the-art methods. The generation examples on MNIST and Yale Face dataset show promising results.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is mostly clearly written and well-organized. The explanations of key concepts like Hilbert space embeddings and MMD are concise and accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While extending GMMN to the conditional setting is a natural and valuable step, the core technical innovation lies in the derivation of the CMMD criterion. The underlying generative process is fairly straightforward. The paper lacks a significant conceptual leap beyond its predecessor.
*   **Empirical Evaluation Could Be Stronger:**  While the results are competitive, they don’t dramatically outperform existing methods. The ablation studies are absent. For example, the impact of the hidden layer size/depth or the choice of kernel function on performance is not explored. Also, comparison with stronger baseline methods would strengthen the evaluation. For example, it would be beneficial to compare against recent advances in conditional VAEs or normalizing flows.
* **Computational Cost:** Estimating CMMD, even with mini-batching, can be computationally expensive due to the kernel matrix computations. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't provide detailed analysis of scalability or discuss efficient approximation techniques beyond mini-batching.
* **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Practical Choices:** The paper mentions smoothness assumptions required for the CMMD objective to be well-defined. However, it quickly dismisses this with an “empirical approximation” argument. A more in-depth discussion of the practical implications of these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Generation Results are Basic:** The generation results on MNIST and Yale Face, while demonstrating the ability to generate samples, are somewhat basic. More sophisticated evaluation metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) are missing and the visual examples could be more compelling, especially compared to the high-quality outputs achievable with GANs or other advanced generative models.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated extension of GMMN to the conditional setting.
*   The CMMD criterion is a novel and potentially useful contribution for learning conditional distributions.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the versatility and competitiveness of CGMMN.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The technical novelty is incremental.
*   The empirical evaluation lacks depth and comparison with strong baselines.
*   The computational cost of CMMD estimation is a potential concern.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **weak accept**. It presents a solid contribution to the field of generative modeling, but the incremental nature of the work and the limitations in the empirical evaluation prevent it from being a strong paper. Addressing the weaknesses, particularly by providing a more comprehensive empirical analysis and discussing scalability issues, would significantly improve the paper’s impact.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Expand the empirical evaluation with comparisons to more recent and competitive baselines.
2.  Include ablation studies to investigate the impact of key hyperparameters.
3.  Discuss the computational cost of CMMD estimation and potential approximation techniques.
4.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the theoretical assumptions underlying the CMMD objective.
5.  Consider using more sophisticated evaluation metrics for generative performance.
