### **Summary**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional distributions p(y|x) using a deep generative model. This work is a direct and logical extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which learn joint distributions by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between model-generated samples and real data. The core contribution is the development of a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. This objective measures the distance between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective kernel mean embedding operators. The model itself is a deep neural network that takes a conditional variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to generate a sample `y`. The parameters of this network are learned via back-propagation by minimizing the CMMD objective. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on a wide range of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

### **Review**

This is a well-written paper that presents a valuable and logical extension to an existing line of work on generative models. The proposed CMMD criterion is theoretically sound and offers a compelling alternative to other conditional generative models like Conditional GANs (CGANs).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of CMMD is well-grounded in the theory of kernel embeddings of conditional distributions, citing the foundational work of Song et al. (2009). The derivation in Section 3.1, while dense, appears correct and provides a principled way to compare conditional distributions without discretizing the conditioning variable `x`. The experimental evaluation is broad, covering three distinct and relevant application areas. This demonstrates the versatility of the proposed framework. The claims are generally well-supported by the experiments, showing that CGMMN is a viable and competitive approach, particularly as a deep generative model (DGM).

However, there are some weaknesses in the evaluation and discussion:
1.  **Computational Cost:** The CMMD objective requires inverting kernel matrices of size `N x N`, where `N` is the mini-batch size. This operation is `O(N^3)`, which is a significant computational bottleneck and limits the practical size of mini-batches. While the paper mentions a mini-batch algorithm, it does not adequately discuss this scalability limitation or compare its computational cost to alternatives like CGANs, whose per-iteration cost is typically lower.
2.  **Generation Quality:** The qualitative results for image generation (Fig. 2) are not state-of-the-art for the time (2016). The generated MNIST digits are noisy and lack sharpness compared to what GAN-based models could produce. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by introducing a two-stage approach where they first train an autoencoder and then apply CGMMN in the latent "code" space to get cleaner samples (Fig. 4). While this is a valid technique, it feels like a workaround that highlights a potential weakness of the CMMD objective for direct, high-dimensional pixel-level generation. The paper would be stronger if it discussed why this might be the case and whether the CMMD loss is less suited for capturing sharp, high-frequency details compared to an adversarial loss. Furthermore, the paper relies solely on visual inspection for generation quality, lacking any quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, log-likelihood estimates) that would allow for a more objective comparison.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the need for conditional models and positions the work within the existing literature. The background section provides the necessary prerequisites on MMD and kernel embeddings. The model architecture and learning algorithm are described clearly.

The main point of confusion is the presentation of the CMMD objective itself in Section 3.1. The derivation is very compact and relies heavily on notation from operator theory (`F ⊗ G`, Hilbert-Schmidt norm) which may be inaccessible to a broad NIPS audience. Remark 1 provides a useful high-level intuition by connecting CMMD to a weighted version of MMD, but the paper would benefit greatly from a more pedagogical explanation or a simplified example illustrating how the conditional embedding operators and their difference capture the discrepancy between `P(Y|x)` and `Q(Y|x)`.

**Originality:** The work is a novel and non-trivial extension of GMMN. While conditional versions of other generative models (like CGANs and CVAEs) existed, this was the first work to develop a principled moment-matching criterion for the conditional setting. The CMMD objective, based on comparing conditional embedding operators, is the key original contribution. The application to distilling Bayesian models is also a creative use of the framework. The paper does an excellent job of citing and differentiating itself from related work.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is significant. GANs are powerful but are often difficult to train due to the unstable min-max optimization dynamics. By providing a conditional generative model with a single, stable objective function to minimize, CGMMN presents a valuable and practical alternative. The ability to model `p(y|x)` is fundamental to a vast range of machine learning problems, and this work significantly broadens the applicability of moment-matching networks, which were previously limited to modeling joint distributions. Researchers and practitioners working on conditional generation, particularly in domains where training stability is a major concern, are likely to find this work useful and build upon it. The idea of using kernel-based distribution metrics for training deep models is an important research direction, and this paper makes a solid contribution to that area.

### **Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Principled Contribution:** Introduces a novel, theoretically sound objective (CMMD) for training conditional generative models.
*   **Significant Extension:** Greatly expands the applicability of GMMNs to the much broader and more useful class of conditional modeling problems.
*   **Alternative to GANs:** Provides a stable training alternative to CGANs, which is a significant practical benefit.
*   **Broad Experimental Validation:** Demonstrates effectiveness across three different and important machine learning tasks (prediction, generation, model distillation).

### **Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** The `O(N^3)` complexity of the objective is a major practical limitation that is not adequately discussed.
*   **Sub-par Generation Quality:** The raw image generation results are weak compared to contemporary methods, and the paper lacks quantitative evaluation of generative performance.
*   **Clarity of Core Concept:** The mathematical exposition of CMMD is dense and could be made more accessible.

**Overall Recommendation:** This paper presents a strong and important contribution. The weaknesses, while notable, do not undermine the novelty and significance of the core idea. The CMMD framework is an elegant and principled contribution to the field of deep generative models. I recommend **acceptance**. The authors should be encouraged to add a discussion on the computational limitations and, if possible, include quantitative metrics for the generation tasks in the final version.
