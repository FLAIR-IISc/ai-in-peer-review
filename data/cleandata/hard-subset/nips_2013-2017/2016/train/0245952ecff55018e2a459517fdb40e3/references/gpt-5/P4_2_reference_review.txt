Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), an extension of Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to conditional distributions. The model generates y given x by concatenating x with auxiliary noise h and passing them through a deep network y = f(x, h; w). Training minimizes a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective: the squared Hilbert–Schmidt norm between the conditional mean embedding operators CY|X of the empirical data and of the generator. The CMMD objective is computed using Gram matrices for inputs (X) and outputs (Y) and includes Tikhonov regularization to ensure invertibility. Gradients backpropagate through the generator via the output Gram matrices; the input-side inverses depend only on the conditioning variables, simplifying differentiation.

This approach contrasts with GANs (unconditional and conditional), which optimize an adversarial min-max, and with VAEs/CVAEs, which use variational bounds. It builds on kernel mean embeddings for (conditional) distributions (Gretton et al., Song et al.) and extends unconditional GMMN (Li et al., Dziugaite et al.) to the conditional case. The paper also positions CGMMN as a student model for Bayesian “dark knowledge” distillation, akin to Korattikara et al., but trained via CMMD rather than KL-based distillation.

Experiments cover: (1) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN (conditioning on images, generating label confidence vectors and predicting via argmax), (2) class-conditional generation on MNIST and identity-conditional generation on Extended Yale Face, and (3) distillation of a Bayesian neural network (PBP) on Boston housing.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual extension of MMD-based training to conditional distributions using conditional mean embeddings. The CMMD objective is well motivated and connects cleanly to established RKHS theory.
- Simple, stable training objective without adversarial min-max; gradients are straightforward and do not require differentiable kernels on X.
- Flexible generator compatible with CNN/MLP architectures; easy sampling.
- Broad applicability illustrated across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation.
- Competitive predictive performance: close to or matching prior deep generative baselines (e.g., MMVA) on MNIST and SVHN without data augmentation or ensembling.
- Qualitative conditional generation results on MNIST and Yale are reasonable; the autoencoder-code variant improves sample quality, echoing prior findings for GMMN.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical guarantees: Theorem 3 gives a sufficient condition (equality of conditional operators implies conditional distributional equality) but no consistency result linking empirical CMMD minimization to convergence in distribution. Assumptions (existence of CY|X in F⊗G, smoothness conditions on CXX) are noted but not verified in practice.
- Kernel choices and hyperparameters (kernels on X and Y, bandwidth selection, regularization λ) are under-specified; no sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Computational cost and scalability: each minibatch requires inverting K+λI (O(B^3)). While independent of w, the inversion is per minibatch. There is no runtime or memory analysis, and no discussion of low-rank/Random Fourier approximations or preconditioning.
- Predictive modeling setup for classification produces a real-valued vector y with kernel matching, then uses argmax. This bypasses a probabilistic calibration and does not compare against strong discriminative baselines with identical architectures/training budgets; several state-of-the-art discriminative results still outperform CGMMN.
- Generative evaluation is qualitative only; there are no quantitative metrics (e.g., FID/IS) or precision-recall tradeoffs. Nearest-neighbor checks are informative but limited.
- Comparisons to conditional baselines (cGAN, CVAE/conditional VAE) on conditional generation are missing, despite being the most closely related alternatives for this task.
- Bayesian distillation experiments are limited (one small dataset), with no analysis of computational speedup vs. teacher, calibration, or uncertainty quality.

Quality
The method is technically sound at a high level and grounded in prior kernel embedding literature. The CMMD estimator is correctly derived from empirical conditional mean embeddings. However, important practical aspects (kernel selection, numerical stability, complexity) and theoretical properties (statistical consistency, generalization) are not explored. Empirical evidence shows competitiveness but not clear superiority; evaluation breadth for generation and distillation is limited.

Clarity
The core idea and training objective are mostly clear, and derivations are readable. Some notation and presentation could be tightened (explicit kernel choices; clarifying that K inverses change per minibatch; specifying how x is sampled/perturbed; consistent discussion of finite vs. continuous X). There are minor typographical issues. Algorithm 1 is helpful.

Originality
The main novelty is applying conditional mean embeddings to train a deep conditional generator via CMMD. While conditional mean embeddings and unconditional GMMNs are known, this combination appears novel and practically useful relative to GAN/variational approaches.

Significance
Moderate. The contribution offers a non-adversarial alternative for learning conditional generators and a kernel-based route to distillation. The approach is likely to be used or extended by researchers interested in stable training objectives. However, without stronger empirical results or theory, the impact is incremental relative to established conditional generative models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative generative metrics and compare against cGAN and CVAE on conditional image generation.
- Report training time, memory, and per-iteration cost; explore scalable approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features) and their effects on performance.
- Detail kernel choices and conduct sensitivity analyses for bandwidth and λ; consider median heuristic vs. learned kernels; discuss kernels for discrete labels.
- Analyze minibatch CMMD’s bias/variance and provide guidance on batch size; explore using unbiased/linear-time variants where applicable.
- Add more substantial distillation experiments (larger datasets, speedup vs. teacher, calibration metrics like NLL/ECE).
- Clarify whether the conditional variables used for generation are exactly the minibatch x or include perturbations; explain how this affects K matrix reuse.
- Discuss failure modes (e.g., mode collapse avoidance vs. GANs; multimodal conditional distributions).

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and technically grounded extension of GMMN to the conditional setting.
- Simple, stable training objective with broad applicability.
- Competitive predictive results and reasonable conditional generation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees and missing analysis of consistency and scalability.
- Incomplete experimental evaluation for conditional generation (no cGAN/CVAE comparisons, no quantitative metrics).
- Sparse distillation results and lack of computational analysis.

Scope
The paper is in scope for NeurIPS (deep generative modeling, kernel methods, probabilistic learning).
