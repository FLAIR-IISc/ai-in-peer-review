### **Summary**

This paper proposes the Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Network (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework for modeling conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The core contributions are twofold. First, the authors introduce a novel objective function, the Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), which measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions. CMMD is theoretically grounded in the kernel mean embedding of conditional distributions, where distributions are represented as operators in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The objective is defined as the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between the operators corresponding to the true data distribution and the model's generated distribution. Second, the paper presents a deep generative architecture that models `p(y|x)` by transforming the conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` through a deep neural network, i.e., `y = f(x,h|w)`. The network parameters are trained by minimizing the CMMD objective via backpropagation.

The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN through a comprehensive set of experiments on three distinct tasks: (1) predictive modeling (classification on MNIST and SVHN), (2) contextual generation (generating MNIST digits and Yale faces conditioned on labels), and (3) distilling knowledge from complex Bayesian models. The results show that CGMMN is a viable and competitive framework for conditional density estimation, offering a stable, non-adversarial training alternative to models like Conditional GANs (cGANs).

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a well-motivated and elegant extension of the MMD-based generative modeling paradigm to the conditional setting. This is a significant step, as conditional modeling is crucial for a vast range of applications. By providing an alternative to the often unstable min-max training of cGANs, this work fills an important gap and broadens the applicability of moment-matching networks, which was a promising recent research direction (e.g., GMMN by Li et al., 2015).

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The proposed CMMD objective is well-grounded in the theory of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009). The paper does a good job of building the theoretical motivation, starting from MMD and extending it to the conditional operator framework. The connection drawn in Remark 1, which frames CMMD as a non-uniformly weighted version of MMD, provides valuable intuition.

3.  **Thorough and Diverse Evaluation:** The experimental validation is a key strength of this paper. The authors go beyond a single application and showcase the flexibility of CGMMN across three different domains: discriminative prediction, conditional generation, and model distillation ("Bayesian dark knowledge"). This diverse evaluation convincingly demonstrates that the proposed framework is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practical tool with wide-ranging potential.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The motivation for conditional modeling is well-articulated, and the technical development from MMD to CMMD is presented logically. The description of the network architecture and training algorithm is easy to follow.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Computational Scalability and Mini-batch Implementation:** The CMMD objective in Equation (2) involves inverting kernel matrices of size `N x N`, where `N` is the number of samples. This has a computational cost of O(N³), which is prohibitive for large datasets. The paper proposes a mini-batch algorithm (Algorithm 1), but the details of how the CMMD objective is estimated on a mini-batch are not provided.
    *   **Question:** Is the CMMD computed only within the mini-batch? If so, this is a biased estimator of the full-batch CMMD, as the operators `C_{Y|X}` are estimated on the full dataset in the original formulation. How does this approximation affect training and performance?
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the mini-batch CMMD estimator. This should include its formulation, an analysis of its properties (e.g., bias), and a justification for its use. A discussion of the computational complexity compared to alternatives like cGANs would also be valuable.

2.  **Quantitative Evaluation of Generative Quality:** The evaluation of the generative models in Section 4.2 is purely qualitative (visual inspection and nearest neighbor analysis). While the generated samples appear reasonable, they also seem less sharp than what was being produced by contemporary GAN models at the time (e.g., DCGAN).
    *   **Suggestion:** To make the results more compelling, a quantitative metric for sample quality and diversity should be included. For instance, comparing the Parzen window log-likelihood estimates on the test set for MNIST, as was done in the original GMMN paper, would provide a more objective comparison against other generative models.

3.  **Details on Experimental Setup:** Some crucial details for reproducibility are missing or relegated to the appendix.
    *   **Kernel Choice:** The performance of MMD-based methods is highly dependent on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidth). The paper does not specify which kernels were used for the outputs `y` in the different experiments (e.g., for the 10-dimensional label confidence vectors in the prediction task, or the image pixels in the generation task) and how their hyperparameters were selected.
    *   **Sampling `x` for Generated Data:** In Section 3.2, it is stated that to generate the "model" dataset, the conditional variables `x` can be sampled from the training set "with/without small perturbation." Clarifying which strategy was used in each experiment is important for understanding the training setup.

4.  **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** In the predictive tasks (Section 4.1), CGMMN is competitive with another generative model (CMMVA) but underperforms state-of-the-art discriminative models like DSN. While this is not entirely unexpected, it would be beneficial to discuss the potential reasons for this performance gap. Is it an inherent limitation of the generative, moment-matching approach for purely discriminative tasks?

### **Decision**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep generative models. The introduction of a conditional MMD objective is a novel and significant extension of an important line of research. The method is theoretically sound and its effectiveness is demonstrated across a diverse set of applications. The main weaknesses relate to a lack of clarity on the practical implementation of the mini-batch CMMD objective and the absence of quantitative evaluation for the generative experiments. However, these are addressable concerns and do not undermine the core contribution of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and principled extension of moment-matching networks to conditional generation.
*   Theoretically well-founded CMMD objective.
*   Provides a stable, non-adversarial alternative to cGANs.
*   Strong empirical validation across three different and important tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   Scalability concerns with the CMMD objective and lack of detail on the mini-batch approximation.
*   Evaluation of generative quality is only qualitative.
*   Missing experimental details regarding kernel selection.

Overall, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance. The authors should use the revision period to address the points raised above, particularly by clarifying the mini-batch CMMD implementation and, if possible, adding quantitative metrics for the generative models.
