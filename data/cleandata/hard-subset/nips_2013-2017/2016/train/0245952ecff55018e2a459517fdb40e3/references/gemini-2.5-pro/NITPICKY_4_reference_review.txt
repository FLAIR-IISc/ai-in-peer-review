**Summary**

This paper introduces the Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Network (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) for modeling conditional distributions p(y|x). The core contribution is the proposal of a training objective called Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). CMMD measures the dissimilarity between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective conditional mean embedding operators. The generative model is a deep neural network that takes a conditioning variable `x` and a random noise vector `h` as input to produce a sample `y`. The model is trained via back-propagation to minimize the CMMD between the empirical data distribution and the model's generated distribution. The authors demonstrate the model's utility on a diverse set of tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, conditional image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and natural problem of extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. This fills a clear gap in the literature, providing an alternative to conditional VAEs and conditional GANs. The proposed CMMD objective, based on established theory of conditional kernel embeddings, is an elegant and principled approach.

2.  **Simple and Stable Training:** A key advantage of the GMMN framework, which this work inherits, is a simpler training objective compared to the min-max adversarial training of GANs. This can lead to more stable and straightforward optimization, which is a significant practical benefit.

3.  **Versatility:** The authors effectively showcase the model's flexibility by applying it to three distinct and relevant problem domains: prediction, conditional generation, and a novel application in "Bayesian dark knowledge". This breadth of experiments strengthens the paper's claim of general applicability.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is appealing, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor and major flaws in its presentation, technical detail, and experimental evaluation that undermine its quality and completeness.

1.  **Clarity and Notational Inconsistencies:** The manuscript lacks the precision expected for a top-tier publication.
    *   There is a significant notational contradiction. In Section 3.1, the datasets for computing CMMD are denoted `DsXY` and `DdXY`. However, in Section 3.2, the training dataset is denoted `DsXY` and the generated dataset is `DdXY`. This is confusing and careless.
    *   The NIPS conference information in the footnote contains a typo ("th Conference...").
    *   Algorithm 1 is ambiguously written. Step 6, "generate a y; and set B' to contain all the generated (x,y)", fails to distinguish the generated output `y_gen` from the true data `y`.
    *   Remark 1 uses non-standard notation `Tr(Ld · 1)` without defining the `·` operator (presumably element-wise product).

2.  **Incomplete Work and Reproducibility:** The paper is critically incomplete as it relies heavily on an appendix that is not provided. Multiple sections refer to the appendix for crucial information, including:
    *   The gradient computation for the CMMD objective (Appendix A.1).
    *   Discussion of the mini-batch algorithm (A.2.1).
    *   Details on how the conditional operator is estimated differently for the generative tasks with finite `X` (A.2.2).
    *   Additional experimental results (A.3, A.4.1, A.4.2).
    Without this information, the work is not verifiable or reproducible. This is a major flaw.

3.  **Technical Soundness:**
    *   The paper briefly mentions the smoothness assumptions required for the CMMD objective to be well-defined but then dismisses them by stating the empirical estimator is "always an element in the tensor product space". This glosses over the important theoretical question of whether the empirical objective is a consistent estimator of a meaningful quantity if the underlying assumptions are violated.
    *   The authors state that for the generation task on MNIST (with a finite number of labels), they estimate the conditional operator differently than the formulation in Eq. (2). The justification for this choice, and why it was not also used for the predictive modeling task (which also has finite labels), is absent (deferred to the missing appendix).

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   **Predictive Performance:** The results on MNIST and SVHN are presented as "competitive". However, they do not surpass the state-of-the-art discriminative models (e.g., DSN) or even the state-of-the-art max-margin generative model (CMMVA). While demonstrating viability, this does not make a strong case for the model's superiority in predictive tasks.
    *   **Generative Quality:** The visual quality of the generated MNIST samples in Figure 2 is quite poor, exhibiting significant noise. The authors acknowledge this and show much better results when combining CGMMN with a pre-trained autoencoder (Figure 4). While an interesting analysis, this suggests that the core CGMMN model is insufficient on its own for generating high-fidelity images, which is a notable weakness. The evaluation lacks any quantitative metrics of sample quality or diversity (e.g., Parzen window log-likelihood estimation), relying solely on visual inspection and a nearest-neighbor sanity check.
    *   **Bayesian Dark Knowledge:** This is an interesting application, but the experiment is conducted only on the small Boston housing dataset. The results simply show that the distilled model does not degrade performance, but it is difficult to draw strong conclusions about the method's effectiveness from this single, small-scale example.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and principled framework for conditional generative modeling using moment matching.
*   The objective function is simpler and potentially more stable to train than that of conditional GANs.
*   The paper demonstrates the model's versatility across a wide range of interesting tasks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is incomplete due to a missing appendix containing critical methodological details and results, hindering reproducibility.
*   Multiple notational inconsistencies and minor errors detract from the paper's overall quality and clarity.
*   The experimental results, while demonstrating proof-of-concept, are not compelling enough. Predictive performance is not state-of-the-art, and generative quality appears low without the help of a separate autoencoder.

**Recommendation**

While the paper presents a novel and promising direction for conditional generative modeling, the execution is unfortunately marred by a lack of rigor. The combination of notational inconsistencies, an incomplete manuscript due to the missing appendix, and experimental results that do not convincingly demonstrate an advantage over existing methods leads me to recommend **rejection**. The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution, but it requires a major revision to address the numerous clarity, completeness, and experimental weaknesses.
