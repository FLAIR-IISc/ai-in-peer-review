This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional distributions `p(y|x)`. The work is a natural and significant extension of the recently proposed Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN), which focused on modeling joint distributions. The core technical contribution is the development of a novel training objective, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). This objective measures the discrepancy between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their kernel mean embedding operators. This allows for a non-adversarial, stable training procedure for conditional deep generative models. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach on a wide range of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on labels), and distilling knowledge from complex Bayesian models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—the CMMD objective—is a principled and elegant extension of MMD to the conditional setting. While building on established work in kernel embeddings of conditional distributions [29], its application as a training objective for deep generative models is novel and significant. It provides a compelling alternative to Conditional GANs (cGANs) [21] by avoiding the difficulties of min-max optimization, and to Conditional VAEs [28] by offering a likelihood-free training paradigm. The extension from GMMN to CGMMN greatly broadens the applicability of moment-matching networks to a much wider and more practical set of problems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical motivation for CMMD is sound and well-explained. The paper clearly connects the matching of conditional embedding operators (`C_{Y|X}`) to the matching of the underlying conditional distributions (`p(y|x)`), grounded in Theorem 3. The derivation of the empirical CMMD estimator (Eq. 2) in terms of kernel matrices is correct and practical. The proposed generative architecture `y = f(x, h|w)` is a standard and effective choice for conditional generation.

3.  **Experimental Breadth and Strength:** A major strength of this paper is the comprehensive experimental evaluation across three distinct and important domains.
    *   **Prediction:** The classification results on MNIST and SVHN are strong, demonstrating that CGMMN is competitive with state-of-the-art deep generative models designed for discriminative tasks, such as max-margin DGMs [18].
    *   **Generation:** The qualitative results on generating MNIST digits and Yale faces conditioned on class labels are convincing. They clearly show that the model learns to control the output based on the conditional input.
    *   **Model Distillation:** The "Bayesian dark knowledge" experiment is a creative and compelling application of the framework, demonstrating that CGMMN can effectively learn a fast student model that approximates the predictive distribution of a complex Bayesian teacher model without significant performance degradation. This highlights the flexibility of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The CMMD objective requires the inversion of kernel matrices (`(K + λI)⁻¹`), which has a computational complexity of O(B³) for a mini-batch of size B. This is a significant computational bottleneck and a potential limitation on scalability compared to cGANs or CVAEs, whose per-batch costs are typically linear in B. The paper proposes a mini-batch algorithm but does not discuss the computational trade-offs or limitations imposed by this cubic complexity. How large a mini-batch is feasible in practice? How does the training time compare to that of a cGAN with a comparable architecture?

2.  **Clarity on Hyperparameters and Kernel Choice:** The performance of MMD-based methods is often sensitive to the choice of kernels (e.g., RBF) and their hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidth). The paper does not provide details on how these were selected for the various experiments. A discussion on the sensitivity of CGMMN to these choices, or the methodology used for their selection (e.g., cross-validation, heuristic), would significantly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility. For example, is a single kernel bandwidth used for all dimensions of `x` and `y`, or a more sophisticated scheme?

3.  **Qualitative Comparison of Generated Samples:** While the generated images are good, they appear somewhat noisy compared to state-of-the-art results from GANs of the same era. The paper mitigates this by using an autoencoder, which is a sensible practical approach. However, a direct qualitative comparison with a cGAN on the same generation task would be very valuable. This would help to better position the trade-offs: CGMMN may offer more stable training, but does this come at the cost of sample fidelity compared to adversarial methods?

4.  **Minor Details in Formulation:** The paper states that for the finite `X` case, `C_{YX}` and `C_{XX}^{-1}` can be estimated directly. It would be helpful to briefly explain in the main text how this is done (as it seems crucial for the generative experiments) rather than deferring entirely to the appendix, as it strengthens the reader's understanding of the practical implementation.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and versatile framework for conditional deep generative modeling. The proposed CMMD objective is a significant contribution that provides a stable, non-adversarial alternative to cGANs. The paper is well-written and the extensive experiments convincingly demonstrate the model's effectiveness across a variety of challenging tasks. The weaknesses, primarily related to computational cost and a lack of detail on hyperparameter selection, are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution. This work clearly advances the state of the art in deep generative models and is likely to inspire follow-up research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and principled CMMD objective for training conditional generative models.
*   Provides a stable, non-adversarial alternative to cGANs.
*   Strong and comprehensive experimental validation on prediction, generation, and a novel model distillation task.
*   Significantly increases the applicability of moment-matching networks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Potential scalability issues due to the cubic complexity of matrix inversion in the CMMD loss.
*   Lack of direct comparison with cGANs on generative tasks.
*   Insufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning for reproducibility.

I recommend acceptance. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the CMMD objective and the impressive breadth of applications, far outweigh the weaknesses. I encourage the authors to address the points above, especially regarding computational cost and hyperparameter selection, in the final version of the paper.
