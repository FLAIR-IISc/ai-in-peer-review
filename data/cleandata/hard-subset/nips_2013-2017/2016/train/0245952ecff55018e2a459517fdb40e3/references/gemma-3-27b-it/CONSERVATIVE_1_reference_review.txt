This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the existing Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to the conditional setting. The core idea is to leverage the simplicity of MMD for generative modeling, now applied to learning conditional distributions p(y|x). The authors present a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) criterion and demonstrate its application to predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

While the extension of GMMN to the conditional case seems natural, and the theoretical justification through CMMD is reasonably presented, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence to support its claims of competitiveness. The experimental section, while broad in scope, feels superficial. Results are presented without sufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning, architectural choices beyond broad strokes (e.g., number of layers, hidden unit sizes), and crucially, a thorough comparison against strong, well-established baselines. The reliance on datasets like MNIST and SVHN, while standard, doesn't fully showcase the potential or limitations of CGMMN. The claim of competitive performance needs stronger substantiation.

A major weakness lies in the limited novelty. The core contribution – adapting MMD-based generative modeling to the conditional setting – feels incremental. The authors acknowledge related work on conditional GANs and variational autoencoders but don’t convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage of CGMMN over these more established approaches. The theoretical connection to kernel embedding and MMD is solid, but the practical benefits in terms of performance or training stability remain unclear. The discussion of assumptions required for CMMD (smoothness for Hilbert-Schmidt norm existence) feels glossed over, and the practical implications aren't fully explored.

The writing is generally clear, though the presentation could be more concise. The mathematical notation is standard and well-defined. However, the organization is somewhat disjointed, jumping between theoretical background, methodology, and experiments without a strong narrative flow. The presentation of results, especially in the experimental section, lacks depth. For example, the Bayesian dark knowledge experiment simply states the RMSE without providing context or comparison to other distillation methods beyond the one cited in [15].

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a logically sound extension of GMMN to conditional distributions.
*   The theoretical foundation based on CMMD and kernel embedding is well-established.
*   The authors demonstrate applicability to several tasks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty is incremental, and the advantages over existing methods (GANs, VAEs) are not clearly demonstrated.
*   The experimental evaluation is superficial and lacks a thorough comparison to strong baselines.
*   The claims of competitive performance are not adequately supported by evidence.
*   The discussion of the assumptions underlying CMMD is insufficient.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards rejection. The paper presents a technically sound idea but lacks the compelling evidence and significant novelty required for acceptance at NIPS. The experiments feel more like a proof-of-concept than a rigorous evaluation. While the authors demonstrate that CGMMN *can* work, they fail to demonstrate that it offers a substantial improvement over existing techniques. Substantial revisions, including a more thorough experimental evaluation with stronger baselines and a more compelling demonstration of the advantages of CGMMN, would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors need to address the limitations discussed above and provide a more convincing justification for the contribution of this work.
