This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the original GMMN framework to handle conditional distributions. The core idea is to leverage the simplicity of MMD-based training for generative models in a conditional setting, offering an alternative to GANs and conditional GANs. The authors propose Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as the objective function and demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN across predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation tasks.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of previous work in generative modeling, specifically referencing GMMNs [19, 3], GANs [5, 21], and MMD [7]. It correctly identifies a gap in extending moment matching approaches to conditional generation, an area dominated by adversarial methods. The connection to Bayesian dark knowledge distillation [15] is also a nice application showcasing the versatility of the proposed method.  The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction of the problem and related work, followed by a detailed explanation of the theoretical background (Hilbert space embedding, MMD) and the proposed CGMMN framework.  

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending GMMNs to the conditional setting is a valuable contribution. It offers a potentially more stable training alternative to GANs for conditional generation.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is grounded in sound mathematical principles (MMD, kernel embeddings). The derivation of CMMD appears logically correct.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN on a diverse set of tasks (predictive modeling, contextual generation, dark knowledge distillation) and datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Boston Housing).
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the key concepts and equations. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 is helpful.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature, citing influential papers in generative modeling and kernel methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison:** While the paper compares CGMMN to several baselines, the comparison isn’t always comprehensive. For instance, in the MNIST predictive task, the comparison to DSN doesn't explicitly mention whether DSN employed data augmentation or model ensembling, which significantly impacts its performance. Similarly, in the generation tasks, visual comparisons to state-of-the-art GANs or VAEs are lacking.  Simply stating "competitive performance" isn't sufficient; a clear quantitative comparison is needed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't adequately address the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters (e.g., kernel bandwidth, regularization parameter λ). A more thorough ablation study would strengthen the conclusions.  The choice of λ, in particular, seems crucial for MMD-based methods, and its impact should be investigated.
* **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions the computational cost of kernel gram matrix calculation and proposes a mini-batch version, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity compared to other methods (e.g., GANs) would be beneficial.  
* **Scaling to High-Dimensional Data:** The performance on more complex datasets beyond MNIST and SVHN is not shown. It remains unclear how well CGMMN scales to higher-dimensional data and more complex conditional distributions.
* **Lack of Error Analysis:** A deeper analysis of the types of errors made by CGMMN would provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to conditional generative modeling.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of CGMMN on a diverse set of tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and methodology.
* The work addresses an important gap in the literature by extending MMD-based generative models to the conditional setting.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison to existing methods is not always comprehensive.
* The paper lacks a thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost.
* The scalability of CGMMN to high-dimensional data is not adequately addressed.
* The lack of error analysis limits our understanding of the method's limitations.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising approach to conditional generative modeling. The theoretical foundations are solid, and the experimental results are encouraging. However, the lack of a comprehensive comparison, detailed hyperparameter analysis, and discussion of computational cost prevents me from recommending acceptance in its current form. 

**Recommendation:**  **Reject with encouragement to revise.**

I recommend the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods, conducting a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, and discussing the computational complexity of the proposed approach.  A more in-depth evaluation on more complex datasets would also significantly strengthen the paper.  If these revisions are made, the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling.
