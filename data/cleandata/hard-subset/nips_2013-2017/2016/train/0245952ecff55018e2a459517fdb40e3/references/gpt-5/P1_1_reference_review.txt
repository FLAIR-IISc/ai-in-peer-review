Summary
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep conditional generative model trained by minimizing a conditional extension of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The key technical contribution is Conditional MMD (CMMD), defined as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X in tensor RKHSs. Under standard assumptions (universal kernels, existence of conditional embeddings), equality of these operators implies equality of conditional distributions. Practically, the authors estimate CY|X by Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤, use kernel Gram matrices to compute the CMMD loss in closed form, and train a conditional generator y = f(x, h | w) that concatenates conditional inputs x and noise h through a DNN, using backpropagation on CMMD. They emphasize that the kernel on x need not be differentiable since K is independent of network parameters.

Experiments span: (a) predictive modeling (classification) on MNIST and SVHN where y is a 10-D vector and prediction is argmax of a sample; (b) conditional generation on MNIST and the Extended Yale Face dataset (label → image), with qualitative samples and a nearest-neighbor sanity check; and (c) distillation of Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge”) by training CGMMN to mimic a PBP teacher on Boston Housing, showing comparable RMSE.

Relation to prior work
This builds directly on GMMN (Li et al., ICML’15; Dziugaite et al., UAI’15) by moving from unconditional MMD to conditional MMD using the kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML’09; Grunewälder et al., ICML’12). It contrasts with conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) by replacing the min-max game with a single objective and avoids density assumptions unlike CVAE (Sohn et al., NIPS’15; Kingma & Welling, 2014). The Laplacian pyramid of conditional GANs (Denton et al., NIPS’15) is discussed as related architecture; conditional SPNs and CRFs are also cited.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: elegant extension of MMD-based training to conditional distributions via operator-valued embeddings. The loss is explicit, stable, and amenable to SGD/backprop, avoiding adversarial training pathologies.
- Theoretical grounding: Theorem 3 ties equality of conditional operators to equality of conditionals under standard RKHS assumptions; the empirical operator is well-defined with regularization.
- Practicality: Simple sampling and training loop; K inverses do not depend on network parameters; works with various DNN backbones (MLP/CNN).
- Breadth of applications: addresses prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation in a unified framework; competitive classification results vs deep generative baselines.
- Useful insights: remark clarifying the relation between MMD and CMMD as uniform vs non-uniform weighting by x-dependent coefficients.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality/Technical
- Scalability: CMMD requires inverting K per minibatch (O(b^3) time, O(b^2) memory). The paper mentions mini-batches and an appendix but lacks runtime, memory profiles, or strategies (Nyström, random Fourier features) to scale to larger batches or higher-dimensional x.
- Kernel choices/hyperparameters: The kernels for x and y (type, bandwidth selection, normalization) and λ are not specified or analyzed. Performance and stability can be sensitive; no ablation or cross-validation details are provided. For classification, what kernel is used on y (one-hot vs continuous logits)? For image generation, what kernel on image space (pixel RBF?) and how is bandwidth chosen?
- Theory vs practice gap: Theoretical guarantees rely on strong assumptions and exact operator equality; empirical CMMD is computed on finite samples with x drawn from training data (often identical in both sets), which effectively reduces to matching at training x with specific non-uniform weights. No discussion of consistency, generalization in x, or sample complexity.
- Gradients and estimators: While K is independent of parameters, L depends on outputs. The paper should clarify gradient expressions and numerical stability for Φ, L, and trace terms, especially with large output dimensions (images).
- Evaluation breadth: The generative evaluation is mostly qualitative with nearest-neighbor checks. There are no quantitative metrics (e.g., Parzen window estimates used contemporaneously, or any likelihood surrogate), nor comparisons against cGAN/CVAE for conditional generation.
- Bayesian distillation: Only one small regression dataset is shown, without runtime gains, calibration, or uncertainty quality (e.g., NLL, predictive intervals). It would be important to demonstrate that the student captures uncertainty, not just RMSE.

Clarity
- Overall readable but contains typos and formatting issues (e.g., broken figure/table references; missing hyperparameter specs). The algorithm section should explicitly list the kernels, bandwidth selection strategy, λ, and batch sizes per task. State whether x in Dd equals x in Ds (it appears so), and discuss the implications.

Originality
- Extending MMD-based training to the conditional case via operator embeddings is a natural but non-trivial step that, to my knowledge, has not been used to train deep conditional generative models before. The methodological novelty is moderate and well-positioned relative to cGAN/CVAE and kernel conditional mean embeddings.

Significance
- The approach provides a practically appealing alternative to adversarial training for conditional modeling, with a principled loss and straightforward optimization. The applications are relevant. However, the empirical section under-explores generation (no rigorous metrics or strong baselines) and the scalability story is incomplete. Strengthening these would increase impact.

Pros
- Simple, stable objective; no adversarial min-max.
- Sound, well-motivated use of conditional mean embeddings.
- Flexible and easy to implement; works with standard DNNs.
- Competitive classification vs generative baselines; diverse conditional samples.
- Nice unification across three application types.

Cons
- Limited quantitative evaluation for generation; missing comparisons to cGAN/CVAE.
- Kernel/hyperparameter details and sensitivity missing; unclear choices for y-kernel.
- Scalability concerns due to Gram matrix inversions; no concrete mitigation or empirical costs.
- Limited scope and depth in Bayesian distillation; no uncertainty metrics.
- Theoretical assumptions not connected to finite-sample guarantees.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide kernel specifications, bandwidth selection procedures, λ, and ablations (batch size, kernel choices) for all tasks; add sensitivity analyses.
- Report training runtime/memory and consider scalable approximations (Nyström, random features) for CMMD.
- Add quantitative generation metrics available at the time (e.g., Parzen-based log-likelihood estimates) and compare against cGAN/CVAE on MNIST and Yale Faces.
- For classification, report calibrated metrics (e.g., NLL) and effect of multiple samples at test time.
- For Bayesian distillation, evaluate NLL and calibration/sharpness of predictive distributions, and show speedups over the teacher.

Overall recommendation
A solid and timely extension of GMMN to the conditional setting with a clear objective and promising results. While the empirical evaluation (especially for generation and scalability) needs strengthening and kernels/hyperparameters should be better specified, the contribution is technically sound and likely to be useful. I lean toward acceptance provided the authors address the evaluation and clarity concerns.
