This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the original GMMN framework to handle conditional distributions. The core idea – leveraging the simplicity of moment matching via kernel methods for conditional generative modeling – is compelling and potentially impactful. The authors correctly identify the importance of conditional generation in several application areas, and the proposed Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective appears theoretically sound. This work builds upon a relatively recent line of research utilizing MMD for generative modeling, offering a potentially simpler alternative to GANs.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of GMMN to the conditional setting is a genuinely novel contribution. While GANs and VAEs dominate conditional generation, a moment-matching approach offers a different perspective and potentially benefits from a more stable training process. The CMMD objective is a clever adaptation of MMD to the conditional case.
* **Simplicity and Elegance:** The approach is conceptually simple and avoids the adversarial training inherent in GANs. The reliance on backpropagation through a deep network is practical and efficient.
* **Broad Applicability:** The authors demonstrate the applicability of CGMMN across several tasks: predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. This versatility strengthens the paper’s potential impact.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical grounding, referencing relevant work on Hilbert space embeddings and MMD. The theorem linking the embedding operator to the equivalence of distributions is particularly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Empirical Validation:** While the authors showcase results on several datasets, the experimental evaluation feels somewhat superficial. The comparisons to baselines, while present, lack depth. For instance, the MNIST results show CGMMN-CNN is competitive but doesn’t decisively outperform other methods. A more thorough ablation study examining the impact of network architecture, kernel choice, and regularization would be beneficial. The generation examples (Figs. 2, 4 & supplementary) are decent, but lack quantitative evaluation (e.g., Inception Score, FID) to assess the quality and diversity of the generated samples.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity. How does the performance of CGMMN vary with different kernel parameters, regularization strengths, and network architectures? This is crucial for understanding the robustness and practical usability of the method.
* **Computational Cost:** While avoiding adversarial training is an advantage, the computational cost of evaluating and optimizing the CMMD objective, especially with large datasets and high-dimensional feature spaces, is not thoroughly addressed. The mini-batch algorithm is mentioned, but a detailed analysis of scalability would be valuable.
* **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** The paper mentions smoothness assumptions for the CMMD objective but doesn't provide sufficient discussion on the practical implications of these assumptions being violated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and conceptually elegant approach to conditional generative modeling.
* The CMMD objective provides a theoretically sound framework for training CGMMNs.
* The demonstrated applicability across multiple tasks suggests broad potential.
* The paper, despite minor flaws, represents a significant step towards simpler and more stable generative modeling techniques.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently compelling to definitively establish the superiority of CGMMN over existing methods.
* The lack of detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost raises concerns about practical usability.
* Some assumptions underpinning the theoretical framework are not adequately discussed in terms of practical implications.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper warrants acceptance despite the mentioned weaknesses. The novelty and elegance of the approach, combined with the potential for a simpler and more stable generative modeling paradigm, are highly appealing. While the empirical validation could be stronger and further analysis is needed to address practical concerns, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses raised in the review in a follow-up work. The potential impact of this research is significant, and I am optimistic that it will stimulate further investigation in moment-matching-based generative models.
