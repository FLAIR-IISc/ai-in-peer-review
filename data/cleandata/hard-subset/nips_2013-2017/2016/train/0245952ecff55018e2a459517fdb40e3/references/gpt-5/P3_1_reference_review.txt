Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a conditional deep generative model trained by minimizing a conditional analogue of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The key technical ingredient is Conditional MMD (CMMD), defined as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X induced by two conditional distributions. Using standard kernel mean embedding estimators, the objective can be evaluated from mini-batches via Gram matrices on X and Y, with backpropagation through the generator output y via a smooth kernel on Y. The generator takes concatenated conditional variables x and auxiliary noise h, and outputs y = f(x,h; w).

This extends GMMN (Dziugaite et al., 2015; Li et al., 2015) from unconditional to conditional distributions, and complements conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015). Theoretical support is given by noting that if CY|X coincide under a characteristic/universal kernel and appropriate regularity, then PY|x are equal for all x. Empirically, the method is evaluated on: (i) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, (ii) label-conditional image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces (with and without an autoencoder code space), and (iii) distilling Bayesian neural networks (PBP) on Boston Housing.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and clean objective: CMMD is a natural and principled extension of MMD to conditional distributions using conditional mean embeddings. The objective admits a tractable minibatch implementation and backpropagation.
- Generality: The framework is agnostic to the choice of generator architecture (MLP/CNN) and kernels. The fact that kernels on X need not be differentiable is practical.
- Breadth of applications: The paper demonstrates applicability to prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge. The distillation experiment shows no loss in RMSE compared to the teacher on Boston Housing.
- Solid connection to RKHS literature: The use of CY|X = C YX CXX^{-1}, the effect of regularization, and the operator-norm objective are well-motivated and connect to established results (Fukumizu et al., Song et al.).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical rigor and baselines:
  - Predictive modeling: For classification, the paper does not include in-house baselines trained with standard cross-entropy using the same architectures. Since for one-hot Y and a linear kernel on Y, CMMD becomes close to a weighted least-squares surrogate, it is important to show whether CMMD offers any advantage over conventional discriminative training. The current comparisons are to strong external baselines, but architectural parity and training details differ.
  - Conditional generation: Qualitative samples are shown, but no quantitative evaluations (e.g., Parzen estimates, FID/Inception score where applicable, human Turing tests) or likelihood proxies are reported. Comparisons to conditional GANs and conditional VAEs on the same tasks would strengthen claims of competitiveness.
  - Distillation: Only one small UCI dataset is presented, and only RMSE is reported. Since the goal is to approximate a predictive distribution, calibration metrics (predictive log-likelihood/NLL, calibration error) should be reported, and additional datasets tested (e.g., concrete, energy, naval, kin8nm) as in prior work.
- Missing or underspecified details:
  - Kernel choices and hyperparameters: The paper does not specify which kernels are used on Y (linear vs Gaussian) and on X, how bandwidths are set (median heuristic?), and how λ is chosen. Since CMMD’s behavior depends critically on kernel choice, this is essential for reproducibility and for interpreting results.
  - Computational cost: The minibatch objective requires O(m^3) inversion per batch (for batch size m). The paper should report runtime, batch sizes used in each experiment, and discuss approximations (e.g., Cholesky updates, Nyström, random features) for scaling beyond a few hundred per batch.
  - Gradients: While it is true that K̃−1 does not depend on w, backprop requires differentiating L with respect to generated y through the kernel on Y. Please clarify the choice of kernel and its gradients, and whether stability issues arise.
- Theory:
  - Theorem 3 is reasonable (operator equality implies conditional distribution equality under characteristic kernels), but the paper would benefit from a brief proof sketch or citation that explicitly connects CY|Xφ(x) equality and characteristicness of the kernel on Y to equality of PY|x, and a discussion of support issues (e.g., x in the support of PX).
  - The estimator ĈY|X = Φ(K + λI)^{-1}Υ⊤ is standard but often presented uncentered; a note on centering and its effect, and on the bias introduced by regularization, would help.
- Positioning: The paper could better situate CMMD relative to other conditional kernel methods, e.g., conditional two-sample tests and kernel conditional independence (Fukumizu et al., 2008; related KCIT/KC2ST variants), and clarify distinctions.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the CMMD objective and gradient-based training are well-posed, and the operator-based view is correct under cited assumptions. The main concerns are empirical: lack of strong, controlled baselines and quantitative evaluation on generation and distillation.

Clarity
Generally clear and readable, with a helpful Remark relating CMMD to weighted MMD. There are several typographical issues and incomplete references (years missing, formatting), and some critical implementation details (kernels, bandwidths, λ) are absent. Adding algorithmic complexity, kernel choices, and ablations would improve clarity and reproducibility.

Originality
The extension from MMD/GMMN to conditional distributions via conditional mean embeddings is natural but, to my knowledge, has not been presented in this particular form for training deep conditional generators. The idea is incremental yet meaningful, bridging two established lines of work (GMMN and conditional RKHS embeddings).

Significance
Moderate. The method is broadly applicable and easy to implement, and could be a useful alternative to cGANs/cVAEs when adversarial training is unstable or likelihoods are hard to specify. However, the current empirical evidence does not clearly advance the state of the art or show substantial benefits over simpler baselines, and the generation quality is not rigorously assessed.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, principled objective extending a popular idea (MMD) to the conditional setting.
- General framework applicable across tasks; avoids adversarial min-max.
- Theoretical grounding via conditional embeddings.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation lacks strong, controlled baselines and quantitative metrics, especially for generation and distillation.
- Important implementation details (kernel choices/hyperparameters) are missing.
- Limited scalability discussion and ablations.

Suggestions
- Add cross-entropy baselines with identical architectures for MNIST/SVHN.
- Report kernel choices, bandwidth selection, λ, and provide ablations on these.
- Include quantitative generation metrics and comparisons to cGAN/cVAE.
- Expand distillation to multiple datasets and report NLL/calibration.
- Discuss computational cost and possible approximations for larger batches.

Overall recommendation: borderline weak accept if empirical section is strengthened with the above additions; otherwise weak reject on the basis of insufficient experimental support for a NeurIPS acceptance. The conceptual contribution is clear and potentially useful.
