Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending GMMN by learning conditional distributions p(y|x). The key technical contribution is Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD), defined as the Hilbert–Schmidt norm between conditional mean embedding operators CY|X estimated from data and from the generator. The generator is a deep network that takes [x, h] (observed context and injected noise) and outputs y; parameters are learned by backprop through the CMMD objective. The paper evaluates CGMMN on (i) supervised prediction (MNIST, SVHN), (ii) conditional generation (MNIST digits given label, Yale faces given identity), and (iii) distilling Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge”) on Boston housing.

This work builds on three strands: (1) MMD-based deep generative models (GMMN) that match kernel mean embeddings (Dziugaite et al.; Li et al.), (2) kernel embeddings of conditional distributions and associated operators (Song et al., Grunewälder et al.), and (3) conditional deep generative models (cGAN, conditional VAE). The theoretical statement that equality of conditional embedding operators (with universal kernels and regularity) implies equality of conditional distributions is standard in the conditional embedding literature; the paper leverages this to define a practical training objective and differentiable generator.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The CMMD objective follows naturally from conditional mean embeddings. The estimator using Φ(K+λI)−1Υ⊤ is standard and the gradients w.r.t. generator parameters are straightforward. The theorem restates a known sufficiency result under universal kernels. The algorithm is coherent and stable (no adversarial min–max).
- Experimental support: The method works across tasks and architectures (MLP/CNN). On MNIST/SVHN classification it is competitive with max-margin deep generative models (CMMVA) but below strong discriminative SOTA (e.g., DSN). Conditional samples on MNIST and Yale are reasonable; combining with autoencoders improves perceptual quality, as in prior GMMN work. The Bayesian distillation experiment shows equal RMSE to the teacher on Boston housing.
- Missing or weak aspects: 
  - Comparative baselines lack conditional GAN (Mirza & Osindero) and conditional VAE (Sohn et al.)—the most natural points of comparison for conditional generation and supervised prediction.
  - No quantitative generative metrics (e.g., log-likelihood proxies, Parzen, or classification-based metrics); only qualitative samples and nearest-neighbor checks.
  - Limited analysis of kernel choices (kernels for X and Y, bandwidth selection, regularization λ), sensitivity, and their effect on performance.
  - Scalability: CMMD requires inverting K+λI per minibatch; while feasible, the paper does not report computational cost, stability, or batch-size trade-offs. The minibatch CMMD estimator’s bias/variance is not discussed.
  - Limited scope in Bayesian distillation (single small dataset), with no runtime/efficiency gains reported post-distillation.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized: background on MMD and conditional embeddings is helpful; the CMMD objective and generator are clearly presented. 
- Some implementation details are under-specified: precise kernels and bandwidth selection, λ values, number of generated samples per x, and whether multi-kernel MMD is used. The computational complexity and memory cost per batch are not analyzed. For classification, the representation of y and kernel choice on labels should be clarified.
- Figures and qualitative results are readable; algorithm description is concise.

Originality
- The idea of matching conditional distributions via conditional embedding operators and training a neural generator with this objective is, to my knowledge, novel as a deep conditional generative modeling approach in 2016. However, the theoretical foundation is largely known from kernel conditional embeddings, and the generator/training mechanism parallels prior GMMN work. Hence the contribution is an incremental but meaningful extension: from unconditional MMD to conditional operator matching with a deep generator.

Significance
- As a concept, CGMMN provides a simple, stable alternative to adversarial training for conditional generation and a flexible conditional model for prediction and distillation. It could be of interest to researchers favoring kernel objectives over adversarial ones.
- Empirically, the gains are modest: classification performance is competitive with other DGMs but below strong discriminative methods; generative results are qualitative; distillation shows parity but limited breadth. The absence of comparisons to cGAN/cVAE weakens the case for impact in conditional generation.

Pros
- Clear, principled objective derived from conditional mean embeddings; no adversarial min–max.
- Simple training by backprop; inverse kernel terms do not depend on network parameters.
- Works across tasks (prediction, conditional generation, distillation) and architectures.
- Qualitative samples are reasonable; classification performance competitive among DGMs.

Cons
- Missing key baselines (cGAN, conditional VAE); limited quantitative evaluation of generation.
- Limited analysis of kernel choices, sensitivity, and computational costs; minibatch CMMD properties not discussed.
- Theoretical novelty is limited; central lemma is known, and the extension from MMD to CMMD is straightforward.
- Distillation evaluation is minimal; no demonstration of efficiency gains or broader applicability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong conditional baselines (cGAN, conditional VAE) and quantitative generative metrics; include more datasets.
- Provide kernel/hyperparameter selection procedure, sensitivity analysis, and ablations (e.g., kernel on X vs label encodings).
- Analyze scalability: complexity per batch, effect of batch size, and possible speed-ups (e.g., random Fourier features, multi-kernel MMD).
- Expand Bayesian distillation experiments (more datasets, runtime savings, calibration, uncertainty).

Recommendation
- Overall: Borderline reject. The approach is elegant and generally sound, but the empirical evaluation lacks key comparisons and quantitative assessments to convincingly establish significance over existing conditional generative methods. With stronger experimental validation and analysis, this could be a solid contribution.

Confidence: Medium.
