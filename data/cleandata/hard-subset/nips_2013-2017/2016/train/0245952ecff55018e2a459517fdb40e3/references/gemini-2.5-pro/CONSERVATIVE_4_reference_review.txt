**Summary**

This paper proposes the Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Network (CGMMN), an extension of the Generative Moment-Matching Network (GMMN) framework to model conditional distributions. The core contribution is the development of a novel training objective, termed Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). This objective is derived from the theory of kernel embeddings for conditional distributions, measuring the Hilbert-Schmidt norm between the conditional mean embedding operators of the true data distribution and the generative model's distribution. The proposed generative model is a deep neural network that takes both the conditional variables and a random noise vector as input to generate samples. The authors evaluate CGMMN on a variety of tasks, including predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN, conditional image generation on MNIST and Yale Faces, and knowledge distillation from a Bayesian model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The theoretical foundation of the paper is solid. The proposed CMMD objective is a natural and well-justified extension of the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) criterion to the conditional setting, directly building upon established work on kernel embeddings of conditional distributions (e.g., Song et al., 2009). This provides a principled, non-adversarial objective for training conditional generative models.

2.  **Training Simplicity:** By avoiding the min-max game characteristic of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), CGMMN inherits the simpler and potentially more stable training dynamics of GMMNs. The objective function is a single loss term that can be optimized directly with standard back-propagation, which is a significant practical advantage.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The progression from MMD to the CMMD objective is logical and easy to follow. The authors clearly position their work relative to GMMNs and other conditional generative models like Conditional GANs and VAEs.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation and Weak Baselines:** While the paper explores several applications, the empirical evidence presented is not strong enough to demonstrate a significant advantage over existing, established methods.
    *   **Predictive Performance:** On both MNIST and SVHN, the CGMMN models are not competitive with state-of-the-art discriminative models (e.g., DSN). More importantly, they do not consistently outperform the most relevant generative baseline, CMMVA, and in some cases perform slightly worse. The motivation for using a generative model for prediction tasks is often to leverage unlabeled data or improve robustness, but the paper does not explore these aspects, leaving the question of *why* one should use CGMMN for prediction unanswered when simpler, better-performing discriminative models exist.
    *   **Generative Performance:** The qualitative results for image generation are underwhelming. The samples generated directly by CGMMN (Fig. 2) are noisy and lack the sharpness and fidelity commonly seen from contemporary GAN-based models. The authors themselves seem to acknowledge this by introducing a pre-trained autoencoder to generate cleaner samples (Fig. 4). While this is a valid technique, it complicates the proposed method and suggests that the CMMD objective alone may be insufficient for learning to generate high-quality images directly in pixel space. A critical omission is the lack of any quantitative or qualitative comparison to other well-established conditional generative models, most notably Conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) or Conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015). Without these direct comparisons, it is impossible to assess whether CGMMN offers any advance in generative quality. The nearest neighbor analysis only establishes that the model is not merely memorizing training examples, which is a very low bar for a generative model.
    *   **Bayesian Distillation:** This experiment is conducted on a small-scale regression dataset (Boston housing). While it shows that the distilled model's performance does not degrade, it fails to provide a compelling demonstration of the method's utility. A comparison against other knowledge distillation techniques would be necessary to establish the significance of this result.

2.  **Limited Novelty:** The main technical contribution, the CMMD objective, is a straightforward application of the conditional mean embedding operator from Song et al. (2009) as a loss function in the context of deep generative models, following the precedent set by GMMN. While the combination is new, the conceptual leap is modest. The paper primarily serves as an incremental extension of an existing framework (GMMN) into the conditional domain.

3.  **Practical and Computational Concerns:** The CMMD objective requires the inversion of kernel Gram matrices, an operation that scales cubically with the number of samples in a mini-batch. While the authors propose a mini-batch training algorithm, the scalability of this approach remains a concern, especially when compared to methods like GANs that do not suffer from this computational bottleneck. The paper does not provide an analysis of the computational trade-offs or demonstrate the method's performance on larger batch sizes or higher-dimensional conditional inputs where these costs could become prohibitive.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a methodologically sound extension of moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. The core idea is elegant and theoretically grounded. However, the work is let down by its empirical evaluation, which is not thorough enough to support the claims of competitive performance. The results do not demonstrate a clear advantage over established baselines in any of the tasks presented, and the generative quality appears to lag behind contemporary adversarial methods. Given the incremental nature of the theoretical contribution, a much stronger and more rigorous experimental section, with direct comparisons to state-of-the-art conditional generative models, would be required to warrant publication at NIPS.
