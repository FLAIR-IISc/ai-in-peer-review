

# Peer Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. The authors propose Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a training objective for conditional generative models, enabling the learning of flexible conditional distributions where some input variables are given. The CGMMN architecture takes both conditional variables and random noise as inputs, passing them through a deep network to generate target samples. The authors evaluate CGMMN on predictive modeling (MNIST, SVHN), contextual generation (MNIST, Yale Face), and distilling Bayesian models (Boston Housing). The paper builds on the theoretical foundation of kernel mean embeddings and MMD, extending it to conditional distributions.

The paper relates to previous work on GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), GMMN (Li et al., 2015), and conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014). It also connects to related work on conditional VAEs and Bayesian dark knowledge (Korattikara et al., 2015).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for CMMD, extending the MMD framework to conditional distributions. The theorems presented (Theorems 1-3) are well-motivated and correctly establish the connection between the conditional embedding operators and the conditional distribution equality.

2. **Simplicity and Practicality**: The CGMMN architecture is simple and easy to implement, leveraging the same generative process as GMMN but extending it to conditional settings. The use of back-propagation for training is a significant practical advantage over adversarial training methods.

3. **Versatility of Applications**: The paper demonstrates the applicability of CGMMN across multiple domains (predictive modeling, contextual generation, Bayesian distillation), showing the framework's flexibility and broad utility.

4. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is well-written with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical foundation to experimental validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison to Conditional GANs**: This is the most significant weakness. The paper mentions conditional GANs as related work but fails to compare CGMMN to them directly. Conditional GANs are the dominant approach for conditional generation tasks, and without this comparison, it's impossible to assess CGMMN's relative performance. For example, on the MNIST generation task (Section 4.2.1), the paper compares to GMMN but not to cGANs, which would be the natural baseline. This omission severely undermines the paper's significance claim.

2. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: While the paper provides theoretical results for CMMD, it lacks a theoretical analysis of the sample complexity or convergence guarantees for the CGMMN training process. The connection between CMMD and the quality of the learned conditional distribution needs more rigorous theoretical grounding.

3. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The paper compares to some baselines (VAE, MMVA, etc.) but misses key competitors in the conditional generative modeling space. For predictive modeling on MNIST (Section 4.1.1), the paper should have compared to state-of-the-art conditional GANs like cGANs or conditional Wasserstein GANs. The comparison to CMMVA is insufficient as CMMVA is also a moment-matching based approach, not the most relevant baseline.

4. **Vague Claims**: The paper claims "CGMMN leads to better performance with fewer training examples" but provides no evidence for this claim. It would be valuable to see performance as a function of training data size, especially since the authors emphasize this advantage.

5. **Missing Discussion of Limitations**: The paper fails to discuss the limitations of CGMMN, such as:
   - How the method scales to high-dimensional conditional variables
   - The sensitivity to kernel choice and regularization parameters
   - Potential issues with the approximation of conditional embedding operators

6. **Reproducibility Concerns**: While the paper provides the algorithm, key implementation details (kernel choice, regularization parameters, dimensionality of noise variables) are not specified, making reproduction challenging. For example, in the MNIST generation task, the paper mentions using a uniform distribution of dimension 20 for noise, but doesn't explain why this dimension was chosen.

## Critical Evaluation

### Quality
The paper is technically sound in its theoretical development, with the CMMD formulation correctly extending the MMD framework to conditional distributions. However, the experimental evaluation is incomplete due to the lack of comparison to conditional GANs and other relevant baselines. The claims about "competitive performance" are not adequately supported by the experimental results. The paper would benefit from more rigorous ablation studies, especially on the choice of kernel and noise dimensionality.

### Clarity
The paper is well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. However, the connection between the theoretical CMMD formulation and the practical implementation could be clearer. Some explanations of the conditional kernel embedding operators (Section 2.3) would benefit from more intuitive examples.

### Originality
The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions is a meaningful contribution, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish this work from existing conditional generative models, particularly conditional GANs. The paper claims that CGMMN "largely extends the capability of GMMN" but fails to clearly articulate how it differs from other conditional approaches. A more thorough comparison to conditional GANs would strengthen the originality claim.

### Significance
The significance of this work is somewhat limited by the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art conditional generative models. While the paper demonstrates competitive performance on several tasks, it doesn't establish that CGMMN offers significant advantages over existing methods. The paper would be significantly stronger if it could demonstrate specific scenarios where CGMMN outperforms conditional GANs or other conditional generative models.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add Comparison to Conditional GANs**: This is the most critical addition. The authors should add experiments comparing CGMMN to conditional GANs (and potentially conditional VAEs) on the same tasks, especially image generation.

2. **Expand Theoretical Analysis**: Include a discussion of sample complexity or convergence guarantees for CGMMN training.

3. **Provide More Implementation Details**: Specify the kernel choice, regularization parameters, and noise dimensionality used in experiments to improve reproducibility.

4. **Include Ablation Studies**: Add experiments showing how performance changes with different kernel choices, noise dimensions, and training data sizes.

5. **Clarify Limitations**: Discuss the limitations of CGMMN more thoroughly, particularly regarding scalability and sensitivity to hyperparameters.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a reasonable extension of the GMMN framework to conditional distributions. The theoretical development is solid, and the experimental results are generally positive. However, the paper's significance is significantly undermined by the lack of comparison to conditional GANs, which are the dominant approach for conditional generation tasks. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether CGMMN offers meaningful advantages over existing methods.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding comparisons to conditional GANs and other relevant baselines, as well as providing more thorough analysis of the method's limitations and implementation details. As it stands, the paper makes a modest contribution to the field but doesn't clearly establish its novelty or advantages over existing conditional generative models.

## Recommendation

**Weak Reject** - The paper has merit but has significant weaknesses that prevent it from making a strong contribution to the field. The most critical issue is the lack of comparison to conditional GANs, which is a major omission for a paper on conditional generative models. If the authors could address this omission and add more thorough comparisons to relevant baselines, the paper would be significantly stronger. As it stands, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate its novelty or advantages over existing methods.

## Pro/Con Acceptance List

**Pros:**
- Solid theoretical foundation for CMMD
- Simple and practical conditional generative architecture
- Demonstrates versatility across multiple applications (prediction, generation, distillation)
- Well-written with clear mathematical exposition

**Cons:**
- Missing comparison to conditional GANs (critical omission)
- Insufficient baseline comparison for key tasks
- Lack of theoretical analysis of sample complexity/convergence
- Vague claims without supporting evidence
- Limited discussion of limitations and implementation details
- Fails to demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods

This paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field, but it requires significant revisions to address the critical weaknesses identified above before it can be considered for acceptance at NIPS.
