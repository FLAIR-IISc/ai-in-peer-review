

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

This paper proposes Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions. The authors introduce Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a training objective for conditional generative models, where the goal is to match the conditional distribution of generated samples to the empirical conditional distribution. The paper claims to extend GMMN's capability to address predictive modeling, contextual generation, and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation.

The paper builds on established work (GMMN, MMD, kernel embeddings) but presents CMMD as a novel extension for conditional modeling. While the mathematical formulation appears technically sound, the novelty is questionable. The conditional MMD formulation seems to be a straightforward extension of the existing MMD framework rather than a significant theoretical advancement. The paper references recent work on conditional GANs [21, 28] but fails to provide a rigorous comparison against these established conditional generative models, which are the current state-of-the-art for many conditional generation tasks.

The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope and depth. On MNIST, CGMMN-CNN achieves competitive results but falls short of the state-of-the-art DSN model, which is explicitly mentioned as outperforming the proposed method. On SVHN, the results are comparable to CMMVA (a max-margin DGM) but not significantly better. The paper claims CGMMN "can obtain competitive performance in all these tasks," but the results don't demonstrate clear superiority over existing methods. For example, the generation results (Fig. 2) show samples that appear reasonable but not notably better than those produced by conditional GANs, which have been extensively validated in the literature.

A major concern is the lack of thorough comparison against the most relevant baselines. Conditional GANs [21] are the dominant approach for conditional generation tasks, yet the paper only mentions them in passing without providing a fair comparison. The authors compare to CMMVA (a max-margin DGM for prediction) but not to conditional GANs, which would be the most natural baseline for the generation tasks. This omission significantly undermines the paper's claims about CGMMN's competitiveness.

The theoretical contribution is minimal. The paper relies heavily on existing results from [29] about conditional kernel embeddings and presents CMMD as a straightforward extension. The authors claim to "fill the research void" for moment-matching networks, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this extension is necessary when conditional GANs and conditional VAEs already exist and have been thoroughly validated.

The Bayesian knowledge distillation experiment (Section 4.3) is interesting but limited to a single regression task (Boston housing). The results show comparable performance to the teacher model, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that CGMMN is a "new solution" for this task when other methods exist.

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear mathematical formulations and a logical flow. However, the lack of rigorous experimental validation against the most relevant baselines and the questionable novelty of the proposed method are significant weaknesses.

Strengths:
- Clear mathematical formulation of the conditional MMD
- Good coverage of multiple application areas (predictive modeling, generation, knowledge distillation)
- Well-structured and readable presentation
- Appropriate use of established kernel embedding techniques

Weaknesses:
- Limited comparison against state-of-the-art conditional generative models (conditional GANs)
- Results don't demonstrate clear superiority over existing methods
- Theoretical contribution appears minimal compared to the claimed novelty
- Insufficient validation for the Bayesian distillation application
- Overstated claims about competitiveness without strong empirical support

The paper makes a modest contribution to the literature on generative modeling but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of being a significant advancement. The method appears to be a straightforward extension of existing techniques without demonstrating compelling advantages over established approaches. The experimental validation is insufficiently thorough, particularly regarding the lack of comparison to conditional GANs, which are the dominant paradigm for conditional generation.

Given NIPS's emphasis on rigorous validation and significant contributions, I believe this paper falls short of the high standards expected at the conference. While the technical content is sound, the lack of substantial empirical evidence demonstrating clear advantages over existing methods and the questionable novelty prevent me from recommending acceptance.

I recommend rejection, as the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and competitive performance. The authors would need to conduct more comprehensive experiments comparing against conditional GANs and other state-of-the-art conditional generative models to strengthen their claims.
