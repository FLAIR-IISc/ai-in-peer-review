### **Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"**

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a method for learning conditional probability distributions `p(y|x)`. The work is a natural and significant extension of the previously proposed Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN), which focused on modeling joint distributions. The core contribution is the development of a novel training objective, Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD). CMMD measures the dissimilarity between two conditional distributions by computing the Hilbert-Schmidt norm of the difference between their respective kernel mean embedding operators. This objective is then used to train a deep neural network that generates samples `y` conditioned on an input `x` and a random noise vector `h`. The authors demonstrate the versatility of CGMMN on a range of tasks, including predictive modeling (classification), contextual image generation, and distilling knowledge from Bayesian models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a well-motivated and principled extension of the moment-matching framework to the conditional setting. While GMMNs offered an alternative to GANs for unconditional generation, their applicability was limited. By introducing a conditional variant, this work significantly broadens the scope and utility of this class of models, making them applicable to a much wider range of important problems like structured prediction, contextual generation, and inference in probabilistic models. This work successfully fills a research void and provides a compelling alternative to Conditional GANs and Conditional VAEs.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The proposed CMMD objective is theoretically well-grounded, building directly upon the established framework of kernel mean embeddings for conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009). The key idea of measuring the distance between the conditional embedding operators is elegant and provides a sufficient condition for the equality of the underlying conditional distributions (Theorem 3). This provides a more stable, single-objective optimization problem compared to the min-max game of adversarial training.

3.  **Experimental Breadth:** A major strength of the paper is the demonstration of CGMMN's effectiveness across three diverse application areas.
    *   The predictive modeling experiments on MNIST and SVHN show competitive performance against other deep generative models.
    *   The contextual generation results on MNIST and Yale Faces successfully demonstrate the model's ability to generate plausible and diverse samples conditioned on class labels.
    *   The application to "Bayesian dark knowledge" is particularly interesting and novel, showcasing a practical use case for CGMMN in model compression and distillation that is not commonly explored for generative models.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Missing Comparison to Key Baselines:** The most significant weakness is the lack of direct experimental comparison to Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (cGANs) [21]. cGANs are arguably the most direct and prominent competitor for the tasks of contextual generation. A comparison of sample quality (both qualitative and quantitative, if possible), training stability, and computational efficiency would be essential to properly situate CGMMN in the landscape of conditional generative models. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed CMMD objective over the adversarial one.

2.  **Computational Scalability:** The CMMD objective in Equation 2 requires computing and inverting kernel Gram matrices (`K+λI`). For a minibatch of size `N`, this is an `O(N^3)` operation, which can be computationally prohibitive. While the authors use a minibatch approach, the chosen sizes (e.g., 500 for MNIST) still imply a substantial computational cost per iteration compared to the simple forward/backward pass through a discriminator in a GAN. A discussion of this computational complexity, its practical limitations, and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., using random Fourier features or the Nyström method to approximate the kernel) would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Lack of Implementation Details:** The paper omits crucial details required for reproducibility. The choice of kernel functions (e.g., RBF, polynomial) and their associated hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidth) is fundamental to the performance of any MMD-based method. The authors do not specify which kernels were used for `x` and `y` variables in each experiment, nor how their parameters were selected. This makes it very difficult for other researchers to build upon or accurately replicate the reported results.

4.  **Generation Quality Assessment:** The evaluation of generative performance is purely qualitative, relying on visual inspection of samples and their nearest neighbors in the training set. While these are useful checks, the field has been moving towards more quantitative metrics to assess sample quality and diversity (e.g., Inception Score, FID, although FID came later). A more rigorous quantitative assessment would make the claims about generative performance more convincing, especially in the absence of a cGAN baseline. The generated MNIST digits in Figure 2, prior to the autoencoder-based refinement, appear somewhat noisy compared to contemporary generative models.

**Recommendation:**

Weak Accept.

The paper introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and significant contribution by extending moment-matching networks to the conditional setting. The proposed CMMD objective is elegant, and the wide range of successful applications demonstrates the model's flexibility. However, the manuscript is weakened by a critical lack of comparison to its most obvious baseline (cGANs) and a failure to discuss the practical scalability concerns of the `O(N^3)` CMMD objective. Furthermore, missing implementation details about kernel selection hinder reproducibility. Despite these shortcomings, the core contribution is strong enough to warrant publication at NIPS. The weaknesses can be addressed in future work and do not invalidate the central ideas presented.
