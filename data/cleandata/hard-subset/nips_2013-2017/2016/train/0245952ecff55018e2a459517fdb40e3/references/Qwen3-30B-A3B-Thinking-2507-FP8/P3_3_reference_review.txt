

# Peer Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary
This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extend Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) to conditional distributions. Building on the kernel embedding framework and maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), the authors propose a conditional MMD (CMMD) objective for training conditional generative models. The framework generates samples by passing conditional variables and random noise through a deep network, avoiding the adversarial training complexities of GANs. The authors demonstrate CGMMN on three tasks: predictive modeling (MNIST/SVHN classification), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on labels), and Bayesian dark knowledge distillation. While the paper extends GMMN to conditional settings, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from existing conditional generative models.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The theoretical foundation is solid, properly building on kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., ICML 2009) and MMD (Gretton et al., JMLR 2012). The CMMD formulation is mathematically well-defined and the paper correctly identifies the connection to previous work.

2. **Practical Simplicity**: The proposed framework avoids the min-max optimization challenges of GANs, using straightforward backpropagation for training. The generative process is simple and intuitive, making it accessible for practitioners.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper demonstrates the approach across multiple application domains (classification, generation, knowledge distillation), showing reasonable performance across tasks. The comparison with relevant baselines (CMMVA, DSN, etc.) is appropriate.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and experimental setup. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is straightforward.

## Weaknesses and Critical Concerns

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: While the paper develops CMMD theoretically, it lacks convergence guarantees or statistical properties of the estimator. The paper doesn't address how the choice of kernel or regularization affects the convergence rate or generalization performance. This is particularly important given the empirical results show CGMMN doesn't match the performance of discriminative models like DSN.

2. **Inadequate Comparison with Conditional GANs**: The paper claims to "fill the research void" for conditional moment-matching networks, but doesn't sufficiently contrast with conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), which are the dominant approach for conditional generation. The paper cites conditional GANs but doesn't discuss their advantages/disadvantages relative to CGMMN. For instance, conditional GANs have demonstrated superior image quality on standard benchmarks, yet the paper doesn't address why CMMD would be preferred for image generation tasks.

3. **Underdeveloped Generative Results**: The generated images (Fig. 2) show artifacts and lack the quality of contemporary generative models (e.g., conditional GANs or VAEs). The authors acknowledge this by combining with an autoencoder (Fig. 4), but this undermines the simplicity claim of their approach. The paper doesn't explain why this hybrid approach is necessary or how it compares to other generative techniques.

4. **Questionable Significance of Bayesian Distillation**: The Bayesian dark knowledge experiment (Section 4.3) shows minimal performance difference (2.574 vs. 2.580 RMSE), which is negligible in practical terms. The authors don't provide sufficient justification for why this application is compelling compared to simpler distillation methods.

5. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach, such as:
   - The cubic complexity of kernel matrix inversion in CMMD (Section 3.1) makes it challenging for large datasets
   - The choice of kernel and regularization (λ) isn't thoroughly analyzed
   - The paper doesn't discuss how the method scales to high-dimensional conditional variables

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound extension of GMMN to conditional distributions with a simple training procedure. While the approach has merit for specific applications where GAN training is problematic, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate significant advantages over existing methods. The experimental results show competitive but not superior performance compared to discriminative baselines, and the generative quality is not state-of-the-art.

The main contribution is the CMMD objective, but this is somewhat incremental given the prior work on conditional GANs and conditional VAEs. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison with these approaches, clearer discussion of when CGMMN would be preferable to other methods, and a more detailed analysis of the theoretical properties.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept** (or borderline)

The paper presents a technically valid approach that extends GMMN to conditional settings. The methodology is sound, the evaluation is comprehensive, and the simplicity of the training procedure is a genuine advantage over GANs. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate why this approach would be preferred in practice over existing conditional generative models, and the performance gains aren't compelling enough to justify the novelty claim. The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical analysis, more meaningful comparisons with state-of-the-art conditional generative models, and a clearer articulation of the specific scenarios where CGMMN would be the optimal choice.

For NIPS, this paper is somewhat incremental and doesn't advance the field as significantly as would be expected for a top-tier conference. While the technical contribution is valid, the significance is limited by the lack of clear advantages over existing methods and the modest performance gains demonstrated. The paper would be suitable for a more specialized venue but doesn't quite meet the bar for NIPS in terms of significance.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a thorough comparison with conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (So et al., NIPS 2015), not just discriminative baselines.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of the kernel choice and regularization parameter (λ) and their impact on performance.

3. Address the limitations of CMMD (e.g., computational complexity) and discuss when CGMMN would be preferable to other methods.

4. Include more qualitative analysis of the generative results to explain why CGMMN produces artifacts and how they might be mitigated.

5. Discuss the theoretical properties of the CMMD estimator, including convergence rates or statistical guarantees.
