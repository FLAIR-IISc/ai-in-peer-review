Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), a deep conditional generative model trained by minimizing a Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) objective. The generator takes as input both the conditioning variables x and auxiliary noise h, and outputs y via a deep network f(x,h|w). The training objective compares the empirical conditional mean embedding operator CY|X of the data with that of the model using the Hilbert–Schmidt norm, building on kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions [29] and the MMD paradigm for unconditional deep generators (GMMN) [3, 19]. The estimator reduces to trace expressions over Gram matrices of x and y with regularization, enabling backpropagation and minibatch training.

Empirically, the paper evaluates CGMMN across: (a) predictive modeling on MNIST and SVHN (x=image, y=class score vector), (b) conditional generation on MNIST (y→x) and Extended Yale Faces (identity→image), including a variant that models autoencoder codes, and (c) distillation of Bayesian predictive distributions (“Bayesian dark knowledge” [15]) from a PBP teacher [10] on Boston Housing. CGMMN is compared to VAE+linear SVM [14, 26], max-margin DGMs [18], and strong discriminative CNN baselines [6, 17, 20, 25, 33]. Results show CGMMN competitive with MMVA/CMMVA in prediction, plausible conditional samples, and no degradation in the small-scale distillation experiment.

Relative to prior work, CGMMN generalizes GMMN [3, 19] from unconditional to conditional modeling via kernel conditional mean embeddings [29], and provides an alternative to cGANs [21] and conditional VAEs [28] without adversarial training or variational bounds. The CMMD objective can be viewed as a non-uniformly weighted MMD that respects the conditioning variables.

Strengths
- Clear formulation connecting conditional mean embeddings [29] with deep generators, yielding a simple, non-adversarial training objective amenable to backprop.
- Practical training recipe with minibatches; the K-inverse depends only on x, simplifying gradients.
- Broad applicability demonstrated across prediction, conditional generation, and Bayesian distillation; results are competitive with prior conditional DGMs in classification, and generated samples are reasonable.
- Insightful remark relating CMMD to MMD with instance-dependent weights.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Theoretical conditions: Theorem 3 asserts that CY|X = CZ|X implies PY|X = PZ|X, assuming F (on X) is universal. For the implication “µY|x = µZ|x ⇒ PY|x = PZ|x,” universality/characteristicness is needed for the RKHS over Y (i.e., G), not X. Please clarify/correct the assumptions (G should be characteristic/universal; assumptions on F are needed for existence/estimation of CY|X via CXY C−1
XX [29, 8]).
- Statistical properties of the CMMD estimator are not discussed. Consistency of ĈY|X with λ→0 and N→∞ is known [29], but the paper does not state whether L̂2
CMMD is a consistent estimator of L2
CMMD, nor how minibatching affects bias/variance. Including at least a brief statement and references would strengthen the justification.
- Kernel choices and hyperparameters are under-specified. For x (high-dimensional images) and y (class-score vectors), the kernels (Gaussian? bandwidth selection?) and regularization λ critically affect CMMD. Please detail kernels, bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic), and sensitivity analyses.
- Computational aspects: Inverting K+λI per minibatch is O(b^3). With b up to 500, this can be a bottleneck. Runtime/memory comparisons with adversarial/variational alternatives would be useful, and any use of cached Cholesky or shared K between data and generated batches (often identical x) should be clarified.
- Predictive modeling: The setup effectively learns a conditional generator p(y|x); however, y is treated as an R10+ vector, and the final decision is argmax. It would be useful to specify the y-kernel and whether the model collapses to a near-deterministic mapping (the low-dimensional noise suggests limited stochasticity, which is appropriate for classification but should be discussed).
- Generation: Evaluation is primarily qualitative. While common circa 2016 for MNIST, adding simple quantitative metrics (e.g., class-conditional Parzen estimates as in [19], or FID on modern datasets where feasible) or comparisons to cGAN/CVAE on MNIST would strengthen claims.
- Distillation: The Boston Housing experiment is small. A larger benchmark (e.g., UCI regression suite as in [10]) or classification with Bayesian teachers would better demonstrate generality.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, but there are several typos/formatting issues (e.g., “MINIST,” “HilbertSchmidt,” “th Conference…,” spacing, “out network”). Figures/tables are referenced, but kernel specifics and training details (batch size per setting, noise dimension choices, perturbation strategy for x in distillation) should be consolidated in the main text or a clear appendix.
- Equation (2): define all Gram matrices explicitly with kernel notation, e.g., Kd[i,j]=kX(xdi, xdj), Ld[i,j]=kY(ydi, ydj), to avoid confusion with inner products φ⊤φ.

Originality
- The main novelty is applying the conditional mean embedding operator to train deep conditional generators via an HS-norm objective (CMMD). While conditional mean embeddings are established [29], and MMD-based training for deep generators is known [3, 19], the specific CMMD training of a deep conditional generator appears to be a natural but nontrivial extension. The connection to weighted MMD is a useful insight. The idea is incremental relative to cGAN [21] and conditional VAE [28], but it provides a distinct, simpler training criterion without adversarial optimization.

Significance
- Methodologically, CMMD offers a stable alternative to adversarial training for conditional generation and a flexible alternative to variational bounds, potentially useful where kernels are advantageous (e.g., small/medium data, structured outputs).
- Empirically, classification results are competitive with MMVA/CMMVA but below the best discriminative baselines; generative results are plausible on MNIST/Yale; distillation results are promising but limited in scope. Overall, the current empirical evidence indicates usefulness, but broader and more rigorous evaluations would increase impact.

Pros and cons for acceptance

Pros
- Conceptually clean extension of GMMN to conditional modeling with an explicit CMMD objective grounded in kernel embeddings.
- Simple, stable training; compatible with standard deep architectures; avoids adversarial min–max.
- Diverse applications with competitive performance in prediction and reasonable conditional generation.

Cons
- Theoretical assumption in Theorem 3 appears misstated (universality should be on G/Y); statistical properties of the CMMD estimator and minibatch approximation are not analyzed.
- Important practical details (kernels, bandwidths, λ) and sensitivity are missing.
- Evaluation is limited: qualitative generation, small-scale distillation, and classification not surpassing strong discriminative baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct/clarify theoretical assumptions (characteristic kernel on Y), and briefly state consistency of ĈY|X and L̂
CMMD with references [29, 8].
- Specify kernel choices, parameter selection, and provide sensitivity analyses; report computational cost.
- Add quantitative generative evaluations and comparisons to cGAN/CVAE on MNIST; expand distillation to more datasets.
- Include ablations on noise dimensionality, effect of sharing x between real/generated batches, and batch size/λ.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and practically useful extension of MMD-based deep generative modeling to the conditional setting, with room for strengthening theory precision and empirical evaluation.
