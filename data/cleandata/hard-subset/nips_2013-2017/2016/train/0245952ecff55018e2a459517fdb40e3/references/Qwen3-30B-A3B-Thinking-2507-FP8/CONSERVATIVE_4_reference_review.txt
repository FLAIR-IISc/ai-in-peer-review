

# Review of "Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks"

## Summary
The paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), extending the Generative Moment Matching Network (GMMN) framework to conditional distributions. CGMMN uses a conditional maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) criterion to train a deep network that generates samples conditional on input variables. The authors claim CGMMN can be used for predictive modeling, contextual generation, and distilling Bayesian models, with experiments on MNIST, SVHN, and Yale Face datasets.

## Strengths
The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for CMMD, building on the well-established MMD framework. The experimental setup is generally sound, with proper comparisons to relevant baselines. The authors demonstrate reasonable performance on standard vision tasks, and the proposed method is conceptually simple to implement.

## Weaknesses

### Methodological Soundness
The core contribution—CMMD—appears to be a straightforward extension of MMD to conditional distributions. However, the paper fails to establish sufficient theoretical justification for why CMMD is superior to other conditional generative approaches. The authors cite Theorem 3 as sufficient to guarantee distributional equality, but provide no empirical evidence that their CMMD objective actually converges to the true conditional distribution. More critically, the paper does not adequately address how CMMD handles the high-dimensional nature of real-world data, which is a known challenge for kernel methods.

### Experimental Validation
The experimental section presents limited validation of the core claim. The MNIST and SVHN results show marginal improvements over existing methods (e.g., CGMMN-CNN achieves 3.13% error on SVHN vs. 1.92% for DSN), but these results are not sufficiently compelling to justify the novelty claim. The paper fails to compare against more recent state-of-the-art conditional generative models, such as conditional GANs (cGANs) or conditional VAEs, which have been extensively studied in the literature. The comparison with CMMVA [18] is insufficient since CMMVA is an older method that has since been surpassed by more advanced approaches.

### Lack of Rigorous Comparison
The paper does not properly address the relationship to existing conditional generative models. The introduction mentions conditional GANs [21] as a related work but fails to demonstrate how CGMMN differs in a meaningful way from these approaches. The authors claim CGMMN "largely extends the capability of GMMN," but provide no evidence that this extension actually yields better performance on challenging tasks. The paper also doesn't address the computational complexity of CMMD compared to alternative approaches, which is particularly important given the cubic complexity of kernel matrix operations mentioned in the paper.

### Limited Theoretical Analysis
The paper lacks rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of CGMMN. The authors state that "the empirical estimator is always an element in the tensor product space which gives as a well-justified approximation," but provide no proof or analysis of the convergence rate or error bounds. This is a significant omission for a paper claiming methodological advancement.

### Significance
The paper's contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. While the authors claim CGMMN "fills the research void to significantly improve the applicability of moment-matching networks," they fail to demonstrate this improvement convincingly. The approach is essentially a specialization of existing kernel-based methods to conditional settings, without providing compelling evidence that it outperforms more established conditional generative approaches.

## Recommendation
This paper presents an incremental extension of existing moment-matching techniques to conditional distributions. While the theoretical foundation is sound, the paper lacks sufficient evidence to justify the novelty claim and demonstrate superior performance over established methods. The experimental validation is limited and fails to compare against more recent state-of-the-art approaches. The paper would benefit from a more thorough theoretical analysis of the CMMD objective and more comprehensive experimental comparisons with modern conditional generative models.

The authors should address the following concerns before reconsideration:
1. Provide a rigorous comparison with recent conditional generative models (cGANs, conditional VAEs)
2. Include a more thorough theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of CGMMN
3. Demonstrate significant performance improvements over existing methods on challenging tasks
4. Clarify how CGMMN differs meaningfully from existing conditional moment-matching approaches

Without these improvements, the paper does not make a sufficiently strong contribution to the field to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. The incremental nature of the contribution, combined with inadequate validation against the most relevant baselines, makes it difficult to justify the novelty claim.
