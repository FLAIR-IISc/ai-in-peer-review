

# Peer Review: Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces Conditional Generative Moment-Matching Networks (CGMMN), which extends the Generative Moment-Matching Networks (GMMN) framework to handle conditional distributions. GMMN, introduced by Li et al. (2015), uses Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) as a training objective for generative models, avoiding the min-max optimization challenges of GANs. The authors propose a conditional version of this framework by introducing Conditional Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD) as a criterion for measuring the difference between conditional distributions.

The paper makes three main contributions: (1) it defines CMMD as a Hilbert-Schmidt norm between kernel mean embeddings of conditional distributions; (2) it proposes a conditional generative model architecture where the network takes both conditional variables and random noise as inputs to generate target samples; and (3) it evaluates CGMMN across multiple tasks including predictive modeling (classification), contextual generation (image generation conditioned on class labels), and distilling Bayesian models (Bayesian dark knowledge).

The work builds on previous research in moment-matching networks (Li et al., 2015) and kernel embedding of conditional distributions (Song et al., 2009), while extending the applicability of these methods to conditional settings. The paper also relates to conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015), though it distinguishes itself by avoiding adversarial training and leveraging the simpler MMD objective.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for CMMD, building on established kernel embedding techniques and extending them to conditional distributions. The authors clearly derive the CMMD objective and connect it to the MMD framework.

2. **Versatile Applications**: The evaluation across multiple tasks (classification, image generation, Bayesian knowledge distillation) demonstrates the broad applicability of the proposed framework. The results show competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods in each domain.

3. **Simplicity of Training**: The CGMMN framework avoids the min-max optimization challenges of GANs and can be trained efficiently using back-propagation, which is a significant practical advantage.

4. **Well-Designed Experiments**: The experimental section is comprehensive, with thorough evaluations on multiple datasets (MNIST, SVHN, Yale Face, Boston housing) and comparisons with relevant baselines. The ablation studies and analysis of generated samples (e.g., nearest neighbors) provide strong evidence against trivial solutions.

5. **Practical Implementation**: The paper provides clear algorithmic details (Algorithm 1) and practical implementation considerations (e.g., handling finite vs. continuous conditional variables).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Conditional GANs**: The paper does not provide a direct comparison with conditional GANs (cGANs), which are the most common approach for conditional generation tasks. While cGANs have their own challenges, a comparison would help contextualize the advantages of CGMMN over the dominant approach.

2. **Computational Complexity Concerns**: The paper acknowledges that the CMMD objective involves matrix inversions that scale cubically with dataset size but does not thoroughly address how this limitation affects scalability to larger datasets or more complex models. The mini-batch approach (Algorithm 1) is mentioned but not extensively evaluated.

3. **Theoretical Justification of Assumptions**: The paper relies on certain smoothness assumptions for the CMMD objective to be well-defined (Section 3.1), but it doesn't provide empirical evidence that these assumptions hold in practice for the datasets and architectures used.

4. **Comparison with Conditional VAEs**: While the paper compares with VAEs in the classification task, it doesn't explore how CGMMN compares to conditional VAEs for the generation tasks, which would be an important comparison point.

5. **Lack of Ablation Studies on Key Components**: The paper doesn't include ablation studies on the impact of different kernel choices, the dimensionality of the noise input, or the network architecture on the CMMD objective.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

### Quality
The paper is technically sound, with a clear theoretical foundation and well-executed experiments. The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses by comparing with multiple baselines across diverse tasks. The claims are well-supported by empirical results, though the paper could have provided more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of the CMMD objective. The work appears to be a complete piece of research rather than a position paper.

### Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction provides a strong motivation for the work, the preliminary section establishes the necessary background, and the methodology is presented logically. The figures (e.g., Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 4) effectively illustrate key concepts and results. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the main results, though some algorithmic details are relegated to the appendix.

### Originality
The extension of GMMN to conditional distributions using CMMD represents a novel contribution to the field. While the building blocks (MMD, kernel embeddings) are not new, their combination to form a conditional generative framework is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly in comparison to conditional GANs and conditional VAEs. The related work section is adequate, though it could better address the relationship to conditional GANs.

### Significance
The results demonstrate that CGMMN can obtain competitive performance across multiple tasks, suggesting practical utility for conditional generative modeling. The framework addresses a significant limitation of existing moment-matching networks by enabling conditional generation, which is crucial for many applications. The approach advances the state of the art in moment-matching based generative models and provides a simple, efficient alternative to conditional GANs. The Bayesian dark knowledge application is particularly interesting and shows a novel use case for the framework.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound extension of moment-matching networks to conditional distributions. The theoretical foundation is solid, the implementation is straightforward, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive across multiple domains. While the paper has some limitations (particularly the lack of direct comparison with conditional GANs), these do not significantly undermine the core contribution.

The work is a meaningful contribution to the field of generative models, particularly for those seeking simpler training procedures than GANs. The CGMMN framework could be valuable for practitioners working on conditional generation and prediction tasks.

## Final Recommendation

**Accept**

The paper makes a clear, novel contribution to the field of generative models by extending moment-matching networks to handle conditional distributions. The work is well-executed, with strong theoretical grounding and comprehensive experimental validation. The simplicity of the training process (avoiding min-max optimization) is a significant practical advantage over conditional GANs, and the framework demonstrates competitive performance across multiple tasks. While some comparisons with conditional GANs would strengthen the paper, the core contribution is substantial and well-supported.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. Consider adding a direct comparison with conditional GANs (cGANs) in the experiments, particularly for the image generation tasks. This would help contextualize the advantages and limitations of the CGMMN approach.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the CMMD objective, including how it scales with dataset size and model complexity, and discuss potential approximations or optimizations for large-scale applications.

3. Include ablation studies on key components of the framework (e.g., kernel choice, noise dimension, network architecture) to better understand their impact on performance.

4. Expand the discussion of the relationship between CGMMN and conditional VAEs, particularly for the generation tasks, to provide a more complete picture of where CGMMN fits in the landscape of conditional generative models.

5. In the theoretical section, provide more concrete evidence that the smoothness assumptions for the CMMD objective hold in practice for the datasets and architectures used.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.
