### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method. The core idea is to select a batch of `q` points for simultaneous evaluation by maximizing an acquisition function that represents the one-step Bayes-optimal decision. This acquisition function, q-KG, measures the expected improvement in the minimum of the posterior mean function after observing the outcomes of the `q` evaluations. To make the maximization of this acquisition function computationally tractable, the authors propose an efficient gradient estimation strategy using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), enabling the use of gradient-based optimizers. The method is evaluated on a set of synthetic benchmark functions and two practical hyperparameter optimization tasks: tuning a logistic regression model on MNIST and a convolutional neural network (CNN) on CIFAR-10. The experiments, conducted in both noise-free and noisy settings, suggest that q-KG is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods and shows a particularly strong advantage when function evaluations are noisy.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The problem of parallel hyperparameter optimization is highly relevant to the machine learning community. As multi-core CPUs and distributed computing environments become standard, methods that can effectively leverage this parallelism to speed up expensive optimization tasks are of great practical importance. This paper directly addresses this significant need.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background on Gaussian processes is concise, and the derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is clear. The technical details of the Monte Carlo estimation and the IPA-based gradient computation are described well, and the experimental setup is understandable.

3.  **Principled Approach:** The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, aiming to find the one-step Bayes-optimal batch of points. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed acquisition function.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The core technical components of the paper, namely the Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy and the use of Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) for gradient estimation, are not novel. More importantly, the application of IPA to maximize a parallel acquisition function in Bayesian optimization has been previously explored for Expected Improvement (q-EI) by Wang et al. (2015). The contribution here appears to be the application of this existing computational technique to a different (though related) acquisition function (q-KG). The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contribution from this highly similar prior work.

2.  **Unconvincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are not sufficient to fully support the paper's claims about practical utility and superiority.
    *   **Missing Wall-Clock Time:** The paper's motivation is to find optima faster in parallel environments, yet all results are presented in terms of "iterations" (batches of evaluations) rather than wall-clock time. The optimization of the q-KG acquisition function via gradient ascent with Monte Carlo estimation is likely computationally expensive. Without measuring this overhead, it is impossible to know if the method provides a real-world speedup over simpler baselines like parallel random search or even sequential Bayesian optimization.
    *   **Missing Baselines:** A comparison to parallel random search is essential, as it is a surprisingly strong and simple baseline for parallel optimization. Furthermore, a comparison to a sequential BO baseline would help quantify the actual benefit gained from parallelization.
    *   **Fixed Batch Size:** All experiments use a fixed batch size `q=4` without justification. This provides no insight into how the method's performance or computational cost scales as `q` increases to tens or hundreds, which are common scenarios in large-scale applications.
    *   **Ambiguous Plots:** The x-axis in the plots is labeled "iterations," which is ambiguous. Furthermore, the plots seem to show different methods starting with different performance levels after the initial design, which is confusing if they all share the same initial random samples.

### Detailed Questions and Comments

The work is interesting and addresses an important problem. However, I have several major concerns that prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.

**Major Questions:**

1.  **Novelty and Comparison to q-EI:** Your work is very similar to Wang et al. (2015), who also use IPA to optimize a parallel acquisition function (q-EI). Could you please explicitly clarify the key differences and contributions of your work beyond applying the same computational machinery to the KG criterion instead of the EI criterion? Why would one expect q-KG to be significantly better than q-EI, especially in the noisy setting? A direct experimental comparison would be very valuable.

2.  **Scalability with Batch Size `q`:** How does the method scale with the batch size `q`? The computational cost of the Cholesky decomposition and Monte Carlo gradient estimation likely scales poorly with `q`. What is the practical limit on `q`? Showing results for a wider range of `q` (e.g., 2, 8, 16) would make the evaluation much more comprehensive and useful for practitioners.

3.  **Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Parallelism:** The proposed method is synchronous, meaning it waits for all `q` evaluations in a batch to complete before selecting the next batch. In practice, evaluation times can vary significantly, making synchronous methods inefficient. Have you considered how this framework could be extended to an asynchronous setting, where new jobs are scheduled as soon as a worker becomes free? This is a critical aspect for real-world deployment.

4.  **Practical Performance and Overhead:** This is a crucial point. What is the wall-clock time overhead for computing each new batch? For example, in the CNN experiment, how long (in minutes or hours) does it take to select the next batch of 4 points, compared to the time it takes to train the 4 CNNs? A plot of performance vs. total wall-clock time (computation + evaluation) is necessary to demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.

5.  **Clarification on Experiments:**
    *   Could you please clarify the x-axis in Figures 3 and 4? Does "iterations" refer to the number of batches (so the total number of function evaluations is `iteration * q`), or the total number of function evaluations? Does it include the initial `2d+2` points?
    *   In the plots, why do the different algorithms start with different mean regrets at the first iteration after the initial design? Since the initial points are supposedly the same across runs for a given seed, the initial posterior and thus the initial regret should be identical.

**Minor Comments:**

*   **Eq (4.1):** The notation `En [ minx∈A µ(n+q)(x)|y(z(1:q)) ]` is slightly confusing, as `µ(n+q)` is itself a function of the random outcomes `y(z(1:q))`. The dependency seems circular. Please clarify the notation.
*   **Section 6.1.2:** "our automatic algorithm improves it to around 11%." -> "our automatic algorithm achieves a test error of around 11%."
*   **Citations:** When referring to open-source implementations, it is clearer to cite the paper associated with the method, e.g., "...the parallel EI method of Wang et al. [25], for which we use the implementation in [24]."
*   The paper credits Wang et al. (2015) in relation to gradient-based methods. It should be noted that Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) [14] is a much older and more general technique.
