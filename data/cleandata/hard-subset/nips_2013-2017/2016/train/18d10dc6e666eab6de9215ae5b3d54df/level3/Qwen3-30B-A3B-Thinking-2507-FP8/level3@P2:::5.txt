

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that extends the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the batch setting. The authors derive q-KG as the Bayes-optimal batch acquisition function for minimizing the expected minimum of the Gaussian process predictor when only one batch remains. They address the computational challenge of optimizing q-KG by proposing an infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) approach to efficiently estimate its gradient. The paper demonstrates that q-KG consistently outperforms other batch Bayesian optimization methods (including parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration) on synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper builds on several strands of prior work. The sequential KG method was introduced by Frazier et al. (2009) for the non-batch setting, and the authors correctly acknowledge this as the foundation for their work. The paper also relates to recent efforts to extend Bayesian optimization to parallel settings, including the work by Wang et al. (2016) on parallel Bayesian global optimization and the GP-UCB-PE method by Contal et al. (2014). The authors correctly note that most prior parallel BO methods construct batches in a greedy, iterative fashion rather than optimizing over the entire batch simultaneously, which is the key distinction of their approach.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical foundation. The derivation of q-KG as the Bayes-optimal batch acquisition function is well-motivated. However, the computational approach in Section 5.2 requires more detail to assess its technical merits. The IPA gradient estimator is presented as a key contribution, but the paper lacks a thorough comparison of its computational efficiency against previous batch KG methods (e.g., the nested batch knowledge gradient approach by Wang et al. (2016), which the authors cite but don't properly contrast with). The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with both synthetic and real-world experiments, but the comparison to Expected Improvement (EI) is problematic as EI assumes noise-free observations while the authors test in noisy settings without clarifying if they used a noise-aware EI variant. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of computational complexity.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical formulations. However, Section 5.2 (the core computational contribution) is insufficiently detailed. The IPA approach is introduced as "an unbiased estimator" but lacks sufficient explanation of why it's better than alternative gradient estimation methods (e.g., Monte Carlo). The paper redefines "IPA" at line 162, which is confusing and should be corrected. The experimental setup is well-described, but the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of implementation choices (e.g., why M=1000 for discretization).

**Originality:** The core contribution appears to be the computational approach for efficiently optimizing the batch KG acquisition function, rather than the acquisition function itself (which has been previously considered in other works). The authors cite Wang et al. (2016) as having studied a "nested batch knowledge gradient policy," but they don't sufficiently clarify how their work differs from this prior contribution. The paper would benefit from a more precise statement of what is novel about their computational approach compared to existing batch KG methods.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important practical problem in Bayesian optimization—parallel evaluation of multiple points—which has significant implications for hyperparameter tuning of machine learning models. The empirical results showing superior performance, especially in noisy settings, are promising and would be valuable for practitioners. However, the significance would be stronger with a clearer demonstration of why the q-KG approach provides advantages over the prior batch KG methods rather than just other batch BO methods.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Addresses an important practical problem (parallel Bayesian optimization)
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over state-of-the-art batch BO methods
- Provides a theoretically grounded acquisition function with a clear decision-theoretic foundation
- Includes comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world problems

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Fails to sufficiently clarify novelty relative to prior batch KG work (Wang et al. 2016)
- Insufficient detail on the computational approach (Section 5.2) to assess its technical merits
- Problematic comparison to EI without addressing the noise assumption mismatch
- Missing comparative analysis of computational efficiency with prior batch KG methods

This is a strong paper with clear practical relevance, but it requires significant clarification of its novelty and more detailed explanation of the computational approach. The authors should explicitly compare their computational method to prior batch KG approaches and clarify the noise assumptions in their comparisons. With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept (with major revisions). The paper has significant potential but requires substantial clarification of its novelty and more detailed technical exposition of the key computational contribution. The authors should address the concerns regarding novelty relative to prior batch KG work, provide more detail on the IPA approach, and clarify the noise assumptions in their comparisons.
