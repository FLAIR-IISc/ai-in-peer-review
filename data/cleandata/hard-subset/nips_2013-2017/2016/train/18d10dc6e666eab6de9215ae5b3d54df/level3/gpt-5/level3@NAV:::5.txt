This paper proposes a batch (parallel) knowledge gradient (q-KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization and an IPA-based Monte Carlo gradient estimator to optimize it. The method is motivated by a decision-theoretic one-step Bayes-optimal criterion for batches, estimated via Monte Carlo under a GP posterior, and optimized with stochastic gradients. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning report improvements over q-EI variants and batch UCB methods, especially under observation noise.

Relation to prior work: The formulation connects naturally to sequential KG (Frazier et al.) and to batch acquisition functions such as q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.; Wang et al.) and batch ES (Shah & Ghahramani). However, batch KG per se has been explored in adjacent literatures (e.g., correlated KG extensions, and nested/batch KG in simulation/materials). The paper should more clearly delineate what is new: is the novelty the specific Bayes-optimal batch derivation for continuous A, or primarily the stochastic gradient estimator and practical optimizer?

Quality: The decision-theoretic objective is sound, and the reparameterization for Monte Carlo estimation is standard. Yet Section 5.2 (the IPA gradient estimator) is too terse: assumptions ensuring unbiasedness and interchange of gradient and expectation, handling of ties/nonsmooth minima, and variance control are deferred. Please expand proofs or provide a rigorous sketch and complexity/variance analyses. Critically, if the gradient estimator is the main contribution, include empirical/theoretical comparisons against prior batch KG implementations (if any) and against alternative estimators used for q-EI (e.g., reparameterization/score-function), reporting wall-clock time, optimizer convergence, and sensitivity to M, q, and dimension. The discretization of A is central to the Bayes-optimality claim; discuss its bias/variance tradeoffs.

Clarity: Overall clear, but Section 5.2 needs substantially more detail. The dual use of “A” (optimization domain vs. discretization set) is confusing; consider distinct symbols. Fix the minor issue where “IPA” is redefined at line 162.

Originality: As written, novelty over prior batch KG work is not fully established. Please position the contribution relative to earlier batch KG variants and clarify what is fundamentally new.

Significance: If substantiated, a noise-robust, jointly optimized batch rule is valuable. However, comparisons to EI are problematic: classical EI assumes noiseless observations. Clarify whether a noise-aware q-EI (with observation noise/fantasization) was used and analyze how noise modelling affects Section 6.2.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled batch objective; compelling noisy-BO results.
- Practical stochastic gradient optimization with code.

Cons for acceptance:
- Unclear novelty vs prior batch KG; missing direct comparisons.
- Insufficient detail/analysis in Sec. 5.2; limited wall-clock and sensitivity studies.
- EI comparison under noise may be unfair without noise-aware baselines.
