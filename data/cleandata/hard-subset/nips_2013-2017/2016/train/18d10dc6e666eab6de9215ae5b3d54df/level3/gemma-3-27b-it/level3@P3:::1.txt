This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization, offering a theoretically grounded approach to selecting batches of points for parallel evaluation. The core idea – extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient to a parallel setting by maximizing expected information gain – is promising and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate through experiments that q-KG often outperforms existing batch BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluations.

**Quality:** The paper is generally technically sound. The derivation of q-KG follows a logical progression from the sequential KG, and the use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to address the computational challenges of gradient estimation is clever. The experiments provide evidence supporting the effectiveness of the method, and the inclusion of both synthetic and real-world problems strengthens the claims. However, the authors don’t fully explore the computational cost of q-KG versus simpler methods, a potential weakness discussed later. The discussion of GP hyperparameters (ARD Matérn 5/2 kernel, MLE for noise variance) is standard and appropriate. A minor error (Matérn parameter 2/5 stated in the text) detracts slightly from the meticulousness.

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly well-written and organized. The notation is generally clear, though some sections (particularly those involving the gradient calculations) are dense and require careful reading. Algorithm 1 is helpful, but lacks crucial detail regarding hyperparameter re-estimation/updating – a significant omission (see questions below). Section 5.2’s definition of ‘g’ needs clarification (max or min?). The flow could be improved by explicitly highlighting the two distinct ‘A’s used in the equations – the discretization used for computation versus the feasible domain.

**Originality:** The extension of the Knowledge Gradient to a parallel batch setting is a novel contribution. While parallel BO is not new, the decision-theoretic foundation and the q-KG criterion represent a significant advance. The use of IPA for efficient gradient computation is also a valuable addition. However, the related work section is incomplete (see below), diminishing the perception of novelty slightly.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a crucial problem in Bayesian Optimization – leveraging parallelization for faster optimization. The potential impact is high, particularly in areas like hyperparameter tuning for deep learning, where function evaluations are expensive. The demonstrated improvements over existing methods, especially in noisy settings, suggest q-KG could become a valuable tool for practitioners. The authors correctly highlight the potential societal impact of parallelizing BO.

**Strengths:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation based on decision theory.
*   Effective gradient estimation technique (IPA).
*   Empirically demonstrated performance gains over state-of-the-art methods.
*   Well-defined algorithm and clear presentation of the method.
*   Open-source code availability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incomplete related work section (omissions of Chevalier et al. and prior work on EI integration).
*   Lack of analysis of the computational speed-ups gained from batch processing.
*   Algorithm 1 lacks detail regarding hyperparameter updating.
*   Ambiguity in Section 5.2 regarding the definition of ‘g’ (max or min?).
*   Unclear guarantees regarding the smoothness of ‘g’ in Section 5.2.
*   Potential issues with the differentiability of the Cholesky factor.
*   Minor typo in Matérn parameter value.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements, particularly in noisy settings.
*   The code availability facilitates reproducibility and wider adoption.
*   The potential impact of efficient parallel BO is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The related work section is incomplete and contains imprecise claims.
*   The paper does not adequately address the computational overhead of q-KG compared to simpler methods.
*   Several minor clarifications and corrections are needed to enhance clarity.

**Specific Questions/Concerns:**

1.  **Domain Assumptions:** Is the domain *A* assumed to be compact, and is the function *f* assumed to be continuous? These assumptions are implicit but important for the theoretical guarantees.
2.  **Initial Design:** Why is the initial design *A* restricted to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS)? Could other sampling strategies (e.g., random sampling) be used, and what impact would that have?
3.  **Hyperparameter Updating:** Algorithm 1 states “re-train the hyperparameters of the GP by MLE,” but doesn’t specify *how and when* these hyperparameters are updated. Is this done after every batch evaluation, and what optimization algorithm is used for MLE?
4.  **Definition of ‘g’:** In Section 5.2, is *g* defined using a max or a min? The text lacks clarity on this point.
5.  **Smoothness of ‘g’:** The smoothness of *g* in Section 5.2 is not obviously guaranteed. What conditions on *f* and *K* ensure smoothness, and why is this important for the optimization process?
6.  **Cholesky Factor Differentiability:** The differentiability of the Cholesky factor *D* used in the derivations may not be obvious. Could this pose a problem for the gradient-based optimization?
7. **Experimental Verification:** Could the GPs used in Figure 1 be fitted using different software packages (e.g., GPy) to verify the correctness of the implementation?

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization, and the potential impact is significant. Addressing the concerns raised above – particularly clarifying the related work, providing details on hyperparameter updating, and addressing the questions regarding assumptions and smoothness – will strengthen the paper considerably.
