This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, which generalizes the knowledge gradient policy to the parallel setting. The authors derive a batch-version of the knowledge gradient and propose a Monte Carlo gradient estimator to find optimal batches. The paper demonstrates that the q-KG method finds global optima significantly faster than previous batch Bayesian optimization algorithms on both synthetic test functions and when tuning hyperparameters of practical machine learning algorithms, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and the proposed method. The background on Gaussian processes and the notation used in the paper are well-explained. The authors also provide a detailed description of the q-KG algorithm and its computational implementation.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of the batch-version of the knowledge gradient and the proposal of the Monte Carlo gradient estimator. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the q-KG method through extensive numerical experiments on synthetic functions and real-world problems. The results show that the q-KG method outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art benchmark algorithms, especially in the noisy setting.

However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the paper. The batch knowledge gradient policy is not new, and the authors should clarify the contribution of their work relative to prior batch KG work. Additionally, the comparison to Expected Improvement (EI) is problematic because EI assumes noiseless observations, and the authors should clarify whether a noise-aware EI variant was used and discuss how noise affects the results.

The paper's organization is clear, and the authors provide a detailed description of the related work, the proposed method, and the numerical experiments. However, some sections, such as Section 5.2, need more detail and elaboration.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

The paper's clarity is good, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

The paper's originality is good, and the authors propose a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm. However, the novelty of the paper is somewhat diminished by the fact that the batch knowledge gradient policy is not new.

The paper's significance is high, and the results have important implications for Bayesian optimization and machine learning. The authors demonstrate that the q-KG method can be used to tune hyperparameters of practical machine learning algorithms, and the results show that the method is effective in both noise-free and noisy settings.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that generalizes the knowledge gradient policy to the parallel setting.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the q-KG method through extensive numerical experiments on synthetic functions and real-world problems.
* The results show that the q-KG method outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art benchmark algorithms, especially in the noisy setting.
