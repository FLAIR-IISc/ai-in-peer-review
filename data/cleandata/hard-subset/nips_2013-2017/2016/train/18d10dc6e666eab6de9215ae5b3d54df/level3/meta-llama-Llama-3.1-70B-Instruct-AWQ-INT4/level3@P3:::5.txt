The paper "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization" presents a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method. This method is derived using a decision-theoretic analysis that chooses the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior when there is only one batch of points remaining.

The authors' contributions are threefold. First, they propose a novel batch BO method that measures the information gain of evaluating q points via a new acquisition function, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). Second, they develop a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to evaluate q-KG's gradient efficiently, allowing its efficient optimization. Third, they demonstrate that q-KG consistently finds better function values than other parallel BO algorithms, such as parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and its advantages. The experiments are thorough, and the results are impressive, showing that q-KG outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art benchmark algorithms on several synthetic functions and in tuning practical machine learning algorithms.

However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the batch KG method. The reviewer questions whether the batch KG is truly new, as it seems to be an extension of previous work on batch KG. The authors should clarify their contribution relative to prior batch KG work and provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods.

Additionally, the reviewer suggests that the main contribution of the paper might be the gradient estimator, and the authors should empirically or theoretically compare its efficiency/effectiveness against previous batch KG methods. The authors should also provide more detail and elaboration on Section 5.2, which is central to the paper.

Another issue is the comparison to Expected Improvement (EI), which assumes noiseless observations. The authors should clarify whether a noise-aware EI variant was used and discuss how noise affects the results in Section 6.2.

Minor issues include the redefinition of the abbreviation "IPA" at line 162, which should be fixed.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and adequately informs the reader. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce their results.

The originality of the paper is good, as it proposes a novel batch BO method and develops a new gradient estimator. The authors clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions and provide adequate references to related work.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The authors demonstrate that their method advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides unique conclusions on existing data.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel batch BO method that outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art benchmark algorithms.
* The authors develop a new gradient estimator that allows for efficient optimization of the q-KG method.
* The experiments are thorough, and the results are impressive, showing the advantages of the q-KG method.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The novelty of the batch KG method is questionable, and the authors should provide more clarification on their contribution relative to prior batch KG work.
* The comparison to Expected Improvement (EI) assumes noiseless observations, and the authors should clarify whether a noise-aware EI variant was used.
* Minor issues, such as the redefinition of the abbreviation "IPA", should be fixed.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and its advantages. While there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the batch KG method and the comparison to Expected Improvement, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results.
