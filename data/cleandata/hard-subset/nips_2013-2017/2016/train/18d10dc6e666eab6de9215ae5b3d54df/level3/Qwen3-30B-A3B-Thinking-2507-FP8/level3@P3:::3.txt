

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) that extends the knowledge gradient framework to the parallel evaluation setting. The authors develop a decision-theoretic acquisition function that selects multiple points to evaluate simultaneously (a "batch"), rather than sequentially, which is particularly useful when function evaluations can be parallelized. The key innovation is the derivation of q-KG, which is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor when only one batch remains. To address the computational intractability of maximizing q-KG, the authors propose using Monte Carlo sampling combined with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate gradients for optimization.

The paper relates to previous work on Bayesian optimization (BO) by building upon the knowledge gradient (KG) method [7, 17], which is itself an extension of expected improvement (EI) for sequential settings. The authors correctly position their work as extending the KG framework to batch settings, distinguishing it from parallel EI methods [2, 3, 5, 18, 25] that construct batches greedily by sequentially adding points.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation. The authors provide a rigorous derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and a well-motivated computational approach using IPA. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over benchmarks, particularly on noisy problems. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity of their approach and how it scales with batch size q. The specific issue with equation (3.1) is a notable clarity concern: the notation K(x, x^(1:n)) suggests x^(1:n) is a vector, but the paper defines it as a sequence of points, creating confusion about whether the kernel function is being evaluated at individual points or as a vector. This should be clarified in the revision.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical development to experiments. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear and concise. The experimental setup is well-explained, and the results are presented effectively with appropriate visualizations. The authors make good use of the supplementary materials to provide additional technical details.

**Originality:** The main contribution is the extension of KG to batch settings (q-KG) and the computational approach using IPA. While the core idea of batch Bayesian optimization is not entirely novel, the authors provide a theoretically grounded acquisition function that considers the batch collectively rather than sequentially. The novelty is somewhat limited, as the IPA-based gradient estimator is a standard technique in optimization. The paper could better highlight how their approach differs from the recent "nested batch knowledge gradient" work [26], which also extends KG to batch settings but for a discrete feasible set.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important practical problem: parallelizing Bayesian optimization when multiple evaluations can be performed simultaneously. This is highly relevant for machine learning applications where hyperparameter tuning can be parallelized across multiple machines. The empirical results demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms or competes with state-of-the-art methods, especially in noisy settings. The practical utility of the method is well-supported by experiments on both synthetic functions and real machine learning problems (logistic regression and CNN tuning on MNIST and CIFAR10).

**Strengths:** 
- Well-motivated problem with clear practical relevance
- Strong theoretical foundation based on decision theory
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on diverse test problems
- Clear presentation and well-organized structure
- Practical implementation details and open-source code

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited novelty in the core approach (extension of KG to batch settings with standard IPA gradient estimation)
- The equation (3.1) notation is confusing regarding the sequence vs. vector interpretation of x^(1:n)
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability with large batch sizes
- Could better distinguish from related work (e.g., [26])

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pro:**
- Solves a practical problem with clear relevance to machine learning practitioners
- Provides a theoretically grounded alternative to greedy batch selection methods
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements, especially in noisy settings
- Well-written with strong experimental validation
- Offers a practically useful implementation (open-source code)

**Con:**
- Novelty is limited to extending KG to batch settings with standard computational techniques
- The core idea does not represent a significant theoretical advance over prior work
- The computational approach (IPA) is not novel in this context
- Could benefit from more thorough comparison with the most recent batch BO methods

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a practical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the work is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear practical value. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the notation issue in equation (3.1) and providing a more detailed comparison with the most recent batch BO methods. Given the practical utility and strong empirical results, I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2016.
