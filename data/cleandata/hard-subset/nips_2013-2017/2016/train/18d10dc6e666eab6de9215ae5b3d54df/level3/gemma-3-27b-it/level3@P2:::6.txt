## Review of “The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization”

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm, the parallel Knowledge Gradient method (q-KG), designed for scenarios where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The core idea is to formulate a parallel acquisition function based on the knowledge gradient, aiming for Bayes-optimal batch selection, and to efficiently compute gradients of this acquisition function using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method is demonstrated on synthetic functions and applied to hyperparameter tuning of logistic regression and convolutional neural networks, showing competitive and, particularly in noisy settings, superior performance compared to existing parallel BO algorithms.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation in Bayesian optimization and leverages recent advances in parallelizing acquisition function maximization. The authors correctly identify the growing need for efficient batch BO methods, especially given the increasing availability of parallel computing resources. The connection to the sequential knowledge gradient and the decision-theoretic justification for the proposed method are well-articulated. The background on Gaussian processes is thorough and the technical details of the q-KG formulation and gradient computation are clearly described. The experimental setup is also reasonable, encompassing both synthetic test functions and real-world applications.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed q-KG method offers a principled approach to parallel batch BO, explicitly aiming for Bayes-optimality. While KG and IPA are not novel in isolation, their combination within this specific parallel batch framework represents a contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function based on information gain and the use of IPA for efficient gradient computation provide a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The results demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with other parallel BO methods, and shows noticeable improvements, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios. The application to CNN tuning is a strong indicator of practical relevance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The technical details are explained with sufficient clarity, making the methodology accessible to researchers familiar with Bayesian optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While comparisons are made to several parallel BO algorithms, a crucial benchmark is missing: random search. Without comparing to random search, it's difficult to assess the practical benefits of q-KG, especially considering the computational overhead involved. Furthermore, a comparison to *sequential* Bayesian Optimization would establish if the parallelization is truly beneficial.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The method's scalability to larger batch sizes (q) is not adequately addressed. The experiments consistently use q=4. The authors should discuss the computational complexity as q increases and provide insights into potential bottlenecks. The expected benefits of larger q (e.g., improved exploration, reduced overhead per evaluation) are not explicitly articulated.
*   **Lack of Wall-Clock Time Measurements:** The experiments report iteration counts but lack wall-clock time measurements. This information is crucial for assessing the practical efficiency of q-KG, particularly in comparison to simpler methods like random search.  The overhead of computing each batch needs to be quantified.
*   **Relationship to Prior Work:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, specifically Wang et al. (2015) and their use of IPA-based gradient maximization for parallel Expected Improvement (q-EI). A clear delineation of the differences between q-KG and q-EI is needed. The claim that IPA is introduced by this work is incorrect and should be corrected.
*   **Experimental Details:** The meaning of “iterations” on the x-axis of the performance plots is unclear. Is this the number of batches, the total number of function evaluations, or something else? The different starting values across methods need justification.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian optimization.
*   The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance, particularly in noisy settings.
*   The code is publicly available, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of comparison to random search and sequential BO makes it difficult to assess the practical benefits of the method.
*   The scalability to larger batch sizes is not adequately addressed.
*   The absence of wall-clock time measurements hinders a comprehensive evaluation of the method's efficiency.
*   The relationship to existing IPA-based methods (e.g., Wang et al., 2015) needs clarification.



**Specific Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Have you compared q-KG to Wang et al. (2015) / parallel EI (q-EI), and can you clearly state the key differences in methodology and performance?
2.  How does the computational cost of q-KG scale with increasing batch size (q)? What are the expected benefits of using larger values of q?
3.  Can the synchronous evaluation scheme of q-KG be extended to an asynchronous setting, where function evaluations become available at different times?
4.  What are the wall-clock performance results of q-KG compared to random search and sequential BO? What is the overhead associated with computing each new batch?
5.  Please clarify the x-axis of the plots (e.g., batches, function evaluations), explain why starting values differ across methods, and indicate whether the initial design points are included in the reported iterations.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Equation (4.1) appears to be missing a z^(1:n) term in the minimization over x ∈ A.
*   "achieve" should be "achieves" in the concluding paragraph.
*   The phrasing of the citation of Wang et al. (2015) needs refinement.
*   The statement crediting the introduction of IPA is inaccurate.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new method for parallel Bayesian optimization. However, the lack of comprehensive comparative analysis and scalability considerations limit its impact. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly the inclusion of random search comparisons and wall-clock time measurements, would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential contribution to the field. Currently, I lean towards **weak reject**, but would be open to reconsidering with a major revision addressing the above points.
