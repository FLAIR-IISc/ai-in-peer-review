The paper "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization" proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, which computes Bayes-optimal batches and reportedly outperforms existing methods. The authors provide a clear and well-written description of their approach, making it easy to follow and understand the underlying concepts.

The q-KG method is derived using a decision-theoretic analysis, which chooses the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior when there is only one batch of points remaining. The authors demonstrate that the q-KG method finds global optima significantly faster than previous batch Bayesian optimization algorithms on both synthetic test functions and when tuning hyperparameters of practical machine learning algorithms, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a thorough review of related work in Bayesian optimization, including the limitations of existing methods and the contributions of their approach. The theoretical analysis is strong, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the q-KG method and its underlying assumptions.

However, the experimental evaluation is limited, and the authors could have provided more comprehensive benchmarks to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The use of test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic, as threshold-based metrics are suboptimal, and the authors could have considered using more robust metrics such as AUC or log loss. Additionally, the experimental benchmarks are limited to MNIST and CIFAR10, and the authors could have included broader HPO benchmarks such as HPOlib.

Furthermore, the authors only used two datasets and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN), and it would have been beneficial to include more datasets and algorithm variety, as optimizers can vary by task. Despite these limitations, the paper presents an elegant solution to parallelizing Bayesian optimization, and the theoretical contribution is valuable.

The authors are careful about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, and they provide a clear discussion of the limitations of their method.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization, and the q-KG method is a significant contribution to the field.
