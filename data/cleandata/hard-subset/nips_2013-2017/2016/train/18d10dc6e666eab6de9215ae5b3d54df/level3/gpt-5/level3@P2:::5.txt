Summary
This paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) extension of the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization, targeting settings where q function evaluations can be performed in parallel. The key decision-theoretic idea mirrors sequential KG: choose the batch that maximizes the expected decrement in the posterior mean’s minimum after observing the batch outcomes, without restricting the final recommendation to lie among sampled points. Because this value must be computed under the posterior over future observations, the authors use Monte Carlo with a pathwise (IPA) gradient estimator to optimize q-KG jointly over the q points. For continuous domains, they approximate the inner minimizations using a dynamically updated discrete set based on samples from the GP posterior. Experiments on synthetic test functions and hyperparameter tuning (logistic regression on MNIST; CNN on CIFAR-10) suggest that q-KG often outperforms parallel EI variants and UCB-based batch methods, with particularly pronounced gains under observation noise.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on the KG literature for correlated normal beliefs and GP surrogates in the sequential setting, and on batch/parallel BO methods such as parallel EI (including closed-form and gradient-based optimizers), GP-BUCB / GP-UCB-PE, and batch entropy search (PPES). The paper also discusses a nested batch KG approach in discrete spaces. However, the relationship to existing batch KG variants (e.g., nested-batch KG and earlier batch KG formulations in discrete settings) should be clarified and strengthened, especially in terms of computational complexity and statistical properties. The gradient-based approach is close in spirit to recent developments for differentiable q-EI; an explicit runtime/variance comparison would help position the contribution.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The decision-theoretic formulation of q-KG is natural and correct; the reduction to q-EI in the noiseless/restricted-recommendation regime is a useful sanity check. The IPA-based gradient derivation is central but currently underdeveloped in the manuscript. In particular:
  - The interchange of gradient and expectation (and the use of pathwise derivatives through a min over a discretization) requires clear regularity conditions (e.g., almost-sure unique minimizer; handling of non-differentiability via Clarke subgradients or tie-breaking; dominated convergence conditions).
  - Unbiasedness and variance properties of the gradient estimator should be stated (with proofs or precise references), including how they depend on q, M (discretization size), dimension, and the number of MC samples.
  - The dependence of computational cost on q and on the GP hyperparameter re-fit per iteration should be quantified.
- Experiments: The empirical section is solid in breadth, shows consistent improvements, and highlights noise robustness. However:
  - The EI baseline in noisy settings needs clarification. Vanilla EI is for noise-free evaluations; if a noise-aware variant was used (e.g., fantasization, marginal EI, or other standard noisy-EI approaches), specify exactly which, and ensure a fair comparison in Section 6.2.
  - Report wall-clock time and MC sample sizes to substantiate efficiency claims relative to q-EI optimizers and PPES, especially as q grows.
  - Provide ablations on discretization size M and the posterior sample generator for the candidate set; discuss the bias-variance tradeoff from discretization and its impact on quality.
- Reproducibility: Code availability is good. Please document optimizer settings (number of restarts, step sizes, stopping criteria), the number of MC samples for objective and gradient estimates, and GP hyperparameter estimation details (priors, restarts, treatment of noise variance).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and the problem is crisply stated. That said, Section 5.2 needs significantly more detail, as it is the methodological core in practice. Specific suggestions:
- Present the precise conditions under which equation (5.3) holds and discuss non-unique minimizers.
- Give pseudocode for stochastic gradient ascent, including MC sample allocation and variance reduction (e.g., common random numbers, antithetic sampling).
- Clarify the construction of An: “drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior” is ambiguous. If this is Thompson sampling followed by minimization, say so and cite appropriately. Explain how z(1:q) is “folded into” An during optimization without biasing the estimate.
- Clarify the computational complexity per iteration (asymptotics and practical constants) and how it scales with q, n, d, and M.
- Minor: “IPA” is redefined at line 162—please remove the redefinition and ensure consistent terminology.

Originality
The decision-theoretic extension to batch acquisitions is well motivated, and positioning KG as a noise-robust batch criterion is appealing. However, batch KG ideas have appeared previously in discrete/nested-batch contexts; the novelty here lies mainly in:
- A continuous-domain, joint optimization framework for q-KG, and
- An efficient IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator to optimize the batch jointly.
To solidify the originality claim, please:
- Contrast theoretically and empirically with earlier batch KG variants (discrete nested-batch KG, other KG-based batch heuristics), highlighting efficiency and performance differences.
- Compare gradient estimator efficiency (variance, convergence speed) and runtime with established q-EI optimizers and PPES.

Significance
Parallel BO is practically important. A noise-robust batch acquisition that is demonstrably competitive or superior to q-EI and UCB-style methods is valuable. The experimental results suggest meaningful gains, especially with noise. Clearer efficiency analyses and stronger baselines in the noisy case would increase the impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear decision-theoretic formulation of batch KG.
- Practical, differentiable objective enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical results across synthetic and real tasks, particularly in noisy regimes.
- Public code.

Cons for rejection
- Central gradient estimator (Section 5.2) lacks rigorous detail; conditions and proofs are not sufficiently spelled out.
- Insufficiently clear comparison to prior batch KG work; novelty positioning needs tightening.
- Noisy baselines for EI may be mismatched; fairness of Section 6.2 comparisons is unclear.
- Limited analysis of computational efficiency and scaling; no wall-clock results.

Questions for the authors
1. Precisely which EI variants are used in noisy experiments? If not noise-aware EI/q-EI, can you add such baselines or justify the choice?
2. What are the exact regularity conditions for (5.3)? How do you handle ties/non-differentiability in min operations?
3. How many MC samples are used for objective and gradient estimation per iteration? Any variance reduction?
4. What is the per-iteration runtime as a function of q and M, and how does it compare to q-EI gradient methods and PPES?
5. How is An constructed concretely (Thompson sampling? local minima? optimization restarts)? Sensitivity to M?
6. Do the results change if GP hyperparameters (including noise variance) are marginalized rather than re-fit by MLE?
7. Can you provide an ablation demonstrating the claimed reduction to q-EI in the noiseless/restricted-recommendation setting?

Minor comments and typos
- Fix the “IPA” redefinition and ensure consistent abbreviations.
- Clarify the statement “draw M samples from the global optima of the posterior distribution of the GP.”
- Check notation: distinguish clearly the two uses of A in (4.1)/(4.2).
- Provide details on initialization (Latin hypercube parameters, bounds) and the number of BO iterations.

Overall assessment
A promising and practically relevant contribution that likely improves the state of the art for batch BO under noise. With stronger theoretical exposition of the gradient estimator, clearer positioning w.r.t. prior batch KG work, and fairer/more transparent baselines in the noisy regime, this paper would be a strong accept. In its current form, I lean positive but request substantial clarifications and minor additions.
