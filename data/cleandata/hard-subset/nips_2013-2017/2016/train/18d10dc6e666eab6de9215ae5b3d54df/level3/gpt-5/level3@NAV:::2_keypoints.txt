- Introduces parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm selecting Bayes-optimal batches via one-step lookahead.  
- Extends sequential KG to the parallel setting and develops an efficient gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA).  
- Positions work relative to prior batch BO methods (q-EI, GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE, PPES) and shows reduction to q-EI in noiseless/restricted cases.  
- Theoretical contribution: unifies decision-theoretic Bayes-optimality with practical, differentiable computation for batch selection.  
- Technical development is sound; Bayes-optimality argument, IPA-based gradient estimator, and discretization strategy are clear.  
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient and risks optimistic bias by optimizing test-set error directly instead of using a validation protocol.  
- Metric choice is weak: using 0–1 loss is insensitive; log loss, AUC, or multiclass AUC would be more informative.  
- Benchmark scope is too narrow (only MNIST/CIFAR10 and two learners: logistic regression and one CNN); needs standard HPO suites (e.g., HPOlib) and diverse models (SVMs, tree ensembles, GBMs, kernels).  
- Noise modeling assumption of independent Gaussian observations is unrealistic for HPO; CV induces correlated/heteroscedastic errors and SGD/minibatching introduces bias.  
- Clarity: paper is well organized with clean notation and algorithmic details.  
- Originality: novel and elegant extension of KG to compute Bayes-optimal batches with a scalable gradient estimator.  
- Significance: potentially high conceptual importance, but empirical limitations reduce practical persuasiveness.  
- Pros: strong theory, principled Bayes-optimal batch selection, efficient gradient-based optimization, and clear writing.  
- Cons: problematic experimental protocol, limited datasets/metrics/model diversity, and misaligned noise assumptions.  
- Overall: positive assessment of theory and approach, but demands substantial expansion and corrected evaluation for wide adoption.
