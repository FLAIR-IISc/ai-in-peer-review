Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization method based on the knowledge gradient (KG), termed q-KG. The acquisition is defined as the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after collecting a batch of q evaluations, which is Bayes-optimal in the one-batch-to-go sense. The paper shows that in the noise-free case with the final recommendation restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to q-EI (parallel expected improvement). Because evaluating and especially optimizing q-KG is computationally demanding, the authors develop a Monte Carlo estimator and an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based optimization over the batch z(1:q). The acquisition is approximated by discretizing the domain A using samples drawn from the posterior’s implied distribution over minima, augmented with previously sampled points and current candidates. Experiments on synthetic functions and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR10) indicate that q-KG is competitive in the noiseless setting and often superior in the noisy setting, compared to q-EI (MOE and Spearmint heuristic), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE.

The work builds on classical KG for sequential settings (Frazier et al., Scott et al.) and relates to prior batch BO acquisitions including q-EI (e.g., MOE; Wang et al. 2015) and batch versions of UCB and (predictive) entropy search (e.g., Contal et al., Hernández-Lobato et al., Shah & Ghahramani). The key algorithmic novelty is extending KG to truly joint batch selection and deriving a practical IPA-based stochastic gradient for this objective in continuous domains.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic formulation: q-KG is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch and does not restrict the final recommendation to sampled points.
- Sound stochastic gradient estimator via IPA with a careful decomposition that is implementable and reuses standard GP machinery (Cholesky, conditional sampling).
- Empirically competitive to strong baselines; gains are pronounced under observation noise, consistent with KG’s treatment of noisy evaluations.
- Good exposition of background and algorithmic details; practical engineering choices (hyperparameter refits, discretization strategy) are described.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical efficiency and scalability are under-characterized. There are no wall-clock measurements or overhead analyses vs. (i) random search, (ii) sequential BO, or (iii) q-EI, making it difficult to judge time-to-good-solution.
- Batch size is fixed at q = 4. There is no study of scaling to larger q (e.g., 8–64), even though the method targets parallel environments. The computational cost grows with q (e.g., O(q^3) factorizations per evaluation and higher-dimensional optimization), and the benefit with larger parallelism is unclear from the paper.
- Novelty is incremental: KG and IPA are established; gradient-based joint batch selection for q-EI already exists (e.g., Wang et al. 2015; Marmin et al. 2015). The paper’s main contribution is the specific q-KG objective and its optimization; a more explicit, head-to-head comparison and discussion would strengthen originality and significance.
- Some presentation details remain unclear (e.g., what “iterations” denote in plots, fairness/consistency of initial designs across methods).

Quality
- The derivations are reasonable and consistent with GP regression and Monte Carlo estimation. The IPA-based gradient estimator is plausible under the stated differentiability conditions, though formal assumptions (e.g., uniqueness of argmins almost surely) should be spelled out and backed by citations or lemmas in the supplement.
- The discretization of A is a standard and practical approximation, but no sensitivity analysis (M, sampling strategy) is reported. Likewise, there are no regret bounds or convergence guarantees, which is acceptable but should be acknowledged explicitly.
- Experimental comparisons are fairly broad, but omitted baselines (random search, sequential BO) and missing wall-clock metrics limit the practical conclusions.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The algorithm description is clear, and the relationship to EI in the noise-free case is helpful.
- Please clarify figures: what does the x-axis “iterations” represent (batches or total function evaluations)? Does it include the initial design? Are the initial design points identical across methods within each replicate? The differing starting values suggest otherwise; for fairness, they should be shared per run.
- Minor notation: in Eq. (4.1), please ensure conditioning and the role of z(1:q) in the expectation are unambiguous and consistent with Section 5 (e.g., clearly distinguish A in the acquisition vs. the domain for optimizing z).

Originality
- Extending KG to joint batch selection with a practical stochastic gradient is a reasonable, if incremental, contribution. Prior work has applied IPA to q-EI and optimized batch acquisitions jointly. The distinctiveness here is the acquisition itself (q-KG) and its particular gradient form.

Significance
- Batch HPO is highly relevant in practice. The empirical gains under noisy evaluations are promising. Demonstrating consistent wall-clock advantages and scaling to larger q would substantially increase impact.

Questions for the authors
1) Comparison to Wang et al. (2015) q-EI: You compare against MOE’s q-EI and the Spearmint heuristic, but please clearly state how q-KG differs conceptually and computationally from q-EI with IPA-based gradients (and cite Marmin et al. 2015). Can you provide ablations where both methods use identical discretization/optimizers to isolate the acquisition’s effect?
2) Scaling with q: What are the computational and statistical scaling properties for q ∈ {1, 4, 8, 16, 32}? Please report per-batch overhead, total wall-clock to reach a target regret, and any degradation/improvement in sample efficiency.
3) Asynchrony: Can q-KG be extended to asynchronous settings with stragglers or heterogeneous runtimes? For example, via fantasizing pending evaluations or receding-horizon updates akin to “constant liar” or asynchronous q-EI. Any obstacles?
4) Wall-clock performance: Please report end-to-end time vs. random search and sequential BO on the ML tuning tasks, including GP fitting and acquisition optimization overheads. What fraction of total time is spent computing the batch vs. training models?
5) Plot semantics and fairness: Define “iterations,” include or exclude initial design consistently, and ensure identical initial designs across methods in each run.

Minor comments
- Fix grammar (e.g., “achieve” → “achieves”).
- Clarify citation attributions; Wang et al. (2015) did not introduce IPA. Cite L’Ecuyer for IPA foundations and correctly attribute q-EI gradient work (e.g., Marmin et al., Wang et al.).
- Check Eq. (4.1) notation; ensure all variables are defined (e.g., z(1:q), x(1:n)) and conditioning is explicit.
- Consider reporting sensitivity to M (discretization size) and number of Monte Carlo samples for q-KG/gradient estimation.

Pros
- Principled batch acquisition; Bayes-optimal in one-step setting.
- Strong performance under noise; relevant to real-world HPO.
- Clear algorithm and implementation details; public code.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to existing q-acquisitions with IPA-based optimization.
- No wall-clock/time-to-solution analysis; unclear scalability to larger q.
- Fixed q=4 across experiments; missing baselines (random search, sequential BO).
- Some clarity issues in figures/notation.

Overall evaluation
Borderline reject. The approach is sound and potentially useful, with encouraging results in noisy settings. However, the paper falls short on demonstrating practical efficiency and scalability, and the novelty over existing q-acquisitions optimized via IPA is limited. If the authors can add rigorous and fair wall-clock comparisons, scaling studies over q, and a sharper comparison to q-EI (including methodological distinctions and controlled ablations), I would lean toward acceptance.
