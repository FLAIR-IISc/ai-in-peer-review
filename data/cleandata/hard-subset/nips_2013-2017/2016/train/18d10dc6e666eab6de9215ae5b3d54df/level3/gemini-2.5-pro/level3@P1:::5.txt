This paper addresses the problem of batch Bayesian optimization, where multiple function evaluations can be performed in parallel. The authors propose the "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) method, an acquisition function that extends the sequential knowledge gradient criterion to the batch setting. The q-KG acquisition function is designed to be one-step Bayes-optimal, selecting the batch of points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality. Recognizing that directly optimizing q-KG is computationally challenging, the main technical contribution is a method for efficiently computing its gradient using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). This allows for the use of gradient-based optimizers to find the optimal batch. The authors present empirical results on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, demonstrating that q-KG often outperforms other batch BO methods like parallel EI and parallel UCB variants, with particularly strong performance in noisy settings.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper presents a complete piece of work with a well-motivated algorithm and extensive experiments. The claims of superior performance are, for the most part, well-supported by the results presented. However, a key aspect of the experimental quality is questionable: the comparison against Expected Improvement (EI) in the noisy setting (Section 6.2). Standard EI assumes noise-free function evaluations. If the authors compared against this standard version, the baseline is inappropriate for the noisy setting, and the observed performance gap may be misleading. Clarification on this point is necessary to fully assess the technical soundness of the experimental claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation and high-level description of the algorithm are clear. However, the clarity suffers significantly in the most critical technical section. Section 5.2, which details the proposed gradient estimator, is the core of the paper's computational contribution but is presented too concisely. The derivation of the gradient of q-KG is non-trivial, and key steps and assumptions (e.g., the validity of interchanging expectation and differentiation) are not discussed in the main text. This lack of detail makes it difficult for an expert reader to fully understand the method and reproduce the results without heavy reliance on the supplementary material. A minor clarity issue is the re-definition of the abbreviation "IPA" on line 162 after it has already been introduced in the introduction.

**Originality:** This is a major concern. The paper frames the parallel knowledge gradient as a novel contribution, but the idea of extending KG to the batch setting is not new. Prior work has explored batch KG policies, for example for discrete optimization problems (as noted in the paper's citation [26]) and in other contexts (e.g., Scott et al., SIAM J. Optim., 2011; Frazier, INFORMS TutORials, 2012). The paper fails to adequately position itself against this body of work. A thorough discussion is needed to clarify what is fundamentally new about this formulation of q-KG compared to previous batch KG proposals. If the primary novelty lies not in the acquisition function itself but in the specific gradient-based method for optimizing it, this should be stated explicitly and made the central focus of the paper's contributions.

**Significance:** The problem of batch Bayesian optimization is highly significant and of great interest to the NIPS community, particularly for applications like large-scale hyperparameter tuning. The paper's results, showing substantial improvements over existing methods, suggest that the contribution could be very impactful. The demonstrated strength in noisy environments is particularly relevant for real-world applications where function evaluations are often inexact. However, the significance is contingent on resolving the questions of originality and the fairness of the experimental comparisons. If the proposed method is indeed a novel and more efficient way to optimize a batch KG acquisition function, and it robustly outperforms fairly-chosen baselines, then the contribution would be significant.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  The paper tackles a very important and practical problem in the machine learning community: efficient parallel black-box optimization.
2.  The proposed q-KG method is principled, derived from a sound decision-theoretic framework of one-step Bayes-optimality.
3.  The empirical results are strong and demonstrate significant performance gains over several state-of-the-art baselines on both synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks. The method appears particularly effective in noisy settings.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

1.  The paper's novelty is not clearly established. The contribution is presented as a new parallel KG method, but prior work on this topic is not adequately discussed or distinguished from, diminishing the perceived originality.
2.  The core technical contribution—the gradient estimator in Section 5.2—is not explained with sufficient detail in the main paper, which hurts the paper's clarity and reproducibility.
3.  The experimental comparison with Expected Improvement in the noisy setting is potentially flawed. Using a standard noise-free EI formulation as a baseline would be an unfair comparison and could exaggerate the performance of q-KG.

### Detailed Comments and Questions:

*   Could you please elaborate on the novelty of your q-KG formulation and optimization approach in relation to prior work on batch or parallel Knowledge Gradient methods? A more detailed comparison in the related work section would greatly strengthen the paper.
*   If the main contribution is the IPA-based gradient estimator, could you provide an empirical or theoretical comparison of its efficiency/effectiveness against optimization strategies used in previous batch KG literature? This would help establish the significance of the computational method itself.
*   Section 5.2 is crucial but very brief. Could you expand this section in the main text to include more details on the derivation of the gradient? Specifically, please discuss the conditions that permit the interchange of the gradient and expectation operators and provide more intuition on the calculation of the gradient of the Cholesky factor.
*   In the noisy experiments (Section 6.2), which variant of parallel Expected Improvement did you use as a baseline? Was it a version specifically designed for noisy observations (e.g., Noisy EI)? If not, the comparison may be unfair, and the results should be re-evaluated with a more appropriate baseline.
*   Minor: The abbreviation "IPA" is defined in both the introduction and in Section 5.2. Please fix this redundancy.
