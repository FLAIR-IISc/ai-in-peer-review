Summary: The paper proposes a batch Bayesian optimization method, q-KG, that computes the Bayes-optimal parallel batch under a one-step lookahead and optimizes its acquisition via stochastic gradients estimated with infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method discretizes the candidate set for evaluating the minimum of the posterior mean and uses Monte Carlo to evaluate/optimize q-KG. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR-10) show q-KG is competitive in noiseless settings and outperforms prior methods in noisy settings. The work relates to sequential KG, multi-point EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger), gradient-based q-EI (e.g., Wang et al. 2015/MOE), and PPES.

Quality: The decision-theoretic formulation is sound, and the IPA-based gradient estimator is reasonable. However, empirical claims lack wall-clock analyses and baselines (random search, sequential BO), and the fixed q=4 leaves scalability untested. Important comparisons to q-EI (Wang et al., 2015) are missing or underdeveloped.

Clarity: The background and algorithmic description are generally clear; experiments are understandable but some figure axes/initialization choices are unclear.

Originality: The combination of KG with IPA for parallel batches is a natural extension; novelty over q-EI with IPA-like gradients is incremental. Clearer differentiation from Wang et al. (2015) and related batch methods is needed.

Significance: Parallel HPO is impactful and widely relevant. If computational overhead is modest and scaling to larger q is effective, the approach could be useful in practice, particularly under noise.

Major questions for authors:
1) Comparison to Wang et al. (2015)/q-EI: Please include direct empirical comparisons and clearly articulate methodological differences.
2) Scalability with batch size: How does performance and compute scale to q in the tens/hundreds? Any theoretical or empirical guidance?
3) Asynchrony: Can q-KG be extended to asynchronous evaluations; what changes are required?
4) Practical runtime: Provide wall-clock performance vs. random search and sequential BO, including per-batch optimization overhead.
5) Plot axes: Clarify whether “iterations” count batches or function evaluations, whether the initial design is included, and why starting values differ across methods.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear decision-theoretic grounding; strong performance under noise.
- Timely topic with practical impact for multi-core/GPU settings.
- Generally well-written and reproducible.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing key baselines and runtime evidence; unclear scalability.
- Limited novelty relative to existing IPA-based batch EI.

Minor comments:
- Eq. (4.1): make conditioning on y(z(1:q))/z(1:q) explicit; possible missing notation for the candidate set under expectation.
- Grammar: “achieve” -> “achieves.”
- Citations: avoid phrasing that credits Wang et al. (2015) with introducing IPA; cite foundational IPA work (e.g., L’Ecuyer, 1990).
