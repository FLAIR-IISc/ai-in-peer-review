

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG), which aims to select multiple points to evaluate simultaneously in parallel computing environments. The authors derive a Bayes-optimal batch acquisition function based on the knowledge gradient principle, and develop an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients for acquisition maximization. The method is theoretically grounded in decision theory and aims to be Bayes-optimal for the parallel setting.

The paper relates well to previous work on Bayesian optimization, particularly the sequential knowledge gradient method by Frazier et al. (2009), which is extended here to the batch setting. It also connects to parallel EI methods (Wang et al. 2015) and UCB variants (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), though the novelty claims require careful examination.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for the q-KG acquisition function, with well-explained background on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization. The computational approach using IPA to estimate gradients is technically sound, and the experimental section demonstrates the method's performance on both synthetic and real-world problems. The clarity of the exposition and experimental design is commendable, making the paper accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's main weakness is insufficient experimental evidence to support its claims. The authors demonstrate that q-KG outperforms other batch BO methods on noiseless and noisy problems, but fail to provide wall-clock time measurements or comparisons to random search or sequential BO to establish practical speedups. Without these, it's difficult to assess whether the method provides meaningful efficiency gains in real-world applications. Additionally, the paper does not justify why batch size q=4 is used across all experiments, which is a significant limitation given that batch size is a critical hyperparameter in parallel optimization.

**Novelty concerns:** The paper's novelty claim appears overstated. Wang et al. (2015) already proposed parallel EI with IPA-based gradient estimation, which is directly comparable to the approach presented here. The authors do not clearly articulate how their method differs from Wang et al.'s work or why their approach would be superior. The paper should explicitly address this gap and provide a clear technical distinction between their method and existing IPA-based parallel acquisition functions.

**Major questions that need addressing:**
1. The paper should clearly compare to Wang et al. (2015) and state the differences between parallel EI and q-KG, including how the acquisition functions differ and why q-KG would outperform parallel EI.
2. The scalability to larger batch sizes (q=10, 100, etc.) is not addressed. The paper should discuss how computational complexity scales with q and what benefits are expected for larger parallel runs.
3. The extension to asynchronous parallel evaluations is not considered, which would be important for practical distributed implementations.
4. Wall-clock performance versus random search or sequential BO needs to be reported, along with the computational overhead to compute each new batch.
5. The x-axis "iterations" in the plots should be clarified—whether these represent batches or function evaluations, whether they include initial design points, and why starting values differ across methods.

**Other concerns:** The paper contains several minor issues: a possible missing z^(1:n) in Equation (4.1), a grammar typo ("achieve"→"achieves"), and an incorrect citation that credits Wang et al. (2015) with introducing IPA (which was developed earlier by others).

**Overall assessment:**
- Quality: Good technical foundation, but claims lack sufficient empirical support. Missing wall-clock time comparisons and scalability analysis weaken the evaluation.
- Clarity: Excellent explanation of background, methods, and experiments.
- Originality: The core idea is not novel, as similar IPA-based parallel acquisition functions exist (Wang et al. 2015). The paper needs to better distinguish itself from existing work.
- Significance: Parallel HPO is highly relevant to many application domains, making this work potentially impactful if the claims are properly validated.

This paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian optimization with potential real-world applications. However, the lack of clear novelty differentiation from Wang et al. (2015), the absence of wall-clock time measurements, and insufficient scalability analysis undermine the paper's contribution. The authors should address these concerns before the paper can be considered for acceptance. While the theoretical foundation is solid, the experimental evidence does not fully support the claims of superior performance, particularly regarding practical speedups in real-world applications. The paper would benefit from additional experiments comparing wall-clock time, scalability to larger batch sizes, and clearer differentiation from existing parallel BO methods.
