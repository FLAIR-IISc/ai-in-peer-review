Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces the parallel (batch) Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization (BO). Like the classical (sequential) KG, q-KG is derived from a one-step Bayes-optimal decision-theoretic principle: with one batch remaining, choose z(1:q) to maximize the expected reduction in the posterior predictive minimum over A. The authors show q-KG reduces to q-EI in the noise-free setting when the recommendation is restricted to previously sampled points. Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG is challenging, they propose an efficient stochastic gradient approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), leveraging a reparameterization of the posterior mean after a batch via a standard Normal Zq and the Cholesky factor of the posterior covariance of y(z(1:q)). For continuous A they discretize the inner minimization domain (used only inside the q-KG definition) by drawing M candidate points from the posterior “global optima” sampling scheme, augmented with past and candidate points. Experiments on common synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks (MNIST logistic regression and a CIFAR-10 CNN) show that q-KG is competitive or superior to parallel EI (two implementations) and batch-UCB variants, with especially strong gains in noisy settings.

The work builds directly on KG for sequential BO and is complementary to batch acquisition functions such as q-EI (closed-form for small q; gradient-based optimization in later work), GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE, and PPES. It is closest in spirit to methods that jointly value batches, differing from greedy “constant liar” or posterior-integrated EI heuristics.

Strengths
- Principled, decision-theoretic criterion that is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch.
- Solid computational contribution: a practical stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG via IPA that scales to meaningful batch sizes and dimensions.
- Clear reduction to q-EI in a special case helps position the method within the BO landscape.
- Strong empirical performance, particularly under noise, where KG-style criteria often excel; open-source implementation builds on an established library.
- Parallel BO has high practical and societal impact by increasing throughput on modern compute (GPUs/TPUs, clusters).

Weaknesses and missing pieces
- The paper does not quantify wall-clock speedups or throughput benefits from moving sequential to batch-sequential (e.g., time-to-target comparisons vs. q and vs. available workers), which would strengthen the significance for practitioners.
- Related-work section contains imprecise or incomplete attributions:
  - Integrating EI with respect to the posterior over pending outcomes and the Constant Liar heuristic were already discussed in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization.”
  - Chevalier and Ginsbourger’s CL-mix strategy and later work on differentiating/optimizing multipoint EI (e.g., “Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design”) should be cited and contrasted, including natural-gradient approaches for q-EI maximization.
  - A comparison to PPES/PPES variants would be informative.
- Some technical assumptions and smoothness arguments are under-specified (see questions below).
- The discretization of A inside q-KG can influence results; there is limited sensitivity analysis to M, sampling strategy, and restart budgets.

Technical questions and requests for clarification
- Assumptions on A and f: To ensure minima exist in minx∈A µ(n)(x) and minx∈A µ(n+q)(x), is A assumed compact and µ (hence f under the GP prior) continuous? Please state these assumptions explicitly. If A is unbounded, what guarantees the minimizer exists for the posterior mean?
- Initial design: Why require a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS)? Other space-filling designs (e.g., Sobol’) or problem-informed initializations might work as well; is LHS essential to the analysis or simply a practical choice?
- Algorithm 1 hyperparameters: Step 5 says “re-train the hyperparameters of the GP by MLE.” Please detail:
  - Frequency (after every batch?), optimizer, priors/regularization, and treatment of noise variance.
  - Whether you ever marginalize hyperparameters (vs. point estimates). The Bayes-optimality statement technically conditions on the posterior; using MLE could introduce bias—please discuss its impact.
- Section 5.2 definition of g: You define q-KG(z(1:q),A) = EZq[g(z(1:q),A,Zq)] with g = minx µ(n)(x) − minx[µ(n)(x)+σ̃n(x, z(1:q))Zq]. Please ensure the minimization convention is consistently used (no sign flips/max vs. min confusion).
- Smoothness of g: Because x*(after) = argminx over a finite set An depends on Zq and can switch between candidates, g is piecewise smooth and non-differentiable on measure-zero boundaries. Your IPA unbiasedness claim requires conditions (e.g., unique minimizers almost surely, continuously differentiable µ and K). Please state these conditions and provide a brief argument or cite the supplementary proof (e.g., Clarke regularity, dominated convergence), including how ties are handled.
- Differentiability of the Cholesky factor: D(n)(z(1:q)) as a function of z(1:q) is differentiable if the covariance matrix remains SPD and the kernel is smooth. Please add a citation and a sentence on this (e.g., Smith, 1995, “Differentiation of the Cholesky algorithm”; modern AD treatments show Cholesky is smooth almost everywhere on SPD matrices). Clarify how you handle potential numerical rank issues.
- Complexity and scalability: Provide the per-iteration computational complexity in terms of q, d, M, number of MC samples for Zq, and gradient steps/restarts. Empirical runtime vs. q (e.g., 2, 4, 8, 16) would be valuable.
- Sensitivity to discretization: Report sensitivity of performance to M and to the sampling strategy for AMn (number of samples from posterior “global optima” and whether these are re-used between iterations).
- Noisy setting: Since q-KG is evaluated using Monte Carlo over Zq but also re-estimates noise via MLE, how robust is the method to mis-specified noise models? Consider reporting performance under heteroscedastic noise.
- Experimental verification: Could the same GPs used in Figure 1 (and other figures) be fit with an independent library (e.g., GPML/GPy/GPyTorch) to cross-check predictive means/variances and reproducibility?

Minor issues and typos
- Matérn parameter should be 5/2 (not “2/5”).
- Algorithm 1: “Main Stange” → “Main Stage.”
- Please define all symbols at first use in Sec. 5 (e.g., Zq, D(n)(·), σ̃n) and keep notation consistent.

Quality
- Technical derivation appears sound, with a clear reduction to known special cases. The IPA-based estimator is a solid contribution; more explicit assumptions and a brief complexity analysis would improve rigor. Experiments are reasonably thorough, with strong results in noisy settings.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. Strengthen the assumptions, smoothness/differentiability discussion, and related-work precision. Add details on hyperparameter learning and computational costs.

Originality
- Extends KG to the batch setting with an efficient gradient estimator; to my knowledge this is novel relative to prior q-EI/PPES/BUCB lines.

Significance
- High practical impact: principled batch selection that outperforms strong baselines, particularly under noise. Parallelizing BO is societally impactful due to modern parallel compute.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, Bayes-optimal criterion for batches
- Efficient and implementable optimization via IPA
- Strong empirical gains, especially with noise
- Public code and clear practical relevance

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete/imprecise related work and missing comparisons (e.g., CL-mix, PPES, differentiating q-EI)
- Limited analysis of wall-clock speedups and computational scaling
- Some theoretical assumptions and smoothness details not fully spelled out

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper makes a meaningful, well-motivated contribution that advances the state of the art in batch BO, with solid empirical evidence and practical importance. Addressing the related-work gaps, clarifying assumptions/differentiability, and adding runtime/speedup analyses would further strengthen the final version.
