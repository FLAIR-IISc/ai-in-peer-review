Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that selects q points jointly by maximizing the expected one-step reduction in the posterior predictive minimum. q-KG is a principled, decision-theoretic generalization of the knowledge gradient (KG) to the parallel setting: it is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch, without restricting the final recommendation to be among sampled points. The authors show that in the noise-free case, when restricting the final recommendation to previously evaluated points, q-KG reduces to q-EI. Because the exact evaluation of q-KG is generally intractable, the paper develops an efficient Monte Carlo estimator and an unbiased stochastic gradient via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based batch selection. A practical discretization of the domain used within the inner min (separate from the continuous domain over which z(1:q) is chosen) completes the algorithm.

The work is positioned relative to sequential acquisition rules (PI, EI, UCB, ES, KG) and prior batch methods: greedy/heuristic q-EI (e.g., Constant Liar), analytical/gradient-based q-EI optimizers, GP-UCB variants (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), and parallel ES. The main conceptual advance over these is a collective, Bayes-optimal one-step batch rule with an efficient gradient estimator. Empirically, q-KG outperforms or matches strong baselines on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, with especially large gains under noise.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation of q-KG from the one-step Bayes-optimal principle is correct. The IPA-based gradient estimator is well-motivated and appears unbiased under stated differentiability assumptions. The discretization strategy and optimization loop are reasonable in practice.
- Empirical support: Experiments cover standard noiseless benchmarks, noisy variants, and two ML hyperparameter-tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10). Results consistently favor q-KG, particularly with noise, aligning with the theory that KG-like policies value information under observation noise.
- Missing theory: Beyond one-step optimality, there are no finite-sample or asymptotic performance guarantees (e.g., error/regret bounds, consistency) for the iterative policy, nor a quantitative complexity analysis. These gaps do not negate the contribution, but they limit theoretical assurance.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a careful exposition of the GP background and algorithm. However, a few notational/grammatical issues (listed below) create avoidable friction. It would also help to more explicitly distinguish the two roles of A throughout.

Originality
Extending KG to the batch setting with an efficient unbiased stochastic gradient (via IPA) is novel and important. While batch selection “as a set” has been explored for q-EI and q-ES, a Bayes-optimal one-step batch KG with practical optimization is a valuable addition. The connection showing reduction to q-EI in a special case is insightful.

Significance
Parallel evaluation is central to modern ML practice. A strong, noise-robust batch BO rule that can be implemented efficiently is likely to be used and extended. The empirical gains under noise are particularly compelling.

Main concerns and suggestions
- Modeling assumptions in high dimensions: The GP prior with standard ARD Matérn kernels may be ill-suited for very high-dimensional, structured inputs (e.g., neural architecture or large hyperparameter spaces). Please discuss:
  - Practical limits on dimensionality for which q-KG remains effective.
  - Alternatives such as domain-specific kernels, embeddings, or low-dimensional structure assumptions.
  - The impact of discretization size M and batch size q on performance and runtime as dimension grows.
- Theoretical results requested:
  - Provide error bounds for the iterative q-KG policy (e.g., convergence of the recommended solution to the true minimizer under standard GP assumptions), or at least outline conditions under which such bounds might hold.
  - A quantitative complexity analysis that makes explicit the dependence on q, |A_n| (the discretization size), the Monte Carlo sample count, and input dimension d. For example, break down per-iteration costs due to Cholesky factorizations, gradient evaluation, and optimizer steps, and discuss how these scale with q and d.
- Comparison to additional batch BO literature:
  - Briefly contrast with local penalization for batch BO (González et al., 2016) and clarify trade-offs versus q-KG (principled one-step optimality vs. heuristic scalability).
  - Elaborate on how q-KG’s Monte Carlo/gradient-based optimization compares in practice to analytic/closed-form q-EI approximations (e.g., Chevalier & Ginsbourger) in terms of both wall-clock time and solution quality.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the discretization scheme and M? Please provide an ablation varying M and reporting both regret and runtime.
- How does the algorithm scale with q (e.g., q=8,16)? Include timing breakdowns and solution quality.
- For noisy settings, how robust is q-KG to mis-specified noise variance? Any evidence of over-exploitation or instability when noise estimates are biased?
- Do you warm-start the batch optimizer over z(1:q)? Any observations on local minima and multi-start strategies?

Minor issues and clarifications
- l.36: Grammar: “the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal…” → rephrase to avoid the dangling “that is”.
- l.95: Define A upon first use (domain/feasible set); currently implicit.
- l.104: Clarify the argument of μ(n)(·) and the notation for posterior quantities (inputs vs. indices).
- l.128: Briefly discuss parallel EI variants (e.g., Constant Liar, fantasizing) before introducing q-KG, and position your method relative to both heuristic and exact/gradient-based q-EI.
- l.140: Avoid reusing A for both the continuous feasible set and the discretized set within the inner min; adopt distinct symbols (e.g., A for domain, Ãn or S for discretization).
- Typo: “Main Stange” → “Main Stage”.
- Provide the computational budget (MC samples, gradient iterations) used per batch in experiments.

Pros
- Principled, Bayes-optimal one-step batch acquisition.
- Efficient unbiased gradient estimator enabling practical optimization.
- Strong empirical results, especially under noise.
- Clear connection to q-EI in the noiseless restricted-recommendation case.
- Well-written and reproducible with released code.

Cons
- No error/regret bounds for the iterative policy; limited theory beyond one-step optimality.
- Scalability and complexity vs. dimension and q not quantified.
- GP prior appropriateness in high-dimensional/structured spaces not addressed.
- Some notation and minor editorial issues.

Overall assessment
A solid and timely contribution to batch BO. The method is sound, the computational ideas are well-executed, and the experiments are convincing. Addressing the requested theoretical clarifications and providing a quantitative complexity analysis would strengthen the paper further. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions.
