This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) algorithm. The core idea is to construct batches of points to evaluate simultaneously by maximizing an acquisition function derived from a decision-theoretic analysis, aiming for the Bayes-optimal batch in a single iteration. The authors address the computational complexity of this optimization through an efficient gradient estimation technique based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The paper is well-structured and clearly written, presenting a mathematically sound approach to parallel BO, building on prior work in sequential BO like the Knowledge Gradient method [17] and extending it to the parallel setting. It appropriately references related work in parallel BO, such as parallel EI [2, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and parallel UCB with exploration [3].

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The method’s derivation from a decision-theoretic perspective is a significant strength. Establishing a Bayes-optimal approach, even for a single remaining batch, provides a solid foundation.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Addressing the computational challenge of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function with IPA is crucial and demonstrates practical consideration.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the methodology is explained in a logical manner.
*   **Novelty:** The approach of jointly optimizing over a batch of points, rather than iteratively adding them as in previous methods [2, 3, 5], represents a genuinely novel contribution.
*   **Potential Significance:** If the empirical results were more robust, the algorithm could significantly improve the efficiency of parallel BO, particularly in scenarios where function evaluations are costly, such as hyperparameter tuning of complex machine learning models.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limiting Assumption of Noise Distribution:** The assumption of independent, normally distributed noise is a significant limitation. In practical hyperparameter optimization (HPO), evaluation metrics often exhibit structural bias and correlated errors from techniques like cross-validation. This assumption may not hold, potentially impacting the algorithm’s performance in real-world scenarios.
*   **Weak Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is the paper's most substantial weakness. Using test set error as the optimization criterion is not ideal. Metrics like Area Under the Curve (AUC) or log loss are generally preferred for HPO as they provide a more robust evaluation of model ranking and performance.
*   **Limited Benchmark Scope:** The benchmarks are limited to MNIST and CIFAR10 with only logistic regression and a CNN. This narrow scope hinders the generalizability of the conclusions. Expanding to broader HPO benchmarks like HPOlib [https://hpolib.readthedocs.io/en/latest/](https://hpolib.readthedocs.io/en/latest/) would be a valuable addition.
*   **Lack of Dataset & Algorithm Variety:** The small number of datasets and algorithms used makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's robustness and applicability across different problem domains. Hyperparameter optimizers often exhibit varying performance depending on the specific task.
*   **Missing Comparison to Recent Methods:** While the paper references relevant prior work, it doesn't compare to some very recent advancements in parallel BO.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution is valuable and offers a novel perspective on parallel BO.
*   The IPA technique for efficient gradient computation is well-developed and practical.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the methodology.
*   The initial experimental results, while limited, suggest promising performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumption of independent Gaussian noise restricts the applicability of the algorithm.
*   The limited empirical evaluation fails to convincingly demonstrate superior performance across a diverse set of problems.
*   The choice of the test set error as an optimization metric is suboptimal.
*   The lack of comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods weakens the claim of improvement.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. However, the limited and somewhat flawed empirical evaluation raises concerns about the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.  While the theoretical innovation is noteworthy, the paper requires more comprehensive experimental validation before it can be confidently accepted. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly strengthening the empirical evaluation with more diverse benchmarks, appropriate evaluation metrics, and comparisons to recent state-of-the-art methods. Investigating the sensitivity of the algorithm to deviations from the Gaussian noise assumption would also be beneficial.
