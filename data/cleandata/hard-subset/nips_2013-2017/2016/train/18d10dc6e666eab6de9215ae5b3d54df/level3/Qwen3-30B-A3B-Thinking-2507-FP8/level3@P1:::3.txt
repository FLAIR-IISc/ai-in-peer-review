

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework to the parallel setting where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors propose a novel acquisition function that measures the expected information gain from evaluating a batch of q points, derived from decision-theoretic principles that select the Bayes-optimal batch when only one iteration remains. The key innovation lies in efficiently computing this acquisition function through Monte Carlo sampling combined with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients, addressing the computational intractability of direct optimization.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance across both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly excelling in noisy evaluation scenarios where it significantly outperforms existing batch methods like parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration. The implementation is well-documented with code availability, and the experimental design thoroughly covers both noise-free and noisy settings across multiple dimensions.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong connection to related work in Bayesian optimization (BO) and parallel BO methods. The authors appropriately acknowledge prior work, including the sequential KG method [7, 17], parallel EI approaches [2, 19, 25], and UCB extensions [3, 5]. The comparison to these methods is thorough and well-motivated, demonstrating q-KG's advantages in both synthetic and real-world applications.

Regarding quality, the theoretical foundation is sound, with clear derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and proper justification for the IPA-based gradient estimation approach. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple well-known test functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), with appropriate statistical reporting (mean and standard deviation across 20-100 random initializations). The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly regarding computational complexity for large q, and provides a reasonable discretization strategy to address this.

The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with well-chosen examples and figures that effectively illustrate the method's advantages. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and implementable. However, there is a minor concern with Equation (3.1), where the notation for the posterior mean and kernel functions appears inconsistent with the use of sequences x^(1:n) rather than vectors, potentially causing confusion for readers. This could be clarified with a brief notation explanation.

For originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by extending the KG framework to batch selection, which is a non-trivial extension beyond the existing parallel BO literature. While the computational approach (Monte Carlo + IPA) is standard, its application to this specific acquisition function is novel and well-executed. The authors properly position their work against related contributions, particularly distinguishing from [26] which focuses on discrete feasible sets with different computational requirements.

The significance of this work is substantial, as batch Bayesian optimization is increasingly important for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning where parallel evaluations are common. The paper demonstrates clear practical advantages, especially in noisy settings where the method shows particularly large performance gains. The results on CNN tuning (improving test error from ~14% to ~11%) demonstrate tangible real-world impact.

Strengths:
- Novel extension of KG to batch setting with strong theoretical foundation
- Effective computational approach using IPA for gradient estimation
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation across synthetic and practical problems
- Clear presentation and well-motivated comparison to existing methods
- Practical significance for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty in the computational approach (IPA is standard)
- Equation (3.1) notation could be clarified for better readability
- Limited discussion of computational complexity for large q
- Slightly less emphasis on why q-KG outperforms parallel EI in noisy settings (beyond the theoretical connection)

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature, advancing the state of the art in batch selection while providing a practically useful algorithm. The empirical results consistently demonstrate advantages over existing methods, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios where they are most needed. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and practically implemented.

I recommend acceptance. The paper is well-written, addresses a meaningful problem with a clear contribution, and demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods in both synthetic and real-world applications. While the computational approach is not entirely novel, its application to this specific acquisition function is well-executed and effectively enables the batch optimization framework. The minor concerns about notation and computational complexity do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.
