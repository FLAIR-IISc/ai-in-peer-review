## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces a batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) algorithm, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), aiming to improve upon existing methods by leveraging a decision-theoretic analysis for optimal batch selection. The authors propose an efficient gradient estimation strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable optimization of the q-KG acquisition function and demonstrate improved performance on synthetic and practical hyperparameter tuning problems, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

The core idea of extending the Knowledge Gradient to a batch setting is logically sound, building upon a strong foundation in decision theory. However, the novelty of a batch KG is not thoroughly established. The paper acknowledges existing batch KG work but doesn't provide a detailed comparative analysis differentiating its approach from, for example, [1] and [2]. The contribution seems to primarily lie in the proposed Monte Carlo gradient estimator and its efficient implementation via IPA, and this should be more clearly articulated.

A significant weakness lies in the insufficient detail surrounding Section 5.2, which describes the crucial gradient estimation. While the authors mention IPA, the explanation lacks clarity regarding the practical implementation, computational complexity, and specific challenges overcome. A more thorough elaboration on how the gradient is actually computed and optimized is essential.

The comparison to Expected Improvement (EI) in Section 6.2 is problematic. EI's assumptions of noiseless observations are not addressed. The paper should clarify whether a noise-aware EI variant was used for a fair comparison and discuss the influence of noise levels on the observed performance differences. 

A minor issue: the abbreviation “IPA” is redefined on line 162, which should be corrected for consistency.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The proposed q-KG method demonstrates promising empirical results, consistently outperforming benchmark algorithms, particularly in noisy scenarios.
*   The use of IPA for gradient estimation offers a potentially efficient approach for optimizing the acquisition function.
*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in Bayesian Optimization – efficient batch acquisition.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clear differentiation from prior work on batch KG.
*   Insufficient detail regarding the core technical contribution – the gradient estimator (Section 5.2).
*   Flawed comparison with EI due to the assumption of noiseless observations.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of batch Bayesian Optimization, but it requires substantial revisions to address the concerns regarding novelty, technical detail, and experimental rigor. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage resubmission after addressing the outlined weaknesses. If the authors can sufficiently clarify the contribution and provide a more comprehensive explanation of their technical approach, the paper could be a significant addition to the literature.
