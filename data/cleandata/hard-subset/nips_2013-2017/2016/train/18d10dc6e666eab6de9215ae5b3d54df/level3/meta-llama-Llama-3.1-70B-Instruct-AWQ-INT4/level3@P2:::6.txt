This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), which provides the one-step Bayes optimal batch of points to sample. The authors derive the q-KG acquisition function using a decision-theoretic analysis and develop an efficient strategy for computing this Bayes-optimal batch of points. They demonstrate the effectiveness of the q-KG method on both synthetic test functions and tuning hyperparameters of practical machine learning algorithms, especially in the presence of noisy function evaluations.

The main ideas of this paper relate to previous work on Bayesian optimization (BO) and parallel BO methods. BO algorithms typically put a Gaussian process prior distribution on the function and choose the next point or points to evaluate by maximizing an acquisition function. Most previous work in BO assumes sequential evaluations, but a few recent papers have considered parallel evaluations. The authors build upon this work by proposing a novel batch BO method that measures the information gain of evaluating q points via a new acquisition function, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG).

The strengths of this paper include its clear and well-organized presentation, its thorough background and technical details, and its understandable experiments. The authors provide a detailed description of the q-KG algorithm and its computational method, which is valuable for readers who want to implement the algorithm. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the q-KG method, especially in the presence of noisy function evaluations.

However, there are some weaknesses and concerns. The reviewer finds some aspects of the paper unclear and is not fully convinced by the experimental evidence. Specifically, the paper lacks wall-clock time measurements and comparisons to random search or sequential BO to show practical speedups. Additionally, the reviewer questions the justification for fixing the batch size q = 4 across all experiments. The novelty of the paper is also questioned, as similar IPA-based parallel selection (parallel EI/q-EI) exists in previous work.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on clarity and originality, but less well on significance and quality. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, but the reviewer has some concerns about the experimental evidence and the novelty of the method. The significance of the paper is high, as parallel hyperparameter optimization is a highly relevant topic in many application domains. However, the quality of the paper could be improved by addressing the reviewer's concerns and providing more comprehensive experimental evaluations.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that is effective in both noise-free and noisy settings.
* The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of the algorithm and its computational method.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the q-KG method, especially in the presence of noisy function evaluations.
* The significance of the paper is high, as parallel hyperparameter optimization is a highly relevant topic in many application domains.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper lacks wall-clock time measurements and comparisons to random search or sequential BO to show practical speedups.
* The reviewer questions the justification for fixing the batch size q = 4 across all experiments.
* The novelty of the paper is questioned, as similar IPA-based parallel selection (parallel EI/q-EI) exists in previous work.
* The reviewer has some concerns about the experimental evidence and the quality of the paper.

Overall, I would recommend acceptance of this paper, but with some revisions to address the reviewer's concerns. The authors should provide more comprehensive experimental evaluations, including wall-clock time measurements and comparisons to random search or sequential BO. Additionally, the authors should clarify the justification for fixing the batch size q = 4 across all experiments and address the novelty concerns by clearly stating the differences between their method and previous work.
