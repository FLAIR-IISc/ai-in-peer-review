This paper proposes the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm designed to efficiently identify Bayes-optimal batches of points for parallel evaluation. The core idea, building upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient method, is to maximize the expected incremental value of evaluating a batch of `q` points, formulated as a decision-theoretic problem. The authors provide a computationally efficient strategy based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize this acquisition function. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the underlying concepts and technical details.

The strength of this work lies in its theoretical foundation and elegant approach to parallel Bayesian optimization. Determining the Bayes-optimal batch represents a valuable contribution, offering a principled method for leveraging parallel evaluations. The IPA-based gradient estimation technique is a clever solution to the computational challenges associated with maximizing the q-KG acquisition function. The paper effectively connects to existing work in Bayesian optimization, particularly highlighting the limitations of existing parallel methods that rely on greedy iterative approaches. The code release is also a positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

However, the empirical evaluation presents a significant weakness. The use of test set error as the optimization criterion, particularly in the context of hyperparameter optimization (HPO), is problematic. Test set error is a noisy and often non-smooth metric, and optimizing directly for it can lead to unstable and suboptimal results. More robust metrics like Area Under the Curve (AUC) or log-loss are generally preferred in HPO settings. This choice undermines the convincingness of the empirical results, as improvements may not translate to meaningful gains in generalization performance.

The experimental benchmarks are also relatively limited. While MNIST and CIFAR10 are commonly used datasets, the evaluation is restricted to logistic regression and a single CNN architecture. This narrow scope raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. Including a broader range of HPO benchmarks, such as those available in the HPOlib library, would strengthen the paper considerably. Furthermore, testing on a greater variety of machine learning algorithms would demonstrate the robustness of q-KG across different problem characteristics. The paper mentions the advantage of q-KG in noisy settings; however, the noise level in the experiments could be more systematically varied and analyzed to better demonstrate this claim.

The assumption of independent normally distributed observation noise is also a potential limitation. In practical HPO scenarios, particularly when using cross-validation to estimate performance, structural bias and correlated errors are often present. This assumption may not hold in these cases, potentially affecting the performance of the algorithm. A discussion of the robustness of q-KG to violations of this assumption would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and a novel, principled approach to parallel Bayesian optimization.
*   Elegant solution to the computational challenges associated with maximizing the q-KG acquisition function.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Code release promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited and potentially problematic experimental evaluation (test set error as optimization criterion).
*   Restricted benchmark datasets and machine learning algorithms.
*   Assumption of independent normally distributed noise may not be realistic in many HPO scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations in the empirical evaluation, the theoretical contributions of this paper are significant. The q-KG method offers a promising approach to parallel Bayesian optimization, and the authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the underlying principles. While the current experimental results are not entirely convincing, the potential of the method is evident.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with major revisions. The authors should significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation by:

1.  Switching to more appropriate evaluation metrics (AUC, log-loss).
2.  Expanding the benchmark datasets to include a wider range of HPO benchmarks (e.g., HPOlib).
3.  Testing on a more diverse set of machine learning algorithms.
4.  Investigating the robustness of q-KG to violations of the independent noise assumption.

Addressing these points will significantly enhance the credibility and impact of this work.
