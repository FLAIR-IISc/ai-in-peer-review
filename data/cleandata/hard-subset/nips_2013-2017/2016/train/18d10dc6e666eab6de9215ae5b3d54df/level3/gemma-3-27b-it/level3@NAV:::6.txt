## Review of “The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization”

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), aiming to efficiently select batches of points for parallel evaluation. The core idea leverages the Knowledge Gradient framework, extended to the parallel setting, and employs infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute gradients for acquisition function maximization. The authors demonstrate through synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter optimization on CIFAR10/MNIST that q-KG is competitive with, and often outperforms, existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy environments.

The proposal is a logical extension of sequential BO to the parallel realm, addressing a practically important need for optimizing expensive functions in multi-core environments. The use of KG, offering a decision-theoretic optimality guarantee in the single-batch case, is a sensible approach. The technical details regarding the IPA-based gradient estimation appear well-described, allowing for a clear understanding of the computational aspects. The experimental setup is also understandable, testing the method across a range of problems and comparing it to relevant baselines. 

However, despite the clear presentation, several concerns hinder a strong endorsement. Firstly, while the paper claims novelty, the use of KG and IPA is not groundbreaking; similar IPA-based parallel selection (e.g., parallel EI) has been explored previously (Wang et al., 2015 is referenced, but a direct comparison is lacking – see Major Question 1). The authors need to clearly delineate the differences and advantages of their approach compared to this existing work. 

Secondly, the experimental validation, while comprehensive, lacks crucial practical considerations. Notably absent are wall-clock time measurements and comparisons to simpler methods like random search or sequential BO (Major Question 4). Demonstrating actual speedups over these baselines is critical to justify the added complexity of q-KG. 

Furthermore, the authors fix the batch size (q=4) across all experiments without providing a justification (Technical critique). Understanding how the method scales with larger values of *q* (Major Question 2) and the potential benefits of increased parallelism are essential. Finally, the synchronous nature of the method is not addressed; exploring potential extensions to asynchronous parallel evaluations (Major Question 3) could broaden the applicability of the proposed approach. The definition of “iterations” on the x-axis of the plots also requires clarification (Major Question 5).

**Arguments for acceptance:** The method addresses a relevant problem (parallel HPO), provides a theoretically grounded approach (Bayes-optimal batch selection), and shows promising empirical results.
**Arguments against acceptance:**  The novelty is incremental, the experimental evaluation lacks crucial practical metrics, the fixed batch size is not justified, and the potential for scaling and asynchronous evaluation remains unexplored. 

**Overall Assessment:**  While the paper presents a solid technical contribution, the concerns regarding novelty, experimental validation, and scalability prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. Addressing the major questions outlined above would significantly strengthen the work.
