- Paper introduces q-KG, a batch knowledge-gradient acquisition for GP-based Bayesian optimization that generalizes sequential KG to parallel evaluations via expected one-step decrement in the posterior predictive minimum.  
- q-KG is Bayes-optimal for the one-step decision to minimize posterior predictive minimum and reduces to parallel EI in noise-free, sample-point-restricted cases.  
- Computation uses Monte Carlo estimation of q-KG and an IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator to enable gradient-based batch optimization.  
- Continuous domains are handled by discretizing the design space into a dynamic finite set An, updated via posterior sampling heuristics.  
- Empirical results show q-KG is competitive with or outperforms parallel EI variants, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with particularly strong gains under observation noise.  
- Decision-theoretic foundation is clear and principled, filling a gap between sequential KG and practical batch BO.  
- The IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator is a sound computational strategy enabling scalable optimization for moderate q.  
- Strong empirical performance and practical relevance for parallel hyperparameter tuning and expensive black-box optimization; open-source code provided.  
- Need explicit statement of regularity assumptions for interchanging differentiation and expectation (cite or lemma, e.g., L’Ecuyer 1990).  
- Clarify handling of nondifferentiability from argmin changes and justify that nondifferentiable events are measure-zero for IPA.  
- Justify differentiability through Cholesky factorization and note practical safeguards (e.g., jitter) to ensure positive definiteness.  
- Fix sign/definition ambiguity in Sec. 5.2 so g is consistently defined (min vs. max).  
- Clarify discretization: explain how An is constructed (e.g., Thompson-type posterior samples), number of draws, optimizers used, and approximation error/stability vs. M.  
- Improve literature precision and attribution (add Ginsbourger 2010, Chevalier & Ginsbourger CL-mix, Marmin et al. gradients for q-EI, etc.).  
- Clarify GP hyperparameter treatment: re-fitting frequency, which parameters (including noise variance), and whether marginalization/prior were considered.  
- Missed evaluation of wall-clock speedups and acquisition optimization overhead for practical impact assessment.  
- Ask authors to confirm assumptions on A (compactness) and f (continuity under GP) required for IPA regularity.  
- Question why LHS was used for initial design and whether other space-filling designs (Sobol) affect results.  
- Ask whether hyperparameters were re-estimated each batch and whether Bayesian marginalization was tried to stabilize early gradients.  
- Request details on whether An includes z(1:q) to ensure differentiability as z moves and on sensitivity to M.  
- Ask for reproducibility checks (independent GP implementation) to rule out software artifacts.  
- Ask whether comparisons include entropy-based batch methods (PPES) or justify their omission.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled Bayes-optimal batch acquisition, novel efficient stochastic gradient computation, strong empirical gains in noisy settings, high practical relevance, open-source code.  
- Cons for acceptance: lack of wall-clock/throughput analysis, imprecise literature attributions and omissions, unstated technical assumptions for IPA/differentiability, and under-specified discretization impact.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept, conditional on addressing clarity, attribution, technical assumptions, and computational-overhead discussion.
