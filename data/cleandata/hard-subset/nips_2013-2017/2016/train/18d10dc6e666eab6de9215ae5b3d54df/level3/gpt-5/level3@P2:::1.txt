Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization (BO). Analogous to the (sequential) KG policy, q-KG is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective: with one batch remaining, it selects the set of q points that maximizes the expected decrement in the GP posterior predictor’s minimum. The paper shows that q-KG reduces to q-EI in the noiseless case when the final recommendation is constrained to lie among sampled points, and develops an efficient stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based optimization of the acquisition. A practical discretization strategy is used when the feasible set is continuous. Empirically, q-KG outperforms or matches state-of-the-art batch BO methods (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE, PPES) on synthetic benchmarks and in tuning ML models, especially with observation noise.

Relative to prior work, this paper generalizes KG to the batch setting and contributes an efficient Monte Carlo gradient estimator. It compares to established batch BO methods, including q-EI with joint optimization [2, 25], UCB-based batch methods [3, 5], and batch entropy search [18]. The paper should also acknowledge earlier batch EI heuristics and constant liar strategies in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization” (Ginsbourger et al., 2010), the closed-form q-EI computation and optimization in Chevalier & Ginsbourger (2013), and the differentiability/gradient-based optimization of q-EI in Marmin et al. (2015). The nested batch KG setting in [26] is noted but differs in domain assumptions and computational approach.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The decision-theoretic formulation of q-KG is well-motivated. The stochastic gradient estimator via IPA is sensible and leverages the GP linear-Gaussian structure; however, several smoothness and differentiability assumptions need to be stated more precisely:
  - The function g in Sec. 5.2 involves argmin operators; differentiability is not obvious because the minimizer can switch as Zq varies. Under standard IPA conditions (unique minimizer almost surely; ties on measure-zero sets), the estimator is unbiased. Please state these conditions (e.g., L’Ecuyer, 1990; related IPA references).
  - The derivative of the Cholesky factor D with respect to inputs exists for SPD matrices and smooth kernels but should be justified/cited (e.g., Smith, 1995; Murray, 2016). Numerical stability near ill-conditioned covariance matrices should be discussed.
  - Clarify the definition of g in Eq. (5.2): ensure consistency of min vs max signs with Eq. (4.1). Currently it reads as min-min; double-check that the direction matches the acquisition being maximized.
- Empirics: The experiments are well-chosen and strong, including both noiseless and noisy regimes and practical hyperparameter tuning. q-KG’s advantage with noise is convincing. One missing element is a study of wall-clock speedups when moving from sequential to batch-sequential BO (e.g., speedup vs. q under a fixed parallel budget), which would strengthen the practical impact claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a helpful derivation and algorithm summary. A few points need clarification:
  - Assumptions: Is A assumed compact and is f continuous (or do you rely solely on discretization An)? State the conditions under which the Bayes-optimality claim holds.
  - Algorithm 1: Step 5 says “re-train hyperparameters by MLE.” Please specify the frequency (every batch), any priors/regularization, and whether hyperparameter uncertainty is integrated (fully Bayesian marginalization) or ignored. This affects both acquisition computation and fair comparison.
  - Initial design: Why restrict to LHS? Any space-filling design (e.g., Sobol, maximin) would work; clarify that LHS is a practical choice, not a requirement.
  - Sec. 5.3: The evolving discretization An (posterior global optima samples + past points + candidate batch) is reasonable. Provide a brief sensitivity discussion of q-KG to M and to the choice of sampling measure for AMn.
  - Minor: Ensure consistent notation for σ̃n and D(n); define shapes explicitly to avoid ambiguity.

Originality
- Extending KG to the parallel setting and providing an efficient IPA-based gradient estimator is novel and valuable. While batch acquisition functions that jointly value points exist (q-EI, PPES), the Bayes-optimal last-batch objective under general noise with recommendation not restricted to sampled points is distinctive. The computational contribution (stochastic gradients for q-KG) is also original.

Significance
- The contribution is significant for practitioners who have parallel resources and must optimize expensive, noisy objectives (e.g., hyperparameter tuning). The consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly under noise, suggest this can become a default choice for batch BO. The approach is general and should be reusable in other last-step Bayes-optimal objectives.

Questions for the authors
- What precise assumptions ensure unbiasedness of the IPA estimator for q-KG? Please formalize uniqueness/tie conditions and provide a brief proof sketch or reference.
- Can you comment on stability of differentiating Cholesky and provide a reference (e.g., Smith, 1995; Murray, 2016)?
- Is A compact and f continuous? If not, how do you ensure existence/measurability of the min and Bayes-optimality statements?
- How sensitive is performance to M=1000 in discretization? Any guidance for scaling M with d and q?
- Hyperparameters: re-fit by MLE after each batch—did you compare to marginalization over hyperparameters (e.g., via MCMC) or to using priors to stabilize early iterations?
- Could you report wall-clock speedups for varying q and acquisition optimization overhead?
- For Fig. 1 (and other GP fits), did you cross-validate GP implementations (e.g., GPML/GPy) to verify consistent hyperparameters?

Pros
- Well-posed, decision-theoretic batch acquisition; Bayes-optimal for one remaining batch.
- Strong empirical results, especially with noise; practical impact for ML tuning.
- Efficient stochastic gradient estimator enabling scalable optimization.
- Clear connections to sequential KG and q-EI; reduction in noiseless constrained case.

Cons
- Some theoretical conditions (differentiability/IPA, Cholesky derivatives) are implicit.
- Literature review could better acknowledge prior batch EI developments (constant liar, closed-form q-EI, gradient-based q-EI).
- Missing analysis of wall-clock speedups versus sequential BO and overhead of optimizing q-KG.
- Minor notational ambiguities and a few clarity issues.

Minor comments and typos
- Matérn kernel: use 5/2 (not “2/5”).
- Please ensure consistent use of min/max in Sec. 5.2 when defining g.
- Consider citing: S. P. Smith (1995) “Differentiation of the Cholesky algorithm” and I. Murray (2016) “Differentiation of the Cholesky decomposition.”

Recommendation
- Overall: 8/10 (accept). High practical and scientific value; clarifications above would further strengthen the paper.
- Confidence: 4/5.
