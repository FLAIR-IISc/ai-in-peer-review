

This paper proposes a batch version of Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) for parallel Bayesian optimization, claiming to provide a Bayes-optimal batch selection method. The authors derive a Monte Carlo gradient estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to efficiently optimize the acquisition function, and report superior performance over batch EI, batch UCB, and other methods on synthetic and real-world problems, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper's main strength lies in its clear theoretical derivation of the batch KG acquisition function and its practical implementation for parallel BO. The experimental results are comprehensive, covering multiple synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, and demonstrate consistent advantages for q-KG, especially with noisy evaluations.

However, the paper suffers from significant issues that undermine its contribution. First, the novelty claim is questionable: as noted in the key points, batch KG methods already exist (e.g., [26]), and the paper fails to adequately distinguish its contribution from prior work. The authors should clarify how their computational approach differs from existing batch KG methods. Second, Section 5.2, which describes the gradient estimation technique, is insufficiently detailed and lacks necessary mathematical rigor to assess the proposed IPA-based estimator's theoretical properties. Third, the comparison to Expected Improvement (EI) is problematic since EI assumes noiseless observations, while the experiments include noisy settings. The paper doesn't clarify whether they used a noise-aware EI variant, creating a fundamental methodological inconsistency in the experimental comparison.

The minor issue of redefining "IPA" at line 162 should be addressed. While the paper makes a useful contribution to parallel BO, the lack of clear differentiation from prior batch KG work, insufficient technical detail in the key computational method, and methodological issues in the experimental comparison significantly weaken the paper's scientific contribution.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong experimental results, practical implementation, and clear theoretical derivation of the acquisition function.

Arguments against acceptance: Unclear novelty relative to prior work, insufficient technical detail in the key method, and problematic comparison to noise-sensitive EI.

Overall, the paper makes a useful incremental contribution to parallel BO but fails to sufficiently establish its novelty or provide adequate technical justification for its core computational method.
