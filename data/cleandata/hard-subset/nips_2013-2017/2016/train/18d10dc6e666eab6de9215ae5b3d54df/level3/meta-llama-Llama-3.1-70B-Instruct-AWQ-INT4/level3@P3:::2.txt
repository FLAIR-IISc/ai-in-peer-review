This paper proposes a novel parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), which computes Bayes-optimal batches and reportedly outperforms existing methods. The authors provide a well-written and easy-to-follow paper that presents an elegant solution to parallelizing Bayesian optimization.

The paper begins by reviewing the background of Bayesian optimization and the challenges of parallelizing it. The authors then introduce their novel acquisition function, q-KG, which measures the information gain of evaluating q points collectively. They derive this function using a decision-theoretic analysis that chooses the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior when there is only one batch of points remaining.

The authors also provide an efficient strategy for computing the q-KG acquisition function and its gradient using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). This allows for efficient optimization of the acquisition function using standard gradient-based optimization algorithms.

The paper presents experimental results on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning algorithms, demonstrating that q-KG consistently finds better function values than other parallel Bayesian optimization algorithms, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

The theoretical analysis in the paper is strong, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their methodology. However, the experimental evaluation is limited, with only two datasets (MNIST and CIFAR10) and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN) used. The authors should consider including more datasets and algorithm variety to demonstrate the robustness of their method.

Moreover, the authors assume independent normally distributed observation noise, which can be unrealistic in hyperparameter optimization due to structural bias and correlated errors from empirical estimates. This assumption should be discussed further, and the authors should consider exploring more realistic noise models.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel acquisition function and a new approach to parallelizing Bayesian optimization. The authors also provide a clear comparison to previous work in the field, highlighting the advantages of their method.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the efficiency of Bayesian optimization in parallel settings. The authors demonstrate that their method can find better function values than existing methods, especially when function evaluations are noisy. This has important implications for applications where function evaluations are expensive, such as in hyperparameter optimization of machine learning algorithms.

In conclusion, the paper presents a well-written and theoretically sound contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. While the experimental evaluation is limited, the authors demonstrate the potential of their method to improve the efficiency of Bayesian optimization in parallel settings. With further experimentation and exploration of more realistic noise models, this method has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
