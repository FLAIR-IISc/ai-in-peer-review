## Review of “The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization”

This paper presents the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) designed for scenarios where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors derive q-KG from a decision-theoretic analysis, aiming for Bayes optimality in the final evaluation batch. They also provide an efficient computational strategy based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable practical optimization of the acquisition function. Empirical validation on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks demonstrates superior performance compared to existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

The problem addressed – accelerating expensive black-box optimization through parallel evaluation – is highly relevant and important within the NIPS community, with connections to hyperparameter optimization for deep learning, robotics, and materials science. The core idea of jointly optimizing the batch of points, rather than iteratively selecting them, is a compelling approach. Prior work in parallel BO, such as extensions of Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB), often relies on greedy or sequential strategies. q-KG’s focus on directly optimizing the expected information gain from the entire batch distinguishes it and offers a potentially significant advantage.  The derivation from a decision-theoretic perspective provides a strong theoretical grounding for the method.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of q-KG is well-motivated and clearly presented. The use of IPA to facilitate gradient-based optimization is a clever and effective solution to the computational challenge.  The paper demonstrates a good understanding of Gaussian Process methodology and Bayesian Optimization principles.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing. The consistent outperformance of q-KG across a variety of test functions and practical applications (logistic regression and CNN tuning) provides strong evidence for its effectiveness. The particularly strong performance in noisy settings is noteworthy.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is mostly clear, and the algorithmic steps are easy to follow. The code availability is a significant benefit for reproducibility.
* **Originality:** The q-KG method offers a novel approach to batch Bayesian Optimization. While building on the Knowledge Gradient framework, the generalization to the parallel setting and the efficient computation strategy represent a significant contribution. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Gaussian Process Assumption:**  The paper heavily relies on the Gaussian Process prior. While GPs are common in BO, their performance can degrade significantly in high-dimensional input spaces, such as those encountered when tuning complex neural network architectures. The authors do not adequately address this limitation or explore potential alternatives (e.g., using dimensionality reduction techniques or other surrogate models).
* **Theoretical Analysis:**  While the derivation of q-KG is clear, the paper lacks a rigorous theoretical analysis of its performance. It would be valuable to provide error bounds for the algorithm, quantifying its convergence rate or regret bounds. A quantitative complexity analysis – assessing the computational cost as a function of input dimension, batch size, and number of observations – would also strengthen the paper. 
* **Minor Clarifications and Typos:** There are a few minor issues that detract from the clarity.  Line 36 contains a grammatical awkwardness ("the set of points to evaluate next that is"). Line 95 would benefit from a definition of *A* before its use.  Line 104 could clarify the input to μ<sup>n</sup>. Line 128 could benefit from a more substantial discussion of parallel EI. Line 140 should avoid reusing the notation *A* for the feasible domain within q-KG and the outer optimization problem.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The method is technically sound and offers a novel contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas and results.
* The code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The reliance on a Gaussian Process prior limits the applicability of the method to high-dimensional problems.
* The lack of rigorous theoretical analysis weakens the justification for the algorithm’s performance and limits its generalizability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the literature on Bayesian Optimization. The proposed q-KG method is a promising approach for accelerating expensive function evaluations. While the limitations regarding the Gaussian Process assumption and the absence of a thorough theoretical analysis are noteworthy, the strength of the empirical results and the novelty of the approach outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** contingent on the authors addressing the minor points raised regarding clarity and considering a discussion on the scalability of the method to high-dimensional problems, perhaps suggesting avenues for future work addressing this limitation.
