## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces q-KG, a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization designed for parallel function evaluations. The core idea – proposing *q* points at each iteration and efficiently computing the resulting acquisition function – is a valuable contribution to the field, addressing the important problem of accelerating expensive black-box optimization, particularly relevant in areas like hyperparameter tuning. The method is theoretically grounded in a decision-theoretic analysis, aiming for Bayes optimality in the one-step-ahead setting, and supported by empirical results demonstrating superior performance compared to existing batch BO algorithms, especially in noisy evaluation scenarios. 

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of existing work in Bayesian optimization, clearly positioning itself within the landscape of parallel BO methods like parallel EI and UCB variants. It effectively highlights the limitations of iterative greedy approaches and proposes a more holistic batch selection strategy. The connection to the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) method is also well-articulated, demonstrating a logical extension to the parallel setting. 

A significant strength of the paper lies in the development of an efficient method for evaluating q-KG’s gradient using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). This addresses a key practical challenge and enables optimization of the acquisition function even for larger batch sizes. The experimental results on both synthetic functions and real-world machine learning tasks (logistic regression and CNN tuning) are convincing, showcasing the method’s effectiveness.

However, the reliance on a Gaussian process prior is a potential limitation, particularly for high-dimensional input spaces common in neural network architecture search. The paper doesn’t adequately address the potential for GP performance degradation in such scenarios. Furthermore, while the empirical results are strong, the paper would benefit from theoretical results establishing error bounds for the algorithm and a more detailed complexity analysis, specifically concerning the computational cost as a function of input dimension *d*. 

**Minor issues:** The paper contains a few minor grammatical errors (l.36) and lacks definitions for variables like *A* (l.95). Clarification of the input to *μ<sup>n</sup>* (l.104) would also improve readability. A brief discussion of parallel EI’s limitations (l.128) and avoiding the reuse of notation *A* (l.140) would further enhance clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Sound methodology, convincing experimental results, well-written and clearly presented, addresses an important problem.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Reliance on GP prior, lack of theoretical error bounds and complexity analysis.

**Overall:** This is a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. Despite the limitations, the proposed q-KG method offers a promising approach to accelerate expensive function evaluations, and the paper is of high quality. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.
