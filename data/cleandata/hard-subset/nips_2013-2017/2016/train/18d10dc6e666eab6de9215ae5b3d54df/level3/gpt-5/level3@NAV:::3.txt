This paper proposes a batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), that generalizes the sequential KG to select q points jointly under a GP prior. The objective is to be Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch: maximize the expected decrement in the GP posterior predictor’s minimum after observing a batch. Because exact evaluation/optimization of batch KG is intractable, the authors estimate the acquisition and its gradient via Monte Carlo and an infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) estimator, and then use gradient-based optimization over the batch. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization set adaptively. Empirically, q-KG is competitive with or better than parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE, and PPES-like methods, with especially strong gains under noise, consistent with prior observations that KG handles noisy evaluations well. The work connects clearly to prior NIPS literature on BO (EI, UCB, ES/PPES) and earlier batch BO (q-EI and its gradients) while extending the KG line of work to parallel settings.

Quality: The derivation is largely sound, and the Monte Carlo + IPA approach is reasonable and standard for stochastic gradients of acquisition functions. The empirical study spans synthetic functions and practical hyperparameter tuning with careful implementation. Some technical assumptions behind unbiasedness (differentiability, unique minimizers) and the effect of domain discretization on the Bayes-optimality claim could be stated more explicitly. Complexity/variance analysis of the estimator as a function of q, M, and n would strengthen the work.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. Specific issue: equation (3.1) is unclear—μ and K are defined on vectors, but x(1:n) is not explicitly stacked; please clarify notation and dimensions (e.g., treat x(1:n) as a matrix with rows x(i)). Similarly, define D(n), Zq, and the conditioning in (4.1) precisely. Report the number of Monte Carlo samples, step sizes, and stopping criteria to aid reproducibility.

Originality: Moderate. The main novelty is extending KG to batches and using a standard IPA estimator; related efforts on q-EI gradients (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.) and PPES are acknowledged. The decision-theoretic framing for batches is clean but incremental.

Significance: High practical relevance. Parallel evaluations are common, and the method shows consistent gains, particularly with noise. The code release increases impact.

Pros:
- Clear decision-theoretic acquisition; principled extension of KG to batches
- Strong empirical performance, especially in noisy regimes
- Practical, parallel-friendly optimization with stochastic gradients
- Generally clear exposition and available code

Cons:
- Limited novelty beyond adapting KG + IPA to batch settings
- Theoretical guarantees limited to the discretized, one-step setting
- Missing complexity/variance analysis and some notation ambiguities
- Comparisons could include batch Thompson sampling

Overall: Weak accept for a well-executed, practically useful contribution.
