This paper proposes a parallel batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), aiming to improve upon existing approaches by directly optimizing a decision-theoretic acquisition function. The core idea is to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected incremental value of a measurement, building on the sequential Knowledge Gradient policy. A key contribution is a gradient estimator based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to make optimization tractable. Experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning demonstrate improved performance, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper builds on a substantial body of work in Bayesian Optimization (BO), particularly extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient [17] to the parallel batch setting. Prior work in parallel BO includes methods based on Expected Improvement (EI) [2, 19, 25], Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) [3, 5], and Entropy Search [18]. The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, noting that many existing methods employ a greedy iterative approach, optimizing a single-point acquisition function while holding others fixed.  The q-KG method, by contrast, aims for a more holistic batch selection.

**Strengths:**

* **Sound Technical Foundation:** The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is rigorous and well-motivated. The IPA-based gradient estimator appears to be a clever solution to a significant computational challenge.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over benchmark algorithms, both on synthetic test functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks. The observation of particularly strong performance in noisy environments is noteworthy.
* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of efficient batch Bayesian optimization, especially in parallel computing environments, and provides a compelling rationale for the proposed approach.
* **Code Availability:** Providing code is excellent and facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Novelty Concerns:** The reviewer’s concern regarding novelty is valid. While the paper extends KG to the parallel setting, the text acknowledges existing parallel KG work (e.g., [26]) though dismisses it due to a different optimization approach. A more detailed and direct comparison to prior parallel KG methods, specifically addressing their limitations and how q-KG overcomes them, is crucial.  The authors should explicitly discuss how their gradient estimation differs from approaches used in [15, 25] for parallel EI, even if the focus is different acquisition functions.
* **Insufficient Detail in Section 5.2:**  This section, detailing the gradient estimation, is the heart of the method. The current presentation is concise, relying heavily on references to supplementary materials. A more detailed exposition within the main paper is essential.  The equations and explanations need to be more accessible. Specifically, the connection between the perturbation analysis and the resulting gradient estimator needs further clarification.  What are the practical considerations for choosing the perturbation size?
* **Problematic EI Comparison & Noise:** The comparison to Expected Improvement (EI) is potentially flawed. The paper acknowledges that standard EI assumes noiseless observations, but it’s unclear if a noise-aware variant of EI was used for comparison. If not, this undermines the validity of the comparison, particularly given the authors emphasize q-KG’s superiority in noisy settings. A clear explanation of the EI implementation used, and a discussion of how noise impacts EI’s performance, are needed. Did they use a Monte Carlo EI estimator?
* **Minor Typo:** The redefinition of “IPA” at line 162 is a minor, but easily fixable, issue.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, although the lack of detail in Section 5.2 and the potentially flawed EI comparison raise concerns. The empirical results provide strong support for the claims, but a more thorough comparison to related parallel KG methods is needed.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but Section 5.2 needs significant improvement in terms of clarity and detail. The notation is consistent, but the mathematical derivations could benefit from more explanation.

**Originality:** While the idea of extending KG to the parallel setting isn’t entirely novel, the proposed gradient estimation technique and the direct optimization of the batch acquisition function contribute to the originality of the work. A stronger articulation of how this differs from existing parallel KG methods is needed.

**Significance:** The results suggest that q-KG is a significant advance in parallel Bayesian optimization, particularly for noisy problems. The potential for faster hyperparameter tuning and improved performance in real-world applications makes this work highly relevant to the machine learning community.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical results demonstrating improved performance.
* Technically sound approach based on a solid theoretical foundation.
* Addresses a practically important problem.
* Code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Concerns regarding novelty, requiring a more thorough comparison to related work.
* Insufficient detail in Section 5.2 hindering understanding and reproducibility.
* Potentially flawed comparison to Expected Improvement.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with major revisions**. The paper presents a promising approach to parallel Bayesian optimization. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly providing a more detailed explanation of the gradient estimation, a more thorough comparison to related work (especially other parallel KG methods), and clarifying the EI comparison – is crucial before publication.  The revisions should prioritize clarity and completeness.
