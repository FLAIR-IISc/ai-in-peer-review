This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The core idea is decision-theoretic: with a Gaussian process model, the batch is chosen to maximize the expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean after one additional batch of evaluations. The paper shows that, in the noiseless case and when the final recommendation must be among queried points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Computing q-KG requires Monte Carlo, and the authors develop an infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA)–based stochastic gradient estimator to optimize q-KG efficiently over a continuous domain by discretizing A adaptively. Empirically, q-KG is compared against parallel EI (two variants) and parallel UCB methods (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on standard synthetic functions and on hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR-10), with noticeably stronger gains in noisy settings. Code is released.

Relation to prior work: KG for sequential settings is well established [Frazier et al.; Scott et al.], and several batch acquisition functions consider the batch jointly: closed-form/gradientable q-EI [Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.], PPES [Shah & Ghahramani], and UCB-based GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE. The paper also cites nested batch KG [Wang et al., SIAM SISC], which addresses finite discrete sets. The main claimed contributions here are (i) extending KG to continuous-domain batch selection with Bayes optimality for a single remaining batch, and (ii) an efficient stochastic gradient computation using IPA to optimize the Monte Carlo–estimated objective.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic objective that directly targets simple regret, with a principled connection to KG and a clean reduction to q-EI in the noiseless/restricted-recommendation case.
- Practical optimization strategy via a differentiable Monte Carlo estimator and IPA, paired with adaptive discretization of A.
- Strong empirical performance across synthetic benchmarks and realistic hyperparameter tuning, particularly under observation noise, which is a regime where EI variants are known to degrade.
- Open-source implementation and use of established baselines enhance reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Central technical contribution (Section 5.2) is under-specified. The interchange of gradient and expectation under IPA given the nonsmooth min operator, dependence of the optimizer x*(after) on z(1:q), and differentiation through the Cholesky factor all require careful regularity conditions. The supplement is referenced but not summarized; more of this should be in the main text.
- The baseline comparison in noisy settings needs clarification. EI is typically defined for noiseless observations; one should specify whether a noise-aware q-EI (e.g., integrating over latent f, or NEI-style variants) was used. Otherwise, comparisons in Section 6.2 may overstate gains.
- No wall-clock comparisons are reported. Since q-KG involves Monte Carlo, Cholesky, and gradients through a discretized A, runtime vs. q, dimension d, M (number of discretization points), and number of Monte Carlo samples would contextualize practical efficiency against q-EI (closed-form/gradients) and PPES.
- Convergence guarantees for the stochastic gradient ascent are not discussed (beyond unbiasedness). A brief statement of conditions and step-size schedules would strengthen the methodology.

Clarity
- Section 5.2 needs more detail: derivative of D(n)(z(1:q)) (Cholesky) computation; handling ties/nondifferentiability of the argmin; Monte Carlo sample sizes; number of restarts; and stopping criteria.
- The discretization scheme (Section 5.3) is described only at a high level. Please specify how posterior sample minima are drawn (e.g., sampling functions via random features and local optimization?), computational cost, and sensitivity to M, as well as whether including z(1:q) in An introduces bias during optimization.
- Notational overload between the feasible set A for optimization and the A used inside q-KG evaluation is confusing. A consistent notation (e.g., use S for the inner discretization set) would help.
- Minor issues: “Main Stange” typo; “IPA” is introduced twice with inconsistent phrasing; fix the abbreviation and line references.

Originality
- A batch version of KG has appeared in the literature for discrete domains (nested batch KG), and several works optimize batch acquisitions jointly. The paper should more explicitly position its novelty: continuous-domain batch KG with a differentiable MC estimator and IPA-based gradients. A direct comparison (theoretical and empirical) to prior batch KG methods would clarify the incremental contribution.
- The reduction to parallel EI under noiseless conditions is a nice connection but not entirely new in spirit; emphasize what is unique here beyond that link.

Significance
- The empirical gains in noisy settings are compelling for practical hyperparameter optimization. If the computational overhead is manageable, q-KG could be adopted by practitioners.
- Additional ablations (effect of q, M, Monte Carlo samples; sensitivity to GP hyperparameter estimation via MLE vs. marginalization) would increase confidence and guide usage.

Requests for clarification and additions
- Precisely define the EI baselines in the noisy experiments: are they noise-aware? If not, add a noise-aware q-EI baseline or discuss why this is infeasible.
- Provide wall-clock time and scaling with q, d, M, and number of Monte Carlo samples, compared to q-EI (closed-form gradients) and PPES.
- Expand Section 5.2 with assumptions ensuring unbiasedness of the gradient estimator and validity of exchanging min/expectation/derivatives; include a brief sketch of the argument in the main text.
- Detail the discretization procedure (how posterior sample minima are generated), sensitivity to M, and an ablation varying M.
- Discuss the impact of re-estimating GP hyperparameters by MLE at each iteration; consider reporting results with hyperparameter marginalization (as in Snoek et al.) for robustness.
- Clarify the relationship to nested batch KG and any other prior batch KG formulations; if feasible, add a comparison on a discrete domain to isolate the computational benefits of the proposed gradient estimator.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled acquisition function tailored to simple regret with a clear decision-theoretic grounding.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise, on both synthetic and real tasks.
- Useful practical contribution (code, scalable implementation) and a generally well-executed study.

Arguments against acceptance
- Novelty relative to prior batch KG work is not fully clarified; incremental nature is possible.
- Methodological core (IPA gradient) lacks sufficient exposition and empirical efficiency analysis in the main text.
- Baseline setup in noisy settings may be unfavorable to EI if not noise-aware; fairness needs to be established.

Overall: A solid and practically relevant contribution; with clarifications on novelty and stronger methodological detail and baselines in the noisy regime, this would make a strong paper. I lean toward acceptance if the authors address the concerns above.
