

This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, a significant extension of the knowledge gradient (KG) approach to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously, providing the one-step Bayes optimal batch under the assumption of a single remaining batch. This is a substantial theoretical contribution, as most prior work on parallel BO (e.g., parallel EI [2, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], GP-UCB-PE [3]) uses iterative greedy approaches that optimize single-point acquisition functions while holding other batch points fixed. The q-KG method stands out by considering the batch collectively, with theoretical guarantees of Bayes-optimality for the one-batch setting.

The paper's theoretical foundation is strong, with clear derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and rigorous justification for its Bayes-optimal properties. The authors address the computational challenge of optimizing q-KG through infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), which enables efficient gradient computation. This technical contribution is well-executed, with thorough treatment of the gradient estimation process and discretization strategy for infinite domains. The method generalizes the sequential KG approach [7, 17] to the parallel setting while overcoming the computational barriers that prevent direct application of KG to batch optimization.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and accessible presentation of complex concepts. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow. The authors effectively contextualize their work within the growing literature on Bayesian optimization, particularly noting how their method differs from existing parallel approaches.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates promising results, showing that q-KG consistently outperforms benchmarks on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks. The paper reports significant improvements over parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, particularly in noisy settings. However, the experimental evaluation has notable limitations that weaken the empirical claims.

The most significant weakness is the choice of optimization criterion: the authors use test set error as the objective function for hyperparameter optimization. This is problematic because test set error is a poor metric for optimization, as it does not directly correspond to the ultimate goal of model performance. In hyperparameter optimization, metrics like AUC, log loss, or F1 score would be more appropriate, as they better reflect model quality and are commonly used in practice. The authors should have used these more appropriate metrics to evaluate the quality of the hyperparameters they found.

The experimental scope is also limited. The paper evaluates only two datasets (MNIST and CIFAR10) and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). This narrow scope fails to demonstrate the method's generalizability across diverse problem types and algorithm families. The hyperparameter tuning literature (e.g., HPOlib [1, 2]) provides a much broader set of benchmarks that would better establish the method's value. The lack of diverse benchmarking makes it difficult to assess whether the performance gains are consistent across different problem domains.

Furthermore, the assumption of independent normally distributed observation noise may not hold in practical hyperparameter optimization. In real-world HPO, noise often arises from cross-validation or other empirical estimation techniques that induce correlated errors across hyperparameter configurations. The authors should have addressed how their method would perform under such realistic noise structures, or at least acknowledged this limitation.

Despite these weaknesses, the theoretical contribution is strong and the paper presents a valuable method for batch Bayesian optimization. The q-KG approach is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and the computational technique for efficient optimization is clever and well-executed. The method fills an important gap in the literature by providing a non-greedy, decision-theoretic approach to parallel BO.

The significance of this work is substantial for applications where parallel function evaluations are possible, such as distributed hyperparameter tuning. The method's advantage in noisy settings is particularly valuable, as many real-world optimization problems involve noisy evaluations. However, the limited experimental evaluation prevents a fully convincing demonstration of this significance.

In conclusion, this is a strong theoretical contribution to Bayesian optimization with clear practical value. The paper's main weaknesses lie in the experimental evaluation, particularly the choice of optimization metric and limited benchmark scope. These weaknesses prevent the paper from fully demonstrating its significance, but they do not undermine the theoretical novelty and technical soundness of the proposed method. The authors should address these limitations in future work, particularly by using more appropriate evaluation metrics and expanding the benchmark scope. For NIPS 2016, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the theory of Bayesian optimization with promising practical implications.
