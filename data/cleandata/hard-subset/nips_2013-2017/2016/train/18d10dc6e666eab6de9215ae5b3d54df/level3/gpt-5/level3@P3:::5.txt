Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function derived via a one-step Bayes-optimal knowledge-gradient (KG) objective for selecting q points jointly. The authors show that q-KG reduces to q-EI in the noiseless setting when the final recommendation is constrained to sampled points. Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG is computationally demanding, they propose a Monte Carlo pathwise/IPA gradient estimator to enable gradient-based optimization of the batch. They report strong empirical results on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning, with especially pronounced gains under observation noise.

Relative to prior work, the paper builds on sequential KG (Frazier et al., 2009; Scott et al., 2011) and contrasts q-KG with batch EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger, 2013; Marmin et al., 2015), batch UCB (Desautels et al., 2014; Contal et al., 2013), and batch ES (Shah & Ghahramani, 2015). There is also directly related “nested-batch” KG work (Wang et al., 2016, cited as [26]) that values batches collectively but (i) restricts to discrete feasible sets and (ii) uses a different nesting structure. The present paper appears to be the first to provide a decision-theoretic (one-step Bayes-optimal) batch KG for continuous domains along with a scalable pathwise gradient estimator for its optimization.

Strengths
- Conceptual: A principled batch acquisition (q-KG) that is Bayes-optimal in the one-step setting and reduces to q-EI in the appropriate noiseless limit.
- Algorithmic: A practical MC pathwise gradient (IPA) estimator that enables joint optimization of batches rather than greedy construction.
- Empirical: Consistent improvements over common batch BO baselines; gains are largest under noise, where q-KG is expected to shine due to its decision-theoretic derivation with noisy observations.
- Clarity of positioning vs q-EI/UCB/ES and connection to the sequential KG literature.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty and positioning vs prior batch KG: While the paper cites [26], prior batch KG-like methods beyond [26] should be discussed more deeply. Please explicitly contrast the objective (q-KG vs nested-batch KG), domain assumptions (continuous vs discrete), computational approach (pathwise gradient vs enumeration/simulation), and guarantees. If other batch KG variants exist (e.g., discrete batch KG in simulation optimization, alternate relaxations), please cite and clarify how your method differs in objective and algorithm.
- If the key contribution is the MC gradient estimator, its efficiency should be compared against alternatives:
  - Theory: Provide conditions (smoothness, boundedness) under which ∂/∂z E[g(z, Z)] = E[∂g/∂z] holds, with a short proof sketch (dominated convergence / interchange of limit and expectation). State unbiasedness formally and discuss variance and bias under finite discretization An. Clarify stability of differentiating through the Cholesky factor and how you compute ∂D/∂z (closed-form triangular solves vs autodiff).
  - Complexity: Detail per-iteration time: O(q^3) for Cholesky of K(n)(z, z)+Σ, O(|An| q^2) for computing σ̃n over An, number of MC samples used for function value vs gradient, and how complexity scales with q and |An|.
  - Empirics: Compare your gradient estimator’s sample efficiency and wall-clock time to (i) gradient-free optimizers on q-KG (CMA-ES), (ii) score-function/REINFORCE gradients, and (iii) finite-difference SPSA on z. Report gradient variance vs iterations and convergence speed. Ablate common random numbers and antithetic variates.
- Section 5.2 requires more detail. This section is central to the contribution but currently leaves key steps to the supplement. Please:
  - Provide a clear, self-contained derivation of the gradient, including the handling of the argmin over An and nondifferentiability (almost-sure uniqueness of minimizers; subgradient if ties).
  - Explain how you handle the derivative through the “after” argmin x*(after) in practice (straight-through estimator, envelope theorem for piecewise-smooth minima over a discrete set).
  - Include pseudo-code for stochastic gradient ascent, including choice of step sizes, restarts, batch size of MC samples per update, and stopping criteria.
- Discretization of A in q-KG(z, An): The phrase “drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior distribution of the GP” is ambiguous. If you sample from p(x* | data) as in PES, please say so and briefly describe how you approximate that distribution. Otherwise, clarify whether you sample around local minima of the posterior mean, use Thompson samples of f and then minimize, or another scheme. Provide an ablation on M, the resampling schedule, and whether including previously evaluated points and z(1:q) materially affects performance. Discuss approximation error introduced by discretization and its impact on unbiasedness of the gradient estimator.
- Comparison to EI under noise (Section 6.2): Parallel EI baselines in MOE and Spearmint are typically defined for noiseless or plug-in settings. Please clarify:
  - Did you use a noise-aware variant (e.g., noisy q-EI with fantasy observations / marginalization over observation noise)? If not, the comparison under noise is tilted in favor of q-KG. Either include a properly noise-aware q-EI (e.g., MC-based qNEI analogue) or explicitly state the limitation and discuss how noise affects EI’s performance.
  - Report results where the noise level used by baselines matches the learned σ^2, and analyze sensitivity to misspecification.
- Experimental reporting: Include wall-clock time per batch selection (including hyperparameter refits), the number of MC samples for value and gradient estimates, and performance vs time in addition to evaluations. Show results for larger q (e.g., 8, 16) to stress-test scalability. Provide seeds and configuration details sufficient for reproduction.
- Minor: “IPA” is redefined at line 162; define once (Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis) and use consistently.

Quality
The decision-theoretic formulation is sound and aligns with sequential KG. The pathwise estimator is appropriate for Gaussian reparameterization and, if conditions are met, is unbiased. However, the core computation section (5.2) needs significantly more detail, and the noise-aware baseline comparison should be clarified to support claims about noise robustness.

Clarity
Writing is generally clear and well-organized, but Section 5.2 and the discretization procedure need elaboration. Add pseudo-code, complexity analysis, and a precise description of the An sampling scheme. Clarify the EI baseline under noise and fix the IPA abbreviation.

Originality
Moderate to good. Extending KG to joint batch selection in continuous domains with a practical gradient estimator is valuable. Please strengthen the discussion of prior batch KG methods and make the distinct contributions explicit.

Significance
High practical relevance for parallel, noisy BO. If the computational method scales in q and |An| and the noise-aware comparisons are addressed, the contribution should be impactful for practitioners.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Principled batch acquisition function with strong empirical gains, especially under noise.
- Practical gradient-based optimization via pathwise estimator.
- Clear link to established KG theory and relation to q-EI.

Cons:
- Insufficient detail in the core gradient estimation section and discretization procedure.
- Limited analysis of estimator variance/complexity and lack of empirical comparisons to alternative estimators.
- Potentially unfair EI comparisons under noise unless a noise-aware variant is used.

Recommendation
Promising and likely to be impactful, contingent upon clarifying novelty relative to prior batch KG, expanding Section 5.2 with theory and implementation details, and strengthening the noisy baselines. I lean toward acceptance after these revisions.
