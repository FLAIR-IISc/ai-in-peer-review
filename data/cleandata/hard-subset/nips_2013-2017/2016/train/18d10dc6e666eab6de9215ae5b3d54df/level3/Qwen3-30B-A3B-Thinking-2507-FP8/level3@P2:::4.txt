

# Review for "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, which addresses the important problem of parallel function evaluation in expensive black-box optimization. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that selects q points simultaneously, providing the one-step Bayes optimal batch under the assumption of a single remaining batch. The key contribution is the efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis to evaluate and optimize the q-KG acquisition function, which is otherwise computationally prohibitive. The paper demonstrates significant performance improvements over existing methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, parallel UCB with exploration) on both synthetic test functions and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work on Bayesian optimization, particularly the sequential knowledge gradient method [17] and parallel extensions like parallel EI [2, 25]. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from iterative batch methods by optimizing the acquisition function jointly over all q points rather than greedily selecting them one-by-one. This distinction is well-justified with theoretical arguments and empirical evidence.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation. The experimental design is thorough, including both noise-free and noisy synthetic functions and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The authors carefully compare against relevant baselines and provide open-source code. However, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees on error bounds or computational complexity with respect to input dimension, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. The Gaussian process assumption may also be questionable for high-dimensional inputs like neural network architectures, a concern that should be addressed.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical exposition and a logical flow from problem statement to method to experiments. The algorithm description is precise, and the figures effectively illustrate the performance gains. Some minor notational issues could be improved: the authors reuse the symbol A for both the feasible domain and the discretized set in the optimization (line 5.3), which causes confusion. Defining A explicitly in line 95 and clarifying the input to μ^(n) (line 104) would enhance clarity.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the parallel Bayesian optimization literature. The decision-theoretic derivation of a batch acquisition function that optimizes all q points collectively rather than sequentially is novel. The computational technique using IPA to efficiently evaluate the acquisition function's gradient represents a meaningful advance over previous approaches that rely on Monte Carlo sampling or iterative greedy selection. The authors appropriately cite related work, including recent NIPS papers on parallel BO [3, 5, 18, 25].

**Significance:** The work addresses a pressing practical problem in machine learning: speeding up hyperparameter tuning for expensive models through parallel evaluation. The demonstrated performance gains, especially in noisy settings where q-KG outperforms alternatives by a wider margin, have clear practical implications for practitioners. The method's ability to handle noisy evaluations is particularly valuable given the prevalence of noisy measurements in real-world applications.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient method that delivers consistent empirical improvements over state-of-the-art batch BO approaches. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic benchmarks and practical machine learning applications. The code availability enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:** The lack of theoretical analysis (error bounds, complexity with respect to dimension) is the most significant limitation. The potential limitation of GP priors for high-dimensional problems should be acknowledged. Some minor notational inconsistencies could be clarified.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**
*Pro:* This is a solid contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature with clear theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and addresses a practically important problem with significant advantages over existing approaches, especially in noisy settings. The paper is well-written and the code is available.

*Con:* The paper lacks theoretical guarantees on performance or computational complexity. The GP assumption may be questionable for high-dimensional applications like neural architecture search. The authors could strengthen their claims by addressing these limitations.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The method is sound, the experiments are convincing, and the paper is well-written. The minor weaknesses do not undermine the significant contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the notational issues and acknowledge the limitations of the GP assumption for high-dimensional problems.
