This paper proposes a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), that selects q points per iteration to support parallel evaluations. It generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) decision-theoretic criterion to the batch setting, selecting the batch that maximizes the expected reduction in the posterior mean minimum after one additional round of measurements. The authors show that q-KG recovers parallel EI when evaluations are noise-free and the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points. Because exact optimization of q-KG is computationally intensive, they approximate the acquisition via Monte Carlo and optimize it using a stochastic gradient estimated by infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). Empirically, q-KG is competitive or superior to parallel EI variants and UCB-based batch methods across synthetic test functions and two realistic hyperparameter-tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10), with especially strong gains under noise.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on the sequential KG literature for Gaussian processes and on recent batch BO methods, including q-EI (Ginsbourger et al., Chevalier & Ginsbourger; and gradient-based optimization of q-EI), GP-BUCB and GP-UCB-PE for batch UCB, and parallel entropy search (PPES). The novelty is to extend the Bayes-optimal one-step lookahead principle from sequential KG to batch selection, coupled with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator for optimizing the batch jointly. The approach of evaluating/optimizing a batch acquisition by Monte Carlo with reparameterization/IPA is also aligned with more recent gradient-based treatments of q-EI and PPES.

Quality: The technical development is sound overall. The one-step optimality of q-KG (for minimizing the posterior mean minimum) is standard in the KG framework; the paper carefully adapts it to the batch case, where exact evaluation is intractable, and provides an unbiased IPA gradient estimator under differentiability assumptions on the GP mean/kernel and in the absence of ties (measure-zero events). The computational strategy—Cholesky reparameterization of the conditional posterior and stochastic gradients—appears well engineered and leverages existing GP infrastructure. Experiments are solid and relevant, showing consistent improvements, particularly in noisy settings where KG’s value of information is most helpful. However, theory beyond the one-step optimality claim is limited: there are no convergence/regret guarantees for the batch, approximate, and discretized setting; and the effect of discretization and Monte Carlo error on performance is not characterized.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear motivation, background, and algorithmic description. The use of two “A”s (domain and discretization set) is noted and somewhat clarified, though notational hygiene could be improved (see suggestions below). The empirical section clearly states implementation details and baselines. One specific notational issue: in Eq. (3.1) µ and K are applied to x(1:n). Since x(1:n) denotes a sequence, please clarify shapes explicitly, e.g., K(x,x(1:n)) as a 1×n vector, K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) as an n×n matrix, and µ(x(1:n)) as an n-vector; similarly for diag{σ2(·)}. This will help avoid ambiguity.

Originality: Moderate. The contribution is primarily an extension of the KG idea to batch selection with a Monte Carlo/IPA gradient to enable joint batch optimization. While principled and useful, both the idea of joint batch optimization and the use of stochastic gradients for multipoint acquisitions have been explored for q-EI and PPES. The decision-theoretic, Bayes-optimal framing is a distinguishing angle.

Significance: High practical relevance. Batch BO is widely needed; noisy settings are common in ML hyperparameter tuning; and a method that outperforms strong baselines is valuable. The code availability is a plus. The approach should be adopted by practitioners and may stimulate further work on scalable, gradient-based optimization of value-of-information criteria.

Strengths:
- Principled derivation: q-KG retains the KG one-step optimality in the batch context.
- Efficient computation via IPA-based stochastic gradients and Cholesky reparameterization.
- Strong empirical results, especially under noise, on both synthetic and realistic ML tasks.
- Clear write-up with implementation details and open-source code.
- Sensible connections to and differentiation from q-EI and UCB-based batch methods.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Novelty is incremental relative to existing multipoint acquisitions and stochastic-gradient optimization.
- Evaluation of q-KG relies on discretizing A; the impact of M and the sampling scheme on accuracy and performance is not quantified.
- No theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence with increasing samples, or bounds relating discretization/MC error to acquisition accuracy).
- Limited analysis of computational scaling with q, dimension, and number of Monte Carlo samples. Results focus on q=4.
- Comparisons omit some contemporaneous batch information-based methods (e.g., PPES) in experiments.
- The “Bayes-optimal” claim holds in the idealized one-step setting; in practice, refitting hyperparameters and discretization/MC approximations may bias the decision. A discussion would help.

Detailed comments and questions:
- Please report sensitivity to M (size of An), number of MC samples for q-KG and its gradient, and the number of gradient steps/multi-starts. This would illuminate runtime–performance trade-offs.
- Discuss handling of ties/nonsmoothness in min over A when using IPA; clarify whether you use subgradients or rely on the measure-zero assumption.
- Provide wall-clock comparisons, not only regret, especially for larger q, to demonstrate practical throughput benefits.
- Include results for larger batch sizes and, if possible, asynchronous settings (a common deployment scenario).
- Consider adding PPES (or explain why it is infeasible/fairness issues) and the closed-form q-EI baseline of Chevalier & Ginsbourger where applicable.
- Hyperparameter learning: MLE is reasonable, but BO performance can be sensitive. Comment on robustness or consider marginalization (even approximately) in future work.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Principled batch acquisition extending KG with a clean, general computational approach.
- Consistent empirical gains, particularly in noisy settings that matter in practice.
- Clear presentation and available code increase impact and reproducibility.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited novelty compared to prior multipoint acquisitions and gradient-based optimization techniques.
- Lack of theoretical guarantees and limited analysis of discretization/MC effects.
- Experimental scope could be broader (larger q, additional baselines, wall-clock metrics).

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The method is well motivated, carefully executed, and practically useful, with solid empirical evidence. While the conceptual novelty is moderate, the contribution meaningfully advances batch BO practice through a principled KG-based approach and efficient optimization.
