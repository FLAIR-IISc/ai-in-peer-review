### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, termed the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The goal is to select a batch of `q` points to evaluate in parallel to find the global optimum of an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function. The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, aiming to find the one-step Bayes-optimal batch of points; that is, the set of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final recommended solution value.

This work extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting. Unlike many existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods that construct the batch greedily (e.g., "constant liar" heuristics or iterative additions to the batch), q-KG jointly optimizes the entire batch of `q` points. The authors recognize that naively maximizing the q-KG acquisition function is computationally intractable. The main technical contribution is an efficient method for maximizing q-KG using a gradient-based optimizer. This is achieved by deriving an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for the acquisition function via Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). The method is evaluated on several synthetic benchmark functions and two real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression and a convolutional neural network), demonstrating superior or competitive performance against state-of-the-art parallel Bayesian optimization methods, particularly in noisy settings.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of parallelizing expensive function evaluations is of high practical importance in machine learning and other scientific domains. The proposed q-KG method is a principled and novel extension of the knowledge gradient framework to the batch setting. The joint optimization of the entire batch is a key conceptual advantage over more heuristic or greedy approaches for batch construction.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is sound and well-motivated from a decision-theoretic standpoint. The core technical contribution—the use of IPA to derive an efficient and unbiased gradient estimator for maximizing q-KG—is clever and essential for making the method practical. This allows the use of standard gradient-based optimizers for a problem that would otherwise be extremely difficult to solve.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The paper includes comparisons against multiple relevant baselines (parallel EI from two different libraries, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE) on a good variety of problems, including standard synthetic functions and two practical hyperparameter tuning applications. The results strongly support the authors' claims, showing that q-KG is often superior to competitors, with a particularly notable advantage in the presence of evaluation noise. This latter point is a significant practical strength, as many real-world optimization problems involve noisy measurements.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the proposed method is described in sufficient detail, and the connection to previous work is well-established. The authors provide enough information for an expert to understand and potentially reproduce the work, and they have also made their code available.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** While the method is empirically strong and well-motivated, the paper lacks theoretical results regarding its performance. Many papers in the Bayesian optimization literature, particularly those introducing UCB-style algorithms, provide theoretical guarantees such as cumulative regret bounds or convergence rates. The absence of such analysis makes it difficult to formally characterize the algorithm's behavior, its sample complexity, or how its performance might depend on problem characteristics.

2.  **Complexity Analysis:** A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of optimizing the q-KG acquisition function would be beneficial. The paper states the method is "efficient," but a quantitative analysis of how the optimization cost scales with the batch size `q`, the input dimension `d`, and the size of the discretization set `M` would help practitioners understand the practical limitations of the approach.

3.  **Limitations of the GP Model:** The method inherits the standard limitations of Gaussian Process-based optimization. While the CNN tuning experiment has 8 dimensions, the paper could do more to discuss the potential challenges of using a standard GP prior in higher-dimensional spaces, which are common when tuning large neural network architectures. The assumption of a GP prior may become inappropriate or computationally burdensome in such settings. While this is a general critique of the field, acknowledging it would strengthen the paper.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

-   l.36: There is a minor grammatical issue in the sentence fragment: "the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal...". Suggest rephrasing for clarity, perhaps to "chooses the set of points... that is optimal".
-   l.95: In Section 4, the expression `min_{x∈A} µ^(n)(x)` is used. While `A` is defined as the feasible domain in the introduction, it would be clearer to reiterate here what `A` refers to in the context of computing the KG factor, especially before the discretization is introduced in Section 5.3.
-   l.104: The algorithm summary uses `argmin_{x∈A}µ^(I+Nq)(x)`. Is the input to `µ^(n)` a single point `x`, or is the notation meant to represent the posterior mean function after `n` observations? Please clarify for consistency.
-   l.128: The paper states that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under specific conditions. For readers not deeply familiar with parallel EI, it would be helpful to briefly state what the parallel EI acquisition function represents, to make the comparison more self-contained.
-   l.140: The notation `A` is reused for both the continuous feasible domain of the optimization problem (as in `z(1:q)⊂A` in eq. 4.2) and the finite set over which the minimum of the posterior mean is computed (as in eq. 4.1). In Section 5.3, this is further complicated by the introduction of `An`. Using a different notation for the discretized set, for example `A_d`, throughout Sections 4 and 5 would significantly improve clarity and avoid confusion.
