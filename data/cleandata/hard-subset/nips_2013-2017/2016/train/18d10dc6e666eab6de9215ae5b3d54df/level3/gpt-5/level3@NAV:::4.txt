This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization method that generalizes the knowledge gradient to select q points jointly per iteration. The acquisition is defined as the expected one-step reduction of the GP posterior minimum after taking a batch, which is Bayes-optimal when a single batch remains. To make optimization feasible, the authors derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and use a discretization of the inner minimization over A. Empirically, q-KG outperforms parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks, with clear gains in the noisy setting. This extends prior work on KG (sequential) and parallels recent multi-point EI and entropy-search approaches by optimizing the batch collectively rather than greedily.

Quality: The derivation is sound, the connection to q-EI in the noiseless case is correct, and the IPA-based estimator is appropriate under the stated differentiability assumptions. Experiments are carefully executed and convincing. Two missing pieces would strengthen the work: (i) theoretical guarantees (e.g., Bayes simple-regret bounds or consistency) for the batch policy; and (ii) a quantitative complexity analysis in q, d, M (discretization size), number of Monte Carlo samples, and GP hyperparameter updates, including wall-clock comparisons.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and well organized, and code availability is a plus. Minor issues/clarifications:
- l.36: grammar (“the set of points to evaluate next that is” → rephrase).
- l.95: define A upon first use.
- l.104: clarify the input/arguments to μ^n(·).
- l.128: briefly discuss parallel EI variants and how q-KG differs.
- l.140: avoid reusing the notation A for both the feasible domain and the discretized set (use distinct symbols).

Originality: Proposes a principled, Bayes-optimal batch KG with an efficient gradient estimator; distinguishes itself from parallel EI, UCB-based batch policies, and parallel ES/PES, and from discrete nested-batch KG.

Significance: Addresses an important practical need—reducing wall-clock time via parallel evaluations. The empirical improvements, particularly under noise, suggest immediate impact for practitioners.

Concerns and suggestions:
- The GP prior may be poorly matched to high-dimensional, structured spaces (e.g., architectures). Please discuss applicability and alternatives (e.g., additive/embedded kernels, input warping, deep kernels) and how performance scales with d.
- Provide sensitivity to M and the number of Monte Carlo samples; discuss convergence and failure modes of the nonconvex acquisition optimization.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, principled method with efficient implementation.
- Strong empirical gains, especially in noisy settings.
- Clear contribution beyond greedy batch construction.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing theoretical error/complexity guarantees.
- Limited discussion of high-dimensional applicability and scalability.
