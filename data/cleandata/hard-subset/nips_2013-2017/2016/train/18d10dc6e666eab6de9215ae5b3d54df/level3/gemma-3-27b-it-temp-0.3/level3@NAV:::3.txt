## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization designed for scenarios where multiple objective function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The core idea is to extend the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework – a decision-theoretic approach selecting points maximizing information gain – to batch settings. Recognizing the computational intractability of directly maximizing the batch KG, the authors cleverly employ Monte Carlo sampling coupled with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate gradients and optimize the acquisition function. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, presenting a logical flow from background on Gaussian Processes to the proposed algorithm and its implementation details. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating q-KG’s competitive or superior performance compared to existing batch Bayesian optimization methods (parallel EI, batch UCB) on both synthetic test functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy evaluation settings. The availability of code further enhances the paper’s practical value.

However, the novelty of the work feels somewhat incremental. While the extension of KG to a batch setting and the application of IPA for gradient estimation are sound contributions, they represent a natural progression of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new breakthrough. The KG framework itself is well-established (Frazier et al., 2016), and IPA is a standard tool for gradient estimation. The main contribution lies in the combination and application of these techniques to the specific problem of parallel Bayesian optimization. 

A specific technical point requires clarification: Equation (3.1) is unclear. The authors define μ and K for vectors, but then apply them to sequences x^(1:n). It’s unclear how the vector-valued functions are being extended to operate on sequences, and a more explicit explanation would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practically relevant problem (parallel BO) with a well-motivated approach.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance, particularly in noisy settings.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – primarily an extension of existing techniques.
*   Technical clarity issue with Equation (3.1).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. While the novelty is not groundbreaking, the practical benefits and clear presentation make it a worthwhile addition to the conference. I recommend **acceptance**, with a minor revision to clarify Equation (3.1).
