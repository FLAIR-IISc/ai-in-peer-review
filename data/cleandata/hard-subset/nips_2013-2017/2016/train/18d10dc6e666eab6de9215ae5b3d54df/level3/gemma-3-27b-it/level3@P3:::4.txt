This paper proposes q-KG, a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, and provides an efficient method for its computation. The core idea – proposing q points in parallel to maximize information gain – is a sound and potentially impactful contribution to the field, particularly given the increasing prevalence of expensive function evaluations in machine learning (e.g., neural network hyperparameter tuning). The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the method’s superiority over existing batch BO algorithms, especially in noisy settings. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the underlying concepts and technical details.

The work builds upon the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework [17], extending it to the parallel setting. Previous work in parallel BO, such as parallel EI [2, 19, 25] and batch UCB [3, 5], often relies on iterative greedy approaches, optimizing single-evaluation acquisition functions sequentially. q-KG distinguishes itself by considering the batch of points collectively, aiming for a joint optimization of the acquisition function. This is a conceptually strong approach, and the paper effectively argues its benefits. The connection to existing work is well-established, and relevant papers are appropriately cited.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The q-KG acquisition function represents a significant advance in batch Bayesian optimization, offering a theoretically grounded approach to parallel evaluation.
* **Soundness:** The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is rigorous and well-explained.
* **Efficiency:** The proposed Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) [25] method provides a practical solution to the computational challenges associated with maximizing q-KG.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results on both synthetic and real-world problems demonstrate the effectiveness of q-KG, particularly in noisy environments.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Gaussian Process Prior Limitation:** The reliance on a Gaussian process prior may limit the applicability of q-KG to high-dimensional input spaces, such as those encountered when optimizing neural network architectures. The paper acknowledges this but does not explore potential alternatives or mitigation strategies.
* **Theoretical Gaps:** While the paper provides a strong empirical validation, it lacks formal theoretical results regarding error bounds for the algorithm or a quantitative complexity analysis with respect to input dimension. Establishing such guarantees would further strengthen the contribution.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** Several minor issues detract slightly from the overall polish. Line 36 contains a grammatical error ("the set of points to evaluate next that is").  A is not defined in line 95. Line 104 requires clarification regarding the input to mu^n.  The relationship to parallel EI could be more thoroughly discussed in line 128. Line 140 reuses the notation A, which is confusing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The method is fundamentally sound and represents a significant improvement over existing approaches to batch Bayesian optimization.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical benefits of q-KG.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explains the technical details, and provides a thorough comparison to related work.
* The open-source code availability encourages reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of theoretical guarantees and a discussion of the limitations associated with the Gaussian process prior represent weaknesses that should be addressed.
* The minor technical issues, while not critical, detract from the overall quality of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. While the absence of formal theoretical guarantees is a notable weakness, the compelling empirical results and the novelty of the proposed method outweigh this concern.  I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the minor technical issues and, ideally, including a discussion of potential future work to address the theoretical gaps and limitations of the Gaussian process prior. Specifically, the authors should address the points raised regarding the minor technical errors, and briefly discuss (perhaps in a limitations section) the scalability to higher dimensions and alternative prior choices.
