This paper proposes a new method for batch Bayesian optimization called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The core idea is to select a batch of `q` points for simultaneous evaluation by maximizing the expected improvement in the final solution quality, assuming this is the last batch of evaluations to be performed. This makes the selection of the batch a one-step Bayes-optimal decision. This work extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) framework to the parallel setting. Unlike many existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods that build batches greedily or heuristically (e.g., constant liar EI, batch UCB), this method considers the joint value of the entire batch of points. To make the maximization of the q-KG acquisition function computationally tractable, the authors propose using an infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased estimator for its gradient, allowing for efficient gradient-based optimization.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution of this paper is the principled, decision-theoretic derivation of a batch acquisition function. Formulating the batch selection problem as a one-step Bayes-optimal decision is an elegant and theoretically sound approach. This provides a valuable contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature, moving beyond the more common heuristic or greedy methods for parallelization. This work has the potential to influence future research in batch-mode active learning and optimization.

2.  **Quality of Theoretical Contribution:** The theoretical development is strong. The paper clearly defines the q-KG factor and provides a practical method for its optimization. The application of IPA to derive an unbiased gradient estimator for the Monte Carlo estimate of the q-KG factor is a clever and crucial component that makes the method feasible. This technical contribution is solid and well-executed.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating their work within the existing literature, and explaining their proposed method step-by-step. The notation is consistent and the algorithm is clearly presented.

### Weaknesses

The main weakness of this paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which does not provide fully convincing evidence for the method's claimed superiority in practical hyperparameter optimization (HPO) settings.

1.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is restricted to only two machine learning models (Logistic Regression and CNN) on two datasets (MNIST and CIFAR-10). The performance of optimization algorithms can be highly dependent on the characteristics of the objective function landscape. Claims of general-purpose utility require validation on a wider variety of tasks and model types. The use of established HPO benchmark suites, such as HPOlib, would have provided a more rigorous and standardized comparison against other state-of-the-art optimizers.

2.  **Choice of Optimization Objective:** The experiments use test set error (i.e., classification accuracy) as the objective function for optimization. This is a suboptimal choice for several reasons. Accuracy is a threshold-based metric that is often piecewise constant and non-smooth, making it a difficult target for gradient-based optimizers and GP models. It provides a less informative signal about model performance compared to metrics like log-loss or AUC, which are continuous and better reflect model confidence. Optimizing a smoother proxy for performance often leads to better results.

3.  **Questionable Noise Assumption for HPO:** The paper claims significant advantages in noisy settings. However, the noise model used is additive, independent, and normally distributed. While this is a standard assumption in GP literature, it may not accurately reflect the nature of noise in real-world HPO. For example, when test error is estimated on a small validation set, the noise is binomial, not Gaussian. More importantly, noise from stochastic training procedures (e.g., due to random mini-batch selection or weight initialization) can be complex, with structural biases and correlations that violate the i.i.d. Gaussian assumption. The strong performance shown might be partly an artifact of the synthetic noise model aligning well with the GP's assumptions, and may not fully translate to practical HPO scenarios.

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this paper presents a theoretically strong and elegant contribution to the field of parallel Bayesian optimization. The derivation of the Bayes-optimal q-KG acquisition function and the efficient method for its optimization are significant achievements. The paper is also exceptionally clear and well-written.

However, the empirical validation is not on par with the theoretical contribution. The limited scope of the experiments and questionable choices regarding the objective metric and noise model undermine the convincingness of the practical results. While the method is promising, its claimed superiority in real-world HPO tasks is not yet fully substantiated.

Despite the experimental weaknesses, the novelty and theoretical significance of the proposed method are high enough to warrant acceptance. The paper introduces an important idea that is likely to be influential. The authors should, however, be strongly encouraged to address the experimental limitations in the final version by acknowledging the choice of objective metric and expanding their discussion on the noise model's applicability to HPO. Including experiments on more diverse benchmarks would greatly strengthen the paper.
