- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition based on the knowledge gradient, Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch.  
- Defines acquisition as expected reduction in the GP posterior mean minimum after q evaluations; reduces to q-EI in noise-free, sampled-point recommendation case.  
- Develops Monte Carlo estimator and an unbiased IPA-based stochastic gradient to enable gradient-based joint optimization of batch z(1:q).  
- Approximates acquisition by discretizing domain A using samples from the posterior-implied minima plus past and candidate points.  
- Empirical results show q-KG is competitive in noiseless settings and often superior under observation noise versus q-EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE.  
- Builds on prior KG and batch BO work; novelty is extending KG to joint batch selection and deriving a practical IPA stochastic gradient in continuous domains.  
- Strength: clear decision-theoretic formulation that does not restrict final recommendation to sampled points.  
- Strength: sound, implementable IPA-based gradient that reuses standard GP machinery (Cholesky, conditional sampling).  
- Strength: empirical gains under noisy evaluations align with KG’s strengths; good exposition and practical engineering details.  
- Weakness: practical efficiency and scalability under-characterized—no wall-clock or overhead comparisons to random search, sequential BO, or q-EI.  
- Weakness: experiments use fixed batch size q=4 with no study of scaling to larger q (e.g., 8–64) despite rising computational costs.  
- Weakness: novelty seen as incremental since KG and IPA exist and gradient-based joint batch selection for q-EI has been done; needs clearer head-to-head novelty argument.  
- Concern: some presentation details unclear (e.g., meaning of “iterations” in plots, fairness/consistency of initial designs).  
- Quality: derivations are reasonable; IPA gradient plausible but formal assumptions (e.g., uniqueness of argmins) should be stated and cited.  
- Quality: discretization is practical but lacks sensitivity analysis (M, sampling strategy); no regret or convergence guarantees provided.  
- Clarity: generally well-written, but clarify plot semantics, initial design sharing, and minor notation issues (Eq. 4.1).  
- Originality: reasonable incremental contribution—distinct q-KG acquisition and gradient form, but related IPA/q-EI work must be acknowledged.  
- Significance: promising for noisy batch HPO, but impact limited until wall-clock benefits and scaling to larger q are demonstrated.  
- Key author-action requests: compare explicitly to Wang et al. (2015) and Marmin et al. (2015), run ablations with identical discretization/optimizers, and report scaling with q, asynchrony potential, and wall-clock times.  
- Minor comments: fix grammar, correct citations for IPA and q-EI gradient work, clarify Eq. (4.1) notation, and report sensitivity to discretization and Monte Carlo sample size.  
- Pros summary: principled Bayes-optimal batch acquisition, strong noisy-performance, clear algorithm and public code.  
- Cons summary: limited novelty vs. prior q-acquisitions, missing wall-clock/time-to-solution analysis, fixed q=4, and some clarity/experimental fairness issues.  
- Overall evaluation: borderline reject—method is sound and promising, but paper needs wall-clock comparisons, q-scaling studies, and clearer novelty and controlled comparisons to merit acceptance.
