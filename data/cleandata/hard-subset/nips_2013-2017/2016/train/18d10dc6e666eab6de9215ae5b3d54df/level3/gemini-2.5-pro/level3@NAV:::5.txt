This paper proposes a batch Bayesian optimization method called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The method is derived from a decision-theoretic framework and aims to select a batch of points that is one-step Bayes-optimal. The primary technical contribution is an efficient Monte Carlo gradient estimator, based on Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), which allows for the optimization of the otherwise computationally intensive q-KG acquisition function. The authors present experiments on synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, demonstrating that their method improves upon other batch Bayesian optimization algorithms, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of batch Bayesian optimization, which has significant applications in parallel computing environments.
*   The q-KG method is theoretically well-motivated, extending the principles of the Knowledge Gradient to the batch setting to maximize the expected value of information from a parallel evaluation.
*   The development of an IPA-based gradient estimator is a valuable contribution that makes the optimization of the q-KG acquisition function practical.
*   The empirical evaluation is thorough, including comparisons on both synthetic benchmarks and challenging, real-world machine learning tasks. The demonstrated strength in noisy environments is a particularly significant result, as this is a common real-world scenario.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The claim of novelty for the batch KG method needs to be better substantiated. The generalization of KG to batch settings has been previously explored (e.g., Scott et al., 2011; Wang & Powell, 2016). The paper should more clearly position its contribution relative to this prior work. If the primary novelty lies in the gradient-based optimization approach for continuous domains, the authors should explicitly compare the efficiency and effectiveness of their proposed gradient estimator against computational methods used in previous batch KG literature.
*   **Clarity of Core Contribution:** Section 5.2, which details the IPA gradient estimator, is central to the paper's contribution but lacks sufficient detail. The derivation is highly condensed, making it difficult to fully assess the technical soundness and reproducibility of the method from the main text alone. This section should be expanded with more intuition and explanatory steps.
*   **Experimental Comparison:** The comparison against Expected Improvement (EI) in the noisy setting (Section 6.2) is potentially flawed. Standard EI is designed for noiseless observations. The authors must clarify if they compared against a noise-aware variant of EI. If not, the comparison is unfair, and the paper should include a discussion on how observation noise fundamentally affects different acquisition functions, which would further strengthen the claims about q-KG's advantages.

**Minor Issue:**
*   The abbreviation “IPA” is defined in the introduction and then redefined on line 203, which should be corrected for clarity.

In summary, this paper presents a promising and well-motivated approach for batch Bayesian optimization with strong empirical results. However, its acceptance is contingent on addressing significant concerns regarding the framing of its novelty, the clarity of its core technical section, and the fairness of its experimental comparisons.
