Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization algorithm, q-KG, that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to select q points per iteration. The method models f with a GP and chooses the next batch by maximizing the expected reduction in the posterior-predictive minimum of f’s mean after observing the batch. Because evaluating and optimizing the batch KG objective is intractable in closed form, the authors use a Monte Carlo reparameterization (sampling a standard normal Zq) and apply infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator, enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch. They further handle continuous domains by discretizing A adaptively at each iteration. Experiments on standard test functions and hyperparameter tuning (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10) show q-KG outperforms or competes with parallel EI variants and UCB-based batch policies, particularly under observation noise.

The paper is squarely grounded in the KG literature for sequential decisions (e.g., Frazier et al., 2008/2009; Scott et al., 2011), and relates to batch EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger, 2013; Marmin et al., 2015), parallel UCB/PE (Desautels et al., 2014; Contal et al., 2013), “constant liar” heuristics (Ginsbourger et al., 2010), and parallel entropy search (Shah & Ghahramani, 2015). The main contribution is extending the decision-theoretic KG criterion to the batch setting and making it computationally viable via MC+IPA.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation of q-KG as a one-step Bayes-optimal policy for minimizing minx µ(n)(x) in the batch setting is standard KG logic. The reparameterization µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z(1:q))Zq is correct, and using IPA to move the gradient through the Monte Carlo expectation is reasonable under differentiability and regularity assumptions.
- Assumptions and caveats: The “Bayes-optimal” claim strictly holds for a fixed GP with fixed hyperparameters and a finite set A; in practice the method discretizes A each iteration and re-fits hyperparameters by MLE, which breaks the strict one-step optimality guarantee. The unbiasedness of the IPA gradient assumes uniqueness of argmins and differentiability of µ and K; ties and non-smooth kernels warrant subgradient treatment. These caveats should be stated more explicitly.
- Computational considerations: Each q-KG evaluation requires a Cholesky on a q×q matrix (O(q^3)) and operations over the discretization An of size M; with S Monte Carlo samples, the per-evaluation cost is roughly O(S(q^3 + Mq)). This is acceptable for modest q and M but may become expensive as either grows. A short complexity discussion and the actual S, step sizes, and variance-reduction choices would help reproducibility.
- Empirics: The experiments are solid and align with the motivation: q-KG’s advantage is larger under noise. Including PPES in the comparison set would strengthen the case further, as would reporting sensitivity to M and S, and scaling results for larger q.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, with helpful placement of related work and clear algorithmic description. Code availability is a plus.
- Notation issues: Equation (3.1) is unclear as written. You define µ(x) and K(x1, x2), but then use µ(x(1:n)) and K(x(1:n), x(1:n)) where x(1:n) is a sequence. Please define X = [x(1), …, x(n)] ∈ A^n, µ(X) := [µ(x(1)), …, µ(x(n))]^T, and K(X,Y) as the matrix with (i, j)-entry K(x(i), y(j)). Also clarify diag{σ^2(X)}. This would avoid confusion between vectors, sets, and sequences. Similarly, overloading A in Eq. (4.1) and in the optimization domain for z(1:q) is confusing; consider renaming the discretized set used inside q-KG to S or An throughout.
- Please specify the number of MC samples used for evaluating q-KG and its gradient, whether you use common random numbers across iterations for variance reduction, and the optimization details (learning rates, restarts).

Originality
- The conceptual step—extending KG to batch selection—is natural and has appeared in related forms (e.g., nested batch KG on discrete sets), but this paper provides a clean continuous-domain treatment with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator. Using IPA for BO acquisition gradients has been used for q-EI; applying it to q-KG is a reasonable, if not surprising, adaptation. Overall, novelty is moderate: a thoughtful generalization and solid engineering more than a fundamentally new idea.

Significance
- Practical impact is likely: batch BO is common, and a decision-theoretic criterion that shines under noise is valuable. The empirical gains over UCB-based approaches and heuristic EI are convincing on the chosen tasks. If the method scales to higher dimensions and larger q with careful implementation, practitioners will adopt it, especially given the released code.

Specific comments and questions
- One-step optimality: Please qualify the Bayes-optimal claim to the discretized An and fixed hyperparameters; discuss how re-estimating hyperparameters affects the interpretation.
- Discretization: How sensitive are results to M and the way AMn is sampled? Could you leverage adaptive trust-region grids or local penalization instead?
- Gradient estimator: What S do you use for the objective vs. gradient? Do you use CRN and antithetic sampling? Any issues with non-unique argmins in practice?
- Baselines: Consider adding PPES (Shah & Ghahramani, 2015) and recent q-EI-gradient implementations as baselines.
- Scaling: Report wall-clock comparisons and scaling with q (e.g., q = 2, 4, 8, 16) and d.

Pros
- Clear decision-theoretic formulation; principled extension of KG to batches.
- Efficient MC+IPA gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise; code released.
- Well written and organized.

Cons
- Limited novelty: primarily a batch extension of KG with a standard IPA estimator.
- “Bayes-optimal” claim relies on discretization and fixed hyperparameters; needs qualification.
- Notation ambiguities (notably Eq. 3.1); overloaded symbols.
- Missing some relevant baselines; limited analysis of computational scaling and sensitivity.

Overall recommendation
A well-executed and practically useful contribution to batch BO. While the novelty is incremental, the method is carefully derived, competently implemented, and empirically strong, particularly in noisy settings. I lean toward acceptance after clarifying notation, qualifying claims, and expanding experimental comparisons/sensitivity.
