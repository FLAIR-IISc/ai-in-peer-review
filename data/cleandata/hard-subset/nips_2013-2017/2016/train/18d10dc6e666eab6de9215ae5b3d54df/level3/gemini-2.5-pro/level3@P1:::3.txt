This paper addresses the problem of batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), where the goal is to optimize an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function by selecting multiple points for parallel evaluation in each iteration. The authors propose a novel acquisition function, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which extends the decision-theoretic framework of the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to the batch setting. By construction, the q-KG acquisition function measures the expected improvement in the final solution quality from evaluating a batch of `q` points, making it a one-step Bayes-optimal policy.

Recognizing that the direct optimization of the q-KG acquisition function is computationally intractable, the core technical contribution of the paper is an efficient method for its maximization. The authors propose a Monte Carlo based approach to estimate the q-KG value. Crucially, they derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for this value using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). This allows the use of standard gradient-based optimizers to find the optimal batch of points. The paper's approach is positioned against other parallel BO methods, such as those based on Expected Improvement (EI) or Upper Confidence Bounds (UCB), noting that many of these construct batches greedily, whereas q-KG jointly optimizes the entire batch. The method is evaluated empirically on several synthetic benchmark functions and on hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression and convolutional neural networks, demonstrating performance that is competitive with or superior to existing methods, especially in noisy environments.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical motivation for q-KG as a one-step Bayes-optimal policy is sound and provides a strong foundation for the method. The derivation of the gradient estimator via IPA is correct and provides a practical path to implementation. The claims of the paper are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation. The authors test their method against several relevant baselines on a good mix of synthetic and real-world problems. The inclusion of both noise-free and noisy settings is a particular strength, as it highlights the robustness of the proposed method in a common practical scenario. The work is presented as a complete and usable algorithm.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for batch BO is clearly articulated, the proposed q-KG method is introduced logically, and the computational challenges and their solutions are explained in detail. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly. One minor point of confusion is the notation in Equation (3.1). The notation `x(1:n)` is used to represent the set of `n` previously evaluated points, but the mean and kernel functions `µ(x)` and `K(x1, x2)` are typically defined for single vector arguments. While the meaning is clear from context (e.g., `K(x(1:n), x(1:n))` refers to the Gram matrix), explicitly defining this notation would improve precision.

**Originality:** The primary contribution is the generalization of the KG framework to the batch BO setting. Formulating a joint, decision-theoretically optimal acquisition function for the entire batch is a novel and valuable contribution that moves beyond the more common greedy or heuristic batch construction strategies. However, a potential weakness lies in the originality of the constituent parts. The work is a direct extension of the established KG framework to a new setting (batch optimization), and the primary computational tool used, IPA for gradient estimation, is a well-known technique. Therefore, while the application and combination of these ideas are novel and effective, the work could be seen as an incremental, though important, contribution rather than a fundamentally new approach.

**Significance:** The paper has high practical significance. As parallel computing resources become more accessible, batch black-box optimization is an increasingly important problem, particularly for expensive tasks like hyperparameter tuning of deep neural networks. The proposed q-KG method provides a principled and effective tool for this problem. The strong empirical results, especially the superior performance in noisy settings, suggest that this algorithm could be of great interest to practitioners. The fact that the authors have made their code available further enhances the potential impact and likelihood that others will use and build upon this work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper tackles the important and practical problem of batch Bayesian Optimization.
2.  It proposes a principled, decision-theoretically motivated acquisition function (q-KG) that jointly optimizes the selection of all points in the batch, which is a conceptual advantage over many greedy heuristics.
3.  It develops an efficient and practical gradient-based optimization scheme using IPA, making the principled approach computationally feasible.
4.  It demonstrates strong empirical performance against several state-of-the-art benchmarks, with particularly notable gains in the realistic and challenging noisy evaluation setting.
5.  The paper is very clearly written and the contributions are well-communicated.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The novelty could be viewed as somewhat limited, as the work combines two existing and well-known concepts: the Knowledge Gradient policy and the Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) gradient estimator. While the application to batch BO is new, the core ideas themselves are not.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its practical relevance, sound theoretical motivation, strong empirical results, and high clarity—significantly outweigh the minor concern about the level of originality. It represents a solid and valuable contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature.
