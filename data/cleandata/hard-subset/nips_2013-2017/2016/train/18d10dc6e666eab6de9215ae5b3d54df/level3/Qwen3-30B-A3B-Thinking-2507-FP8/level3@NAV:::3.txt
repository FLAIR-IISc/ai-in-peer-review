

This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that extends the sequential Knowledge Gradient framework to batch selection. The authors develop an efficient computational strategy using Monte Carlo estimation combined with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to optimize the batch acquisition function, which would otherwise be computationally intractable. The method is evaluated on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, showing consistent performance improvements over existing batch Bayesian optimization approaches, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, with q-KG outperforming parallel EI, batch UCB, and other benchmarks on multiple test problems. The writing is clear and well-organized, and the practical utility of the method is well-justified through extensive experiments. The computational approach using IPA is sound and appropriately motivated.

However, the novelty of the work is somewhat limited. While the extension of KG to batch selection is conceptually straightforward, the paper does not substantially advance the underlying theoretical understanding beyond standard extensions of the KG framework. The core computational technique (IPA for gradient estimation) is well-established in the literature, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from related work such as [2, 5, 18, 25] that also addresses batch Bayesian optimization.

A specific technical concern is equation (3.1), where the authors define μ and K for vectors but apply them to sequences x^(1:n), creating notational confusion. This appears to be a fundamental issue that could undermine the mathematical foundations of the method.

Strengths: Well-written, strong empirical validation, practical relevance, clear problem statement. Weaknesses: Limited theoretical novelty, technical notational issue in equation (3.1), computational approach relies on standard techniques.

The paper makes a modest but useful contribution to the field of batch Bayesian optimization. While it does not represent a major theoretical advance, the practical benefits demonstrated in the experiments are significant, particularly for noisy optimization problems. The work is technically sound and well-executed, though the novelty is somewhat constrained. Given the practical utility and strong empirical results, I recommend acceptance, though the authors should address the notational issue in equation (3.1) and more clearly articulate how this work differs from related batch Bayesian optimization approaches.
