This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, for optimizing expensive-to-evaluate functions in parallel computing environments. The authors derive the q-KG method from a decision-theoretic analysis and develop an efficient strategy for computing the Bayes-optimal batch of points to sample. They demonstrate the effectiveness of q-KG in finding global optima significantly faster than previous batch Bayesian optimization algorithms on both synthetic test functions and real-world problems, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise background on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization. The q-KG algorithm is thoroughly explained, and the authors provide a detailed description of how to compute the q-KG factor and its gradient using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The experimental results are impressive, showing that q-KG outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art benchmark algorithms on several synthetic functions and real-world problems.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address the problem of parallel Bayesian optimization in a novel and effective way. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the q-KG method and demonstrate its effectiveness in a variety of settings. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough review of related work in the field.

However, there are some concerns and technical critiques that need to be addressed. One of the concerns is that the paper lacks wall-clock time measurements and comparisons to random search or sequential BO to show practical speedups. This is an important aspect of evaluating the effectiveness of a new algorithm, and the authors should provide more information on this topic.

Another concern is that the paper does not provide a clear justification for fixing the batch size q = 4 across all experiments. This is an important parameter that can affect the performance of the algorithm, and the authors should provide more information on why they chose this value and how it affects the results.

Additionally, the paper does not provide a clear comparison to other parallel Bayesian optimization algorithms, such as parallel EI or parallel UCB. The authors should provide more information on how q-KG differs from these algorithms and why it is more effective in certain settings.

In terms of novelty, the paper builds on previous work in the field, but the use of IPA to estimate gradients for acquisition maximization is a new contribution. However, the authors should provide more information on how their work differs from previous work in the field and why it is an important contribution.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a clear and concise explanation of the q-KG algorithm. However, there are some concerns and technical critiques that need to be addressed to make the paper more complete and effective.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that is effective in finding global optima significantly faster than previous algorithms.
* The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the q-KG method and demonstrate its effectiveness in a variety of settings.
* The paper is well-organized and well-written, making it easy to follow and understand.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks wall-clock time measurements and comparisons to random search or sequential BO to show practical speedups.
* The paper does not provide a clear justification for fixing the batch size q = 4 across all experiments.
* The paper does not provide a clear comparison to other parallel Bayesian optimization algorithms, such as parallel EI or parallel UCB.

Major questions:

* Have the authors compared their method to Wang et al. (2015) / parallel EI (q-EI), and can they clearly state the differences?
* How does the method scale to larger q (e.g., tens/hundreds), and what benefits are expected for more parallel runs?
* Can the synchronous method be extended to asynchronous parallel evaluations?
* What is the wall-clock performance vs random search or sequential BO, and what is the overhead to compute each new batch?
* Can the authors clarify the x-axis “iterations” in plots — batches or function evaluations, does it include initial design, and why do starting values differ across methods?
