- Proposes q-KG, a one-step Bayes-optimal batch acquisition that jointly selects q points to maximize expected final solution quality.
- Uses Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive a stochastic gradient estimator for optimizing the q-KG acquisition function.
- Extends sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) to the parallel/batch setting and connects to prior parallel BO work (EI, UCB, Entropy Search).
- Empirically competitive on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning; particularly strong in noisy settings.
- Addresses an important, practical problem (parallel hyperparameter optimization) with a principled decision-theoretic criterion.
- The method is theoretically well-motivated and clearly presented, with reproducible derivations and explanations.
- Experimental results are promising but the evaluation lacks critical practical details.
- No wall-clock time comparisons are reported, leaving unclear whether q-KG reduces total time-to-solution versus simpler baselines.
- All experiments use a fixed batch size q=4 without justification, so scalability with larger q is unknown.
- Key technical components (KG criterion and IPA) are not novel; similar IPA application to q-EI exists (Wang et al. 2015).
- Assumes synchronous parallel evaluations, limiting applicability to asynchronous real-world settings; no extension discussed.
- Review recommends clearer delineation of novelty relative to Wang et al. [25] and other prior work.
- Asks for explicit comparison and clarification of differences/advantages over q-EI (Wang et al. 2015).
- Requests experiments showing scalability of performance and computational cost for larger batch sizes (e.g., q=16, 32).
- Suggests exploring or describing an asynchronous extension for varied evaluation times.
- Requests wall-clock time measurements and per-batch selection overhead compared to parallel random search and sequential BO.
- Asks for clarification of plot x-axes (iterations = batches or evaluations) and why methods start at different initial performance.
- Notes minor typographical and citation issues (notation typos, grammar, mixed citations, and IPA attribution).
