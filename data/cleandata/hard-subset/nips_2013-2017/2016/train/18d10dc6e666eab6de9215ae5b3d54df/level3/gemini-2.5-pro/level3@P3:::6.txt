### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization (BO), called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective and aims to select a batch of `q` points that is one-step Bayes-optimal, maximizing the expected improvement in the final solution quality. To overcome the computational challenge of optimizing this acquisition function over a batch of points, the authors leverage infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator, enabling the use of efficient gradient-based optimization methods. The approach is evaluated on several synthetic benchmark functions and two hyperparameter optimization (HPO) tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), comparing its performance against other parallel BO methods like parallel Expected Improvement and parallel UCB variants. The results suggest that q-KG is competitive in noise-free settings and particularly strong in noisy settings. This work extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) policy to the parallel setting, a valuable contribution to the BO literature.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The problem of parallelizing BO is highly relevant for the machine learning community. With the increasing availability of multi-core/multi-machine computing environments, methods that can efficiently leverage parallel resources for expensive tasks like HPO are of great practical importance. This paper addresses a key need in the field.

2.  **Principled Approach:** The q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated from a decision-theoretic standpoint, seeking the one-step Bayes-optimal batch of points. This provides a strong theoretical foundation compared to more heuristic approaches for constructing batches.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and organized. The background on Gaussian processes is concise, and the derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and its gradient estimation via IPA is described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the technical approach. The experimental setup is also clearly explained.

4.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The experiments, which cover a range of synthetic and real-world problems, demonstrate that q-KG is a competitive method. Its reported superior performance on noisy problems is particularly noteworthy, as many real-world black-box optimization tasks involve stochastic evaluations.

**Weaknesses and Concerns**

Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that temper my enthusiasm and leave me unconvinced of the practical utility and novelty of the proposed method in its current form.

1.  **Novelty in Context of Prior Work:** While extending KG to the parallel setting is a contribution, the core technical machinery is not new. Specifically, the use of IPA to compute gradients for a Monte Carlo-based parallel acquisition function is very similar to the approach used for parallel Expected Improvement (q-EI) in Wang et al. (2015). The paper cites this work but fails to adequately discuss the relationship and clearly delineate its novel contributions beyond applying the same technique to a different (KG) acquisition function. This makes it difficult to assess the paper's originality.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evidence is not fully convincing due to several key omissions:
    *   **Lack of Wall-Clock Time:** The primary motivation for parallel BO is to reduce the total time to find a good solution. The experiments only report performance versus the number of function evaluations (or "iterations"). This ignores the computational overhead of the BO algorithm itself, which for q-KG involves Monte Carlo sampling and gradient-based optimization. Without wall-clock time comparisons to strong baselines like sequential BO and parallel random search, it's impossible to know if the method provides a real-world speedup.
    *   **Fixed Batch Size:** All experiments are conducted with a fixed batch size of `q=4`. No justification is provided for this choice, nor is there any analysis of how the method's performance (both in terms of sample efficiency and computational overhead) scales with `q`. This is a critical question for a parallel method intended for modern computing environments where `q` could be much larger.
    *   **Unclear Plots:** The x-axis in the plots is labeled "iterations," which is ambiguous. It should be clarified whether this refers to the number of batches or the cumulative number of function evaluations. Furthermore, in several plots (e.g., Figure 3), the performance of different methods at the first iteration appears to differ, suggesting they might not have started from the same initial design, which would be a flaw in the experimental protocol.

3.  **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses on a synchronous batch setting, where the algorithm waits for all `q` evaluations in a batch to complete. This is often inefficient in practice, as evaluation times can be heterogeneous. A discussion of how the method might be extended to the more practical asynchronous setting would have significantly strengthened the paper.

**Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify the points above, I would appreciate responses to the following questions:

1.  Could you please elaborate on the novelty of your work compared to Wang et al. (2015)? Given that they also use IPA to optimize a Monte Carlo-based batch acquisition function (q-EI), what are the key technical or conceptual differences in the gradient estimation and optimization procedure, beyond the choice of the acquisition function itself?

2.  How does the method's performance and computational overhead scale as the batch size `q` increases into the tens or hundreds? What are the computational bottlenecks, and is the method practical for massively parallel scenarios?

3.  Could the proposed synchronous method be extended to an asynchronous setting, where new points are chosen as soon as a worker becomes free? What challenges would this entail?

4.  What is the typical wall-clock time overhead for computing a batch of points with q-KG? How does the total time-to-solution compare against sequential BO and parallel random search?

5.  In the experimental plots, does the x-axis "iterations" refer to batches or cumulative function evaluations? Why do the methods appear to start with different performance values after the initial design?

**Minor Comments**

*   Eq. (4.1): The conditioning in the expectation should probably be on `x(1:n), y(1:n)` and the sampling decision `z(1:q)`, not `y(z(1:q))`. Please clarify.
*   Section 6.1.2: "achieve a test error around 14%, and our automatic algorithm improves it to around 11%." Should be "achieves".
*   The phrasing "in [2, 19, 25]" is slightly awkward. It's better to integrate citations into the sentence structure.
*   Wang et al. (2015) should not be credited with introducing IPA, which is a much older technique (e.g., L'Ecuyer, 1990). The citation should be more precise about their contribution.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces a principled and interesting method for batch Bayesian optimization. However, its contributions are not sufficiently distinguished from prior work, and the experimental evaluation is missing key components (wall-clock time, analysis of `q`) needed to demonstrate practical value. While the idea is promising, the paper in its current state feels incomplete. A major revision that addresses the questions regarding novelty, experimental rigor, and scalability would be required for me to recommend acceptance.
