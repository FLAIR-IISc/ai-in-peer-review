This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO). The core idea is to extend the single-point Knowledge Gradient policy to a batch setting, aiming to select a set of points that are Bayes-optimal in the average-case for a single remaining batch evaluation. To address the computational challenges, the authors develop an infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) based approach for gradient estimation, enabling efficient optimization of the q-KG acquisition function. Empirical results on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning demonstrate superior performance compared to existing batch BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios.

The paper builds upon a rich history of work in Bayesian Optimization, starting with foundational work on Gaussian Processes (GPs) [16] and acquisition functions like Expected Improvement (EI) [13] and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) [21].  Recent years have seen a surge of interest in BO for machine learning hyperparameter tuning [8, 9, 19, 22], motivating the extension to parallel evaluation settings [3, 5, 18, 25].  Previous parallel BO approaches have largely focused on iteratively constructing batches using greedy strategies or extensions of single-point acquisition functions. This work differentiates itself by explicitly considering the batch as a whole and formulating a globally optimal selection criterion.  However, it’s crucial to contextualize this contribution relative to existing work on batch KG.  Specifically, the authors need to more clearly position their work with respect to related works like [26], which also explore batch KG policies, and [1], [2] which propose other parallel batch methods.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical grounding and its attempt to address a practically relevant problem—efficient batch BO, especially in noisy environments. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function based on decision-theoretic principles is a solid contribution. The development of the IPA-based gradient estimator is also a key strength, providing a pathway to scale the method to higher-dimensional problems. The empirical results, while not overwhelming, consistently demonstrate q-KG’s competitive performance, particularly when noise is present.  The code availability is also a significant positive aspect.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The primary concern is the lack of sufficient detail regarding the computational aspects of the proposed method, specifically Section 5.  The description of the IPA-based gradient estimation is rather high-level, making it difficult to assess its practical efficiency and robustness. More detailed explanations of the derivations and implementations are needed.  Specifically, the reviewer correctly points out that Section 5.2 is central to the paper’s contribution, and lacks sufficient elaboration. A discussion on the computational cost, memory requirements, and sensitivity to numerical stability would be valuable.  Further, a comparison of the IPA-based gradient estimator with alternative approaches (e.g., finite difference methods) would strengthen the argument for its effectiveness.

Another critical point is the comparison with Expected Improvement (EI).  As the reviewer notes, standard EI assumes noiseless observations, while the experiments involve noisy evaluations. The authors need to clarify whether a noise-aware variant of EI was used for comparison, and if not, explain how the noise affects the results and the fairness of the comparison.  Simply stating that q-KG performs better in noisy settings isn’t enough; a more nuanced discussion of the impact of noise on different acquisition functions is needed.

Finally, while the empirical results are positive, they lack a comprehensive analysis of the sensitivity of the algorithm to its hyperparameters. A sensitivity study would provide valuable insights into the robustness of q-KG and its applicability to different problem settings. The reviewer also correctly identifies a minor issue with the inconsistent use of the IPA abbreviation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel batch BO method based on a sound theoretical foundation.
*   The proposed IPA-based gradient estimator offers a potentially efficient solution to a challenging computational problem.
*   The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance, particularly in noisy settings.
*   The code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the computational aspects of the proposed method.
*   The comparison with EI is potentially misleading due to the noise assumption.
*   The empirical analysis is limited and lacks a sensitivity study.
*   The positioning relative to other batch KG methods could be more precise.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to batch Bayesian Optimization. However, the lack of sufficient detail and the limitations in the empirical analysis prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit, addressing the concerns outlined above. Specifically, the authors must significantly expand Section 5, clarify the comparison with EI, and provide a more thorough empirical evaluation.  Addressing these issues will substantially strengthen the paper and increase its contribution to the field.
