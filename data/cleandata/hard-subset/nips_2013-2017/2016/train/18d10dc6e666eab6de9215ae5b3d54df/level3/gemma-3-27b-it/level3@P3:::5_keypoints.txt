- Proposes q-KG: a parallel batch Bayesian optimization method that directly optimizes a decision-theoretic acquisition function.  
- Core idea: select a batch maximizing the expected incremental value of a measurement, extending sequential Knowledge Gradient to batches.  
- Key technical contribution: an IPA-based gradient estimator to make batch acquisition optimization tractable.  
- Empirical results: consistent improvements over benchmarks on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks.  
- Notable strength: particularly strong performance in noisy problem settings.  
- Well-positioned within BO literature, contrasting with parallel EI, UCB, and Entropy Search approaches.  
- Strength: rigorous decision-theoretic derivation and sound technical foundation.  
- Strength: code provided, aiding reproducibility.  
- Weakness: novelty concerns — needs clearer distinction from prior parallel KG work (e.g., [26]) and other parallel acquisition methods.  
- Weakness: Section 5.2 (gradient estimation) lacks sufficient detail in the main text and relies too much on supplements.  
- Weakness: unclear whether comparisons to EI used a noise-aware EI variant; the EI comparison may be flawed.  
- Weakness: minor typo (redefinition of “IPA”) needs fixing.  
- Quality: technically sound overall, but clarity and comparison gaps weaken reproducibility and claims.  
- Clarity: generally well-written, but mathematical derivations and Section 5.2 require more explanation.  
- Originality: extension to parallel KG plus the gradient estimator contributes novelty, but clearer contrasts to prior work are needed.  
- Significance: promising advance for parallel BO, especially for noisy, real-world hyperparameter tuning.  
- Acceptance rationale: strong empirical results, solid theory, practical relevance, and available code.  
- Rejection concerns: novelty questions, insufficient methodological detail, and potentially invalid EI comparisons.  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions focused on expanding Section 5.2, strengthening comparisons to related parallel KG and EI methods, and clarifying implementation details.
