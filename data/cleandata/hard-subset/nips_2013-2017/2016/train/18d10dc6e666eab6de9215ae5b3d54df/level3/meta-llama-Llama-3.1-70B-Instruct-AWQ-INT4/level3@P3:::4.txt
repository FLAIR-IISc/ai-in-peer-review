This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), which outputs q points and provides an efficient way to evaluate it. The method tackles an important problem in speeding up expensive function evaluations via distributed/batch evaluation. The idea of proposing q points at each iteration is interesting and can substantially improve efficiency.

The authors provide a thorough background on Gaussian processes and define the notation used later in the paper. They also review related work in Bayesian optimization, highlighting the differences between their approach and existing methods. The q-KG algorithm is derived using a decision-theoretic analysis, and the authors provide an efficient strategy for computing the Bayes-optimal batch of points.

The paper presents empirical results on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning algorithms, demonstrating that q-KG finds global optima significantly faster than previous batch Bayesian optimization algorithms, especially when function evaluations are noisy. The authors also provide a detailed description of their implementation and compare it to open-source implementations of benchmark methods.

The paper's strengths include its solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, its sound method, and convincing experiments. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and results. The use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of q-KG is a notable contribution, as it allows for efficient optimization of the acquisition function.

However, there are some minor issues and typos that need to be addressed. For example, on line 36, the grammar is incorrect ("the set of points to evaluate next that is"). Additionally, the authors should define A on line 95 and clarify the input of mu^n on line 104. On line 128, the authors should briefly discuss parallel EI, and on line 140, they should avoid reusing the notation A.

One concern is that assuming a Gaussian process prior may be inappropriate for high-dimensional inputs such as neural network architectures. The authors should provide more discussion on the limitations of their approach and potential extensions to address this issue.

Furthermore, the authors should provide theoretical results, including error bounds for the algorithm and a quantitative complexity analysis with respect to input dimension. This would strengthen the paper's contribution and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the q-KG algorithm's performance.

In terms of originality, the paper's contribution is significant, as it proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that addresses an important problem in the field. The idea of proposing q points at each iteration is new and can substantially improve efficiency. The paper's results are also novel, as they demonstrate the effectiveness of the q-KG algorithm on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning algorithms.

In terms of significance, the paper's results are important, as they demonstrate the potential of batch Bayesian optimization in speeding up expensive function evaluations. The paper's contribution can be applied to various fields, including machine learning, where Bayesian optimization is widely used for hyperparameter tuning.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and results. The q-KG algorithm is a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, and the paper's results demonstrate its effectiveness on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning algorithms.
