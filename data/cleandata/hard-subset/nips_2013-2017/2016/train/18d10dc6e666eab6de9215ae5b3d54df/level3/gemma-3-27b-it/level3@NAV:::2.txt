## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization, aiming to select Bayes-optimal batches of points for parallel evaluation. The core idea of maximizing information gain through a novel acquisition function (q-KG) is a valuable theoretical contribution, building upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient framework and extending it effectively to the parallel setting. The authors demonstrate a computationally efficient method for optimizing this acquisition function using infinitesimal perturbation analysis. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, making it easy to follow the technical details. 

The strength of this work lies in its theoretical foundation and elegant approach to parallelization. Determining Bayes-optimal batches is a significant advancement, and the IPA technique provides a pragmatic solution to the computational challenges. The paper appropriately situates the contribution within the existing literature on parallel BO, contrasting it with approaches like parallel EI and UCB. 

However, the empirical evaluation is a significant weakness. Using test set error as the optimization criterion for hyperparameter tuning is problematic. Metrics like Area Under the Curve (AUC) or log loss are far more suitable for evaluating performance, particularly in the early stages of optimization where test set error can be highly variable. Furthermore, the experimental benchmarks are limited to MNIST and CIFAR10 with only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and a CNN). This narrow scope hinders the generalizability of the findings. A broader evaluation using HPOlib or other comprehensive HPO benchmarks would considerably strengthen the paper. Given the task and algorithm variation in real-world HPO scenarios, assessing the method's performance across a wider range of datasets and algorithms is crucial.

The assumption of independent normally distributed observation noise is also a potential limitation, as structural bias and correlated errors are common in HPO settings (e.g., due to cross-validation). While acknowledged, its potential impact isn’t thoroughly explored.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical grounding and elegant solution to parallel BO.
*   Valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Weak empirical evaluation with a suboptimal optimization criterion.
*   Limited benchmark datasets and algorithms.
*   Potentially unrealistic noise assumption.



**Overall:** Despite its theoretical merits, the limited empirical validation raises concerns about the practical significance of the proposed method. I recommend **acceptance with major revisions**, contingent on addressing the weaknesses in the experimental setup and providing a more convincing demonstration of the algorithm's performance on a broader set of HPO problems.
