This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel approach to batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) designed for scenarios where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The core idea is to extend the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to a batch setting, aiming to select a set of points that maximizes the expected incremental value of a single batch evaluation. The authors provide a computationally efficient strategy for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function, leveraging infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). Empirical results on synthetic test functions and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms existing batch BO algorithms, especially in noisy evaluation settings.

The paper builds upon the extensive body of work in Bayesian Optimization, notably the foundational work of Jones et al. (1998) and the subsequent development of various acquisition functions like Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB). Recent efforts have focused on extending BO to parallel evaluation settings, as highlighted in the related work section. The authors correctly identify and build upon previous parallelization strategies such as those based on parallel EI (Ginsbourger et al., 2010) and parallel UCB (Contal et al., 2014). The key innovation lies in the q-KG formulation, which, by construction, aims for Bayes-optimality in a single batch setting. This differentiates it from iterative greedy approaches that sequentially add points based on single-evaluation acquisition functions. 

The strengths of this work are considerable. The theoretical foundation is sound, deriving the q-KG criterion from a decision-theoretic perspective. The efficient computation of q-KG’s gradient, enabled by IPA, is a significant contribution, making the method practically viable. The empirical validation is thorough, demonstrating consistent performance gains over existing methods on a diverse set of problems, including both synthetic benchmarks and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks. The inclusion of noisy evaluation scenarios further strengthens the results, as q-KG exhibits particularly strong performance in these cases. The availability of the code is also a significant positive aspect.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the performance improvements are demonstrated, a more detailed analysis of the speedups achieved through parallelization would be valuable. The paper focuses on improving the *quality* of the batch, but doesn’t explicitly quantify the *time* saved by leveraging parallel evaluations.  The related work section, while extensive, contains some imprecisions.  The authors state that integrating EI with respect to the posterior and the “Constant Liar” approach are novel, but these were introduced in Ginsbourger et al. (2010).  Furthermore, the work overlooks relevant contributions such as Chevalier et al. (2016), which discusses the CL-mix strategy and natural-gradient maximization for q-EI.  

Several clarifying questions remain. The paper assumes a Gaussian process prior but doesn’t explicitly state whether the domain *A* is assumed to be compact, or whether the function *f* is assumed to be continuous. These assumptions are important for the theoretical guarantees of Gaussian process regression.  The use of a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) for the initial design is a choice, and the rationale for this specific sampling strategy could be elaborated upon.  Algorithm 1 mentions re-training the GP hyperparameters, but the frequency and mechanism for this Bayesian updating are not fully detailed.  A clarification is also needed regarding the definition of *g* in Section 5.2 – is the minimization performed within *g*? 

There are also some technical concerns. The smoothness of *g* in Section 5.2 isn’t immediately obvious and could benefit from further justification.  Similarly, the differentiability of the Cholesky factor used in the derivations requires careful consideration.  Finally, a minor point: the Matérn parameter is stated as 2/5 but should be 5/2.

The experimental section would be strengthened by exploring the reproducibility of the results. Specifically, could the GPs used in Figure 1 be fitted using different software implementations for verification?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to batch Bayesian Optimization.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
*   Efficient computational strategy for maximizing the acquisition function.
*   Thorough empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world problems.
*   Availability of code for reproducibility.
*   Potential for significant impact in areas relying on expensive black-box optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Minor imprecisions and omissions in the related work section.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of speedups from parallelization.
*   Some clarifying questions regarding assumptions and algorithmic details.
*   Technical concerns regarding smoothness and differentiability.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The q-KG method offers a principled and efficient approach to batch evaluation, demonstrating promising results in various settings. The potential impact of this work is significant, particularly in areas where parallelizing expensive function evaluations is crucial. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarifying questions and strengthen the related work section.
