This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), aiming to select Bayes-optimal batches of points for parallel evaluation. It builds upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, extending it to the parallel setting and providing an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy for the resulting acquisition function. The work is positioned within the growing field of parallel BO, addressing the need for algorithms that effectively leverage parallel function evaluations, particularly relevant in expensive applications like hyperparameter tuning. Existing parallel BO methods often rely on iterative greedy approaches or UCB-based strategies, whereas q-KG directly optimizes a batch selection criterion based on information gain.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. The motivation is strong, the theoretical background is concisely and accurately summarized, and the derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is logically structured. The introduction of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable efficient gradient computation is a clever and elegant solution to a significant computational challenge. The theoretical analysis appears solid, justifying the approach and providing insights into its behavior.

A major strength of this work lies in its theoretical contribution: framing batch selection as a decision-theoretic problem and pursuing the Bayes-optimal batch is a valuable and promising direction. The q-KG method represents a principled approach to parallel BO, going beyond simple extensions of sequential methods. The algorithm’s construction, aiming for optimal information gain, is intuitively appealing and theoretically sound.

However, the empirical evaluation significantly weakens the paper's overall impact. The primary concern is the use of test set error as the optimization criterion in the hyperparameter tuning experiments. This is a known suboptimal practice in BO – threshold-based metrics like Area Under the Curve (AUC) or log loss are generally preferred, as they provide a smoother and more informative signal for the optimizer, particularly in early stages. Optimizing directly for test set error can lead to overfitting to the validation set and unstable optimization trajectories.

Furthermore, the experimental benchmarks are limited. While MNIST and CIFAR10 are standard datasets, the lack of broader HPO benchmarks such as HPOlib hinders generalizability claims. The method is evaluated on only two datasets and with only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). This narrow scope raises concerns about whether the observed performance gains will hold across different tasks and algorithm classes. Optimizers are task-dependent, and a more diverse set of algorithms would provide a more robust assessment of q-KG's effectiveness.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and elegant solution to parallel BO.
*   Novel approach to batch selection based on Bayes-optimality.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Efficient gradient computation via IPA.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Weak empirical evaluation due to suboptimal optimization criterion (test set error).
*   Limited experimental benchmarks (only two datasets and two algorithms).
*   Lack of broader generalization tests across different tasks and optimizers.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its theoretical strengths and elegant approach, the limited and potentially misleading empirical evaluation prevents a strong endorsement. The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution, but the lack of convincing empirical evidence undermines its practical impact. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to address the concerns regarding the experimental evaluation. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Re-evaluate the algorithms using more appropriate metrics (AUC, log loss) for hyperparameter optimization.
2.  Expand the benchmark suite to include more diverse datasets and learning algorithms, ideally leveraging existing HPO benchmark libraries.
3.  Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters within q-KG itself.

Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and demonstrate the true potential of the proposed q-KG method. As it stands, while promising, the empirical results do not convincingly support the claims of superior performance.
