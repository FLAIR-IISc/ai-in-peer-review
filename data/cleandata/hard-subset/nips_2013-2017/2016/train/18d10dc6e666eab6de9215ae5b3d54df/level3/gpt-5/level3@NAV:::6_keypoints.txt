The paper proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization method that finds the Bayes-optimal parallel batch under a one-step lookahead and optimizes its acquisition via stochastic gradients estimated with IPA.  
q-KG evaluates the posterior-mean minimum on a discretized candidate set and uses Monte Carlo to evaluate and optimize the acquisition.  
Experiments on synthetic benchmarks, MNIST logistic-regression HPO, and CIFAR-10 CNN HPO show q-KG is competitive in noiseless settings and outperforms prior methods under noise.  
The method is related to sequential KG, multi-point EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger), gradient-based q-EI (Wang et al., 2015/MOE), and PPES.  
Strength: decision-theoretic formulation is sound and the IPA-based gradient estimator is reasonable.  
Weakness: empirical evaluation lacks wall-clock runtime analyses and key baselines (random search, sequential BO).  
Weakness: only q=4 is tested, leaving scalability to larger batch sizes unexamined.  
Major missing comparison: direct empirical and methodological comparison to q-EI/Wang et al. (2015) is absent or underdeveloped.  
Clarity: background and algorithm description are generally clear, but some figure axes and initialization choices are unclear.  
Originality: combining KG with IPA for batches is a natural/ incremental extension; novelty over IPA-based q-EI is limited.  
Significance: parallel HPO is impactful; q-KG could be useful if computational overhead is modest and it scales to larger q, especially in noisy settings.  
Major question: include direct comparisons to Wang et al. (2015)/q-EI and clarify methodological differences.  
Major question: provide theoretical or empirical guidance on scalability as batch size q grows into the tens/hundreds.  
Major question: discuss whether q-KG can be extended to asynchronous evaluations and what modifications are required.  
Major question: report practical wall-clock runtimes versus random search and sequential BO, including per-batch optimization overhead.  
Major question: clarify plot axes—whether “iterations” count batches or function evaluations, inclusion of initial design, and why starting values differ across methods.  
Pros: clear decision-theoretic grounding and strong performance under noise.  
Pros: addresses a timely and practically relevant problem for multi-core/GPU settings.  
Pros: generally well-written and reproducible.  
Cons: missing key baselines and runtime evidence, and unclear scalability.  
Cons: limited novelty relative to existing IPA-based batch EI methods.  
Minor: make conditioning in Eq. (4.1) explicit and clarify candidate-set notation.  
Minor: fix grammar (e.g., “achieve” → “achieves”).  
Minor: revise citations—do not attribute IPA origin to Wang et al. (2015); cite foundational IPA work (e.g., L’Ecuyer, 1990).
