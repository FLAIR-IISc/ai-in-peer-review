**Summary:**
This paper introduces a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, termed the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The method extends the decision-theoretic motivation of the sequential Knowledge Gradient to the batch setting, where the goal is to select a set of `q` points that jointly maximizes the expected improvement in the optimal value of the posterior mean. The primary technical contribution is the development of a Monte Carlo gradient estimator for the q-KG acquisition function, leveraging Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). This enables the use of efficient gradient-based methods to find the optimal batch of points to evaluate. Through experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, the authors show that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms other state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization methods, particularly in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of batch (or parallel) Bayesian optimization.
*   The proposed q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated from a decision-theoretic perspective, defining a one-step Bayes-optimal policy for improving the final recommended solution.
*   The use of a stochastic gradient-based approach to optimize the otherwise intractable q-KG acquisition function is a valuable contribution that makes the method practical.
*   The empirical results presented are promising, showing strong performance relative to several baselines, especially in noisy environments, which are common in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed to solidify its contribution.

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The claim of proposing a "novel batch BO method" needs more careful framing. The concept of a batch Knowledge Gradient policy has been explored before (e.g., [26] cited in the paper). While the authors distinguish their work from [26] by highlighting the continuous domain and a more efficient computation, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough review and comparison with prior batch KG formulations. It is not entirely clear whether the primary novelty lies in the specific formulation of the q-KG factor or in the gradient-based method for optimizing it. A clearer positioning against existing work is needed to properly assess the paper's originality.

2.  **Insufficient Detail in Core Technical Section:** Section 5.2, which describes the gradient estimator, is the technical core of the paper but lacks sufficient detail. The derivation is almost entirely deferred to the supplementary materials, which makes it difficult for a reader to understand and verify the central contribution from the main paper alone. The authors should:
    *   Briefly state the regularity conditions on the GP's mean and kernel functions required to justify the interchange of the expectation and differentiation operators (Eq. 5.3), which is fundamental to the IPA estimator.
    *   Provide a high-level sketch of the derivation of the gradient of `g`. As it stands, the presentation is too much of a "black box," undermining the paper's clarity and completeness.

3.  **Evaluation of the Proposed Gradient Estimator:** A key claim is that the proposed IPA-based estimator allows for *efficient* optimization of q-KG. However, this claim is not directly substantiated. The paper would benefit from an analysis comparing this estimator to other potential optimization strategies for batch KG. For instance, how does its computational cost and the quality of the solution found compare to the non-gradient-based method used in [26] or other gradient-free optimization methods? This would provide concrete evidence for the superiority of the proposed computational approach.

4.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison to Expected Improvement (EI):** The superior performance of q-KG in the noisy experiments (Section 6.2) is a major result, but the comparison to EI is questionable. Standard EI is formulated for noise-free settings, measuring improvement over the best *observed* function value. KG, in contrast, naturally handles noise by focusing on the improvement of the posterior *mean*.
    *   The paper must clarify which variant of parallel EI was used. Was it a properly noise-aware EI that compares against the current estimate of the optimum (e.g., `min µ(n)(x)`)?
    *   If a standard EI was used, its underperformance in a noisy setting is expected, and the comparison may be unfair. The authors should include a more nuanced discussion of how noise fundamentally affects the behavior of EI versus KG and ensure the baseline is as strong as possible. Comparing against a state-of-the-art noise-aware EI variant would make the empirical results far more convincing.

**Minor Issue:**
*   The abbreviation “IPA” (infinitesimal perturbation analysis) is introduced in the contributions paragraph of the introduction and then defined again in Section 5.2. It should be defined only at its first use.
