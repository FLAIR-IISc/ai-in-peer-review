Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select a set of q points by maximizing the expected single-iteration reduction in the posterior mean minimum. The approach is Bayes-optimal for the one-step setting, in the sense of minimizing the expected minimum of the posterior mean after one batch. Because exact evaluation is intractable in the batch and continuous-domain setting, the paper proposes: (i) a Monte Carlo estimator of the acquisition via a representation of the updated posterior mean as an affine function of Gaussian noise, (ii) an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based optimization over the batch, and (iii) a practical discretization scheme of the candidate objective-domain for evaluating the inner minimum (drawing samples from the posterior optima distribution and augmenting with previously sampled and candidate points). Empirically, q-KG is compared to parallel EI variants and parallel UCB-style methods on synthetic functions and two hyperparameter optimization (HPO) problems (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), with stronger improvements reported under observation noise.

Relation to prior work is appropriate: the paper positions itself relative to parallel EI (including closed-form and gradient-based treatments), GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE, and parallel entropy search (PES). The novelty lies in extending KG’s decision-theoretic objective to joint batch selection with an efficient gradient estimator, contrasting with prior greedy constructions and EI/ES-based joint criteria.

Quality
- Theory/algorithmic soundness: The derivation of q-KG as the expected one-step improvement in the posterior mean minimum is sound and clearly relates to sequential KG. The IPA-based stochastic gradient is a technically solid contribution; however, its unbiasedness relies on differentiability and (effectively) uniqueness of minimizers with probability one—please clarify tie-breaking and the impact of non-differentiability at kinks in the min operator. The discretization strategy is standard but introduces approximation error; a sensitivity or convergence discussion would strengthen the work.
- Assumptions: The observation model assumes independent Gaussian noise with known or MLE-estimated variance. In HPO, noise is often heteroscedastic and correlated (e.g., from cross-validation folds, early stopping, or shared data subsampling), violating independence and Gaussianity. Discuss robustness to mis-specification and whether q-KG can incorporate general noise covariance (e.g., via a GP noise model or repeated evaluations).
- Empirical evaluation: While synthetic results are thorough, the HPO evaluation is limited: only two datasets and two learners, and the objective uses test-set error during the optimization loop. Optimizing on the test set is problematic; a validation metric should be optimized, with final evaluation on an untouched test set. Moreover, 0-1 error is a threshold-based, non-proper scoring rule; AUC or (regularized) log loss would be preferable for probabilistic models. Runtime/overhead comparisons against parallel EI and UCB baselines are missing; given the Monte Carlo and gradient computations, wall-clock efficiency is important in batch BO.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and easy to follow. Notational clarity could be improved by avoiding overloading A for both the optimization domain and the discretized set used inside q-KG; consider distinct symbols (e.g., X for the domain and S for the discretized set). Provide concrete values for: number of Monte Carlo samples for acquisition and gradient estimates, batch sizes explored, number of restarts, and stopping criteria for the inner optimizer. A brief complexity analysis (as a function of q, |An|, and MC samples) would aid practitioners.

Originality
Extending KG to the batch setting with a practical, unbiased gradient estimator is novel and nontrivial. While parallel acquisition functions (q-EI, q-PES) exist, the decision-theoretic KG objective differs materially and the IPA-based optimization is an elegant contribution. The connection that q-KG recovers parallel EI in the noiseless, restricted-candidate case is insightful.

Significance
Selecting Bayes-optimal batches is a valuable objective with practical relevance in parallel compute environments. If robust and efficient, q-KG could see adoption in HPO and simulation optimization, particularly under noisy evaluations where the paper shows the largest gains. However, the empirical scope (two HPO tasks/datasets) and metric choice limit the strength of the claim. Broader benchmarks (e.g., HPOlib tasks across SVM/RF/NNs, additional image/text datasets, and nonstationary/heteroscedastic settings) and runtime analyses would substantially increase impact.

Pros
- Clear, principled extension of KG to batch BO with a well-motivated objective.
- Technically strong use of IPA to obtain unbiased gradients for joint batch optimization.
- Solid connections to and distinctions from prior parallel EI/ES work; recovers q-EI in a special case.
- Consistent gains on synthetic problems; especially strong under noise.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation for HPO is too narrow (two datasets, two learners) and uses test-set error as the optimization target.
- Metric choice (0-1 error) is suboptimal; lacks results on proper scoring metrics.
- Assumes independent Gaussian noise; no study of robustness to correlated/heteroscedastic noise common in HPO.
- No runtime/compute overhead comparisons versus baselines; scalability with large q or |An| not quantified.
- Discretization sensitivity (M, construction of An) and stability of the method are not analyzed.

Questions for the authors
1. What are the MC sample sizes for estimating q-KG and its gradient, and how sensitive are results to these budgets? Any variance-reduction techniques used?
2. How does wall-clock time compare to q-EI/q-PES/GP-BUCB for the same q and dimensionality? Please report per-iteration overhead and scaling with q.
3. How robust is q-KG to noise mis-specification and correlated noise? Can the framework incorporate non-diagonal noise covariances?
4. How sensitive are results to the discretization size M and the construction of An? Any guidance for practitioners?
5. In HPO, can you re-run optimizing a validation metric (e.g., log loss) and report a separate held-out test performance?

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace test-set optimization with validation-set optimization; report final test performance. Include AUC/log loss where applicable.
- Expand HPO benchmarks (e.g., HPOlib), add more datasets/algorithms, and include nonstationary or heteroscedastic cases.
- Provide runtime scalability plots and ablations (q, M, MC budget).
- Clarify assumptions for IPA unbiasedness and tie-breaking; add a short complexity analysis.
- Disambiguate notation for the domain vs. discretized evaluation set.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper’s theoretical and algorithmic contributions are strong and potentially impactful, particularly under noisy evaluations. However, the empirical section should be broadened and corrected (validation vs. test, metrics, runtime) to fully substantiate the claims.
