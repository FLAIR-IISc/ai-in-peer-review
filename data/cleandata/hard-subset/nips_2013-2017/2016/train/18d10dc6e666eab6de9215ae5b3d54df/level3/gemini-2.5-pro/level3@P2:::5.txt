This paper proposes a new method for batch Bayesian optimization called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective and aims to select a batch of `q` points that is one-step Bayes-optimal. Specifically, it seeks to maximize the expected improvement in the final recommended solution (the minimum of the posterior GP mean) after observing the outcomes of the batch evaluation. As direct optimization of the q-KG acquisition function is computationally challenging, the authors' main technical contribution is a method for efficiently computing an unbiased stochastic estimate of its gradient using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). This allows the use of gradient-based methods to find the optimal batch of points. The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation on synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, in both noise-free and noisy settings. The results suggest that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms other state-of-the-art parallel Bayesian optimization methods, including parallel Expected Improvement and parallel UCB variants, with particularly strong performance in the presence of observation noise.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically solid. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is a natural and well-motivated extension of the sequential Knowledge Gradient policy to the batch setting. The use of a gradient-based approach to optimize the acquisition function is sophisticated and addresses the high-dimensional optimization challenge inherent in batch selection. The experimental methodology is comprehensive, testing the algorithm on a good range of problems, including practical machine learning applications like tuning logistic regression and CNNs. The claim that the method performs particularly well in noisy settings is well-supported by the experiments.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation, background, and proposed algorithm are introduced logically. However, the clarity suffers significantly in Section 5.2, which describes the gradient estimator. This section is the technical heart of the paper, yet it is presented in a very condensed manner, omitting key details of the derivation. While the supplement may contain more information, the main paper should be sufficiently self-contained for an expert to grasp the core technical contribution. Without more detail, it is difficult to fully assess the correctness and complexity of the proposed estimator.

**Originality:** The problem of batch Bayesian optimization is an active and important area of research. While the proposed q-KG method is presented as novel, the extension of the Knowledge Gradient to the batch setting has been considered in prior work. The primary novelty appears to be the specific computational method developed for optimizing the q-KG factor, namely the use of an IPA-based gradient estimator for continuous domains. The paper would be strengthened by more clearly situating its contribution in the context of previous batch KG methods and highlighting how the proposed optimization strategy improves upon them.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant. Batch Bayesian optimization is crucial for effectively using modern parallel computing resources to solve expensive black-box optimization problems, such as hyperparameter tuning. A principled and computationally efficient batch method could have a substantial impact on both research and practice. The strong empirical results, especially in the noisy setting which is common in real-world applications (e.g., when evaluating models on a subset of data), suggest that this method is a valuable contribution to the state of the art.

### Detailed Comments

1.  **Novelty and Relation to Prior Work:** The core idea of extending the Knowledge Gradient to a batch setting is powerful. However, it appears that similar batch KG policies have been explored previously (e.g., in discrete optimization settings). Could you please clarify the novelty of your formulation compared to this prior work? If the primary contribution is the gradient-based optimization method, it would be very helpful to see a direct comparison (e.g., in terms of computational efficiency or optimization quality) against the methods used to optimize the batch KG acquisition function in those previous papers. This would help solidify the paper's contribution.

2.  **Clarity of Gradient Estimation (Sec 5.2):** Section 5.2 is central to the paper's contribution but is currently too brief. The derivation of the gradient estimator is non-trivial, and its presentation would greatly benefit from more detail and intermediate steps. For reproducibility and proper evaluation of the technical claims, a more thorough explanation of how the gradient of `g` with respect to `z_ij` is derived via IPA is needed in the main text.

3.  **Comparison with Expected Improvement in Noisy Settings:** In Section 6.2, you demonstrate strong performance in noisy settings. The comparison with parallel EI is a key benchmark. However, the standard EI acquisition function is derived under the assumption of noise-free observations and can perform poorly when this assumption is violated. Could you clarify which version of parallel EI was used in the noisy experiments? If it was the standard, noise-unaware version, this could explain much of the performance gap, as q-KG is designed to handle noise. A comparison against a noise-aware EI variant would make the experimental claims in this important setting more convincing.

4.  **Minor Issue:** The abbreviation “IPA” is defined on line 42 ("infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA)") and then again on line 162 ("This technique is called infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA)"). The second definition is redundant and should be removed.
