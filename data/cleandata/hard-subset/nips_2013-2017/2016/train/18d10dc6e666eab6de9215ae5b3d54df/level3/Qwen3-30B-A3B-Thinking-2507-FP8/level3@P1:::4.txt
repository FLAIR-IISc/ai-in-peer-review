

This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, addressing an important problem in optimizing expensive-to-evaluate functions where multiple evaluations can be performed in parallel. The authors propose a novel acquisition function that selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously by maximizing the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor. The key contribution is the derivation of a decision-theoretically optimal acquisition function for batch selection, along with an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to evaluate the gradient of this acquisition function. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments that q-KG consistently outperforms existing batch Bayesian optimization methods, particularly in noisy settings, on both synthetic test functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems for machine learning algorithms.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization by addressing the parallel setting, which has received limited attention compared to sequential approaches. Previous work on batch Bayesian optimization has largely adopted greedy iterative approaches (e.g., parallel EI, GP-BUCB), while q-KG considers the batch collectively, leading to more optimal sampling decisions. This aligns with the knowledge gradient approach for sequential optimization [17], but extends it to the parallel setting in a theoretically sound way. The connection to prior work is well established in Section 2, with appropriate citations to related methods including parallel EI [2, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and parallel UCB with exploration [3].

The quality of the paper is high. The theoretical foundation is sound, with a clear derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and a well-motivated computational approach using IPA. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical machine learning problems (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show q-KG outperforming benchmarks, with particularly strong gains in noisy settings. The paper includes detailed implementation information and makes code publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The writing is precise, well-organized, and accessible to the NIPS audience. The mathematical derivations are presented clearly, with sufficient detail to understand the key insights. The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented effectively with appropriate visualizations. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is particularly clear.

The originality of the work is strong. While batch Bayesian optimization has been studied before, the decision-theoretic approach taken by q-KG, which is Bayes-optimal for the one-step remaining problem, represents a significant conceptual advance. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly noting that previous methods (e.g., [2, 5, 18, 25]) construct batches iteratively rather than optimizing the batch collectively.

The significance of the work is substantial. Parallel Bayesian optimization is increasingly important as machine learning applications require tuning complex models with expensive evaluations. The q-KG method provides a practical solution that can significantly reduce the time required for hyperparameter optimization, especially in noisy settings where other methods struggle. The demonstrated improvements on real-world machine learning tasks (e.g., improving CNN test error from ~14% to ~11% on CIFAR10) highlight the practical impact of the work.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed. The concern about Gaussian process priors being potentially inappropriate for high-dimensional inputs (such as neural network architectures) is valid and should be acknowledged. While the paper demonstrates success on problems with up to 8 hyperparameters, the scalability to higher dimensions remains an open question. Additionally, the paper would benefit from theoretical results including error bounds and a quantitative complexity analysis with respect to input dimension, which would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

The paper contains minor typographical issues that should be corrected: "the set of points to evaluate next that is" (line 36) should be "the set of points to evaluate next that are"; "define A" (line 95) should be clarified; "input of mu^n" (line 104) needs definition; a brief discussion of parallel EI would help contextualize the comparison (line 128); and the paper should avoid reusing notation A (line 140).

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in batch Bayesian optimization. The method is technically sound, the experiments are convincing, and the paper is well-written. The minor weaknesses noted above are not substantial enough to outweigh the significant contributions of the work. The results on both synthetic and real-world problems provide strong evidence of the method's practical value.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, efficient computational approach, comprehensive empirical evaluation, clear writing, practical significance, and demonstrated improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

Con: Limited discussion of scalability to high-dimensional problems (where GP priors may become problematic), lack of theoretical error bounds, and minor typographical issues.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization and should be accepted for publication at NIPS 2016.
