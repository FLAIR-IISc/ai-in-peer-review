- Proposes q-KG, a novel batch Knowledge Gradient algorithm for parallel Bayesian optimization.  
- Extends sequential Knowledge Gradient to batch settings via decision-theoretic analysis.  
- Introduces an IPA-based, gradient-driven optimizer to efficiently maximize the q-KG acquisition function.  
- Experiments show improved performance over existing batch BO methods on synthetic and real hyperparameter tasks, especially with noisy evaluations.  
- Code release enhances reproducibility.  
- Paper is well-motivated and conceptually sound for parallelizable optimization problems.  
- Lacks clear positioning relative to prior batch KG and related work (e.g., Wang et al. 2016, Shah & Ghahramani 2016).  
- Unclear which component is the main novelty: batch formulation, IPA-based gradient estimator, or both.  
- Needs more thorough empirical comparison to existing batch KG implementations on runtime and performance.  
- If IPA gradient estimator is primary contribution, requires direct comparison to prior gradient estimation techniques.  
- Section 5 (q-KG computation and gradient) is under-detailed; derivations and justifications for Section 5.2 are too brief.  
- Recommend expanding IPA explanation, providing intermediate derivation steps, and including pseudocode for gradient estimation.  
- Discretization strategy (Section 5.3) is reasonable but needs analysis of sensitivity to the number of samples (M).  
- Comparison to Expected Improvement is problematic because standard EI assumes noiseless observations; authors must clarify whether a noise-aware EI was used.  
- Should use a noise-aware EI for fair comparison or explicitly acknowledge limitations of comparing to noiseless EI.  
- Minor issue: the abbreviation “IPA” is redefined inconsistently and should be corrected.  
- Strengths summarized: technically sound batch KG extension, efficient IPA-based optimization, strong noisy-case empirical results, and released code.  
- Weaknesses summarized: insufficient related-work coverage, inadequate gradient-estimation detail, potentially unfair EI comparisons, and limited discretization sensitivity discussion.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel contribution, promising empirical gains, and reproducible code release.  
- Arguments against acceptance: novelty not clearly emphasized, insufficient technical detail for reproducibility, and potentially misleading comparisons to EI.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept contingent on clarifying positioning vs prior work, expanding gradient-estimation details, and ensuring fair comparisons.
