

This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, extending the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting where multiple evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor when only one batch of evaluations remains. They propose an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function, enabling its optimization over a batch of points. The authors demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms existing batch BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, parallel UCB with exploration) on synthetic test functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the growing literature on parallel Bayesian optimization. While the sequential KG acquisition function has been well-established [7, 17], extending it to the parallel setting presents unique computational challenges. The authors correctly identify that naively maximizing q-KG would be computationally prohibitive, and their IPA-based gradient estimator provides a practical solution. However, the novelty claim requires careful clarification: the paper acknowledges prior work on batch KG [26], but the distinction between their approach and this prior work is not sufficiently emphasized. Specifically, [26] studies a nested batch knowledge gradient policy for discrete feasible sets, while the current paper addresses continuous domains with a more efficient gradient-based optimization method. The authors should more clearly articulate this distinction to establish their contribution relative to prior work.

The technical core of the paper (Section 5.2) is the most critical component but also the most underdeveloped. The IPA-based gradient estimator is central to the method's practical implementation, yet the presentation lacks sufficient detail to assess its computational complexity or to replicate the approach. The authors state that the gradient can be computed efficiently but do not provide theoretical complexity bounds or empirical comparisons with alternative gradient estimation techniques. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the IPA approach compared to other methods (e.g., finite differences) would strengthen the paper. The current description would leave a practitioner struggling to implement the method.

The experimental evaluation is generally strong, with thorough testing on both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show that q-KG outperforms benchmarks, especially in noisy settings where the performance gap is most pronounced. However, the comparison to Expected Improvement (EI) requires clarification. The paper states that q-KG reduces to parallel EI in the noise-free case with the solution restricted to sampled points, but the experimental comparisons use parallel EI implementations that assume noiseless evaluations. The authors should explicitly state whether they used a noise-aware variant of EI in their experiments, as this is critical for fair comparison in the noisy settings presented in Section 6.2.

The paper demonstrates good quality through its technical approach and experimental validation, though the theoretical justification for the IPA method could be stronger. The writing is clear and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to method to evaluation. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproduction, though Section 5.2 would benefit from additional mathematical rigor. The originality is somewhat limited by the close relation to sequential KG and prior batch KG work, but the authors make a valuable contribution by providing a practical computational method for the parallel setting. The significance is high, as batch BO is increasingly important for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning, where parallel evaluations are common.

Strengths:
- Provides a decision-theoretically sound acquisition function for batch BO
- Demonstrates consistent performance improvements over existing methods, especially in noisy settings
- Offers a practical implementation with open-source code
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across synthetic and real-world problems

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient clarification of novelty relative to prior batch KG work
- Underdeveloped technical details in the central computational method (Section 5.2)
- Ambiguous comparison to Expected Improvement due to noise assumptions
- Minor issue with IPA definition (redefined at line 162)

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the parallel Bayesian optimization literature, addressing a practical need in machine learning hyperparameter tuning. While the novelty claim could be strengthened with clearer differentiation from prior work, the method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. The computational technique proposed is a significant step toward making batch Bayesian optimization practically applicable for real-world machine learning problems. The paper advances the state of the art in batch Bayesian optimization by providing an efficient implementation of a theoretically sound acquisition function, particularly valuable for noisy evaluation scenarios.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a significant practical problem (batch evaluation in BO)
- Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated method
- Offers a computationally efficient solution to a challenging optimization problem
- Demonstrates clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods

Arguments against acceptance:
- Novelty claim requires stronger justification relative to prior work
- Central technical contribution (Section 5.2) lacks sufficient detail
- Comparison to EI needs clarification regarding noise assumptions

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with practical implications for machine learning hyperparameter tuning. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. With minor revisions to clarify the novelty and provide more detail on the computational method, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS 2016.
