Summary and relation to prior work: The paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that selects Bayes-optimal batches in the one-step lookahead sense. Building on the knowledge gradient literature for sequential BO, the authors extend KG to the parallel setting and develop an efficient gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis to optimize the Monte Carlo–defined acquisition. The work is positioned against prior batch BO methods such as q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.), GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE, and Predictive Entropy Search (PPES). Theoretically, the method unifies decision-theoretic optimality with practical, differentiable computation for batch selection, and it reduces to q-EI in the noiseless/restricted-recommendation case.

Quality: The technical development is sound and carefully written. The Bayes-optimality argument for a single remaining batch and the IPA-based gradient estimator are clear, and the discretization strategy is reasonable. However, the empirical evaluation is insufficient to substantiate broad practical gains: (i) the objective optimizes test-set error directly, which risks leakage and optimistic bias; a proper protocol should optimize validation (e.g., CV) and report test only once; (ii) the 0–1 loss used is threshold-based and insensitive; log loss or (for binary/multiclass) AUC/multiclass AUC would be more informative; (iii) benchmarks are limited to MNIST/CIFAR10 and two learners (logistic regression, a single CNN), which is too narrow for HPO claims; include standard suites (e.g., HPOlib) and diverse models (SVMs, tree ensembles, GBMs, kernel methods). The assumed independent Gaussian observation noise is also unrealistic in HPO, where CV induces correlated, heteroscedastic errors and SGD/minibatching introduces structural bias; modeling correlated/heteroscedastic noise or using appropriate likelihoods would strengthen claims.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and easy to follow; notation and algorithmic details are presented cleanly.

Originality: Extending KG to compute Bayes-optimal batches with a scalable gradient estimator is a novel and elegant contribution beyond existing q-EI/PPES/UCB variants.

Significance: Potentially high—computing Bayes-optimal batches is conceptually important—but empirical breadth is currently too limited to convince practitioners.

Pros:
- Strong theory; principled Bayes-optimal batch selection.
- Efficient gradient-based optimization of a challenging acquisition.
- Clear writing.

Cons:
- Experimental protocol (using test error) is problematic.
- Limited datasets, metrics, and model diversity; no standard HPO benchmarks.
- Noise model assumptions misaligned with real HPO (correlated/heteroscedastic noise).

Overall: Positive on theory and approach; empirical scope needs substantial expansion and corrected evaluation to justify wide adoption.
