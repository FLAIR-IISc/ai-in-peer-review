Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a parallel (batch) Bayesian optimization method based on the knowledge gradient, dubbed q-KG. The acquisition measures the expected reduction in the posterior GP predictor’s minimum after collecting a batch of q evaluations. The method is Bayes-optimal for the one-step remaining decision problem. Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG requires Monte Carlo for the parallel case, the authors derive an infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) estimator of the gradient of the Monte Carlo objective and use stochastic gradient ascent to optimize the batch. For continuous domains, they discretize the argmin set via samples from the posterior optima (akin to ES/Thompson-style candidate sets) and include previously sampled points and the current candidate batch.

Empirically, the paper evaluates on synthetic benchmarks and two hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST; a CNN on CIFAR10). Results suggest q-KG is competitive in noiseless settings and performs best in noisy settings, outperforming parallel EI variants and UCB-based batch methods.

The work builds on: (i) sequential KG (Frazier et al., 2008; Scott et al., 2011), (ii) parallel acquisitions including q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger, 2013) and its IPA-based stochastic gradients (Wang et al., 2015; Marmin et al., 2015), and (iii) q-PES (Shah & Ghahramani, 2015) and UCB-based batch BO (Desautels et al., 2014; Contal et al., 2013). The main step beyond prior work is a batch KG formulation with an IPA gradient estimator and an implementation that targets noisy objectives.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic formulation: q-KG is Bayes-optimal in the one-step batch setting and reduces to q-EI in the noiseless/restrict-to-sampled-points limit.
- Sound stochastic gradient approach: the IPA derivation is appropriate for Monte Carlo optimization and exploits GP structure efficiently.
- Relevance and potential impact: parallel HPO is practically useful in multi-core/cluster environments where many models can be trained simultaneously.
- Empirical results show consistent gains under noise; code availability aids reproducibility.
- Writing is generally clear; background and technical details are well explained; experiments are understandable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical efficiency is not quantified. There are no wall-clock measurements, no comparison to parallel random search or sequential BO, and no accounting of the overhead to optimize q-KG versus the evaluation time of ML models. This limits claims about speedups “in parallel environments.”
- Batch size is fixed to q = 4 across all experiments without justification or analysis of scaling behavior as q grows.
- Novelty is incremental. The use of KG plus IPA-based gradient estimation closely parallels earlier IPA-based parallel acquisition optimization for q-EI (e.g., Wang et al., 2015; Marmin et al., 2015). The paper should more clearly delineate what is new in the analysis, estimators, or implementation beyond swapping EI for KG.
- Comparison set omits strong recent batch methods (e.g., q-PES/PPES). The empirical evidence, while favorable, does not yet feel conclusive.
- Some details are unclear (see major questions), particularly regarding the definition of “iterations,” initialization, and discretization sensitivity.

Technical comments and suggestions
- Complexity and scalability: Please quantify the per-batch optimization cost. With An of size M, each Monte Carlo/gradient step involves a q×q Cholesky (O(q^3)) and operations over M candidates; overall complexity appears O(M q^2) to O(M q^3) per sample/gradient step. How does runtime and memory scale with q, d, M, and the number of restarts?
- Discretization: The evolving candidate set An constructed from samples near posterior optima may bias the argmin approximation. Provide sensitivity to M and the sampling scheme; compare against alternative discretizations.
- Noisy setting: Clarify how the final recommendation is selected under noise and whether you average replicates or use the GP posterior mean with noise variance learned by MLE.

Major questions for the authors
1) Comparison and differences vs Wang et al. (2015) q-EI: Did you compare against the IPA-optimized q-EI in MOE (or equivalent), and can you clearly state methodological and computational differences? In what regimes does q-KG offer principled or practical advantages over q-EI (beyond the one-step optimality argument)?
2) Scaling to larger q: How does the method perform for q in the tens or hundreds, both in optimization overhead and in sample efficiency? What benefits and diminishing returns do you expect as q increases, and how does discretization or the IPA estimator behave numerically?
3) Asynchronous evaluations: Can your approach support asynchronous batches (pending evaluations) via fantasization/hallucination, similar to parallel EI heuristics? If so, outline how to adapt q-KG; if not, discuss limitations.
4) Wall-clock and overhead: Please report end-to-end runtime vs. (a) parallel random search with the same q, and (b) sequential BO (q=1) running q times in parallel. Also report the average time to compute a new batch (number of MC samples, gradient steps, restarts), and the fraction of total time spent in acquisition optimization versus function evaluation.
5) Experimental axes and initialization: In all plots, clarify whether the x-axis “iterations” denotes batches or individual function evaluations; whether the initial design is included; and why starting values differ across methods. Ideally, use the same initial design and report performance vs. total evaluations.

Minor comments
- Equation (4.1): check notation consistency for z(1:q) vs z^(1:q); ensure the conditioning is clearly written as En[minx∈A μ(n+q)(x) | y(z(1:q))]. If A is later discretized to An, make that explicit in the equation used in practice.
- Typos: “Main Stange” → “Main Stage”; “achieve” → “achieves” (CNN paragraph).
- Citations: Do not credit Wang et al. (2015) with introducing IPA; IPA is classic (e.g., L’Ecuyer, 1990). Please also cite Marmin et al. (2015) on differentiating q-EI and Chevalier & Ginsbourger (2013) more precisely for multi-point EI.
- Consider adding q-PES/PPES to baselines if feasible.

Evaluation against review criteria
- Quality: The decision-theoretic formulation and IPA gradient estimator are technically sound; empirical methodology is reasonable but lacks crucial runtime analyses and broader baselines.
- Clarity: Generally clear and reproducible; a few notational and presentation details should be tightened.
- Originality: Moderate. The batch KG objective is natural; the optimization machinery and implementation strategy are close to prior IPA-based batch acquisition work.
- Significance: High potential impact given the importance of parallel HPO; stronger practical evidence (wall-clock, larger q, asynchronous) would substantiate claims.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled batch acquisition (q-KG) with solid derivation and implementation.
- Demonstrated gains under noise on both synthetic and real HPO tasks.
- Topic is timely and broadly relevant; code is available.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing wall-clock and parallel-efficiency evidence; unclear practical speedups vs simpler baselines.
- Fixed small q without scaling study; no asynchronous variant.
- Incomplete comparisons to the strongest alternatives (IPA q-EI, q-PES); novelty over prior IPA-based batch selection is limited.
