- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that generalizes the knowledge gradient to select q points per iteration.
- q-KG maximizes expected reduction in the posterior mean minimum after one additional round of measurements.
- Recovers parallel EI under noise-free evaluations when final recommendation is restricted to sampled points.
- Exact q-KG is intractable; the paper uses Monte Carlo approximation and optimizes the acquisition with an IPA-based stochastic gradient.
- Uses Cholesky reparameterization of the conditional posterior to enable efficient sampling and gradients.
- Empirically competitive or superior to parallel EI variants and UCB-based batch methods on synthetic functions and two hyperparameter-tuning tasks.
- Shows particularly strong gains in noisy settings where KG’s value-of-information is most helpful.
- Builds on sequential KG literature and recent batch BO methods (q-EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE, PPES); novelty is extending one-step Bayes-optimality to batches with stochastic-gradient optimization.
- Technical development is sound; provides an unbiased IPA gradient estimator under differentiability and no-tie assumptions.
- Lacks broader theoretical analysis beyond one-step optimality: no convergence/regret guarantees for the batch/approximate/discretized setting.
- Does not characterize the impact of discretization (M) or Monte Carlo error on performance.
- Paper is generally well written and organized, but some notational hygiene issues (e.g., shapes in Eq. (3.1)) need clarification.
- Originality is moderate since joint batch optimization and stochastic gradients have been explored for q-EI and PPES.
- High practical significance: batch BO is widely needed and the method’s open-source code aids adoption and reproducibility.
- Strength: principled KG-based derivation retaining one-step optimality in batch context.
- Strength: efficient computation via IPA gradients and Cholesky reparameterization; strong empirical results and clear implementation details.
- Weakness: novelty incremental relative to existing multipoint acquisitions and gradient-based methods.
- Weakness: limited analysis of computational scaling with q, dimension, and MC samples; experiments focus mainly on q=4.
- Weakness: comparisons omit some contemporaneous information-based methods (e.g., PPES) and certain q-EI baselines.
- Requests: report sensitivity to discretization size M, number of MC samples, gradient steps and multi-starts to show runtime–performance trade-offs.
- Requests: discuss handling of ties/nonsmoothness in min over A when using IPA and clarify use of subgradients or measure-zero assumptions.
- Requests: provide wall-clock comparisons, results for larger batch sizes, and consider asynchronous settings.
- Notes on hyperparameters: MLE refitting may bias decisions; marginalization or robustness analysis is suggested.
- Acceptance arguments: principled extension of KG, consistent empirical gains (esp. noisy cases), clear presentation, and available code.
- Rejection arguments: limited novelty, lack of theoretical guarantees and analysis of discretization/MC effects, and somewhat narrow experimental scope.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
