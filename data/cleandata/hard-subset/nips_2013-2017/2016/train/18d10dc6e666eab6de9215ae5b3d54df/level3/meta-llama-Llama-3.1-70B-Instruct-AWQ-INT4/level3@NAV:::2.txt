This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method, which computes Bayes-optimal batches and outperforms existing methods. The algorithm is well-written, easy to follow, and presents an elegant solution to parallelizing Bayesian optimization.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a new acquisition function, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which measures the information gain of evaluating q points. The authors provide a strong theoretical analysis of the q-KG algorithm, showing that it is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the predictor of the Gaussian process if only one decision is remaining.

The experimental evaluation is weak, however. The authors only use two datasets (MNIST and CIFAR10) and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN), which is not sufficient to demonstrate the algorithm's performance on a broader range of problems. Additionally, the authors use test set error as the optimization criterion, which is problematic, and threshold-based metrics are suboptimal. The authors should consider using more robust metrics such as AUC or log loss.

The paper also assumes independent normally distributed observation noise, which can be unrealistic in hyperparameter optimization due to structural bias and correlated errors from empirical estimates. The authors should discuss the limitations of this assumption and potential ways to address it.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, but the experimental evaluation needs to be strengthened to demonstrate the algorithm's performance on a broader range of problems.
