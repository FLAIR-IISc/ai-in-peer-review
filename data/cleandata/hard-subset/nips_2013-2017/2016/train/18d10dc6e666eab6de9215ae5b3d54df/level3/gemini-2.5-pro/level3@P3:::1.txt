### **Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"**

#### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel acquisition function for batch-sequential Bayesian optimization (BO). The method extends the decision-theoretic foundation of the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG), which is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining evaluation step, to the parallel setting where a batch of `q` points can be evaluated simultaneously. The q-KG acquisition function quantifies the expected improvement in the final solution quality after observing the results from a batch of `q` candidate points.

To make the maximization of q-KG computationally tractable, the authors propose an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy. They derive an unbiased estimator for the gradient of q-KG using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), which allows for the use of stochastic gradient ascent. The method is evaluated on a suite of synthetic benchmark functions and two practical hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), under both noise-free and noisy evaluation settings. The numerical experiments demonstrate that q-KG often outperforms or is competitive with other state-of-the-art batch BO algorithms, with a particularly strong advantage in the presence of noise.

#### **Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The problem of parallelizing Bayesian optimization is of high practical importance. As computational resources become more parallelized (e.g., multi-core CPUs, GPUs, cloud computing), methods that can effectively leverage this parallelism to speed up expensive black-box optimization are in high demand. This work directly addresses this crucial need.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The paper presents a principled, decision-theoretically motivated acquisition function for the batch setting. Unlike many existing batch BO methods that rely on heuristics (e.g., "Constant Liar") or greedy sequential construction, q-KG values the batch of points collectively and is derived as the one-step Bayes-optimal policy. The development of an efficient gradient estimator using IPA is a solid technical contribution that makes the method practical.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors compare q-KG against multiple strong baselines, including different parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) variants. The consistent and often significant performance gains, especially on noisy problems, provide compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness and robustness. The application to tuning real machine learning models further strengthens the paper's practical relevance.

#### **Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, there are several areas where it could be improved, primarily concerning the literature review, technical clarity, and experimental details.

**1. Related Work:**
The literature review could be more precise and comprehensive.
*   The claim that prior work parallelizes EI by "iteratively constructing a batch... averaging over the posterior distribution" or by using "constant liar" seems to attribute these ideas to [10, 19]. However, both the concept of integrating EI with respect to the posterior over pending evaluations and the Constant Liar heuristic were first introduced in Ginsbourger et al. (2010), "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization." This seminal work should be more accurately credited.
*   There are notable omissions of highly relevant work. Chevalier & Ginsbourger (2013) presented CL-mix, a sophisticated variant of the Constant Liar heuristic. More importantly, Marmin et al. (2015), in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design," also studied gradient-based maximization of a batch acquisition function (q-EI), which is a very close parallel to the technical approach taken in this paper for q-KG. This work should be cited and discussed.

**2. Technical Clarity and Assumptions:**
Several technical points would benefit from further clarification.
*   **Assumptions:** The paper does not explicitly state the assumptions on the domain `A` (e.g., compactness) and the objective function `f` (e.g., continuity), which are typically required for the GP model and optimization guarantees.
*   **Smoothness of `g`:** The application of IPA in Section 5.2 requires interchanging the expectation and derivative operators. This relies on the function `g` being sufficiently smooth with respect to the decision variables `z`. However, due to the `min` operator inside its definition, `g` is not obviously smooth, as the `argmin` can jump discontinuously. This point is critical for the validity of the gradient estimator and deserves justification in the main text, not just the supplement.
*   **Differentiability of Cholesky Factor:** The gradient derivation involves differentiating the Cholesky factor `D(n)`. The differentiability of the Cholesky decomposition is non-trivial and requires the matrix to be continuously positive definite. This assumption should be stated and briefly discussed.
*   **Definition of `g`:** In Section 5.2, the objective is to minimize `f(x)`, so the reward is the reduction in the minimum of the posterior mean. The definition of `g` seems consistent with this. However, to avoid any confusion for readers accustomed to maximizing acquisition functions, it might be helpful to briefly re-emphasize that `g` represents the *improvement* in a *minimization* problem.

**3. Algorithmic and Experimental Details:**
*   **Hyperparameter Updates:** Algorithm 1 states "re-train the hyperparameters of the GP by MLE" within the main loop but does not specify the frequency or strategy. Is this done at every iteration? For every new batch? This is an important and potentially costly implementation detail that should be clarified.
*   **Initial Design:** The initial design is restricted to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS). While this is a reasonable choice, a brief justification or discussion of alternatives would be welcome.
*   **Performance Metrics:** The paper convincingly shows that q-KG requires fewer iterations than its competitors. However, it does not analyze the computational cost per iteration or provide wall-clock time comparisons. A discussion of the trade-off between iteration cost and sample efficiency would provide a more complete picture of the practical benefits of parallelization.
*   **Minor Correction:** In Section 6, the kernel is specified as "ARD Matérn 2/5". This appears to be a typo and should likely be "ARD Matérn 5/2", which is the standard choice.
*   **Reproducibility:** For verification purposes, it would be interesting to know if the results are sensitive to the specific GP implementation (e.g., could the GPs in Figure 1 be fitted using other standard libraries like GPy or GPflow to check for similar outcomes?).

#### **Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper with a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It introduces a principled and effective method for an important practical problem. The weaknesses identified are largely addressable through revision, particularly by improving the literature review and clarifying technical details. I strongly recommend acceptance.
