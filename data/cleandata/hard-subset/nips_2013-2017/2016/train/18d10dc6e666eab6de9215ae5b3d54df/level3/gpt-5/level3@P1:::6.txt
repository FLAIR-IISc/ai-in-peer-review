This paper proposes a batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which selects a batch of q points by maximizing the expected one-step reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean. The authors connect q-KG to the sequential knowledge gradient and show that, under noiseless evaluations and when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Because direct optimization of q-KG is computationally difficult, they propose a Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG together with an unbiased gradient estimator derived via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling stochastic gradient-based optimization over batches. For continuous domains, the acquisition is evaluated on a dynamically updated discretization constructed from samples near posterior optima. Empirically, q-KG is compared to several batch BO baselines (parallel EI variants, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on standard synthetic functions and on hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10). The method is competitive in noiseless settings and shows the largest improvements when evaluations are noisy. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well situated among BO acquisition functions (PI, EI, UCB, ES, KG) and batch methods (parallel EI, q-EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE, q-ES). The main conceptual contribution is extending the decision-theoretic KG to batch selection with noise and providing a practical gradient estimator for joint batch optimization. Closest prior art includes parallel EI with joint optimization using IPA/gradient-based methods (e.g., Chevalier & Ginsbourger 2013; Marmin et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2015), and parallel ES (Hernandez-Lobato et al. 2014; Shah & Ghahramani 2015). The manuscript would benefit from a clearer positioning against q-EI with stochastic gradients and q-ES, especially in noisy settings.

Quality: The formulation of q-KG, its conditional normal representation, and the IPA-based gradient estimator are technically sound, and the assumptions for unbiasedness are stated (smooth kernels/means; details in the supplement). The discretization strategy is standard but introduces approximation error that is not analyzed. Empirical results are positive, particularly under noise. However, the experimental section omits key measurements relevant to practical utility: end-to-end wall-clock time (including acquisition optimization) and comparisons to strong baselines like random search and sequential BO. The fixed batch size q = 4 lacks justification, and scalability to larger batches is not evaluated. These omissions limit the strength of the claims about practical speedups in parallel environments.

Clarity: The paper is generally clearly written, with helpful background and implementation details. A few notation choices and figure conventions are confusing (see minor comments). The description of the evolving discretization is brief; clarifying how M is chosen and its computational impact would help reproducibility.

Originality: Extending KG to parallel batch selection with noise and providing an IPA gradient estimator for joint batch optimization is a reasonable incremental contribution. The use of IPA for acquisition gradients is not novel per se; the novelty lies in the specific q-KG objective and its efficient gradient evaluation. The paper should more explicitly distinguish itself from prior q-EI gradient-based methods and q-ES, both conceptually (objective being optimized) and practically (noise handling, compute).

Significance: Batch BO for hyperparameter tuning is a high-impact application area. If q-KG consistently outperforms q-EI/q-ES and asynchronous heuristics at similar wall-clock cost, it would be a valuable contribution. The current experiments suggest promise, especially with noise, but lack of scalability studies and wall-clock comparisons make it difficult to judge likely adoption.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear decision-theoretic objective for batch selection; reduction to q-EI in a special case is insightful.
- Sound stochastic gradient estimator that enables practical optimization of a challenging acquisition.
- Consistent empirical gains in noisy settings across synthetic and real HPO tasks; code released.
- Topic is timely and relevant for parallel HPO.

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing wall-clock evaluations and comparisons to random search and sequential BO; practical speedups remain unsubstantiated.
- No experiments varying q or demonstrating scalability to larger batches; fixed q = 4 throughout.
- Incomplete positioning vs. q-EI/q-ES with joint batch optimization; novelty is incremental.
- Synchronous-only setting; no discussion or experiments on asynchronous parallelization.

Major questions for the authors:
1) Comparison to q-EI with joint optimization: Please include experiments against Wang et al. (2015)/q-EI with stochastic gradients (and, if feasible, q-ES), and clearly articulate the conceptual and practical differences (especially under noise).
2) Scalability in q: What is the computational complexity per gradient evaluation in q, M, and number of MC samples? How does performance and overhead scale for q in {1, 4, 8, 16, 32}? Are there diminishing returns?
3) Asynchrony: Can q-KG be adapted to asynchronous settings (e.g., via fantasization of pending observations)? If so, outline the changes; if not, discuss limitations.
4) Wall-clock performance: Report end-to-end times to reach a target regret/test error, including acquisition optimization overhead, versus random search and sequential BO using the same total function evaluations and compute budget.
5) Experimental reporting: Clarify the x-axis in all plots (batches vs. function evaluations), inclusion of the initial design, and why starting values differ across methods. Ensure all methods share identical initial designs/random seeds when averaging, or explain differences.

Suggestions and improvements:
- Provide an ablation on the discretization size M and the sampling scheme for An, covering accuracy vs. computational cost.
- Report the number of MC samples used for q-KG and its gradient, and show sensitivity.
- For HPO tasks, include cumulative wall-clock and the proportion of time spent on the acquisition vs. training models.
- Discuss numerical stability (e.g., Cholesky with jitter) and hyperparameter refitting frequency.
- Consider including a higher-dimensional synthetic task (d ≥ 10) to test robustness of the discretization approach.

Minor comments:
- Eq. (4.1): double-check conditioning and notation; ensure z(1:q) appears consistently inside the expectation and clarify the overloading of A (the text notes two uses; perhaps rename the discretized set An).
- Typo: “achieve a test error around 14%” → “achieves”.
- Citations: attribute IPA to earlier simulation optimization literature (e.g., L’Ecuyer 1990) and avoid suggesting Wang et al. (2015) introduced IPA; also standardize citation phrasing and fix broken URLs.
- Figures: add captions specifying whether the y-axis is immediate or simple regret and whether lower is better.

Overall, this is a well-written and promising paper that could be impactful for noisy batch BO. Its main weaknesses are empirical: lack of wall-clock and scalability evidence and unclear positioning against the strongest batch baselines. Addressing the major questions would substantially strengthen the submission.
