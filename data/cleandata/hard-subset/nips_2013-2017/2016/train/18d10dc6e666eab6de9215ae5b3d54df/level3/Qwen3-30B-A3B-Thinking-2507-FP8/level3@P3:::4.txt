

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces q-KG, a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization that outputs q points at each iteration, addressing the important problem of speeding up expensive function evaluations through parallel computation. The authors propose a decision-theoretic approach that constructs a batch of q points collectively rather than iteratively, providing a Bayes-optimal batch of points to evaluate when only one batch remains. The paper develops an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to evaluate and optimize the acquisition function, which would otherwise be computationally intractable.

The work builds upon previous Bayesian optimization (BO) literature that has primarily focused on sequential evaluation. While prior batch BO methods (e.g., parallel EI, batch UCB) construct batches iteratively by optimizing a sequential acquisition function with fixed other points, the proposed q-KG considers the entire batch jointly. The paper relates to related work including the knowledge gradient method for sequential BO [7, 17], parallel EI extensions [2, 19, 25], and UCB-based parallel methods [3, 5]. The authors clearly distinguish their approach by emphasizing the collective optimization of the batch rather than iterative selection.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field. The method is technically sound, with clear theoretical motivation for the acquisition function. The computational approach using IPA is clever and well-explained. The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating consistent improvements over benchmark methods on both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, especially in noisy settings. The experiments cover a range of dimensions (from 2D to 6D) and include both noise-free and noisy evaluations, with thorough comparisons against relevant baselines.

The paper excels in clarity and organization, with well-structured sections that guide the reader through the technical development. The code is publicly available, enhancing reproducibility. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian optimization.

However, the paper has two significant limitations. First, the assumption of a Gaussian process prior may be questionable for high-dimensional inputs like neural network architectures, which are common applications of batch BO. The authors do not address this limitation or provide evidence of q-KG's performance on such high-dimensional problems. Second, the paper lacks theoretical results on error bounds and a quantitative complexity analysis with respect to input dimension, which would strengthen the theoretical foundation of the approach.

Minor issues require attention:
- Line 36: "the set of points to evaluate next that is" should be "the set of points to evaluate next"
- Line 95: The notation A is not defined in the context of the acquisition function
- Line 104: The input of μ^n needs clarification
- Line 128: A brief discussion of parallel EI would be helpful for context
- Line 140: The notation A is reused for different purposes; clarification is needed

The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The method is sound, the experiments are well-designed and convincing, and the paper is well-written. The problem addressed is important for real-world applications where function evaluations are expensive and parallel computation is available. The q-KG approach provides a meaningful improvement over existing batch BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

In terms of the NIPS criteria:
- Quality: High. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results support the claims.
- Clarity: High. The paper is well-organized and clearly written.
- Originality: Moderate to high. While batch BO is not entirely new, the decision-theoretic approach to batch selection is novel and well-motivated.
- Significance: High. The method addresses an important practical problem and demonstrates clear empirical advantages.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, with practical implications for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning. While the theoretical limitations regarding high-dimensional inputs and lack of complexity analysis are notable, these do not undermine the core contribution. The proposed method is likely to be widely adopted by practitioners working with expensive-to-evaluate functions in parallel computing environments.

I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a valuable method for batch Bayesian optimization with strong empirical validation, and the minor issues noted above are easily addressable in the camera-ready version. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, particularly for noisy optimization problems where the method shows significant advantages.
