This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), which extends the concept of the knowledge gradient from the sequential setting to the parallel setting. The authors derive the q-KG acquisition function using a decision-theoretic analysis that chooses the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior when there is only one batch of points remaining. The algorithm is shown to be Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the predictor of the GP if only one decision is remaining.

The authors also develop an efficient strategy for computing the q-KG acquisition function, which involves using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of q-KG. This allows for the use of standard gradient-based optimization algorithms to maximize q-KG.

The paper presents empirical results on both synthetic functions and real-world problems, including tuning hyperparameters of machine learning algorithms. The results show that q-KG outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art benchmark algorithms, especially in the presence of noisy function evaluations.

The paper is well-written and appears to be practically useful. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the q-KG algorithm and its underlying principles. The empirical results are also well-presented and provide a thorough evaluation of the algorithm's performance.

However, one concern is that the novelty of the paper may be limited, as it mainly extends the knowledge gradient framework to the batch setting and uses a standard IPA-based gradient estimator. While the authors do provide some new insights and results, the paper may not be significantly different from previous work in the area.

A specific technical comment is that equation (3.1) is unclear because μ and K are defined for vectors, not sequences x^(1:n). It would be helpful to clarify this notation to ensure that readers understand the equations correctly.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, this paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, and significance.
