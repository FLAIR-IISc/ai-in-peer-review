- Paper introduces q-KG: a decision-theoretic batch extension of KG optimized jointly via Monte Carlo with a pathwise (IPA) gradient estimator, using dynamic discretization for continuous domains.  
- q-KG aims to maximize expected decrement in posterior mean’s minimum without restricting final recommendation to sampled points.  
- Empirical results show q-KG often outperforms parallel EI variants and UCB-based batch methods, notably under observation noise.  
- Relationship to existing batch KG (nested-batch/discrete) and other batch BO methods needs clearer comparison and positioning.  
- Gradient-based approach is similar to differentiable q-EI; explicit runtime and variance comparisons are needed.  
- The decision-theoretic formulation is sound and reduces to q-EI in noiseless/restricted cases, a useful sanity check.  
- The IPA-based gradient derivation is underdeveloped; interchange of gradient and expectation requires stated regularity conditions.  
- Handling non-differentiability/ties (unique minimizer, Clarke subgradients, tie-breaking) must be addressed.  
- Unbiasedness and variance of the gradient estimator should be proven or referenced, with dependence on q, M, dimension, and MC samples.  
- Computational cost dependence on q and GP hyperparameter re-fitting per iteration should be quantified.  
- Experiments are broad and show consistent improvements, but noisy EI baseline needs clarification (which noise-aware EI variant was used).  
- Report wall-clock time and MC sample sizes to substantiate efficiency claims vs q-EI optimizers and PPES, especially for larger q.  
- Provide ablations on discretization size M and the posterior sample generator; discuss bias-variance tradeoffs from discretization.  
- Reproducibility: document optimizer settings, MC sample counts for objective/gradient, GP hyperparameter estimation details (priors, restarts, noise handling).  
- Section 5.2 (methodological core) requires more detail: precise conditions for key equations, pseudocode for stochastic gradient ascent, and MC allocation/variance reduction strategies.  
- Clarify construction of An (how posterior-optima samples are drawn—Thompson sampling or other) and how z(1:q) is integrated without bias.  
- Clarify per-iteration computational complexity scaling with q, n, d, and M (asymptotics and constants).  
- Remove redundant IPA redefinition and ensure consistent terminology and notation (e.g., A in (4.1)/(4.2)).  
- Novelty mainly in continuous-domain joint optimization for q-KG and IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator; strengthen contrasts with prior batch KG work.  
- Compare gradient estimator efficiency (variance, convergence speed) and runtime with q-EI optimizers and PPES to solidify originality claims.  
- Parallel BO significance is high; clearer efficiency analyses and stronger noisy baselines would increase impact.  
- Pros: clear decision-theoretic batch KG formulation, differentiable objective for joint optimization, strong empirical results (esp. noisy), public code.  
- Cons: gradient estimator lacks rigorous detail, unclear relation to prior batch KG, potentially unfair noisy EI baselines, limited analysis of efficiency and scaling.  
- Key author questions: which EI variants were used in noisy experiments; regularity conditions for (5.3) and tie handling; MC sample counts and variance reduction; per-iteration runtime vs q and M; concrete construction and sensitivity of An; effect of marginalizing GP hyperparameters; ablation demonstrating reduction to q-EI.  
- Minor fixes: correct IPA redefinition, clarify statement about drawing M samples from global optima, and provide BO initialization/details and iteration counts.  
- Overall: promising, noise-robust batch BO contribution that needs stronger theoretical exposition, clearer prior-work positioning, fair noisy baselines, and efficiency analysis to be a solid accept.
