## Review of “The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization”

This paper introduces q-KG, a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization designed for parallel function evaluations. The core idea – leveraging a decision-theoretic approach to select a batch of *q* points that maximize information gain – is compelling and addresses a significant bottleneck in Bayesian optimization, namely the computational cost of expensive function evaluations which can be mitigated by parallelization. The authors effectively build upon the knowledge gradient framework, extending it to the parallel setting and providing a practical method (IPA-based gradient computation) for optimizing the resulting acquisition function. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, presenting the methodology in a logical manner, starting with background and motivation, and culminating in comprehensive experimental validation.  It correctly positions the work within the landscape of existing parallel Bayesian optimization techniques like parallel EI and UCB, highlighting the advantage of considering the batch collectively rather than iteratively. The empirical results on both synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems (logistic regression and CNNs) convincingly demonstrate the superiority of q-KG, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios. The provision of code further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

However, a key limitation lies in the reliance on a Gaussian process prior. As highlighted by the authors’ supervisor, this assumption might be restrictive when dealing with high-dimensional input spaces, such as those encountered in complex neural network architecture search. While GPs are a common choice, exploring alternative priors or incorporating techniques to handle high dimensionality would strengthen the method's applicability. 

Crucially, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees beyond the Bayesian optimality for a single remaining batch. Providing error bounds for the algorithm's convergence or a more detailed complexity analysis with respect to the input dimension *d* would significantly elevate the contribution. How does the computational cost of q-KG scale with *d* and *q*? Answering this would be valuable for practitioners considering its adoption. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and sound methodology for parallel Bayesian optimization.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating improved performance over existing methods.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
*   Publicly available code promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Reliance on a Gaussian process prior may limit applicability to high-dimensional problems.
*   Lack of theoretical analysis (error bounds, complexity analysis).

**Minor Issues:**

*   l.36: "the set of points to evaluate next that is" – rephrase for conciseness (e.g., “the next set of points to evaluate”).
*   l.95: Define *A* when it’s first used.
*   l.104: Clarify the input of *μ<sup>n</sup>*.
*   l.128: Briefly discuss how parallel EI is handled/compared.
*   l.140: Avoid reusing notation *A*.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. While the lack of theoretical guarantees and the reliance on a GP prior are weaknesses, the sound methodology, compelling empirical results, and clear presentation justify acceptance. Addressing the theoretical aspects in future work would further enhance the impact of this research.
