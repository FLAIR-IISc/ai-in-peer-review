This paper proposes the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization (BO). The goal is to select a batch of `q` points to evaluate in parallel, which is a critical problem for accelerating BO in applications like hyperparameter tuning where distributed computing resources are available. The proposed q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, selecting the batch of points that provides the largest one-step expected improvement in the final objective value, where the final recommendation is the minimum of the posterior mean. This principled approach generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient [7] to the parallel setting.

A key challenge is that naively optimizing the q-KG function is computationally intractable. The authors' main technical contribution is an efficient method to optimize it using a stochastic gradient-based approach. They derive an unbiased estimator for the gradient of the q-KG factor using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), which allows for the use of standard gradient ascent optimizers. The paper relates this work to prior art in parallel BO, correctly pointing out that many existing methods (e.g., based on parallelizing EI or UCB) rely on greedy or heuristic batch construction, whereas q-KG jointly optimizes the entire batch of `q` points.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The paper tackles an important and highly relevant problem. As machine learning models become more complex and expensive to train, the need for efficient parallel optimization methods grows. This work provides a practical and high-performing solution for batch BO, which is likely to be used by both researchers and practitioners.

2.  **Principled Approach:** The q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated from a decision-theoretic standpoint, representing the one-step Bayes-optimal choice of points. This provides a stronger theoretical foundation than many heuristic approaches to batch selection and naturally handles noisy evaluations, which is a major advantage.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The method for efficiently computing the stochastic gradient of q-KG via IPA is a significant technical contribution. This makes the principled but complex acquisition function practical to optimize. The proposed discretization scheme to handle continuous domains is also sensible and builds upon existing techniques in the literature.

4.  **Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough, comparing q-KG against several state-of-the-art parallel BO methods on a range of synthetic benchmarks and two real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR-10). The results convincingly demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or superior to existing methods, with particularly strong performance in the noisy evaluation setting. This empirical evidence strongly supports the paper's claims.

5.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The motivation is clear, the method is described in sufficient detail, and the connection to prior work is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Applicability to High-Dimensional Problems:** The method relies on a Gaussian Process (GP) prior. While effective in the low-to-moderate dimensional problems tested (d<=8), standard GPs are known to scale poorly and perform less reliably in high-dimensional spaces. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this limitation, especially in the context of tuning very high-dimensional models like large neural network architectures where the assumptions of a GP may be less appropriate.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper's claims are supported entirely by empirical results. While the decision-theoretic motivation is strong, the work would be more complete with some formal theoretical analysis. For instance, providing regret bounds for the algorithm, similar to those developed for UCB-style methods, would provide stronger guarantees. Additionally, a quantitative analysis of the computational complexity with respect to the input dimension `d`, batch size `q`, and number of discretization points `M` would be very valuable for users to understand the trade-offs of the method.

3.  **Minor Issues and Clarifications:**
    *   l.36: There is a minor grammatical error: "...the set of points to evaluate next that is". This should likely be "...the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal...".
    *   l.95: In equation (4.1), the notation `q-KG(z(1:q),A)` is introduced. It would be clearer to explicitly define `A` in this context as the domain over which the minimum of the posterior mean is taken. This becomes clearer later in Section 5, but defining it upfront would improve readability.
    *   l.104: In Section 5.1, `µ(n)(z(1:q))` is used. To avoid ambiguity, it might be helpful to clarify that `µ(n)` is the posterior mean function, and it is being evaluated at the `q` candidate locations in the vector `z(1:q)`.
    *   l.128: When introducing the gradient computation, it might be useful to briefly contrast this IPA-based approach with gradient-based methods used for parallel EI (e.g., [15, 25]), as this would better highlight the novelty of the computational technique.
    *   l.140: The notation `A` is somewhat overloaded. It is used for the continuous feasible domain in (4.2), and then `An` is used for its discretization in (5.4). Consider using a distinct symbol for the finite set used to approximate the expectation in order to avoid any potential confusion between the optimization domain and the set used for the inner computation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a solid and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is theoretically sound, the computational approach is clever and enables its practical use, and the experimental results are strong and convincing. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. Despite the lack of theoretical bounds and potential scalability concerns in very high dimensions, the work clearly advances the state of the art in batch Bayesian optimization. I recommend acceptance.
