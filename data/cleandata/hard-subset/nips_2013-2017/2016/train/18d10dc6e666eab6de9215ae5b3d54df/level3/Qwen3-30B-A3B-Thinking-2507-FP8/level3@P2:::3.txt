

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) that selects multiple points to evaluate simultaneously in parallel computing environments. The authors generalize the knowledge gradient (KG) framework, originally designed for sequential optimization, to the batch setting. They derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the expected minimum of the Gaussian process predictor when only one batch remains. To address the computational intractability of directly maximizing this acquisition function, they develop an efficient method using Monte Carlo sampling combined with infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients for optimization.

The paper builds upon a solid foundation of previous work in Bayesian optimization. It correctly references the sequential KG method (Frazier et al., 2009) and recent extensions to parallel settings like parallel EI (Ginsbourger et al., 2010), GP-BUCB (Contal et al., 2014), and GP-UCB-PE (Desautels et al., 2011). The authors also appropriately distinguish their work from related approaches such as the nested batch knowledge gradient (Wang et al., 2016), noting differences in problem structure and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**

*Quality*: The paper presents a technically sound approach with proper theoretical grounding. The derivation of q-KG is clear and well-motivated, and the authors provide a careful analysis of the computational challenges. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, testing on multiple synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). Results consistently show that q-KG outperforms or competes with state-of-the-art batch BO methods, especially in noisy settings where the advantage is particularly pronounced.

*Clarity*: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are detailed and accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian optimization. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is straightforward, and the experimental setup is well-explained. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation details and hyperparameter choices.

*Originality*: While the core idea of extending KG to batch settings is not entirely novel, the authors provide a principled decision-theoretic derivation that differs from previous iterative approaches. The computational approach using IPA to estimate gradients is a useful technical contribution that makes the method practically applicable. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work on batch BO.

*Significance*: The problem addressed is highly relevant to practical machine learning applications where parallel evaluations are common (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple GPUs). The demonstrated performance improvements, particularly in noisy settings, make this method potentially valuable for practitioners. The code availability further enhances its practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**

*Originality*: The novelty is somewhat limited as the paper primarily extends existing knowledge gradient concepts to the batch setting with a standard gradient estimation technique. The key innovation appears to be the specific application of IPA to this problem rather than a fundamentally new approach. The authors could better articulate how this differs from the parallel EI optimization methods described in Chevalier et al. (2013) and Marmin et al. (2015).

*Clarity*: Equation (3.1) appears to have a notational inconsistency where µ and K are defined for vectors rather than sequences x^(1:n), which could confuse readers. The paper would benefit from clarifying this notation.

*Quality*: The theoretical analysis could be strengthened by providing more rigorous guarantees about the quality of the IPA approximation. While the empirical results are strong, a more thorough comparison with the most recent batch BO methods (e.g., Shah and Ghahramani, 2015) would further establish the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a practical problem with significant relevance to machine learning practitioners
- Provides a theoretically grounded approach with clear empirical advantages over state-of-the-art methods
- Well-written with comprehensive experiments demonstrating consistent improvements
- Code is made publicly available, enhancing reproducibility

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Novelty is somewhat limited as it primarily extends existing knowledge gradient concepts
- The computational technique (IPA) is standard rather than novel
- Some key distinctions from related work could be more clearly articulated

**Final recommendation:** This paper presents a useful extension to batch Bayesian optimization with clear empirical advantages, particularly in noisy settings. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a practical problem with significant relevance to the machine learning community. The method could be valuable for practitioners working on hyperparameter tuning and other expensive black-box optimization problems. The minor issues with notation and novelty could be addressed in a revision, but the paper is already strong as submitted.

I recommend acceptance.
