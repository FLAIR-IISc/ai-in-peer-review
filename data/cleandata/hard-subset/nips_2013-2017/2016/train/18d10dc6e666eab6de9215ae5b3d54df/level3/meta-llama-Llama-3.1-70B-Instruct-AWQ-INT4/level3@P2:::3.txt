This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, for optimizing a derivative-free expensive-to-evaluate function. The algorithm is designed to select multiple sampling points per iteration, allowing for parallel objective evaluations. The authors generalize the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework to batch selection, which is computationally intractable, and use Monte Carlo plus Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients.

The q-KG algorithm is closely related to the KG method for the non-batch (sequential) setting, but the authors extend it to the parallel setting and develop more sophisticated computational techniques to optimize the acquisition function. The algorithm is derived using a decision-theoretic analysis that chooses the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior when there is only one batch of points remaining.

The paper is well-written and appears to be practically useful. The authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm and its components, including the Gaussian process prior, the acquisition function, and the optimization method. The empirical results show that the q-KG method outperforms or competes with confidence-bound-based approaches on several test problems, including synthetic functions and real-world applications.

However, I have some concerns about the novelty of the paper. While the authors extend the KG framework to batch selection, the main contribution of the paper seems to be the application of IPA to estimate gradients, which is a standard technique in gradient estimation. The authors also mention that the q-KG algorithm reduces to the parallel EI algorithm if function evaluations are noise-free and the final recommendation is restricted to the previous sampling decisions, which suggests that the q-KG algorithm may not be significantly different from existing methods.

In terms of technical comments, I found equation (3.1) to be unclear because mu and K are defined for vectors, not sequences x^(1:n). The authors should clarify this notation to avoid confusion.

Overall, I would rate the quality of the paper as follows:

* Quality: 8/10 (the paper is well-written, and the algorithm is clearly explained, but the novelty of the paper is limited)
* Clarity: 9/10 (the paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm, but some notations may be unclear)
* Originality: 6/10 (the paper extends the KG framework to batch selection, but the main contribution is the application of IPA to estimate gradients, which is a standard technique)
* Significance: 8/10 (the paper provides empirical results that show the q-KG method outperforms or competes with confidence-bound-based approaches on several test problems)
