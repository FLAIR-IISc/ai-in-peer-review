- Contribution: Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that generalizes sequential knowledge gradient to select q points jointly under a GP prior.  
- Objective: Defines batch KG to maximize expected decrease in the GP posterior predictor’s minimum after observing a batch.  
- Methodology: Uses Monte Carlo and an infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) estimator to approximate acquisition and gradients, then performs gradient-based batch optimization.  
- Continuous domains: Handles inner minimization by adaptive discretization of the domain.  
- Empirical results: q-KG is competitive with or outperforms parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE, and PPES-like methods, with strong gains in noisy settings.  
- Relation to prior work: Clearly connects to EI/UCB/ES/PPES literature and extends KG to parallel settings, acknowledging related q-EI gradient work.  
- Technical quality: Derivation is largely sound; Monte Carlo+IPA approach is reasonable and standard.  
- Missing theory/analysis: Needs explicit statement of technical assumptions (differentiability, unique minimizers), and lacks complexity/variance analysis of the estimator w.r.t. q, M, n.  
- Notation/clarity issues: Eq. (3.1) and definitions of μ, K, x(1:n), D(n), Zq, and conditioning in (4.1) need clearer, dimension-explicit notation.  
- Reproducibility details: Should report number of Monte Carlo samples, step sizes, and stopping criteria.  
- Originality: Moderate—main novelty is batch extension of KG using standard IPA; incremental relative to existing q-EI/PPES work.  
- Significance: High practical relevance for parallel evaluations, especially under noise; open-sourced code increases impact.  
- Pros: Principled decision-theoretic batch acquisition, strong empirical performance in noise, practical stochastic-gradient optimization, clear exposition and released code.  
- Cons: Limited novelty, theoretical guarantees confined to discretized one-step setting, missing complexity/variance analysis, some notation ambiguities, and missing comparison to batch Thompson sampling.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — well-executed, practically useful contribution.
