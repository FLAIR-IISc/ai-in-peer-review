This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), an acquisition function for batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization that jointly selects q points by maximizing the expected one-step improvement in the GP posterior’s predicted minimum. The method is decision-theoretically motivated: it is Bayes-optimal for the last batch when the final recommendation is the minimizer of the posterior mean over the continuous domain, and it reduces to parallel EI under noiseless evaluations when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points. The authors develop an efficient Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG and an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization over batches. For continuous domains, they approximate the inner min over A by a dynamically updated discrete set built from posterior samples of likely optima, augmented with previously evaluated and candidate points. Experiments on synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and CNNs (CIFAR-10) show consistent improvements over parallel EI (both heuristic and q-EI implementations), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with particularly strong gains under observational noise. Code availability is a plus.

Relation to prior work: The paper extends the knowledge gradient literature [7, 17] from sequential to batch settings, distinct from parallel EI and its differentiable variants [2, 15, 25], and from parallel UCB algorithms focused on cumulative regret [3, 5]. It also complements parallel entropy search [11, 18]. Compared to nested batch KG on discrete spaces [26], this work targets continuous domains and leverages differentiability to obtain efficient stochastic gradients.

Quality: The formulation is sound and clearly connects to the Bayes-optimal one-step decision under a GP prior. The Monte Carlo computation of q-KG and the IPA gradient estimator are reasonable; conditions for unbiasedness (differentiable kernel, tie events of measure zero) are discussed. The discretization strategy is standard and practical. Empirical evaluation is thorough for the intended scope and demonstrates robustness to noise, with reasonable baselines and publicly available implementations. However, the paper would benefit from:
- Theoretical results on approximation error: bounds induced by finite Monte Carlo sampling and by discretizing A (e.g., how q-KG(z, A_n) approximates q-KG(z, A) as a function of M and posterior smoothness).
- Complexity analysis: explicit per-iteration computational and memory costs as functions of n (data size), d (dimension), q (batch size), M (discretization size), and S (number of Monte Carlo samples). For example, the cost of Cholesky factorizations for q×q and n×n matrices, and the amortized cost of gradient evaluations.
- Discussion of scalability and sensitivity: performance in higher-dimensional settings and under GP mis-specification, particularly for neural architecture-like spaces where Euclidean kernels may be inappropriate.

Clarity: The manuscript is generally well-written and organized, with clear algorithmic summaries and empirical setups. Some small issues and clarifications would improve readability:
- l.36: grammar: “the set of points to evaluate next that is” → “the set of points to evaluate next that are” or rephrase to avoid ambiguity.
- l.95: explicitly define A upon first use (domain, constraints, and whether it is continuous or discrete).
- l.104: clarify the arguments and dependence of µ(n)(x) (posterior mean after n observations; whether hyperparameters are re-estimated each iteration via MLE, as later used).
- l.128: briefly recap parallel EI variants and their optimization challenges to motivate why q-KG’s joint, Bayes-optimal formulation is preferable.
- l.140: avoid reusing A for both the continuous domain and its discretized proxy; adopt distinct notation (e.g., A for domain and A_n or Ā_n for the evolving discretization).
- Minor typos in Algorithm 1 (“Main Stange” → “Main Stage”).
- Specify the number of Monte Carlo samples and gradient-ascent settings (learning rate, restarts, stopping criteria) to aid reproducibility.

Originality: Extending KG to batch selection with an efficient stochastic gradient via IPA is a novel and nontrivial contribution, distinct from prior parallel EI/ES/UCB approaches. The decision-theoretic interpretation (Bayes-optimal one-step in the batch setting) is compelling and differentiates the method from heuristics and regret-minimization policies.

Significance: Batch BO is practically important in distributed environments. Demonstrated gains, especially with noisy observations, are meaningful and likely to influence subsequent work and practice. The open-source implementation and comparisons against widely used toolkits (MOE, Spearmint, Gpoptimization) increase impact. The main limitation is the reliance on GP assumptions and discretization, which may challenge very high-dimensional or highly structured spaces; even so, the approach is an important advance for moderate-dimensional BO.

Requests for authors:
- Provide finite-sample error bounds for the q-KG estimator (Monte Carlo and discretization) and discuss convergence as M,S → ∞.
- Add a quantitative complexity analysis and empirical runtime scaling with q, n, d, M, S; include wall-clock comparisons alongside regret curves.
- Discuss applicability and robustness in higher-dimensional or structured search spaces (e.g., neural architectures), including potential extensions (input warping, kernels on discrete structures, additive or sparse GPs).
- Include sensitivity analyses to GP hyperparameters, noise variance estimation, and discretization size M; report ablations on the choice of candidate set construction.
- Consider larger batch sizes (e.g., q ≥ 8) to illustrate scaling and behavior.

Pros:
- Clear decision-theoretic foundation; Bayes-optimal one-step batch criterion.
- Efficient and principled stochastic gradient via IPA; practical algorithm.
- Strong empirical results, especially under noise; code released.
- Joint batch optimization rather than greedy selection.

Cons:
- No theoretical error bounds or convergence guarantees for the approximate estimator.
- Limited complexity and scalability analysis; unclear behavior in high d.
- Dependence on GP prior and discretization may limit applicability to complex or structured domains.

Overall assessment: Solid contribution. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and empirically convincing, with clear advantages in noisy batch BO. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions addressing clarity and with an encouragement to include complexity analysis and estimator error bounds.
