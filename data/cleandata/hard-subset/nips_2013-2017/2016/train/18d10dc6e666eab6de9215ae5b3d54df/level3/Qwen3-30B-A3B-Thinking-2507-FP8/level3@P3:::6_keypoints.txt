- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition function derived from the knowledge gradient principle for parallel evaluations.  
- Develops an IPA-based estimator to compute gradients for acquisition maximization.  
- The method is theoretically grounded in decision theory and extends sequential KG to the batch setting.  
- Relates to prior BO work, especially Frazier et al. (2009) sequential KG and parallels to GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE.  
- Strength: clear theoretical foundation and accessible exposition of GP and BO background.  
- Strength: technically sound IPA-based computational approach.  
- Strength: experiments show q-KG outperforming other batch BO methods on synthetic and real tasks.  
- Weakness: insufficient experimental evidence—no wall-clock time measurements or comparisons to random search/sequential BO to demonstrate practical speedups.  
- Weakness: no justification for fixed batch size q=4 across experiments; batch size sensitivity not studied.  
- Novelty concern: claim appears overstated because Wang et al. (2015) proposed parallel EI with IPA-based gradients; the paper does not clearly distinguish q-KG from that work.  
- Major request: explicitly compare to Wang et al. (2015), detailing acquisition differences and why q-KG should outperform parallel EI.  
- Major request: analyze scalability to larger batch sizes (e.g., q=10,100) and report computational complexity dependence on q.  
- Major request: discuss/extend to asynchronous parallel evaluations for practical distributed use.  
- Major request: report wall-clock performance and computational overhead per batch.  
- Major request: clarify the x-axis "iterations" in plots (batches vs. evaluations, inclusion of initial design, differing starting values).  
- Minor issues: possible missing z^(1:n) in Eq. (4.1), a grammar typo ("achieve"→"achieves"), and incorrect attribution of IPA's origin to Wang et al. (2015).  
- Overall: solid technical work with good clarity and potential impact, but empirical validation and novelty differentiation are currently insufficient for strong acceptance.
