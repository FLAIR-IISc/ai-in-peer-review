- Proposes q-KG: a batch BO acquisition that selects q points by maximizing expected one-step reduction in the GP posterior mean minimum.  
- Shows q-KG reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless, sampled-points final-recommendation setting and connects to sequential KG.  
- Introduces a Monte Carlo estimator and an unbiased IPA-based gradient estimator to enable stochastic gradient optimization of the joint batch objective.  
- Uses a dynamically updated discretization (samples near posterior optima) to optimize acquisition in continuous domains.  
- Empirical results: competitive in noiseless cases and shows largest gains under noisy evaluations; tested on synthetic functions and HPO (MNIST logistic, CIFAR-10 CNN); code released.  
- Well situated among BO acquisition and batch methods; closest prior work includes q-EI with joint optimization and parallel ES—needs clearer distinction from these.  
- Technical soundness: conditional normal formulation and IPA gradient derivation are valid with stated smoothness assumptions; unbiasedness assumptions detailed in supplement.  
- Discretization strategy is standard but introduces unquantified approximation error that is not analyzed.  
- Experimental gaps: missing end-to-end wall-clock timings (including acquisition overhead), no comparisons to random search or sequential BO, and fixed batch size q=4 without scalability analysis.  
- Scalability concern: no experiments varying q or reporting how computation/time scale with q, M (discretization size), or MC samples.  
- Clarity: generally clear with good background and implementation details, but some notation/figure conventions are confusing and the evolving-discretization description is brief (M choice and cost unclear).  
- Originality: incremental—IPA for acquisition gradients is known; novelty lies in the specific q-KG objective and its efficient gradient evaluation but needs stronger distinction from q-EI/q-ES.  
- Significance: high potential for parallel HPO if it outperforms alternatives at similar wall-clock cost, but lacking scalability and timing evidence limits assessment of practical impact.  
- Pros for acceptance: clear decision-theoretic objective, sound stochastic gradient estimator for a hard acquisition, consistent noisy-case empirical gains, and released code.  
- Cons against acceptance: missing wall-clock and stronger baseline comparisons, no q variation/scalability studies, incomplete positioning vs. q-EI/q-ES, and limited to synchronous setting (no asynchrony experiments/discussion).  
- Major requests: compare to q-EI with stochastic gradients (and q-ES), provide complexity per gradient in q/M/MC, evaluate performance for q∈{1,4,8,16,32}, discuss/adapt to asynchronous settings, and report end-to-end times to reach targets.  
- Suggested experiments/details: ablate discretization size M and sampling for An, report MC sample counts and sensitivity, break down acquisition vs. training time in HPO, discuss numerical stability and hyperparameter refit frequency, and test a higher-dimensional synthetic task (d≥10).  
- Minor fixes: correct Eq. (4.1) conditioning and A overloading, fix typos, attribute IPA to earlier work (e.g., L’Ecuyer 1990), standardize citations/URLs, and clarify figure axes (immediate vs. simple regret and whether lower is better).
