Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), an acquisition function for batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization that selects q points jointly by maximizing the expected reduction in the GP posterior predictive minimum after one batch of evaluations. By construction, q-KG is Bayes-optimal for the one-step setting (minimizing simple regret of the posterior predictor after a single remaining batch). The authors show that in the special case of noiseless observations and when the final recommendable point is constrained to be among evaluated points, q-KG reduces to batch EI, thereby connecting it to a widely used heuristic.

Computationally, evaluating and optimizing q-KG is challenging because the inner expectation depends on the outcomes of the entire batch. The paper derives a reparameterization of the updated posterior mean via a standard normal Zq and uses infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator of q-KG, enabling gradient-based optimization over the q points. For continuous domains, the paper approximates the inner minimization by a dynamically updated finite set An constructed from samples near posterior optima, augmented with previously sampled points and the current batch. Experiments on standard synthetic functions and on tuning logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR10) show consistent improvements over batch EI (two implementations), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with especially large gains when evaluations are noisy. The work extends the knowledge gradient literature from sequential (e.g., [Frazier et al.]) to batch settings and complements prior batch BO methods including batch EI [Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.; MOE], GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE [Desautels et al.; Contal et al.], and entropy-search-based approaches [Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani].

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic foundation: q-KG is the Bayes-optimal one-step batch policy for the GP posterior predictor.
- Solid computational contribution: a practical stochastic gradient estimator via IPA/reparameterization and a reasonable discretization scheme; the method is implementable and code is released.
- Empirical performance: consistent improvements over strong batch baselines across both synthetic and real ML tuning tasks; the gains are pronounced under observation noise, a regime where heuristics can underperform.
- Good connection to prior work: reduction to batch EI in the noiseless, constrained-recommendation setting helps position the contribution.
- Writing is generally clear and well-organized.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theory beyond one-step optimality: The paper would benefit from guarantees such as simple-regret bounds, asymptotic consistency, or performance bounds quantifying the effect of discretization An and Monte Carlo error. Even high-level assumptions under which q-KG is consistent would strengthen the contribution.
- Complexity and scaling: Please provide a quantitative complexity analysis in terms of q (batch size), d (dimension), M = |An|, and the number of Monte Carlo samples and gradient steps (e.g., per acquisition evaluation and per outer optimization iteration). Empirical wall-clock comparisons (time-to-target) would also help.
- High-dimensional settings: While GP surrogates work well up to moderate dimensions, their inductive bias may be mismatched for very high-dimensional or structured spaces (e.g., neural architectures). A discussion of applicability limits, potential use of embeddings/ARD/input warping, or structured kernels would be useful.
- Discretization sensitivity: Results fix M = 1000; please report sensitivity to M and to the construction of An (e.g., number of posterior-optima samples, inclusion of local minima, effect on variance of the gradient estimator).
- Baselines: Given the emphasis on valuing batches jointly, a direct comparison to parallel ES/q-PES (e.g., Shah & Ghahramani) would further contextualize the gains, or please justify its omission (e.g., computational budget or implementation availability).
- Noise modeling: The method assumes known or homoscedastic Gaussian noise estimated by MLE. A brief discussion of robustness to misspecified noise (heteroscedasticity, heavy tails) would be helpful.

Quality
The technical development is sound. The derivation of the reparameterization and the use of IPA for unbiased stochastic gradients are appropriate; pointers to supplemental proofs are noted. Experimental design is careful, with multiple benchmarks and realistic tuning tasks; results are convincing, especially under noise. The main gaps are the absence of regret/error bounds and a detailed complexity analysis.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-structured. The distinction between the continuous domain and the discretized set used inside the inner minimization is important and largely well handled. Suggestions for clarity and minor edits are listed below.

Originality
Extending KG to the batch setting with an efficient, differentiable Monte Carlo objective and an IPA-based gradient estimator is novel and useful. The connection to parallel EI and the explicit Bayes-optimality characterization in the one-batch setting are valuable.

Significance
The problem is important for leveraging parallel resources in expensive black-box optimization. Given the strong empirical results and the principled foundation, practitioners and researchers are likely to adopt/build on q-KG, particularly for noisy evaluations.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled acquisition with clear one-step Bayes-optimality.
- Practical gradient-based optimization via IPA; open-source implementation.
- Strong empirical gains over widely used batch BO baselines, especially with noise.
- Clear relation to and generalization of existing methods (batch EI).

Cons for acceptance
- Lacks regret/consistency/error bounds and a formal complexity analysis.
- Limited discussion of high-dimensional applicability and discretization sensitivity.
- Missing comparison to other joint-batch acquisitions like q-PES.

Minor comments and typos
- l.36: Grammar: “the set of points to evaluate next that is Bayes-optimal” (or rephrase to avoid double “that”).
- l.95: Define A explicitly at first use (e.g., A ⊆ R^d, feasible domain).
- l.104: Clarify the input of µ(n): write µ(n)(x) consistently; when writing minx∈A µ(n)(x), ensure x is the argument.
- l.128: When introducing related work, briefly summarize how parallel EI is typically constructed (greedy vs. joint optimization) before presenting your reduction of q-KG to EI in the noiseless, constrained case.
- l.140 and Section 5: Avoid reusing A for both the continuous domain and the finite discretization in (4.1). You already introduce An; consider using X for the continuous domain and A (or S) for the discrete set to prevent confusion throughout.
- Provide details on the number of Monte Carlo samples for evaluating q-KG and its gradient, variance-reduction techniques (if any), stopping criteria, and batch-size sensitivity.
- Consider reporting wall-clock time per acquisition optimization and per iteration, and time-to-best metrics.

Overall recommendation
This is a solid, well-executed paper with a principled acquisition function, an efficient optimization strategy, and strong empirical results on relevant tasks. Addressing the requests for theoretical bounds and a complexity analysis would further strengthen it, but I view these as enhancements rather than blockers. I lean toward acceptance.
