- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization method that generalizes knowledge gradient to select q points jointly per iteration.  
- Defines acquisition as expected one-step reduction of the GP posterior minimum after a batch, Bayes-optimal when one batch remains.  
- Derives an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and discretizes inner minimization over A for tractability.  
- Empirically outperforms parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning, with clear gains under noise.  
- Correctly extends sequential KG and parallels multi-point EI/entropy-search by optimizing the batch collectively instead of greedily.  
- Derivation is sound; connection to q-EI in the noiseless case is valid and IPA estimator is appropriate under differentiability assumptions.  
- Experiments are carefully executed, convincing, and code availability is a positive.  
- Missing theoretical guarantees: no Bayes simple-regret bounds or consistency results for the batch policy.  
- Missing quantitative complexity analysis in q, d, M (discretization size), Monte Carlo samples, and GP hyperparameter updates, including wall-clock comparisons.  
- Minor clarity issues: grammatical fixes (l.36), define A at first use (l.95), clarify μ^n(·) inputs (l.104), discuss parallel EI variants (l.128), avoid reusing A for domain and discretization (l.140).  
- Originality: principled Bayes-optimal batch KG with efficient gradient estimator, distinct from parallel EI, UCB-based policies, and parallel ES/PES.  
- Significance: addresses practical need for parallel evaluations; empirical improvements, especially in noisy settings, imply immediate practitioner impact.  
- Concern: GP priors may not suit high-dimensional or structured spaces (e.g., architectures); discuss alternatives like additive/embedded kernels, input warping, or deep kernels and scaling with d.  
- Suggestion: provide sensitivity analysis to discretization size M and Monte Carlo sample count; discuss acquisition optimization convergence and failure modes.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid principled method, efficient implementation, and strong empirical gains in noisy settings.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of theoretical error/complexity guarantees and limited discussion of high-dimensional applicability and scalability.
