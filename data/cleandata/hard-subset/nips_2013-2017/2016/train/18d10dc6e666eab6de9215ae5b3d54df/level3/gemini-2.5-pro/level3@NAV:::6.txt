This paper proposes the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), a batch Bayesian optimization method for parallel hyperparameter optimization. The method selects a batch of points that is one-step Bayes-optimal by maximizing the expected improvement in the final solution quality. To make the acquisition function maximization tractable, the authors propose using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute an unbiased stochastic gradient. This work extends the sequential knowledge gradient to the parallel setting and is related to previous work on parallel acquisition functions like parallel Expected Improvement (q-EI), particularly methods that also use IPA for gradient-based optimization (e.g., Wang et al., 2015).

The paper's main strengths are its focus on a significant and practical problem and its clear presentation. Parallel hyperparameter optimization is highly relevant for practitioners using modern multi-core systems. The background material and technical derivations are well-explained, making the proposed method easy to follow.

However, there are several major concerns regarding the paper's novelty and the completeness of its experimental evaluation. The primary concern is the originality of the technical contribution. The use of IPA to optimize a parallel acquisition function is not new, as demonstrated by Wang et al. (2015) for q-EI. The paper must clearly articulate its novelty in relation to this prior work. Have the authors compared their method, q-KG, against the IPA-based q-EI from Wang et al.?

The experimental evidence is not fully convincing. A critical omission is the lack of wall-clock time comparisons. Without measuring the computational overhead of selecting each batch and comparing it to baselines like parallel random search or sequential BO, the practical speedup of the method remains unclear. Furthermore, the batch size is fixed at q=4 across all experiments without justification. How does the method's performance and computational overhead scale as `q` grows to tens or hundreds, which are common in large-scale settings? The plots are also unclear: does the x-axis "iterations" refer to batches or cumulative function evaluations?

Additional questions for the authors include: can this synchronous method be extended to an asynchronous setting, which is often more practical? Finally, there are minor issues: a potential missing term in Eq.(4.1), a typo (“achieve”→“achieves”), and the citation to Wang et al. (2015) incorrectly crediting them with introducing IPA.

In summary, while the paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach, the claims of superiority are not sufficiently supported, and the novelty needs to be better situated within existing literature. Major revisions are required to address these experimental and clarity issues.
