### **Review**

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper proposes the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, a novel algorithm for batch Bayesian optimization. The core idea is to select a batch of `q` points that jointly maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality, assuming it is the last batch to be evaluated. This formulation makes the selection criterion one-step Bayes-optimal. To overcome the computational challenge of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function, which lacks a closed-form expression, the authors employ infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an efficient stochastic gradient estimator, enabling the use of gradient-based optimizers.

This work extends the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy [7, 17] to the parallel, batch setting. It contributes to a growing literature on parallel Bayesian optimization, which includes methods based on Expected Improvement (e.g., [10, 25]), Upper Confidence Bounds (e.g., [3, 5]), and Entropy Search [18]. Unlike many heuristic or greedy batch construction methods, q-KG offers a more principled, decision-theoretic foundation for selecting the entire batch jointly. The use of IPA for gradient-based optimization of a parallel acquisition function is similar to the approach used for parallel EI by Wang et al. [25], a connection that could be discussed in more detail. The authors evaluate the method on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression and CNNs, showing it is competitive in noise-free settings and superior in noisy settings compared to other parallel BO methods.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The problem of parallelizing expensive black-box optimization is highly relevant and important, particularly for hyperparameter optimization in deep learning where parallel compute resources are widely available. Methods that can effectively use this parallelism are of great interest to the community.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The proposed q-KG method is theoretically well-motivated, providing a principled, Bayes-optimal criterion for one-step batch selection. This is an elegant alternative to more heuristic approaches for constructing batches.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are promising. The method demonstrates strong performance, especially in noisy environments, which is a common and important practical scenario (e.g., when evaluating models on subsets of data or dealing with stochastic algorithms).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background, the derivation of the q-KG factor, and the computational approach using IPA are explained clearly, making the work understandable and potentially reproducible.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** While the results are positive, the experimental evaluation is not fully convincing and lacks key details needed to assess the method's practical utility.
    *   The experiments are missing wall-clock time comparisons. The primary motivation for parallel optimization is to reduce total time to solution. Without reporting the computational overhead required to select each batch, it is impossible to judge if q-KG offers a real-world speedup over simpler baselines like parallel random search or even running multiple instances of sequential BO.
    *   The batch size is fixed at q=4 across all experiments without justification. This leaves open important questions about the method's performance and computational scalability as `q` increases.
*   **Novelty:** The core technical components, the Knowledge Gradient criterion and the use of Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis, are not new. The use of IPA to optimize a Monte Carlo-based parallel acquisition function has been previously explored for parallel EI by Wang et al. [25]. The paper should more clearly delineate its conceptual and technical novelty in this context.
*   **Practical Limitations:** The method assumes a synchronous parallel setting, where all `q` evaluations start and end at the same time. This limits its applicability in common asynchronous environments where evaluation times can vary significantly. An extension to the asynchronous case is not discussed.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Addresses an important and timely problem in machine learning (parallel HPO).
    *   Proposes a principled, decision-theoretic acquisition function (q-KG) for batch selection.
    *   Demonstrates strong empirical performance, particularly in the challenging and practical noisy setting.

*   **Con:**
    *   The experimental evaluation is incomplete; the lack of wall-clock time measurements makes it difficult to assess practical impact.
    *   The novelty of the technical approach is questionable, given prior work using IPA for parallel EI.
    *   Key practical aspects, such as scalability with batch size `q` and applicability to asynchronous settings, are not addressed.

**Detailed Questions and Suggestions**

To strengthen the paper, I would encourage the authors to address the following points:

**Major Questions:**

1.  **Comparison to Wang et al. (2015):** The paper cites Wang et al. [25] but the contribution relative to this work is not clearly stated. Wang et al. also use IPA to optimize a parallel acquisition function (q-EI). Could you explicitly compare your method to theirs and clarify the differences and advantages of q-KG over q-EI?
2.  **Scalability with Batch Size (q):** How does the method's performance (both in terms of optimization progress and computational cost) scale to larger batch sizes (e.g., q=16, 32, or more)? Understanding this is critical for users with access to large-scale parallel environments.
3.  **Asynchronous Extension:** Many practical parallel settings are asynchronous. Can the proposed synchronous method be extended to handle asynchronous function evaluations, where new points are chosen as soon as workers become free?
4.  **Wall-Clock Time Performance:** What is the wall-clock time performance of q-KG compared to parallel random search and sequential BO (e.g., running `q` independent sequential BO optimizers)? What is the computational overhead required to select each new batch? This is a crucial metric for evaluating a parallel optimization algorithm.
5.  **Clarification of Plots:** The x-axis in the experimental plots is labeled "iterations." Does this refer to the number of batches evaluated or the total number of function evaluations? Does it include the initial design points? Furthermore, why do the different methods appear to start at different performance levels at iteration 0/1? The initial design should be identical for a fair comparison.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In Eq. (4.1), the notation `y(z(1:q))` is used, but the points are `z(1:q)`. Is `z(1:n)` from the previous text a typo for `x(1:n)`?
*   In Section 6.1.2, "achieve a test error around 14%" should likely be "achieves".
*   In the related work, the citation for Wang et al. [25] seems to be mixed with the description of [15].
*   The text suggests Wang et al. [25] introduced IPA, but IPA [14] is a much older technique. Please clarify that they applied it in the context of parallel EI.
