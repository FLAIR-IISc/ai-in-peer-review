

**Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"**

**Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper proposes a batch Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG), extending the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting where multiple points can be evaluated simultaneously. The authors derive a batch version of KG that is Bayes-optimal for the parallel setting and propose a Monte Carlo gradient estimator based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently optimize the acquisition function. The paper claims q-KG outperforms previous batch BO methods on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, especially in noisy settings.

The paper builds on sequential KG [7, 17] and cites [2, 15, 25] as prior work on parallel acquisition functions. However, the authors fail to sufficiently distinguish their contribution from these prior works. [2] proposes a closed-form solution for parallel EI, [15] develops gradient-based optimization for parallel EI, and [25] studies a nested batch KG policy for finite discrete sets. The paper's claim of novelty is undermined by insufficient comparison to these works, particularly since [25] also uses gradient-based methods.

**Strengths**

1. The paper provides a theoretically grounded acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for the parallel setting, which is a valuable contribution to batch BO theory.

2. The experimental results are comprehensive, covering synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical ML applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10).

3. The paper demonstrates q-KG's particular advantage in noisy settings, a practically important observation.

4. The code is publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Novelty Concerns**: The paper does not clearly establish how its batch KG differs from prior work. The authors cite [25] as "recently studying a nested batch knowledge gradient policy," but their approach differs significantly in that [25] optimizes over a finite discrete feasible set where KG gradients don't exist, making their computation less efficient than the authors' approach. The paper should explicitly address this distinction and provide theoretical or empirical comparison of computational efficiency.

2. **Insufficient Detail on Section 5.2**: Section 5.2 (estimating the gradient of q-KG) is the paper's key technical contribution but is extremely brief. The authors need to provide more detail on the IPA implementation, computational complexity, and comparisons to alternative gradient estimation methods. A table comparing computational complexity with prior batch KG methods would be valuable.

3. **Problematic Comparison to EI**: The paper compares to Expected Improvement (EI) in Section 6.2 but EI assumes noiseless observations. The authors should clarify whether they used a noise-aware EI variant (e.g., as in [19]) or if they are comparing to noiseless EI on noisy problems. This comparison is problematic without addressing the noise assumption mismatch.

4. **Minor Issue**: The abbreviation "IPA" is redefined at line 162, which is inconsistent with standard practice. The authors should define it earlier in the paper.

**Additional Concerns**

The paper claims q-KG "reduces to parallel EI" in noise-free settings with final solution restricted to sampled points, but this is not clearly justified. The authors should explain why this is a special case rather than a fundamentally different acquisition function.

**Recommendation**

The paper addresses an important practical problem in Bayesian optimization, but the current version lacks sufficient novelty justification and technical detail to be compelling.

**Pro Arguments for Acceptance**:
- The method shows consistent empirical improvements over benchmarks, especially in noisy settings.
- The IPA-based gradient estimator is a potentially useful computational technique.
- The experimental design is thorough and covers both synthetic and practical problems.

**Con Arguments for Rejection**:
- The novelty of batch KG is not clearly established relative to prior work.
- Section 5.2 lacks sufficient detail to understand the computational contribution.
- The comparison to EI is invalid due to noise assumption mismatch.
- The paper doesn't provide empirical or theoretical comparison of the gradient estimator's efficiency.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to batch Bayesian optimization, but the current version doesn't sufficiently establish its novelty or technical contribution. The authors should address the novelty concerns by more clearly distinguishing from prior work, provide substantially more detail on the gradient estimator in Section 5.2, and clarify their comparison to EI by using noise-aware acquisition functions. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As written, it's borderline due to the novelty concerns and insufficient technical detail.
