

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which extends the sequential knowledge gradient method to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously, providing the one-step Bayes-optimal batch under the assumption of a Gaussian process prior. The paper's key technical contribution is an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize this acquisition function, which would otherwise be computationally prohibitive.

The paper builds on prior work in Bayesian optimization (BO), particularly the knowledge gradient (KG) method [7, 17] for sequential settings. It distinguishes itself from previous parallel BO approaches [3, 5, 18, 25] by considering the batch of points collectively rather than iteratively constructing the batch. This represents a meaningful theoretical advancement over existing methods that use greedy sequential optimization of single-point acquisition functions.

**Strengths:**

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong and well-motivated. The decision-theoretic justification for q-KG provides a principled approach to batch selection, and the IPA-based computational strategy is elegant and efficient. The paper is well-written with clear mathematical exposition, making the method accessible to the NIPS audience. The experimental evaluation demonstrates consistent improvements over benchmark algorithms (parallel EI, GP-UCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy settings where the gains are most pronounced.

**Weaknesses:**

The experimental evaluation has significant limitations that undermine the paper's claims of empirical superiority. The authors use test set error as the optimization criterion, which is problematic for hyperparameter tuning as it doesn't align with standard machine learning evaluation practices (e.g., AUC or log loss are more appropriate for model selection). The benchmarking is limited to just two datasets (MNIST and CIFAR10) and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN), which is insufficient to demonstrate the method's broad applicability. The paper would benefit from comparing against more comprehensive HPO benchmarks like HPOlib [1], which includes diverse machine learning tasks and algorithms. Additionally, the assumption of independent normally distributed observation noise is questionable in hyperparameter optimization, where structural bias and correlated errors from empirical estimates (e.g., cross-validation) are common.

**Quality:** The theoretical foundation is sound, and the computational approach is well-executed. However, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient breadth and depth to fully support the claims of superior performance.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and sufficient detail to reproduce the results. The mathematical notation is consistently used, and the algorithms are clearly described.

**Originality:** The extension of knowledge gradient to the parallel setting with a joint batch optimization is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the BO literature. The comparison to prior work in Section 2 is thorough and appropriately contextualizes the contribution.

**Significance:** The problem of batch Bayesian optimization is important for parallel computing environments, and the method has clear practical applications in hyperparameter tuning. However, the limited experimental evaluation reduces the significance of the contribution, as it's unclear how widely applicable the method is across different machine learning tasks.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Strong theoretical foundation with a principled decision-theoretic approach
- Elegant computational strategy using IPA for efficient optimization
- Well-written and clearly organized paper
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines, especially in noisy settings

*Con:*
- Experimental evaluation lacks breadth (only two datasets and two algorithms)
- Uses inappropriate optimization metric (test error) instead of standard ML evaluation metrics
- Assumption of independent normal noise is unrealistic for hyperparameter optimization
- Limited comparison against state-of-the-art HPO benchmarks

In conclusion, this paper presents a theoretically sound and well-executed extension of Bayesian optimization to the parallel setting. The method is promising and represents a meaningful advancement over existing approaches. However, the limited experimental evaluation, particularly the use of test set error as the optimization criterion and insufficient benchmarking across diverse machine learning tasks, significantly weakens the empirical claims. To strengthen the paper, the authors should expand their experimental evaluation to include more comprehensive HPO benchmarks and use standard machine learning evaluation metrics for hyperparameter tuning.

The paper is an interesting contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature, but the current empirical evaluation is insufficient to fully support the claims of superiority. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the experimental limitations in a revised version.
