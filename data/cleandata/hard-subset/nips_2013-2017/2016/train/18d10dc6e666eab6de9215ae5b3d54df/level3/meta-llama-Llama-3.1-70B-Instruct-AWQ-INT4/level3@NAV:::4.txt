This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG) for optimizing expensive black-box functions in parallel settings. The authors provide a decision-theoretic analysis to derive the q-KG acquisition function, which measures the information gain of evaluating q points collectively. They also develop an efficient strategy for computing the q-KG acquisition function and its gradient using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA).

The paper tackles an important problem in Bayesian optimization, which is speeding up expensive function evaluations via distributed or batch evaluation. The idea of proposing q points at each iteration is interesting and can substantially improve efficiency. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of q-KG through extensive experiments on both synthetic test functions and real-world problems, including tuning hyperparameters of practical machine learning algorithms.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical foundations. The experimental results are convincing, and the comparison with other state-of-the-art methods is thorough.

However, there are a few concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the assumption of a Gaussian process prior may not be appropriate for high-dimensional inputs, such as neural network architectures. The authors should discuss the limitations of their approach and potential extensions to more complex models.

Secondly, the paper lacks theoretical results, such as error bounds for the algorithm and a quantitative complexity analysis with respect to input dimension. Providing such results would strengthen the paper and provide a more complete understanding of the proposed method.

Minor issues and typos need to be addressed, such as the grammar error in line 36, the definition of A in line 95, the clarification of the input of mu^n in line 104, and the discussion of parallel EI in line 128.

Overall, the paper makes a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, and the proposed method is sound and well-evaluated. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, the paper has the potential to be a strong contender for acceptance.
