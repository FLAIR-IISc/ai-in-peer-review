Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition to select q points jointly per iteration. KG is known to be Bayes-optimal for a single remaining (sequential) evaluation under a simple-regret objective when the final recommendation need not be one of the sampled points (Frazier et al., 2008; Scott et al., 2011). The authors extend this decision-theoretic construction to the parallel setting by defining the expected reduction in the posterior GP predictor’s minimum after a batch of size q. They show that in the noise-free case with the recommendation restricted to previously sampled points, q-KG reduces to q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger, 2013; Marmin et al., 2015), thereby connecting to a well-studied parallel EI line of work.

Computationally, evaluating and optimizing q-KG exactly is intractable. The paper introduces a Monte Carlo estimator using the standard “fantasies” representation and derives an unbiased stochastic gradient via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling multi-start stochastic gradient ascent. For continuous domains, the inner minimization is approximated by discretizing A using samples from the posterior “near minima” (as in ES/PES; Hernández-Lobato et al., 2014; Shah & Ghahramani, 2015), augmented with previously sampled locations and the candidate batch itself.

Empirically, q-KG outperforms or matches strong baselines on synthetic benchmarks (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and on hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST; CNN on CIFAR-10), with particularly strong gains under observation noise. Comparisons include two implementations of parallel EI (MOE; Spearmint) and UCB-based batch policies (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE).

Strengths
- Sound decision-theoretic formulation: q-KG is Bayes-optimal for one remaining batch under simple-regret, a principled objective for BO.
- Practical stochastic gradient optimization via IPA with clear derivations for the finite/discretized setting; implementation details are provided and code is released.
- Strong empirical performance, especially in noisy settings where batch EI and UCB methods often struggle.
- Clear connection to prior acquisition functions: reduces to q-EI in the appropriate limit; relates to ES/PES family through the discretization strategy.
- Writing is generally clear, with useful algorithmic summaries and thorough experimental setup descriptions.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Novelty is incremental: extending KG to batch selection and using IPA-based gradients is a natural (though nontrivial) combination of known ingredients; prior work has considered batch EI optimization with similar stochastic gradient techniques.
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to one-step Bayes optimality; no analysis of finite-time performance, regret, or approximation error from discretization/MCMC/gradient estimation.
- Comparisons omit recent information-theoretic batch methods (e.g., parallel PES [Shah & Ghahramani, 2015]) in experiments, despite being discussed. Including these would better position the contribution.
- Scalability: computational complexity per acquisition optimization appears cubic in q (Cholesky) plus O(M q^2) per MC sample and gradient step. Wall-clock comparisons vs. baselines (especially UCB variants) would help quantify overhead.
- Discretization sensitivity: performance may depend on M and the sampling scheme for A_n. An ablation varying M and the sampling strategy (e.g., random LHD vs. posterior-minima sampling) would clarify robustness.
- Notational clarity: Equation (3.1) mixes vector/matrix and sequence notation. Please define µ(n)(x(1:n)) as a vector and K(x(1:n), x(1:n)) as the n×n Gram matrix explicitly; similarly for K(x, x(1:n)) and the noise diagonal.
- Gradient validity: the interchange of expectation and differentiation (Eq. 5.3) and differentiability through the “min” operator require conditions (e.g., almost-sure uniqueness of minimizers, smooth kernels). Summarize key assumptions and practical handling of ties/nondifferentiability in the main text.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction of q-KG and its Monte Carlo-IPA estimator is well-motivated and consistent with GP regression identities. The gradient estimator is standard under IPA; stronger justification of regularity assumptions would improve rigor.
- Empirical support: Results span multiple synthetic and two real tasks, with clear improvements in noisy settings. However, more diverse dimensions and larger q values would better test scalability; wall-clock or function-evaluations-per-hour metrics would balance effectiveness vs. overhead.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. Algorithm description is easy to follow. Improve notation around Eq. (3.1) and the dual use of A (domain vs. discretized set in the inner min). Consider renaming the discretized set (e.g., Ā_n) to avoid confusion.

Originality
- Moderate. The main idea—batch generalization of KG with Monte Carlo and IPA gradients—is a natural extension. The link to q-EI under noise-free restrictions is nice, and the finite-difference-free gradient derivation is useful but follows established techniques.

Significance
- Practical impact is solid: a myopic, Bayes-optimal-for-one-batch acquisition that empirically excels under noise fills a gap where EI/UCB can underperform. If computational overhead is acceptable, practitioners may adopt q-KG for noisy parallel tuning.

Detailed comments and questions
- Please report computational costs: time per acquisition optimization and total wall-clock per iteration across methods, and how costs scale with q and M.
- How many MC samples and gradient steps are used? Provide sensitivity plots vs. MC budget and restarts.
- Provide an ablation of the discretization M and the “posterior-minima” sampling strategy versus random or grid-based discretization.
- Discuss extension to constraints (e.g., Snoek et al., 2014/2015) and non-constant noise; is q-KG compatible with heteroscedastic likelihoods?
- For the CNN experiment, specify the search ranges and whether log-transforms were used for scale-sensitive parameters (e.g., learning rate, l2).

Pros for acceptance
- Principled acquisition with clear advantages under noise
- Solid empirical gains across multiple tasks
- Public code and practical implementability
- Bridges KG and batch BO with a workable optimization method

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to prior KG and batch EI work
- Missing comparisons to information-theoretic batch methods (PPES)
- Limited theoretical analysis beyond one-step myopic optimality
- Scalability and computational overhead not fully characterized

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper is well executed, practically useful, and demonstrates compelling improvements in noisy parallel BO. Addressing the missing comparisons and clarifying computational trade-offs would further strengthen the contribution.

Reviewer confidence: High on BO and acquisition design; moderate on implementation-specific scalability details.
