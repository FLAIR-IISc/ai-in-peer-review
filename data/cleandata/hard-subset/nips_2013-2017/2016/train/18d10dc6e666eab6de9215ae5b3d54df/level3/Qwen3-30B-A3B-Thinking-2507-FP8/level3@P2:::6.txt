

# Review for "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), designed to select multiple points for evaluation simultaneously. The method extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting, providing a Bayes-optimal batch of points to sample under the assumption of only one batch remaining. The authors develop an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function, enabling practical optimization. The method is evaluated on synthetic test functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression and CNNs on MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on Bayesian optimization, particularly the knowledge gradient method [17] and parallel Bayesian optimization approaches [3, 5, 18, 25]. While the paper builds on these foundations, the key innovation is the decision-theoretic derivation of a batch acquisition function that optimizes the collective value of multiple points, rather than sequentially adding points as in most prior work.

## Strengths
1. The theoretical foundation is sound, with a clear decision-theoretic justification for the q-KG acquisition function that extends the sequential KG method to the parallel setting.
2. The paper provides a detailed explanation of the IPA-based computational approach, which is well-motivated and technically rigorous.
3. The experimental evaluation covers both synthetic benchmarks and practical machine learning applications, demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines, particularly in noisy settings.
4. The implementation is made publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.
5. The paper is well-organized, with clear technical explanations that make the method accessible to the NIPS audience.

## Weaknesses
1. **Novelty concerns**: The paper does not adequately distinguish itself from Wang et al. (2015) ["Parallel Bayesian Global Optimization of Expensive Functions"] which also proposes an IPA-based parallel acquisition function (q-EI). The paper should clarify how q-KG differs from and improves upon this prior work, particularly since the authors cite Wang et al. but do not address their IPA approach.

2. **Experimental limitations**: The paper lacks critical comparisons to establish practical utility:
   - No wall-clock time measurements to show actual speedups over sequential BO or random search
   - No justification for fixing batch size q=4 across all experiments (why not test larger q?)
   - No comparison to sequential BO (which would be the natural baseline for parallel methods)
   - No discussion of computational overhead for computing each batch

3. **Clarity issues**: 
   - The x-axis in the plots ("iterations") is ambiguous - does it represent batches or total function evaluations? The starting values differ across methods, suggesting inconsistent initial designs.
   - Minor grammatical errors (e.g., "achieve" should be "achieves") and notation inconsistencies (missing z^(1:n) in Eq. 4.1).

4. **Scalability concerns**: The paper does not address how the method scales to larger batch sizes (q=10, 100+), which is crucial for practical parallel optimization scenarios.

## Major Questions for Authors
1. How does q-KG specifically differ from Wang et al. (2015) in terms of acquisition function and computational approach? The current paper does not clearly differentiate their method from prior IPA-based parallel EI approaches.

2. What are the expected benefits and limitations when scaling to larger batch sizes (e.g., q=10, 100)? The current evaluation with fixed q=4 does not address this important practical consideration.

3. Can the synchronous method be extended to handle asynchronous parallel evaluations, which are common in distributed computing environments?

4. What is the wall-clock time performance compared to random search and sequential BO? How much overhead does the batch computation add per iteration?

## Recommendation
The paper addresses a significant practical problem in hyperparameter optimization, where parallel evaluations are increasingly common. While the theoretical foundation is sound and the experimental results are promising, the lack of critical comparisons (particularly wall-clock time) and insufficient differentiation from prior work on IPA-based parallel optimization weaken the contribution.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Clarifying the novelty relative to Wang et al. (2015) and other parallel EI approaches
2. Providing wall-clock time measurements and comparisons to sequential BO
3. Justifying the choice of batch size q=4 and exploring larger batch sizes
4. Addressing the scalability to larger q and potential asynchronous extensions

Given the importance of parallel Bayesian optimization for real-world applications and the promising results on noisy problems, I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address these concerns. The paper has significant potential but requires more thorough experimental validation and clearer articulation of its contributions relative to prior work.
