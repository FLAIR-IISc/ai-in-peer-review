This paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that selects the Bayes-optimal set of q points to evaluate when one batch remains. The method generalizes the classical knowledge gradient (KG) from the sequential setting to parallel evaluation, defining the objective as the expected decrease in the GP posterior predictive minimum due to a batch of measurements. The authors show that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under noiseless evaluations when the final recommendation is restricted to previously sampled points, thus providing a unifying perspective. Because q-KG must be evaluated via Monte Carlo in the batch setting, the paper develops a stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling efficient gradient-based optimization over the batch. The implementation discretizes the candidate set for computing the inner minimum and regenerates this set at each iteration from samples near posterior optima. Empirically, q-KG is compared against parallel EI (two variants), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10. Results indicate clear gains, especially under noisy evaluations.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on KG for correlated normals and GP regression (Frazier et al., 2008; Scott et al., 2011), and contrasts with batch extensions of EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger, 2013; Marmin et al., 2015) and entropy-based methods (PES and PPES; Hernández-Lobato et al., 2014; Shah & Ghahramani, 2015). Compared to UCB-based batch policies (Desautels et al., 2014; Contal et al., 2013), q-KG explicitly optimizes immediate simple regret rather than cumulative regret, which aligns better with BO.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized exposition with a crisp decision-theoretic formulation that cleanly generalizes KG to the batch setting.
- Elegant computational approach: IPA-based stochastic gradients for the Monte Carlo objective are well motivated and appear to be unbiased and practical.
- Sound theoretical grounding: derivations for the gradient estimator and the reduction to parallel EI in the noise-free restricted-solution case are correct and insightful.
- Addresses noisy evaluations explicitly, where many batch BO methods (notably EI variants) are weaker; empirical results reflect this advantage.
- Code availability and reuse of established GP infrastructure increase reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Noise model: The method assumes independent, homoscedastic Gaussian observation noise. In hyperparameter optimization, noise is often heteroscedastic and correlated (e.g., k-fold CV reuses data, early stopping and resource allocation induce structural bias). The paper should discuss robustness, or consider models with heteroscedastic/structured noise (e.g., input-dependent noise GPs, Student-t likelihoods, or kernels over folds/replicates).
- Evaluation metric choice: Optimizing test set error for classification is threshold-dependent and non-smooth; metrics like cross-entropy (log loss) or AUC provide better signal and align with probabilistic GP modeling. Please justify the metric choice, or include results with log loss/AUC.
- Benchmark breadth: The empirical section is narrow—two datasets and two algorithms (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10). Given that BO performance is task-dependent, include broader HPO benchmarks (e.g., HPOlib/OpenML suites) and diverse learners (SVMs, random forests, gradient boosting, deeper CNNs) to substantiate generality.
- Baseline coverage: Comparisons omit some strong batch BO methods, notably PPES and more recent q-EI optimizers. Including these would position q-KG more convincingly.
- Computational aspects: While IPA helps, the approach relies on discretization of A and repeated Cholesky factorizations. The paper would benefit from complexity analysis and wall-clock results (acquisition optimization overhead vs evaluation time), scaling with q, dimension d, and discretization size M. Sensitivity to M, number of MC samples, and gradient variance should be reported.
- Practicalities: Re-fitting GP hyperparameters by MLE at every batch can interact with noisy observations; consider reporting robustness to mis-specified kernels/lengthscales and the effect of estimating the noise variance.

Quality: High on theory; the derivations and algorithm design are technically sound. The experimental methodology is adequate for an initial demonstration but too limited to fully support the strong claims of superiority across tasks, especially given the narrow set of metrics and benchmarks.

Clarity: Very good. The decision-theoretic formulation and the IPA gradient estimator are explained clearly. Minor editorial issues (e.g., “Main Stange”) aside, the manuscript is easy to follow. Clarifying the two roles of A (domain vs discretized set for the inner minimum) earlier would help.

Originality: The extension of KG to the batch setting with an efficient, unbiased stochastic gradient estimator is novel and nontrivial. The unifying connection to parallel EI under noiseless restrictions is a nice theoretical touch.

Significance: Potentially high. A Bayes-optimal batch policy tailored to simple regret is valuable, and noisy settings are particularly relevant in practice. The current empirical scope limits immediate impact claims; stronger, broader evidence would elevate significance.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is q-KG to the choice of discretization set AMn and M? Can you provide ablations and guidance?
- What are typical MC sample sizes for stable gradients, and how does computation scale with q? Any variance-reduction techniques used?
- Can q-KG be adapted to heteroscedastic or correlated noise models, and does the IPA derivation still go through?
- How do results change when optimizing log loss or AUC for classification tasks?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and elegant theory; principled Bayes-optimal batch acquisition.
- Addresses an important gap (noisy batch BO), with promising results.
- Clear writing and available code.

Cons against acceptance
- Narrow empirical validation (datasets, algorithms, metrics).
- Simplistic noise assumptions relative to HPO practice.
- Missing comparisons to some strong batch BO baselines; limited computational scaling analysis.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution and practical promise are clear, but the paper would be considerably stronger with broader benchmarks, more appropriate evaluation metrics, and comparisons to additional batch BO methods.
