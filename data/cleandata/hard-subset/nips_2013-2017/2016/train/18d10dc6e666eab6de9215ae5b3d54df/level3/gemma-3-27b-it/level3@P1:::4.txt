## Review of “The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization”

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization, designed to efficiently select a batch of points for parallel evaluation. The core idea – leveraging a decision-theoretic analysis to select the batch that is Bayes-optimal in the average case, given a single remaining batch of evaluations – is compelling and addresses a crucial challenge in Bayesian optimization: accelerating expensive function evaluations through parallelization. The authors complement this with an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function and demonstrate strong empirical performance on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

Recent work in Bayesian optimization has increasingly focused on extending sequential methods to parallel scenarios [3, 5, 18, 25]. Existing approaches often rely on iteratively constructing a batch, optimizing a single-evaluation acquisition function while holding others fixed. In contrast, q-KG considers the batch collectively, offering a more holistic optimization strategy. The paper situates itself well within the existing literature, acknowledging and building upon prior efforts like parallel EI [2, 19, 25] and batch UCB [5], and provides a clear comparison of performance. The connection to the Knowledge Gradient (KG) method [7, 17] for sequential BO is also appropriately highlighted.  This work’s contribution differentiates itself by generalizing KG to the parallel setting and introducing a sophisticated computational approach to overcome the associated challenges.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function from a decision-theoretic perspective is rigorous and well-justified. The use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute the gradient is a clever and significant contribution, enabling practical optimization of the acquisition function.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experiments across both synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of q-KG. The consistent outperformance over competing methods, especially in noisy environments, is noteworthy.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clear (with a few minor exceptions noted below), and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner. The code availability further enhances reproducibility and facilitates adoption.
*   **Originality:** While building on existing Bayesian optimization techniques, the q-KG method represents a novel and principled approach to batch acquisition. The combination of the theoretical foundation with the efficient computational strategy is a unique and valuable contribution.
*   **Significance:** The ability to accelerate Bayesian optimization through efficient batch evaluation is highly significant, particularly in resource-intensive applications like deep learning hyperparameter tuning. The demonstrated improvements in performance and efficiency are likely to be of interest to a broad audience of researchers and practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Gaussian Process Assumption:** The reliance on a Gaussian Process (GP) prior may limit the applicability of q-KG to high-dimensional input spaces commonly encountered in problems like neural architecture search. While GPs are flexible, their performance degrades in high dimensions. The paper does not discuss potential alternatives to mitigate this limitation.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a strong empirical validation, it lacks theoretical results regarding the convergence rate or error bounds of the q-KG algorithm. A quantitative complexity analysis with respect to input dimension would be beneficial.
*   **Minor Clarifications/Typos:** Several minor issues detract slightly from the clarity of the paper. Specifically:
    *   Line 36: "the set of points to evaluate next that is" could be rephrased for improved grammar.
    *   Line 95: The meaning of 'A' should be explicitly defined upon its first use.
    *   Line 104: Clarify the input of µ<sup>n</sup> in equation (3.1).
    *   Line 128: A brief discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of parallel EI would provide valuable context.
    *   Line 140: Reusing the notation 'A' for both the feasible domain and as an argument to q-KG is confusing. Consider using distinct symbols.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong technical foundation and clear justification of the proposed method.
*   Convincing empirical results demonstrating significant performance improvements over existing approaches.
*   The efficient computational strategy makes the method practical for real-world applications.
*   Addresses an important problem in Bayesian optimization with a novel and principled solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of theoretical guarantees regarding convergence or complexity.
*   Potential limitations due to the reliance on Gaussian Process priors in high-dimensional spaces.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The q-KG method represents a significant advance in batch acquisition function design, and the empirical results are compelling. While the lack of theoretical guarantees and the potential limitations of the GP prior are valid concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity issues and a request for the authors to consider discussing potential strategies for handling high-dimensional input spaces in future work.
