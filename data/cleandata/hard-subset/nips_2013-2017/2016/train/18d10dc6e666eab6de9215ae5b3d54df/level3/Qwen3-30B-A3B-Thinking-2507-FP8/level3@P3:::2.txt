

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which computes Bayes-optimal batches of points to evaluate simultaneously. The authors derive q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective, extending the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting. By construction, q-KG selects the batch that maximizes the expected reduction in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor after one additional batch of evaluations. The paper develops an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize q-KG, avoiding the prohibitive cost of naive Monte Carlo approaches. The method is evaluated on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), with claims of superior performance over parallel EI, GP-UCB variants, and other batch BO methods, especially under noisy evaluations.

**Strengths:**  
The paper excels in theoretical contribution and clarity. The derivation of q-KG as the Bayes-optimal batch acquisition function is elegant and well-motivated, addressing a key gap in parallel BO literature. The IPA-based computational approach is technically sound and efficiently implemented. The writing is exceptionally clear, with well-structured exposition and thorough background on Gaussian processes and BO. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, including the Bayes-optimality guarantee and computational complexity analysis. The method represents a meaningful advancement over iterative greedy batch strategies (e.g., parallel EI), which optimize batches point-by-point rather than jointly.

**Weaknesses:**  
The empirical evaluation significantly undermines the paper’s claims. The use of *test set error* as the optimization criterion is fundamentally flawed for hyperparameter tuning, as it conflates model selection with model evaluation. This metric is suboptimal for HPO (e.g., it ignores class imbalance, calibration, and threshold sensitivity) and risks overfitting to the test set. Proper metrics like AUC, log loss, or cross-validated validation error should be used to evaluate tuning performance. Furthermore, the experimental benchmarks are severely limited: only two datasets (MNIST, CIFAR10) and two learning algorithms (logistic regression, CNN) are tested. This neglects the diversity of HPO problems (e.g., tabular data, NLP, different model families) and fails to use standard HPO benchmarks like HPOlib, which includes 20+ diverse tasks. The absence of ablation studies on hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., batch size, noise variance) or comparisons to modern state-of-the-art methods (e.g., ParEGO, Batch-BO) weakens the empirical support. The noise assumption (independent normal noise) is also unrealistic for HPO, where observations are often correlated due to cross-validation or shared data splits, introducing structural bias that the method does not address.

**Overall Assessment:**  
*Quality:* Strong theoretical foundation (✓), but weak empirical validation (✗). The experimental design flaws prevent robust support for the claimed performance gains.  
*Clarity:* Excellent (✓). The paper is exceptionally well-written and accessible.  
*Originality:* High (✓). The joint optimization of batches via Bayes-optimal acquisition is novel and well-distinguished from prior work.  
*Significance:* Theoretical contribution is significant for BO, but empirical limitations reduce practical significance for HPO. The narrow benchmarks fail to demonstrate generalizability across common HPO scenarios.  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pros:* Theoretical novelty is compelling; the IPA technique is a valuable computational contribution; writing is exemplary.  
*Cons:* Critical flaws in experimental design (test error as criterion, narrow benchmarks) prevent convincing empirical validation; unrealistic noise assumption ignores real-world HPO challenges.  

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to batch BO, but the empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the broad claims of "significantly faster" optimization. For NIPS, the theoretical advance is valuable, but the experimental shortcomings prevent it from being a compelling *scientific contribution* to the field. To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) replace test error with proper HPO metrics (e.g., AUC on validation folds); (2) expand benchmarks to include HPOlib tasks and diverse model types; (3) address the noise assumption’s realism in HPO. As-is, the empirical claims are not fully justified. **Recommendation: Weak Accept.** The theoretical work is strong, but the empirical weaknesses prevent it from being a definitive contribution to the field.
