This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, which is derived from a decision-theoretic analysis. The q-KG method measures the information gain of evaluating q points via a new acquisition function and chooses the batch to jointly optimize this acquisition function. The authors develop an efficient strategy for computing this Bayes-optimal batch of points using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients for acquisition maximization.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise background on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization. The experimental results show that the q-KG algorithm outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art benchmark algorithms on several synthetic functions and in tuning practical machine learning algorithms, especially in the noisy setting.

However, there are some concerns and questions that need to be addressed. Firstly, the reviewer is not fully convinced by the experimental evidence, as the results are not consistently better than the benchmarks across all experiments. Secondly, the paper lacks wall-clock time measurements and comparisons to random search or sequential BO to show practical speedups. Additionally, the reviewer questions the justification for fixing the batch size q = 4 across all experiments.

Furthermore, the novelty of using KG and IPA is not entirely new, as similar IPA-based parallel selection (parallel EI/q-EI) exists in the literature. The authors should clearly state the differences between their method and existing work.

The reviewer also raises several major questions, including: (1) Have the authors compared their method to Wang et al. (2015) / parallel EI (q-EI), and can they clearly state the differences? (2) How does the method scale to larger q (e.g., tens/hundreds) and what benefits are expected for more parallel runs? (3) Can the synchronous method be extended to asynchronous parallel evaluations? (4) What is the wall-clock performance vs random search or sequential BO, and what is the overhead to compute each new batch? (5) Can the authors clarify the x-axis “iterations” in plots — batches or function evaluations, does it include initial design, and why do starting values differ across methods?

Overall, the paper presents a promising new approach to batch Bayesian optimization, but the reviewer believes that the authors need to address the concerns and questions raised above to strengthen the paper.

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: The paper is a good fit for the conference, but the authors need to revise the paper to address the concerns and questions raised above. The reviewer recommends acceptance pending revisions.
