- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that measures expected reduction in the GP posterior minimum and is one-step Bayes-optimal for batch decisions.  
- Uses Monte Carlo to evaluate q-KG and derives an IPA estimator for the gradient, optimized via stochastic gradient ascent.  
- Discretizes the argmin set with posterior-optima samples and includes past samples plus current batch candidates.  
- Empirical results: competitive on synthetic/noiseless tasks and superior on noisy benchmarks, including two hyperparameter tuning tasks; code released.  

Strengths:
- Decision-theoretic formulation is clear and reduces to q-EI in the noiseless/discrete limit.  
- IPA-based stochastic gradient derivation is sound and exploits GP structure efficiently.  
- High practical relevance for parallel hyperparameter optimization in multi-core/cluster settings.  
- Empirical gains under noise and reproducible code bolster impact.  
- Writing and presentation are generally clear with understandable experiments.  

Weaknesses / concerns:
- No wall-clock or end-to-end runtime measurements; overhead vs. evaluation time and comparisons to parallel random/sequential BO are missing.  
- Fixed batch size q=4 across experiments with no analysis of scaling behavior as q increases.  
- Novelty appears incremental relative to prior IPA-based parallel q-EI work; need clearer delineation of new contributions.  
- Missing comparisons to strong recent batch methods (e.g., q-PES/PPES), leaving empirical claims inconclusive.  
- Some experimental and methodological details are unclear (definitions of “iterations,” initialization, discretization sensitivity).  

Technical comments:
- Need explicit per-batch optimization cost and scaling (likely O(M q^2)–O(M q^3) per sample/gradient step); report runtime and memory vs q, d, M, restarts.  
- Discretization via posterior-optima samples may bias argmin approximation; provide sensitivity analysis over M and sampling scheme and alternatives.  
- Clarify how final recommendation is selected under noise (averaging replicates vs. GP posterior mean with learned noise variance).  

Major questions for authors:
- How does q-KG compare to IPA-optimized q-EI (Wang et al., 2015) methodologically and computationally; in what regimes is q-KG preferable beyond one-step optimality?  
- How does the method scale for larger q (tens or hundreds) in terms of optimization overhead, sample efficiency, and numerical stability of IPA/discretization?  
- Can q-KG handle asynchronous evaluations (fantasization/hallucination) like parallel EI heuristics; if so, how would it be adapted?  
- Provide wall-clock comparisons vs (a) parallel random search with same q and (b) sequential BO repeated q times; report average time to compute a batch and fraction of time spent on acquisition vs evaluation.  
- Clarify experimental axes: does “iterations” mean batches or individual evaluations, is initial design included, and why do starting values differ across methods? Use same initial design and plot vs total evaluations.  

Minor comments:
- Fix notation consistency in Eq. (4.1) for z(1:q) vs z^(1:q) and make discretization of A explicit.  
- Typos and phrasing to correct (e.g., “Main Stange” → “Main Stage”; “achieve” → “achieves”).  
- Attribution: IPA predates Wang et al. (2015) (cite L’Ecuyer, 1990); also cite Marmin et al. (2015) and Chevalier & Ginsbourger (2013) appropriately.  
- Consider adding q-PES/PPES baselines if feasible.  

Evaluation summary:
- Quality: sound decision-theoretic and IPA derivations, but empirical methodology lacks crucial runtime and broader baselines.  
- Clarity: generally clear and reproducible with some notational fixes needed.  
- Originality: moderate—natural extension of KG with IPA-based optimization but close to prior IPA batch work.  
- Significance: high potential impact if practical runtime, larger-q, and asynchronous behavior are demonstrated.  

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled batch acquisition with solid derivation and implementation; demonstrated noisy-case gains and available code.  

Cons for acceptance:
- Lacks wall-clock/efficiency evidence, scalability/asynchronous analysis, and comparisons to strongest alternatives; novelty over prior IPA-based methods is limited.
