## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, designed for scenarios where function evaluations can be performed in parallel. The core idea is to extend the sequential Knowledge Gradient criterion to a batch setting, aiming for Bayes-optimal batch selection. The authors provide a computationally efficient strategy for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function and demonstrate its superior performance on both synthetic test functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

The work builds upon a rich history of Bayesian optimization, particularly recent efforts to extend it to parallel evaluation. The paper correctly positions itself within this landscape, referencing key works on parallel EI, UCB, and ES. The introduction of q-KG represents a logical progression, aiming to leverage the theoretical optimality of the Knowledge Gradient in a batch context. The focus on noisy evaluations is a valuable contribution, as this is a common scenario in many practical applications.

**Strengths:** The paper’s primary strength lies in its theoretical foundation and promising empirical results. The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is well-motivated, and the experimental validation demonstrates consistent improvements over existing state-of-the-art algorithms. The code availability is also a significant positive aspect. The emphasis on noisy evaluations is a practical and relevant consideration.

**Weaknesses:** While the paper is strong overall, several points require attention. The literature review contains some imprecisions. The claim that integrating EI w.r.t. the posterior and the “Constant Liar” method appeared in prior work is inaccurate; these were presented in Chevalier et al. (2016). Additionally, the paper omits relevant work by Chevalier et al. (2015) on CL-mix and differentiating EI for batch design. The paper does not investigate the speed-ups achieved by moving from sequential to batch-sequential optimization, which is a key motivation for parallel BO. Several assumptions are made without explicit justification: Is the domain *A* assumed to be compact, and is *f* assumed to be continuous? The restriction to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) for the initial design *A* is not explained. 

**Questions/Clarifications:** Algorithm 1 lacks detail regarding hyperparameter re-estimation or updating within the Bayesian framework. In Section 5.2, it's unclear whether *g* is defined using a max or min operation. The smoothness of *g* in Section 5.2 isn't guaranteed, raising concerns about the validity of gradient-based optimization. The differentiability of the Cholesky factor used in the derivations should be explicitly addressed. A minor correction: the Matérn parameter should be 5/2, not 2/5. Finally, could the GPs used in Figure 1 be fitted using different software for verification?

**Overall Assessment:** Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The proposed q-KG method offers a theoretically sound and empirically effective approach to parallel Bayesian optimization. I **strongly recommend acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding the literature review and clarification of assumptions. The potential for parallelizing BO and accelerating hyperparameter tuning has substantial societal impact.
