## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization. The core idea is to extend the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to the parallel setting, aiming to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected incremental value of a single further evaluation, considering the posterior distribution over possible function values. The authors provide a computationally efficient strategy based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize the q-KG acquisition function and demonstrate its superior performance on both synthetic test functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, especially in noisy environments.

The paper builds directly on the well-established field of Bayesian optimization, particularly extending the KG method [Frazier et al., 2008].  Recent NIPS proceedings demonstrate significant interest in BO, with numerous papers focusing on acquisition function design (e.g., [Snoek et al., 2012], [Hernández-Lobato et al., 2015]) and parallelization strategies [Contal et al., 2014, Wang et al., 2016]. The q-KG method distinguishes itself by explicitly addressing the batch selection problem through a decision-theoretic framework, attempting to optimize the batch collectively rather than iteratively constructing it. This is a valuable contribution, positioning it within the broader trend of improving batch-mode Bayesian optimization.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Technical Foundation:** The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function from a decision-theoretic perspective is sound and clearly presented. The use of IPA to enable efficient gradient-based optimization is a significant technical contribution.
* **Empirically Validated:** The extensive experimental results across diverse synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems consistently demonstrate that q-KG outperforms competing batch BO algorithms, particularly in noisy settings. The improvements are often substantial.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making the key ideas accessible. The algorithm is clearly articulated in Algorithm 1.
* **Practical Relevance:** Parallelizing Bayesian optimization is crucial for addressing expensive function evaluations, common in machine learning applications. The presented method has a high potential for practical impact.
* **Code Availability:** The public availability of the code is a significant strength, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Literature Review Gaps:** The related work section, while covering relevant papers, has some omissions and imprecise statements. For example, the claim regarding the integration of EI with the posterior and the “Constant Liar” method is already present in Ginsbourger et al. [2010]. Furthermore, the work of Chevalier et al. [2014] on CL-mix and q-EI natural-gradient maximization should be cited.
* **Assumptions & Clarifications:** The paper doesn't explicitly state assumptions regarding the domain A (compactness) and the function f (continuity). These assumptions are important for the theoretical guarantees.
* **Initial Design Choice:** The rationale for restricting the initial design points to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) is not fully explained. Other sampling strategies might be equally or more effective.
* **Hyperparameter Updates:** Algorithm 1 mentions re-training hyperparameters but doesn’t detail the frequency or method of this Bayesian updating. Clarification on this point is needed.
* **Gradient Smoothness:** The smoothness of the function *g* in Section 5.2 is not obviously guaranteed, which could impact the reliability of the gradient estimation. Similarly, the differentiability of the Cholesky factor used in the IPA derivations deserves further attention.
* **Missing Analysis:** While demonstrating superior performance, the paper does not quantify the speed-ups achieved by transitioning from sequential to batch-sequential optimization. This is a relevant consideration given the motivation for parallelization.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a few minor weaknesses, this is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The q-KG method is theoretically sound, empirically validated, and has the potential to be widely adopted by practitioners. The demonstrated improvements, especially in noisy settings, are compelling. The concerns regarding literature gaps and assumptions are relatively minor and can be addressed with further clarification.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The method represents a novel and principled approach to batch Bayesian optimization.
* The empirical results are strong and consistently demonstrate the superiority of q-KG.
* The efficient computation strategy based on IPA is a valuable technical contribution.
* The availability of the code enhances reproducibility and usability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The literature review has some omissions and imprecise claims.
* Some assumptions and design choices are not fully justified.
* The paper doesn't analyze the speed-ups gained from parallelization.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the related work section to include Chevalier et al. [2014] and address the inaccuracies in the current presentation.
2.  Explicitly state the assumptions made regarding the domain A and function f.
3.  Provide a justification for using an LHS for the initial design.
4.  Clarify the frequency and method of hyperparameter updating in Algorithm 1.
5.  Address concerns regarding the smoothness of *g* and differentiability of the Cholesky factor.
6.  Correct the typo regarding the Matérn parameter to 5/2.
7.  Consider discussing the computational complexity and potential scaling issues of the method with increasing batch size *q*.
