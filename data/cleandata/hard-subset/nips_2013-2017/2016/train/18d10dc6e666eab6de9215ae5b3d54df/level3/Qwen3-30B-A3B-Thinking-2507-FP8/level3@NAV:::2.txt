

This paper proposes the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, extending the sequential knowledge gradient approach to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that selects Bayes-optimal batches of points to evaluate, providing a theoretically sound solution to parallelizing Bayesian optimization. The work builds on previous BO methods like sequential KG [17] and extends parallel approaches such as parallel EI [2, 5, 18, 25], offering a more principled batch selection strategy than iterative greedy methods.

The paper excels in theoretical contributions and clarity. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is elegant, and the computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis to efficiently optimize the acquisition function is well-executed. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the paper is exceptionally well-written with clear exposition of complex concepts.

However, the empirical evaluation suffers from significant limitations that undermine the claimed advantages. The authors use test set error as the optimization criterion, which is suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning where metrics like AUC or log loss better capture model performance. The experimental benchmarks are narrowly focused on MNIST and CIFAR10 with only two algorithms (logistic regression and CNN), neglecting broader HPO benchmarks like HPOlib. This limited scope fails to demonstrate the method's robustness across diverse optimization problems and algorithm types, where optimizer performance can vary substantially.

The assumption of independent normally distributed observation noise is also problematic for real-world hyperparameter optimization, where correlated errors from cross-validation and structural biases are common. While the theoretical contribution is strong, these empirical shortcomings prevent a convincing demonstration of the method's practical value.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to batch Bayesian optimization, but the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient breadth and appropriate metrics to support the strong claims. To strengthen the paper, the authors should expand their benchmarks to include standard HPO libraries, adopt more appropriate performance metrics, and address the noise model limitations. The theoretical foundation is promising, but the empirical validation needs substantial expansion to fully support the paper's claims.
