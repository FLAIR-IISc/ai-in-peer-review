This paper introduces a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient to the parallel setting, where a batch of `q` points can be evaluated simultaneously. From a decision-theoretic perspective, q-KG selects the batch of points that is Bayes-optimal under a one-step lookahead, maximizing the expected improvement in the final solution quality. A key contribution is an efficient computational strategy to maximize the q-KG acquisition function, which involves Monte Carlo estimation and an unbiased gradient estimator derived using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). The authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression and convolutional neural networks, demonstrating that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms state-of-the-art parallel Bayesian optimization methods, particularly in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Quality and Originality**

The paper presents a high-quality and original contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The generalization of the knowledge gradient criterion to the batch setting is a natural but non-trivial extension. The derivation of the q-KG factor and, more importantly, the development of an efficient gradient-based optimization scheme using IPA are significant technical contributions that make the proposed method practical. The work is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by extensive experiments.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the proposed method is described in detail, and the algorithm is presented explicitly. However, certain areas could benefit from additional clarification:

1.  **Literature Review:** The related work section contains some imprecise claims. The idea of iteratively constructing a batch by maximizing EI averaged over the posterior of previously selected points, as well as the "Constant Liar" heuristic, appeared prior to some of the cited works in Ginsbourger et al., "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (2010). Furthermore, the literature survey would be more complete by including Chevalier et al.'s work on CL-mix and Marmin et al.'s "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design" (2015), which also studies gradient-based maximization of a parallel acquisition function (q-EI).

2.  **Technical Assumptions:** The paper does not explicitly state the assumptions on the domain `A` (e.g., compact) or the objective function `f` (e.g., continuous). These assumptions are often standard but should be stated for completeness.

3.  **Algorithm Details:** In Algorithm 1, it is mentioned that GP hyperparameters are re-trained, but the frequency and method are not specified. Is this done after every batch evaluation using MLE, as suggested in Section 6? Clarifying this would improve reproducibility.

4.  **Gradient Computation (Sec 5.2):** The derivation relies on interchanging expectation and differentiation. The function `g` involves an `argmin`, which can make it non-smooth. While IPA is a powerful tool, the paper would be strengthened by briefly justifying the validity of this step, perhaps by noting that the points of non-differentiability of the `argmin` operator occur with measure zero. Similarly, the differentiability of the Cholesky factor `D(n)` with respect to the locations `z(1:q)` is not immediately obvious and could be briefly commented on.

5.  **Definition of `g` (Sec 5.2):** The objective is function minimization, so `g` is correctly defined as `min(current) - min(future)`. A small note clarifying that `g` represents the *improvement* (a positive quantity to be maximized) might help avoid confusion.

**Significance**

The significance of this work is high. Parallel computing resources are widely available, and methods that can effectively leverage them to speed up expensive black-box optimization are of great practical importance. Bayesian optimization is a critical tool for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning, and this paper provides a principled and empirically strong algorithm for the parallel setting. Given its excellent performance, especially with noisy evaluations (a common real-world scenario), the proposed q-KG method is likely to be adopted by practitioners and serve as a strong baseline for future research in this area.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and principled acquisition function (q-KG) for the important problem of batch Bayesian optimization.
*   Significant technical contribution in the form of an efficient gradient-based method for maximizing the acquisition function.
*   Strong and comprehensive empirical results showing state-of-the-art performance, especially on noisy problems.
*   High potential for practical impact in the machine learning community and beyond.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   A minor weakness is that the experimental section does not include a study of the speed-up or performance gain obtained by moving from a sequential approach to the proposed batch method. This would help quantify the practical benefit of parallelization.
*   The literature review has some minor inaccuracies and omissions that should be corrected.

**Additional Questions and Minor Points:**
*   In Algorithm 1, why is the initial design restricted to a Latin Hypercube Sample? While a common choice, mentioning the rationale would be helpful.
*   There may be a typo in the manuscript regarding the Matérn kernel; please ensure the parameter is consistently written as 5/2.
*   For verification, have the authors considered fitting the GP models with an alternative software package to ensure the results are not implementation-specific? This is not a requirement but would further strengthen the experimental claims.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. I strongly recommend this paper for publication at NIPS. The weaknesses identified are minor and can be addressed in a final version.
