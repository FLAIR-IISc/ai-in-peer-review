- Proposes q-KG, a batch acquisition function that jointly selects q points by maximizing expected reduction in the GP posterior predictive minimum (one-step Bayes-optimal).
- Shows q-KG reduces to batch EI in the noiseless setting when the final recommendation is restricted to evaluated points, linking to existing heuristics.
- Derives a reparameterization using a standard normal and applies infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG.
- Uses a dynamic finite discretization An for the inner minimization in continuous domains, augmented with sampled posterior optima and previous points.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over batch EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic functions and ML tuning tasks, with large gains under observation noise.
- Code is released, and the method is implementable with practical gradient-based optimization.
- Strength: principled decision-theoretic foundation as the one-step Bayes-optimal batch policy for GP predictors.
- Strength: solid computational contribution (reparameterization + IPA) and a reasonable discretization scheme.
- Strength: strong empirical performance across benchmarks, especially in noisy regimes.
- Strength: clear relation to prior work via reduction to batch EI and positioned within KG literature.
- Weakness: lacks theoretical guarantees beyond one-step optimality (no simple-regret bounds, consistency, or discretization/MC error bounds).
- Weakness: missing quantitative complexity analysis in terms of q, d, M = |An|, MC samples, and gradient steps; no wall-clock time or time-to-target comparisons.
- Weakness: limited discussion on applicability to high-dimensional or structured search spaces and possible remedies (embeddings, ARD, structured kernels).
- Weakness: sensitivity to discretization An not analyzed (M fixed at 1000); need sensitivity studies on M and An construction.
- Weakness: missing comparison to parallel entropy search methods (e.g., q-PES); omission should be justified or benchmarked.
- Weakness: assumes known/homoscedastic Gaussian noise via MLE; robustness to misspecified or heteroscedastic/heavy-tailed noise not discussed.
- Quality: technical derivations and IPA gradient estimator are sound; experiments are careful and convincing, particularly under noise.
- Clarity: paper is mostly clear and well-structured but could improve notation and distinction between continuous domain and discretization.
- Originality: novel extension of KG to batch settings with a differentiable Monte Carlo objective and IPA-based gradients; useful connection to EI.
- Significance: important for parallel expensive black-box optimization; likely to be adopted given empirical strength and principled basis.
- Pros for acceptance: principled Bayes-optimal acquisition, practical IPA-based optimization, open-source implementation, and strong empirical gains.
- Cons for acceptance: missing regret/consistency bounds, formal complexity analysis, discussion of high-dimensional limits, discretization sensitivity, and q-PES comparison.
- Minor issues: grammar fixes, define A at first use, consistent notation for µ(n)(x), avoid reusing A for domain/discretization, report MC sample counts/variance reduction/stopping criteria, and give wall-clock/timing metrics. 
- Overall recommendation: solid paper with valuable contributions and strong empirical results; reviewer leans toward acceptance pending theoretical and complexity additions.
