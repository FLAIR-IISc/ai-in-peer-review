### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for batch Bayesian optimization, termed the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method. The work addresses the common practical setting where multiple expensive function evaluations can be performed in parallel. The core idea is to generalize the decision-theoretic framework of the Knowledge Gradient (KG), which is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining sequential evaluation, to the batch setting. The proposed q-KG acquisition function quantifies the expected improvement in the final solution quality (the minimum of the posterior mean) from evaluating a batch of `q` points.

Maximizing this acquisition function over the `q*d` dimensional space of batch locations is computationally challenging. To make this tractable, the authors propose a gradient-based optimization strategy. They formulate the q-KG factor as an expectation over a standard normal random vector and derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using Monte Carlo sampling combined with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), a standard technique for gradient estimation of expectations.

The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation on several synthetic benchmark functions and two machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), considering both noise-free and noisy evaluation settings. The results demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or significantly outperforms existing batch Bayesian optimization methods, including parallel versions of Expected Improvement and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB), with a particularly strong advantage in the presence of observation noise.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper tackles the important and practical problem of parallel Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is derived from a sound decision-theoretic principle, extending the one-step Bayes-optimal KG policy to the batch setting. This provides a more fundamentally grounded approach than many heuristic or greedy batch construction methods.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are comprehensive and compelling. The method is shown to outperform or be highly competitive with several state-of-the-art baselines across a variety of problems. The demonstration of its superior performance in noisy settings is a key strength, as many real-world black-box optimization problems involve stochastic evaluations. The application to tuning a CNN on CIFAR-10 and improving upon a human-tuned baseline further highlights its practical utility.

3.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining theKG framework, and detailing their computational approach for maximizing the acquisition function. The inclusion of a link to the source code is a commendable step towards reproducibility and practical adoption.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** While the application is novel and the results are strong, the core technical components are extensions of existing work. The main conceptual contribution is the generalization of the KG policy to the batch setting, which is a natural, albeit valuable, extension. The primary technical contribution for making this tractable—using Monte Carlo with an IPA-based gradient estimator—is a well-established technique for optimizing expectations. The work is thus more of a successful and elegant combination of known ideas rather than a contribution of fundamentally new theory or methods.

2.  **Computational Overhead:** The method relies on stochastic gradient ascent, where each gradient estimation requires Monte Carlo sampling. This is likely to be significantly more computationally expensive than simpler batch selection strategies, such as the greedy approaches used by Spearmint's parallel EI or the analytical updates in batch UCB methods. The paper focuses on the number of expensive function evaluations but does not discuss the wall-clock time trade-off, which could be a limiting factor in scenarios where the selection overhead is non-negligible compared to the function evaluation time.

### **Detailed Comments**

-   **Equation (3.1) Clarity:** The notation in equation (3.1) for the posterior mean and kernel is slightly ambiguous. The functions µ and K are defined on `A` and `A x A` respectively, which typically take vector arguments. However, the equation uses `x^(1:n)` as an argument (e.g., `K(x, x^(1:n))`), which is defined as a sequence or set of vectors. I assume `µ(x^(1:n))` is meant to be the vector `[µ(x(1)), ..., µ(x(n))]^T` and `K(x, x^(1:n))` is the row vector `[K(x, x(1)), ..., K(x, x(n))]`. This is standard practice but could be stated explicitly to avoid any confusion for the reader.

-   **Discretization Strategy:** The optimization of the final recommendation is performed over a discretized set `An`. This set is constructed by sampling from the optima of the GP posterior, which is a reasonable and clever heuristic. However, the quality of the q-KG acquisition function is dependent on this discretization. It would be useful to understand how sensitive the algorithm's performance is to the size of this discretization (`M`). Is there a risk of missing the true posterior minimum if `M` is too small, thereby underestimating the potential knowledge gain?

-   **Comparison with Parallel ES:** The paper compares against parallel EI and UCB variants. A comparison with parallel Entropy Search (ES) [18] would have been valuable, as ES also attempts to reason about the location of the optimum in a more global fashion than EI. Both q-KG and ES are computationally intensive and rely on Monte Carlo methods, making for a natural comparison.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that presents a practical and effective algorithm for an important problem. The principled derivation and excellent empirical results are major strengths. The primary weakness is a relative lack of deep novelty in the core techniques, but the successful synthesis and application of these techniques to produce a state-of-the-art method is a significant contribution in its own right. I recommend acceptance.
