- Proposal: q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm extending knowledge gradient to select q points using a GP, Monte Carlo reparameterization, and IPA-based stochastic gradients with adaptive discretization.  
- Related work: Positioned within KG literature and connected to batch EI, parallel UCB/PE, constant liar heuristics, and PPES.  
- Main contribution: Extends decision-theoretic KG to batch selection and makes it computationally viable via MC+IPA gradient estimation.  
- Technical soundness: Derivation and reparameterization are correct; IPA is reasonable under differentiability and regularity assumptions.  
- Bayes-optimality caveat: The one-step Bayes-optimal claim only holds for a fixed GP with fixed hyperparameters and a finite discretized set.  
- Hyperparameter/re-discretization issue: Re-fitting hyperparameters and adaptive discretization in practice break the strict one-step optimality guarantee.  
- IPA assumptions: Unbiasedness requires unique argmins and differentiability; ties or non-smooth kernels need subgradient handling.  
- Computational complexity: Per-evaluation cost roughly O(S(q^3 + Mq)) with Cholesky on q×q and operations over discretization of size M; may become expensive as q or M grows.  
- Reproducibility needs: Paper should report S (MC samples), step sizes, variance-reduction choices, and optimization details (learning rates, restarts).  
- Empirics: Strong performance, especially under noise; experiments on test functions and hyperparameter tuning support claims.  
- Missing comparisons: Should include PPES and recent q-EI-gradient implementations as additional baselines.  
- Sensitivity/scaling analyses needed: Report sensitivity to M and S, scaling with larger q (e.g., q = 2,4,8,16), dimensionality d, and wall-clock times.  
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized, code release is a plus.  
- Notation issues: Eq. (3.1) and overloading of A are confusing; recommend explicit definitions for sequences, matrices, and renaming discretized set (e.g., An or S).  
- Specific questions requested: Clarify MC sample counts for objective vs gradient, use of common random numbers/antithetics, and behavior with non-unique argmins.  
- Originality: Moderate — a natural extension of KG with standard IPA adaptation; more engineering than a fundamentally new idea.  
- Significance: Likely practical impact for batch BO in noisy settings if it scales well; practitioners may adopt it given the code.  
- Pros: Principled decision-theoretic formulation, efficient MC+IPA estimator, good empirical results under noise, clear writing, and released code.  
- Cons: Incremental novelty, qualified Bayes-optimality, notation ambiguities, missing some baselines and limited computational/sensitivity analysis.  
- Recommendation: Lean toward acceptance contingent on clarifying notation, qualifying Bayes-optimal claims, and expanding experimental comparisons and sensitivity/scaling analyses.
