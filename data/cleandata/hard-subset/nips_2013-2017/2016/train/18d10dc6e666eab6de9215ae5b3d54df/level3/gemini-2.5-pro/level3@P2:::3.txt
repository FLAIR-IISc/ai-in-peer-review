This paper proposes a new method for batch Bayesian optimization, called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The goal is to select a batch of `q` points to evaluate in parallel to find the minimum of an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function. The work builds upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, which is a decision-theoretic approach that selects the point maximizing the expected improvement in the final solution quality after one more evaluation. The authors generalize this concept to the batch setting, proposing an acquisition function, q-KG, that measures the expected value of jointly evaluating a batch of `q` candidate points.

Since the direct optimization of the q-KG acquisition function is computationally intractable, the main technical contribution of the paper is a method to make this optimization feasible. The authors propose a stochastic gradient-based approach. The q-KG factor and its gradient are estimated using Monte Carlo (MC) sampling. Crucially, an unbiased gradient estimator is derived using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), allowing for efficient optimization of the batch selection problem. The method is evaluated on a suite of synthetic benchmark functions and two practical hyperparameter tuning problems (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10). The experiments compare q-KG against several other batch Bayesian optimization methods, including variants of parallel Expected Improvement and parallel UCB. The results demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms these baselines, with a particularly significant advantage in settings where function evaluations are noisy.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed q-KG method is well-grounded in decision theory, providing a principled approach to batch selection that is Bayes-optimal under a one-step lookahead assumption. The derivation of the stochastic gradient estimator using IPA is sound and provides a key component that makes the method practical. The claims of the paper are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation. The experiments cover both synthetic and real-world problems and, importantly, investigate both noise-free and noisy evaluation settings. The inclusion of mean and standard deviation over multiple runs adds confidence to the empirical results. The work is presented as a complete and usable algorithm.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem of batch Bayesian optimization, explaining the theoretical underpinnings of the q-KG method, and detailing their computational approach. The connection to and distinction from prior work is clearly articulated.

One minor point for improvement would be to clarify the notation in the Gaussian Process background section. For example, in Equation (3.1), `x(1:n)` is defined as a set of points, but the equation uses it in contexts (e.g., `K(x, x(1:n))`) where it is treated as a matrix of stacked vectors. While this is common shorthand in the GP literature, explicitly stating this convention would improve clarity for a broader audience.

**Originality:** The paper makes a solid and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. While the core components—the Knowledge Gradient policy and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis—are existing techniques, their synthesis to create a practical and effective method for batch Bayesian optimization in continuous domains is new and non-trivial. Previous batch optimization methods often rely on greedy, sequential construction of the batch or heuristics. In contrast, this work proposes a method that jointly optimizes the entire batch of points based on a principled acquisition function. The authors also clearly distinguish their contribution from concurrent work [26] that tackles a related problem but in a discrete setting with a less efficient computational method.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is of high practical importance. With the prevalence of parallel computing, methods that can efficiently leverage multiple workers for tasks like hyperparameter tuning are highly valuable to both researchers and practitioners. The proposed q-KG algorithm appears to be a state-of-the-art method for this problem. The strong empirical results, especially in the noisy setting (which is common in practice, e.g., when evaluating models on subsampled data), suggest that this method could be widely adopted. The authors' decision to release their code further increases the potential for impact and adoption by the community.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of batch Bayesian optimization.
*   Proposes a principled, decision-theoretic acquisition function (q-KG) that jointly optimizes the selection of all points in the batch.
*   Develops a non-trivial and computationally efficient method (MC + IPA) to optimize the acquisition function, making the approach practical.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance against several state-of-the-art benchmarks on a variety of tasks, with a notable advantage in the common noisy setting.
*   The paper is well-written, and the work is reproducible (code is provided).

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The primary concern regarding novelty is that the work combines existing concepts (Knowledge Gradient and IPA). However, the application and technical execution for this specific, important problem represent a significant and non-obvious contribution.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and technically sound solution to an important problem. The strengths in terms of quality, significance, and empirical validation substantially outweigh the minor concern regarding the novelty of the base components. The work is a valuable contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature and is likely to be impactful.
