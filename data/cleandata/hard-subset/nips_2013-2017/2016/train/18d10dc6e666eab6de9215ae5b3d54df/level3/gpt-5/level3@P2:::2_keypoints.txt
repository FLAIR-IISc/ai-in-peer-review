- Introduces q-KG: a batch Bayesian optimization method generalizing Knowledge Gradient to select q points by maximizing expected one-step reduction in posterior mean minimum.
- Bayes-optimal in the one-step setting, but exact evaluation is intractable for batch/continuous domains.
- Proposes three practical components: MC estimator via affine-Gaussian representation, an unbiased IPA-based stochastic gradient, and a discretization scheme for inner minimization.
- Positions work relative to parallel EI, GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE, and parallel PES; novelty is extending KG to joint batch selection with an efficient gradient estimator.
- Derivation of q-KG and IPA-based gradient is technically solid, but IPA unbiasedness depends on differentiability and uniqueness of minimizers—tie-breaking and kinks need clarification.
- The discretization strategy is standard but introduces approximation error; sensitivity/convergence analysis is missing.
- Assumes independent Gaussian observation noise with known/MLE variance; real HPO noise is often heteroscedastic or correlated, raising robustness concerns.
- No study of whether q-KG can incorporate general noise covariance or handle noise mis-specification.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough on synthetic functions but limited for HPO: only two datasets/two learners and optimizing on the test set (improper practice).
- Metric choice (0-1 error) is suboptimal for probabilistic models; prefer AUC or log loss and validation-set optimization with held-out test evaluation.
- Missing runtime/compute overhead comparisons and scalability analysis versus q-EI/q-PES/GP-BUCB despite potentially high MC and gradient costs.
- Paper is well organized and readable, but notation overload (A used twice) and missing concrete algorithmic details (MC sample counts, batch sizes, restarts, stopping criteria) reduce clarity.
- Suggest adding a brief complexity analysis as a function of q, |An|, and MC samples for practitioner guidance.
- Originality: novel and nontrivial extension of KG to batch setting with elegant IPA-based optimization; recovers q-EI in a special case.
- Significance: potentially impactful for noisy parallel BO and HPO, but empirical scope and metric issues limit the strength of claims.
- Pros: principled batch KG objective, technically strong unbiased gradient estimator, clear relation to prior work, consistent synthetic gains especially under noise.
- Cons: narrow HPO evaluation and improper test-set optimization, poor metric choices, Gaussian/independent noise assumption, no runtime comparisons, and missing discretization sensitivity analysis.
- Key questions for authors: MC sample sizes and sensitivity, wall-clock time vs baselines and scaling with q, robustness to correlated/heteroscedastic noise, sensitivity to discretization M and An construction, and re-run HPO using validation metrics with held-out test results.
- Suggestions: optimize on validation set and report test results, include AUC/log loss, expand HPO benchmarks (HPOlib, more datasets/algorithms), add runtime/ablation studies (q, M, MC), clarify IPA assumptions/tie-breaking, and disambiguate notation.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — strong theoretical/algorithmic contributions but empirical evaluation needs broadening and corrections to substantiate claims.
