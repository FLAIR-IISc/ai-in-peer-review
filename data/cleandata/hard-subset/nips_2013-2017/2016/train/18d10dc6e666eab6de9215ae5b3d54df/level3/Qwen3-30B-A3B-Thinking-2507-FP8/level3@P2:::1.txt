

# Review for "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), extending the knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that maximizes the expected incremental value of a batch of q points, and develop an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize this acquisition function. The method is evaluated on synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, with results demonstrating consistent improvements over existing batch Bayesian optimization algorithms, particularly in noisy settings.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound contribution to Bayesian optimization. The theoretical foundation is well-motivated, with the q-KG acquisition function derived from a decision-theoretic perspective. The computational approach using IPA is clever and well-justified. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple state-of-the-art batch BO methods on both synthetic and real-world problems. However, the paper could better address the computational speed-up gained by parallelization (noting that while batch evaluation is faster, the optimization of the acquisition function may be more expensive). The experimental section could also clarify how hyperparameters are updated in Algorithm 1 (the paper mentions "re-train the hyperparameters of the GP by MLE" but doesn't specify when this occurs relative to the main loop).

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, though some technical details require clarification. Section 5.2 contains an ambiguity regarding whether g is defined using a max or a min (the paper uses "min" in the text but the mathematical expression appears to use max). The smoothness of g and differentiability of the Cholesky factor used in the gradient derivation could be better justified. The paper also contains a typo: the Matérn kernel parameter should be 5/2, not 2/5. The justification for restricting the initial design to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) is not fully explained.

**Originality**: The extension of knowledge gradient to the parallel setting is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the literature. However, the literature review contains some imprecise claims. The paper states that "integrating EI w.r.t. posterior and Constant Liar appeared in prior work" but fails to cite the correct references (Chevalier et al. "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" for the Constant Liar method and Marmin et al. "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement" for natural-gradient maximization of q-EI). This omission is significant as these papers present related work that should be properly acknowledged.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is highly relevant, as parallel Bayesian optimization has growing importance in machine learning hyperparameter tuning where multiple evaluations can be performed simultaneously across distributed systems. The results demonstrate that q-KG provides significant advantages over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings where the performance gains are larger than in noise-free scenarios. The practical impact is evident from the results on tuning logistic regression and CNNs, where q-KG achieves better test error rates than competitors.

**Pro-acceptance arguments**: 
1. The q-KG method is theoretically well-founded and provides a decision-theoretic approach to batch Bayesian optimization.
2. The computational method using IPA is efficient and well-motivated.
3. The experimental results consistently demonstrate superior performance, especially in noisy settings.
4. The method has clear practical applications in machine learning hyperparameter tuning.

**Con-acceptance arguments**:
1. The literature review fails to properly acknowledge key prior work on parallel EI and natural-gradient optimization.
2. Some technical details (smoothness of g, differentiability of Cholesky factor) require better justification.
3. The paper does not analyze the computational speed-up gained by parallelization.
4. Certain technical claims in the literature review are inaccurate or imprecise.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, with clear theoretical grounding and strong empirical results. The minor issues identified (literature review inaccuracies, technical clarifications) are not fundamental to the contribution and can be addressed in a revised version. The method has significant potential for practical impact in machine learning hyperparameter tuning, particularly in settings where parallel evaluation is possible and function evaluations are noisy.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS 2016. The contribution is novel, well-executed, and addresses an important practical problem with demonstrable advantages over existing methods. The minor issues noted can be easily addressed in the camera-ready version.
