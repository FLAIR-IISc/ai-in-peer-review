

This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization method using the knowledge gradient (q-KG) acquisition function, combined with infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for efficient gradient estimation. The authors claim their method provides the Bayes-optimal batch of points to sample in parallel settings, with experiments showing competitive performance on noiseless problems and superior results on noisy ones, including synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning for CNNs/logistic regression on CIFAR10/MNIST.

The paper's strengths include clear technical descriptions of the q-KG formulation and IPA implementation, well-organized experiments, and relevance to practical multi-core optimization scenarios. The background on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization is solid, and the experimental setup is understandable.

However, significant concerns limit the paper's impact. The novelty claim is questionable as similar IPA-based parallel selection (e.g., Wang et al. 2015) already exists, and the paper fails to clearly differentiate its approach. Crucially, the authors omit wall-clock time comparisons against random search or sequential BO, making it impossible to assess practical speedups. The batch size q=4 is fixed without justification, despite the method's potential for larger q. The experimental evidence lacks depth, particularly in showing why q-KG outperforms existing methods in noisy settings beyond the reported results.

The paper also fails to address several critical questions: How does the method scale to larger q? What is the actual wall-clock performance versus sequential BO? How does the "iterations" axis in plots relate to function evaluations? The authors incorrectly attribute IPA to Wang et al. (2015), which is misleading.

While the problem of parallel hyperparameter optimization is highly significant, the paper's technical contributions are incremental. The lack of comparative runtime data and insufficient novelty justification prevent it from making a strong case for acceptance. The paper would benefit from addressing the major questions raised, particularly the comparative speedup analysis and clearer differentiation from prior work. The minor technical issues (e.g., grammar errors, citation inaccuracies) further undermine the paper's quality.
