This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) algorithm, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, designed for scenarios where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The method builds upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient approach, extending it to a batch setting via a decision-theoretic analysis. A key contribution is an efficient gradient-based method, utilizing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), to optimize the q-KG acquisition function. Experimental results demonstrate improved performance compared to existing batch BO algorithms on both synthetic test functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in the presence of noisy function evaluations.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical need in parallelizable optimization problems. The core idea of extending the Knowledge Gradient to a batch setting is conceptually sound, and the use of IPA to enable efficient optimization is a positive step. The experimental results, demonstrating consistent gains over existing methods, particularly in noisy scenarios, are encouraging. The code release is also a strong positive aspect.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clear positioning within the existing literature on batch Knowledge Gradient methods. While the introduction mentions related work, it doesn't adequately address prior attempts to extend KG to batch settings (e.g., [1], [2] as noted in the key points). The novelty of the proposed approach needs to be more clearly articulated – is the core contribution the batch formulation itself, the IPA-based gradient estimator, or a combination of both? A more thorough comparison to existing batch KG implementations, specifically regarding computational efficiency and performance, is crucial. If the primary contribution is the IPA-based gradient estimator, a dedicated empirical comparison against the gradient estimation techniques used in prior work would substantially strengthen the paper.

Section 5, detailing the computation of q-KG and its gradient, is central to the paper's claims but lacks sufficient detail. While the authors mention the use of IPA, the explanation of how the gradient is actually computed, especially the derivation and justification of the equations in Section 5.2, is quite brief. Expanding this section with a more thorough explanation of the IPA application, including intermediate steps and potential approximations, would greatly improve clarity and reproducibility. Providing pseudocode for the gradient estimation process would also be beneficial. The discretization strategy described in Section 5.3 is reasonable but could benefit from a discussion of the sensitivity of results to the number of samples (M) used for discretization.

The comparison to Expected Improvement (EI) in Section 6 is problematic. As the reviewer correctly points out, standard EI assumes noiseless observations. The authors should clarify whether a noise-aware variant of EI was used for comparison and, if not, discuss how the inherent assumption of noiselessness in EI impacts the results presented in Section 6.2. A fairer comparison would involve using a noise-aware version of EI or explicitly acknowledging the limitation of comparing against a method that doesn’t directly account for noise.

A minor point: the abbreviation “IPA” is redefined on line 162 and should be corrected to maintain consistency.

**Strengths:**

*   Technically sound approach to extending Knowledge Gradient to batch BO.
*   Efficient gradient-based optimization method using IPA.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating improved performance, particularly in noisy settings.
*   Code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Insufficiently addresses related work on batch Knowledge Gradient methods.
*   Lacks detailed explanation of the gradient estimation process in Section 5.
*   Potentially unfair comparison to Expected Improvement due to noise assumptions.
*   Limited discussion of sensitivity to discretization parameters.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods in certain scenarios.
*   The code release facilitates reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is not sufficiently emphasized, and the paper lacks a thorough comparison to related work.
*   The technical details of the gradient estimation process are insufficient for full understanding and reproducibility.
*   The comparison to Expected Improvement is potentially misleading due to noise assumptions.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above is crucial. Specifically, the authors need to more clearly position their work within the existing literature on batch KG, provide a more detailed explanation of the gradient estimation process, and ensure a fair comparison to alternative methods. I recommend a **weak accept** contingent upon the authors addressing these points in a revised version. The potential impact of this work, especially in applications with noisy function evaluations, justifies its acceptance if these concerns are adequately addressed.
