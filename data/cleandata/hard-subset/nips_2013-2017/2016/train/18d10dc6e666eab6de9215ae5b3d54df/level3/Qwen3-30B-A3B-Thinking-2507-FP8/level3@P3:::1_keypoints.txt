- Introduces q-KG, a novel batch Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient to evaluate q points jointly rather than greedily.  
- Provides a decision-theoretic justification for q-KG and contrasts it with iterative batch methods like parallel EI and Constant Liar.  
- Develops an efficient gradient estimation and optimization strategy for q-KG using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA).  
- Demonstrates via extensive experiments (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann; MNIST logistic regression; CIFAR10 CNN) that q-KG consistently outperforms state-of-the-art batch BO methods, especially under noise.  
- Claims significant practical impact by enabling effective parallelization of Bayesian optimization for expensive ML pipelines.  
- Clearly articulates the core intuition and differences of q-KG relative to prior approaches.  
- Literature review contains imprecise claims and omits important related work (e.g., Chevalier et al.’s CL-mix, Marmin et al.’s q-EI natural-gradient maximization).  
- Misattributes or overstates prior parallelization claims (e.g., on Ginsbourger et al. 2010).  
- Does not state key assumptions (compactness of domain A, continuity of f) needed for theoretical guarantees.  
- Algorithm 1 lacks implementation detail on hyperparameter update frequency and offers no justification for using Latin Hypercube Sampling for initialization.  
- Theoretical exposition has ambiguities in Section 5.2 (unclear definition of g as max vs. min) and questionable smoothness/differentiability arguments, including Cholesky factor differentiability.  
- Minor errors: Matérn parameter incorrectly listed as 2/5 instead of 5/2.  
- Omits empirical analysis of actual wall-clock speed-up from sequential to batch BO, an important practical metric.  
- Experimental verification could be strengthened by confirming GP fits across different software implementations.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound with solid empirical support, but some theoretical and literature clarifications are needed.  
- Clarity: generally well written, though some technical definitions and novelty distinctions require clearer exposition.  
- Originality: presents a novel batch acquisition function and an innovative IPA-based computational approach, but literature gaps weaken novelty positioning.  
- Significance: high practical importance with demonstrated performance gains, particularly in noisy settings.  
- Recommendation: strong accept with minor revisions to address literature omissions, clarify theoretical details, and fix errors.
