This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel batch acquisition function for Bayesian optimization (BO). The core idea is to extend the decision-theoretic foundation of the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) to the parallel setting, where a batch of `q` points must be selected simultaneously. The q-KG acquisition function measures the expected improvement in the final solution quality (defined as the minimum of the posterior mean) that would result from evaluating a given batch of `q` points. This contrasts with myopic or greedy batch construction methods. To make the maximization of the q-KG function computationally tractable, the authors propose an efficient method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate its gradient, allowing for the use of standard gradient-based optimizers. The paper relates this new method to prior work in parallel BO, such as extensions of Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bounds (UCB).

The paper presents a strong contribution to the important and rapidly growing field of parallel Bayesian optimization. The strengths and weaknesses are detailed below.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is a principled, decision-theoretically motivated acquisition function for batch BO. Moving beyond greedy or heuristic batch construction is a critical step for the field, and q-KG provides a well-founded approach. The problem of efficiently parallelizing expensive black-box evaluations is of high practical importance in machine learning and beyond, making this work highly significant.

2.  **Quality of Empirical Results:** The numerical experiments are comprehensive, comparing q-KG against several state-of-the-art batch BO algorithms on a suite of synthetic benchmarks and two practical hyperparameter tuning problems. The results are compelling, demonstrating that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms existing methods. Its performance advantage is particularly pronounced in the noisy setting, which is a common and challenging scenario in practice (e.g., when evaluating models on a subset of data).

3.  **Technical Contribution:** The development of an efficient gradient-based optimization scheme for the q-KG function is a key technical strength. Naively optimizing q-KG would be computationally prohibitive. The use of IPA to derive an unbiased gradient estimator is elegant and enables the practical application of the method.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Literature Review Precision:** While the related work section is generally good, some claims could be more precise and a few key references seem to be missing.
    *   The paper suggests that parallelizing EI by iteratively constructing a batch while averaging over the posterior distribution is from [10, 19]. This idea, along with the "constant liar" heuristic, was originally proposed in Ginsbourger et al. ("Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization", 2010).
    *   The literature review omits the CL-mix heuristic from Chevalier et al. ("Fast computation of the multi-points expected improvement...", 2012).
    *   The topic of gradient-based maximization of q-EI was also studied in Marmin et al. ("Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design," 2015), which would be a relevant citation in the context of the proposed gradient-based approach for q-KG.

2.  **Lack of Speed-up Analysis:** A minor weakness is that the paper does not study the computational speed-ups gained by parallelization. While the focus is on sample efficiency, a discussion or experiment showing the reduction in wall-clock time compared to a sequential approach would further bolster the practical motivation for the method.

3.  **Clarifications and Technical Details:** Several points in the methodology would benefit from further clarification:
    *   **Assumptions:** Are the domain A and the function f assumed to be compact and continuous, respectively? These assumptions are standard in BO and are likely implicitly made, but should be stated for clarity.
    *   **Algorithm 1 Details:**
        *   The algorithm specifies using a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) for the initial design. Is there a strong motivation for this choice over other space-filling designs?
        *   The algorithm states "re-train the hyperparameters of the GP by MLE". How often is this performed? After every batch? Is it a full MLE re-estimation, or is a Bayesian update applied? This detail can have a significant impact on performance and runtime.
    *   **Section 5.2 Concerns:**
        *   The definition of `g` appears to involve `min_x mu(n)(x) - min_x (...)`. Please confirm this formulation.
        *   The interchange of the derivative and expectation in equation (5.3) is justified by the smoothness of `g`. However, since `g` is defined via a `min` operation over a set of functions, its smoothness is not immediately obvious. The authors should provide justification or a reference for this step.
        *   The derivation relies on the derivative of the Cholesky factor `D(n)`. While computable, the differentiability of the Cholesky decomposition is not guaranteed everywhere (e.g., for matrices that are not positive-definite). A brief comment on this would strengthen the technical rigor.

4.  **Minor Points:**
    *   **Typo:** Please check the Matérn kernel notation; it should likely be 5/2 rather than 2/5 if that typo exists anywhere.
    *   **Experimental Verification:** For reproducibility and to rule out implementation-specific artifacts, have the authors considered verifying their GP fits using an alternative software package for one of the synthetic examples? This is a suggestion for strengthening the work, not a required change.

**Summary:**

This is a high-quality, well-written paper that presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is theoretically sound and empirically powerful, especially on noisy problems. While there are some minor weaknesses concerning the precision of the literature review and a need for clarification on some technical details, these points are addressable. The strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses. I have a strong recommendation for publication and believe this work will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
