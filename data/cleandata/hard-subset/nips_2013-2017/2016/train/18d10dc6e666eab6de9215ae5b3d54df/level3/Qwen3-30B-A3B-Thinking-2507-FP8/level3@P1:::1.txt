

The paper "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization" proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that selects multiple points to evaluate simultaneously, addressing a significant practical limitation in standard Bayesian optimization where only one point can be evaluated at a time. The authors extend the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting by introducing the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) acquisition function, which is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective to choose the optimal batch of q points to evaluate next. The q-KG acquisition function measures the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor when adding a batch of q points, making it Bayes-optimal for minimizing simple regret when only one batch remains.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it rigorously derives the q-KG acquisition function, which differs from previous parallel approaches that typically optimize single-point acquisition functions iteratively. Second, it develops a computationally efficient method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute the gradient of q-KG, enabling efficient optimization of the acquisition function even for larger batch sizes. Third, the authors provide extensive empirical evidence demonstrating that q-KG consistently outperforms state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization methods on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems, with particularly significant improvements in noisy evaluation settings.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear derivation of the q-KG acquisition function from first principles. The experimental results are comprehensive, covering four well-known synthetic functions and two practical machine learning problems (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN tuning on CIFAR10), with consistent evidence that q-KG finds better solutions with fewer evaluations than alternatives like parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE. The implementation is well-executed, with open-source code provided, and the paper demonstrates particularly strong performance in noisy settings, which is crucial for real-world applications. The authors also make a compelling case for the practical importance of parallel Bayesian optimization in hyperparameter tuning for machine learning.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. The literature review contains inaccuracies: it claims that "integrating EI w.r.t. posterior and Constant Liar appeared in prior work" but fails to properly cite Ginsbourger et al. (2010), which explicitly presents the Constant Liar method and discusses parallel EI. The paper also omits key related work, including Chevalier et al. (2013) who presented CL-mix and q-EI, and Marmin et al. (2015) who studied natural-gradient maximization for q-EI. These omissions need to be corrected with proper citations and comparisons. 

Several technical concerns require clarification. The paper assumes the domain A is compact and f is continuous but doesn't explicitly state these assumptions. The choice of Latin Hypercube Sampling for the initial design is not justified, and Algorithm 1 doesn't clarify how hyperparameters are updated (the paper mentions "re-train the hyperparameters of the GP by MLE" but doesn't specify the update frequency). Section 5.2 contains a potential confusion: the definition of g should use a min, not a max, as it's the minimum of the GP predictor. The smoothness of g and differentiability of the Cholesky factor used in gradient derivation are not properly justified. Additionally, the paper states the Matérn parameter as 2/5 in the abstract, but it should be 5/2.

The paper also fails to quantify the speed-up gained by parallelization, which is a crucial practical consideration for the approach's value. While it demonstrates that q-KG finds better solutions faster, it doesn't address how much wall-clock time is saved by evaluating multiple points in parallel.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to batch Bayesian optimization. The theoretical foundation is sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the approach has clear practical value for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning. With minor revisions to address the literature review issues, clarify technical points, and provide more context about the speed-up from parallelization, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

Pro: Rigorous theoretical foundation, strong empirical results across diverse problems, particularly strong performance in noisy settings, well-implemented code.
Con: Inaccurate literature review, omission of key related work, insufficient technical justification for some assumptions, lack of discussion about speed-up from parallelization.
