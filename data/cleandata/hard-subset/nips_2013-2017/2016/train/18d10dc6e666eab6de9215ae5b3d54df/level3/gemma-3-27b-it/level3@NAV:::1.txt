## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), designed to efficiently leverage parallel function evaluations. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the increasing prevalence of parallel computing environments in machine learning, where simultaneous evaluation of, for example, different neural network configurations is possible. The core contribution lies in extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient criterion to a batch setting, providing a theoretically grounded approach to selecting optimal batches of points for evaluation. They also present a practical method, based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), to efficiently compute gradients for optimizing the q-KG acquisition function. Empirical results on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning demonstrate competitive performance, often outperforming state-of-the-art batch BO algorithms, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of existing BO literature, correctly positioning q-KG within the landscape of parallel acquisition functions like parallel EI and UCB. However, the literature review contains some imprecisions. The claim that integrating EI w.r.t. the posterior and the “Constant Liar” algorithm appeared in prior work is inaccurate – both were introduced in Ginsbourger et al. (2016) as noted in their citation of "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization". The omission of Chevalier et al. (2015) presenting CL-mix and the work on differentiating EI for batch design (Marmin et al., 2016) is also a noticeable oversight. 

**Strengths:** The q-KG method is theoretically sound, clearly motivated, and empirically promising. The IPA-based gradient computation is a significant contribution, enabling efficient optimization of the acquisition function. The experimental results are well-presented and demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The potential for societal impact through parallelizing BO, especially in computationally intensive ML tasks, is high.

**Weaknesses:** A key weakness is the lack of analysis regarding speed-ups achieved by moving from sequential to batch-sequential optimization. While the paper demonstrates q-KG’s performance *within* a batch setting, it doesn’t quantify the benefits of batching itself. Several assumptions, like the compactness of domain A and the continuity of f, are not explicitly stated. The restriction to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) for the initial design A is not justified.  Algorithm 1 lacks clarity regarding hyperparameter re-estimation – how and when are GP hyperparameters updated? Furthermore, the definition of 'g' in Sec. 5.2 is unclear (max or min?), and its smoothness isn’t guaranteed. The differentiability of the Cholesky factor used in the derivations is another potential concern. A minor error exists – the Matérn parameter should be 5/2, not 2/5. Finally, verifying the GP fits in Figure 1 with different software would strengthen the reproducibility of the results.



**Overall:** Despite minor concerns, this is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach. I **strongly recommend acceptance**, with the expectation that the authors address the points raised regarding clarity, assumptions, and literature review. The potential impact of efficient parallel BO is significant.
