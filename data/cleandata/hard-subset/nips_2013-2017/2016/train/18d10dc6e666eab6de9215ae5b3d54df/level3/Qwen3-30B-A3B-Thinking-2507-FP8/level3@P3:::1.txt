

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm based on the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function, extending the sequential KG approach to the parallel setting. The authors propose a batch-sequential acquisition function called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that measures the information gain from evaluating q points simultaneously. Unlike prior work that constructs batches iteratively (e.g., parallel EI or Constant Liar), the q-KG method optimizes the batch collectively rather than greedily. The paper provides a detailed derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and develops an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate its gradient, enabling practical optimization. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems that q-KG consistently outperforms state-of-the-art batch-sequential BO algorithms, particularly in noisy settings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant technical contribution by generalizing the knowledge gradient principle to the batch-sequential setting. The authors provide a decision-theoretic justification for their acquisition function and develop an efficient computational strategy to overcome the otherwise prohibitive computational cost of maximizing q-KG.

2. **Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, covering both synthetic test functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show that q-KG outperforms competing methods, especially in noisy settings.

3. **Practical Impact:** The paper addresses a significant practical problem - parallelizing Bayesian optimization to leverage modern computing infrastructure. This has high societal impact as it enables faster optimization of expensive machine learning pipelines, which is increasingly important as models grow larger and more complex.

4. **Clarity of Core Idea:** The paper clearly articulates the intuition behind the q-KG acquisition function and how it differs from previous approaches like parallel EI and Constant Liar.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Literature Review Issues:** The literature review contains imprecise claims. The statement "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" appears to misattribute prior work (Ginsbourger et al. 2010, which the authors cite, actually presents a different parallelization approach). The paper also omits important related work, including Chevalier et al.'s CL-mix method and Marmin et al.'s natural-gradient maximization for q-EI.

2. **Assumptions Clarification Needed:** The paper does not explicitly state whether the domain A is assumed compact or if f is assumed continuous. These assumptions are crucial for the theoretical guarantees of the method.

3. **Implementation Details:** The paper does not explain when hyperparameters are updated in Algorithm 1 (it states "re-train the hyperparameters of the GP by MLE" but doesn't specify the update frequency). The choice of Latin Hypercube Sampling for the initial design also lacks justification.

4. **Theoretical Concerns:** Section 5.2 contains a critical ambiguity - it's unclear whether g is defined using a max or min. The smoothness of g in this section is not obviously guaranteed, and the differentiability of the Cholesky factor used in the gradient derivation is non-trivial.

5. **Minor Errors:** The Matérn parameter is incorrectly stated as 2/5 (should be 5/2) in Section 6. The paper does not study the speed-up gained by moving from sequential to batch-sequential BO, which is an important practical consideration.

6. **Experimental Verification:** The paper does not address whether the same GPs used in Figure 1 could be fitted using different software for verification, which would strengthen the experimental claims.

**Evaluation by Criteria:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with well-supported experimental results. The theoretical foundation is solid, though some theoretical details require clarification. The authors are generally careful about evaluating both strengths and weaknesses of their method.

*Clarity:* The paper is generally well-written and organized, but some technical details (like the definition of g in Section 5.2) require clarification. The omission of key literature weakens the clarity of the contribution's novelty.

*Originality:* The paper presents a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, though it builds on the sequential KG work. The computational approach using IPA is innovative for this context. However, the literature review does not adequately distinguish this work from prior batch-sequential methods.

*Significance:* The method has significant practical importance, as parallel Bayesian optimization is increasingly valuable for large-scale machine learning applications. The consistent performance gains over state-of-the-art methods, especially in noisy settings, demonstrate clear advancement of the field.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a strong technical contribution to Bayesian optimization with a well-motivated and effective algorithm. The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. While the paper has some weaknesses in the literature review and theoretical exposition, these are addressable in the camera-ready version. The high societal impact of parallelizing Bayesian optimization, combined with the strong empirical results, makes this a significant contribution to the field.

I strongly recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the literature review issues, clarify the theoretical details, and correct the Matérn parameter. The paper is of high quality and would be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
