This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization (BO). The core problem addressed is the efficient optimization of expensive-to-evaluate black-box functions when multiple function evaluations can be performed in parallel. The proposed q-KG method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, selecting a batch of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality after one parallel step. This contrasts with many existing methods that construct batches greedily. To make the joint optimization of the `q` points computationally feasible, the authors leverage infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an efficient, unbiased estimator for the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function. This allows the use of standard gradient-based optimizers. The proposed method is a generalization of the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to the parallel setting and is shown to relate to parallel Expected Improvement (EI) under certain simplifying assumptions (noise-free evaluations, solution restricted to sampled points).

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance and Timeliness:** The problem of parallelizing expensive function evaluations is highly relevant, especially for hyperparameter tuning of large machine learning models where parallel compute resources are readily available. This work provides a principled and effective solution to an important practical problem.

2.  **Principled Approach:** The derivation of q-KG is a key strength. Instead of using heuristics or greedy sequential selection to form a batch, the method provides a one-step Bayes-optimal policy for selecting the entire batch of points jointly. This decision-theoretic foundation is theoretically appealing.

3.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The naive optimization of q-KG is computationally prohibitive. The development of an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy using IPA is a significant technical contribution that makes the proposed method practical.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare q-KG against several state-of-the-art parallel BO algorithms (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on a variety of synthetic benchmarks and two real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10). The results consistently demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms existing methods, with particularly strong performance in the presence of noise. This latter point is important, as many real-world evaluations are inherently noisy (e.g., due to stochastic optimization or variable test set performance).

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees. The submission would be significantly strengthened by a formal analysis of the algorithm's properties. For instance, providing theoretical error bounds (e.g., simple regret bounds) would help to formally characterize its convergence properties. Furthermore, a quantitative analysis of the computational complexity with respect to the input dimension `d`, batch size `q`, and number of observations `n` would be valuable for understanding the method's scalability.

2.  **Limitations of the GP Prior:** The method relies on a Gaussian Process (GP) prior, which is standard in BO. However, the suitability of standard GP kernels (like the ARD Matérn used here) for high-dimensional and complex search spaces, such as those encountered when tuning neural network architectures, can be a concern. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of this limitation and potential ways to mitigate it (e.g., using specialized kernels, random embeddings, or input warping).

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**
-   The paper is generally well-written and clear. However, there are a few minor points that could be improved for clarity:
    -   Line 36: The grammar in "...chooses the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal..." is slightly awkward. Suggest rephrasing, e.g., "...chooses the set of points that is, on average, optimal to evaluate next..."
    -   Line 95: The definition of the q-KG factor in Eq. (4.1) uses `min x∈A µ(n)(x)`. While `A` is defined in the introduction as the feasible domain, it would be helpful to explicitly state here that this minimization is over the continuous domain `A`.
    -   Line 104: It's stated that `µ(n+q)(x)` is a random vector. It is a random field (or process); for a fixed `x`, it is a random variable. Please clarify this phrasing.
    -   Line 128: The paper states that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under specific conditions. It would be helpful to add a sentence briefly explaining how parallel EI is itself typically computed (e.g., via greedy selection or Monte Carlo optimization) to better frame the comparison.
    -   Line 140: The notation `A` is used for the continuous feasible domain in Eq. (4.2), while `An` is used for its discretization in Eq. (5.4). This is good, but the abstract notation in Section 5.1 and 5.2 (e.g., `q-KG(z(1:q),A)`) refers to a finite set `A`. This reuse of `A` for both the continuous domain and a generic finite set can be confusing. It would be clearer to use a distinct symbol for the finite set in this section.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid and valuable contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The paper introduces a well-motivated, principled, and practical algorithm for an important problem. The technical approach for optimizing the acquisition function is novel, and the strong empirical results, especially in noisy settings, convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness. While the lack of theoretical analysis is a weakness, the quality of the proposed method and the thoroughness of the experiments make this a strong paper for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
