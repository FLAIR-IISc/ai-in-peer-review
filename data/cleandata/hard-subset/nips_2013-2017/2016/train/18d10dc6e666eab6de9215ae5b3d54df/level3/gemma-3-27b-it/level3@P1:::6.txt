This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization, aiming to select the Bayes-optimal batch of points for parallel evaluation. The core contribution lies in extending the Knowledge Gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting and utilizing Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) for efficient gradient computation, enabling optimization of the acquisition function. The method is evaluated on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression and CNNs) in both noise-free and noisy scenarios, demonstrating competitive or superior performance compared to existing parallel BO algorithms.

The work builds upon a well-established line of research in Bayesian Optimization, particularly extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient method [Frazier et al., 2008] to a parallel framework. It addresses the growing need for efficient parallel optimization techniques, motivated by the increasing availability of parallel computing resources and the time-consuming nature of evaluating complex functions, such as training deep neural networks. This paper relates to previous parallel BO approaches like parallel Expected Improvement [Ginsbourger & Le Riche, 2010] and parallel Upper Confidence Bound [Contal et al., 2014], aiming to improve upon them through a more theoretically grounded approach.  The use of IPA for gradient estimation is also a known technique within the optimization community [L'Ecuyer, 1988], though its application to this specific problem is a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, aiming for Bayes-optimality within the constraints of a single batch evaluation. This offers a strong theoretical grounding, setting it apart from purely heuristic approaches.
* **Efficient Gradient Computation:** The application of IPA for gradient estimation is a crucial practical contribution, making optimization of the q-KG acquisition function feasible.
* **Empirical Performance:**  The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on synthetic benchmarks and practical machine learning problems, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios.  The consistent improvement over baseline algorithms is a positive indicator.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and experimental setup.  The background on Gaussian processes is concise and accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to closely related work, specifically Wang et al. [2015] and their parallel EI (q-EI) implementation. The differences between q-KG and q-EI should be explicitly highlighted and justified.
* **Scalability Concerns:**  The paper does not address the scalability of the method to larger batch sizes (q).  The benefits of increasing the level of parallelism beyond q=4 are not explored, and there's no discussion of computational limitations as q grows.
* **Lack of Practical Performance Metrics:**  Crucially, the paper lacks wall-clock time measurements.  Comparing the total time to solution against random search or sequential Bayesian Optimization is essential to demonstrate the practical advantages of this parallel approach. Without these metrics, it's difficult to assess the actual speedup achieved.
* **Batch Size Justification:**  The choice of a fixed batch size (q=4) across all experiments is not justified.  An investigation into the optimal batch size for different problem characteristics would strengthen the results.
* **Synchronous Evaluation:** The method appears to assume synchronous parallel evaluation. The potential benefits and challenges of extending it to asynchronous evaluations (where function evaluations complete at different times) are not discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable addition to the field of parallel Bayesian Optimization.
* The IPA-based gradient computation is a significant practical contribution.
* The empirical results demonstrate promising performance, particularly in noisy environments.
* The method addresses a relevant and important problem in the context of modern machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of comparison to closely related work (Wang et al., 2015) and the absence of practical performance metrics (wall-clock time) weaken the impact of the empirical results.
* The scalability to larger batch sizes is not addressed.
* The fixed batch size and assumption of synchronous evaluation limit the generalizability of the method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of parallel Bayesian Optimization, but it falls short of being a truly compelling submission. The theoretical foundation and IPA-based optimization are valuable, but the lack of thorough comparison and practical performance analysis detracts from its impact. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the following key issues:

1. **Clarify the relationship to Wang et al. (2015) and provide a direct comparison.**
2. **Investigate the scalability of the method to larger batch sizes (q) and discuss computational limitations.**
3. **Present wall-clock time performance comparisons to random search and sequential BO.**
4. **Provide a justification for the fixed batch size (q=4) or explore its sensitivity.**
5. **Address the possibility of extending the method to asynchronous parallel evaluations.**

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact on the field.
