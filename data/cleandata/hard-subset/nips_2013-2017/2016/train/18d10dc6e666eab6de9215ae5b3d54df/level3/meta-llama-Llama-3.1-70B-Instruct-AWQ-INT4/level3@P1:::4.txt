This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), which provides the one-step Bayes optimal batch of points to sample. The authors derive this method using a decision-theoretic analysis and develop an efficient strategy for computing this Bayes-optimal batch of points. They demonstrate that q-KG finds global optima significantly faster than previous batch Bayesian optimization algorithms on both synthetic test functions and when tuning hyperparameters of practical machine learning algorithms, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

The main ideas of this paper relate to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere in the field of Bayesian optimization. The authors build upon the knowledge gradient method for non-batch (sequential) settings and extend it to the parallel setting. They also compare their method to other parallel Bayesian optimization algorithms, such as parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration.

The strengths of this paper include its sound methodological contribution, convincing experiments, and well-written presentation. The authors tackle an important problem in speeding up expensive function evaluations via distributed/batch evaluation, and their idea of proposing q points at each iteration is interesting and can substantially improve efficiency.

However, there are some weaknesses and concerns. One concern is that assuming a Gaussian process prior may be inappropriate for high-dimensional inputs such as neural network architectures. Additionally, the authors do not provide theoretical results, such as error bounds for the algorithm and a quantitative complexity analysis with respect to input dimension.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but there are some minor issues and typos that need to be addressed. For example, the grammar on line 36 needs to be corrected, and the input of mu^n on line 104 needs to be clarified.

Overall, I would rate the quality of this paper as strong, and I believe it makes a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. However, I would like to see the authors address the concerns mentioned above and provide additional theoretical results to support their method.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that can substantially improve efficiency in expensive function evaluations.
* The authors provide convincing empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning algorithms.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a wide range of audiences.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The authors assume a Gaussian process prior, which may not be appropriate for high-dimensional inputs such as neural network architectures.
* The paper lacks theoretical results, such as error bounds for the algorithm and a quantitative complexity analysis with respect to input dimension.
* There are some minor issues and typos that need to be addressed to improve the clarity and readability of the paper.
