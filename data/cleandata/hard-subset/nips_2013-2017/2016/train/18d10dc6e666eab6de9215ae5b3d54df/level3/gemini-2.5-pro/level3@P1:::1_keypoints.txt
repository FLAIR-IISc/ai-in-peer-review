- Introduces q-KG, a principled decision-theoretic batch acquisition for Bayesian optimization that extends Knowledge Gradient to select q points jointly.  
- q-KG measures expected improvement in final solution quality via the posterior-mean minimum, moving beyond myopic/greedy batch methods.  
- Proposes an efficient gradient estimator for q-KG using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the acquisition.  
- Empirical results are comprehensive and show q-KG is competitive or superior to state-of-the-art batch BO methods, especially in noisy settings.  
- Technical contribution: the IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator makes otherwise intractable q-KG optimization practical.  
- Literature review needs precision: parallelizing EI and the "constant liar" heuristic were originally proposed by Ginsbourger et al. (2010).  
- Related work omissions: the CL-mix heuristic (Chevalier et al., 2012) is not cited.  
- Related work omissions: gradient-based maximization of q-EI (Marmin et al., 2015) is a relevant citation for differentiable batch acquisition.  
- Paper lacks analysis of wall-clock speed-ups from parallelization; only sample efficiency is studied.  
- Methodology clarifications needed: state assumptions on domain compactness and function continuity used in BO.  
- Algorithm details unclear: motivation for Latin Hypercube Sampling as initial design and frequency/method of GP hyperparameter re-training (full MLE vs. Bayesian update) should be specified.  
- Section 5.2 technical concerns: confirm the definition of g (involving min_x mu(n)(x) differences) and justify interchange of derivative and expectation given non-smoothness from min operations.  
- Clarify differentiability assumptions for the Cholesky factor D(n), since Cholesky derivatives are not guaranteed everywhere (e.g., non–positive-definite cases).  
- Minor issues: possible Matérn kernel notation typo (should be 5/2, not 2/5) and suggestion to verify GP fits with an alternative software package for reproducibility.  
- Overall verdict: high-quality, significant, and well-written paper with addressable minor issues; strong recommendation for publication.
