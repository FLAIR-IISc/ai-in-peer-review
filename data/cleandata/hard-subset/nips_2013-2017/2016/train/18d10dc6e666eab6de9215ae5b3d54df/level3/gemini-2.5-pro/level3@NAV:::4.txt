This paper proposes a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), designed for settings where `q` expensive function evaluations can be performed in parallel. The method generalizes the well-known knowledge gradient (KG) policy from the sequential to the batch setting and is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective as the one-step Bayes-optimal choice of points. A key contribution is an efficient method to optimize the q-KG function using a gradient-based approach, leveraging infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute an unbiased gradient estimator. The paper correctly positions this work relative to previous batch Bayesian optimization methods, such as those based on Expected Improvement (EI) or Upper Confidence Bounds (UCB).

The paper's strengths are numerous. It tackles the highly significant and practical problem of accelerating optimization through parallelization. The proposed q-KG method is a principled and original contribution that moves beyond the often greedy or heuristic construction of batches in prior work. The technical development of an efficient gradient-based optimizer for the acquisition function is a strong point, making the method practical. The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing, with experiments on synthetic benchmarks and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression and convolutional neural networks. The results demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms state-of-the-art methods, showing particularly significant gains in noisy environments. The paper is also very clearly written and well-organized.

My main concerns relate to the underlying modeling assumptions and the lack of theoretical analysis. While standard for Bayesian optimization, the assumption of a Gaussian process prior may be a limitation for very high-dimensional problems like neural network architecture search, and a brief discussion of this would be welcome. Furthermore, the paper would be substantially stronger with theoretical results, such as error bounds for the algorithm or a formal complexity analysis of the optimization procedure with respect to input dimension `d` and batch size `q`.

Several minor points could improve clarity:
- l.36: Awkward grammar in "the set of points to evaluate next that is".
- l.95: The domain A in `q-KG(z(1:q),A)` should be explicitly defined or clarified.
- l.104: The input of `mu^n` in the equation for `min x in A mu^(n)(x)` should be `mu^(n)(x)`.
- l.128: A brief discussion comparing the derivation of q-KG to parallel EI would be helpful.
- l.140: Reusing the notation A for both the feasible domain and the discretized set for computation is confusing.

Overall, this is a solid and high-quality contribution. The method is sound, the experiments are compelling, and the work addresses an important problem. Despite the lack of theoretical guarantees, it represents a clear advance for batch Bayesian optimization. I recommend acceptance.
