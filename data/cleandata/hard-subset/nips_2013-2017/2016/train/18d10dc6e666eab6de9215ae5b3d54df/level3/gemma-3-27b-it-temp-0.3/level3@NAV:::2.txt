## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces a novel parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method. The core idea is to compute Bayes-optimal batches of points to evaluate, leveraging a decision-theoretic analysis. The authors provide an efficient gradient-based approach for maximizing the proposed acquisition function, and demonstrate improvements over existing parallel BO methods on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks. 

The paper builds upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient method by extending it to the parallel setting. It clearly positions itself within the existing literature on parallel BO, contrasting its approach – jointly optimizing over a batch – with iterative greedy methods like parallel EI and UCB. The theoretical foundation is strong, with a clear derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and a detailed explanation of the infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) used for efficient gradient computation. The writing is excellent, making the complex concepts accessible and well-organized.

However, while the theoretical contributions are valuable, the empirical evaluation is a significant weakness. The use of test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic; threshold-based metrics like AUC or log-loss would be more appropriate for evaluating hyperparameter optimization performance. Furthermore, the experimental benchmarks are limited to MNIST and CIFAR10 with only logistic regression and a CNN. This narrow scope hinders the generalizability of the findings. A more comprehensive evaluation using benchmarks like HPOlib, and a wider variety of datasets and learning algorithms (e.g., SVMs, Random Forests, different CNN architectures) is crucial to demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method. The assumption of independent normally distributed observation noise is also a limitation, as it may not hold in realistic HPO scenarios with structural bias and correlated errors from cross-validation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and elegant solution to parallelizing BO.
*   Novel approach to computing Bayes-optimal batches.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak empirical evaluation with limited benchmarks and inappropriate evaluation metrics.
*   Restrictive assumption of independent normally distributed noise.
*   Limited demonstration of generalizability beyond two datasets and two algorithms.



**Overall:** This paper presents a promising theoretical contribution to parallel Bayesian optimization. However, the limited empirical validation significantly weakens its impact. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to expand the experimental evaluation and address the limitations outlined above. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its potential for significant impact within the field.
