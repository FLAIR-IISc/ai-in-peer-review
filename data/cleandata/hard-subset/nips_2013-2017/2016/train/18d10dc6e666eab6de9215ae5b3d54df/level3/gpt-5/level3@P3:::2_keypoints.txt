- Introduces q-KG: a Bayes-optimal batch BO acquisition defined as expected reduction in minimum posterior mean, generalizing KG to parallel evaluations.
- Shows q-KG reduces to parallel EI in noiseless settings when final recommendation must be among sampled points.
- Addresses optimization of q-KG via Monte Carlo estimation and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to obtain unbiased stochastic gradients.
- Discretizes the inner decision domain using samples near posterior optima to compute q-KG.
- Novelty: decision-theoretic batch acquisition with efficient stochastic gradient optimization is a meaningful, original advance.
- Strength: conceptually elegant and rigorous—Bayes-optimal for the final batch and clear relation to EI.
- Strength: strong theoretical derivations, including unbiased gradient estimator under reasonable smoothness conditions.
- Strength: clear, well-organized exposition and an easy-to-follow algorithmic pipeline.
- Strength: empirical results show consistent gains, especially in noisy problem settings.
- Weakness: assumes independent, homoscedastic Gaussian noise—unrealistic for many HPO scenarios with heteroscedastic or correlated noise.
- Weakness: HPO experiments optimize test-set error, risking data leakage and optimistic bias; probabilistic validation metrics are preferable.
- Weakness: narrow benchmarks—only MNIST and CIFAR-10 with two learners (logistic regression, CNN); lacks broader datasets and algorithmic diversity.
- Weakness: limited practical-deployment study—synchronous-only batches, no asynchronous variants, no wall-clock or cost-aware analysis.
- Quality: technically sound—derivations and unbiased gradient estimator are correct; discretization approach is standard and justified.
- Empirical support: mixed—synthetic tests strong, but HPO experiments limited (few seeds) and missing sensitivity analyses (M, q, dimension, GP hyperparameters).
- Assumptions concern: noise-modeling mismatch with real HPO; should be acknowledged with guidance on extensions (e.g., multi-output or latent-noise GPs).
- Clarity: paper is well written; should explicitly state computational complexity in q, |A_n|, and MC samples and discuss IPA stability/variance-control strategies.
- Significance: high potential impact if supported by broader, more rigorous HPO experiments and proper evaluation protocols.
- Requested clarification: how to extend q-KG and IPA gradients to heteroscedastic and correlated noise models (e.g., multi-output GP over CV folds or latent noise GP).
- Requested clarification: rerun HPO experiments optimizing validation metrics (not test), report final test once, and use probabilistic metrics (AUC/log loss) with appropriate averaging.
- Requested clarification: expand benchmarks to HPOlib-style tasks with diverse learners (SVM, RF/GBM, GP classifiers, matrix factorization) and more datasets and seeds.
- Requested clarification: report sensitivity/ablation studies for discretization size M, number of MC samples/fantasies, kernel choices, batch size q, and runtime/wall-clock comparisons; analyze higher-dimensional behavior.
- Requested clarification: discuss and experiment with asynchronous q-KG and cost-aware variants (e.g., time-weighted KG).
- Requested clarification: avoid repeatedly peeking at the test set during optimization to prevent data leakage.
- Pros for acceptance: strong theory, principled acquisition, IPA-based gradients that enable practical optimization, and consistent synthetic/noisy improvements.
- Cons for acceptance: limited and potentially biased HPO evaluation, unrealistic noise assumptions, and insufficient analysis of scalability, sensitivity, and asynchronous use.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept—the method is solid and promising but needs stronger, broader HPO experiments and discussion/handling of realistic noise to increase impact.
- Reviewer confidence: medium.
