- Proposes q-KG, a novel batch Bayesian optimization method using knowledge gradient and IPA to maximize one-step-ahead information gain.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance on synthetic benchmarks and HPO for logistic regression (MNIST) and CNNs (CIFAR10), especially in noisy settings.

Strengths:
- Sound technical foundation: decision-theoretic grounding and practical IPA-based gradient estimation.  
- Clear writing and organization with reproducible technical details.  
- Tackles a relevant problem—parallel HPO with potential for substantial speedups.  
- Empirical validation shows competitiveness on noiseless problems and superiority in noisy evaluations.

Weaknesses:
- No scalability analysis: unclear how computational cost grows with batch size q.  
- Unjustified fixed batch size (q=4) without sensitivity analysis or rationale.  
- Missing comparison to key baselines, notably Wang et al. (2015) / parallel EI (q-EI).  
- No comparison to random search or sequential BO with comparable wall-clock budget.  
- Absence of wall-clock time measurements prevents assessment of practical efficiency.  
- Plots are unclear: "iterations" axis ambiguous (batches vs evaluations) and varying start values unexplained.

Reviewer questions / required additions:
- Must compare and clearly contrast q-KG with Wang et al. (2015) / q-EI and other IPA-based parallel methods.  
- Discuss computational complexity and expected benefits when scaling to larger q.  
- Comment on feasibility and challenges of extending the synchronous method to asynchronous parallel evaluations.  
- Provide wall-clock time measurements and quantify per-batch computational overhead versus baselines.  
- Clarify plot labeling: define "iterations" (batches or evaluations) and state inclusion of initial design points.

Minor comments:
- Equation (4.1) likely missing z^(1:n) in the minimum expression.  
- Typo: “achieve” should be “achieves.”  
- Citation phrasing inconsistent and should be reviewed.  
- Do not credit Wang et al. (2015) with inventing IPA—IPA is established earlier.

Overall assessment:
- Promising and well-founded approach but weakened by missing baselines, scalability analysis, and wall-clock measurements; experimental clarity issues remain.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel application of knowledge gradient to parallel BO.  
- Strong theoretical foundation and practical IPA implementation.  
- Demonstrated gains in noisy evaluation scenarios.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks comparison to strong baselines (e.g., Wang et al. 2015).  
- Insufficient analysis of scalability with batch size.  
- Missing wall-clock performance and practical speedup evidence.  
- Unjustified batch size choice and unclear experimental details.

Recommendation:
- Reject with request for major revision; require thorough baseline comparisons, scalability analysis, wall-clock timings, and clarification of experiments.
