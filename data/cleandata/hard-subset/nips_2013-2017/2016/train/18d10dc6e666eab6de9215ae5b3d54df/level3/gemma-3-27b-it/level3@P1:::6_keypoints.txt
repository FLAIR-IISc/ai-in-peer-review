- Introduces parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) for batch Bayesian optimization to select Bayes-optimal batches for parallel evaluation.  
- Extends sequential Knowledge Gradient to parallel setting and leverages Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) for efficient acquisition-gradient computation.  
- Evaluated on synthetic functions and ML hyperparameter tuning (logistic regression, CNNs) in noise-free and noisy settings, showing competitive or superior performance to existing parallel BO methods.  
- Builds on established BO literature (e.g., Frazier et al. 2008) and relates to prior parallel approaches like q-EI and parallel UCB.  
- Theoretical foundation: decision-theoretic derivation aiming for Bayes-optimality within single-batch constraints.  
- Efficient gradient computation via IPA makes optimizing the q-KG acquisition function practically feasible.  
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over baselines, particularly in noisy evaluations.  
- Paper is well-written, logically organized, with clear exposition and accessible GP background.  
- Lacks comprehensive comparison to closely related work, notably Wang et al. (2015) and parallel EI (q-EI).  
- Does not address scalability or computational limitations as batch size q increases beyond small values.  
- Missing practical wall-clock time measurements, preventing assessment of real speedup versus random search or sequential BO.  
- Uses a fixed batch size (q=4) without justification or sensitivity analysis across different problem settings.  
- Assumes synchronous parallel evaluations and does not discuss asynchronous extensions or their challenges/benefits.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid theory, valuable IPA-based practical contribution, and promising empirical performance in noisy scenarios.  
- Arguments against acceptance: inadequate comparison to related work, missing wall-clock timings, and unaddressed scalability and generalizability issues.  
- Overall assessment: solid and valuable contribution but not compelling due to gaps in empirical comparison and practical evaluation; recommend rejection with revisions.  
- Recommended revisions: clarify relation to Wang et al. (2015) and add direct comparisons; investigate scalability with larger q; report wall-clock times versus baselines; justify or explore batch-size sensitivity; discuss asynchronous evaluation extensions.
