This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization (BO). The method extends the decision-theoretic principles of the sequential Knowledge Gradient to the parallel setting, defining a one-step Bayes-optimal criterion for selecting a batch of points. The authors also derive an efficient gradient-based strategy for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function. This work builds upon a growing body of literature aimed at parallelizing BO, differentiating itself from greedy or heuristic extensions of Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) by jointly optimizing the value of the entire batch.

The paper's primary strength lies in its principled approach and significant practical implications. The problem of parallelizing BO is of high importance to the machine learning community, and q-KG presents a novel and well-motivated solution. The empirical results are a major asset, demonstrating through comprehensive experiments on synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks that q-KG is highly competitive and often outperforms state-of-the-art batch BO algorithms, especially in noisy environments. Given its strong performance and the importance of the problem, I strongly recommend this paper for publication.

Despite my enthusiasm, there are several areas that could be improved. The literature review contains imprecise claims; for instance, integrating EI w.r.t. the posterior and the Constant Liar heuristic were proposed by Ginsbourger et al. ("Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization"). Key related works, such as Chevalier et al. on CL-mix and Marmin et al. on natural-gradient maximization of q-EI, are also omitted.

Furthermore, several technical points require clarification. The paper should explicitly state its assumptions (e.g., is the domain A compact and f continuous?). The derivations in Section 5 rely on the smoothness of g and differentiability of the Cholesky factor, which are not obviously guaranteed and warrant discussion. Algorithmically, it is unclear why the initial design is restricted to a Latin Hypercube Sample or how GP hyperparameters are updated during the optimization loop. The paper would also benefit from a study of the computational speed-ups achieved by using the batch-sequential method over a purely sequential one.

Minor points:
