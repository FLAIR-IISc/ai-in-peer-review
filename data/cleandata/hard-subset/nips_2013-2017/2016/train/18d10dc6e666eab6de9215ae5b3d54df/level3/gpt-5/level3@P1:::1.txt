Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces a batch knowledge gradient acquisition function (q-KG) for Bayesian optimization with Gaussian process (GP) priors. q-KG is defined as the expected one-step decrement in the GP posterior predictive minimum after observing a batch of q evaluations. This generalizes the classical (sequential) knowledge gradient (KG) to the parallel setting. The authors show that q-KG is Bayes-optimal for the one-step decision problem when the goal is to minimize the posterior predictive minimum, and reduces to parallel EI in the special case of noise-free evaluations and when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points. Computation relies on Monte Carlo estimation of q-KG and an IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator to enable gradient-based optimization of the batch. Because the exact KG is intractable in continuous domains, the method discretizes the objective for evaluating the inner minimum, with the discrete set evolving over time via posterior sampling heuristics. Experiments on standard synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks show that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms parallel EI (both exact and heuristic), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with especially strong gains under observation noise.

The work is well-positioned relative to parallel BO using EI (e.g., constant liar, integrated EI) and UCB-based approaches, and complements entropy-search variants (e.g., PPES). It advances the KG literature by moving from sequential KG (Frazier et al.) and correlated KG (Scott et al.) to batch selection, and by developing a differentiable Monte Carlo estimator suitable for continuous design spaces.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic foundation: q-KG optimizes a principled, one-step Bayes-optimal criterion for batches, bridging a gap between sequential KG and practical batch BO.
- Sound computational strategy: unbiased IPA-based gradient estimator for q-KG enables scalable gradient-based optimization even when q is moderately large.
- Strong empirical performance: across noise-free and especially noisy settings, q-KG improves simple regret faster than well-known batch methods (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE).
- Practical relevance: addresses parallel evaluation settings common in ML hyperparameter tuning and other expensive black-box optimization scenarios.
- Open-source implementation and careful experimental setup.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Interchange of differentiation and expectation (Sec. 5.2): The IPA argument requires regularity conditions (e.g., compact domain, continuous kernels, unique argmins with probability 1, positive-definite covariance). Please explicitly state assumptions under which ∂/∂z E[g] = E[∂g/∂z] holds, and provide a precise lemma or cite a theorem (e.g., L’Ecuyer, 1990).
- Smoothness of g: g involves minima over x in A (or An), which induces nonsmoothness when the argmin changes. Clarify how measure-zero nondifferentiability events are handled and the role of randomization ensuring almost-sure differentiability for IPA.
- Differentiability through Cholesky: Gradients w.r.t. D(n)(z(1:q)) are used. Please justify differentiability of the Cholesky factorization under positive definiteness, and note practical safeguards (e.g., jitter) to avoid degeneracy.
- Clarification in Sec. 5.2: Ensure g is consistently defined using min (not max). The current description suggests potential sign confusion; an explicit definition will avoid ambiguity.
- Discretization of A: The method samples a dynamic finite set An “from the global optima of the posterior.” This phrasing is unclear. If you mean maximizing posterior draws (Thompson-type representer points), please state so and provide details (number of draws, optimizer used). Some discussion of approximation error versus M and stability across iterations would be helpful.

Clarity
- Literature precision: Earlier work on parallel EI via integrated EI and constant liar predates several references cited here (see Ginsbourger et al., “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization,” 2010). Please correct attributions. Also include Chevalier & Ginsbourger’s CL-mix and the natural-gradient or analytic gradients for q-EI (e.g., Marmin et al., 2015), which are closely related to your gradient-based batch optimization approach.
- Hyperparameter treatment: Algorithm 1 mentions re-fitting GP hyperparameters by MLE after each batch. Clarify frequency (every batch vs intermittently), which parameters are re-estimated (including noise variance), whether priors/marginalization were considered, and any safeguards against overfitting/hyperparameter churn.
- Minor typographical fix: “Matérn 5/2” (not 2/5).

Originality
- The extension of KG to the batch setting with a practical stochastic gradient estimator is novel and nontrivial. The connection to q-EI in special cases is well articulated. Compared to nested batch KG on discrete sets, this work substantially broadens applicability to continuous spaces with differentiable computation.

Significance
- The empirical results are compelling, especially under noise, a common regime in ML tuning. The approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners needing efficient batch BO.
- A missed opportunity is to quantify wall-clock speedups from parallelization (e.g., throughput vs. overhead of acquisition optimization), which would strengthen the case for practical impact.

Questions for the authors
- Assumptions: Is A assumed compact and f continuous (under the GP prior this often holds), ensuring minima exist and IPA regularity conditions apply?
- Initial design: Why restrict to LHS? Would Sobol sequences or space-filling designs with better discrepancy change results?
- Hyperparameters: Are they re-estimated every batch? Did you try Bayesian marginalization over hyperparameters, which may stabilize gradients for q-KG in early iterations?
- Discretization: Specify how AM_n is constructed exactly, M’s sensitivity, and whether An includes z(1:q) to ensure differentiability as z moves.
- Reproducibility/verification: Could you confirm the GP fits used in Fig. 1 (and elsewhere) with an independent implementation to rule out software artifacts?
- Baselines: Did you compare to PPES or other entropy-based batch methods? If not, a brief rationale would help.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, Bayes-optimal batch acquisition with clear derivation.
- Novel and efficient stochastic gradient computation enabling practical optimization.
- Strong and consistent empirical gains, particularly with noisy observations.
- High practical relevance and open-source code.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete empirical assessment of wall-clock/parallel speedups and acquisition overhead.
- Some literature attributions are imprecise; a few important related works are omitted.
- Technical assumptions for IPA and differentiability are not fully spelled out.
- Discretization procedure and its impact on accuracy are under-specified.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper makes a solid, well-motivated contribution to batch Bayesian optimization with demonstrated empirical benefits and a principled methodology. Addressing the noted clarity and attribution issues, and adding brief discussion of assumptions and computational overhead, would further strengthen the final version.
