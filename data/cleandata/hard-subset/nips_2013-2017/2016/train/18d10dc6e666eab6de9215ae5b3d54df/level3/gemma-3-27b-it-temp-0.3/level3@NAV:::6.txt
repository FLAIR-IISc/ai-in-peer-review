## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a novel approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization (BO) – the parallel Knowledge Gradient method (q-KG) – leveraging the knowledge gradient framework and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for efficient acquisition function maximization. The core idea of computing a Bayes-optimal batch is appealing, particularly in multi-core environments where parallel function evaluations are readily available. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the background, technical details, and experimental setup. The inclusion of code is a definite plus.

The strength of this work lies in its attempt to address parallel BO from a decision-theoretic perspective, aiming for a truly optimal batch selection. The experiments demonstrate competitive performance on noiseless problems and, importantly, outperform other methods on noisy evaluations – a scenario often encountered in real-world hyperparameter optimization (HPO). The application to both synthetic benchmarks and practical machine learning problems (CNN/logistic regression) strengthens the evaluation.

However, I have several concerns regarding the novelty and practical impact of the proposed method. While the combination of KG and IPA is presented as novel, it builds upon existing work, specifically IPA-based parallel selection methods like Wang et al. (2015) for parallel Expected Improvement (q-EI). The paper lacks a clear differentiation from this prior art; a direct comparison to Wang et al. (2015) is crucial to establish the unique contribution of q-KG. 

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation feels incomplete. Critically, the paper lacks wall-clock time measurements and comparisons to simpler baselines like random search or sequential BO. Demonstrating actual speedups over these methods is essential to justify the complexity of q-KG. The fixed batch size of q=4 across all experiments is also unexplained; a sensitivity analysis exploring the scaling of the method with larger *q* values (e.g., tens or hundreds) and the expected benefits of increased parallelism would be valuable. 

Finally, the synchronous nature of the method raises questions about scalability. Can the approach be extended to asynchronous parallel evaluations, which are more common in distributed computing environments? 

**Major Questions:**

1.  Have you compared to Wang et al. (2015) / parallel EI (q-EI), and can you clearly state the differences?
2.  How does the method scale to larger *q* (e.g., tens/hundreds) and what benefits are expected for more parallel runs?
3.  Can the synchronous method be extended to asynchronous parallel evaluations?
4.  What is the wall-clock performance vs random search or sequential BO, and what is the overhead to compute each new batch?
5.  Clarify the x-axis “iterations” in plots — batches or function evaluations, does it include initial design, and why do starting values differ across methods?

**Minor Comments:**

*   Possible missing z^(1:n) in Eq.(4.1)
*   Grammar typo “achieve”→“achieves”
*   Citation phrasing needs refinement.
*   Wang et al. (2015) should not be credited with introducing IPA.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a theoretically sound and well-explained method, the lack of comparative analysis against relevant baselines and the absence of practical runtime measurements weaken its impact. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the raised concerns and provide a more comprehensive evaluation. The idea is promising, but the current presentation doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant advancement over existing parallel BO techniques.
