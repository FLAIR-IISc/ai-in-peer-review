This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, leveraging the knowledge gradient and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for efficient acquisition function maximization. The core idea is to select batches of points that are Bayes-optimal in a one-step-ahead sense, aiming to maximize information gain. The method is demonstrated on synthetic benchmarks and applied to hyperparameter optimization for logistic regression (MNIST) and CNNs (CIFAR10), showing competitive performance, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of previous work in Bayesian optimization (BO) and extends it to the parallel setting. The authors appropriately cite relevant literature including work on parallel EI/UCB and knowledge gradient methods. The problem of parallel HPO is highly relevant given the increasing availability of multi-core environments and the computational cost of training complex models. The approach of optimizing a batch acquisition function is a sensible direction, given the potential for significant speedups.

**Strengths:**

*   **Sound Technical Foundation:** The paper presents a clear and well-defined methodology, grounding the q-KG method in decision-theoretic principles. The use of IPA for gradient estimation is a practical approach to tackling the computational complexity.
*   **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, with a clear explanation of the background, methodology, and experimental setup. The technical details are sufficiently presented to allow for reproduction of the results.
*   **Relevant Problem:** Parallel HPO is a crucial area with direct applicability to numerous machine learning tasks and offers substantial potential benefits in terms of reduced optimization time.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, while not definitive, demonstrate that q-KG is competitive on noiseless problems and outperforms existing methods when dealing with noisy function evaluations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Scalability Analysis:** The paper does not address how the method scales with increasing batch size 'q'. Understanding the computational cost and potential benefits of larger batch sizes (e.g., tens or hundreds) is crucial for practical adoption.
*   **Unjustified Batch Size:** The fixed batch size of q=4 across all experiments is not justified. A sensitivity analysis or explanation of why this value was chosen is needed. Different problems may benefit from different batch sizes.
*   **Limited Baselines & Comparison:** While comparisons are made to several existing methods, there's a significant omission: comparison to Wang et al. (2015) / parallel EI (q-EI). This is a critical oversight as it prevents a direct assessment of the method’s novelty and improvement. Moreover, a comparison to simple random search or sequential BO with a comparable computational budget (wall-clock time) is missing, hindering the evaluation of practical speedups.
*   **Missing Wall-Clock Time:** The absence of wall-clock time measurements makes it difficult to assess the practical efficiency of the proposed method.
*   **Experimental Clarity:** The x-axis labeled "iterations" in the plots is unclear. Does it represent batches or function evaluations? Does it include the initial design points? The differing starting values across methods need explanation.

**Addressing the Reviewer Questions:**

1.  **Comparison to Wang et al. (2015) / parallel EI (q-EI):** This is a critical point. The authors *must* address this in a revised version. It’s important to clearly articulate the differences between q-KG and existing IPA-based parallel selection methods, specifically focusing on how the knowledge gradient approach differs from maximizing expected improvement.
2.  **Scaling to Larger q:** The paper needs to discuss the computational complexity of the IPA-based gradient estimation as 'q' increases. What are the expected benefits of larger 'q' in terms of parallelization and improved exploration?
3.  **Asynchronous Parallel Evaluations:** Extending the synchronous method to asynchronous parallel evaluations would be a valuable addition, addressing potential issues with straggler tasks and improving overall efficiency. The authors should comment on the feasibility and potential challenges of this extension.
4.  **Wall-Clock Performance:** Providing wall-clock time measurements compared to random search and sequential BO is essential for demonstrating practical speedups. The overhead of computing each new batch using q-KG also needs to be quantified.
5.  **Clarification of "Iterations" Axis:** The plots need clear labeling, specifying whether "iterations" refer to batches or function evaluations, and whether the initial design points are included. The differing starting values across methods require explanation.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Equation (4.1) appears to be missing z^(1:n) in the minimum expression.
*   “achieve” should be “achieves” in the text.
*   Citation phrasing should be reviewed for consistency.
*   Wang et al. (2015) should not be credited with introducing IPA – IPA is a well-established technique.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to parallel Bayesian optimization. However, the lack of comparisons to key baselines, the absence of scalability analysis, and the missing wall-clock time measurements weaken the conclusions. The clarity issues with the experimental results further contribute to this concern.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of the knowledge gradient to the parallel BO setting.
*   Sound theoretical foundation and efficient implementation using IPA.
*   Demonstrated performance gains in noisy evaluation scenarios.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of comparison to strong baselines (e.g., Wang et al. 2015).
*   Insufficient analysis of scalability with respect to batch size.
*   Missing wall-clock time measurements and practical speedup analysis.
*   Unjustified choice of batch size and unclear experimental details.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with Request for Major Revision.** The paper has potential, but requires significant revisions to address the outlined weaknesses before it can be considered for acceptance. Specifically, a thorough comparison to relevant baselines, a scalability analysis, and the inclusion of wall-clock time measurements are crucial. Addressing the minor comments will also improve the clarity and quality of the paper.
