

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG), which extends the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors develop a computationally efficient strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize the q-KG acquisition function for selecting batches of q points to evaluate simultaneously. The method is theoretically grounded as being Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor when only one batch remains. The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression and CNNs, showing consistent improvements over existing batch BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper's strengths are significant. The technical exposition is clear and well-structured, with thorough background on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic benchmarks (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical applications (MNIST, CIFAR10). The results demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with particularly notable improvements in noisy environments. The authors provide a well-maintained open-source implementation, enhancing reproducibility.

However, the paper has several important weaknesses that affect its quality and novelty claims. Most critically, the authors fail to adequately distinguish their work from Wang et al. (2015), which already introduced a parallel EI method using IPA. The paper claims novelty in using IPA for batch selection, but this technique was previously applied to parallel EI (Wang et al. 2015), and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how q-KG differs from these existing approaches. The claim of "novel batch BO method" is overstated given this prior work.

The experimental section lacks crucial comparisons that would establish practical significance. The authors don't provide wall-clock time measurements to demonstrate actual speedups in real-world settings, nor do they compare against random search or sequential BO to show the value of parallelization. Without these, it's unclear whether the performance gains justify the computational overhead of batch selection. The paper also fixes batch size q=4 across all experiments without justification, making it difficult to assess how the method scales to larger parallelism levels (e.g., q=10 or 100), which is critical for real-world applications.

The paper contains several technical ambiguities. The x-axis in the figures is labeled "iterations" without clarification whether this represents batches or total function evaluations, and the starting points differ across methods, making direct comparison challenging. The paper also fails to address whether the synchronous method can be extended to asynchronous parallel evaluations, which is a significant limitation for practical implementation.

Regarding originality, while the theoretical extension of KG to the parallel setting is valid, the computational approach using IPA is not novel as it was previously applied to parallel EI (Wang et al. 2015). The paper's contribution appears to be primarily a specific application of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new method. The authors should explicitly acknowledge and contrast their work with Wang et al. (2015) and clarify the distinct advantages of q-KG over parallel EI.

The significance of the work is clear in theory, as parallel BO is important for hyperparameter tuning in large-scale machine learning. However, the experimental evidence doesn't fully support the claimed practical significance due to the missing wall-clock time comparisons and lack of scaling analysis. The paper demonstrates the method works well in the scenarios tested but doesn't prove it's substantially better than alternatives in real-world deployment contexts.

The paper is well-written and technically sound in its core methodology, but the lack of clear novelty justification and missing practical validation metrics are significant drawbacks. The authors should address these gaps to strengthen their contribution. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to Wang et al. (2015), explicit discussion of scaling behavior with q, and wall-clock time measurements to demonstrate practical utility.

In summary, this paper makes a technically sound contribution to batch Bayesian optimization with a well-executed experimental evaluation. However, the lack of clear novelty differentiation from prior work, absence of practical speedup evidence, and missing scaling analysis prevent it from making a strong case for acceptance. The method shows promise, but the current evidence doesn't sufficiently demonstrate its superiority over existing approaches in practical terms. I recommend rejection due to insufficient novelty and missing validation of practical impact.
