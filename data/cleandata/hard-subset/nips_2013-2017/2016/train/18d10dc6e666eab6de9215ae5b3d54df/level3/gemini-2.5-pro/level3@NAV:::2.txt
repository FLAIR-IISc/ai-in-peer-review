This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel algorithm for batch Bayesian optimization (BO). The central idea is to extend the decision-theoretic foundation of the Knowledge Gradient method to the parallel setting, allowing for the selection of a one-step Bayes-optimal batch of points to evaluate. This approach provides a more principled alternative to common heuristics for parallelizing BO, such as the greedy construction of batches used in some parallel Expected Improvement or UCB variants. The authors also propose an efficient gradient-based method for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function.

The strengths of this paper are significant. The proposed method is an elegant and theoretically sound solution to the challenging problem of parallelizing BO. The formulation of a Bayes-optimal batch is a valuable contribution to the field, moving beyond ad-hoc or greedy selection strategies. The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, clearly explaining the method's derivation and its relation to prior work. The quality of the theoretical analysis is high, providing a solid foundation for the algorithm.

Despite these strengths, the empirical evaluation is a major weakness that undermines the convincingness of the results. First, the assumption of independent, normally distributed observation noise can be unrealistic for hyperparameter optimization (HPO), where structural biases or correlated errors from techniques like cross-validation are common. Second, the experimental design is limited. Using test set error directly as the optimization objective is problematic, as threshold-based metrics are often noisy and less informative than alternatives like AUC or log loss. More importantly, the experimental benchmarks are narrow, relying only on MNIST and CIFAR-10 with two model types (logistic regression, CNN). The absence of standard, diverse HPO benchmarks (e.g., HPOlib) makes it difficult to assess the method's generalizability, as optimizer performance is known to be task-dependent.

In summary, the paper presents an original and important theoretical contribution with a high-quality presentation. However, the limited scope and design of the experimental validation prevent a full demonstration of the method's practical significance. While the theoretical novelty is a strong argument for acceptance, the work would be substantially improved by a more rigorous and comprehensive empirical study on a wider variety of benchmarks and learning tasks.
