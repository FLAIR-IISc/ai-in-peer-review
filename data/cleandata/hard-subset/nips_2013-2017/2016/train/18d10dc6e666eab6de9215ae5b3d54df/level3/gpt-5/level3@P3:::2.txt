Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces q-KG, a batch/parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) method that selects Bayes-optimal batches in the one-step lookahead sense. It generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) from the sequential setting to parallel evaluations by defining the acquisition as the expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean after adding a batch of q evaluations. The authors show that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under noiseless observations when the final recommendation must be among sampled points. They address the computational challenge of optimizing q-KG by (i) using Monte Carlo to estimate the acquisition and (ii) applying infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to obtain unbiased stochastic gradients, enabling gradient-based optimization. The method discretizes the inner “decision domain” when computing q-KG using samples near posterior optima.

Relative to prior work on batch BO—including parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE, and parallel entropy search—q-KG is an elegant, decision-theoretically grounded approach that directly optimizes the expected value of information of a batch. The connection to sequential KG is clear, and the computational contribution (stochastic gradients for a collective batch objective) is novel and practically relevant.

Strengths
- Conceptual elegance and rigor: the acquisition is Bayes-optimal for the last batch. The reduction to parallel EI clarifies its placement among existing methods.
- Strong theory and careful derivations, including unbiased gradient estimation via IPA under reasonable smoothness conditions.
- Clear, well-organized writing; the algorithmic pipeline and computational steps are easy to follow.
- Empirical results show consistent gains, with particularly strong improvements in noisy settings.

Weaknesses
- Noise model: assumes independent, homoscedastic Gaussian noise. In hyperparameter optimization (HPO), observational noise is often heteroscedastic and correlated (e.g., due to cross-validation folds, data reuse, or early stopping), making the independence assumption unrealistic.
- Experimental design: uses test-set error as the optimization criterion. This conflates model selection and evaluation and risks optimistic bias. Metrics based on thresholds (0–1 error) are also suboptimal; probabilistic metrics (AUC, log loss) are preferable, especially with class imbalance or calibration concerns.
- Benchmarks are narrow: only MNIST and CIFAR-10, and only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and a CNN). BO performance can vary significantly across tasks; broader benchmarks (e.g., HPOlib suites) and more algorithmic diversity (SVMs, random forests/GBMs, Bayesian models, kernel methods) are needed for convincing empirical claims.
- Limited study of practical deployment aspects: synchronous-only batches; no analysis of asynchronous variants, wall-clock cost, or cost-aware objectives.

Quality
- Technical soundness: High. The derivation of q-KG, the relationship to EI, and the unbiased gradient estimator are correct and well motivated. The discretization approach is standard and justified.
- Empirical support: Mixed. Synthetic tests are helpful and show clear advantages, and the noisy setting analysis is a plus. However, HPO experiments are limited in scope, use problematic objectives (test error), and include few runs (e.g., only 5 seeds for CNN), which undermines statistical confidence. Sensitivity to discretization size M, batch size q, dimension, and GP hyperparameter learning is not explored.
- Assumptions: The independent Gaussian noise assumption and constant noise variance are at odds with common HPO settings (heteroscedasticity, correlated replicates). This should at least be acknowledged and discussed as a limitation, with guidance on extensions.

Clarity
- The paper is very well written. The exposition of the acquisition, computation, and algorithm is clean. Suggestions: explicitly discuss the computational complexity in q, |A_n|, and MC sample count; add a short discussion on the stability of IPA gradients given the min operator and practical variance-control strategies.

Originality
- Extending KG to Bayes-optimal batch selection with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator is novel and impactful. While parallel EI and entropy-search variants exist, optimizing a truly collective, decision-theoretic batch objective with gradients is a meaningful advance.

Significance
- Potential for impact is high: a principled, scalable acquisition for batch BO is valuable in practice. However, the paper’s significance would be more convincingly demonstrated with stronger, broader HPO experiments and proper evaluation protocols.

Requested clarifications and suggestions
1) Noise modeling: How would q-KG extend to heteroscedastic and correlated noise (e.g., multi-output GP over CV folds or a latent noise GP)? Can the IPA/gradient machinery carry over?
2) Objective choice in HPO: Please redo HPO experiments optimizing a validation metric (not test) and report final test performance once, using probabilistic metrics (AUC/log loss) and macro/micro averaging for multi-class.
3) Benchmarks: Add HPOlib-style tasks with diverse learners (SVM, RF/GBM, GP classifiers, matrix factorization) and more datasets; include more seeds for robustness.
4) Sensitivity and ablations: Report sensitivity to discretization size M, number of fantasies/MC samples, kernel choices, and batch size q; include runtime/wall-clock comparisons; analyze performance in higher-dimensional spaces.
5) Practicality: Discuss/experiment with asynchronous q-KG; consider cost-aware variants (e.g., using time-weighted KG).
6) Data leakage: Avoid repeatedly peeking at the test set during optimization.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theory and a principled acquisition; elegant connection to EI.
- Clear computational contribution (IPA-based gradients) that makes the method practical.
- Consistent improvements on synthetics and noisy settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical validation on HPO is too narrow and uses problematic objectives (test error).
- Unrealistic noise assumptions for realistic HPO; no discussion of extensions.
- Limited analysis of scalability, sensitivity, and asynchronous scenarios.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical and algorithmic contributions are solid and well presented, and the approach is promising. Strengthening the HPO experiments (protocols, breadth, metrics) and discussing/handling more realistic noise would substantially increase the paper’s impact.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
