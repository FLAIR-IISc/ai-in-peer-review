This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method. The core idea is to generalize the decision-theoretic Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, which is one-step Bayes-optimal for sequential selection, to the parallel setting where a batch of `q` points can be evaluated simultaneously. As the direct optimization of the q-KG acquisition function is computationally intractable, the authors propose an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy. This involves estimating the acquisition function and its gradient using Monte Carlo simulation combined with an Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) estimator, a standard technique in stochastic optimization. This work fits into a growing body of literature at NIPS and elsewhere focused on parallelizing Bayesian optimization, which has previously explored extensions of Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) policies.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its practical significance and strong empirical performance. It addresses the increasingly important problem of parallel black-box optimization, crucial for applications like large-scale hyperparameter tuning. The proposed method is well-motivated, extending the principled, one-step optimal KG policy in a natural way. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing q-KG against several state-of-the-art parallel Bayesian optimization methods on a variety of synthetic and real-world machine learning problems. The results are compelling, demonstrating that q-KG is highly competitive and often outperforms baselines, with a particularly notable advantage in noisy settings. The paper is also very well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
The main concern is the limited novelty of the contribution. The work can be viewed as a straightforward combination of two established ideas: extending the KG policy to a batch setting and applying a standard IPA-based gradient estimator to optimize the resulting stochastic objective. While the combination is effective and the engineering valuable, the conceptual leap is modest. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly articulated the unique technical challenges or insights that arose from this specific combination, beyond the direct application of known techniques.

Additionally, there is a minor point of unclarity in the technical presentation. In equation (3.1), the notations µ(x(1:n)) and K(x,x(1:n)) are used, where x(1:n) is defined as a sequence/set of points. However, the prior mean µ and kernel K are typically defined for single vector inputs. This should be clarified to explicitly denote the vector of mean evaluations and the matrix of kernel cross-covariances for improved precision.
