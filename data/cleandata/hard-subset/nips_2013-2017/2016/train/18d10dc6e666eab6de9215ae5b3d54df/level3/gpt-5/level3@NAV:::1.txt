This paper introduces the batch-sequential Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) acquisition for Bayesian optimization and an efficient IPA-based gradient estimator to optimize it. q-KG is defined analogously to the classical (sequential) KG: it maximizes the expected one-step reduction in the GP posterior predictive minimum after a batch of size q. The paper shows that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under noise-free evaluations when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points. Empirically, across synthetic benchmarks and ML hyperparameter tuning (including noisy settings), q-KG typically outperforms parallel EI (including heuristic variants), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE. This work is clearly within NIPS scope; it relates to prior batch BO work on q-EI and PES, and to sequential KG.

Quality: The decision-theoretic formulation is sound, and the Monte Carlo/IPA machinery for differentiable q-KG is well motivated. The experiments are broad and suggest strong performance, especially with noise. However, an important missing analysis is the wall-clock speed-up from transitioning sequential to batch-sequential (vs. iteration-based regret only).

Clarity: Overall clear and well organized, with helpful algorithmic detail. A few points require clarification (see below), and the literature review contains imprecise claims and omissions.

Originality: Extending KG to the batch setting with a practical, differentiable estimator is novel and significant relative to prior q-EI (closed-form/MC) and parallel ES. The connection to KG’s Bayes-optimality is a conceptual strength.

Significance: High. Efficient parallel BO is impactful for large-scale ML and scientific design. The reported gains in noisy settings are especially compelling.

Pros:
- Clear decision-theoretic foundation; principled generalization of KG to batches.
- Efficient gradient-based optimization via IPA; practical implementation.
- Strong empirical results versus state of the art, particularly under noise.
- Potentially high societal/engineering impact from parallelizing BO.

Cons and requests for clarification:
- Please report actual speed-ups (wall-clock or sample throughput) from batch-sequential vs. sequential policies.
- Literature: integrating EI over the posterior and Constant Liar were established (e.g., “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization”); omissions include CL-mix (Chevalier et al.) and natural-gradient maximization of q-EI (“Differentiating the multipoint EI…”). Cite and position accordingly.
- Assumptions: Is A compact and is f continuous? These ensure existence of minima in (4.1)/(4.2).
- Why restrict initial design to LHS? Would other space-filling designs suffice; how sensitive are results?
- Algorithm 1: clarify hyperparameter updates (frequency, warm starts, priors); is pure MLE used every batch?
- Sec. 5.2: clarify whether g uses min or max; the sign convention is easy to confuse.
- Smoothness of g and differentiability through the Cholesky factor: please justify conditions (SPD covariance, non-degenerate K) and discuss corner cases (duplicate points) and measure-zero non-differentiabilities.
- Typo: Matérn parameter is 5/2, not 2/5.
- Reproducibility: can the Figure 1 GPs be refit with an independent toolbox for verification?

Overall: I strongly recommend acceptance.
