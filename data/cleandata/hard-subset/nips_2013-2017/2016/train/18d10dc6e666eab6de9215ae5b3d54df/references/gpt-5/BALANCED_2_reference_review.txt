Summary
This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization method that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to selecting q points jointly. The acquisition function is defined as the expected reduction in the posterior GP predictor’s minimum after observing a batch of q noisy or noiseless evaluations. The authors show q-KG reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless setting when the final recommendation is restricted to previously sampled points. Because exact evaluation/optimization of q-KG is intractable, they (i) estimate q-KG via Monte Carlo using a reparameterization of the posterior via a standard normal Zq, and (ii) derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based batch selection. For continuous domains, they approximate the inner minimization via a dynamically refreshed discretization built from samples drawn near posterior optima, augmented with previously sampled and candidate points. Experiments on standard synthetic test functions and on tuning logistic regression (MNIST) and CNNs (CIFAR-10) show that q-KG is competitive or better than parallel EI (two implementations), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with particularly strong gains in noisy settings. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
- Extends the KG framework (Frazier et al.; Scott et al.) from sequential to batch selection, maintaining KG’s decision-theoretic one-step Bayes optimality (with respect to the GP predictor) for a single remaining batch.
- Contrasts with batch EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Wang et al.) and UCB variants (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), which either greedily construct batches or optimize a collective EI objective. Also relates to batch ES/PES (Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani), which target information gain about the argmin.
- Main technical novelty is an efficient gradient estimator for the batch KG objective using IPA under differentiability assumptions, plus a practical discretization/optimization strategy.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean objective: direct expected improvement in final decision quality under noise, without restricting the final recommendation to sampled points.
- Sound derivation via GP posterior algebra and reparameterization, leading to a tractable MC estimator for both value and gradient.
- IPA-based gradient is well-motivated and enables efficient joint optimization over the batch (as opposed to greedy selection).
- Empirical results are strong, especially under observation noise, aligning with KG’s theoretical appeal in noisy settings.
- Implementation details (integration with MOE; C++ code) and open-source release improve reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality
- Computational complexity and scalability are insufficiently quantified. The method involves (i) MC over Zq, (ii) Cholesky of a q×q matrix per evaluation, and (iii) inner minimization over a discretized set An; but wall-clock and asymptotic costs as functions of q, d, M, and number of MC and gradient steps are not reported. Experiments use q=4; it is unclear how performance and runtime scale for larger q or higher d.
- The discretization strategy (constructing An via “samples from the global optima of the posterior”) is underspecified. How exactly are these samples drawn (e.g., via random restarts of posterior-mean minimization, Thompson sampling, or ES-style sampling)? Sensitivity to M, the sampling procedure, and whether adding z(1:q) into An biases the acquisition are not analyzed.
- IPA unbiasedness relies on differentiability and uniqueness of the argmin in the discretized set, which can fail on ties. The paper mentions conditions in the supplement; a brief main-text discussion of tie-breaking, almost sure differentiability, and the derivative through the Cholesky factor would improve confidence.
- GP hyperparameters (including noise variance) are re-estimated by MLE after each batch, which can be brittle in noisy settings; no comparison to marginalization over hyperparameters (e.g., MCMC as in Snoek et al.) is provided, and uncertainty in hyperparameters is not propagated.
- Limited ablations: no study of the effect of MC sample size, gradient step schedules, multi-starts, M, or the discretization refresh policy. No comparison of the final recommendation as argmin over A vs over sampled set.
- Baselines: While parallel EI and UCB baselines are included, results vs the closed-form q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger) with modern joint optimizers and vs batch PES are not shown in the noisy setting, where q-KG is claimed to shine.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but several notational and editorial issues: two different uses of A (feasible domain vs discretized set in the inner minimization) are easy to confuse; suggest renaming the discretization to Ãn or S(n). Minor typos (e.g., “Main Stange”).
- Algorithm 1 lacks concrete MC and optimization settings (number of Zq samples per gradient step, learning rate/optimizer, stopping criteria).
- Please report the complexity per acquisition optimization and per BO iteration; include wall-clock times alongside regret.

Originality
- The extension of KG to batch with an efficient unbiased gradient estimator is novel and technically interesting. The discretization-with-refresh approach is a reasonable practical device, though similar ideas exist in ES/PES.

Significance
- Batch BO is widely used; a robust, noise-aware batch acquisition that outperforms q-EI and UCB variants would be impactful. The improvements in noisy scenarios are particularly valuable. Broader adoption will depend on demonstrated scalability beyond q=4 and clearer computational characterization.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled acquisition function with solid motivation; bridges a gap in batch BO under noise.
- New gradient estimator and practical optimization scheme.
- Strong empirical performance, especially with noise.
- Open-source implementation.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scalability analysis; only q=4 results and no wall-clock comparisons.
- Discretization strategy and sensitivity insufficiently justified.
- Sparse ablations and missing baselines in some regimes.
- Dependence on MLE hyperparameter fitting without uncertainty quantification.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide detailed computational complexity and wall-clock benchmarking, including scaling with q, d, and M.
- Clarify and justify the construction of An; study sensitivity to M and the sampling method; include an ablation where An excludes z(1:q) to test bias.
- Expand experiments to larger q and higher-dimensional problems; include wall-clock overhead for acquisition optimization.
- Add comparisons to strong q-EI implementations (closed-form with joint optimization) and batch PES in the noisy setting.
- Report MC sample sizes, gradient optimizer settings, multi-start counts, and stopping criteria. Include a study of the variance/unbiasedness of the IPA estimator.
- Discuss and, if possible, test marginalizing GP hyperparameters.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a principled and practically effective batch KG method with a novel gradient estimator and compelling results under noise. Addressing the computational/scalability and experimental-detail gaps would strengthen the contribution and its adoption.
