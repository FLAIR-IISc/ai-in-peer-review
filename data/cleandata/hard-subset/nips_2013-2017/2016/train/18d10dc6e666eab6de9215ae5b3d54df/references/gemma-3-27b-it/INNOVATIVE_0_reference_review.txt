This paper presents a novel approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization (BO) called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea – extending the single-iteration optimal Knowledge Gradient policy to a batch setting, and deriving an acquisition function based on expected incremental value – is compelling and represents a significant departure from existing parallel BO methods which often rely on greedy iterative construction of batches. The derivation, leveraging a decision-theoretic analysis, appears sound, and the proposed infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for efficient gradient computation is a crucial contribution enabling practical application.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The q-KG approach is conceptually elegant and offers a principled way to balance exploration and exploitation in a parallel setting. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over several established parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios, which is a realistic setting for many applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning). The availability of code is a major plus, facilitating reproducibility and further research. The authors clearly position their work within the existing literature, identifying the limitations of previous approaches and highlighting the novelty of their contribution.

However, the paper isn't without its weaknesses. While the IPA technique is ingenious, the description remains somewhat terse. A more detailed explanation of the practical implementation challenges and potential limitations of the gradient estimation would be beneficial. While the results are strong, the experimental setup could be expanded. It would be valuable to include comparisons on a wider range of problems, potentially including high-dimensional spaces and more complex objective functions. The improvement over existing methods, while consistent, isn’t always dramatic, raising the question of the practical significance of the gains in some scenarios. Additionally, the discussion around the discretization of the domain *A* feels a little brief – how sensitive are the results to the number of samples (M) used in the discretization?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The q-KG approach is a genuinely novel contribution to parallel BO, moving beyond iterative greedy methods to a more holistic, decision-theoretic framework.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the acquisition function and the IPA technique appear theoretically well-founded.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superiority of q-KG over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings.
* **Practical Impact:** The method has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of expensive optimization tasks in various fields.
* **Code Availability:** Facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Depth in IPA Explanation:** The description of the IPA implementation could be more detailed.
* **Modest Gains in Some Cases:** The performance improvement isn't always overwhelming.
* **Experiment Scope:** While the experiments are solid, expanding the benchmark suite would further strengthen the paper.
* **Discretization Sensitivity:** The impact of the discretization parameter 'M' isn't thoroughly explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor shortcomings, this is a strong paper that introduces a significant new idea to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The novelty of the approach, combined with the sound theoretical foundation and promising empirical results, outweighs the minor concerns. I believe this work has the potential to influence future research in parallel BO and its applications. I recommend **acceptance**, with a request for the authors to consider expanding the experimental validation and providing a more detailed explanation of the IPA implementation in a future version. The authors have clearly prioritized a bold, innovative direction, which aligns with the spirit of NIPS.
