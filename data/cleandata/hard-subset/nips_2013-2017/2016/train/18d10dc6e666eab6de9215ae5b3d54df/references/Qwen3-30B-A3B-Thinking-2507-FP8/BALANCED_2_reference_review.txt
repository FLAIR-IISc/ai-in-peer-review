

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that jointly optimizes a batch of q points rather than sequentially adding points. They develop an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize the q-KG acquisition function, which would otherwise be computationally prohibitive. The method is evaluated on both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR10), with results showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

This work builds on the knowledge gradient method (Frazier et al., 2009) and extends it to the parallel setting, addressing a gap in the literature where most previous work on parallel BO (e.g., parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) uses iterative greedy approaches rather than jointly optimizing the batch.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation for the q-KG acquisition function. The derivation of the acquisition function is well-motivated, and the authors provide a detailed explanation of the computational approach using IPA. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with both noise-free and noisy settings across multiple synthetic functions and real-world ML tuning tasks. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations in the experimental setup (e.g., using a constant noise variance in the noisy setting).

However, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of the regret bounds for the q-KG method, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. The comparison to the recent NIPS 2016 paper "Parallel Bayesian Global Optimization of Expensive Functions" (Wang et al.) is insufficiently detailed, making it difficult to fully assess the novelty of the proposed approach relative to the state of the art.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with Bayesian optimization, and the mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The algorithm description is clear, and the experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The figures effectively present the comparative results across different problem settings.

## Originality
The core contribution of generalizing knowledge gradient to the parallel setting is meaningful and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The authors' use of IPA to efficiently optimize the q-KG acquisition function represents a valuable computational innovation. However, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from recent parallel BO work, particularly the 2016 NIPS paper by Wang et al. which also proposes a batch BO method with a different acquisition function. The related work section mentions this paper but doesn't clearly explain how the current approach differs in both theory and practice.

## Significance
The method provides practical improvements in batch Bayesian optimization, which is highly relevant for real-world applications where parallel evaluations are possible (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines). The consistent performance gains over baselines, especially in noisy settings, demonstrate the method's practical value. The application to real ML tasks (logistic regression, CNN tuning) shows the method's relevance to current machine learning practice.

## Strengths
1. The decision-theoretic foundation for the acquisition function is well-motivated and theoretically sound.
2. The computational approach using IPA is clever and addresses a key challenge in batch BO.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic and real-world problems.
4. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized.

## Weaknesses
1. Insufficient differentiation from recent NIPS work (Wang et al., 2016) on parallel BO.
2. Lack of theoretical analysis of regret bounds for the q-KG method.
3. The paper doesn't provide a detailed computational complexity analysis of the proposed method compared to alternatives.
4. The improvement over existing methods, while consistent, is somewhat marginal in some settings (e.g., Branin2 in noise-free setting).

## Recommendation
The paper presents a meaningful extension to Bayesian optimization with practical value, but the novelty is somewhat limited compared to recent NIPS work. While the method is well-motivated and the experiments are thorough, the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with Wang et al. (2016) and a clearer explanation of the theoretical advantages of the proposed approach. The lack of theoretical analysis of regret bounds is a significant omission for a NIPS paper.

**Decision: Weak Reject**

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Conduct a more detailed comparison with Wang et al. (2016), specifically explaining how the q-KG acquisition function differs theoretically and practically from their approach.
2. Add a theoretical analysis of regret bounds for the q-KG method to strengthen the theoretical contribution.
3. Include a computational complexity analysis comparing q-KG to alternative batch BO methods.
4. In the experiments, provide a more detailed analysis of the number of function evaluations saved compared to baselines, rather than just reporting test error.
5. Clarify the relationship between the proposed method and the "nested batch knowledge gradient" mentioned in the related work (Wang et al., 2016), and explicitly state how the current approach differs.

The paper has strong technical content and practical relevance, but would benefit from addressing these issues to better establish its novelty and theoretical contribution for a NIPS audience.
