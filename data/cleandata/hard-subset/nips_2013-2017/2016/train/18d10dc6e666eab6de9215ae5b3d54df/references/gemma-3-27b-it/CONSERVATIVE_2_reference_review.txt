This paper proposes a new batch Bayesian optimization (BO) method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), aiming to improve upon existing parallel BO algorithms. The core idea is to maximize the expected incremental value of a batch of evaluations, extending the single-point Knowledge Gradient to the parallel setting. The authors also present a gradient-based optimization approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to address the computational complexity of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function.

While the motivation for parallel BO is well-established and the extension of the Knowledge Gradient concept is natural, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant and robust advance over existing methods. My primary concern lies with the level of experimental validation. While the experiments on synthetic functions and machine learning hyperparameter tuning show improvements in *some* cases, the results are not consistently superior, and the advantages are not always substantial. The authors frequently state q-KG “performs better,” but the provided figures often show marginal improvements or statistical ties with existing algorithms.

The claim of significant improvement "especially in problems with noisy evaluations" is supported, but the choice of noise level (σ = 0.5) feels arbitrary. Further investigation into the sensitivity of q-KG to noise levels and a more thorough comparison with methods specifically designed for noisy BO would be necessary. The MNIST experiment, while promising, involves a relatively simple hyperparameter tuning problem. The CIFAR10 experiment is more compelling, but the improvements, though reported as substantial, require more detailed statistical analysis. Simply stating the test error improvements isn't sufficient to justify the increased complexity of q-KG.

The computational efficiency achieved through IPA is a positive contribution, but the description relies heavily on referring to previous work ([25] for IPA itself) and the supplementary materials for detailed derivations. This lack of self-contained explanation hinders a complete understanding of the method. The discretization strategy described in Section 5.3, while reasonable, seems ad-hoc. A more principled discussion of its impact on performance, and a comparison with alternative discretization approaches, would strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, while the paper thoroughly reviews related work, it lacks a critical discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. For example, the assumption of Gaussian process priors is not always realistic, and the computational cost of q-KG, even with IPA, may be prohibitive for very high-dimensional problems or large batch sizes. The authors briefly acknowledge the need for further research in the conclusions, but a more detailed discussion of open challenges would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The extension of the Knowledge Gradient to the parallel setting is a logical and potentially valuable contribution.
*   The IPA-based gradient estimation is a clever technique for improving computational efficiency.
*   The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly conclusive, suggest that q-KG can outperform existing methods in certain scenarios.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental results lack consistent superiority over established benchmarks. The reported improvements are often marginal and require further statistical validation.
*   The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach.
*   The explanation of the IPA-based gradient estimation is not fully self-contained.
*   The performance gain on practical problems, though promising, is not sufficient to justify the added complexity relative to existing methods.
*   The choice of experimental setup and parameters (e.g., noise level) seems somewhat arbitrary.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the idea is interesting and the technical work is sound, the paper does not present a compelling case for a significant advancement in the field of parallel Bayesian optimization. The experimental validation is insufficient to justify the added complexity of the q-KG method, and the lack of a detailed discussion of limitations raises concerns about its practical applicability. The paper would benefit from more extensive experimentation, a more thorough analysis of the results, and a more critical assessment of its own shortcomings. It feels like a promising proof of concept, but not a mature contribution ready for NIPS.
