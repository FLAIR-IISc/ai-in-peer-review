- Proposes q-KG, a principled extension of sequential knowledge gradient to jointly optimize batches in Bayesian optimization.  
- Defines q-KG as the expected improvement in the GP-predicted minimum after adding a batch of q points.  
- Introduces an IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator (Equation 5.3) to efficiently optimize the q-KG acquisition function.  
- Empirical results on synthetic functions and ML hyperparameter tasks show q-KG consistently outperforms parallel EI, batch UCB, and GP-UCB-PE, especially in noisy settings.  
- Claims joint batch optimization distinguishes q-KG from prior iterative batch methods.  
- Paper is technically sound with a clear derivation linking q-KG to decision-theoretic KG framework.  
- Gradient estimation approach is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and implemented practically.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple benchmarks, real ML tasks (MNIST, CIFAR10), and appropriate baselines.  
- Results are well-presented with mean and standard deviation across runs and clear figures.  
- Provides algorithmic pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and makes code available for reproducibility.  
- Does not report statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for empirical performance differences.  
- Limited analysis of computational scaling with batch size q; no runtime quantification or evidence for large q.  
- Insufficiently detailed comparison with closely related gradient-based parallel EI and other recent batch BO methods ([15, 25, 18]).  
- Fails to discuss limitations of the discretization approach for high-dimensional problems.  
- Minor clarity issues: "q-KG" acronym usage, ambiguity between feasible domain and discretized set A, and some IPA derivations lack intuitive explanation.  
- Original contribution: novel principled batch KG extension and novel use of IPA for acquisition gradient estimation.  
- Could better contrast q-KG with nested batch KG [26] and other parallel BO approaches in more depth.  
- Highly relevant to practical parallel hyperparameter tuning; demonstrates meaningful improvements in noisy scenarios.  
- Experiments focus on small batch sizes (q=4); lacks exploration of performance for larger batch sizes common in distributed settings.  
- Overall assessment: strong, well-founded contribution with solid empirical support; minor methodological and comparative gaps remain.  
- Recommendation: accept — contribution is significant, practical, and advances batch Bayesian optimization despite noted weaknesses.
