This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization. The core idea is to extend the sequential Knowledge Gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting, aiming to jointly optimize the batch selection based on an expected incremental value of information. The authors address the computational complexity of maximizing this function via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization. Experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning demonstrate q-KG's effectiveness, particularly in noisy environments.

While the motivation for parallel Bayesian Optimization is strong – leveraging parallel function evaluations is crucial for many real-world applications – and the knowledge gradient framework is theoretically appealing, I find this paper falls short of convincing me of the practical benefit of q-KG compared to established methods. My primary concern revolves around the extensive computational machinery introduced to make the method tractable. While IPA is presented as a solution to the computational burden, its complexity seems to offset potential gains, and its implementation details are not sufficiently clear for independent verification. The authors claim efficiency gains, but without a detailed computational complexity analysis comparing q-KG with simpler alternatives (e.g., parallel EI with a fixed number of iterations), this assertion remains unsubstantiated.

The paper is reasonably well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. However, the presentation of the mathematical derivations, particularly surrounding IPA, could be more concise and accessible. The notation is mostly standard, but the numerous indices and subscripts contribute to a sense of complexity. The related work section is adequate, but could benefit from a more thorough discussion of existing parallel BO methods, especially their limitations and the specific niche q-KG aims to fill.

The originality lies in the adaptation of the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting and the proposed IPA-based optimization strategy. However, the paper doesn’t fully establish a clear advantage over existing, simpler parallel acquisition functions. While the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance, the gains aren't substantial enough to justify the increased algorithmic complexity. The reliance on a discretized search space for practical implementation (Section 5.3) introduces a further approximation that isn't thoroughly discussed regarding its impact on performance and scalability. Furthermore, the experiments lack a clear ablation study to isolate the contributions of the different components of the method (e.g., the IPA-based gradient estimation).

The significance is limited. While q-KG demonstrates promising results, especially in noisy scenarios, it’s unclear whether the benefits outweigh the computational costs and implementation complexity. The problem addressed – efficient parallel BO – is undoubtedly important, but this work doesn’t present a definitive breakthrough. The fact that the performance is "competitive" rather than demonstrably superior to simpler alternatives weakens the argument for adoption.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting.
*   Reasonable performance on both synthetic and real-world problems.
*   Addresses an important problem in Bayesian optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Significant computational complexity and implementation challenges.
*   Performance gains are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity.
*   Lack of a thorough complexity analysis and ablation study.
*   Discretization of the search space introduces approximations not fully addressed.
*   The paper does not convincingly outperform well-established alternatives in a manner commensurate with its increased complexity.



Overall, I believe this paper is a reasonable contribution but doesn't meet the high bar for acceptance at NIPS. The method is interesting, but the practical benefits are not sufficiently demonstrated. I recommend rejection, with encouragement to further refine the computational efficiency and conduct a more comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating a clear advantage over existing parallel BO methods. Specifically, a detailed comparison of running time and scaling behavior with simpler methods like parallel EI is critical.
