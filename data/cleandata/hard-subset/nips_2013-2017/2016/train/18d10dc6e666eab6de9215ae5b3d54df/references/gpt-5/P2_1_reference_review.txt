Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the Knowledge Gradient (KG) to the case where q points are evaluated simultaneously. q-KG is defined as the expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean after observing a batch, i.e., minx µ(n)(x) − E[minx µ(n+q)(x)]. It is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the final posterior mean if only one batch remains. The authors show that in the noiseless case, when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI (q-EI), connecting it to a widely used acquisition.

The main technical contribution is an efficient stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), which enables joint (non-greedy) optimization over the batch. Since evaluating q-KG and its gradient requires Monte Carlo due to coupling across batch points, they discretize the inner minimization over A via a resampled set concentrating near posterior optima, unioned with previously sampled points and the proposed batch.

Empirically, q-KG is compared against two implementations of parallel EI (MOE’s exact q-EI and a Spearmint heuristic), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, on standard synthetic benchmarks (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) with q=4, and on two hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). Results show q-KG is competitive or better, with especially strong gains under observation noise, and the authors provide open-source code.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The acquisition is well-defined and grounded in decision theory (one-step Bayes optimality). The reduction to q-EI in the stated noiseless/restriction setting is correct and illuminating. The IPA-based gradient estimator is a reasonable choice; the paper states conditions (differentiability of µ and K) and points to a supplement for justification of unbiasedness. One caveat is that the min over a finite set creates nondifferentiabilities at argmin switches; the IPA argument typically requires technical conditions (e.g., uniqueness almost surely) that should be explicitly stated. Differentiation through the Cholesky factor also deserves either a reference or implementation detail (stable and efficient formulas exist).
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are standard for BO papers and indicate consistent improvements, most pronounced with noise. However, some important details are missing: number of Monte Carlo samples for q-KG and its gradient, step sizes and optimizer settings, number of restarts, sensitivity to the discretization size M and sampling scheme, and wall-clock/runtime comparisons versus baselines (q-KG is likely more computationally intensive). Without these, it is hard to assess practical scalability to larger q or higher dimensions. The largest d appears to be 8 (CNN hyperparameters), and q=4; this is moderate.
- Reproducibility: Code availability is a plus, and the GP/kernel choices are standard. Still, the paper should include enough detail (MC sample counts, stopping criteria, discretization algorithm) to reproduce results without relying solely on the repository.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized, with a helpful algorithm box. The distinction between the two uses of A (inner discretized set for evaluating the acquisition vs. the continuous domain over which z(1:q) is optimized) is important and called out, but could still be clarified further. Some minor typos (“Main Stange”) exist. A complexity discussion (per acquisition evaluation and gradient step as a function of q, M, and n) would improve clarity and help practitioners assess feasibility.

Originality
Extending KG to the batch setting with a tractable, unbiased stochastic gradient estimator is novel and nontrivial. The paper distinguishes itself from:
- q-EI methods (including gradient-based optimization) by optimizing a different, decision-theoretic objective that is Bayes-optimal in the last step and naturally handles noise.
- UCB-based batch methods that target cumulative regret (and are known to underperform for BO’s simple regret objective).
- Parallel PES (PPES) and related entropy-based methods, which the paper cites but does not compare against.
- Prior nested batch KG work on discrete domains.

Significance
Batch BO is impactful in practice. The empirical gains, particularly under noise, are valuable and align with KG’s strengths. The open-source implementation increases practical relevance. That said, significance would be strengthened by:
- Runtime/cost-to-solution comparisons, since end-to-end efficiency matters to practitioners.
- Inclusion of stronger information-theoretic baselines (e.g., PPES) and perhaps batch Thompson sampling.
- Sensitivity/ablation studies on discretization quality (M, sampling procedure) and MC budget, as these design choices likely affect performance and stability.

Strengths
- Principled, decision-theoretic acquisition with a clean connection to q-EI in a special case.
- Efficient stochastic gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Solid empirical improvements, especially with noisy observations.
- Code release and use of standard GP machinery.
- Clear relation to prior work and positioning within BO literature.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on discretization of A for evaluating the inner min; lack of sensitivity analysis.
- Missing baselines (PPES, batch Thompson sampling); comparisons are mainly to q-EI and UCB variants.
- No runtime/complexity evaluation; scalability to larger q/d is unclear.
- Theoretical analysis limited to one-step Bayes optimality; no regret or asymptotic guarantees.
- Some technical details relegated to the supplement (conditions for unbiased gradients, differentiation through Cholesky), and missing practical hyperparameters (MC samples, optimizer configs).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add wall-clock comparisons and complexity discussion.
- Include PPES and/or batch Thompson sampling baselines.
- Report MC sample sizes, optimizer details, and sensitivity to M and discretization strategy.
- State the technical conditions for IPA unbiasedness and provide a reference for differentiating Cholesky.
- Minor edits for typos and to further clarify the two “A”s.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a meaningful and novel contribution to batch BO with a well-motivated acquisition and an efficient optimization approach, and demonstrates empirical gains, particularly under noise. Addressing the missing baselines and practical/runtime aspects would further strengthen the case.

Reviewer confidence: medium-high.
