This paper introduces a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization (BO), termed the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The goal of batch BO is to select a set of `q` points to evaluate in parallel at each iteration to find the global optimum of an expensive-to-evaluate function. The proposed q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, quantifying the expected improvement in the final recommended solution (defined as the minimum of the posterior mean) after observing the outcomes of a batch evaluation. This extends the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy, which is known to be one-step Bayes-optimal, to the parallel setting.

A key challenge is that naively maximizing the q-KG acquisition function is computationally prohibitive. The central technical contribution of this work is an efficient method for maximizing q-KG using a gradient-based optimizer. The authors derive an unbiased estimator for the gradient of the q-KG factor using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), which allows the gradient to be computed via Monte Carlo sampling. To handle continuous domains, the method relies on a dynamic discretization scheme that approximates the domain with a finite set of points, including previous samples and points sampled from the posterior distribution over the location of the global optimum.

This work relates to a growing body of literature on parallel BO. It contrasts with greedy methods that build a batch sequentially (e.g., GP-BUCB, "constant liar" EI) by instead jointly optimizing the entire batch of points. It also distinguishes itself from parallel Expected Improvement (EI) by using a different optimality criterion; q-KG focuses on the value of the final posterior minimum, whereas EI is optimal when the final recommendation must be one of the evaluated points.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Quality & Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically strong. The formulation of q-KG is principled, stemming from a clear decision-theoretic objective. The main technical contribution—the application of IPA to derive an unbiased gradient estimator for the Monte Carlo approximation of q-KG—is sound and enables the practical optimization of this complex acquisition function. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering four synthetic benchmarks and two real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems. The inclusion of both noise-free and noisy settings is a significant strength, as it demonstrates the method's robustness. The results compellingly show that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms several state-of-the-art parallel BO methods.

2.  **Originality & Novelty:** The paper presents a novel acquisition function for the batch BO setting. The generalization of the KG principle to a parallel, joint-optimization framework is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. While other methods for joint batch optimization exist (e.g., for parallel EI or Entropy Search), this work introduces a new, well-motivated objective function into this space. The use of IPA for gradient estimation in this specific context is also a novel application.

3.  **Significance:** The problem of parallelizing expensive black-box optimization is of high practical importance, particularly for hyperparameter tuning of large machine learning models where parallel computation is readily available. The proposed method demonstrates significant performance gains, especially in the noisy setting. This is highly relevant, as many real-world optimization problems involve stochastic objectives (e.g., model performance evaluated on a data subset). The strong empirical results suggest that q-KG could be a widely adopted method by practitioners and a strong baseline for future research. The public release of the code further enhances the paper's potential impact and reproducibility.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity:** While the paper is generally well-written, certain aspects could be clarified.
    *   The dual use of the symbol `A`—both for the feasible domain over which candidate points `z` are optimized (in Eq. 4.2) and for the domain over which the posterior minimum is computed (in Eq. 4.1)—can be initially confusing. The authors clarify this in Section 5, but an earlier, more explicit distinction would improve readability.
    *   The derivation of the gradient in Section 5.2 is very concise, with details deferred to supplementary material. While common, including a brief intuition in the main text about why the interchange of derivative and expectation is valid under IPA and what the terms in the final gradient expression represent would be helpful for the reader.

2.  **Quality & Technical Soundness:**
    *   **Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational overhead of the proposed method. The q-KG acquisition function requires Monte Carlo estimation for both the function value and its gradient, followed by a stochastic gradient ascent procedure at each BO iteration. This is likely to be significantly more computationally expensive than non-Monte-Carlo methods like GP-BUCB or even other Monte-Carlo methods that may require fewer samples or a simpler optimization landscape. A discussion of the trade-off between wall-clock time per iteration and the number of iterations required would provide a more complete picture of the method's practicality.
    *   **Discretization Sensitivity:** The method's performance relies on the quality of the domain discretization `An` used to approximate the `min` operator. The paper proposes a specific strategy and sets `M=1000`, but it does not analyze the sensitivity of the algorithm to the size `M` or the choice of discretization strategy. An ablation study or discussion on how the approximation quality affects performance would strengthen the claims.

### **Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and well-motivated acquisition function (q-KG) for the important problem of batch Bayesian optimization.
*   Provides a sound and non-trivial technical contribution by developing an efficient gradient-based optimization scheme using IPA.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance against multiple state-of-the-art baselines across a range of synthetic and real-world problems.
*   The method shows particularly compelling advantages in the noisy setting, which is a common and practical scenario.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational cost of the method is not analyzed or compared to baselines, which is an important practical consideration.
*   The sensitivity of the method to the key discretization step is not explored.
*   Minor clarity issues could be addressed to improve the manuscript's accessibility.

Overall, the paper presents a high-quality, novel, and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The strengths, particularly the principled derivation and strong empirical results, substantially outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to missing analyses (computational cost, sensitivity) that could be addressed in future work.
