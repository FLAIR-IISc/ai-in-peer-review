**Summary:**

This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO). The method is derived from a decision-theoretic framework, aiming to select a batch of `q` points that maximally improves the expected value of the final recommended solution, defined as the minimum of the posterior mean function. This contrasts with methods like Expected Improvement (EI), which focus on improving upon the best-observed value so far. A key technical contribution is a computationally efficient method for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function. Since direct optimization is intractable, the authors use Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator of the q-KG factor. This enables the use of gradient-based methods to find the optimal batch of points to evaluate. For continuous domains, the authors propose a dynamic discretization scheme. The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression, CNN), demonstrating that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms existing parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy environments.

This work builds upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) method [7, 17] and successfully generalizes its principles to the parallel setting. While other methods for parallel BO exist, such as heuristic extensions of EI [19] and UCB [3, 5], or more principled joint optimization approaches for EI [2, 25] and Entropy Search (ES) [18], this paper's contribution is a novel, well-founded acquisition function combined with a sophisticated computational technique (IPA) to make its optimization practical.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled and Novel Acquisition Function:** The derivation of q-KG is a significant strength. It is based on a one-step lookahead analysis of the expected value of the final recommendation, which is directly aligned with the goal of BO (finding the global optimum, not just minimizing cumulative regret). This provides a stronger theoretical motivation than many heuristic or greedy approaches to parallelization.

2.  **Efficient Optimization via IPA:** The primary technical contribution—the use of IPA to derive an unbiased gradient estimator for the Monte Carlo-based q-KG factor—is impressive and crucial for the method's feasibility. This allows for efficient, gradient-based maximization of a complex, non-analytic acquisition function, moving beyond finite-difference or derivative-free methods which would be prohibitively slow.

3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough, covering a range of synthetic and real-world problems. The method consistently performs at or above the level of state-of-the-art benchmarks. The performance improvement is particularly pronounced in the noisy setting, which is a very common and practical scenario in hyperparameter tuning (e.g., due to stochastic optimization or evaluation on a subset of data). The successful application to tuning an 8-parameter CNN on CIFAR-10, improving on a human-tuned baseline, is a compelling demonstration of the method's practical significance.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivating the problem and explaining the proposed method. The authors' decision to provide open-source code is highly commendable and greatly enhances the reproducibility and potential impact of their work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Overhead Analysis:** While the IPA-based gradient is efficient relative to alternatives, the overall process of optimizing the q-KG acquisition function (involving Monte Carlo sampling and stochastic gradient ascent) is likely more computationally expensive than some of the benchmarks, especially non-Monte Carlo methods like GP-BUCB. A discussion of the wall-clock time required to select a batch would be very valuable for practitioners to understand the trade-offs.
    *   *Suggestion:* Please add a discussion or a small experiment comparing the wall-clock time for acquisition function optimization between q-KG and the baselines. This would provide a more complete picture of the method's practicality.

2.  **Sensitivity to Discretization:** The method's performance for continuous domains hinges on the quality of the discretization `An`. The paper follows prior work but does not analyze the sensitivity of the results to the size of the discretization, `M`. A small `M` might lead to a poor approximation of the true posterior minimum, while a large `M` increases computational cost.
    *   *Suggestion:* An ablation study showing how performance varies with `M` would strengthen the paper. Even a brief discussion on the choice of `M=1000` and the potential limitations of this approximation would be beneficial.

3.  **Scalability with Batch Size `q`:** The experiments are conducted with a fixed batch size of `q=4`. The Cholesky decomposition within the q-KG computation scales as O(q³), suggesting that the computational cost per gradient step may grow rapidly with the batch size.
    *   *Suggestion:* Please discuss the scalability of the proposed method with respect to `q`. If computationally feasible, including results for a larger batch size (e.g., q=8 or q=16) would make the evaluation more comprehensive.

4.  **Missing Comparison:** The paper cites parallel Entropy Search (ES) [18] as a related method that also performs joint optimization of the batch from a principled (information-theoretic) standpoint. However, parallel ES is not included as a benchmark in the experiments. A comparison against this method would have been very informative.
    *   *Suggestion:* If possible, a comparison to parallel ES would be a strong addition. If not, it would be useful to briefly acknowledge this and explain why it was omitted (e.g., lack of a robust public implementation).

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Pro:** Principled, decision-theoretic derivation of the q-KG acquisition function.
*   **Pro:** Novel and efficient use of IPA for gradient-based optimization of the acquisition function.
*   **Pro:** Strong and extensive empirical validation, showing state-of-the-art performance, especially in noisy settings.
*   **Pro:** Clear presentation and availability of code for reproducibility.
*   **Con:** Lack of analysis on the computational overhead of optimizing the acquisition function.
*   **Con:** The impact of the discretization choice (`M`) is not analyzed.
*   **Con:** Scalability with respect to batch size `q` is not discussed or evaluated.

**Decision:**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and well-motivated method for a significant problem in the NIPS community. The combination of a principled acquisition function and a sophisticated computational technique for its optimization is a strong contribution. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach. While there are minor weaknesses regarding the analysis of computational complexity and sensitivity, they are addressable and do not detract from the core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
