

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that extends the sequential knowledge gradient acquisition function to the batch setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that optimizes the expected incremental value of a batch of q evaluations, and develop a computationally efficient gradient-based optimization approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis. The paper demonstrates promising empirical results on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, with particular strength in noisy settings.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a clear contribution to the batch Bayesian optimization literature by providing a theoretically grounded acquisition function that optimizes over the batch collectively rather than iteratively. The connection to the sequential knowledge gradient and the careful discussion of how q-KG reduces to parallel EI under specific conditions are well-articulated. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks. The paper's code availability is a plus.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Notational inconsistencies:** The paper uses inconsistent notation for the minimum operation. In Algorithm 1, it states "x* = argminx∈A µ(I+Nq)(x)", but in the q-KG definition (equation 4.1), it writes "min x∈A µ(n)(x)" without specifying this is over a discretized set. The paper later clarifies discretization in Section 5.3, but the initial definition creates confusion about whether the minimum is over the continuous domain or the discretized set.

2. **Mathematical notation errors:** In equation (5.1), the authors write "µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z(1:q))Zq" where σ̃n is a scalar but Zq is a vector. The notation implies a scalar multiplication, which is inconsistent with the vector nature of Zq. This should be a vector σ̃n(x, z(1:q)) multiplied by Zq.

3. **Missing theoretical justification:** The paper claims "q-KG will reduce to the parallel EI algorithm if function evaluations are noise-free and the final recommendation is restricted to the previous sampling decisions" but provides no formal proof or derivation. This is a significant claim that would strengthen the paper.

4. **Implementation details:** The paper states "we use a gradient-based optimizer" (Section 5.2) but fails to specify which specific optimization algorithm was used (e.g., SGD, Adam, etc.), making reproducibility difficult. The computational complexity of the IPA approach for large q is also not discussed.

5. **Statistical significance:** The paper claims "q-KG is significantly better" on several functions (Section 6.1.1), but provides no statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) for these comparisons. This is a critical omission in empirical evaluation.

6. **Reference formatting:** The reference list contains inconsistent formatting, such as "Optimization test problems. http://www.sfu.ca/~ssurjano/optimization, D. Bingham, , , " with multiple commas and missing information.

7. **Code availability:** The paper states "The code in this paper is available at https://github.com/wujian16/qKG" but does not include a working link in the manuscript (only text description). This is a minor but important detail for reproducibility.

8. **Typographical errors:** In the Acknowledgments, "AFOSR FA9550-16-10046" should be "AFOSR FA9550-16-1-0046" (with a hyphen after the 1).

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents a novel method with promising empirical results, the numerous minor but significant technical inconsistencies and omissions in the manuscript undermine its technical soundness. The notational errors, missing theoretical justifications, and lack of statistical significance testing in the empirical evaluation are particularly concerning. The paper makes important contributions to the field, but the current version contains too many minor but critical flaws to be accepted at NIPS. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for publication.

**Recommendation:** Reject
