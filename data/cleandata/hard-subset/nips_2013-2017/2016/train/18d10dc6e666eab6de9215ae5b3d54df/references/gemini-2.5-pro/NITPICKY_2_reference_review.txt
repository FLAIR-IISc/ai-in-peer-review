**Summary**

This paper proposes a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method is derived from a decision-theoretic standpoint, aiming to select a batch of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final recommended solution, where the solution is the minimum of the posterior mean. This contrasts with methods like Expected Improvement (EI), which typically optimize for the best value among observed points. To make the maximization of the q-KG acquisition function tractable, the authors propose an efficient gradient-based optimization scheme. The gradient of the q-KG factor, which is an expectation over the future observations, is estimated via Monte Carlo simulation using an Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) estimator. To handle continuous domains, the paper suggests an adaptive discretization strategy. The empirical evaluation on synthetic and hyperparameter tuning tasks shows that q-KG is competitive with, and often outperforms, other state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, particularly in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled Derivation:** The q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated from a decision-theoretic perspective, extending the sequential knowledge gradient framework to the batch setting in a natural way. It directly optimizes for the expected quality of the final recommendation over the entire domain, which is the ultimate goal of BO.
2.  **Novel Computational Approach:** The use of IPA to derive a stochastic gradient estimator for the q-KG factor is a significant contribution. This allows for the use of efficient gradient-based methods to optimize the acquisition function over the `q*d` dimensional space of candidate points, which is a major challenge in batch BO.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method demonstrates strong performance, especially on noisy problems. The experiments are conducted on a reasonable set of synthetic functions and two relevant machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems, comparing against several established baselines.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novel contributions, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor flaws, technical omissions, and presentation issues that collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Methodological Opacity and Potential Flaws:**
    *   **Discretization Dependency:** The proposed discretization scheme in Section 5.3 creates a problematic circular dependency. The discrete set `An` over which the minimum is computed is defined as `An = AMn ∪ x(1:n) ∪ z(1:q)`. This means the domain of the inner optimization problem within the q-KG factor depends on the variables `z(1:q)` being optimized. This makes the objective function `q-KG(z(1:q), An)` potentially ill-behaved, as the set of points `An` changes discontinuously as `z` changes. More importantly, the gradient derivation in Section 5.2 implicitly assumes that `A` is fixed with respect to `z(1:q)`. The authors do not address how the IPA-based gradient estimator remains valid when the domain of the `min` operator is a function of the differentiation variable. This is a subtle but potentially critical flaw in the proposed optimization procedure.
    *   **IPA Assumptions:** The paper invokes IPA to justify swapping the gradient and expectation operators in Eq. (5.3) by simply citing the supplementary material. The validity of this interchange hinges on specific regularity conditions (e.g., dominated convergence) which are not stated or justified in the main text. Furthermore, the gradient of the `g(...)` term involves the derivative of a `min` function, which is non-differentiable wherever the minimum is not unique and can "jump" as a function of `z(1:q)`. This lack of smoothness is a known challenge for IPA, and the paper completely ignores this technical nuance.
    *   **Notational Ambiguity:** The definition of q-KG in Eq. (4.1) is slightly confusing. The notation `En[...|y(z(1:q))]` is redundant, as `En` is already defined as the expectation with respect to the posterior after `n` evaluations, and the randomness being integrated out is precisely that of the future observations `y(z(1:q))`. This suggests a lack of precision in the mathematical formalism.

2.  **Experimental and Implementation Details:**
    *   **Computational Cost:** Algorithm 1 mentions re-training GP hyperparameters at each step via MLE. This is a computationally intensive operation, especially as the number of observations grows. The paper focuses on the efficiency of optimizing the acquisition function but fails to discuss the overall wall-clock time or how the cost of this refitting step compares to the acquisition optimization.
    *   **Baseline Implementations:** The baselines are drawn from three different open-source packages (MOE, Spearmint, Gpoptimization). While convenient, this makes a fair comparison difficult. These packages may have different underlying GP implementations, hyperparameter priors, or acquisition function optimizers. The authors do not state what efforts were made to ensure a controlled comparison (e.g., using the same GP kernel, hyperparameter optimization strategy, etc., across all methods).
    *   **Statistical Significance:** For the 8-dimensional CNN tuning problem, results are averaged over only 5 independent runs. Given the high variance inherent in both the initialization and the optimization process for such a problem, it is difficult to draw firm conclusions about significance from such a small sample size.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   **Formatting Errors:** The paper contains distracting formatting issues. The footer on the first page contains leftover template text ("th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.").
    *   **Reference Quality:** The reference list is notably sloppy. Several references are simply URLs with no author, year, or publication information (e.g., [1], [6], [24]). Reference [16] (Rasmussen) is incomplete, containing only the author's name. This lack of attention to detail is unbecoming of a top-tier conference submission.

**Decision**

While the core idea of a parallel knowledge gradient is valuable and the results appear promising, the paper is marred by a significant number of methodological ambiguities, a lack of technical rigor in its derivations, and numerous presentation flaws. The unaddressed circular dependency in the discretization scheme and the glossing over of the technical requirements for the IPA gradient estimator are particularly concerning. The accumulation of these issues suggests the work is not yet mature enough for publication at NIPS. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled acquisition function for an important problem (batch BO).
*   Develops an efficient gradient-based optimization method, a key challenge in this area.
*   Shows strong empirical results, especially in the practical and challenging noisy setting.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Serious, unaddressed methodological issue concerning the optimization over a variable-dependent discretization set.
*   Lack of rigor in justifying the use of IPA and the interchange of gradient and expectation.
*   Insufficient discussion of overall computational costs and potential confounding factors in the experimental setup.
*   Poor presentation quality, including a sloppy and incomplete reference list.
