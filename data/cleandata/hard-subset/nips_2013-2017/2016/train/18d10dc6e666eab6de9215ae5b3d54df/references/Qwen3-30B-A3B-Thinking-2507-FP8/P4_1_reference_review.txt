

# Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which extends the sequential knowledge gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that jointly optimizes a batch of q points rather than selecting them sequentially. They address computational challenges by developing an efficient gradient estimation method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling practical implementation. The method is evaluated on both synthetic functions and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, demonstrating consistent improvements over existing parallel Bayesian optimization approaches, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper builds upon the knowledge gradient method for sequential Bayesian optimization [7, 17] and relates to recent work on parallel Bayesian optimization [2, 3, 5, 18, 25]. It distinguishes itself from prior approaches that construct batches iteratively by optimizing a single-point acquisition function while holding other points fixed, instead choosing the batch collectively to maximize the acquisition function. The authors provide a thorough comparison with relevant prior work, including parallel EI [2, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and parallel UCB with exploration [3].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation for the q-KG acquisition function. The authors carefully address the computational challenges of evaluating and optimizing q-KG through their IPA-based approach, providing detailed derivations and implementation strategies. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing on both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical machine learning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show q-KG outperforms or matches state-of-the-art benchmarks, with particularly strong performance in noisy settings. The authors demonstrate appropriate care in evaluating both strengths and weaknesses of their method.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization is thorough yet concise, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with the field. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and its computational implementation is presented step-by-step with clear mathematical notation. The experimental setup and results are described with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors provide clear pseudocode for their algorithm (Algorithm 1) and discuss implementation details.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the knowledge gradient method to the parallel setting with a fundamentally different approach than existing methods. While previous work on parallel BO typically constructs batches iteratively, the authors propose a method that optimizes the batch collectively. This represents a meaningful advancement over prior approaches that optimize acquisition functions sequentially. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from related contributions, including a comparison with recent methods like parallel ES [18] and gradient-based parallel EI [25]. The extension of knowledge gradient to parallel settings appears to be novel in the context of NIPS proceedings.

**Significance:** The method demonstrates practical value in machine learning hyperparameter tuning, a significant application area for Bayesian optimization. The consistent performance gains over benchmarks, especially in noisy settings (where the advantage is even more pronounced), make this contribution impactful. The ability to effectively handle noisy function evaluations is particularly valuable for real-world applications where measurements are often noisy. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in Bayesian optimization with a solution that advances the state of the art.

**Strengths:** The paper's main strengths include its novel acquisition function design, the efficient computational method using IPA, comprehensive experimental evaluation, and clear theoretical foundation. The authors provide a thorough comparison with relevant prior work, demonstrating the advantages of their approach. The implementation details and code availability further enhance the paper's impact.

**Weaknesses:** The paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis of the computational complexity of the IPA-based optimization approach, particularly how it scales with batch size q and dimensionality. While the experiments show good performance, the paper could provide more insight into the trade-offs between computational cost and performance gains. The discussion of the discretization strategy (Section 5.3) could be strengthened with more detail on how the choice of M affects performance and scalability.

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization by introducing a novel parallel acquisition function with a theoretically grounded decision-theoretic foundation. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing approaches. The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. While the theoretical analysis could be expanded, the empirical results and computational approach are compelling. The work represents a significant advancement in batch Bayesian optimization, particularly for noisy function evaluations.

The paper meets all four criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel extension to existing knowledge gradient methods (Originality), and demonstrates significant practical value (Significance). The work advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization and has potential to impact both theoretical and applied machine learning research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel extension of knowledge gradient to parallel settings with collective batch optimization
- Efficient computational approach using IPA for practical implementation
- Consistent empirical improvements over benchmarks, especially in noisy settings
- Thorough experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world problems
- Clear theoretical foundation and well-written presentation

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of computational complexity and scalability
- Could strengthen comparison with very recent parallel BO methods (e.g., Wang et al. 2016)
- Limited discussion of high-dimensional performance (beyond 6D tests)

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field with significant technical merit and practical relevance. The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization and should be accepted.
