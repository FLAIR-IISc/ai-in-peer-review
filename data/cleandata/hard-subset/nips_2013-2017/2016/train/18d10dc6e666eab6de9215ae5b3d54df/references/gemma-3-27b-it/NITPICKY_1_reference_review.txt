## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, extending the sequential KG approach to the parallel setting. The core idea is to maximize the expected incremental value of a batch of function evaluations, rather than iteratively constructing a batch with single-point optimizations. The authors also present a computationally efficient method for gradient estimation using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and demonstrate empirical improvements over existing parallel BO methods on both synthetic and real-world problems, particularly in noisy settings.

While the paper addresses an important problem – efficient parallel Bayesian optimization – and presents a novel approach, my assessment is leaning towards rejection due to a multitude of seemingly minor, yet collectively substantial, issues concerning clarity, technical rigor, and presentation. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting is a logical and worthwhile contribution. The q-KG formulation is conceptually sound.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms benchmark algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluations. This is a significant result.
* **IPA for Gradient Estimation:**  Developing an efficient gradient estimation method for q-KG is crucial for its practical application, and the authors’ use of IPA is a reasonable approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Notation:** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in several areas. The notation is inconsistent. For example, 'z(1:q)' is used to denote a batch of points, but 'x(1:n)' denotes a sequence of previously evaluated points. Using consistent notation would improve readability. The explanation of the core idea behind the q-KG (Section 4) is rushed and relies heavily on referring the reader to [7] without adequately bridging the gap for those unfamiliar with the sequential KG method. The two uses of "A" (feasible domain and discretization set) are confusing and not clearly delineated throughout.
* **Technical Rigor – IPA Details:** The derivation of the IPA gradient estimator (Section 5.2) is asserted to be detailed in the supplementary materials, but even with that detail, the justification for several steps lacks sufficient explanation. Specifically, the claim that the gradient can be computed analytically based on the provided equations feels suspect and requires further justification.  The reliance on continuous differentiability of µ and K without explicitly stating and verifying this assumption for the employed kernels is problematic.
* **Discretization Strategy:** The strategy for discretizing the search space (Section 5.3) – drawing samples from the global optima of the posterior – is not well-motivated.  While mentioned in related work, the authors don't sufficiently explain *why* this is a superior strategy to, for example, a random or Latin Hypercube sampling approach. The use of M = 1000 feels arbitrary and lacks sensitivity analysis.
* **Experiment Details:** The experimental setup lacks critical details. For example, in the CNN tuning experiment, the specific network architecture is referenced, but not fully described in the paper itself. Reporting the computational cost (wall-clock time) for each algorithm would provide important context for the observed performance gains. The claim of "improving" the test error to 11% compared to a "human expert" requires a more detailed comparison, including the expert's exact configuration.
* **Related Work Gaps:** The paper cites [26] as focusing on a "nesting structure" not present in the current setting. However, the core idea of optimizing over a discrete set is relevant, and a more nuanced comparison to [26] regarding the computational trade-offs is necessary.
* **Minor Errors:** Throughout the paper, there are minor grammatical errors and inconsistencies (e.g., "th Conference" in the title).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The method shows promising empirical results, particularly in noisy settings.
* The use of IPA for gradient estimation is a valuable contribution.
* The idea of extending the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting is conceptually sound.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of clarity and rigor significantly detracts from the paper’s overall quality.
* The insufficient explanation of the IPA derivation and discretization strategy raises concerns about the validity of the approach.
* The experimental details are incomplete, making it difficult to assess the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
* The numerous minor errors detract from the professionalism of the presentation.



**Overall:**

While the q-KG method has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, the current manuscript is marred by too many technical and presentational shortcomings. The paper feels rushed and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the issues raised in this review and resubmit in the future. A significant rewrite, focusing on clarity, technical rigor, and completeness of the experimental details, is necessary before the paper can be considered for acceptance.
