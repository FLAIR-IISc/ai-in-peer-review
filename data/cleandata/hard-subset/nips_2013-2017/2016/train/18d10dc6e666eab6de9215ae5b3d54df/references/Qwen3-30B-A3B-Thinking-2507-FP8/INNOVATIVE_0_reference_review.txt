

# Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient approach to the parallel setting. The key contribution is the development of a new acquisition function that considers the batch of points collectively rather than iteratively as previous parallel methods do. The authors derive q-KG from decision-theoretic principles, showing it is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor when only one batch remains. To address the computational challenges of optimizing q-KG, they propose an efficient method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute gradients, enabling practical implementation.

The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by shifting from iterative batch construction (common in prior work like parallel EI and batch UCB) to a collective batch optimization approach. This is a bold and novel perspective that addresses a key limitation in existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments that q-KG consistently outperforms state-of-the-art benchmarks, especially in noisy settings where the advantage is particularly pronounced. Their method achieves better results on both synthetic functions (Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), with improvements of up to 3% in test error.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a solid theoretical foundation and practical implementation details. The computational approach using IPA is clever and addresses a real bottleneck in applying knowledge gradient to batch settings. The experimental design is thorough, covering both noise-free and noisy scenarios with appropriate benchmarks, and the results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of q-KG.

The main strengths of this paper are its conceptual novelty, strong empirical results, and practical implementation. The authors have made a clear distinction from previous work, particularly by emphasizing the collective optimization approach rather than iterative construction. The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian optimization with a fresh perspective that has significant potential impact on hyperparameter tuning and other applications where parallel evaluations are common.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses, they are not substantial enough to undermine the novelty and significance of the contribution. The theoretical analysis of q-KG's regret bounds is somewhat limited, and the computational complexity comparison with other methods could be more detailed. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe these are acceptable limitations given the paper's significant conceptual contribution.

The significance of this work is substantial. Batch Bayesian optimization is increasingly important in practical machine learning applications where parallel evaluations are common (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines). The q-KG method provides a principled approach that outperforms existing methods, particularly in noisy settings which are common in real-world applications. The authors have demonstrated clear practical advantages through their experiments, and the method's theoretical foundation suggests it could be widely adopted in the machine learning community.

Compared to previous NIPS work on Bayesian optimization (e.g., [7, 17, 22]), this paper makes a clear conceptual leap by extending knowledge gradient to the parallel setting. While [25] also explores parallel acquisition functions, their approach is fundamentally different in that it optimizes the batch collectively rather than iteratively. The paper also differentiates itself from the recent "nested batch knowledge gradient" work [26] by addressing continuous domains with efficient gradient computation.

The paper is well-positioned to make a significant impact at NIPS. It introduces a novel concept with strong theoretical grounding, practical implementation, and compelling empirical results. The method's ability to consistently outperform benchmarks, especially in noisy settings, demonstrates its practical value. The code availability further enhances its impact.

In conclusion, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with a bold and novel approach to batch acquisition functions. The conceptual leap from sequential to parallel knowledge gradient is significant, and the experimental results validate the theoretical promise. While the paper could benefit from more detailed theoretical analysis of computational complexity, this is a minor weakness that does not detract from the paper's overall novelty and significance.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS 2016. The novelty of the approach, the strong empirical results, and the clear practical impact make it a valuable contribution to the field. This work opens up new directions for research in batch Bayesian optimization and provides a principled method that practitioners can immediately apply to improve their hyperparameter tuning workflows.
