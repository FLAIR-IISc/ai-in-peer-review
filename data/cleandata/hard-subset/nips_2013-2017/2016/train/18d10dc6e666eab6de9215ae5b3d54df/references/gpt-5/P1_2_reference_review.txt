Summary
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. q-KG is defined as the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after observing a batch of q evaluations, i.e., the Bayes-optimal one-step decision under the terminal objective of minimizing the posterior mean’s minimum (without restricting the final recommendation to a previously evaluated point). The acquisition must be computed via Monte Carlo in the batch case; the paper develops an efficient stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based maximization over the q points. For continuous domains, the authors discretize the inner minimizations using samples drawn from an approximate posterior over minimizers (as in entropy search). Experiments on synthetic functions (up to d=6) and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR-10) with q=4 show that q-KG typically outperforms batch EI (both heuristic and exact variants) and batch UCB methods, with particularly strong gains under noisy evaluations. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Extends classic KG for discrete domains and sequential decisions (Frazier et al. 2008/2009; Scott et al. 2011) to batch selection with continuous domains via Monte Carlo and IPA.
- Contrasts with batch EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Wang et al.) and heuristic parallelization (constant liar; MOE; Spearmint), and batch UCB methods (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE). Unlike greedy construction, q-KG optimizes the batch collectively.
- Related to PES/qPES (Hernández-Lobato; Shah & Ghahramani) in using posterior samples of the minimizer for discretization, but with a different objective.
- The paper clarifies that in the noiseless case with the final recommendation restricted to the sampled set, q-KG reduces to q-EI.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic foundation: Bayes-optimal one-step policy for the targeted terminal loss.
- Methodological contribution: an unbiased IPA-based stochastic gradient for q-KG that enables practical joint optimization over batches.
- Empirical performance: consistent and often sizable improvements over strong batch BO baselines, especially under noise—an important regime in practice.
- Practical relevance: includes hyperparameter tuning tasks; code is open-sourced.
- Connection to and differentiation from prior batch BO methods is well articulated.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scalability and runtime are not quantified. q-KG involves O(q^3) linear algebra per Monte Carlo sample plus optimization over R^{qd}, and uses a discretization of size M=1000 in inner minimizations. Results are limited to q=4 and low/moderate d; no wall-clock comparisons or large-q evidence despite claims of efficiency.
- The IPA gradient relies on differentiability and uniqueness of argmins of the discretized inner problems; these conditions are subtle due to the min operator and may be violated in practice. Assumptions and robustness are deferred to the supplement; more discussion in the main paper would help.
- The discretization An depends on z(1:q) (they include z and previously sampled points), which makes the objective piecewise defined as z changes. It is unclear whether gradients account for this dependence; likely they treat An as fixed during differentiation. The bias introduced and sensitivity to M are not analyzed.
- No theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence or regret bounds) are provided for the batch policy under continuous domains and noisy observations.
- Experimental reporting lacks ablations (effect of M, number of MC samples, gradient variance control such as common random numbers), and uncertainty over GP hyperparameters is not addressed (MLE only).
- Comparisons focus on EI and UCB variants; batch ES/qPES is conceptually related but absent from experiments.

Quality
- Technically sound overall with a principled objective and a plausible stochastic gradient estimator. However, key technical assumptions (differentiability, uniqueness of argmins, treatment of discretization dependence on z) are under-explained in the main text.
- Empirical evaluation is convincing on the chosen benchmarks, particularly in the noisy case. Still, missing runtime/scalability analyses, larger q, and higher-dimensional tests limit the strength of practical claims.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable. The definition of q-KG and its relation to EI are clear. The computational sections are concise but could better detail the IPA conditions and the discretization procedure (how posterior minimizer samples are generated and updated). Small editorial nits exist but do not impede understanding.

Originality
- Extends KG to the batch setting with a novel, implementable stochastic gradient approach. While the objective is a natural generalization, the combination of IPA for q-KG and continuous-domain optimization is original and differentiates from batch EI/UCB and PES-based methods.

Significance
- Batch BO remains important; a robust method that fares well under noise is valuable. If the method scales to larger q/d and remains competitive in runtime, it is likely to be adopted in practice. The released code increases impact.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Provide wall-clock time per iteration and per acquisition optimization, and plot performance versus time as well as evaluations. Include q values beyond 4 to substantiate efficiency claims.
- Clarify in the main text the assumptions under which the IPA gradient is unbiased, and how ties in the argmin are handled. Discuss the use of common random numbers for variance reduction and whether you re-use Zq during gradient ascent.
- Explain how An is sampled from the posterior over minimizers (e.g., MAP minimizer samples via Thompson sampling/posterior draws of the function), and assess sensitivity to M and to the choice of discretization strategy. Consider fixing An during optimization over z to avoid dependency-induced nonsmoothness, and state whether you do.
- Compare against (or at least discuss) batch ES/qPES empirically, or motivate why it is omitted (e.g., computational burden).
- Report uncertainty across runs more systematically and add ablations on noise variance learning and GP hyperparameter optimization.
- Discuss potential extensions: asynchronous batch selection, constraints, high-dimensional embeddings.

Pros
- Principled, Bayes-optimal one-step objective for batches.
- Solid empirical gains, especially under noise.
- Practical stochastic gradient method; code available.
- Clear relation to and improvement over common baselines.

Cons
- Missing runtime/scalability evidence; only q=4 shown.
- Theoretical conditions and treatment of discretization dependence not fully transparent in the main text.
- Limited comparisons (no qPES); few high-d/large-q benchmarks.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The contribution is principled and useful, with promising empirical results, but would benefit from stronger analysis of computational efficiency and more thorough experimental characterization.

Confidence: High (familiar with BO, KG, and batch acquisitions).
