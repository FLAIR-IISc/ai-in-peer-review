

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a new batch Bayesian optimization method based on the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function, called parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The authors generalize the sequential KG method to the parallel setting, deriving a decision-theoretic acquisition function that maximizes information gain from evaluating q points simultaneously. They develop an efficient gradient-based optimization method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to handle the computational complexity of evaluating q-KG. The paper demonstrates empirical superiority over existing parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a clear contribution to the batch Bayesian optimization literature by proposing a theoretically grounded acquisition function that considers the batch collectively rather than sequentially. The computational approach using IPA is novel and well-motivated for handling the high-dimensional optimization of q-KG. The experimental results are comprehensive, covering both synthetic functions and practical machine learning tuning tasks, and consistently demonstrate the superiority of q-KG over benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Inconsistencies:** The paper contains multiple minor but significant technical inconsistencies. The definition of q-KG in (4.1) uses inconsistent notation for the minimization operator (sometimes written as "min x∈A" instead of "min_{x∈A}"). In Section 5.1, the equation (5.1) contains a formatting error with "z (1:q)" instead of "z(1:q)". The paper claims that "q-KG will reduce to parallel EI" under certain conditions but fails to provide the necessary mathematical derivation to support this claim.

2. **Missing Technical Justification:** The critical claim in Section 5.2 that "under the condition that µ and K are continuously differentiable, one can show that ∂/∂zij q-KG = E_Zq(∂/∂zij g)" is unsupported. The paper refers to "supplementary materials" which are not provided, making it impossible for readers to verify this key technical point.

3. **Implementation Details:** The choice of M=1000 for domain discretization (Section 5.3) is arbitrary without justification. The paper should provide sensitivity analysis showing how different M values affect performance, as this choice could significantly impact results. Additionally, the paper doesn't clarify whether the sampling from the posterior is based on the mean or the full posterior distribution.

4. **Mischaracterization of Related Work:** The paper states that "the parallel methods above construct the batch of points in an iterative greedy fashion" while citing [25] which explicitly uses gradient-based optimization rather than greedy selection. This mischaracterization undermines the paper's positioning against existing work.

5. **Formatting and Language Issues:** The paper inconsistently capitalizes "q-KG" (sometimes as "q-KG", sometimes as "q-KG"). The phrase "when there is only one batch of points remaining" in the abstract is grammatically incorrect and should be "when there is only one batch remaining." The paper also states "the final recommendation is restricted to the previous sampling decisions" without clarifying whether this refers to the final recommendation or the evaluation points.

6. **Experimental Reporting:** The claim that "q-KG makes dramatic progress in early iterations" lacks statistical significance testing. The paper should provide p-values or confidence intervals to support this assertion. Additionally, the repository link (https://github.com/wujian16/qKG) is not a permanent link and lacks a DOI, which is problematic for reproducibility.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a theoretically sound and empirically effective method for batch Bayesian optimization, the numerous technical inconsistencies, missing justifications, and formatting errors undermine its quality as a scientific contribution. The paper makes several important contributions to the field, but the lack of technical precision in these minor details is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. The paper would benefit significantly from addressing these issues before publication.

I recommend rejection due to the accumulation of minor but significant technical flaws that prevent the reader from fully evaluating the paper's claims and reproducibility. These issues, while individually small, collectively indicate a lack of meticulousness expected at NIPS. The authors should address these concerns thoroughly before resubmitting to a venue that values technical precision.
