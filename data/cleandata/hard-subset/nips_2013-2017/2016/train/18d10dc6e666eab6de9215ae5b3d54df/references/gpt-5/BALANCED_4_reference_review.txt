Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The acquisition equals the expected reduction in the posterior predictive minimum after conducting a batch of q evaluations: minx µ(n)(x) − E[minx µ(n+q)(x) | observations at the batch]. For Gaussian process (GP) surrogates with Gaussian noise, this is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch (under fixed hyperparameters), and reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless case when the final recommendation is restricted to evaluated points. Because exact evaluation/optimization is intractable for q > 1, the authors estimate the objective by Monte Carlo (via a linear-Gaussian representation of µ(n+q)(x)) and optimize it using stochastic gradients derived via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). For continuous A, they discretize the inner minimizations using a dynamically constructed finite set An that includes “representer points” (as in entropy search) plus previously sampled points and the candidate batch.

Empirically, q-KG is compared against q-EI (both exact/multipoint versions and a heuristic greedy variant), GP-BUCB and GP-UCB-PE, on synthetic benchmarks (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10. With Gaussian noise and in small-test-set evaluation scenarios, q-KG shows especially strong gains. The work builds directly on KG for sequential BO (Frazier et al., 2008; Scott et al., 2011) and is positioned against batch EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Wang et al., MOE), batch UCB (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.), and batch ES/PPES (Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani). It also discusses nested-batch KG on discrete spaces (Wang et al., SIAM SISC), highlighting computational differences.

Strengths
- Principled objective: q-KG is a clean, decision-theoretic generalization of KG to the batch setting, with a clear Bayes-optimal interpretation for a single remaining batch.
- Works naturally with noisy observations, addressing a known weakness of EI-based batch methods in the noisy regime.
- Optimization method: the IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator is a practical contribution that enables joint batch selection without greedy approximations; it complements earlier gradient derivations for q-EI.
- Empirical results: consistent improvements over strong baselines across synthetic and applied tasks, with especially notable gains under noise. Code release improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond the one-step Bayes-optimality (under fixed GP hyperparameters and discretized A), there are no performance guarantees (e.g., regret bounds), nor convergence guarantees for the stochastic optimization under the proposed discretization. A discussion of conditions ensuring unbiasedness/finite variance of the IPA estimator (argmin uniqueness, ties, dependence of An on z(1:q)) is deferred to the supplement—summarizing key assumptions in the main text would strengthen the paper.
- Discretization: The acquisition is defined over continuous A but evaluated with a finite An that depends on z(1:q). This dependence can make g non-smooth in ways that complicate the gradient estimator; the paper should clarify how the inclusion of z(1:q) in An is handled to avoid bias and whether gradients are stopped through the discrete set construction. Sensitivity to M (e.g., M = 1000) is not reported.
- Computational analysis: While IPA enables gradients, complexity and wall-clock comparisons are missing. Per-iteration costs (Cholesky O(q^3), GP updates, number of Monte Carlo samples, number of gradient steps, multi-starts) and scalability with q, d, and M should be quantified, and runtime vs. q-EI/PPES reported.
- Hyperparameter learning: The Bayes-optimal argument assumes fixed GP parameters, but experiments re-estimate hyperparameters by MLE after each batch. This mismatch is standard in practice, yet a brief discussion of its implications would be appropriate.
- Experimental protocol: For MNIST/CIFAR experiments, the objective appears to be test error measured repeatedly during the BO loop. This risks test leakage; a validation set should be used for BO, with the final test error reported once. The CNN study uses only 5 runs—more repetitions would increase confidence.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, but there are typos and minor inconsistencies (e.g., “Main Stange”). The derivation involving the derivative of the Cholesky factor deserves a short subsection or pointer to an implemented method (e.g., standard reverse-mode differentiation for Cholesky).
- The description “drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior” is unclear; please specify the representer-point construction (e.g., Thompson sampling from the GP posterior over functions and minimizing each sample, as in ES/PPES).

Originality
- Extends KG to the batch setting with a practical gradient-based optimizer; to my knowledge at the time of submission, there was no widely used q-KG with IPA-based joint optimization. The relation to nested-batch KG on discrete spaces is adequately discussed. The reduction to parallel EI in the noiseless constrained-final-selection case is a nice connecting result.

Significance
- Batch BO is important in modern ML workflows; a method that handles noise effectively and selects batches jointly is valuable. The empirical improvements over established baselines suggest practical impact, and the approach could be adopted in existing BO toolkits. A broader runtime/scalability study would further solidify significance.

Questions for the authors
- How many Monte Carlo samples and gradient steps are used per acquisition optimization? How sensitive is performance to these choices and to M?
- How does q-KG perform for larger batch sizes (e.g., q ∈ {8, 16})?
- Can you report wall-clock time per BO iteration vs. q-EI, PPES, GP-BUCB?
- How is the derivative of the Cholesky factor implemented in practice? Any numerical stability issues?
- Could the approach support asynchronous parallel BO (pending evaluations), and how would the acquisition adapt?

Suggestions for improvement
- Use validation sets for HPO, with final test reporting to avoid leakage.
- Add runtime and scalability analyses, larger q, and sensitivity to M and MC samples.
- Clarify and summarize the regularity assumptions for the IPA estimator in the main text.
- Provide more details on the representer-point scheme and consider ablations (static grids vs. dynamic An).
- Improve references and formatting; ensure all citations are complete and accessible.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled acquisition with strong intuition and correct reduction to q-EI.
- Practical, novel optimization via IPA enabling joint batch selection.
- Strong empirical results, particularly under noise; open-source code.
- Topic squarely within NeurIPS and of high practical interest.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theory beyond one-step Bayes optimality; no runtime/scalability analysis.
- Discretization-dependent estimator with limited sensitivity analysis.
- Experimental protocol (test-set usage) and small number of runs in some studies.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The methodological contribution is solid and practically relevant, and the empirical gains are convincing, especially in noisy settings. Addressing the clarity and experimental protocol issues, and adding runtime/scalability data, would make this a clear accept.
