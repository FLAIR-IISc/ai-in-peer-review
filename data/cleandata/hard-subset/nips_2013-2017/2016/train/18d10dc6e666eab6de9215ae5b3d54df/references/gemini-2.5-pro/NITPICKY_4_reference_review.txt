This paper proposes a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient criterion to the parallel setting, based on a decision-theoretic argument of maximizing the expected improvement in the final recommended solution after one additional batch of evaluations. As direct optimization of the q-KG function is computationally intractable, the authors propose a gradient-based optimization approach. They derive an unbiased estimator for the gradient of the q-KG factor using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and employ a dynamic discretization of the search space to make the computation feasible. The proposed method is evaluated on a suite of synthetic benchmark functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks, showing competitive or superior performance against existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods, particularly in noisy settings.

The work builds upon a well-established line of research in Bayesian optimization, specifically extending the knowledge gradient policy [7, 17] to the batch setting. This contrasts with more heuristic extensions of sequential policies like Expected Improvement (EI) or Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) [3, 5, 19], aiming for a more principled, joint evaluation of the candidate batch. The use of IPA for optimizing an acquisition function is related to recent work on parallel EI [25].

**Strengths:**

*   The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function from a decision-theoretic standpoint is principled and provides a solid theoretical motivation for the proposed method. It is a natural and elegant generalization of the sequential KG policy.
*   The paper tackles the important and practical problem of parallel (batch) BO, which is critical for leveraging modern computational resources.
*   The empirical results are promising. The method demonstrates strong performance, especially in the noisy evaluation setting, which is a common and challenging scenario in practice. This provides evidence that properly accounting for the joint information gain of a batch is beneficial.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite its promising contributions, the paper suffers from a significant number of flaws in its technical presentation, clarity, and methodological detail, which collectively undermine the confidence in the work.

1.  **Lack of Technical Rigor and Clarity:**
    *   The justification for the key technical contribution—the gradient estimator—is insufficient. The validity of interchanging the gradient and expectation operators in (5.3) via IPA is non-trivial, especially given the non-differentiability introduced by the `argmin` operator in the definition of `x*(after)`. The `min` operator makes the integrand `g` non-smooth. While the authors state that this can be shown under certain conditions and defer the proof to supplementary material, this is a critical point that warrants at least a high-level justification in the main text.
    *   The explanation for how q-KG reduces to parallel EI is imprecise. The statement `min_{x \in x(1:n)} µ(n)(x)` becomes `min y(1:n)` only under noise-free conditions. The subsequent steps in the derivation are hand-wavy and seem to make implicit, unjustified assumptions.
    *   The notation in Equation (4.1) is confusing. `En` is defined as the expectation conditioned on `n` observations, but the expression is written as `En[... | y(z(1:q))]`. This conditioning is redundant, as the inner term `µ(n+q)` is already a function of the (random) future observation `y(z(1:q))`. The expectation is over the distribution of this future observation. This lack of notational precision pervades the technical sections.

2.  **Omission of Critical Methodological Details:**
    *   The optimization of the acquisition function is described as "multi-start stochastic gradient ascent," but no details are provided. The number of restarts, learning rate schedule, and number of iterations are crucial for reproducibility and for understanding the true computational cost of the method. The performance of BO methods is often highly sensitive to how well the acquisition function is optimized.
    *   The discretization scheme in Section 5.3 is a cornerstone of the practical algorithm, yet it is not adequately explored. The choice of `M=1000` is arbitrary, and no sensitivity analysis is provided. It is unclear if this is sufficient, especially for the higher-dimensional problems (e.g., the 8-D CNN tuning task).
    *   A subtle but important detail is overlooked: the discretization set `An` in (5.4) depends on the optimization variable `z(1:q)`. This means the objective function's definition changes as a function of the variables being optimized. The authors do not discuss the potential complications this introduces to the optimization landscape or the gradient calculation.

3.  **Inadequate Discussion of Computational Cost:**
    *   The algorithm description states that GP hyperparameters are re-trained via MLE at every iteration. This is a very expensive operation, scaling cubically with the number of observations. This cost is never discussed or compared against baselines, making it impossible to assess the practical wall-clock efficiency of the proposed method versus its sample efficiency.

4.  **Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
    *   The paper contains several typographical and grammatical errors (e.g., "Main Stange" in Algorithm 1, "tune totally 8 hyperparameters").
    *   The NIPS conference footnote on the first page incorrectly states "th Conference", which is unprofessional.
    *   Citation formatting is inconsistent (e.g., `PI)[23]` with no space vs. `EI) [13]` with a space).

**Evaluation Summary:**

*   **Quality:** The technical soundness is questionable due to the lack of justification for the IPA-based gradient and several imprecise arguments. The experimental methodology lacks key details required for reproducibility and a full assessment of the method's practicality.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is difficult to follow in key technical sections due to confusing notation and omitted derivations. Critical algorithmic components are not described in sufficient detail. The conversational phrase "The readers should note that there are two As here..." highlights a failure to establish clear notation from the outset.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of extending KG to the batch setting is novel and valuable. The application of IPA to this new acquisition function is a solid contribution, though the technique itself has been used for other acquisition functions.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem, and the proposed method shows strong empirical promise. If the technical and clarity issues were resolved, the work would likely be of high interest to the BO community. However, in its current state, the significance is diminished by the concerns about its correctness and reproducibility.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

While the paper presents a well-motivated and promising method for batch Bayesian optimization, the manuscript in its current form does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The accumulation of minor presentational flaws, coupled with significant omissions of methodological details and a lack of rigor in the core technical arguments, makes the work difficult to trust and reproduce. The authors have a strong foundation for a good paper, but a major revision is required to address these substantial weaknesses.
