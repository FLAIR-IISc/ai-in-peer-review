- Introduces q-KG, a decision-theoretic batch extension of the knowledge gradient that evaluates the expected value of evaluating q points simultaneously using IPA for gradient computation.
- Empirically, q-KG consistently outperforms parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic functions and ML tuning tasks, especially in noisy settings.
- Provides a Bayes-optimality result for q-KG when only one batch remains.
- Novel contribution: extends sequential KG to consider batches collectively rather than by iterative single-point construction.
- Uses infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute gradients, making the method practical.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and includes code and sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- Demonstrates clear practical impact for parallel hyperparameter tuning in ML (e.g., logistic regression, CNNs).
- Lacks a detailed computational complexity analysis or empirical runtime comparisons versus other batch BO methods.
- No sensitivity analysis on the discretization parameter M (used M=1000) to show robustness to this choice.
- Missing direct comparison/discussion of recent gradient-based parallel EI methods (e.g., cited [15,25]).
- Does not provide theoretical convergence guarantees or regret bounds for the full iterative algorithm.
- Experimental suite could be more diverse beyond standard synthetic functions to better showcase strengths.
- Recommendation: accept to NIPS based on novelty, theory, and empirical performance.
- Suggested improvements: add complexity analysis, sensitivity study for M, broader method comparisons, theoretical convergence discussion, and more diverse benchmarks.
- Pro acceptance: solid theoretical extension, novel IPA computational approach, strong empirical gains, clear writing, and practical relevance.
- Con acceptance: limited theoretical complexity/regret analysis, no sensitivity study, and incomplete comparisons with recent methods.
- Final assessment: high-quality, significant, reproducible work that advances batch Bayesian optimization and is well-suited for NIPS.
