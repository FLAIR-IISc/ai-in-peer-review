This paper proposes a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization (BO), termed the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) framework to the parallel setting, where `q` function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The core idea is to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final recommended solution, derived from a decision-theoretic one-step lookahead perspective. As direct optimization of the q-KG factor is computationally intractable, the authors propose an efficient gradient-based optimization scheme using a stochastic gradient estimator derived via Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). The method is evaluated on several synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, where it is compared against established parallel BO methods such as parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and parallel Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) variants.

**Quality and Strengths:**

The paper is technically sound and addresses an important and timely problem in Bayesian optimization. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function from a decision-theoretic standpoint is principled and provides a solid theoretical foundation, contrasting with more heuristic approaches to batch construction. The primary technical contribution—the application of IPA to derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for the q-KG factor—is non-trivial and provides a viable path to optimizing what would otherwise be an intractable acquisition function. The experimental evaluation is reasonably broad, covering both standard synthetic benchmarks and relevant machine learning applications (logistic regression and CNN tuning). The observed strong performance in noisy settings is a notable strength, as this is a common and challenging scenario in practice.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

Despite its strengths, the paper presents an unproven method and leaves several critical questions unanswered, raising concerns about its practical utility and the robustness of the empirical claims.

1.  **Computational Complexity:** A significant omission is the lack of any discussion or empirical comparison of the computational overhead of the proposed q-KG method. Maximizing the acquisition function involves Monte Carlo sampling to estimate both the function value and its gradient, followed by a gradient-based optimization routine. This is almost certainly more computationally expensive than the baselines, especially the greedy parallel EI heuristics and UCB-based methods. For BO to be practical, the time spent selecting the next batch of points must be significantly less than the time spent evaluating the objective function. Without a clear characterization of this trade-off, it is difficult to assess the method's value. Is a marginal improvement in sample efficiency worth a 10x or 100x increase in selection time? The paper provides no information to answer this.

2.  **Reliance on Approximations:** The method's implementation relies on a critical approximation: the discretization of the continuous domain `A` into a finite set `An` to compute the `min` over the posterior mean. The authors propose an adaptive scheme where `An` is constructed by sampling from the optima of the GP posterior. However, the quality of the q-KG estimate and its gradient is fundamentally dependent on the quality and size (`M`) of this discretization. The paper uses `M=1000` without any justification or sensitivity analysis. It is unclear how the method's performance would degrade with a smaller `M` or in higher-dimensional problems where 1000 points may provide very sparse coverage. This approximation introduces a key hyperparameter whose effect is not studied, undermining the methodological rigor.

3.  **Inconsistent Empirical Performance:** While the authors claim strong performance, the results are not universally superior. For instance, on the noise-free Branin2 function, a standard benchmark, q-KG performs slightly worse than the best baseline. A new, more complex, and presumably more expensive method should ideally demonstrate a more consistent advantage over established, simpler baselines. Furthermore, the CNN tuning experiment is based on only 5 independent runs, which is a very small sample size for drawing strong conclusions, especially given the visible variance in the results.

4.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The optimization of the acquisition function relies on a stochastic gradient estimator. The stability and performance of this optimization depend heavily on the number of Monte Carlo samples used to estimate the gradient. The paper does not mention how many samples were used, nor does it provide any analysis of how performance varies with this number. This is a crucial implementation detail required for reproducibility and for understanding the method's practical behavior.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a principled, decision-theoretically motivated acquisition function for the important problem of batch BO.
*   The use of IPA to derive a gradient estimator is a novel and technically sound contribution that makes the method computationally feasible.
*   The method shows particularly promising results in noisy environments, which is a highly relevant setting for many real-world applications.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   The paper completely ignores the computational cost of the proposed method, which is a critical factor for its practical adoption and a major point of comparison against established baselines.
*   The method's performance hinges on a domain discretization scheme, but the paper lacks any sensitivity analysis regarding the size of this discretization (`M`).
*   The empirical evidence is not consistently strong across all benchmarks, and some experiments suffer from a low number of runs.
*   Key implementation details and hyperparameters (e.g., number of Monte Carlo samples for gradient estimation) are not discussed or analyzed, impacting reproducibility and a full understanding of the method.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents an interesting and theoretically motivated approach to batch Bayesian optimization, the validation provided is insufficient to endorse it over more established methods at this time. The lack of any analysis on computational overhead and the reliance on an unanalyzed discretization approximation are significant methodological weaknesses. The empirical results, while promising, are not strong enough to overlook these issues. The work feels more like a proof-of-concept for a new idea rather than a thoroughly vetted and validated method ready for widespread use. Therefore, I lean towards recommending rejection. I would be open to changing my assessment if the authors could provide a compelling analysis of the computational cost and a thorough sensitivity analysis of the key approximations in their rebuttal.
