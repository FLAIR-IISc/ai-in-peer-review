Summary
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. q-KG values a batch by the expected decrease in the posterior mean minimizer after observing that batch, i.e., the expected improvement in the minimum of the GP predictor after q evaluations. The authors show that q-KG coincides with parallel EI under noiseless evaluations when the final recommendation is restricted to observed points. Because exact evaluation and optimization of q-KG is intractable for q > 1, they (i) derive a Monte Carlo estimator using a reparameterization of the posterior via a standard normal Zq, (ii) obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for a discretized candidate set, and (iii) discretize the inner minimization over the domain by sampling likely minima of the GP posterior (augmented by previously sampled and candidate points). Empirically, q-KG outperforms or matches parallel EI (exact and heuristic) and UCB-based batch methods on synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10), with especially strong gains under noise.

Relation to prior work
- Extends sequential KG (Frazier et al. 2008; Scott et al. 2011) to the batch setting, choosing points jointly rather than greedily.
- Compares to batch EI and UCB variants (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Desautels et al.; Contal et al.), and discusses parallel ES (Shah & Ghahramani).
- Uses a “fantasization” style reparameterization similar in spirit to parallel EI optimizers, but applies IPA to obtain stochastic gradients for joint batch selection.
- The discretization of the inner minimization by sampling from likely posterior minima follows ideas from entropy search variants.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound one-step Bayes decision-theoretic objective: q-KG directly maximizes expected one-step improvement in the best posterior mean, which is well-aligned with simple-regret minimization and naturally handles observation noise.
- Clear Monte Carlo formulation via reparameterization; the q-KG estimator and its gradient are derived coherently. The IPA gradient avoids score-function variance and is appropriate for continuous domains after discretization.
- Empirical results are consistent and compelling, especially in the noisy setting where EI degrades. Realistic applications to hyperparameter tuning demonstrate practical impact.

Weaknesses and technical concerns:
- Discretization bias and its effect are not theoretically quantified. The paper lacks guidance on the choice of M (number of candidate discretization points), its scaling with d, and the sensitivity of outcomes to this choice.
- The Bayes-optimality claim holds for a single remaining batch and optimization over the same A used in the objective. In practice, A is discretized adaptively, and hyperparameters are re-estimated by MLE. The implications of these approximations (finite An, hyperparameter uncertainty) on optimality are not analyzed.
- Differentiability assumptions required by IPA (unique minimizers of the inner argmins; smooth kernel) are discussed only briefly. Nondifferentiability of the min operator is common; while almost-sure uniqueness can be induced by jitter, this deserves a more explicit treatment or a subgradient argument (e.g., Clarke subdifferential).
- Computational complexity is not made explicit. Evaluating q-KG and its gradient requires repeated solves and triangular back-substitutions involving the q×q Cholesky and computing σ̃n(x, z(1:q))Zq for all x in An, per Monte Carlo sample. Practical runtime and scaling with q, |An|, and d should be reported.
- Hyperparameter learning is done via MLE at each iteration; uncertainty over kernel and noise parameters is ignored. This can bias acquisition values, especially in early iterations and under high noise.
- Baselines: it would be valuable to include parallel entropy search (PPES) in experiments or justify its omission (e.g., prohibitive cost), and to ensure that wall-clock or function-evaluation budgets are matched fairly given different acquisition optimization costs.

Clarity
- Generally well written, with intuitive motivation and a clean definition of q-KG. The algorithmic outline is clear.
- However, several references are incomplete; section numbering and typos (e.g., “.1” subsections) should be fixed. More detail on the gradient computation (derivative through the Cholesky, numerical stability) would aid reproducibility.
- Report the number of Monte Carlo samples, step sizes/optimizer for the stochastic gradient ascent, and computational budgets used to optimize acquisitions.

Originality
- Extending KG to joint batch selection with a differentiable, reparameterized MC estimator and an IPA gradient is a novel and meaningful contribution at the time. The connection to parallel EI under specific conditions is insightful.
- The use of a data-driven discretization based on samples from likely minima is a reasonable and somewhat novel combination, though related ideas exist in ES/PPES.

Significance
- Batch BO is important in practice; a method that handles noise well and jointly optimizes the batch has clear utility. The empirical gains, especially under noise, suggest practitioners will benefit.
- The lack of theoretical guarantees beyond one-step optimality and the reliance on discretization may limit adoption in very high dimensions or very large batches, but the contribution advances the state of the art.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to M, the number of Monte Carlo samples for Zq, batch size q, and dimensionality d? Please include ablation plots and runtime breakdowns.
- Do you use variance reduction (e.g., common random numbers, antithetics) for the stochastic gradient? If so, how much does it help?
- How do results change if you marginalize GP hyperparameters (e.g., via MAP with Laplace or HMC) rather than point estimates?
- Can the discretization set An omit z(1:q) without degrading performance, or does including it materially help?

Pros
- Principled batch acquisition; robust to noise.
- Unbiased stochastic gradients enable efficient joint optimization over batches.
- Strong empirical performance on synthetic and real tasks; code available.

Cons
- No theoretical analysis of discretization error, convergence, or computational complexity.
- Limited discussion of gradient estimator assumptions and numerical stability.
- Missing comparisons and runtime-normalized assessments.

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept). The paper presents a principled and practically useful batch BO method with clear improvements, and the methodological contribution (q-KG with IPA gradients) is novel and relevant. Strengthening the experimental analysis and clarifying computational aspects would further improve the paper.
