Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) extension of the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization. The objective is the expected reduction in the posterior GP predictor’s minimum after a batch of q evaluations, which is Bayes-optimal if only one batch remains. Because exact evaluation is intractable for q > 1, the paper uses Monte Carlo with an unbiased gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch locations. The acquisition is approximated by discretizing the inner minimization set and recomputed each iteration. Experiments on synthetic functions and two ML tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10) report advantages over q-EI variants and UCB-based batch methods, especially under observation noise.

This sits in a well-studied area (batch BO) and relates to q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.), GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE, and parallel entropy search (PPES; Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani). The work builds on classic KG (Frazier et al.; Scott et al.) by extending it to batch and providing a stochastic gradient estimator.

Strengths:
- Principled decision-theoretic formulation of batch acquisition generalizing KG.
- Clear reduction to q-EI in the noiseless case under the “recommend-from-sampled” restriction.
- IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator for q-KG is potentially useful beyond this setting.
- Empirical results suggest q-KG is robust in noisy settings where q-EI can struggle.
- Open-source code availability (claimed) is a plus.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:

Quality/technical soundness:
- The interchange of differentiation and expectation for g that involves a min over a discretized set requires nontrivial regularity (unique minimizers a.s., piecewise differentiability). The main text asserts conditions (“µ and K continuously differentiable”) but the min breaks differentiability; please state precise conditions (e.g., almost sure uniqueness and Lipschitzness) and provide at least a lemma sketch in the paper (not only in the supplement).
- Gradients through the Cholesky factor D(n)(z(1:q)): differentiating Cholesky is delicate; provide explicit derivative formulas or cite a standard result. Computational stability/complexity of computing ∂D/∂z is not discussed.
- The Bayes-optimality claim strictly holds for the continuous A; after discretization (An), the property changes. This should be clearly delimited; as written, it reads as if it holds post-discretization.
- The method for constructing An is unclear: “drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior distribution of the Gaussian process” is not a standard phrase. Do you mean sampling approximate minimizers of posterior sample paths (as in ES/PES) or sampling promising points from posterior mean minus some exploration term? Please formalize the procedure; otherwise reproducibility and theoretical interpretation of q-KG(·, An) are murky.
- Computational complexity is not analyzed. The per-iteration and per-gradient-sample costs (Cholesky O(q^3), computing σ̃n for each x ∈ An, number of MC samples, number of restarts) should be provided. Without wall-clock comparisons, it’s impossible to assess if the gains justify the overhead relative to q-EI/PPES.
- Noise modeling: you assume homoscedastic Gaussian noise estimated by MLE. Several experiments (MNIST with small test sets) are effectively heteroscedastic/Bernoulli-derived; discuss the misspecification and whether q-KG’s advantage stems from robustness or artifacts.
- Missing baselines: PPES (parallel predictive entropy search) is cited but not compared in experiments, while it is a strong batch BO competitor. At minimum, discuss why it’s omitted; ideally include it.
- The reduction to q-EI is sketched; please provide a rigorous derivation (or pointer to an appendix) with assumptions about final recommendation, uniqueness of minima, and the noiseless GP equivalence µ(n)(x(i)) = y(i).

Clarity:
- The paper has frequent typos and inconsistencies, which hinder readability:
  - “Main Stange” (Algorithm 1), “gaussian” (capitalize), “etal”, missing spaces/commas throughout.
  - Section numbering is broken: subsections appear as “.1”, “.2”; the “Parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG)” section heading numbering is inconsistent with earlier references to Section 4/5.
  - Notation overload: A denotes both the continuous domain and the discrete set used in the inner min; although you mention this, it remains confusing. Use distinct symbols (e.g., X for domain, S for discretization) consistently.
  - “min µ(n+q)(x) is a random vector” – it is a scalar random variable. Also, “We can compute the probability distribution of this difference” – in practice you MC sample it; please correct the phrasing.
  - Some equations could be clearer on dimensions (e.g., σ̃n(x, z(1:q)) is 1×q; Zq ∼ N(0, Iq)).
- Reproducibility details are insufficient:
  - Number of MC samples for q-KG and for its gradient; step sizes/optimizers; number of restarts; stopping criteria; constraints handling; initialization scheme for z(1:q); handling of bounds; batch size q across experiments; total evaluation budgets per task.
  - Hyperparameter learning: priors, MLE procedure, frequency of refits, optimizer, and convergence tolerances.
  - For UCB baselines, specify βt schedules; for Spearmint/MOE, ensure consistent kernels and hyperparameter fitting across methods.
  - For CNN: exact architecture, preprocessing/data augmentation, training schedule, early stopping, hardware; otherwise the reported 11% vs 14% improvement is hard to validate.

Originality:
- Extending KG to batch with an IPA gradient is a meaningful and nontrivial step. However, batch BO with collective acquisitions is well-trodden (q-EI derivatives, PPES); the novelty rests on the specific objective (q-KG) and the gradient estimator. Make the distinction from existing gradientable q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.) and from PPES more explicit, both conceptually and empirically.

Significance:
- If validated with stronger baselines and runtime analysis, q-KG could be impactful for noisy parallel BO. As presented, the lack of PPES comparison, missing runtime, and limited reproducibility weaken the claim.

Pros:
- Principled batch acquisition with clear one-step Bayes-optimal motivation.
- Stochastic gradient estimator via IPA; potentially broadly useful.
- Strong performance in noisy settings across multiple tasks; code release.

Cons:
- Clarity/notation and reference formatting issues pervasive.
- Missing key baselines (PPES), runtime, and ablations/sensitivity (M, q, MC samples).
- Insufficient theoretical and algorithmic details for gradients through min/Cholesky.
- Reproducibility details lacking; fairness of comparisons uncertain.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and likely publishable after significant revision, but the current manuscript has many minor (and some substantive) issues in clarity, completeness, and evaluation that, collectively, do not meet NeurIPS standards.

Minor edits and formatting:
- Fix typos (“Main Stange”, “random vector”, capitalization, punctuation).
- Clean and complete references (authors, titles, venues, years; fix broken URLs).
- Consistent section numbering and notation (distinct symbols for domain vs discretization).
- Provide numerical values in figures (final regrets/errors), not just curves. Add wall-clock plots. Include PPES baseline. Add sensitivity to M and q and MC budget.
