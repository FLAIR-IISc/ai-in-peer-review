- Introduces q-KG, a batch acquisition that generalizes the knowledge gradient to select q points in parallel by maximizing expected reduction in the GP posterior mean minimum.
- Derivation is decision-theoretic one-step lookahead; reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless/restricted-recommendation case.
- Uses Monte Carlo over a Gaussian draw and an IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator to approximate and optimize q-KG for q > 1.
- For continuous domains, inner minimization is approximated via a dynamically updated discretization using samples from likely posterior minimizers.
- Experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning show q-KG is competitive or superior to parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, especially under observation noise.
- Implementation is provided in C++ and code is released to aid reproducibility.
- Extends classic sequential KG to noisy, batch, and continuous settings, contrasting with batch EI and PPES approaches.
- Provides a differentiable objective and scalable gradient estimator improving on nested-batch KG for discrete domains.
- Decision-theoretic derivation is principled and the reduction to parallel EI is a useful sanity check.
- IPA-based gradient estimator is a key technical contribution with careful treatment of derivatives (including Cholesky).
- Discretization procedure for support sets is under-specified; unclear how posterior-optima samples are generated in practice.
- The impact of the number of support points M and sampling method on bias, variance, and performance is not quantified (no ablation).
- Computational complexity and scalability are insufficiently analyzed: inner minimizations per MC draw, Cholesky costs, and optimizing a qd-dimensional nonconvex objective are concerning.
- No runtime comparisons, scaling studies in q and d, or gradient variance diagnostics are provided.
- Differentiability issues at argmin switches and discrete approximations are not empirically addressed; stability and smoothing are not discussed.
- GP hyperparameters are re-fitted by MLE between batches and treated as fixed, which weakens the Bayes-optimality claim; marginalization is not explored.
- Important baselines (fast multipoint q-EI, gradient-based q-EI, and PPES) are missing from experiments.
- Experiments are relatively small-scale (d ≤ 8, q=4); larger q (e.g., 8,16), higher-d problems, and wall-clock comparisons are needed.
- Reproducibility would be improved by reporting MC sample counts, optimizer settings, learning rates, and computational overhead versus baselines.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized, but the discretization procedure and numerical-stability details (triangular solves, Cholesky derivatives) need clearer exposition.
- The batch KG extension with stochastic gradient estimation appears novel and nontrivial relative to prior work.
- Addresses an important practical problem and shows promising empirical gains under noise; adoption depends on resolving computational concerns.
- Pros: principled one-step Bayes-optimal acquisition, joint batch selection, efficient stochastic gradient, strong noisy-results, released code.
- Cons: missing strong baselines and runtime analyses, under-specified discretization and MC/ablation details, limited scaling evidence and gradient-stability discussion.
- Key author questions: how are posterior-optima samples generated; ablation on M and sampling method; typical MC sample sizes and optimizer settings; wall-clock/time comparisons and larger q/d results; empirical comparison to fast q-EI and PPES.
- Overall recommendation: Accept (weak) — technically solid and novel with promising results, but needs stronger baselines and computational analyses.
