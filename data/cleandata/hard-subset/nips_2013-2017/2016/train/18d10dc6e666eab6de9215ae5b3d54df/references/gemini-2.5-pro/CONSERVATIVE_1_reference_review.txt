**Summary**
This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization. The method generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to the parallel setting, where a batch of `q` points is evaluated simultaneously. The q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic standpoint, selecting the batch of points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final recommended solution value, assuming it is the last batch to be evaluated. Recognizing that the naive maximization of this acquisition function is computationally prohibitive, the authors propose an efficient optimization strategy based on estimating the gradient of the q-KG factor using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method is evaluated on several synthetic benchmark functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks, both in noise-free and noisy settings, and is compared against other parallel Bayesian optimization methods like parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and parallel Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) variants. The results suggest that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms these baselines, with particularly strong performance in the presence of noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths**
1.  **Principled Derivation:** The primary strength of this work is its sound theoretical foundation. The q-KG acquisition function is not an ad-hoc heuristic but is derived from a clear decision-theoretic principle: it is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch of evaluations. This provides a solid justification for the method's design, which is a desirable quality compared to more heuristic approaches.
2.  **Generalization of an Established Method:** The work builds upon the well-regarded Knowledge Gradient method from the sequential setting. By extending this established concept to the more practical parallel setting, the paper addresses a clear need in the Bayesian optimization literature.
3.  **Efficient Optimization Strategy:** The authors rightly identify the computational challenge of jointly optimizing a batch of `q` points. The use of IPA to derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for the q-KG factor is a significant technical contribution. This enables the use of standard gradient-based optimizers, making the maximization of the complex, Monte Carlo-based acquisition function tractable.
4.  **Strong Performance on Noisy Problems:** The empirical results consistently highlight the method's advantage in noisy environments. This is a particularly important result, as many real-world optimization problems, such as hyperparameter tuning evaluated on a validation subset, inherently involve noisy measurements.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** A major concern is the practical computational expense of the proposed method, which is not adequately addressed. The q-KG factor and its gradient are estimated via Monte Carlo simulation, and the inner optimization problem relies on a discretization of the domain. The overall cost appears to be substantial, likely scaling poorly with the batch size `q`, the problem dimension `d`, and the number of discretization points `M`. The paper provides no runtime analysis or comparison against the baselines, making it difficult to assess the trade-off between optimization performance and computational overhead. For a method to be widely adopted, it must be practical, and the current presentation leaves significant doubts about its practicality for larger-scale problems.
2.  **Sensitivity to Approximations:** The method relies on two key approximations: Monte Carlo sampling for the expectation and a discretization of the domain `A` for computing the minimum of the posterior mean. The paper lacks a rigorous analysis of how these approximations affect performance.
    *   How many Monte Carlo samples are required for a stable gradient estimate? No sensitivity analysis is provided.
    *   The choice of `M=1000` for the discretization set size seems arbitrary. Is this sufficient for the higher-dimensional problems (e.g., Hartmann6 or the 8D CNN tuning problem)? The quality of this discretization is critical to the performance of the KG method, yet its impact is not explored. A new method should be accompanied by a thorough validation of its components.
3.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** While the experiments cover both synthetic and practical problems, the evaluation could be more comprehensive. The number of independent runs is small for the expensive CNN tuning task (5 runs), which weakens the statistical significance of the results. Furthermore, while the baselines are relevant, the landscape of batch BO methods is broad, and comparison against a wider array of state-of-the-art techniques would provide a more convincing case for q-KG's superiority.
4.  **Clarity on Novelty:** The paper extends KG to the parallel setting, which is a valuable contribution. However, joint optimization of a batch is not a new concept in itself; methods for jointly optimizing parallel EI and parallel Entropy Search already exist and are cited by the authors. The core contributions are the application of the KG principle and the use of IPA for gradient estimation. While solid, this could be viewed as a skillful combination of existing concepts rather than a fundamentally new paradigm. A more detailed discussion of *why* the KG principle is theoretically better suited for noisy, parallel optimization than, for instance, the EI principle, would strengthen the paper's claims of significance beyond the empirical results.

**Overall Assessment**
The paper presents a method, q-KG, that is well-motivated and technically sound. The decision-theoretic derivation is elegant, and the use of IPA to enable optimization is a clever and necessary component. The empirical results, particularly in noisy settings, are promising.

However, the work falls short in its validation and analysis of the method's practical aspects. The high computational cost is a major concern that is not addressed, and the sensitivity of the method to its core approximation schemes (Monte Carlo and domain discretization) is not studied. As a reviewer who values rigor and established practice, I am cautious about endorsing a new, complex method without a thorough understanding of its computational trade-offs and robustness. While the ideas are strong, the paper in its current form feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully-vetted, practical algorithm ready for widespread use. The authors have developed a powerful new tool, but they need to provide more evidence regarding its efficiency and reliability.
