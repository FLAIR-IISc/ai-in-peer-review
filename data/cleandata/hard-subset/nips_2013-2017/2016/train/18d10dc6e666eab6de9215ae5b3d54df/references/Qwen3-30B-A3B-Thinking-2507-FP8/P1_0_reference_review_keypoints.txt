- Proposes q-KG: extends the knowledge gradient to batch/parallel Bayesian optimization with a decision-theoretic, Bayes-optimal objective when one batch remains.  
- Introduces IPA-based gradient estimation to efficiently optimize the q-KG acquisition function.  
- Empirically outperforms existing batch BO methods, especially in noisy settings, on synthetic benchmarks and ML tasks.  
- Demonstrates practical hyperparameter tuning gains (e.g., CNN test error reduced from ~14% to ~11%).  
- Thorough experimental evaluation: multiple synthetic functions, logistic regression and CNN tuning, noise-free and noisy settings, and comparisons to relevant baselines.  
- Code is released (GitHub), enhancing reproducibility.  
- Technical derivation and decision-theoretic motivation are sound; algorithm and notation are clear.  
- Well-organized and clearly written paper; minor clarifications suggested for the IPA explanation.  
- Novel contribution: collective batch KG extension and novel use of IPA for gradient estimation.  
- Lacks theoretical performance guarantees such as regret or convergence bounds.  
- No detailed computational complexity analysis; unclear how IPA scales with batch size q and dimension d.  
- Experiments focus on low-dimensional problems (d ≤ 6 synthetic, d = 4–8 practical); scalability to high dimensions (d > 10) not addressed.  
- Related work omits some more recent parallel BO methods (post-2016), weakening state-of-the-art claims.  
- Limited discussion of limitations, e.g., large batch sizes (q > 10) and constrained problems.  
- Recommendation: accept — strengths outweigh weaknesses.  
- Suggested improvements: add regret/convergence discussion, include complexity/scalability analysis, update related work, expand limitations, and compare to recent batch BO methods [27,28].  
- Pros summarized: decision-theoretic foundation, IPA efficiency, comprehensive experiments, significant noisy-setting gains, practical relevance, code availability.  
- Cons summarized: limited theoretical guarantees, limited scalability discussion, incomplete complexity analysis and recent-method comparisons.
