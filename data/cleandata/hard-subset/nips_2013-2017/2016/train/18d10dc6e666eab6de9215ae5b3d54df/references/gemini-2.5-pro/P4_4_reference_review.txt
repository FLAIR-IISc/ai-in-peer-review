### Paper Summary

This paper introduces a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), termed the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The goal is to select a batch of `q` points for simultaneous evaluation to optimize an expensive black-box function. The proposed method is derived from a decision-theoretic framework, generalizing the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to the parallel setting. The q-KG acquisition function quantifies the expected improvement in the final optimal solution (defined as the minimum of the posterior mean function) that would result from evaluating a given batch of `q` points. This formulation is distinct from parallel Expected Improvement (EI), which is Bayes-optimal under the more restrictive assumption that the final solution must be one of the points already evaluated.

Recognizing that the direct optimization of the q-KG acquisition function is computationally intractable, the authors' main technical contribution is a method for its efficient maximization. They derive an unbiased stochastic estimator for the gradient of the q-KG factor using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). This allows the use of standard gradient-based optimizers to find the optimal batch of points. To handle the inner minimization over a continuous domain `A` required by the KG factor, the authors employ a dynamic discretization strategy, similar to methods used in Entropy Search, which is updated at each iteration of the BO algorithm. The proposed q-KG method is empirically evaluated against several state-of-the-art parallel BO algorithms on synthetic benchmark functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, showing competitive or superior performance, especially in the presence of noisy function evaluations.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is principled and well-grounded in decision theory. The application of IPA to obtain a stochastic gradient is a sound and non-trivial technical step that makes the proposed approach practical. Claims are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation that includes multiple strong baselines (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) implemented in well-known open-source packages. The experiments cover both noise-free and noisy settings, demonstrating the robustness of the method. The results, particularly the significant advantage in noisy settings, are compelling.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation for a parallel KG policy is clearly articulated, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a coherent manner. The overall algorithm is clearly summarized. However, a key detail concerning reproducibility could be improved. Section 5.3 describes the discretization of the domain `A` by drawing `M` samples from the posterior distribution of the global optima. While the paper cites prior work for this technique, it would benefit from a more self-contained description of the specific sampling procedure used. Clarifying this would make it easier for other researchers to replicate the results precisely.

**Originality:**
The work is original. While parallel versions of other acquisition functions like EI and Entropy Search have been proposed, this paper presents the first practical formulation and efficient optimization algorithm for a parallel Knowledge Gradient policy in a continuous domain. It successfully generalizes the core idea of KG—valuing information based on its direct impact on the final recommended solution—to the important batch setting. This contribution is novel and meaningfully advances the landscape of acquisition functions for parallel Bayesian optimization.

**Significance:**
The paper's contribution is significant. It introduces a new, principled, and high-performing algorithm for a practical and increasingly common problem: batch optimization of expensive functions. The demonstrated superior performance in noisy settings is particularly important, as many real-world BO applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning via cross-validation) inherently involve noisy evaluations. By showing strong results and releasing the code, the authors provide a valuable new tool for practitioners and a solid foundation for future research in this area.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Principled Approach:** The paper introduces a novel and theoretically-grounded acquisition function (q-KG) for batch BO, filling a gap in the literature.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to be highly effective, outperforming or matching several state-of-the-art baselines, with a particularly noteworthy advantage in noisy settings, which are common in practice.
3.  **Sound Technical Contribution:** The use of IPA to derive a stochastic gradient provides a computationally viable way to optimize the otherwise intractable acquisition function, representing a solid technical contribution.
4.  **Practical Relevance and Impact:** The paper addresses an important practical problem and provides open-source code, increasing its potential for broad adoption and impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper does not provide a discussion or empirical analysis of the computational overhead of the q-KG method compared to baselines. The method involves Monte Carlo estimation for both the acquisition function and its gradient, as well as a dynamic discretization step, which is likely to be more computationally intensive per iteration than some of the compared methods. A discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Limited Scope of Batch Size Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted with a fixed batch size of `q=4`. An investigation into how the relative performance of q-KG changes with different batch sizes would provide a more complete understanding of its properties.

In conclusion, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty, technical soundness, and strong empirical performance on a significant problem, substantially outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.
