This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO). The goal is to select a batch of `q` points for simultaneous evaluation to find the minimum of an expensive-to-evaluate function. The proposed method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, generalizing the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy. The q-KG acquisition function measures the expected improvement in the final recommended solution (the minimum of the posterior mean) after observing the outcomes of the `q` parallel evaluations. This provides a principled one-step Bayes-optimal policy.

The main technical contribution of the paper is a computationally tractable method for maximizing this complex acquisition function. The authors propose a Monte Carlo approach to estimate the q-KG value and, crucially, use Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic estimator for its gradient. This enables the use of efficient gradient-based optimization. To handle the inner optimization problem over a continuous domain `A` within the q-KG definition, the authors employ a dynamic discretization scheme, where `A` is approximated by a finite set of points sampled from the current posterior's high-probability regions.

This work builds upon the sequential KG literature [7, 17] and extends it to the challenging batch setting. It distinguishes itself from prior work in parallel BO, such as heuristic modifications of Expected Improvement (EI) like "constant liar" [10] or sequential batch construction with UCB [3, 5]. It offers a more principled, fully-Bayesian approach that jointly considers all points in the batch, similar in spirit to parallel EI [2, 25] and Parallel Entropy Search (PES) [18].

### Strengths

1.  **Principled Derivation:** The q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated and elegant. Its foundation in decision theory as a one-step optimal policy provides a strong theoretical justification that is lacking in more heuristic batch BO methods. The explicit goal of improving the final posterior minimum is directly aligned with the objective of global optimization (i.e., minimizing simple regret).

2.  **Significant Technical Contribution:** The primary strength lies in the development of a viable optimization strategy for the proposed acquisition function. Naively optimizing q-KG is intractable. The use of IPA to obtain a stochastic gradient is a novel and powerful technique in this context, allowing the difficult optimization-within-an-expectation problem to be tackled with standard gradient-based methods. This computational machinery is a valuable contribution in its own right.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental validation is comprehensive, covering four synthetic benchmarks and two real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression and CNNs). The method is shown to be competitive or superior to several state-of-the-art parallel BO algorithms. The particularly strong performance in noisy settings is a key result and is consistent with the known benefits of KG-style methods, which naturally account for the variance reduction from new observations.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The methodology is presented logically, from the theoretical definition of q-KG to the practical details of its computation. The public release of the code is a commendable step that enhances reproducibility and facilitates adoption by the community.

### Weaknesses and Questions

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The main drawback of the proposed method is its computational cost. It involves Monte Carlo sampling for both the objective and gradient estimation, followed by an iterative optimization procedure (e.g., multi-start stochastic gradient ascent) at each BO step. This is likely significantly more expensive than the baselines. The paper lacks a discussion on wall-clock time or computational complexity analysis. How does the cost scale with the batch size `q`, dimension `d`, and the number of Monte Carlo samples? A characterization of this performance-computation trade-off is essential for practitioners.

2.  **Potential Issue with Gradient Estimation:** The dynamic discretization strategy, where `A_n = A_M^n ∪ x^{(1:n)} ∪ z^{(1:q)}`, is clever but raises a subtle technical concern. The set `A_n`, over which the inner minimum is computed, depends on the optimization variable `z`. This means that as `z` changes, the argmin `x*(after)` can jump between points, potentially creating discontinuities in the acquisition function that would violate the interchangeability of expectation and differentiation assumed by IPA. The authors state that differentiability holds (Sec 5.2), but it is not immediately obvious how this is guaranteed when the domain of the `min` operator is also a function of `z`. This point requires more careful justification, perhaps by showing that such discontinuities occur on a set of measure zero.

3.  **Missing Key Baseline:** The paper compares against parallel EI and UCB variants. However, a highly relevant and similarly principled competitor is Parallel Entropy Search (PES) [18]. Like q-KG, PES is a non-myopic, information-theoretic batch acquisition function. A comparison against PES would be necessary to more definitively position q-KG as the state of the art among fully-Bayesian batch BO methods.

4.  **Sensitivity to Discretization:** The performance seems dependent on the quality of the discretization `A_n`. The paper sets the number of sampled points `M` to 1000 without justification. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with `M` would strengthen the paper's claims and provide practical guidance.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and decision-theoretically sound acquisition function for batch BO.
*   Introduces a sophisticated and effective computational technique (IPA for stochastic gradients) to make the method practical.
*   Demonstrates strong and consistent empirical performance, especially in the important case of noisy evaluations.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and provides code for reproducibility.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Lacks a critical discussion of the high computational cost and its scaling properties.
*   A potential technical flaw in the gradient estimation due to the dynamic discretization scheme is not adequately addressed.
*   Fails to compare against a key competitor in the literature, Parallel Entropy Search.

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The proposed q-KG method is both theoretically elegant and empirically powerful. The development of an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy for such a complex acquisition function is a notable achievement. While there are valid concerns regarding computational cost and a subtle technical point in the gradient derivation, the strengths of the paper—its originality, technical depth, and strong results—outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the points raised above, particularly by adding a discussion of computational complexity and clarifying the validity of the IPA application.
