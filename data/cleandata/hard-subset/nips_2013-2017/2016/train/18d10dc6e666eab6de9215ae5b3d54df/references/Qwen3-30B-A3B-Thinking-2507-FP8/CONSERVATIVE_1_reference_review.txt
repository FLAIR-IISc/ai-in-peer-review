

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a new batch Bayesian optimization method called parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), extending the sequential knowledge gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors claim that q-KG outperforms existing parallel BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, GP-UCB-PE) in both synthetic and practical machine learning tuning tasks, particularly in noisy settings.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a theoretically grounded extension of knowledge gradient to batch optimization, which is a natural progression from the sequential setting. The use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute gradients of the acquisition function is a technically sound contribution. The experimental section demonstrates the method on several well-known synthetic functions and practical ML tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), and the results appear promising on many benchmarks.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising results, the paper has significant shortcomings that prevent it from being accepted at NIPS. 

First, the experimental validation is insufficiently rigorous. The authors test on only four synthetic functions and two practical ML problems, which is inadequate for claiming "consistent" performance improvements. For a paper claiming to advance the state-of-the-art in Bayesian optimization, the authors should have tested on a broader range of challenging problems. The paper also fails to provide statistical significance testing for the performance differences reported, making it impossible to assess whether the observed improvements are meaningful.

Second, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from recent work. The authors cite [25] as using gradient-based methods to optimize parallel EI, yet they don't compare against this specific approach. Their claim that "q-KG consistently finds better function values" is undermined by the fact that on Branin2 (a well-known test function), their method performs "slightly worse than the best of the other benchmarks" without any explanation. The paper also fails to address how their method differs from the nested batch knowledge gradient policy in [26], which they reference but don't compare against directly.

Third, the paper makes claims about "especially large value when function evaluations are noisy" but provides no theoretical justification for why q-KG should be particularly effective in noisy settings. The experimental results show better performance in noisy settings, but without analysis of the underlying reasons, this claim lacks substance. The paper doesn't investigate why q-KG would be more robust to noise than other batch methods.

Fourth, the paper doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method. While they claim their IPA approach is "efficient," they don't provide runtime comparisons with existing methods. For a method claiming to be practical for real-world applications, this is a significant omission.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a minor extension of knowledge gradient to batch optimization with a new computational technique. However, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this extension provides substantial improvements over existing methods that also optimize batches of points. The current experimental results are suggestive but not sufficiently rigorous to support the paper's claims.

**Comparison to Related Work:** The authors correctly cite relevant work on batch Bayesian optimization, but they fail to engage deeply with the most recent and relevant methods. For example, [2] and [15] already propose gradient-based methods to optimize parallel EI, which is conceptually similar to what the authors propose. The paper doesn't explain why their approach is better than these methods, despite claiming their method "consistently finds better function values."

**Significance Assessment:** While batch Bayesian optimization is an important problem, this paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The improvements claimed are not substantial enough to warrant publication at NIPS, especially given the lack of rigorous experimental validation. The paper would be more convincing with a broader experimental evaluation, statistical significance testing, and a clearer theoretical justification for why q-KG outperforms existing methods.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound extension of knowledge gradient to batch optimization, the experimental validation is insufficiently rigorous, the claims about performance improvements are not fully supported, and the contribution does not represent a significant advance over existing methods. The paper would require substantially more experimental validation and a clearer theoretical justification to be considered for publication at NIPS.
