Summary
This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The idea is decision-theoretic: when only one batch remains, the Bayes-optimal policy (for minimizing the posterior mean predictor’s minimum) is to choose the batch that maximizes the expected decrement in the minimum of the GP posterior mean, i.e., minx μ(n)(x) − E[minx μ(n+q)(x)]. The authors show that in the noiseless case, if the final recommendation is restricted to previously sampled points, q-KG reduces to q-EI. Because exact evaluation/optimization of q-KG is intractable, the paper introduces a Monte Carlo estimate using the standard-normal reparameterization of the posterior, and proposes an efficient stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize the batch jointly. For continuous domains, they discretize the domain adaptively by sampling from the posterior’s optima (as in entropy search) plus previously sampled and candidate points. Experiments on standard synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR-10) show that q-KG is competitive or superior to strong baselines (q-EI/MOE, Spearmint’s heuristic parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), particularly under noisy evaluations. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Extends sequential KG (Frazier et al.; Scott et al.) to batch selection. Closest related batch information-theoretic works include q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; MOE implementation with gradients), gradient-based q-EI optimization (Marmin et al.), and batch ES/PES (Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani). The paper also compares to BUCB/PE methods designed for cumulative regret (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.).
- Distinguishes from nested batch KG over discrete domains (Wang et al.) by handling continuous domains and enabling efficient gradient optimization.
- The IPA-based gradient estimation parallels the reparameterization-based estimators used for q-EI in MOE, but is adapted to the q-KG functional.

Quality
- The decision-theoretic formulation is sound and consistent with KG literature. The reduction to q-EI in the noiseless restricted-recommendation case is correctly argued.
- The stochastic reparameterization of μ(n+q)(x) and Monte Carlo estimator are standard and appropriate.
- The IPA gradient estimator is plausible, but the derivation requires careful assumptions. Two issues need clarification/correction:
  1) In Section 5.2 (finite A independent of z), the gradient of g(z, A, Z) should not include ∂μ(n)(x*(before))/∂z terms, since μ(n) does not depend on z and A is fixed; that term should be zero. If the discrete set A depends on z (as in Section 5.3), this must be stated and then the unbiasedness argument must account for a z-dependent feasible set, which typically invalidates the basic IPA interchange unless handled explicitly (e.g., treating the “before” term as a constant plus a Clarke subgradient due to changing argmin set membership).
  2) The unbiasedness of the IPA gradient relies on a.s. unique minimizers and smoothness of μ and K. Please state precise conditions (e.g., distinctness and non-degeneracy under Gaussian perturbations), and discuss the measure-zero cases where x*(after) is non-unique. Also, differentiating through the Cholesky factor should be specified carefully (triangular solves rather than explicit inverse).
- The discretization strategy is reasonable but can bias the objective and produce non-smooth dependence on z (since A_n includes z). An analysis of approximation error vs. M and sensitivity to how AM_n is constructed would strengthen the work.
- Empirics are solid and relevant. However, important experimental details are missing:
  - Computational cost: wall-clock comparisons for acquisition optimization per batch, as q and d vary.
  - Ablations: sensitivity to M, number of Monte Carlo samples, batch size q, and noise level.
  - Statistical confidence: number of runs for CIFAR-10 is small (5); include CIs/variance bands consistently across experiments.

Clarity
- Overall well organized with clear motivation and positioning. Some notational overloading around A (feasible domain vs. discretized set for evaluating the inner min) is acknowledged but still confusing; consider distinct symbols (e.g., X for the continuous domain, and S_n for the discrete support).
- Fix minor typos/grammar (e.g., “Main Stange”, “gaussian”, Matérn accents), and improve the algorithm box (specify I, N, batch size q explicitly, and initialization for z optimization).
- State the Bayes-optimality claim formally (finite vs. continuous A) and provide a proof sketch or pointer to the supplement.

Originality
- The extension of KG to the batch setting with a practical, differentiable Monte Carlo estimator and IPA-based joint optimization is novel to my knowledge.
- While q-KG is conceptually a natural generalization, making it computationally viable with gradient estimators is a meaningful contribution beyond existing q-EI and batch ES/PES work.

Significance
- Batch BO is widely used in hyperparameter tuning and simulation optimization; a robust method that outperforms q-EI and BUCB, especially under noise, is impactful.
- The released implementation and comparisons to commonly used toolkits (MOE, Spearmint, Gpoptimization) increase practical relevance.
- The paper would be stronger with runtime/scalability evidence and sensitivity analyses, but even as is, the empirical gains (particularly in noisy regimes) suggest value to practitioners.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear decision-theoretic foundation and connection to KG; principled handling of noise.
- Practical algorithm with gradient-based optimization; code released.
- Strong empirical performance on synthetic and real ML tuning tasks; consistent advantage under noise.
- Novel batch KG formulation with efficient gradient estimator.

Cons / concerns
- Technical gap/mistake in gradient derivation for finite A (spurious derivative of “before” term); unclear treatment when A depends on z via discretization.
- Lack of runtime/scalability analysis and sensitivity to discretization/MC parameters.
- Limited number of runs on some real tasks; incomplete reporting of variance/CIs.
- No theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency as M→∞, q fixed), though common in BO papers.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Clarify the gradient derivation and unbiasedness conditions; fix the derivative of the “before” term in Section 5.2, and rigorously handle z-dependent discretization in Section 5.3.
- Report wall-clock times and scaling with q, d, and M; include the number of MC samples used.
- Provide ablations on M and MC budget; show robustness to discretization choices (e.g., random Latin hypercube vs. PES-based sampling).
- Compare against batch PES (Shah & Ghahramani, 2015) and Thompson sampling batched variants if feasible.
- Discuss/illustrate performance in higher dimensions (d ≥ 10) and larger q (e.g., q=8,16).
- Add confidence intervals in all plots; increase runs for CIFAR-10 if possible.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a principled and practical extension of KG to batch BO and demonstrates consistent empirical improvements, particularly in noisy settings. Addressing the gradient derivation clarity and adding computational/sensitivity analyses would make it a strong accept. Confidence: high.
