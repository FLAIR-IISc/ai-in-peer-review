Summary
This paper proposes q-KG, an acquisition function for batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The objective is the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after observing the batch. The authors show that in the noise-free case with the final recommendation restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG is computationally demanding, they estimate the objective via Monte Carlo (“fantasies”) and optimize it using a stochastic gradient derived by infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization set A adaptively. Experiments on synthetic test functions and on hyperparameter tuning (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR10) indicate that q-KG is competitive or superior to several baselines, with larger gains under noise.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on KG for sequential BO and on batch acquisition functions such as q-EI and UCB-based policies (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE). Unlike greedy batch construction (“constant liar”), q-KG evaluates a joint objective over the entire batch. The closest prior is sequential KG and nested batch KG over discrete sets; the contribution here is a continuous-domain, batch formulation with a differentiable stochastic objective and an IPA-based gradient estimator. Parallel entropy search (PES/pES) and gradient-based q-EI are related; the latter provides joint batch selection with efficient gradients.

Strengths
- Conceptually principled: q-KG is Bayes-optimal for the surrogate objective (min posterior mean) when one batch remains, and recovers q-EI in an appropriate special case.
- A plausible and useful direction: batch selection that directly targets value of information is attractive, especially with noise.
- Computational contribution: IPA-based stochastic gradients to optimize the Monte Carlo acquisition are valuable in practice.
- Empirical evidence suggests competitive or better performance than common batch heuristics, particularly under noise.
- Code is provided (though this should be anonymized in a double-blind review).

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological/theoretical:
- The Bayes-optimality statement pertains to a surrogate objective (min of the posterior mean) with a single batch remaining; there are no regret/consistency guarantees or analysis beyond this one-step criterion. The paper does not quantify bias introduced by the discretization of A or the Monte Carlo approximations.
- The interchange of gradient and expectation for IPA requires nontrivial regularity; with a min over a set that itself depends on z (because An = AMn ∪ x(1:n) ∪ z(1:q)), non-smoothness and set-valued argmins can be problematic. The paper defers details to the supplement and does not discuss failure modes (e.g., ties, non-unique minimizers) or provide variance bounds for the gradient estimator.
- The construction of AMn is under-specified (“samples from the global optima of the posterior” referencing ES/PES); without precise description, reproducibility and the effect of this choice on performance are unclear.

Computation/scalability:
- There is no complexity analysis or practical runtime comparison. q-KG requires Cholesky factorizations, Monte Carlo with potentially many samples, and minimization over M = 1000 candidates per gradient evaluation. It is unclear how it scales with q, d, M, and the number of fantasies, or how many gradient steps are used.
- The sensitivity to M and to the number of Monte Carlo samples is not studied; these are critical knobs that trade off accuracy vs. cost.

Experimental evaluation:
- Baselines are incomplete given the literature surveyed. In particular, there is no comparison to recent efficient implementations of q-EI with exact/analytic gradients, nor to batch entropy search (pES), which also jointly considers the batch. The two EI implementations compared (MOE, Spearmint heuristic) may not reflect the strongest q-EI baselines.
- Fairness and reproducibility: different toolboxes are used for different baselines; it is unclear whether GP hyperparameters (including noise variance) are treated identically across methods (all re-optimized by MLE each batch?). No wall-clock time or CPU/GPU budget normalization is reported.
- Evaluations are confined to low/moderate dimensions (up to d=6) and batch size q=4. The approach’s behavior for larger q/d is unknown.
- No statistical tests are provided; plots of mean and standard deviation are shown but without confidence intervals across seeds or rigorous significance assessment.

Clarity/presentation:
- Several notational ambiguities and typos (e.g., “Main Stange”), and the discussion of the two roles of A could be clearer. The discretization procedure and its dependence on z need a precise, implementable description.
- Important derivations (IPA conditions, handling of non-differentiability, derivative of the Cholesky factor, choice of optimization hyperparameters) are relegated or omitted.

Quality: Sound high-level idea, but the lack of theoretical analysis beyond the one-step optimality and the absence of runtime/variance characterization reduce confidence. The evaluation is encouraging but not fully convincing due to missing strong baselines and absent computational cost reporting.

Clarity: Generally understandable, but key algorithmic details are underspecified. The paper would benefit from tightening notation and expanding the discretization and gradient-estimation sections.

Originality: Moderate. Extending KG to batch and providing an IPA-based optimizer is a reasonable contribution, though conceptually close to existing KG/q-EI frameworks.

Significance: Potentially useful in practice, especially under noise. However, the lack of strong comparative baselines and scalability analysis limits the demonstrated impact.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely define how AMn is sampled, and study sensitivity to M. What are the number of Monte Carlo samples and gradient steps used, and what is the acquisition optimization runtime relative to baselines?
- Provide variance estimates for the stochastic gradient and discuss handling of non-unique minimizers.
- Compare against strong q-EI implementations (analytic/gradient-based) and batch PES; include wall-clock comparisons.
- Evaluate for larger q and higher d; include ablations (effect of noise misspecification, kernel misspecification).

Pros
- Principled batch acquisition derived from KG.
- Handles noisy evaluations naturally.
- Empirically promising on several tasks.
- Provides a differentiable, optimizable objective and public code.

Cons
- Insufficient theoretical and computational analysis of the proposed estimator and optimizer.
- Underspecified discretization; potential bias and non-smoothness issues.
- Missing strong baselines and no runtime comparisons.
- Limited scale of experiments; some clarity issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is appealing and likely valuable, but the paper, as written, lacks rigorous analysis and sufficiently strong, fair empirical validation to justify acceptance at this time. I would reconsider with stronger baselines, detailed computational analysis, and clearer methodological exposition.
