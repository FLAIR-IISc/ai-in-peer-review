Summary
This paper introduces q-KG, a batch/parallel extension of the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization (BO). The authors derive q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective as the Bayes-optimal batch policy for minimizing the posterior mean’s minimum when only one batch remains. They show that in the noiseless case with the final recommendation restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI, providing a unifying perspective. Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG is challenging, they develop an efficient stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), which enables joint optimization of the full batch rather than greedy construction. For continuous domains, they approximate q-KG via discretization with a dynamically updated candidate set informed by samples from the posterior’s distribution over optima. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10) show consistent gains over parallel EI variants and UCB-based batch methods, with particularly strong improvements under observation noise. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Extends classic KG (Frazier et al.; Scott et al.) from sequential to batch settings.
- Connects to and generalizes q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.) by recovering EI under noiseless constraints.
- Complements UCB-based batch methods (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) and entropy-based batch methods (PPES), with a different value-of-information objective.
- The main algorithmic novelty is an efficient IPA-based stochastic gradient for optimizing a collective batch KG objective, plus a practical discretization strategy for continuous domains.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a principled, Bayes-optimal one-step batch VOI criterion that subsumes parallel EI in a special case.
- Practical optimization: an elegant IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator makes direct, non-greedy batch selection feasible.
- Empirical performance: strong results across synthetic and real tasks, especially under noise where VOI-style criteria often shine.
- Reproducibility: code available; clear description of GP modeling and hyperparameter treatment; reasonable experimental protocol.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Discretization dependency on z(1:q): A_n includes the candidate batch z and is regenerated each iteration. This induces an implicit dependence of the objective on z via the discrete set, which is ignored in the gradient. The resulting stochastic gradient may be biased relative to the continuous objective; more discussion or bounds on this approximation error would improve confidence.
- Limited baselines: Missing comparisons to parallel entropy-based methods (e.g., PPES) and batch Thompson sampling, which are natural competitors for noisy, parallel BO. Including these would strengthen claims of superiority.
- Computational characterization: No wall-clock/runtime, scaling, or complexity breakdown (cost vs q, |A_n|, MC samples). Given that the method relies on MC and repeated Cholesky factorizations and min-operations over A_n, explicit timing and scaling would be valuable.
- Theoretical development: Bayes-optimality is one-step; longer-horizon or asymptotic guarantees (even consistency under standard GP assumptions) are not discussed. While not strictly necessary, a brief discussion would situate the method versus bandit-style regret guarantees.
- Differentiability/min-operator: The gradient through the min over A_n is delicate (ties, non-smoothness). The paper asserts unbiasedness under smoothness assumptions; clearer statements of conditions and practical handling of ties would help.
- Hyperparameters: Re-estimation by MLE per batch is standard but can introduce myopic behavior; a brief ablation (fixed vs refit vs fully Bayesian) would be informative.
- Clarity/minor: Some typos, and Section 5.3’s description of drawing candidates from the “global optima of the posterior” is underspecified; is this PES-style sampling of x_* or Thompson sampling over functions with local optimization?

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a solid decision-theoretic objective and a credible stochastic gradient estimator. The main caveats are the discretization-induced approximation and missing runtime analysis.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable, with helpful links between q-KG and existing acquisition functions. Section 5.3 needs more detail on sampling A_n. A few typos can be fixed easily.

Originality
High. Extending KG to parallel selection with an efficient gradient estimator is a meaningful conceptual and algorithmic advance beyond existing q-EI and UCB-based batch methods.

Significance
High. Batch BO is practically important, and a VOI-based method that excels in noisy settings is valuable. The unification with EI and strong empirical results suggest impact for both researchers and practitioners.

Questions for the authors
- How is A_n constructed exactly? Do you sample x_* via Thompson sampling or PES-style posterior over optima? How many samples, and how are they optimized?
- How many MC samples are used for q-KG and its gradient? What is the variance of the estimator, and are variance-reduction techniques used?
- How does runtime scale with q, |A_n|, and MC samples? Please include wall-clock comparisons to q-EI/PPES.
- How sensitive is performance to M=1000 and to the discretization scheme? Any ablation?
- Do you handle asynchronous batch settings? If not, how might q-KG be adapted?
- How do you handle ties and non-smoothness in the min operator when computing gradients?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add PPES and batch Thompson sampling baselines; include wall-clock measurements and scaling studies.
- Clarify and ablate the discretization scheme; discuss the effect of including z(1:q) in A_n on bias/gradients.
- Provide pseudocode for the gradient estimator and details on step sizes/optimization.
- Discuss extensions to heteroscedastic noise and asynchronous scheduling.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a novel and principled batch VOI acquisition with an efficient optimization scheme and demonstrates clear empirical gains, especially under noise. While some computational and theoretical details could be strengthened, the contribution opens a promising direction and is likely to influence future work and practice.

Confidence
High.
