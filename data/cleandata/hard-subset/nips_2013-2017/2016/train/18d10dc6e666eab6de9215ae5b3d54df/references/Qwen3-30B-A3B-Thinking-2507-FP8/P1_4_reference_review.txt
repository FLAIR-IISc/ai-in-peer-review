

# Peer Review: "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization (BO) method called "Parallel Knowledge Gradient" (q-KG), which extends the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors derive q-KG as the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor after evaluating a batch of q points, rather than sequentially. The key innovation is a computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate the gradient of q-KG, making it feasible to optimize even for moderately large q. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that q-KG outperforms state-of-the-art batch BO methods, particularly in noisy settings, on both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks.

The work builds upon the sequential KG method [17] and extends it to the parallel setting, distinguishing itself from previous parallel approaches like parallel EI [2, 19, 25] and parallel UCB [3, 5] that construct batches iteratively rather than optimizing the batch collectively. The paper also relates to recent work on parallel ES [18] and gradient-based parallel EI optimization [15, 25].

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation and Novelty**: The paper provides a sound theoretical derivation of the q-KG acquisition function as a decision-theoretic extension of sequential KG to the parallel setting. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous parallel methods that optimize the batch incrementally rather than jointly. This is a significant theoretical contribution to the batch BO literature.

2. **Computational Innovation**: The use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute the gradient of q-KG is a clever and well-justified computational technique. This addresses a major practical barrier to implementing the q-KG acquisition function, which would otherwise be prohibitively expensive to evaluate.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The paper includes thorough experiments on both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical machine learning tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show q-KG outperforms or matches state-of-the-art methods, with particularly strong advantages in noisy settings.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The paper demonstrates clear practical value by applying the method to real hyperparameter tuning problems in machine learning, where batch evaluation is common due to parallel computing resources. The improvements in test error rates (e.g., from 14% to 11% on CIFAR10 CNN) are meaningful.

5. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, with clear mathematical notation, well-structured sections, and effective figures that communicate the experimental results. The algorithm description is precise and reproducible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper presents an efficient method for optimizing q-KG, it lacks a thorough analysis of how computational complexity scales with q (the batch size). The authors note that "naively maximizing q-KG would be extremely computationally intensive," but don't provide concrete analysis of how their IPA-based method scales with q, which is crucial for understanding the method's practical limits.

2. **High-Dimensional Scalability**: The experiments are limited to problems with dimensions up to 6 (Hartmann6), which is relatively low for modern machine learning applications. The paper doesn't address how the method scales to high-dimensional problems (d > 10), which is a significant practical limitation for real-world BO applications.

3. **Comparison with Recent Work**: While the paper cites relevant parallel BO methods, it could better contrast with the most recent approaches. For instance, the paper references [18] on parallel ES but doesn't explicitly compare against their method, which is a state-of-the-art parallel acquisition function. The authors should clarify how q-KG differs from and improves upon this recent work.

4. **Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper provides strong empirical results but lacks theoretical convergence guarantees for the q-KG method in the batch setting. While the sequential KG has known theoretical properties, the paper doesn't extend these to the parallel case.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to batch Bayesian optimization by providing a theoretically grounded acquisition function that optimizes the batch collectively rather than incrementally. The computational approach using IPA is novel and well-executed, addressing a major practical barrier to implementing the q-KG acquisition function. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods, especially in noisy settings, which is particularly relevant for real-world applications.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The main weaknesses are the limited analysis of computational complexity with respect to batch size q, the lack of high-dimensional experiments, and the incomplete comparison with some recent parallel BO methods.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions.

## Pro/Con for Acceptance

**Pros:**
- Novel theoretical contribution to batch Bayesian optimization
- Efficient computational method using IPA for gradient estimation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing clear advantages over state-of-the-art
- Strong practical relevance to machine learning hyperparameter tuning
- Well-written with clear mathematical presentation

**Cons:**
- Limited analysis of computational complexity with respect to batch size q
- Experiments limited to low-dimensional problems (d ≤ 6)
- Incomplete comparison with some recent parallel BO methods
- Lack of theoretical convergence guarantees for batch setting

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a section discussing how computational complexity scales with q, possibly with some complexity analysis or empirical measurements.

2. Extend the experiments to include higher-dimensional problems (d > 10) to better demonstrate the method's scalability.

3. Add a more detailed comparison with recent parallel BO methods like the parallel ES approach in [18], highlighting the specific advantages of q-KG.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of theoretical properties of the q-KG method, even if only in the context of how it extends the known properties of sequential KG.

5. Clarify the practical limitations of the method in terms of computational resources required for larger batch sizes.

This paper represents a solid contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature with clear practical value. The proposed method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated, making it an excellent fit for NIPS. The suggested revisions are relatively minor and would strengthen an already strong paper.
