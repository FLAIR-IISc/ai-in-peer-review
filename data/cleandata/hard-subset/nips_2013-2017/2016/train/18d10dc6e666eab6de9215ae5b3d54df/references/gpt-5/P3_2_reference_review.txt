Summary
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to parallel evaluations. q-KG selects a batch z(1:q) by maximizing the expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean after observing q new evaluations. The authors show that q-KG coincides with parallel EI in the noiseless case if the final recommendation is restricted to previously sampled points, while retaining KG’s principled handling of noise and allowing the final recommendation to be any point in A. Because exact evaluation and optimization of q-KG are intractable, the paper (i) expresses the posterior mean after a batch via a reparameterization with a standard normal Zq, (ii) evaluates q-KG by Monte Carlo, and (iii) derives an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling efficient gradient-based optimization of the batch. For continuous domains, the inner min over A is approximated by a data-dependent discretization that samples putative global minimizers of the posterior (as in ES/PPES). Empirically, q-KG outperforms parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on standard synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning tasks, with particularly strong gains under observational noise.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The decision-theoretic objective (expected reduction in posterior mean minimum) is well-motivated and Bayes-optimal for one remaining batch (for a given GP posterior), generalizing the classical KG. The reparameterization with Zq is standard and enables Monte Carlo evaluation. The IPA-based gradient estimator is plausible; the interchange of expectation and differentiation under a min operator requires care (uniqueness of argmin almost surely, differentiability of kernel/mean), which the authors defer to the supplement. Please ensure these regularity conditions and measure-zero tie events are precisely stated, and justify differentiation through the Cholesky factor (or cite a standard result).
- Algorithmic details: The paper provides the key ingredients (MC estimator, gradient via IPA, multi-start stochastic ascent), but is light on optimization details: batch initialization, step sizes, stopping tolerance, and the number of MC samples used for value/gradient. A complexity discussion would help (per-iteration cost in terms of q, M, N_MC), including the cost of constructing K(n)(z, z) and K(n)(x, z) over the discretization.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are broad and relevant (q=4), include both noiseless and noisy settings, and leverage widely used baselines. The gains under noise are convincing and in line with KG’s strengths. However, results would be stronger with: (i) wall-clock comparisons including acquisition optimization time, (ii) sensitivity to q (e.g., q=1, 4, 8), (iii) ablations on M and the MC budget, (iv) robustness to hyperparameter learning (e.g., integrating over GP hyperparameters vs. MLE).

Clarity
- The presentation is mostly clear and well-organized. The definition in Eq. (4.1) could be notationally tightened: En[·] already conditions on current data; “| y(z(1:q))” inside the expectation is confusing—clarify that the expectation is over the predictive distribution of y(z(1:q)).
- The distinction between the two roles of A (the inner minimization domain vs. the feasible set for z) is helpful; emphasize earlier that the inner A is discretized to An and how AMn is produced. The phrase “draw M samples from the global optima of the posterior” is vague; explicitly describe the sampling scheme (e.g., function draws from the GP posterior and argmin extraction as in ES/PPES).
- Minor: correct typos (e.g., “Main Stange”), and ensure consistent notation for σ̃n and D(n).

Originality
- Extends KG to the parallel setting with a principled objective and provides a practical stochastic gradient method to jointly optimize the batch. While other batch methods jointly optimize q-EI (e.g., Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.) or entropy-based criteria (PPES), q-KG is a distinct, decision-theoretic criterion that naturally handles noise and does not constrain the final recommendation to be among sampled points. To my knowledge, a gradient-based optimization of batch KG over continuous spaces is novel relative to nested-batch KG on finite sets.

Significance
- Batch BO is widely used; a robust, noise-aware batch acquisition that can be optimized efficiently is valuable. The empirical gains under noise and on practical hyperparameter tuning indicate potential impact. Provided the computational overhead is manageable relative to evaluation cost, this could see adoption.

Pros
- Principled extension of KG to batch setting; Bayes-optimal for one remaining batch.
- Handles noisy evaluations naturally; strong empirical gains in noisy scenarios.
- Practical stochastic gradient estimator via IPA and reparameterization.
- Joint batch optimization rather than greedy construction.
- Code release and use of established toolkits.

Cons
- Theoretical justification of IPA under the min operator and Cholesky differentiation is deferred; clearer assumptions/proofs are needed.
- Discretization of A is crucial but underspecified; no study of its effect on performance.
- Missing comparisons: PPES/PPES variants and parallel Thompson sampling; recent local penalization methods for batch BO (e.g., Gonzalez et al., 2016) would be relevant.
- Limited analysis of computational cost and wall-clock overhead; only q=4 is shown.
- Hyperparameter uncertainty is ignored (MLE only); no study of its impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concise theorem (main paper) stating conditions under which the IPA gradient is unbiased, including uniqueness-of-argmin arguments and a citation for differentiating through Cholesky.
- Detail the discretization procedure (how AMn is sampled), and include an ablation on M and its runtime/accuracy trade-off.
- Report wall-clock time per iteration and the fraction spent optimizing the acquisition; include scalability with q and n.
- Add comparisons to PPES and parallel Thompson sampling; if infeasible, discuss expected behavior relative to these methods.
- Evaluate sensitivity to GP hyperparameter learning (e.g., MAP vs. MCMC marginalization) and to heteroskedastic noise.
- Clarify optimization details (initializations, learning rates, MC samples) to aid reproducibility.

Recommendation
This is a solid and timely contribution with a principled objective, a credible optimization scheme, and strong empirical performance, especially under noise. With clearer theoretical justification and more thorough experimental characterization (runtime, discretization, broader baselines), it would be a strong accept. As it stands, I lean toward accept, contingent on addressing clarity and comparison concerns. Confidence: high.
