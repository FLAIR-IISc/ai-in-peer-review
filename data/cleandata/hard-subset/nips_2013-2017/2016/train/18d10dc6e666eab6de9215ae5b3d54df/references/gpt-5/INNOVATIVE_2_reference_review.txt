Summary
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The key idea is decision-theoretic: choose the batch that maximizes the expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean after one additional batch, i.e., minx µ(n)(x) − E[minx µ(n+q)(x)]. This is Bayes-optimal in the one-step setting for minimizing the posterior minimizer, without restricting the final recommendation to lie among sampled points. Computing and optimizing q-KG is challenging; the authors provide a Monte Carlo estimator via a reparameterization µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z(1:q))Zq, and derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch. A discretization strategy for the inner min over x is used when A is continuous. Empirical results on standard synthetic benchmarks and two ML hyperparameter-tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10) show consistent gains over parallel EI (exact and heuristic) and UCB-based batch methods, with a notably larger advantage in noisy settings. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated within BO: sequential acquisition functions (EI, UCB, ES, KG) and batch extensions (parallel EI via fantasies/constant liar, q-EI with gradients, GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE, PPES). It builds on the KG literature by extending to batches with a collective objective and shows the noise-free case reduces to q-EI when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points. The IPA-based gradient optimization is a nontrivial computational contribution beyond prior KG and q-EI work. I encourage citing batch BO via local penalization (e.g., González et al., 2016) for completeness.

Strengths
- Novelty/significance: A principled decision-theoretic batch acquisition that is Bayes-optimal in the one-step setting is a meaningful conceptual advance beyond greedy “fantasization” heuristics. The reduction to q-EI in the noiseless/“recommend-from-sampled” regime elegantly unifies lines of work.
- Methodological contribution: The IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient for q-KG under reparameterization is clever and practically important—enabling joint optimization over the batch rather than greedy construction.
- Empirical performance: Strong, consistent improvements across synthetic tests and two real ML tuning tasks, particularly under noise where KG’s information-use is advantageous. The noisy experiments are a valuable stress test and strengthen the narrative.
- Clarity of core ideas: The formulation, the reparameterization, and the high-level algorithm are clearly presented; the discretization vs. search domain distinction is helpful. Released code increases impact.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond the one-step Bayes-optimality, there are no consistency/regret guarantees or analysis of the discretization error introduced for continuous A. While this is common in BO, a brief discussion or bounds on the bias due to discretization would add rigor.
- Assumptions for IPA: The gradient interchange and unbiasedness require differentiability and (almost sure) uniqueness/stability of the argmin before/after sampling, despite the nonsmooth min operator and Cholesky dependencies. Please state precise conditions in the main text and summarize the key lemmas from the supplement. What happens at ties or near-degenerate posteriors?
- Computational complexity and scaling: The paper lacks a clear complexity/runtime analysis. How does cost scale with q, |An|, number of MC samples, and dimension? Provide wall-clock comparisons vs. q-EI and PPES for q ∈ {2,4,8,16}, and report gradient variance/sample efficiency. Derivatives through the Cholesky factor can be expensive; any use of shared factorizations or low-rank updates?
- Sensitivity to discretization: The choice M=1000 is fixed; how sensitive are results to M and to the AMn construction (number of fantasy samples, diversity)? Does q-KG degrade if the discretization misses narrow basins, especially in higher dimensions?
- Fairness of comparisons: Ensure all baselines use the same kernel (ARD Matérn 5/2), hyperparameter learning schedule (MLE each iteration), and evaluation budgets. The text implies reuse of third-party implementations; please confirm settings are harmonized.
- Scope/limitations: Experiments are up to 6D synthetic and 8 hyperparameters for CNN. Discuss expected behavior in higher dimension (e.g., d≥15), where discretization and posterior mean minimization may be brittle. Also comment on misspecified noise models and heteroskedasticity.

Minor comments
- 4.1: “We can compute the probability distribution…”—in practice you use MC via the reparameterization; consider rephrasing.
- Typos: “Main Stange” → “Main Stage”; “Matérn” diacritics; a few punctuation/spacing issues in equations.
- It would help to explicitly connect the fantasy-based view to your reparameterization and contrast with parallel EI’s fantasies.

Reproducibility
- Code release is a plus. Please include default MC sample counts, step-size and stopping criteria for the stochastic optimizer, and seeds/common random numbers to reduce variance.

Reasons to accept
- Clear, principled, and genuinely novel extension of KG to the batch setting with a practical optimization scheme.
- Strong empirical gains, especially under noise—a practically relevant regime for ML model tuning.
- Bridges a conceptual gap between KG and q-EI, offering a unifying perspective.

Reasons to reject
- Limited theoretical analysis beyond one-step optimality; reliance on discretization without error characterization.
- Missing runtime/scaling study; practicality at larger q/d not demonstrated.
- Some ambiguity in fairness of baseline configurations.

Overall evaluation: Accept (8/10)
Confidence: 4/5
