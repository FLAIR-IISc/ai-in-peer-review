

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces a novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization by extending the knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting. The authors propose a new acquisition function called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that optimizes a batch of q points collectively rather than sequentially, which is a significant departure from existing parallel BO methods. The key insight is that the q-KG acquisition function measures the information gain of evaluating q points simultaneously, derived from a decision-theoretic analysis that chooses the optimal batch of points to evaluate next when only one batch remains.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) a theoretically grounded acquisition function for batch BO that optimizes the batch collectively; (2) an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of q-KG; and (3) thorough empirical validation showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper's strengths are substantial. First, the novelty is clear and significant - while previous work on parallel BO either iteratively constructs batches or extends single-point acquisition functions to the batch setting, this paper fundamentally changes the approach by optimizing the batch as a whole. This represents a meaningful conceptual advance in Bayesian optimization. Second, the theoretical foundation is solid, building on the established knowledge gradient method while extending it to the parallel setting with a decision-theoretic justification. Third, the empirical results are convincing, demonstrating consistent improvements across multiple synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, with particularly strong performance in noisy settings where other methods struggle.

The paper also excels in clarity and organization. The mathematical formulations are well-presented, the algorithm is clearly described, and the experimental setup is thorough and well-justified. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from related work, particularly highlighting how their method differs from parallel EI and UCB extensions.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses, they are largely outweighed by its conceptual novelty. The computational approach using IPA is efficient but doesn't provide theoretical complexity guarantees for large q. The paper doesn't analyze regret bounds for the proposed method, which is typical for acquisition function-based BO methods but would have strengthened the theoretical foundation. The experiments are limited to low-dimensional problems (max dimension 6), which is understandable given the computational challenges of batch BO, but would be strengthened by higher-dimensional examples.

The significance of this work is considerable. Batch Bayesian optimization is increasingly important as many optimization problems (like hyperparameter tuning in machine learning) can evaluate multiple points in parallel. The paper demonstrates that q-KG provides a meaningful improvement over existing methods, especially in practical scenarios with noisy evaluations. This represents a substantial advance over incremental improvements in the field.

The paper's novelty and conceptual contribution clearly outweigh its minor methodological limitations. In the context of NIPS, where novel approaches are highly valued, this paper offers a bold new direction in batch Bayesian optimization. It's not merely an incremental improvement but a fundamentally different approach to the problem, which aligns perfectly with the conference's emphasis on innovative ideas.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field. While it doesn't solve all problems in batch BO, it provides a significant new tool that practitioners and researchers can build upon. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the value of the approach across diverse problem domains.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The parallel knowledge gradient method represents a meaningful conceptual advance in batch Bayesian optimization that is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field, and their approach is likely to influence future research and applications in Bayesian optimization.
