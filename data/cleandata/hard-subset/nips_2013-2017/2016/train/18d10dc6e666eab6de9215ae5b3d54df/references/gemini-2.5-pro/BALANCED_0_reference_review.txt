**Summary**

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel approach for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO). The goal is to select a batch of `q` points to evaluate in parallel to find the minimum of an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function. The proposed acquisition function, q-KG, is derived from a decision-theoretic framework. It quantifies the expected improvement in the final recommended solution (the minimum of the posterior mean) after observing the outcomes of the `q` parallel evaluations. This approach generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) method, which is known to be Bayes-optimal for a single remaining evaluation.

A key challenge is that naively optimizing the q-KG acquisition function is computationally intractable. The authors' main technical contribution is an efficient gradient-based optimization scheme. They derive an unbiased estimator for the gradient of the q-KG factor using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), which allows for the use of standard stochastic gradient methods. To handle continuous domains, the method relies on a dynamic discretization strategy based on sampling from the posterior distribution of the function's global optimum. The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation on synthetic benchmark functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), comparing q-KG against several state-of-the-art parallel BO methods. The results demonstrate that q-KG is competitive or superior to existing methods, with particularly strong performance in noisy environments.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Novel Approach:** The paper presents a well-motivated and principled extension of the powerful Knowledge Gradient policy to the batch setting. The decision-theoretic framing—optimizing for the expected value of the final solution after one more batch—is elegant and provides a clear theoretical foundation. This is a significant contribution to the BO literature, moving beyond heuristic or greedy constructions for batch selection.

2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The development of an efficient, unbiased gradient estimator via IPA is a crucial and non-trivial contribution. This is what makes the q-KG method practical. Without this, optimizing the Monte Carlo-based acquisition function would be prohibitively expensive, relegating the idea to a purely theoretical concept.

3.  **High-Quality Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing.
    *   The authors compare against a strong set of relevant baselines, including different parallel EI and UCB variants implemented in well-known BO libraries.
    *   The experiments cover a good range of problems, including standard synthetic functions of varying dimensionality and two practical, real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks.
    *   The explicit focus on both noise-free and noisy settings is a major strength. The demonstration that q-KG's advantage is particularly pronounced in the presence of noise is an important and impactful finding, as many real-world optimization problems are inherently noisy.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established, the methodology is explained in a logical progression from the definition of q-KG to its computation and optimization, and the related work is appropriately discussed. The inclusion of a link to the source code is commendable and enhances the paper's potential impact and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas where further clarification or analysis would be beneficial:

1.  **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper claims the method is "computationally efficient" but provides no quantitative analysis of its computational cost relative to the benchmarks. The proposed method involves Monte Carlo sampling to estimate the acquisition function and its gradient, followed by a gradient-based optimization procedure at each BO iteration. This seems likely to be more expensive than methods like GP-BUCB or heuristic parallel EI. A discussion of the computational complexity (e.g., in terms of `q`, `n`, and the number of Monte Carlo samples) and/or empirical wall-clock time comparisons would significantly strengthen the paper and provide practitioners with a clearer understanding of the trade-offs involved.

2.  **Sensitivity to Discretization:** The method's performance for continuous domains relies on the quality of the domain discretization `An`. The chosen strategy (sampling `M` points from the posterior over the global optima) is reasonable, but the paper would benefit from a discussion on the sensitivity of q-KG to the size of this set, `M`. The experiments use `M=1000`; how would performance or cost change with a smaller or larger `M`? Is there a risk of the method failing if this discrete set provides a poor approximation of the continuous domain, especially in high-dimensional problems?

3.  **Scalability with Batch Size `q`:** The experiments are conducted with a fixed batch size of `q=4`. While this is a practical choice, the scalability of the method to larger batch sizes is an important practical question for users with access to massively parallel resources. The cost of the Cholesky decomposition and the dimensionality of the Monte Carlo integral both depend on `q`. An experiment or discussion on how performance and computational cost scale as `q` increases (e.g., to `q=8` or `q=16`) would make the contribution more complete.

4.  **GP Hyperparameter Fitting:** In Algorithm 1, the GP hyperparameters are re-trained via MLE in every iteration. This can be the most time-consuming part of a BO loop, especially as the number of observations `n` grows. It would be helpful to clarify if this is done at every single iteration or less frequently, and to briefly comment on its contribution to the overall runtime.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is theoretically well-grounded, technically sound, and empirically shown to be a state-of-the-art method for batch BO, particularly in noisy settings. The strengths—a principled new acquisition function, an enabling technical contribution for its optimization, and a thorough experimental validation—far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily centered on the need for more detailed analysis of computational costs and scalability. The paper is well-written and will likely be of high interest to both researchers and practitioners in the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
