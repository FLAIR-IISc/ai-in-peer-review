Summary
This paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. q-KG is defined as the expected decrease in the posterior GP predictor’s minimum after a batch of q evaluations. Unlike EI-based methods, KG does not constrain the final recommendation to be among sampled points, and naturally handles noisy observations. Because exact evaluation/optimization of q-KG is intractable, the paper proposes: (i) a Monte Carlo estimator of q-KG using a linear-Gaussian update representation; (ii) an infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA)-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator to enable gradient-based joint optimization over the q points; and (iii) a practical discretization of the domain used only for evaluating the inner min over A, built adaptively from samples of likely minima of the GP (as in entropy search), augmented with previously sampled points and the current batch. Empirically, q-KG outperforms or matches parallel EI (both exact and heuristic variants), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on standard synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10), with especially strong gains under noise.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on the classical KG literature (Frazier et al., Scott et al.) and contrasts with batch extensions of EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger, Marmin et al.) and ES/PES (Hernández-Lobato et al., Shah & Ghahramani), as well as UCB-based batch policies (Desautels et al., Contal et al.). Its key conceptual difference from q-EI is the one-step Bayes-optimality (for minimizing the posterior mean minimum) when a single batch remains, and its robustness to observation noise. Compared to prior batch KG work on finite sets (e.g., nested-batch KG in materials science), this paper targets continuous domains via adaptive discretization and develops a scalable gradient estimator for joint batch optimization.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: A principled extension of KG to the parallel setting, with a clean decision-theoretic objective. The reduction to q-EI in the noiseless, recommend-from-evaluations setting is elegant and clarifies relationships among methods.
- Technical contribution: The IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient for q-KG is a practical and nontrivial contribution that enables joint optimization over q points without resorting to greedy construction. The adaptive discretization of A focuses computation where it matters.
- Empirical performance: Strong results across synthetic and real tasks, particularly in the noisy setting where KG is expected to shine. The methodology appears robust and consistently competitive or superior.
- Practicality: Implementation details and code availability increase the likelihood of adoption.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond the one-batch Bayes-optimality (which is immediate from the objective), there are no convergence or regret guarantees for the multi-iteration setting with adaptive discretization and estimated hyperparameters. Some discussion or a sketch of conditions under which discretization error does not dominate would strengthen the work.
- Discretization details: The paper relies on sampling likely minimizers to form An each iteration. More specifics are needed: how are samples drawn; how is M chosen; sensitivity to M; and how performance degrades with smaller M. An ablation on M would be informative.
- Differentiability through argmin: The IPA gradient uses argmin selections “before” and “after.” The supplement presumably addresses regularity assumptions, but a brief discussion in the main text about almost-sure differentiability and ties/nondifferentiability would improve clarity.
- Computational complexity and scalability: A complexity analysis per acquisition/gradient evaluation as a function of n, q, d, and M is missing. Runtime comparisons versus q-EI/ES on higher-dimensional problems and larger q would help assess scalability.
- Fairness of comparisons: Several baselines are taken from different toolkits. Please confirm that all methods re-fit GP hyperparameters in the same way and that acquisition optimizers receive comparable budgets. Also report the wall-clock overhead of q-KG.
- Scope of experiments: Batch size fixed at q = 4. Showing performance across q and for asynchronous settings (common in practice) would broaden impact. The CNN result is based on 5 runs; more repetitions or confidence intervals would increase credibility. How are discrete variables (e.g., kernel size, channels) handled within the GP?
- Hyperparameter uncertainty: The use of MLE point estimates (especially under noise) may understate uncertainty. Briefly discussing or experimenting with marginalization (as in Snoek et al. 2012) would be valuable.

Quality
Technically solid overall, with a sound objective and a plausible gradient estimator. Some proofs and estimator properties are deferred to the supplement; more main-text guidance on assumptions and complexity would help.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. A few typos and minor notational glitches exist. The algorithmic description is understandable, but more detail on discretization and optimization settings would aid reproducibility.

Originality
High. Extends KG to parallel BO with a principled objective and a new gradient estimator. Distinct from q-EI/q-ES and prior finite-set batch KG.

Significance
High practical significance. Batch BO under noise is important, and the method shows consistent gains. Likely to influence subsequent work and practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, novel batch acquisition extending KG; clear conceptual advance.
- Practical stochastic gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical results, especially under noise.
- Code availability and implementation details.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited theory beyond one-step optimality; no regret or convergence results.
- Heavy reliance on discretization with limited analysis of its impact.
- Incomplete clarity on computational cost and fairness of baselines.
- Narrow exploration of batch sizes and asynchronous settings.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a bold and principled direction for batch BO with compelling empirical evidence, and its novelty outweighs the manageable methodological gaps. Confidence: high.
