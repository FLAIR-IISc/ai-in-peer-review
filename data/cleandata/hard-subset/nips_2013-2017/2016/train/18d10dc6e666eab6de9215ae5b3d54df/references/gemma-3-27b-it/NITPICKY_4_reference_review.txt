This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), aiming to improve upon existing parallel BO algorithms. The core idea is to formulate a decision-theoretic approach for selecting a batch of points, maximizing the expected incremental value of evaluating those points, extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient policy to the parallel setting. The authors also present a computationally efficient method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize the q-KG acquisition function. The paper is well-motivated, addressing the important challenge of efficient parallel Bayesian optimization, particularly in scenarios with noisy function evaluations.

However, despite the novelty of the approach and promising experimental results, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor (but accumulating) issues that detract from its overall quality. These span from imprecise language and missing details in the method description to inconsistencies in notation and formatting. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The q-KG approach offers a principled, decision-theoretic framework for parallel BO, extending the well-established Knowledge Gradient method.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experiments demonstrate consistently superior performance compared to existing parallel BO algorithms, especially in noisy settings, on both synthetic and practical problems.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed IPA-based gradient estimation is a crucial contribution, enabling efficient optimization of the otherwise computationally expensive q-KG acquisition function.
*   **Code Availability:** Providing the code is commendable and facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor in Method Description:** The transition between equations (4.1) and (5.1) feels abrupt. The explanation of *why* µ(n+q)(x) can be expressed as in (5.1) relies on the reader already possessing significant familiarity with Gaussian process theory and might not be clear to those less specialized. The assumption of normally distributed errors is stated upfront but then appears implicitly within the derivations without explicit re-statement.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The notation used for sets of points is inconsistent.  Sometimes z(1:q) is used, other times z∗(1:q). This is distracting and indicates a lack of meticulous attention to detail.  A is also used with multiple meanings in (4.1) and (5.4).
*   **Missing Details:** The discretization strategy described in Section 5.3 ("drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior distribution") lacks sufficient detail. How are these "global optima" identified? What criteria are used? The impact of M on performance is not explored.
*   **Imprecise Language:** Phrases like "q-KG makes dramatic progress in early iterations" (Section 6.1.1) are vague and lack quantitative support. Similarly, statements such as "q-KG is making better (more aggressive) progress" (Section 6.2.2) are subjective and should be replaced with concrete observations from the figures.
*   **Formatting Issues:** The reference list includes entries that are not correctly formatted. For instance, the reference "[1]" is simply "Optimization test problems. http://www.sfu.ca/~ssurjano/optimization". There is no author, title, or conference/journal information. Also, several references are listed as ", , ," instead of proper information.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim that q-KG "consistently finds better function values" (abstract) is a strong statement that isn't entirely borne out by the experimental results, which show superiority but not absolute consistency across all test functions and settings.
*    **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the IPA method is presented as efficient, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of q-KG compared to the benchmark algorithms would be valuable.  Specifically, how does the computational time scale with q and the size of the discretization set?
* **Minor Typo:** Equation (3.1) uses `diag{σ2(x(1)), · · · , σ2(x(n))}` which should be `diag([σ2(x(1)), · · · , σ2(x(n)])`.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   The proposed method demonstrates promising empirical results.
*   The IPA-based gradient estimation is a valuable contribution.

**Con:**

*   The paper lacks the polish expected of a NIPS publication due to the numerous minor flaws detailed above.
*   The method description lacks sufficient rigor and detail.
*   The notational inconsistencies and formatting errors detract from the paper's credibility.
*   The claims of superiority are sometimes overstated.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the q-KG method presents a valuable contribution to the field of parallel Bayesian optimization, the numerous minor flaws and lack of meticulousness raise serious concerns.  I believe that addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper. As it stands, I **recommend rejection**. The paper's potential is undeniable, but its current state does not meet the high standards of quality and clarity expected at NIPS. I encourage the authors to revise and resubmit after carefully addressing the points raised in this review. The core idea is strong, but the execution needs substantial refinement.
