## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a strong contribution to the field of parallel Bayesian Optimization. The proposed Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrates compelling empirical performance, particularly in noisy evaluation settings. The authors clearly identify a gap in existing parallel BO methods – the lack of collective batch consideration – and address it effectively. While the computational complexity is a concern, the developed infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for gradient estimation is a significant achievement. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of efficiently optimizing expensive black-box functions using Bayesian Optimization in a parallel setting. Existing parallel BO methods often rely on greedy approaches, optimizing single-point acquisition functions iteratively. This work proposes q-KG, a novel acquisition function that leverages a decision-theoretic analysis to determine the optimal batch of points to evaluate, considering the information gain of the entire batch. The method builds upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) approach, extending it to the parallel case and developing efficient gradient estimation techniques using IPA.

The related work section is comprehensive and well-structured. It accurately positions q-KG within the landscape of existing parallel BO algorithms, highlighting the limitations of iterative greedy approaches (parallel EI, UCB variants) and acknowledging related collective batch optimization efforts (parallel EI with closed-form, gradient-based methods). The authors effectively articulate how q-KG differentiates itself through its theoretical foundation in KG and the efficiency of its gradient computation.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is a major strength.  The connection to the KG method and the optimality guarantee for a single remaining batch are well-explained. 
* **Novelty:**  The core idea of a collective batch acquisition function, directly maximizing information gain across all points in a batch, is a significant contribution.  It moves beyond the limitations of greedy methods.
* **Effective Gradient Estimation:**  The IPA-based approach for efficiently computing the gradient of q-KG is crucial for practical application, particularly as *q* increases. This addresses a major potential bottleneck of the method.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate consistently superior performance compared to established benchmark algorithms (parallel EI, UCB variants) across both synthetic and real-world problems. The particularly strong performance in noisy evaluation scenarios is noteworthy.
* **Code Availability:** The publicly available code is excellent for reproducibility and further research.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the q-KG method.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost (Still a Concern):** While the IPA significantly improves gradient estimation, the computational cost of maximizing q-KG, especially for larger batch sizes (*q*) and high-dimensional problems, is still a potential limitation.  A more detailed discussion of the scalability of the method would be beneficial.  How does the computation time scale with *q* and dimensionality?
* **Discretization of A:** The need to discretize the search space *A* for practical optimization introduces an approximation. While the authors describe a dynamic discretization scheme, a more thorough investigation of the sensitivity of the results to the discretization resolution would be valuable.  Is there a systematic way to choose *M* (the number of samples for discretization) that balances accuracy and computational cost?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention tuning hyperparameters but don’t discuss the sensitivity of the method to these choices.  How robust is q-KG to different kernel parameters, noise levels, or choices of optimization algorithms for maximizing the acquisition function? 
* **Limited Exploration of Theoretical Implications:** While the theoretical foundation is solid, exploring the theoretical implications of using a collective batch acquisition function compared to sequential or greedy approaches in more detail could strengthen the paper. What are the theoretical guarantees (regret bounds) for q-KG in different settings?



**4. Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The method is theoretically well-grounded and offers a novel approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization.
* Empirical results consistently demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods.
* The IPA-based gradient estimation is a significant technical contribution.
* Code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost remains a potential concern, particularly for large-scale problems.
* The discretization of the search space introduces an approximation that requires further investigation.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of maximizing q-KG as a function of *q* and the dimensionality of the problem?
* How does the performance of q-KG vary with different choices of *M* (number of samples for discretization)? Is there a heuristic for selecting an appropriate value of *M*?
*  What is the sensitivity of q-KG’s performance to the choice of kernel hyperparameters and other parameters in the optimization process?
*  Have you considered any further optimizations to the IPA-based gradient estimation to improve its efficiency?
*  Could you discuss potential theoretical regret bounds or convergence properties of q-KG?



**Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The strengths of the method outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.**
