

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG), which generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors derive q-KG through decision-theoretic analysis, choosing the set of q points to evaluate next that is optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior when only one batch remains. The paper addresses the computational challenge of maximizing q-KG through infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling efficient gradient computation. The method is evaluated on synthetic functions and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks.

The work builds directly on the sequential knowledge gradient method [17] and extends it to the parallel setting, distinguishing itself from previous parallel Bayesian optimization approaches. It relates to prior work on parallelizing expected improvement (EI) [2, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and parallel UCB with exploration [3], while also connecting to recent efforts on parallel entropy search [18] and parallel EI optimization [15, 25]. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from these methods, particularly in that they optimize the acquisition function over the batch collectively rather than iteratively.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound method with rigorous theoretical foundations. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is clear and well-justified, and the authors provide a detailed explanation of the IPA-based gradient estimation technique. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical applications (tuning logistic regression on MNIST and CNNs on CIFAR10). The experiments cover both noise-free and noisy settings, with appropriate baselines including parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration. The authors carefully analyze the performance differences, particularly highlighting q-KG's advantages in noisy settings.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear and complete. The experimental setup is thoroughly explained, and the results are presented with appropriate figures and statistical analysis (mean and standard deviation across multiple runs). The connection between the theoretical development and the computational implementation is well-maintained.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by extending the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting, which is not addressed by previous work. The specific use of IPA to efficiently optimize the q-KG acquisition function represents a novel computational technique. The authors thoroughly compare their approach with related work, demonstrating how their method differs from previous parallel Bayesian optimization approaches. The paper is sufficiently distinct from recent NIPS papers on parallel Bayesian optimization, including those on parallel EI [2, 15, 25] and parallel entropy search [18].

**Significance:** The method addresses a practical problem in Bayesian optimization: the ability to evaluate multiple points in parallel, which is increasingly important with modern computing resources. The empirical results demonstrate that q-KG provides significant improvements over existing approaches, particularly in noisy settings where it consistently outperforms benchmarks. The application to tuning machine learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNNs) demonstrates real-world relevance, with the method improving test error rates on practical problems. The availability of code further increases the paper's impact.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Complexity:** While the authors address the computational challenges of maximizing q-KG through IPA, the computational cost of the method still scales with the size of the discretized domain (M=1000 in experiments). The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of how the computational complexity scales with q (the batch size) and M (the discretization size), particularly for larger problems.

**Theoretical Limitations:** The paper does not provide theoretical guarantees for the convergence of the q-KG method in the parallel setting, though this is expected given the complexity of batch Bayesian optimization. A brief discussion of potential theoretical limitations would strengthen the paper.

**Domain Discretization Strategy:** The discretization strategy (using samples from the global optima of the posterior) is well-motivated, but the paper could provide more details on how this choice affects the optimization performance and whether it might lead to suboptimal solutions in certain cases.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- Clear theoretical contribution extending knowledge gradient to parallel setting
- Novel computational approach (IPA) for efficient optimization
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements over benchmarks
- Strong practical relevance to machine learning hyperparameter tuning
- Well-written and clearly organized paper
- Code availability enhances reproducibility and impact

**Against Acceptance:**
- Computational complexity concerns for large batch sizes
- Limited theoretical guarantees for convergence
- Discretization strategy could be better justified

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound and well-executed contribution to batch Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is clearly novel, well-motivated, and demonstrated to provide significant improvements over existing parallel Bayesian optimization approaches, particularly in noisy settings. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear structure and thorough experimental evaluation. While there are some computational considerations that could be addressed in future work, these do not undermine the quality of the current contribution. The method has clear significance for practitioners and researchers working with expensive-to-evaluate functions, particularly in the context of machine learning hyperparameter tuning.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), represents a meaningful advance over previous work (Originality), and addresses a problem with significant practical relevance (Significance). I recommend acceptance.
