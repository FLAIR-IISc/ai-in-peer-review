**Summary**
This paper addresses the problem of parallel (or batch) Bayesian optimization, where the goal is to select a batch of `q` points to evaluate simultaneously to find the minimum of an expensive-to-evaluate function. The authors introduce a novel acquisition function, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which is a direct generalization of the sequential knowledge gradient policy. The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, aiming to select the batch of points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final recommended solution, where this solution is the minimum of the posterior mean and is not restricted to points already evaluated. Recognizing that direct optimization of the q-KG factor is computationally intractable, the authors develop a practical optimization strategy. This involves using Monte Carlo sampling to estimate the acquisition function and, crucially, employing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator. This allows for efficient, gradient-based optimization to find the optimal batch of points. The authors demonstrate through experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, with a particularly notable advantage in noisy settings.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Principled Approach:** The primary strength of this paper is its introduction of a novel, theoretically-grounded acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization. While previous methods have often relied on heuristics (e.g., "constant liar"), greedy sequential selection (e.g., GP-BUCB), or extensions of Expected Improvement, this work generalizes the Knowledge Gradient, which is Bayes-optimal for a one-step lookahead. This provides a strong, decision-theoretic foundation for selecting the entire batch of points jointly, considering their collective impact on the posterior belief about the function's minimum. This is a bold and significant conceptual advance for the field.

2.  **Significant Practical Relevance:** The problem of parallel BO is of immense practical importance, especially in machine learning for tasks like hyperparameter tuning, where parallel compute resources are readily available. The paper's focus on this problem is timely and impactful. The strong performance in noisy environments is a particularly valuable contribution, as real-world evaluations are often stochastic (e.g., due to random seeds in model training or evaluation on data subsets). The demonstration of improving a well-tuned CNN on CIFAR-10 is a compelling piece of evidence for the method's practical utility.

3.  **Enabling Technical Contribution:** The core idea of q-KG would be merely a theoretical curiosity without a practical way to optimize it. The use of Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive a stochastic gradient estimator is a clever and crucial technical contribution. This transforms an intractable optimization problem into one that can be solved efficiently with standard gradient-based methods, making the entire approach viable.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Cost:** The proposed method, relying on Monte Carlo sampling for both the objective and its gradient within an iterative optimization loop, is almost certainly more computationally intensive than competing methods like GP-BUCB or heuristic EI variants. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity or a wall-clock time comparison. While this cost may be acceptable when function evaluations are extremely expensive, it could be a limiting factor in other scenarios. However, for a novel method of this kind, prioritizing performance over computational efficiency is a reasonable trade-off.

2.  **Reliance on Discretization:** To compute the final term in the KG factor (`min_x mu(n+q)(x)`), the authors rely on discretizing the optimization domain `A`. While the proposed dynamic discretization scheme is sensible, it remains an approximation. The quality of this approximation and its effect on the performance of the overall algorithm are not theoretically analyzed. This could be seen as a minor methodological gap.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper presents a strong and exciting contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The introduction of the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) provides a new, principled direction for batch BO that moves beyond the dominant EI-based paradigm.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea is highly novel and theoretically well-motivated, representing a significant conceptual advance over existing batch BO methods.
*   The work addresses a problem of high practical importance, and the empirical results, especially the superior performance in noisy settings, are compelling and demonstrate a clear advance in the state of the art.
*   The technical solution for optimizing the acquisition function via IPA is elegant and makes the novel concept practical.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational overhead is likely high compared to some baselines, and this aspect is not thoroughly discussed.
*   The method relies on an approximation (discretization) whose theoretical properties are not explored.

In my view, the arguments for acceptance are far stronger. This is precisely the kind of bold, conceptually-driven work that NIPS should champion. The paper introduces a significant new idea and provides a complete and effective implementation, supported by strong empirical evidence. The weaknesses identified are minor relative to the novelty and impact of the contribution and can be considered avenues for future refinement rather than fatal flaws. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research and be adopted by practitioners.
