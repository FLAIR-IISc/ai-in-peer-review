

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a new acquisition function called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) for batch Bayesian optimization, extending the knowledge gradient method to the parallel setting. The authors claim that q-KG consistently outperforms existing parallel BO methods like parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration, particularly in noisy settings. The paper builds on established knowledge gradient methods [7, 17] but generalizes them to the batch setting, using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute gradients for optimization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a solid theoretical foundation in Bayesian optimization.
- The experimental evaluation covers a reasonable range of synthetic functions and practical machine learning problems (logistic regression, CNN tuning).
- The authors provide a detailed computational approach for optimizing q-KG using IPA, which addresses a genuine computational challenge in the parallel setting.
- The results in noisy settings show consistent improvements over baselines, which is valuable given that real-world evaluations often involve noise.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The knowledge gradient method for sequential BO is well-established [7, 17], and the extension to batch settings appears to be a natural but not particularly novel generalization. The authors do not sufficiently distinguish their work from recent parallel BO methods like [2, 15, 18] that also consider batch optimization.
- The paper lacks rigorous theoretical analysis of the q-KG method's properties. While the authors claim q-KG is "Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the predictor of the GP if only one decision is remaining," they don't provide a formal proof of this claim or discuss how this optimality extends to the batch setting.
- The experimental validation is insufficiently thorough. The paper reports mean performance across 100 runs for synthetic functions but doesn't provide statistical significance tests to support the claimed improvements. For example, the paper states that q-KG is "significantly better on Rosenbrock3, Ackley5 and Hartmann6" but doesn't provide p-values or confidence intervals to back this claim.
- The computational complexity analysis is lacking. While the authors claim their IPA approach is efficient, they don't provide a thorough comparison of computational costs with existing methods, making it difficult to assess practical utility.
- The paper doesn't adequately address when q-KG might fail or underperform compared to other methods. For instance, the results show q-KG is "slightly worse than the best of the other benchmarks on Branin2," but the authors don't investigate why this occurs or how to address it.
- The comparison with state-of-the-art methods appears limited. For a 2016 NIPS submission, the paper doesn't compare with more recent parallel BO methods that might have been published in 2015-2016, such as the parallel predictive entropy search [18] or the batch EI methods [2, 15].

**Quality:**
The technical content is sound, but the paper lacks the depth of theoretical analysis expected for a NIPS submission. The experimental results are presented but not sufficiently validated with statistical tests. The paper doesn't adequately address limitations or potential failure cases, which undermines its scientific rigor.

**Originality:**
The core idea of extending knowledge gradient to batch settings is not sufficiently novel. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from recent work on parallel Bayesian optimization. While the IPA approach for gradient estimation is presented as novel, it's a standard technique in stochastic optimization and not a major conceptual contribution.

**Significance:**
The paper addresses a practical problem (batch Bayesian optimization) with relevance to hyperparameter tuning in machine learning. However, the claimed improvements over existing methods appear marginal in most cases (e.g., "slightly worse" on Branin2), and the paper doesn't provide strong evidence that this method would be widely adopted in practice. The significant improvement in noisy settings is promising but not sufficiently explained or validated.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents a reasonable extension of existing Bayesian optimization methods, it falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS. The contribution is incremental, the theoretical analysis is insufficient, and the experimental validation lacks rigor. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent methods, stronger theoretical justification, and more detailed statistical validation of the claimed improvements.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper's contribution does not advance the state of the art sufficiently to warrant publication at NIPS. The improvements claimed over existing methods are not convincingly demonstrated, and the theoretical foundation is not sufficiently strong. The paper would require substantial revision to address these concerns, including more rigorous theoretical analysis, comprehensive comparisons with recent methods, and stronger statistical validation of the experimental results.
