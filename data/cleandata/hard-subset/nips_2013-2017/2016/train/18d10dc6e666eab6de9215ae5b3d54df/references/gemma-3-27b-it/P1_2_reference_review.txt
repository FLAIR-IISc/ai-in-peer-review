## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). Building upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) method, q-KG aims to maximize the expected information gain from evaluating a batch of *q* points in parallel. The authors derive the acquisition function through a decision-theoretic analysis, considering the optimal action when only one batch remains. Recognizing the computational challenges of directly maximizing q-KG, they leverage infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate its gradient, enabling optimization.  Empirical results on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning demonstrate that q-KG outperforms existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing body of work on parallel BO, contrasting its approach with methods that iteratively construct batches based on single-point acquisition functions (e.g., parallel EI, batch UCB). It acknowledges the related work on jointly optimizing acquisition functions (e.g., [2, 15, 25]) and highlights its key distinction: a decision-theoretic derivation based on the KG principle rather than maximizing immediate improvement.  The connection to the sequential KG method [17] is well-explained, and the authors correctly identify limitations of [26]’s nested KG approach.  The references are comprehensive and relevant.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is a significant strength.  The authors clearly articulate the motivation for choosing the batch that maximizes expected future improvement, rather than focusing solely on immediate gains.
* **Computational Efficiency:** Addressing the computational burden of q-KG through IPA is crucial for practical application. The gradient-based optimization approach appears well-justified and allows for scaling to larger batch sizes.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate q-KG's superiority over existing parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.  The tests on both synthetic and real-world problems (logistic regression and CNN tuning) strengthen the conclusions. The inclusion of noisy experiments is especially valuable, as real-world function evaluations are often imperfect.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The notation is clearly defined, and the key concepts are explained in a digestible manner. The algorithm is presented in a straightforward way.
* **Code Availability:**  Providing code is a significant positive, enabling reproducibility and facilitating further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization of A:**  The method for discretizing the feasible domain *A* (Section 5.3) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the use of samples from the posterior distribution is reasonable, the impact of the number of samples *M* on performance isn’t extensively explored. A sensitivity analysis regarding *M* would strengthen the results.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve deeply into the sensitivity of q-KG to its own hyperparameters (beyond the batch size *q*).  Understanding how the performance changes with different parameter settings for the GP kernel or the optimization process would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison Depth:** While comparisons are made to several baseline algorithms, the hyperparameter tuning process for those baselines isn't fully detailed.  Ensuring a fair comparison requires careful tuning of all methods.  More detail about the specific configurations used would be helpful.
* **Computational Complexity:** While IPA improves efficiency, the paper doesn't provide a rigorous analysis of the computational complexity of q-KG compared to the baselines, particularly as *q* increases. A theoretical comparison would be valuable.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization.
* The computational challenges are addressed effectively with the IPA technique.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear performance advantage over existing methods, particularly in noisy scenarios.
* The code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The discretization of the search space could be more rigorously justified and analyzed.
* Further investigation into hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the results.
* A more detailed comparison of computational complexity with baselines is desirable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The proposed q-KG method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically effective. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically providing a more detailed discussion of the domain discretization, exploring hyperparameter sensitivity, and potentially including a computational complexity analysis.  The authors should also clarify the hyperparameter tuning process for the baseline algorithms used in the experiments.
