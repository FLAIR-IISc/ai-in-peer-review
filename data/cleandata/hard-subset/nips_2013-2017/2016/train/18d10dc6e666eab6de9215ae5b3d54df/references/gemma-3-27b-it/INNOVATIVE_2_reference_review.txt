This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization, a novel acquisition function derived from decision-theoretic principles. The core idea—extending the single-iteration optimal Knowledge Gradient to a parallel setting—is compelling and represents a significant conceptual advance. While the paper demonstrates empirical success on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning, the novelty of the approach and the clear potential for impact outweigh some minor shortcomings in the validation and presentation.

The strengths of this work are substantial. The q-KG formulation is elegant and theoretically grounded, offering a principled way to balance exploration and exploitation in parallel BO. The IPA-based gradient estimation technique is a clever solution to the computational challenges posed by optimizing the q-KG acquisition function, making the method practical. The empirical results consistently show q-KG outperforming existing parallel BO methods, especially in noisy settings – a particularly valuable contribution. The authors also provide code, which is excellent for reproducibility.

However, there are areas for improvement. While the experiments are thorough, the practical problems (tuning logistic regression and CNNs) feel somewhat limited in scope. Presenting results on a wider range of real-world applications, or scaling the experiments to more complex models, would bolster the claims of practical significance. The discussion of related work, though extensive, could be more critical in highlighting precisely *how* q-KG differs from, and improves upon, existing parallel EI and UCB-based methods beyond simply stating performance gains. For example, a deeper discussion of the connection to, and differences from, nested batch knowledge gradient approaches (reference [26]) would be valuable. The paper occasionally suffers from dense writing, making some sections difficult to follow – a more streamlined presentation would improve clarity. Finally, while the use of Monte Carlo sampling is acknowledged, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost relative to other methods and the sensitivity to the number of samples would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **High Novelty:** The q-KG acquisition function represents a genuinely new approach to parallel BO, moving beyond iterative greedy selection.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:**  The derivation from decision theory provides a principled basis for the method.
* **Empirically Validated:** The consistent outperformance of q-KG across various experiments, especially in noisy environments, supports its effectiveness.
* **Practical Solution:** The efficient gradient estimation technique makes the method applicable to realistically sized problems.
* **Open Source Code:** Enhances reproducibility and accessibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Scope of Real-World Experiments:** The application to practical problems could be expanded for greater impact.
* **Dense Writing:**  Some sections lack clarity and could benefit from simplification.
* **Computational Cost Analysis Missing:** A more thorough analysis of the computational requirements relative to other methods is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The novelty of the q-KG method, coupled with its strong theoretical foundation and empirical validation, makes it a compelling submission.  While the paper isn't perfect – the scope of the practical experiments could be broader, and the writing could be more concise – these are relatively minor issues. I believe the substantial originality and potential impact of this work justify acceptance despite these minor flaws. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting clarification of the writing and expansion of the practical experimental results.
