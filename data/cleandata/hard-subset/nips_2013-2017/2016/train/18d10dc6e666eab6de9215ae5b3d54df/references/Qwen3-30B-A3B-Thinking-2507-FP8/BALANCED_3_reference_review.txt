

# Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "Parallel Knowledge Gradient" (q-KG), which extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) approach to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously, rather than sequentially. They address computational challenges through infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for efficient gradient estimation. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over existing parallel BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, with particularly strong performance in noisy settings.

This work builds on prior NIPS contributions on Bayesian optimization (e.g., [7, 17, 19]) and recent parallel extensions ([3, 5, 18, 25]), but makes a significant contribution by developing a decision-theoretically grounded acquisition function for batch selection rather than using iterative greedy approaches.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound extension of knowledge gradient to the batch setting with rigorous theoretical derivation. The IPA-based computational approach is well-justified and addresses a critical practical challenge. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic benchmarks (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and real-world ML tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show improvements over baselines, especially in noisy settings, with strong statistical significance.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear and meaningful contribution by generalizing knowledge gradient to the batch setting while maintaining theoretical grounding. Unlike previous parallel methods that use iterative greedy approaches (e.g., [2, 3, 5, 10]), q-KG optimizes the batch collectively, which is theoretically more principled. The distinction from related work is well-articulated, particularly regarding the nested batch knowledge gradient in [26] which uses discrete sets rather than continuous optimization.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to practical machine learning applications where function evaluations are expensive and parallelization is possible (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines). The consistent performance gains across multiple datasets and the demonstration of improvements in noisy settings (which are common in real-world ML) make this work particularly valuable. The application to CNN tuning represents a significant practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the acquisition function, it lacks rigorous theoretical guarantees about the convergence properties or optimality of the proposed method. The authors acknowledge the computational challenges but don't provide sufficient analysis of how computational complexity scales with q (the batch size), which is crucial for practical adoption.

**Comparison with Related Work:** The paper could better distinguish its approach from other recent parallel BO methods. For instance, the connection to [2, 15, 25] (which also use gradient-based optimization for parallel EI) needs more explicit discussion. The paper states that their approach is more efficient than [26], but doesn't provide comparative computational analysis.

**Implementation Details:** The discretization parameter M=1000 is used without justification or discussion of its impact on performance. The paper doesn't analyze how this choice affects computational cost or solution quality. Additionally, the paper mentions that the method is "computationally efficient" but doesn't provide empirical evidence of speed comparisons with other methods.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the growing literature on parallel Bayesian optimization. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the practical relevance is clear. The paper meets NIPS standards for technical quality and significance.

However, the lack of theoretical guarantees and insufficient discussion of computational complexity limits the paper's impact. The authors could strengthen their contribution by providing more rigorous analysis of the computational complexity as a function of q and a more detailed comparison with related gradient-based batch optimization methods.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper has clear strengths in theoretical grounding, experimental validation, and practical relevance. To strengthen it further, the authors should:

1. Add a brief theoretical analysis of the computational complexity with respect to q
2. Include a more detailed comparison with gradient-based parallel EI methods ([2, 15, 25])
3. Provide justification for the discretization parameter M and discuss its impact on performance

These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and make it a more compelling addition to the NIPS proceedings. The current work already represents a solid contribution to the field, and with these minor improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
