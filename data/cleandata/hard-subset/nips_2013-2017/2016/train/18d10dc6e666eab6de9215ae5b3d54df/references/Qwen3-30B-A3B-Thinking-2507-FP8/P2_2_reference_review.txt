

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting. The authors derive q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective, defining it as the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor when adding a batch of q new points. The paper addresses the computational challenge of optimizing q-KG by introducing a gradient estimator based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), which enables efficient optimization of the acquisition function. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks that q-KG consistently outperforms existing batch BO methods, especially in noisy settings.

The paper relates to previous work by building on the sequential KG method [7, 17] and extending it to batch optimization. It distinguishes itself from previous parallel BO methods [3, 5, 10, 19, 25] that typically optimize batches sequentially (e.g., by iteratively adding points to the batch) rather than jointly optimizing the entire batch. The paper also discusses related work on parallel EI [2, 15, 25] and parallel UCB [3, 5], noting that q-KG provides a more principled decision-theoretic approach.

## Assessment of Review Criteria

### Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a technically sound extension of the knowledge gradient method to the batch setting. The derivation of q-KG as the expected increment in solution quality when adding a batch of points is logically consistent with the sequential KG framework.
- The computational approach using IPA for efficient gradient estimation of q-KG is well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The paper provides a clear derivation of the unbiased gradient estimator (Equation 5.3) and its implementation details.
- The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, including both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical ML tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), with appropriate baselines (parallel EI, batch UCB, GP-UCB-PE).
- The results are well-presented with clear figures showing mean and standard deviation across multiple runs, and the paper correctly notes that q-KG provides especially large improvements in noisy settings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences (e.g., t-tests comparing q-KG to baselines), which would strengthen the empirical claims.
- The computational complexity analysis is limited to the gradient estimation step but doesn't fully address how the method scales with batch size q. The paper claims "naively maximizing q-KG would be extremely computationally intensive, especially when q is large," but doesn't quantify this or provide evidence of the actual computational time.
- The paper could better address the relationship between q-KG and other recent batch BO methods, particularly [15, 25] which also use gradient-based methods to optimize parallel EI. The authors mention these papers in the related work but don't clearly explain how their approach differs or why it would be superior.
- The paper doesn't discuss the limitations of their discretization approach (Section 5.3) for large-dimensional problems, which could be a significant issue for high-dimensional hyperparameter tuning.

### Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from introduction to related work, background, method, computation, and experiments.
- The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout the paper.
- Algorithm 1 provides a clear step-by-step description of the q-KG algorithm.
- The experimental setup is well-described, including details about the synthetic functions, ML tasks, and hyperparameters.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper uses "q-KG" as an acronym without explicitly stating what it stands for at first use (it's only defined as "parallel knowledge gradient" in the abstract but not in the main text).
- The distinction between the two "A"s (the feasible domain and the discretized set) could be clarified more explicitly in Section 5.3.
- Some of the mathematical derivations (e.g., the gradient computation in Section 5.2) could be more intuitive for readers less familiar with IPA.

### Originality

**Strengths:**
- The paper makes a clear contribution by extending the knowledge gradient method to the batch setting in a principled decision-theoretic way, rather than using iterative approaches.
- The use of IPA for gradient estimation of the q-KG acquisition function is novel in the context of batch BO.
- The paper properly differentiates itself from related work, particularly noting that previous methods like [26] focus on discrete feasible sets where gradient computation is not applicable.

**Weaknesses:**
- While the paper cites recent work on batch BO, it could have more thoroughly compared with [18] (which proposes a parallel version of entropy search) and [25] (which uses gradient-based methods for parallel EI), as these are directly relevant to the proposed approach.
- The paper doesn't clearly articulate how the q-KG method differs from the "nested batch knowledge gradient" in [26], which also considers batch optimization. The authors mention this in the related work but don't provide a detailed comparison.

### Significance

**Strengths:**
- Batch Bayesian optimization is highly relevant to practical applications where multiple evaluations can be performed in parallel (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines), making the paper's contribution practically important.
- The consistent performance improvements over existing methods, especially in noisy settings, demonstrate significant practical value.
- The application to real-world ML problems (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10) shows the method's relevance to current machine learning practice.
- The paper provides code availability, which will facilitate adoption by the research community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't discuss how the method would scale to extremely large batch sizes (e.g., q > 10), which is a common scenario in distributed hyperparameter tuning.
- The paper focuses on batch size q=4, but doesn't explore how the method might perform with larger batches, which would be valuable for practical applications.
- The paper doesn't compare with more recent batch BO methods that have emerged since the NIPS 2016 conference (though this is understandable given the paper's age).

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization by providing a principled, decision-theoretic approach to batch optimization that consistently outperforms existing methods, especially in noisy settings. The technical approach is sound, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the method has clear practical relevance to hyperparameter tuning in machine learning.

**Strengths for Acceptance:**
- Novel extension of knowledge gradient to batch setting with decision-theoretic foundation
- Efficient computational approach using IPA for gradient estimation
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing consistent improvements over baselines
- Practical relevance to machine learning hyperparameter tuning
- Code availability for reproducibility

**Weaknesses for Acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of computational scaling with batch size q
- Some ambiguity in the comparison with related work (particularly [15, 25, 18])
- Missing statistical significance tests for empirical results
- Limited discussion of limitations in high-dimensional settings

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While there are some weaknesses in the paper (particularly regarding computational scaling and statistical significance), the paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of batch Bayesian optimization. The method is theoretically well-founded, computationally efficient, and demonstrates strong empirical performance across a range of problems. The paper's relevance to practical machine learning applications, combined with the comprehensive experimental evaluation, makes it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.

The paper is well-written, addresses an important problem, and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The minor weaknesses noted above are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's strong contributions.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**
