Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes q-KG, an acquisition function for batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The core idea is decision-theoretic: with one batch remaining, select z(1:q) to maximize the expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean, i.e., minx µ(n)(x) − E[minx µ(n+q)(x) | y(z(1:q))]. The authors show that in the noise-free case and when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to multi-point Expected Improvement (q-EI). They address computational challenges by (i) Monte Carlo estimation of the acquisition via a reparameterization µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z)Zq, and (ii) an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based optimization over z(1:q). For continuous A, they approximate the inner min over a discretization An built by sampling likely optima of the GP posterior (as in PES/PPES), augmented with previously sampled points and the current candidate batch.

Empirically, on synthetic benchmarks (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10), q-KG matches or outperforms batch EI (exact and heuristic) and UCB-based batch policies (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), with especially strong gains under observation noise. Code is provided, and implementations reuse established libraries (MOE, Spearmint, Gpoptimization).

This work relates closely to: (i) sequential KG for discrete and continuous domains [Frazier et al. 2008; Scott et al. 2011]; (ii) batch EI and its optimization via closed-form and differentiable surrogates [Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.]; (iii) batch ES [Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani]; and (iv) UCB-based batch policies with regret guarantees [Desautels et al.; Contal et al.]. The paper extends KG to the batch/noisy setting with a tractable gradient estimator and compares empirically to these families.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation: The acquisition objective is well-defined and consistent with the single-step Bayes-optimal criterion for minimizing the posterior mean’s minimum. The reduction to q-EI in the noise-free, “recommend-from-sampled” case is correct and provides a useful sanity check.
- Estimation and optimization: The Monte Carlo estimator using the GP posterior update reparameterization is standard and sound. The IPA-based stochastic gradient is plausible: under differentiability of µ and K and with discretized A (to avoid infinite-dimensional minimization), exchanging gradient and expectation is justified under regularity conditions; ties in the argmin occur with probability zero. Differentiating through the Cholesky factor is feasible but requires care; an explicit mention of how its derivatives are computed (analytic rules vs. autodiff) would improve reproducibility.
- Discretization: Approximating min over A by min over An built from samples of the GP minimizer distribution aligns with PES/PPES practices. However, details of how samples from p(x* | D) are generated (e.g., via Thompson sampling with random Fourier features, MAP restarts, or other approximations) are omitted here and deferred to cited work; including a concrete procedure and its computational cost would strengthen the methodology section.
- Hyperparameters and noise: The derivations assume fixed GP hyperparameters during acquisition optimization, and the implementation re-fits by MLE after each batch, which is standard. The noisy setting assumes homoscedastic Gaussian noise estimated by MLE; this is reasonable, though performance under heteroscedastic noise is not discussed.
- Theory: The work provides no regret or asymptotic guarantees for the batch setting; the result is primarily decision-theoretic and myopic. This matches much of the batch BO literature (e.g., q-EI, PPES), but it leaves open formal performance guarantees.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-structured. The distinction between the two appearances of A (domain of optimization of z vs. the set over which the inner min is evaluated) is helpful, though the notation could be streamlined by consistently denoting the discretized set as An in all relevant formulas when used.
- Algorithmic details that would help reproduction: number of Monte Carlo samples per acquisition/gradient estimate; gradient optimizer (step sizes, restarts, stopping criteria); handling of integer/categorical hyperparameters in the tuning tasks; complexity as a function of q, M, and d; and how D’(z) (derivative of the Cholesky factor) is computed.
- Minor issues: some typos (e.g., “Main Stange”), and inconsistent equation formatting; these do not impede comprehension.

Originality
- Extending KG to the batch setting and providing a practical, unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via IPA for joint optimization over z(1:q) is novel relative to sequential KG. While valuing points collectively is shared with q-EI and PPES, the objective differs materially: expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean vs. expected improvement or entropy reduction. The discretization strategy is borrowed from PES-type methods, but its use within KG is a new combination. Overall, the contribution is incremental but meaningful.

Significance
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly under noise, which is an important regime in practice. The method’s ability to explicitly account for observation noise is a clear advantage over EI-like methods.
- Practicality: The approach is more computationally demanding than greedy batch construction or UCB-based methods due to Monte Carlo estimation and joint optimization in q·d dimensions. The paper does not report wall-clock time or scaling to larger d or q; this limits immediate applicability in high-throughput settings. Nonetheless, for moderate d and q, the gains under noise are potentially impactful for hyperparameter tuning.
- Code availability is a positive for adoption.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, principled formulation of a batch KG objective with a correct reduction to q-EI in a special case.
- Practical, unbiased stochastic gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical performance, especially in noisy settings, on both synthetic and realistic tasks.
- Reuse of established GP implementations and code release.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Lack of theoretical guarantees (e.g., regret bounds or consistency) in the batch setting.
- Missing implementation details: sampling from p(x*), number of MC samples, gradient optimization hyperparameters, and computational cost vs. baselines.
- No wall-clock/time-to-solution comparisons and limited exploration of scalability with higher dimensions and larger batch sizes.
- Evaluation on CNN tuning uses only 5 runs; more repetitions and ablations (varying q, M, noise levels) would strengthen claims.
- Potential fairness concerns due to using different libraries for different baselines; confirming consistent GP priors/hyperparameter learning across methods would be beneficial.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a section detailing the An construction (sampling from the GP minimizer distribution) and provide sensitivity analyses over M and the number of Monte Carlo samples for acquisition/gradient estimation.
- Report computational costs and wall-clock comparisons versus q-EI and PPES for varying q and d.
- Include ablations for batch sizes (e.g., q ∈ {2, 4, 8}) and higher-dimensional synthetic problems to assess scalability.
- Clarify handling of integer/categorical hyperparameters in tuning experiments.
- If possible, provide theoretical insights (e.g., conditions under which the myopic q-KG is consistent as N increases) or connections to existing KG theory.

Overall, this is a solid, well-motivated contribution that advances batch BO with a principled acquisition function and demonstrates practical benefits under noise, albeit with room for more thorough experimental and computational analysis.
