

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces a novel acquisition function called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) for batch Bayesian optimization, extending the sequential knowledge gradient method to the parallel setting. The authors propose a decision-theoretic approach that jointly optimizes a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously, rather than constructing batches iteratively as previous methods do. The key insight is that q-KG measures the information gain of evaluating q points collectively, making it Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor when only one batch remains.

The paper makes a strong contribution by generalizing the knowledge gradient concept to the parallel setting, which has been largely unexplored compared to parallel extensions of expected improvement (EI) and upper confidence bound (UCB). While previous parallel BO methods like parallel EI or batch UCB construct batches sequentially by adding points one at a time, q-KG considers the batch as a whole, which the authors argue leads to better performance, especially in noisy settings.

The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical derivations of the q-KG acquisition function and an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute its gradient. The experimental results are comprehensive, showing consistent improvements over baselines on both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical machine learning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The paper demonstrates particularly strong performance in noisy settings, where q-KG outperforms benchmarks by a larger margin than in noise-free settings.

The novelty of this work is significant. While parallel extensions of EI and UCB exist, the parallel knowledge gradient has not been previously developed. The decision-theoretic foundation of q-KG provides a principled approach that differs fundamentally from existing methods. The paper also makes a useful connection showing that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under specific conditions (noise-free evaluations and final solution restricted to sampled points), providing theoretical consistency.

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear decision-theoretic motivation
- Novel acquisition function that fundamentally differs from existing parallel BO methods
- Comprehensive empirical validation across diverse test functions and real-world applications
- Demonstrated superiority in noisy settings, which is particularly valuable for practical applications
- Well-implemented code available for reproducibility

**Weaknesses:**
- The computational complexity analysis of the IPA-based optimization is somewhat superficial; the paper doesn't provide detailed runtime comparisons with baselines.
- The paper doesn't thoroughly address how q-KG scales with increasing batch size q, which is a critical practical consideration.
- The comparison with the nested batch knowledge gradient in [26] is brief; while the authors claim their method is more efficient, a more detailed comparison would strengthen the argument.
- The paper doesn't provide sufficient theoretical guarantees about the regret bounds or convergence properties of q-KG.

The paper's main contribution is a novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization that addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. While the methodological details could be strengthened with more comprehensive computational analysis, the novelty and empirical performance are compelling. The paper's focus on the decision-theoretic perspective and the collective optimization of the batch represents a bold step beyond the iterative approaches that dominate current parallel BO literature.

This work has significant potential impact for practitioners who need to optimize expensive black-box functions in parallel settings, particularly in machine learning hyperparameter tuning where noisy evaluations are common. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by introducing a new concept (q-KG) and showing its superiority over existing methods.

**Recommendation:** Accept

The paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization that represents a significant conceptual advance beyond existing work. While the computational analysis could be more thorough, the core idea is bold, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, and the paper meets NIPS standards for novelty and significance. The minor methodological gaps do not detract from the paper's overall contribution, which is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance.
