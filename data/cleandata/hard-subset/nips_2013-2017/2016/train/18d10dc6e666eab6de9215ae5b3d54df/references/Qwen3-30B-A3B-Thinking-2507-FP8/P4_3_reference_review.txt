

# Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

This paper proposes a novel parallel Bayesian optimization method called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), extending the knowledge gradient acquisition function from sequential to batch settings. The authors derive q-KG through decision-theoretic analysis, where the acquisition function measures the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor when evaluating a batch of q points. The paper develops an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize q-KG, addressing the computational challenges of evaluating and maximizing the acquisition function in batch settings.

The paper builds upon the knowledge gradient method [7, 17] for sequential Bayesian optimization and extends it to the batch setting, which has been previously explored in works like parallel EI [2, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and parallel UCB with exploration [3]. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from these prior works by noting that their method considers the batch of points collectively rather than constructing batches iteratively, and they provide a thorough comparison to these baselines in their experimental section.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical derivation of q-KG is mathematically rigorous, and the authors provide a detailed explanation of the computational techniques for efficiently optimizing the acquisition function using IPA. The experimental section is comprehensive, evaluating the method on both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show that q-KG outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art baselines, particularly in noisy settings. The authors provide sufficient implementation details and make their code publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical development, computational methods, and experimental validation. The mathematical notation is consistently defined, and the key contributions are clearly articulated in the introduction. The supplementary materials (referenced but not included in the provided text) would likely provide additional details for replication.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the knowledge gradient method to the batch setting, which represents a non-trivial generalization of prior work. The authors provide a detailed comparison to related work, highlighting how their approach differs from iterative batch construction methods and other parallel acquisition functions. The method is not merely a combination of existing techniques but a theoretically grounded extension that addresses limitations in previous approaches. This contribution is novel compared to recent NIPS papers on parallel Bayesian optimization [3, 5, 18, 25, 26].

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important practical problem in Bayesian optimization—efficient parallel evaluation—which has direct relevance to hyperparameter tuning in machine learning where function evaluations are expensive and parallel computation is available. The consistent performance improvements demonstrated in both synthetic and real-world experiments, especially in noisy settings, suggest that this method could have significant practical impact. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically sound method that outperforms existing parallel BO approaches.

**Strengths:**
1. Clear theoretical extension of knowledge gradient to batch setting
2. Efficient computational method using IPA for optimization
3. Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple problem types
4. Strong performance gains, particularly in noisy settings
5. Well-organized and clearly written paper
6. Code made publicly available

**Weaknesses:**
1. Limited theoretical analysis of computational complexity for large batch sizes
2. No detailed regret bounds or convergence analysis provided
3. The paper could provide more insight into why q-KG performs particularly well in noisy settings
4. The comparison to [26] (nested batch knowledge gradient) is mentioned but not deeply analyzed

**Pro/Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically useful extension of Bayesian optimization to the parallel setting
- The computational method using IPA is a significant contribution that makes the approach feasible
- The experimental results are comprehensive and consistently demonstrate the superiority of q-KG
- The method addresses a well-known limitation in existing parallel BO approaches
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized

*Con:*
- The paper does not provide theoretical guarantees about the regret or convergence properties
- The computational complexity analysis for large batch sizes is limited
- The paper could more thoroughly explain the mechanisms behind q-KG's superior performance in noisy settings

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, addressing an important practical problem with a theoretically grounded and empirically validated solution. The method is novel compared to recent NIPS papers, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its advantages over existing approaches. The quality of the theoretical development, the clarity of the presentation, and the significance of the results meet the high standards expected at NIPS. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened with more complexity and regret analysis, the paper's strengths outweigh this limitation. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.
