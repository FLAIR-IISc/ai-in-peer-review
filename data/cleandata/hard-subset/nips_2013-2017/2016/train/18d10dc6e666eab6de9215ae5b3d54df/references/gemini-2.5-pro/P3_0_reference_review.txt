### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper proposes a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The method is derived from a decision-theoretic framework, aiming to select a batch of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality after one more parallel evaluation step. This provides a principled approach to valuing a batch of points jointly, in contrast to many heuristic or greedy methods. The primary technical challenge is the optimization of the q-KG acquisition function, which lacks a closed-form expression. The authors overcome this by developing an efficient, unbiased gradient estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), enabling the use of standard gradient-based optimizers. The paper evaluates q-KG on several synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, demonstrating superior or competitive performance against state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy environments.

**Strengths (Reasons for acceptance):**

1.  **Principled Derivation:** The core strength of this work is its strong theoretical foundation. Unlike many parallel BO methods that are heuristics built upon sequential acquisition functions (e.g., "constant liar" or greedy UCB selection), q-KG is derived from first principles as the Bayes-optimal policy for a single remaining batch. It correctly quantifies the joint information gain from a set of points, which is the central challenge in batch BO.

2.  **Significant Technical Contribution:** The generalization of the Knowledge Gradient to the parallel setting is non-trivial, but the most significant contribution is the computational machinery to make it practical. The use of Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for the q-KG factor is a sophisticated and powerful technique. This allows the method to move beyond discrete optimization domains (as in some prior KG work) and tackle continuous problems efficiently with gradient ascent.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance, Especially in Noisy Settings:** The experimental results are convincing. The method consistently performs at or above the level of strong baselines like parallel EI and batch UCB variants. Crucially, its advantage is most pronounced in the noisy setting. This is a highly significant result, as many real-world expensive-to-evaluate functions are inherently noisy (e.g., due to stochastic optimization of ML models or subsampling of test sets). The paper correctly identifies and demonstrates this key strength.

4.  **High Potential Impact:** Batch BO is of immense practical importance for hyperparameter tuning, A/B testing, and experimental design where parallel resources are available. By providing a well-founded and high-performing algorithm, this work has the potential to become a standard method in the field. The public release of the code further enhances its potential for adoption.

**Weaknesses (Areas for improvement):**

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The primary concern is the computational overhead of optimizing the q-KG acquisition function. The method relies on Monte Carlo sampling to estimate both the function value and its gradient, and this is nested within a gradient-based optimization loop. The paper does not provide any analysis of the wall-clock time required to select a batch compared to the baselines. For a method to be practical, this cost must be reasonable relative to the function evaluation time. A discussion on how the optimization time scales with the batch size `q` (which involves a O(q³) Cholesky decomposition) and the number of Monte Carlo samples would significantly strengthen the paper. The experiments are limited to `q=4`; performance for larger batches remains an open question.

2.  **Dependence on Discretization:** The computation of `min µ(n+q)` over the continuous domain `A` is handled by discretizing `A`. The paper proposes a dynamic discretization scheme by sampling from the posterior optima, which is a reasonable heuristic. However, the performance of q-KG could be sensitive to the quality and size (`M`) of this discrete set. The paper sets `M=1000` without a sensitivity analysis or discussion of this choice. It would be valuable to understand how performance degrades if this discretization is poor or too small.

3.  **Clarity on IPA Conditions:** The application of IPA requires certain smoothness conditions. The paper states that interchangeability of expectation and differentiation holds if `µ` and `K` are continuously differentiable, but relegates the details to supplementary material. While understandable due to space constraints, a brief justification in the main text for why the `argmin` operator does not pose a problem (e.g., by noting that its discontinuities occur on a set of measure zero) would improve the self-containedness and technical clarity of the paper.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

*   In Section 6, could you provide a comparison of the wall-clock time required to select one batch of points for q-KG versus the baseline methods? This would give practitioners a clear idea of the computational trade-off.
*   The connection to parallel EI under noise-free conditions is mentioned. It would be helpful to explicitly state that the key difference is that KG considers the optimum over the entire domain `A`, while EI is restricted to the set of evaluated points. This is a fundamental distinction that explains KG's strength, especially when the function is noisy and the true minimum of `f` might not coincide with the minimum observed value `y`.
*   Regarding the discretization in Section 5.3: Have you experimented with different values of `M`? How sensitive is the algorithm's performance to this hyperparameter?
*   The experiments with noisy logistic regression are excellent for demonstrating a practical scenario. The noise comes from subsampling the test set. Does this violate the i.i.d. Gaussian noise assumption, and if so, how robust is the GP model to this model mismatch? The strong results suggest it is quite robust, which is an interesting finding in itself.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It addresses the important problem of parallel optimization with a theoretically sound and practically effective method. The technical development is solid, and the empirical validation is thorough. While there are some open questions regarding computational cost and sensitivity to certain choices, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and is likely to be influential.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
