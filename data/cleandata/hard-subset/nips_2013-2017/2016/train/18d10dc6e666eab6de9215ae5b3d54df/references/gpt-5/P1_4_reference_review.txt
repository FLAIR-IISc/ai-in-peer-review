Summary
This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) from the sequential to the batch setting. The key idea is decision-theoretic: for one remaining batch of size q, the authors choose the set of points z(1:q) that maximizes the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean over the search domain A. This yields a Bayes-optimal one-step policy for minimizing the posterior predictor (and it coincides with q-EI in the noiseless setting when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points). Because computing q-KG requires expectations over the q-dimensional predictive distribution and optimization of a nontrivial objective, the paper develops a Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG and an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling efficient gradient-based optimization of z(1:q). For continuous A, the authors discretize (only for the purpose of computing q-KG) using samples drawn from posterior minimizers, augmented with evaluated and candidate points.

Empirically, on synthetic test functions (2–6D) and two ML hyperparameter tuning tasks (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR-10 CNN), q-KG matches or outperforms strong batch BO baselines (parallel EI variants, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), with especially large gains under noisy evaluations. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Extends KG (Frazier et al.) from sequential to batch. Prior “nested batch KG” considered discrete domains and a different nesting structure; this work targets continuous A with gradient-based optimization and IPA.
- Complements multipoint EI approaches (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Wang et al.’s MOE/parallel EI), and information-theoretic batch methods like PPES (Shah & Ghahramani). Compared to UCB extensions to the batch setting (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.), q-KG targets simple regret rather than cumulative regret.
- Computationally, follows the line of differentiable parallel acquisition optimization (e.g., differentiating q-EI), but derives stochastic gradients for q-KG through the Cholesky-based reparameterization and the min over a discretized A.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The decision-theoretic derivation is standard for KG and correctly generalized to batches via expectation over the q-dimensional normal. The reduction to q-EI under noiseless evaluations and restricted recommendations is well-argued. The IPA-based gradient derivation for a function defined as a min over a finite set appears plausible; the paper claims regularity conditions (µ, K differentiable) and defers proofs to the supplement. However, the min introduces nondifferentiabilities when the argmin changes; almost-sure differentiability and unbiasedness should be carefully justified (e.g., via Danskin’s theorem/envelope theorem and measure-zero arguments). Please make explicit the conditions and provide a precise theorem with assumptions.
- Computation: The reparameterization µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z)Zq is standard and enables Monte Carlo and gradients. Complexity per evaluation scales with O(q^3) for Cholesky and O(Mq^2 + Md) for traversing the discretization of size M; a brief complexity discussion and variance considerations for the stochastic gradient would help. The discretization scheme is reasonable (samples from posterior minimizers), but sensitivity to M and dimension should be reported.
- Experiments: Broad and convincing for low/moderate dimensions and both noiseless/noisy cases. The gains under noise align with KG’s strengths (not restricting the recommender to sampled points). However: (i) no wall-clock/runtime comparisons are reported; given that q-KG is heavier than greedy batch EI/UCB, a time-to-accuracy plot would be informative; (ii) comparison to PPES/q-PES (or at least PES sequential) is missing despite being a strong batch information-theoretic baseline; (iii) in the ML tuning tasks, using test error as the optimization objective conflates tuning and evaluation; a separate validation set would be preferable, though this is common in BO papers. Reporting variance across runs is good; please include confidence intervals in figures and specify GP hyperparameter learning protocol for all methods (you mention MLE).

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. The distinction between the two uses of A (in the q-KG evaluation vs the domain for optimizing z) is helpful; perhaps rename the discretized set (e.g., A_eval) earlier to avoid confusion.
- Some typos: “Main Stange” → “Stage”; check equation numbering and consistency in notation (e.g., min y(1:n) is shorthand for min over previously sampled points). Define all symbols on first use (e.g., D(n) as Cholesky factor).
- Please state the precise Bayes-optimality statement (for discrete A and one remaining batch) and its scope when A is approximated.

Originality
- Extending KG to the parallel setting with a practical and differentiable estimator is a substantive contribution. The IPA-based gradient for q-KG and the decision-theoretic batch framing are novel relative to prior batch EI/UCB and PPES. The discretization strategy is borrowed from ES/PES, but the overall combination is new.

Significance
- Batch BO is widely used; a method that is principled, handles noise well, and outperforms common baselines is impactful. The public code increases adoption potential. The scope is currently low- to moderate-dimensional continuous BO; demonstrating scalability (e.g., >10D via embeddings/input warping) and asynchronous settings would further boost significance.

Pros
- Clear decision-theoretic motivation; Bayes-optimal for one-step batch.
- Unbiased stochastic gradients enabling efficient joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical results, especially under noise.
- Open-source implementation leveraging established libraries.

Cons
- Reliance on discretization for evaluating q-KG; lack of sensitivity analysis and theoretical impact of discretization.
- Missing comparisons to PPES/q-PES; no runtime analyses.
- Limited theory beyond one-step optimality; no multi-iteration convergence/regret guarantees.
- Some technical details deferred to supplement (IPA justification around mins).

Questions for authors
1. Can you provide a theorem with assumptions for the unbiasedness/validity of the IPA gradient through the min operator?
2. How sensitive is performance to M, the discretization size, and to the choice of sampling scheme for A? Any adaptive schemes?
3. What are typical wall-clock times per acquisition optimization versus parallel EI/PPES? Any engineering tricks to reduce variance or reuse factorizations across z?
4. Have you tried q > 4 and higher-dimensional problems (>10D)? How does performance scale?
5. Could you compare to PPES or explain why it was omitted?

Minor suggestions
- Use a separate validation set for ML tuning; report final test performance only once at the end.
- Include ablations: effect of noise magnitude, q, and M.
- Discuss extensions to asynchronous batch BO and constraints.

Recommendation
Leaning accept. The paper presents a principled and practically useful batch BO method with strong empirical performance and a novel gradient-based optimization approach. Addressing the missing comparisons and clarifying the theoretical justification for the stochastic gradient would strengthen it further.
