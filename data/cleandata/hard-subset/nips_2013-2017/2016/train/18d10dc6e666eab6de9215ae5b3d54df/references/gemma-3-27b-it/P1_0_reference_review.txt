## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) method called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea is to extend the single-point knowledge gradient acquisition function to a batch setting, aiming to maximize information gain from evaluating *q* points simultaneously.  The authors derive this acquisition function using a decision-theoretic analysis and address the computational challenge of maximizing it by employing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate gradients.  Experiments on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning demonstrate that q-KG outperforms existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing area of parallel BO, acknowledging the limitations of extending sequential methods (like parallelizing EI or UCB) and highlighting the benefit of a batch-aware acquisition function. It builds upon the foundational work of the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) method and effectively differentiates itself from recent approaches like parallel EI, batch UCB, and nested batch knowledge gradient policies. The authors accurately note the connection to IPA for gradient estimation, referencing relevant work in that area.  The paper effectively situates itself relative to the existing landscape, clearly articulating its contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Foundation:** The q-KG acquisition function is a genuine contribution. Deriving it from a decision-theoretic perspective provides a strong theoretical foundation and justifies its aim of maximizing information gain.  The extension of KG to the parallel setting is a logical and valuable step.
* **Computational Efficiency:** Recognizing the computational difficulty of maximizing q-KG, the authors successfully leverage IPA to enable efficient gradient-based optimization. This is crucial for practical applicability, especially with larger batch sizes.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experimental results convincingly demonstrate q-KG's superiority over several strong baseline methods across both synthetic and real-world problems.  The consistent performance improvements, especially in the noisy setting, are particularly compelling. The use of both synthetic benchmarks and application to logistic regression and CNN tuning strengthens the findings.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithm is presented in a concise and understandable manner.
* **Code Availability:** Providing publicly available code (linked in the paper) is a significant strength and promotes reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization of A:** The method for discretizing the continuous domain *A* (using samples from the posterior) could be a potential bottleneck, and its impact on performance isn't thoroughly investigated. The choice of *M* = 1000 seems arbitrary, and sensitivity analysis to this parameter would be beneficial.  A discussion of alternative discretization strategies would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper demonstrates strong overall performance, a more detailed analysis of the q-KG's sensitivity to its own hyperparameters (e.g., parameters within the GP kernel or optimization settings for q-KG itself) would improve the robustness assessment.
* **Limited Comparison:** While a good set of baseline algorithms are compared against, it would be beneficial to include a comparison against more recent advancements in parallel BO, if any exist since the paper's submission.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn't delve into practical considerations related to parallel evaluation itself (e.g., communication overhead, potential for worker failures). Discussing these aspects, even briefly, would enhance the paper’s practical relevance.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  The paper demonstrates technical soundness with a clear theoretical basis and robust empirical validation. The arguments are well-supported by both analysis and experiments. This is a complete and well-executed piece of work.
* **Clarity:** The paper is mostly clearly written. While the mathematical details can be dense, the explanations are generally understandable.  The algorithm is presented concisely, and the experimental setup is well-defined.
* **Originality:** The proposed q-KG method represents a novel contribution to the field of parallel BO, offering a distinct approach to batch acquisition function design. The application of IPA for efficient gradient estimation is also a clever innovation.
* **Significance:** The results suggest q-KG could have a significant impact on the efficiency of Bayesian Optimization, particularly for problems with expensive function evaluations.  The improved performance in noisy settings is particularly valuable. The findings are likely to be adopted and extended by researchers and practitioners in the field.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and novel methodology.
* Compelling empirical results demonstrating superior performance.
* Efficient gradient-based optimization through IPA.
* Clear presentation and publicly available code.
* Addresses a practically relevant problem with a valuable solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The discretization of the search space *A* could be a limiting factor.
* Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*  Could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of practical parallel evaluation considerations.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.  The q-KG method represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization, offering a theoretically sound and empirically validated approach to parallel batch evaluation.  The weaknesses are minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The paper is well-written, clear, and provides a valuable addition to the NIPS program. The potential impact of this work is high, and it is likely to stimulate further research in this important area.
