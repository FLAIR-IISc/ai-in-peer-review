Summary
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The acquisition is defined as the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after observing a batch, i.e., the one-step Bayes-optimal decision if only one batch remains. The authors show that in the noise-free setting with the final recommendation restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to q-EI. Because exact evaluation and optimization of q-KG is intractable, the paper (i) uses Monte Carlo to estimate the expectation over pending observations, (ii) derives an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA)/Danskin’s theorem for joint batch optimization, and (iii) discretizes the domain in the inner minimization by sampling from the posterior distribution over the argmin (as in ES/PES). Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning (MNIST logistic regression; CIFAR-10 CNN) show improvements over parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with especially strong gains under observation noise. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Extends the classical KG (Frazier et al.) from sequential to batch. Prior batch selection includes q-EI (Ginsbourger; Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Wang et al. MOE), GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE, and parallel ES/PES (Shah & Ghahramani), as well as heuristics like constant liar. The paper positions itself as a one-step Bayes-optimal average-case criterion that accounts for noise, in contrast to regret-oriented UCB and (q-)EI’s issues under noise.
- IPA-based differentiable estimators for batch acquisition optimization have been used for q-EI; here, the same idea is adapted to q-KG with a different objective (min of posterior mean after fantasizing).
- The discretization of the inner minimization following ES/PES is reasonable and standard in practice.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic grounding: q-KG is the natural one-step Bayes-optimal rule for minimizing the posterior mean’s minimum with a batch, generalizing a well-understood sequential KG policy.
- Handles noisy observations more naturally than (q-)EI; the empirical advantage in noisy settings is a meaningful contribution.
- Joint optimization of the batch (vs. greedy construction) is well-motivated and aligns with recent advances in batch BO.
- Practical algorithmic contribution: an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via IPA that enables efficient gradient-based optimization of a Monte Carlo–defined acquisition.
- Solid empirical results across synthetic functions and realistic ML hyperparameter tuning tasks; improvements in early iterations are particularly compelling for expensive evaluations.
- Released code increases reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical details are light in the main text. The interchange of gradient and expectation (Eq. 5.3) relies on regularity and uniqueness assumptions; ties and non-differentiabilities in the min operator need to be discussed explicitly (Danskin’s theorem). Please clarify the exact conditions and how violations (e.g., near-ties) are handled in practice.
- Complexity and scalability are not analyzed. What is the computational cost per acquisition/gradient evaluation as a function of q, n, |A_n|, and Monte Carlo samples S? How does wall-clock compare to baselines for the same evaluation budget, and how does it scale with q?
- Discretization strategy: Sampling M points from the “posterior over global optima” (via drawing GP functions and minimizing) is expensive and not fully described. How are these samples generated in practice, and with what compute? How sensitive are results to M and the sampling procedure? Including z(1:q) in A_n changes the acquisition dependence; does this introduce discontinuities in the objective as z moves on/off the minimizing set?
- Gradient expression: µ^(n) does not depend on z; the derivatives of µ^(n)(x*(before)) and µ^(n)(x*(after)) in Eq. 5.2 should be zero by Danskin/envelope theorem, with only the σ̃ term contributing. The current presentation is potentially misleading; please correct and provide the exact form used in code.
- Fairness of empirical comparisons: Baselines are drawn from different codebases (MOE, Spearmint, Gpoptimization) with potentially different GP priors, hyperparameter learning, and optimization heuristics. To isolate the acquisition function, all methods should share the same GP model/hyperparameter fitting and inner optimizers where possible. Otherwise, report sensitivity or include a standardized baseline implementation.
- Missing baselines: Recent competitive batch BO methods such as local penalization (e.g., Gonzalez et al., 2016) or PPES are not compared; even if code was unavailable, a discussion is warranted.
- Experimental details: Please report S (Monte Carlo samples), step sizes/optimizer for stochastic gradient ascent, number of restarts, and ablations on M and S. Include results for different batch sizes q and higher-dimensional problems to probe scalability.
- The “Bayes-optimality” statement strictly holds on finite A; after discretization the guarantee is approximate. This is worth stating clearly to avoid overclaiming.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally clear, but notation is occasionally heavy and there are minor typos (e.g., “Main Stange”). Highlight the link to sequential KG when q=1, and explicitly show the reduction to q-EI in the noise-free, recommend-from-sampled-points case.
- Provide a compact algorithmic complexity table and pseudocode indicating the sources of stochasticity.

Significance and impact
- Batch BO is widely used in practice; a robust, noise-aware, jointly optimized acquisition that outperforms q-EI and UCB variants is valuable. The decision-theoretic framing and gradient-based optimization are likely to be adopted. The empirical improvements, especially under noise, are meaningful.

Reproducibility
- Code availability is a plus. To enable full reproduction, include all key hyperparameters (M, S, learning rates, number of iterations), seeds, and runtime budgets.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled extension of KG to batch with a practical, differentiable Monte Carlo estimator.
- Strong empirical gains, especially in noisy settings.
- Joint batch optimization and open-source implementation.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient complexity/scalability analysis and limited ablations.
- Potential fairness issues in baseline implementations.
- Some theoretical and presentation gaps (gradient derivation, discretization details).

Recommendation
Weak accept. The contribution is technically sound and practically useful, with promising results and code. Addressing the noted theoretical clarifications, fairer baselines, and scalability reporting would strengthen the paper further. Confidence: high.
