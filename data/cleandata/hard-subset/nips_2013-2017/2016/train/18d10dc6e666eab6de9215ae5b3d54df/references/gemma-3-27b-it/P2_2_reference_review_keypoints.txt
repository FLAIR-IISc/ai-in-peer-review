- Proposes q-KG, a novel parallel Knowledge Gradient method that selects batches by maximizing expected information gain.  
- Introduces an IPA-based gradient estimator to efficiently optimize the q-KG acquisition function.  
- Empirically outperforms existing parallel BO methods, especially in noisy evaluation settings, on synthetic and real hyperparameter tuning tasks.  
- Extends the decision-theoretic KG framework to the parallel/batch setting, providing a principled foundation.  
- Demonstrates computational practicality through gradient-based optimization, making q-KG applicable to higher dimensions and larger batches.  
- Provides code for reproducibility and further research.  
- Relies on discretizing the search space for gradient computation, introducing approximation and sensitivity to the number of discretization samples M.  
- Does not fully explore the impact of discretization choices (variation of M) in experiments.  
- Lacks discussion and empirical analysis of q-KG’s own hyperparameter sensitivity and guidance for selection.  
- Implementation complexity of the IPA-based approach may hinder adoption compared to simpler baseline methods.  
- Comparison to nested batch KG ([26]) is limited and focuses on computation rather than direct performance evaluation.  
- Highly relevant to NIPS due to importance of scalable parallel BO and solid grounding in GP and KG literature.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and presents a clear theoretical derivation.  
- Writing is generally clear, though IPA and q-KG explanations could use more illustrative examples.  
- Method is original in extending KG to batch settings with an efficient gradient approach.  
- Results are significant with strong empirical gains, particularly for noisy problems and complex hyperparameter tuning.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, novel method, compelling empirical results, efficient optimization, and available code.  
- Arguments against acceptance: discretization approximation, implementation complexity, and limited hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with valuable contributions; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions on discretization and hyperparameter sensitivity.
