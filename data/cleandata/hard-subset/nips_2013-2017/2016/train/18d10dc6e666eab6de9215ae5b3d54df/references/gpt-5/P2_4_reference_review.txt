Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function derived as a one-step Bayes-optimal policy for minimizing the minimum posterior mean when only one batch remains. It generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) to the parallel setting. Because exact evaluation and optimization of q-KG is intractable, the authors (i) express the updated posterior mean after a batch via a standard-normal draw Zq and Cholesky factorization, (ii) estimate q-KG by Monte Carlo over Zq, and (iii) derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch jointly. For continuous domains, they approximate the inner minimum over x by discretization using samples from the posterior distribution of the global minimizer, augmented with previously sampled points and the current candidate batch.

Empirically, q-KG is compared to several batch BO baselines (parallel EI as in MOE and Spearmint heuristics, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE) on standard synthetic functions and two ML hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR-10), in both noise-free and noisy settings. q-KG typically outperforms or matches competitors, with especially strong gains under noise. The method reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless case when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points, providing a consistency check. The paper relates to prior work on batch EI (including differentiable/closed-form variants), parallel UCB, and parallel entropy search, and to sequential KG.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria:

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The decision-theoretic derivation of q-KG as the expected reduction in min_x μ after one batch is clear and correct within the GP model. The Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG is standard. The IPA-based stochastic gradient for jointly optimizing all batch elements is plausible; the paper states unbiasedness under differentiability assumptions, with details in the supplement. Handling of ties and non-smoothness in the “min” over a finite set is discussed implicitly via measure-zero events; this is typical but would benefit from a brief clarification.
- Completeness: The paper covers modeling, acquisition definition, gradient estimation, and an end-to-end algorithm including hyperparameter refitting by MLE. It lacks theoretical analysis of optimization convergence (e.g., to stationary points under the stochastic gradient) and does not provide regret bounds or finite-sample guarantees—consistent with much of BO literature, but still a limitation.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are broad enough (4 synthetic functions up to d=6, two real tuning tasks). Gains under noise are well supported. However, two important omissions reduce the strength of the evidence: (1) no wall-clock/runtime or scalability analysis, despite the computationally heavier acquisition; (2) no experiments for larger batch sizes (only q=4 shown) though the method’s motivation is parallel settings. The CNN study averages over only 5 runs, which is on the low side.

Clarity:
- Overall, the paper is clearly written and well organized. The connection to sequential KG and reduction to EI in the noise-free restricted case are helpful.
- The discretization strategy (drawing M points from the posterior over the optimizer as in ES) is only briefly described; a few more implementation details or ablations (e.g., sensitivity to M, effect of including z(1:q) in An) would aid reproducibility and help readers assess approximation error.
- Minor typos and small clarity issues (e.g., “Main Stange”, consistent notation for Cholesky factors) could be polished.

Originality:
- Extending KG to batch in continuous domains and enabling its joint optimization with an IPA-based gradient estimator is a novel and useful contribution. While related ideas exist for batch EI gradients, and there is prior KG work for discrete/nested batches, the specific acquisition (q-KG) and its efficient stochastic gradient optimization are new. The reduction to EI under special conditions is an insightful link.
- Related work is broadly covered; however, the empirical comparison omits parallel entropy search (despite citing it), which is arguably the closest Bayesian objective-focused competitor.

Significance:
- Batch BO is practically important. A method that is principled, handles noise well, and can jointly optimize batch decisions is valuable. The empirical results suggest q-KG can be preferable to popular heuristics, especially with noise—an impactful setting for hyperparameter tuning.
- Public code increases potential adoption.
- The lack of runtime/scalability data and limited exploration of larger q temper the immediate practical significance claims. Still, the contribution is likely to influence both research and practice.

Pros:
- Principled derivation: Bayes-optimal one-batch lookahead on min posterior mean.
- Efficient and elegant stochastic gradient via IPA enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical performance, particularly under noise, on synthetic and real tasks.
- Clear link to EI and to sequential KG; public implementation.

Cons:
- No runtime or complexity analysis; no wall-clock comparisons to baselines.
- Only q=4 evaluated; scalability to larger batches is untested empirically.
- Discretization for inner minimization is somewhat ad hoc and under-described; no sensitivity analysis to M.
- Missing comparisons to parallel entropy search and other contemporary batch BO methods; CNN study has few repeats.

Questions/clarifications for authors:
- Please report wall-clock time per iteration and total time-to-best for all methods, and discuss how cost scales with q and d.
- How sensitive are results to M=1000 and to the discretization scheme? Any guidance for choosing M?
- Can you show results for larger q (e.g., 8, 16) and higher dimensions?
- How do you handle non-unique argmins when differentiating through the min? Is the gradient estimator robust in practice?
- Did you evaluate against parallel PES? If not, why?
- Any insights on heteroskedastic or non-Gaussian noise?

Minor issues:
- Fix typos (e.g., “Main Stange”), ensure consistent matrix factorization notation, and clarify the “samples from the global optima of the posterior” procedure.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. This is a solid, well-motivated contribution with a principled acquisition and a practical stochastic gradient optimization scheme, showing clear empirical benefits in noisy settings. Addressing runtime/scalability and expanding comparisons would further strengthen the paper.
