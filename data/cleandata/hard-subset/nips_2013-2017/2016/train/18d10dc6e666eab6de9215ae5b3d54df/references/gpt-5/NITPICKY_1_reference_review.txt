Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes q-KG, an acquisition function for batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization. It generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly by maximizing the expected decrease in the GP posterior predictive minimum after assimilating a batch of q observations. Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG is computationally heavy, the authors derive a Monte Carlo estimator and an unbiased stochastic gradient via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch decision. The method is related to classical KG (Frazier et al., 2008) and to batch BO via q-EI (Ginsbourger/Chevalier), GP-UCB variants (Desautels et al., Contal et al.), and batch ES/PPES (Hernández-Lobato et al., Shah & Ghahramani). Empirically, q-KG is compared against parallel EI (two implementations) and UCB variants on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks; it appears particularly strong in the presence of noise.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean decision-theoretic extension of KG to the batch setting, with a clear interpretation (one-step Bayes optimality w.r.t. the GP predictor minimum).
- Practical computational contribution: a stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG via IPA, which is nontrivial due to the min operators and dependence on Cholesky factors.
- Empirical results indicate competitive or superior simple regret, especially with observation noise.
- Open-source implementation and reuse of mature GP codebases increase reproducibility.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality/technical soundness
- Formal definition of q-KG: Eq. (4.1) uses En[·|y(z(1:q))], but the expectation should be w.r.t. the predictive distribution of y(z(1:q)) under the current posterior. As written, conditioning on y while also taking expectation is confusing. Later sections correctly recast this via Zq. Please fix the notation and ensure the equality to the Zq representation is proved or clearly referenced.
- Bayes-optimality claim: q-KG is Bayes-optimal for minimizing minx µ(n+q)(x) with one remaining batch. This is a specific decision problem (choose q sampling points; terminal recommendation minimizes the posterior mean). The paper should state this decision problem explicitly, including assumptions (fixed hyperparameters, noise model, discretization), and contrast it with policies minimizing expected simple regret or cumulative regret.
- Reduction to q-EI: The equivalence under noise-free evaluations and restricting the final recommendation to previously sampled points is plausible, but the derivation is terse and mixes µ(n+q) and y. Please provide a rigorous proof (for general q) or a precise reference.
- IPA gradient: The min operator induces nondifferentiability at ties. Unbiasedness of the IPA estimator typically requires almost-sure uniqueness of the argmin and regularity conditions. These assumptions, and handling of the set-valued subgradient at ties, are pushed to the supplement. At least summarize the conditions in the main text and discuss practical implications (e.g., adding small jitter).
- Differentiating through Cholesky: The gradient formula involves ∂D/∂z. In practice, one differentiates solves rather than the Cholesky factor explicitly. The paper should specify the numerically stable implementation used and complexity per Monte Carlo sample.
- Computational complexity: There is no cost analysis. Please provide asymptotic and practical costs as a function of n, q, M, and number of MC samples S, both for value and gradient (e.g., O(q^3) for batch covariance factorization plus O(M q^2) multiplies, etc.). Also provide runtime comparisons vs. baselines, as time is critical for batch BO.
- Discretization: The construction of An is insufficiently specified. “Samples from the global optima of the posterior” is unclear. ES/PPES use representer points via approximate posterior over minima; Thompson sampling draws GP functions and minimizes numerically. Please describe precisely how AMn is generated and with what optimizer/seeds. Also, including z(1:q) inside An makes the objective depend on the decision-dependent discretization, which can bias gradients and raises smoothness issues; discuss sensitivity to M and consider using a fixed An for the acquisition evaluation.
- Hyperparameters: Algorithm 1 refits GP hyperparameters by MLE after each batch. The Bayes-optimality argument assumes a fixed GP/hyperparameters; re-estimation breaks the decision-theoretic guarantee. Please acknowledge this and, if possible, evaluate q-KG with marginalized hyperparameters or at least show robustness to hyperparameter misspecification.
- Noisy setting: The model assumes homoscedastic Gaussian noise, but the “noisy logistic regression with small test sets” scenario is heteroscedastic (variance depends on hyperparameters and on the number of test points used). Please discuss this mismatch and its effect.

Clarity and presentation
- Writing issues: Numerous typos and formatting problems (e.g., “Main Stange,” “gaussian,” inconsistent capitalization, dangling “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)”), and equations with missing punctuation. Clean these up.
- Notation: Reuse of A both as feasible domain and as discretized set An in (4.1)/(4.2) is confusing. Use distinct symbols and emphasize that only the inner min is discretized.
- Algorithm 1: Step 4 says solve (4.2) but the text later maximizes q-KG(z(1:q),An). Clarify the actual objective optimized (discretized or continuous), optimizer used, initialization, step sizes, and stopping criteria. Report number of restarts and MC samples per iteration.
- References: Several entries are incomplete or malformed (missing years, venues; broken URLs; “E.C. etal”; missing author initials). Please correct.

Originality
- The high-level idea (batch KG) is natural and meaningful. The main novelty is the IPA-based gradient estimator for joint batch selection. Please better position against (i) q-EI with gradients (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.), (ii) PPES/parallel ES, (iii) local penalization batch BO (González et al., 2016), and (iv) batch Thompson sampling. Some of these are not cited or not compared experimentally.

Significance/empirical evaluation
- Synthetic benchmarks are standard and useful, but results rely on figures not included here. Key missing details: number of total evaluations, q values beyond q=4, S and M, and variance across seeds. Please report wall-clock times, not only iteration counts.
- Real tasks: For CIFAR-10, only 5 runs and a tutorial-level CNN with small epoch range (1–10). Without controlling for evaluation time (hyperparameters drastically change runtime), comparing by iterations can be misleading in parallel settings. Please normalize by wall-clock time or show both.
- Baseline parity: Ensure all baselines use identical kernels/priors/normalizations and hyperparameter learning. Spearmint’s default settings differ substantially (e.g., input warping). Report details to ensure fairness.
- Ablations: Show sensitivity to M, S, and batch size q; compare optimization with and without gradient (e.g., random search over batches or CMA-ES); investigate noisy heteroscedastic settings.

Pros
- Clear and well-motivated acquisition function with a principled one-step interpretation.
- Useful stochastic gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Solid empirical gains in noisy settings; open-source code.

Cons
- Notational/derivational clarity issues and missing regularity conditions for IPA.
- Decision-dependent discretization and insufficient detail on discretization procedure.
- No complexity or runtime analysis; limited scalability evidence (q fixed at 4).
- Experimental details and baseline parity are under-specified; limited statistical rigor.
- Numerous presentation and citation issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The core idea is appealing and likely impactful, but the paper in its current form has too many technical and presentation shortcomings and lacks essential computational and experimental details. Addressing the points above (especially clarity of the objective/gradients, discretization, runtime/scalability, and fair/time-normalized comparisons) would substantially strengthen the submission.

Reviewer confidence: High.
