

# Peer Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel parallel Bayesian optimization method, the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which extends the sequential knowledge gradient acquisition function to the batch setting. The authors derive q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective, defining it as the expected improvement in the minimum of the GP predictor after adding a batch of q points. To address the computational challenge of optimizing q-KG, they develop an efficient gradient estimation method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The paper demonstrates q-KG's superiority over existing parallel BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic functions and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks, with particularly strong performance in noisy settings.

The work builds upon the knowledge gradient framework [17] and distinguishes itself from prior parallel BO methods that typically optimize batches greedily by considering the batch collectively. The paper correctly situates itself within the growing literature on parallel Bayesian optimization, acknowledging key related works such as parallel EI [10, 25] and batch UCB [5, 3].

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a strong decision-theoretic justification for the q-KG acquisition function, correctly noting that it is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the GP predictor's minimum with one batch remaining. This contrasts with expected improvement, which assumes the final solution must be among previously evaluated points.

2. **Computational Innovation**: The IPA-based gradient estimation approach is clever and addresses a critical bottleneck in parallel BO. The method efficiently computes gradients of q-KG, which would otherwise require prohibitively expensive Monte Carlo sampling.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental section thoroughly evaluates q-KG on both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical ML hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The noisy setting experiments are particularly convincing, demonstrating q-KG's robustness where other methods struggle.

4. **Practical Impact**: The paper clearly demonstrates real-world value by showing that q-KG can significantly improve machine learning model performance (e.g., reducing CNN test error from 14% to 11% on CIFAR10).

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Scalability Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address scalability to large batch sizes (q) or high dimensions (d). The discretization approach (M=1000 samples) becomes computationally prohibitive for large q or d, which are common in practical applications. The authors should provide more concrete analysis of computational complexity.

2. **Theoretical Justification Gap**: While claiming q-KG is Bayes-optimal for the single-batch setting, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from other acquisition functions in practical terms. For instance, it's unclear how q-KG's performance advantage translates to real-world regret minimization beyond the immediate metric used.

3. **Comparative Analysis**: The paper compares against parallel EI and UCB-based methods but doesn't sufficiently differentiate from other gradient-based batch acquisition functions (e.g., [2, 15, 25]). The theoretical distinction between q-KG and these methods isn't fully clarified.

4. **Discretization Choice**: The choice of M=1000 for discretization seems arbitrary, and the paper doesn't investigate how this parameter affects performance. The authors should explore sensitivity to this choice and potentially propose a principled method for selecting M.

5. **Noisy Setting Assumptions**: While the noisy experiments are compelling, the paper assumes constant noise variance across the domain. In practice, noise variance may vary significantly, and the authors don't discuss how q-KG would handle this more complex scenario.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a significant methodological contribution to batch Bayesian optimization with strong theoretical grounding and compelling empirical results. The q-KG acquisition function addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by considering the batch collectively rather than greedily. The IPA-based computational approach is a notable technical innovation that makes the method practical.

However, the paper has some important limitations regarding scalability and theoretical justification that need to be addressed. The experimental results are strong, but the paper would be significantly improved by:

1. Providing a more thorough analysis of computational complexity and scalability
2. Clarifying the theoretical distinction between q-KG and similar batch acquisition functions
3. Investigating the sensitivity of the discretization parameter M
4. Discussing how the method would extend to non-constant noise variance

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to Bayesian optimization that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. While the weaknesses noted above are important, they don't undermine the core contribution, which is well-motivated and effectively demonstrated. The paper advances the state of the art in parallel Bayesian optimization, particularly for noisy problems where it shows clear advantages.

The authors should address the scalability concerns and provide more detailed theoretical justification in their revision. With these improvements, the paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. The work is clearly relevant to NIPS' focus on machine learning and optimization, and the practical impact on hyperparameter tuning makes it particularly valuable to the ML community.

## Key Arguments for Acceptance
- Novel acquisition function with strong theoretical foundation
- Efficient computational method via IPA
- Comprehensive empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world problems
- Clear performance advantage, especially in noisy settings
- Practical impact on machine learning hyperparameter tuning

## Key Arguments for Revision
- Need for more detailed scalability analysis
- Clarification of theoretical distinctions from related work
- Justification for discretization parameter choice
- Discussion of non-constant noise variance scenarios
