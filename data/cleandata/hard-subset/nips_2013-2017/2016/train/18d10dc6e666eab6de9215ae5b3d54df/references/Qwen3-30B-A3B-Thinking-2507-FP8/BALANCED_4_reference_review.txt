

# Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "Parallel Knowledge Gradient" (q-KG), which extends the knowledge gradient approach to the parallel setting. The authors derive a new acquisition function that measures the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor after evaluating a batch of q points, rather than a single point. They address the computational challenges of maximizing this acquisition function through infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling efficient gradient-based optimization. The paper compares q-KG against several parallel BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, parallel UCB with exploration) on synthetic functions and practical machine learning tuning tasks, demonstrating consistent improvements, especially in noisy settings.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The extension of knowledge gradient to the parallel setting is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper clearly articulates how q-KG differs from previous batch methods (which typically optimize sequentially or use greedy approaches) and provides a decision-theoretic justification for the approach.

2. **Computational Innovation**: The use of IPA to efficiently compute gradients of the q-KG acquisition function is a clever and practical solution to a major computational challenge in batch BO. This makes the method implementable for real-world problems.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering both synthetic benchmarks (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The demonstration of superior performance in noisy settings is particularly valuable, as this is a common real-world scenario.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear, the algorithm is precisely described (Algorithm 1), and the experimental setup is well-justified.

5. **Reproducibility**: The authors provide code at https://github.com/wujian16/qKG, which significantly enhances the paper's impact and reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper addresses computational challenges, it lacks a formal complexity analysis of the IPA-based approach. The authors state that naive maximization would be computationally intensive but don't quantify the computational savings of their method or analyze how the complexity scales with q and d (dimensionality).

2. **Sensitivity Analysis**: The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of q-KG to the choice of M (the number of points used for discretization in Section 5.3). This is a critical hyperparameter that could significantly impact performance, and the paper doesn't provide guidance on selecting M appropriately.

3. **Comparison with Recent Methods**: The paper mentions [15, 25] as related work but doesn't directly compare with these methods in experiments. The authors should include these approaches in their benchmarking to better establish their method's relative standing.

4. **High-Dimensional Performance**: The paper doesn't explore how q-KG performs in high-dimensional settings (d > 10), which is a common challenge in Bayesian optimization. The authors should include experiments on higher-dimensional problems to demonstrate the method's scalability.

5. **Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper states that q-KG is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor when only one batch remains, but doesn't discuss how these guarantees extend to multiple batches (the typical BO scenario). This is an important theoretical limitation that should be addressed.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the batch Bayesian optimization literature. The technical novelty is significant, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. The main weaknesses are the lack of computational complexity analysis, insufficient exploration of high-dimensional performance, and incomplete comparison with some recent methods.

The paper demonstrates that q-KG provides consistent improvements over existing batch BO methods, particularly in noisy settings, which is highly relevant for practical applications. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization that practitioners will find useful.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a formal computational complexity analysis of the IPA-based approach, comparing it to naive maximization and other batch BO methods.

2. Include a sensitivity analysis for the discretization parameter M, providing guidance on appropriate values for different problem settings.

3. Expand the experimental section to include comparisons with [15, 25] and experiments on higher-dimensional problems (d > 10).

4. Discuss how the theoretical guarantees extend from the single-batch case to multiple batches, or clarify the limitations of the current theoretical results.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how q-KG might be adapted to handle constraints, which is an important extension of Bayesian optimization.

This paper is of high quality and makes a valuable contribution to the field. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent candidate for NIPS. The authors have provided a solid theoretical foundation, a practical computational method, and compelling empirical results that demonstrate the value of their approach. The paper is well-positioned to influence both the theory and practice of batch Bayesian optimization.
