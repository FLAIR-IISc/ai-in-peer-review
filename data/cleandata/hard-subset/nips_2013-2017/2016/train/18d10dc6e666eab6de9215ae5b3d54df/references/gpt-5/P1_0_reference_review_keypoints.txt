- Proposal: q-KG, a batch acquisition function generalizing the knowledge gradient to jointly select q points as the expected one-step Bayes-optimal improvement in the minimum posterior mean after a batch observation.  
- Implementation: Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG with an efficient gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based batch optimization.  
- Reduction: In the noise-free case with final recommendation restricted to evaluated points, q-KG reduces to parallel Expected Improvement (parallel EI).  
- Continuous-domain handling: Inner minimizations approximated by discretizing the domain A using samples of the GP posterior optima (as in entropy search).  
- Empirical results: q-KG outperforms or matches strong baselines (parallel EI variants, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, with pronounced gains under observation noise.  
- Open-source: An implementation is provided.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on sequential KG and relates to batch EI, constant liar, UCB-based batch policies, and PES/ES; main novelty is a practical, differentiable stochastic estimator for continuous batch KG.  
- Technical soundness: One-step Bayes-optimal derivation and reduction to parallel EI are correct; Monte Carlo and IPA gradient estimators are plausible given differentiable mean/kernel assumptions.  
- Required clarification: The derivative term ∂/∂zij µ(n)(x*(before)) appears incorrect—needs clarification if it’s a typo or contextual redefinition.  
- Non-differentiability: Gradient through the inner “min over A” is only almost-surely differentiable—describe handling of ties, subgradients, or tie-breaking and state measure-zero assumptions.  
- Complexity details missing: Provide explicit per-iteration cost in terms of q, M (discretization size), and S (Monte Carlo samples); computing σ̃ and its gradient involves O(q^3) Cholesky and O(M q^2) per Z sample.  
- Discretization details: Explain how AM_n is constructed (how GP posterior samples and optimizations are drawn), choice of M, and its impact on q-KG bias/variance.  
- Empirics gap: Wall-clock runtimes and scalability comparisons versus baselines are missing and needed given increased computational burden.  
- Ablations requested: Include ablations on q, M, and number of Monte Carlo samples to show robustness and trade-offs.  
- Missing baseline: No comparison to (parallel) ES/PES; adding this would strengthen empirical claims.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-written but notation is heavy; tighten the dual use of A and fix minor typos (e.g., “Main Stange,” capitalization).  
- Practical details: Clarify how the final recommendation (argmin over µ on continuous A) is solved in practice (e.g., multi-start local search) and state IPA regularity conditions with pointer to proofs.  
- Originality: Moderately high—extends KG to practical continuous batch settings with efficient stochastic gradients, unlike prior discrete/nested batch KG work.  
- Significance: High for BO community due to principled batch handling of noise and collective batch optimization; adoption depends on computational cost.  
- Pros: Decision-theoretic grounding, connection to EI, practical gradient-based optimization via IPA, strong empirical results, and open-source code.  
- Cons: Lacks runtime/scalability analysis, no PES/ES comparison, some technical clarity gaps, and no theoretical regret/convergence discussion.  
- Suggestions: Clarify derivative and nondifferentiability issues, report per-iteration wall-clock times and scaling, detail AM_n construction and sensitivity to M and S, compare to parallel ES/PES, and report sensitivity to kernel choice and hyperparameter refitting frequency.  
- Recommendation: Lean accept, contingent on addressing clarity points and adding runtime/scalability (and ideally PES) comparisons.
