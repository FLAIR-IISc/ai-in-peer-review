Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The acquisition is defined as the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after adding a batch of q evaluations, conditioned on the current posterior. This is Bayes-optimal in the one-batch-to-go setting for minimizing the posterior mean. The authors show that q-KG reduces to parallel Expected Improvement (q-EI) in the noiseless case when the final recommendation is constrained to previously sampled points. Because exact evaluation is intractable for q>1, the paper proposes Monte Carlo evaluation combined with an efficient unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based joint optimization of the batch. For continuous domains, the acquisition’s inner minimization is approximated by discretizing A using a set of “representer” points inspired by predictive entropy search (PES/PPES), extended by previously evaluated points and the candidate batch. Empirically, q-KG is compared against parallel EI (two implementations), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and CNNs (CIFAR10), in both noiseless and noisy settings. q-KG is consistently competitive and often superior, particularly under noise.

This work relates closely to: (i) KG for sequential BO (Frazier et al.), (ii) batch EI including closed-form and gradient-based q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.; Wang et al.), (iii) batch PES/PPES (Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani), and (iv) batch UCB variants (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.). It extends KG to the parallel setting with a continuous domain and differentiable acquisition, and proposes an efficient IPA-based gradient estimator.

Quality
- Technical correctness: The q-KG definition as an expected reduction in min_x μ^(n+q)(x) is sound and matches the one-step Bayes-optimality criterion for minimizing the posterior mean. The reduction to q-EI in the noiseless and “final choice among sampled points” regime is correct.
- Gradient estimator: The IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator relies on differentiability of μ and K and conditions ensuring interchange of differentiation and expectation and handling of the argmin. Non-differentiability at ties in the argmin is a subtle point; the paper notes that details appear in the supplement. Providing explicit conditions (e.g., almost sure uniqueness of the minimizer, Lipschitz continuity) would strengthen confidence.
- Computation: The approach requires Monte Carlo over Z_q and minimization over a discretized set A_n at each sample, with cost scaling in M=|A_n| and q (Cholesky factorization of q×q blocks). This is reasonable for small-to-moderate q and M, and the paper reports using M=1000. A complexity analysis and wall-clock comparisons vs q-EI/PPES would help assess practicality.
- Experiments: Benchmarks are standard (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann; q=4). The real tasks (MNIST logistic regression and CIFAR10 CNN) are appropriate and demonstrate noisy and expensive evaluations. However, experimental details affecting reproducibility and fairness are missing: number of Monte Carlo samples for acquisition/gradient, optimizer settings (learning rates, restarts), batch size sensitivity (q>4), runtime comparisons, and ablations on M and noise variance learning. The reported gains, especially under noise, are compelling but would benefit from these additions.

Clarity
The overall exposition is clear, with a precise definition of q-KG and a concrete algorithm. The derivation of the Monte Carlo estimator and the IPA gradient is sketched adequately for the main paper, but some steps (conditions for unbiasedness, computation of derivatives through the Cholesky factor) are deferred; summarizing key assumptions in the main text would improve clarity. The discretization strategy (“draw M samples from the global optima of the posterior”) is underspecified; explicitly describing the PPES/PES-based sampling procedure or providing pseudocode would aid reproducibility. There are minor typos and formatting issues (e.g., “Main Stange,” inconsistent notation) that can be cleaned up.

Originality
Extending KG to batch selection over continuous domains with a differentiable, jointly optimized acquisition and providing an efficient IPA-based stochastic gradient appears novel relative to prior batch KG work over finite sets and to batch EI/ES methods. The decision-theoretic framing that remains Bayes-optimal in the one-step batch setting distinguishes it from UCB-style methods optimized for cumulative regret. The discretization of the inner minimization is conceptually similar to PES/PPES representer-point approaches but applied in a different acquisition.

Significance
Batch BO is widely used in practice. A principled, noise-robust acquisition that can be optimized efficiently is valuable. The empirical results suggest meaningful gains over strong baselines, especially under observation noise, where q-KG’s information-theoretic objective is expected to help. If the method scales to larger q and dimensions with acceptable runtime, it could have impact in hyperparameter tuning and other black-box optimization tasks. The lack of runtime analysis and limited exploration of larger batch sizes somewhat limit the demonstrated practical significance.

Pros
- Principled acquisition: Bayes-optimal one-batch decision; reduces to q-EI in appropriate limits.
- Novel and generally applicable IPA-based stochastic gradient for joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical performance, particularly in noisy settings; open-source implementation.
- Clear connection to and extension of established BO/KG literature.

Cons
- Inner minimization relies on discretization with limited detail; potential bias and sensitivity to M not analyzed.
- Missing theoretical guarantees beyond one-step optimality (e.g., asymptotic consistency, regret bounds).
- Incomplete experimental reporting: no runtime/complexity comparisons; limited q and dimensionality; few ablations.
- Some clarity issues (assumptions for unbiasedness, details of representer-point sampling) and minor typos.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a computational complexity analysis and wall-clock/runtime comparisons to q-EI (MOE), PPES, and UCB-based methods across q and M.
- Detail the discretization procedure for A_n (sampling of representer points), and include ablations on M and its impact on performance.
- Report the number of Monte Carlo samples used for acquisition and gradient estimates, optimizer details (step sizes, restarts), and stopping criteria.
- Evaluate larger batch sizes (e.g., q=8,16) and higher-dimensional problems; include sensitivity to noise misspecification.
- Clarify conditions ensuring the unbiasedness of the IPA gradient (uniqueness of minimizers, differentiability), and summarize key derivation steps in the main text.
- Proofread and fix minor typos; standardize notation.

Overall, this is a solid and well-motivated contribution to batch BO with promising empirical results and a novel optimization approach for a principled acquisition. The paper would be strengthened by additional algorithmic details, runtime analyses, and broader experimental coverage.
