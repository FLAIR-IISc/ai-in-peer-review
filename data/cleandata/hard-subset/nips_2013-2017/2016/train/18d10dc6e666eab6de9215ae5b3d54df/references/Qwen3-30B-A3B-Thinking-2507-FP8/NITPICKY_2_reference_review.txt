

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG), extending the sequential knowledge gradient approach to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that jointly optimizes a batch of q points, rather than sequentially adding points as in prior work. They develop a computationally efficient method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of the acquisition function, enabling practical optimization. The paper demonstrates that q-KG outperforms several benchmarks on synthetic functions and real machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy settings.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian optimization (parallel evaluation), which is increasingly relevant as optimization is applied to distributed computing environments. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function from decision-theoretic principles is sound, and the computational approach using IPA for gradient estimation is novel and well-motivated. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic benchmarks and practical machine learning tasks, with clear demonstration of superior performance in noisy settings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Inconsistency in Reduction to Parallel EI:** The paper claims "q-KG will reduce to the parallel EI algorithm if function evaluations are noise-free and the final recommendation is restricted to the previous sampling decisions." This claim is incorrect. When evaluations are noise-free and the solution is restricted to previously sampled points, the sequential knowledge gradient does not reduce to expected improvement (EI) - rather, it reduces to a different acquisition function. The paper confuses sequential KG with parallel EI, which creates confusion about the relationship between these methods. The correct statement should be that when evaluations are noise-free and the solution must be selected from the evaluated points, the parallel KG acquisition function reduces to parallel EI. This fundamental error undermines the theoretical foundation of the paper.

2. **Formatting Inconsistencies:** The paper contains several minor but distracting formatting issues. The citation "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain." contains a grammatical error (should be "The" instead of "th"). The notation "q-KG" is inconsistently used throughout, sometimes with a hyphen and sometimes without. The reference list formatting is inconsistent (e.g., some entries have commas before "In", others don't).

3. **Lack of Justification for Discretization Parameter:** The paper uses M=1000 for discretization in Section 5.3 without justification. The choice of this parameter significantly impacts both computational complexity and accuracy, but the authors provide no analysis of how this choice affects the performance of the algorithm or why this specific value was chosen over alternatives. This is a critical detail for reproducibility.

4. **Ambiguous Notation:** The paper consistently uses "min x∈A" throughout, which is unconventional in Bayesian optimization (where maximization is more common). While the authors state they're framing the problem as minimization, this should be clearly stated upfront and consistently used. The notation "min x∈x(1:n)" in Equation (4.1) is particularly confusing since x(1:n) is a set of points, not a domain.

5. **Insufficient Comparison with "Constant Liar":** The paper mentions "constant liar" as a benchmark but fails to properly explain this method or justify its relevance to the comparison. The description is vague, and the authors do not clarify how this method differs from the approaches they're comparing to, making the comparison less meaningful.

6. **Incomplete Experimental Details:** The paper claims q-KG "provides especially large value when function evaluations are noisy," but the experimental results in Section 6.2.2 for noisy logistic regression lack sufficient detail. The paper states that q-KG "improves [CNN] to around 11% test error" but doesn't provide comparative results for other methods, making it difficult to assess the magnitude of the improvement.

**Recommendation:** While the paper presents a novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization with promising experimental results, the technical inaccuracies (particularly the incorrect reduction to parallel EI) and the lack of sufficient detail on key implementation choices undermine the paper's credibility. The formatting inconsistencies and ambiguous notation further detract from the presentation. These issues, while seemingly minor, are fundamental to the paper's technical contribution and reproducibility.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by correcting the technical error regarding the relationship between q-KG and parallel EI, providing a justification for the discretization parameter M, and clarifying the experimental comparisons. Without these revisions, the paper's technical claims are questionable, and the contribution is not as significant as presented.

**Overall:** The paper has a solid foundation but contains critical technical inaccuracies that prevent it from being a strong contribution to the field. I recommend rejection due to these fundamental technical issues, despite the promising experimental results. The authors should address these issues before resubmission.
