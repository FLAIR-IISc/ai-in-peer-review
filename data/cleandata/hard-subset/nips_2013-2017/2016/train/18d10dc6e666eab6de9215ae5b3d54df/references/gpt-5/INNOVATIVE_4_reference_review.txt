Summary
This paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The key idea is to choose the next batch by maximizing the expected one-step reduction in the minimum posterior mean (i.e., Bayes-optimal in the single-batch-to-go setting). The authors show that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under noise-free evaluations when the final recommendation must be one of the evaluated points. Since evaluating and optimizing q-KG is expensive, they derive an efficient unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and use it within multi-start stochastic gradient ascent. For continuous domains, they approximate the inner minimization using a dynamic discretization constructed from samples of the posterior optimizer distribution, augmented with previously evaluated and candidate points. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR-10) show that q-KG is competitive or superior to parallel EI variants, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with particularly notable gains under noise.

Strengths
- Novelty/significance: Extending KG to parallel batch selection with noisy observations is a nontrivial and timely contribution. Unlike popular batch EI/UCB approaches, q-KG is explicitly decision-theoretic and Bayes-optimal for the one-batch remaining case, which is compelling for BO where simple regret matters.
- Methodological contribution: An unbiased IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG (under smoothness conditions), enabling practical joint optimization of q locations. This is technically interesting and broadly useful for other parallel BO objectives that involve minima of random fields.
- Conceptual unification: The reduction to parallel EI in the noise-free restricted-solution case is elegant and clarifies where q-KG gains under noise and when the methods coincide.
- Practical impact: Code is released; empirical results indicate consistent improvements over established baselines, with the largest margins in noisy settings—an important regime in modern hyperparameter tuning.
- Clarity: The setup, relation to prior work, and algorithmic outline are generally clear. The separation between the acquisition’s inner domain (A for evaluating q-KG) and the optimization domain for z(1:q) is called out.

Weaknesses/Questions
- Evaluation scope: Experiments are limited to q=4. Given the motivation (parallel resources, computational efficiency when q is large), results for larger batch sizes (e.g., q=8 or 16) would be informative. Wall-clock comparisons would also help justify computational efficiency claims.
- Comparisons: While the paper compares against parallel EI (MOE, Spearmint) and UCB variants, it does not compare to information-theoretic batch methods such as PPES/parallel ES, or to batch Thompson sampling/local penalization approaches commonly used in practice. Including PPES would strengthen the empirical case given the conceptual proximity.
- Discretization dependence: The q-KG value and gradient are computed over a discretized set An. Performance may hinge on M and the quality of the posterior-optimizer samples. There is no sensitivity analysis to M or the number of Monte Carlo samples for gradient estimation, nor a discussion of bias introduced by this approximation. In higher dimensions, M=1000 may be insufficient.
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond the one-step Bayes-optimality interpretation, there are no regret or asymptotic consistency results (even under standard GP assumptions), and no convergence analysis of the stochastic optimization beyond the unbiasedness of the gradient estimator. This is acceptable for NIPS but should be acknowledged.
- Technical details: 
  - The differentiability assumptions needed for IPA (e.g., uniqueness of argmins with probability 1) and the handling of non-differentiable events are deferred to the supplement; a brief discussion in the main text would help.
  - Gradients through the Cholesky factor are nontrivial; an explicit, stable implementation strategy (e.g., via triangular solves rather than explicit derivatives of D) should be clarified.
  - Retraining GP hyperparameters at each iteration can confound comparisons; reporting fixed-hyperparameter ablations or at least wall-clock would add transparency.
- Minor clarity issues: The dual use of “A” is acknowledged but still confusing; consider renaming the discretized set to S_n or similar. Some typos (“Main Stange”).

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with a sensible derivation of q-KG and a credible IPA estimator. The claims are supported by experiments, particularly in noisy settings, though broader baselines and larger-q studies would improve confidence. Some practical implementation details are deferred to the supplement.

Clarity
Generally well organized and readable. A few notational/terminology fixes would help.

Originality
High. While KG is known in the sequential setting, a practically optimizable, noisy batch extension with an efficient gradient estimator is a meaningful advance.

Significance
High for the BO community. If the empirical gains under noise and for larger q hold, practitioners will likely adopt this.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments with larger batches and report wall-clock times.
- Include PPES (or explain computational reasons for omission) and, if feasible, batch Thompson sampling/local penalization baselines.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for M (discretization size) and Monte Carlo budget, and a brief discussion of the bias-variance trade-off.
- Expand on the IPA assumptions and stable gradient computation through the Cholesky factor in the main text.
- Clarify the effect of hyperparameter re-fitting on results.

Reasons to accept
- Introduces a principled, novel acquisition for batch BO with noise.
- Clever and generalizable stochastic gradient estimator enables practical joint optimization.
- Consistent empirical improvements, especially in noisy regimes of practical relevance.
- Bridges to EI and situates method clearly in literature; code availability.

Reasons to reject
- Missing key baselines (PPES), limited exploration of larger batch sizes, and lack of wall-clock comparisons.
- Dependence on discretization without sensitivity analysis may limit robustness, especially in higher dimensions.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The conceptual novelty and potential practical impact outweigh the experimental limitations, and the work opens a promising direction for information-based batch BO under noise.
