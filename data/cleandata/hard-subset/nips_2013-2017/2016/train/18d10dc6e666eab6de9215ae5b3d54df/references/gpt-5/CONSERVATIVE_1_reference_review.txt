Summary
This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the Knowledge Gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. q-KG maximizes the expected one-step reduction in the GP posterior mean’s minimum, which is Bayes-optimal in the single-remaining-batch setting. Because evaluating and especially optimizing q-KG is computationally demanding, the authors derive a Monte Carlo estimator and an IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator to enable gradient-based optimization. For continuous domains they approximate the acquisition by discretizing A using a set of sampled candidate points, augmented with past evaluations and the current batch. Experiments on standard synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks (including noisy settings) show consistent improvements over parallel EI (two implementations) and UCB-based batch methods, with especially large gains under noise.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself against batch EI [2,15,25], UCB-based batch policies [3,5], and batch entropy search [18]. It builds on sequential KG [7,17] and the continuous-parameter correlated KG [16]. The parallel selection is more similar in spirit to [2,15,18] (jointly optimizing the batch) than to greedy constructions like constant liar. The use of IPA for differentiating a Monte Carlo estimator of a batch acquisition is related to gradient-based q-EI estimation [15,25], but applied here to the KG objective. The authors claim Bayes-optimality in the single-step setting (as in KG) and note that q-KG reduces to parallel EI in a noiseless setting when restricting the final recommendation to sampled points.

Strengths
- Decision-theoretic objective: Clear derivation of a principled, one-step Bayes-optimal batch policy for minimizing the GP posterior mean’s minimum.
- Gradient estimator: The IPA-based stochastic gradient for q-KG is technically interesting and, if correct and stable, can make joint batch optimization practical.
- Empirical performance: On multiple synthetic functions and two ML hyperparameter tuning tasks, q-KG often outperforms strong baselines, especially in noisy evaluations, where EI and UCB can struggle.
- Connections to existing methods: The reduction to parallel EI in a special case is a useful sanity check and helps position the contribution.
- Reproducibility aided by code release.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond the one-step Bayes-optimality interpretation (which applies to the minimum of the posterior mean, not necessarily f itself), there are no regret or consistency results, even in simplified settings. Competing methods like GP-UCB provide such guarantees.
- Discretization dependence on z: The paper approximates q-KG by discretizing A using An = AMn ∪ x(1:n) ∪ z(1:q). This makes the inner minimization set depend on the decision variables z, raising differentiability and bias issues that are not discussed. The unbiasedness of the IPA gradient estimator no longer holds if the feasible set itself depends on z, and the effect of this approximation on optimization quality is not analyzed.
- IPA conditions and non-differentiability: The estimator’s validity requires smooth µ and K, but g involves minima over sets, which are non-differentiable when there are ties or near-ties. The paper defers details to the supplement; however, practical stability (e.g., tie-breaking, subgradient handling) is critical and not discussed.
- Computational complexity and scaling: The method relies on repeated Cholesky factorizations and Monte Carlo sampling. There is no complexity analysis in terms of q, d, and M (discretization size), nor wall-clock comparisons. Experiments use q = 4; claims about “large q” are not substantiated empirically.
- Comparison set: Batch ES/PPES [18] is a closely related, joint, information-theoretic baseline; it is cited but apparently not included in experiments. For EI, it is unclear whether the strongest gradient-based q-EI implementations [15,25] (with careful optimization) were used/fairly tuned, versus default MOE/Spearmint settings. This matters for the strength of the empirical claim.
- Practical details and reproducibility gaps: Important choices are under-specified: number of Monte Carlo samples for q-KG and its gradient, step sizes, stopping criteria, multi-start strategy, and the method used to produce AMn (“samples from the global optima of the posterior” is vague; PES uses a nontrivial procedure). Without these, it is hard to reproduce the results.
- Handling of discrete/integer variables: The tuning tasks include batch size, kernel size, channel counts, etc., which are discrete/integer. The paper does not explain how the GP and acquisition handle such variables (continuous relaxation? rounding?), which can affect fairness versus baselines and performance.
- Statistical rigor: Some experiments use few runs (e.g., 5 for CNN), and the reported metrics focus on test error without runtime. Confidence intervals are reported inconsistently, and significance tests are absent.

Questions for the authors
1. How is AMn constructed concretely? If following [11,18], please detail the sampling and optimization steps. How sensitive are results to M and this procedure?
2. Given An depends on z, does your gradient estimator account for this? If not, can you justify the bias and show empirically that fixing AMn (without including z) yields similar performance?
3. What are the Monte Carlo sample sizes used for estimating q-KG and its gradient, and how do variance and runtime scale with q and d?
4. Did you compare against PPES [18]? If not, why? Can you include it or discuss computational trade-offs and accuracy?
5. How are integer/discrete hyperparameters modeled and selected?
6. Can you provide wall-clock time comparisons per iteration and overall (including GP hyperparameter refits) across methods?
7. How robust is q-KG to GP hyperparameter mis-specification and to different noise levels? Any ablations?

Minor comments
- Several typos and grammatical issues (e.g., “Main Stange”, missing references formatting). Please proofread.
- Clarify the claim that the method reduces to parallel EI; currently the algebra uses min y(1:n) and could be made more precise.
- Provide an explicit complexity discussion and practical limits for q, d, and n.

Evaluation
- Quality: Moderate. The objective is well-motivated; the estimator is plausible but insufficiently justified in the presence of discretization tied to z. Empirics are promising but lack some strong baselines and runtime analysis.
- Clarity: Fair; key implementation details and some derivations are deferred or omitted; discretization procedure is under-specified.
- Originality: Moderate to good; extending KG to batch with a differentiable Monte Carlo estimator is a meaningful contribution beyond sequential KG and existing q-EI work.
- Significance: Potentially significant for practitioners facing noisy parallel BO, but current validation is not yet rigorous enough.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but I would like to see clearer theoretical justification around the estimator under discretization, stronger and fairer baselines (including PPES), and thorough runtime/scaling analyses before acceptance.
