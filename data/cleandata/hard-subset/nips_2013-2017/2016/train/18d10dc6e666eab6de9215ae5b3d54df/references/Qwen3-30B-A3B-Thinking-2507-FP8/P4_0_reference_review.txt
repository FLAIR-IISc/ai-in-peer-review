

**Review**

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method, the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which generalizes the knowledge gradient acquisition function to the parallel evaluation setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretically motivated acquisition function that evaluates a batch of q points collectively rather than sequentially, addressing a key limitation in prior batch BO approaches. They develop an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize q-KG, overcoming the high computational cost of naive maximization. The paper compares q-KG against established batch BO methods including parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration on synthetic functions and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The authors demonstrate consistent improvements, particularly in noisy settings.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novelty and Technical Soundness**: The generalization of knowledge gradient to the batch setting is a clear and significant contribution, as prior batch methods (e.g., iterative parallel EI) do not optimize the batch collectively. The theoretical derivation of q-KG is rigorous, with clear connections to sequential knowledge gradient and a reduction to parallel EI in the noise-free case. The IPA-based gradient computation is technically sound and well-motivated, addressing a critical computational bottleneck.

2. **Experimental Rigor**: The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering diverse synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and two practical machine learning applications. The results consistently show q-KG outperforms baselines, with particularly pronounced advantages in noisy settings (e.g., 3% error reduction on CIFAR10 CNN tuning). The noise-free experiments on MNIST and CIFAR10 demonstrate clear practical relevance.

3. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with logical flow and precise notation. The mathematical derivations are accessible, and the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear. The experimental setup (including MLE for noise estimation, discretization strategy with M=1000) is well-justified and reproducible.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Comparison to Recent Work**: While the paper cites relevant prior work (e.g., [2, 15, 18, 25]), it omits comparison to the 2016 NIPS paper "Parallel Predictive Entropy Search" by Shah et al., which also addresses batch BO using a different acquisition function. A direct comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper emphasizes the efficiency of IPA but lacks a formal complexity analysis of q-KG optimization versus alternatives (e.g., batch UCB). While IPA is computationally efficient, quantifying the scaling with q and dimensionality would better contextualize the contribution.

3. **Discretization Justification**: The choice of M=1000 for discretization is reasonable but not thoroughly justified. The paper acknowledges discretization as an "interesting research topic" but does not discuss potential limitations (e.g., performance degradation in high dimensions or with non-convex domains).

**Significance Assessment**  
The work addresses a critical practical problem: parallelizing Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning, where simultaneous evaluations are common (e.g., distributed computing). The consistent performance gains—especially in noisy settings—demonstrate clear practical significance. The method advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally feasible batch BO approach that outperforms existing methods. The code availability further enhances reproducibility and adoption potential.

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a strong contribution to Bayesian optimization with a novel, well-supported method that delivers consistent empirical improvements. The technical soundness, clarity, and significance meet NIPS standards. While the omitted comparison to Shah et al. (2016) and lack of complexity analysis are minor drawbacks, they do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The experimental results robustly validate the theoretical claims.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary**  
*Pro*: Novel generalization of knowledge gradient to batch setting; rigorous theoretical foundation; efficient IPA-based optimization; strong empirical results across diverse benchmarks; clear writing.  
*Con*: Limited comparison to very recent batch BO methods; minimal computational complexity analysis; discretization strategy lacks thorough justification.  

This paper is technically sound, novel, and significant. It advances the field of Bayesian optimization with a method that is both theoretically grounded and practically effective. I recommend acceptance.
