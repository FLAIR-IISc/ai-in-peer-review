- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that generalizes Knowledge Gradient to jointly select q points via expected reduction in the GP posterior mean minimum.  
- q-KG computed by Monte Carlo; authors develop an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for gradient-based batch optimization.  
- For continuous domains, inner minimizations are discretized using samples from an approximate posterior over minimizers (similar to entropy search).  
- Experiments (d ≤ 6, q=4) on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning (MNIST, CIFAR-10) show q-KG outperforms batch EI and UCB variants, especially under noisy evaluations.  
- Code for the method is released.  
- Extends classical (discrete, sequential) KG to batch continuous settings via Monte Carlo and IPA.  
- Contrasts with batch EI, heuristic parallelization (constant liar), and batch UCB methods by optimizing batches jointly rather than greedily.  
- Related to PES/qPES in using posterior minimizer samples for discretization, but optimizes a different objective.  
- Clarifies that in noiseless settings with recommendations restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to q-EI.  
- Strength: principled Bayes-optimal one-step decision-theoretic foundation for the terminal loss.  
- Strength: methodological novelty in an IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient enabling practical joint batch optimization.  
- Strength: empirical gains are consistent and significant in noisy regimes; practical relevance demonstrated via hyperparameter tuning tasks.  
- Weakness: scalability and runtime not quantified; O(q^3) linear algebra per MC sample and optimization over R^{qd} with M=1000 discretization raises concerns beyond q=4 and low/moderate d.  
- Weakness: IPA gradient assumptions (differentiability, uniqueness of argmins) are subtle and under-explained; robustness deferred to supplement.  
- Weakness: discretization An depends on candidate batch z, making the objective piecewise and possibly non-differentiable; gradient treatment and bias from treating An as fixed are unclear.  
- Weakness: no theoretical guarantees (convergence or regret bounds) for batch policy in continuous/noisy settings.  
- Weakness: experimental ablations missing (effect of M, MC samples, gradient variance reduction, GP hyperparameter uncertainty); comparisons omit batch ES/qPES.  
- Quality: technically sound overall but key technical assumptions and discretization dependence are under-explained in the main text.  
- Clarity: generally well-organized and readable, but computational details (IPA conditions, minimizer sampling) need more exposition.  
- Originality: novel combination of KG extension to batches with IPA-based stochastic gradients; distinct from batch EI/UCB and PES methods.  
- Significance: valuable for batch BO under noise if method scales to larger q/d and remains runtime-competitive; released code increases impact.  
- Suggested improvements: report wall-clock/runtime and performance vs time, test larger q, clarify IPA assumptions and tie handling, detail An sampling and sensitivity to M, and compare to qPES experimentally.  
- Pros: principled objective, strong empirical gains under noise, practical stochastic gradient method, and available code.  
- Cons: missing runtime/scalability evidence, insufficient transparency on theoretical conditions and discretization dependence, limited benchmarks (no qPES, few high-d/large-q tests).  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — principled and useful contribution with promising results but needs stronger computational analysis and experimental characterization.
