### **Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper introduces a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea is to generalize the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy, which is known to be one-step Bayes-optimal, to the parallel setting. The q-KG acquisition function quantifies the expected improvement in the value of the final recommended solution (the minimum of the posterior mean) that would be gained by evaluating a batch of `q` points.

This work builds upon a rich literature in BO. It extends the sequential KG framework [7, 17] to the more practical batch setting. Critically, the authors recognize that a naive optimization of the q-KG function, which involves an expectation over an optimization problem, is computationally intractable. The main technical contribution is the development of an efficient, gradient-based optimization scheme. By expressing the posterior update in a form amenable to Monte Carlo sampling and then applying Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), they derive an unbiased stochastic estimator for the gradient of the q-KG factor. This allows for the efficient optimization of the batch selection.

The proposed method distinguishes itself from prior work in parallel BO in several key ways. Unlike greedy or heuristic batch construction methods (e.g., "constant liar" [10], GP-BUCB [5]), q-KG jointly optimizes the entire batch of `q` points. While other joint-optimization methods exist for parallel Expected Improvement (EI) [2, 15, 25] and Entropy Search (ES) [18], this paper provides the first practical application of the KG principle in this context. The decision-theoretic motivation of KG, which focuses on the final recommendation quality rather than the value at sampled points, is particularly well-suited for noisy optimization, a claim well-supported by the experiments.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Well-Motivated Approach:** The q-KG acquisition function is a natural and principled generalization of the sequential KG. The one-step Bayes-optimality framework provides a strong theoretical motivation that is lacking in more heuristic parallelization schemes. The distinction from EI (which is optimal if the solution must be a sampled point) is important and highlights the generality of the KG approach.

2.  **Significant Technical Contribution:** The primary strength of the paper lies not just in defining q-KG, but in making its optimization computationally feasible. The application of IPA to derive an unbiased gradient estimator for the Monte Carlo objective is a sophisticated and crucial contribution. This moves the method from a theoretical curiosity to a practical algorithm.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough, comparing q-KG against a strong suite of modern parallel BO algorithms across both synthetic benchmarks and realistic machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks. The results compellingly demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods.

4.  **Demonstrated Advantage in Noisy Settings:** The paper's most significant empirical finding is the pronounced advantage of q-KG in noisy environments. This is a key result, as many real-world expensive-to-evaluate functions (e.g., validation error on a data subsample) are inherently noisy. This aligns with the theoretical motivation of KG, which directly models the impact of noisy observations on the posterior mean landscape.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The paper rightly claims its optimization method is efficient compared to a naive approach, but a discussion of its absolute computational cost is missing. The method requires Monte Carlo sampling to estimate both the function and its gradient, followed by a gradient-based optimization at each BO iteration. This seems substantially more expensive than, for example, GP-BUCB or parallel EI implementations that rely on closed-form (or simpler) acquisitions.
    *   What is the typical wall-clock time per iteration compared to the baselines?
    *   How many Monte Carlo samples are required for a stable gradient estimate?
    *   How does the computational cost scale with the batch size `q`? The experiments are limited to `q=4`, but the Cholesky decomposition of the `q x q` covariance matrix inside the MC loop suggests the cost might grow non-trivially. A discussion on the practical limits of `q` would be valuable.

2.  **Reliance on Discretization:** The computation of the `min` operator inside the q-KG expectation requires discretizing the continuous domain `A`. The paper employs a reasonable strategy of sampling from the GP posterior optima, similar to [11, 18]. However, the quality and performance of the overall algorithm are now tied to the quality of this discretization.
    *   How sensitive are the results to the number of discretization points `M`? The choice `M=1000` seems reasonable but is not justified.
    *   Could a poor discretization (e.g., if the posterior optima are not representative of the true function's behavior) lead to a poor selection of the batch `z(1:q)`? This coupling could be a potential failure mode and warrants discussion.

3.  **Clarity on Hyperparameter Learning:** The paper states that GP hyperparameters are re-trained via MLE in each iteration (line 5 of Algorithm 1). This is standard practice, but it interacts with the gradient calculation. The derivation of the gradient in Section 5.2 assumes fixed GP hyperparameters. Does this mismatch (optimizing q-KG with fixed hyperparameters, then re-fitting them) affect the performance or theoretical grounding of the method? A brief comment on this would be helpful.

### **Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It presents a novel, theoretically-grounded acquisition function for the practical batch setting and, more importantly, provides the necessary computational machinery to make it usable. The technical solution using IPA for gradient estimation is elegant and effective. The empirical results are strong and highlight a particularly important niche where the method excels: noisy optimization.

While there are minor concerns about computational cost and the sensitivity to the discretization scheme, these do not detract from the core contribution. The work clearly advances the state of the art in parallel BO and is likely to be influential and widely used by both researchers and practitioners. The paper is well-written, methodologically sound, and addresses a problem of high interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
