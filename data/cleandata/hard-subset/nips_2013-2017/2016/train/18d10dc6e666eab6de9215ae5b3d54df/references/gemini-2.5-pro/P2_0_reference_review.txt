### **Summary**

This paper introduces a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). Derived from a decision-theoretic framework, q-KG selects a batch of `q` points by maximizing the expected improvement in the final optimal value after observing the outcomes at those points. This provides a principled one-step lookahead policy for batch selection, moving beyond the greedy or heuristic approaches common in prior work. The main technical contribution is a computationally efficient method for optimizing this otherwise intractable acquisition function. The authors leverage infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic estimator of the q-KG gradient, enabling the use of standard gradient-based optimization methods. The proposed method is evaluated on a suite of synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, demonstrating competitive or superior performance against state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, with particularly strong results in noisy environments.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled Formulation:** The q-KG acquisition function is grounded in a strong decision-theoretic principle. Unlike methods that greedily build a batch point-by-point (e.g., GP-BUCB, constant liar EI), q-KG values the batch of `q` points collectively, directly modeling their joint information gain with respect to the final recommendation. This is a more direct and less myopic approach to the batch selection problem.
2.  **Novel Computational Method:** The core contribution of making q-KG optimization tractable is significant. The application of IPA to compute an unbiased stochastic gradient of the q-KG factor is clever and effective. This moves the method from a conceptual idea to a practical algorithm and is a valuable contribution to the BO literature.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough, covering four synthetic functions and two challenging real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10). The comparison against multiple strong baselines (two parallel EI variants, two parallel UCB variants) is comprehensive. The results consistently show that q-KG is a top performer, especially in the noisy setting, which is a very common scenario in practice (e.g., when evaluating models on a subset of data).
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written, and the authors make their code publicly available, which is a significant plus for reproducibility and community adoption.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Complexity:** While the IPA-based optimization is far more efficient than naive methods, it is still likely more computationally expensive than the baselines. The optimization of the acquisition function involves Monte Carlo sampling for the gradient estimation and a gradient-based ascent procedure. The paper does not provide a comparison of the wall-clock time required to select a batch, which is a critical practical consideration. An analysis of how the computational cost scales with batch size `q` and the discretization size `M` would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Reliance on Discretization:** The computation of the q-KG factor relies on discretizing the continuous domain `A` into a finite set `An` to find the minimum of the posterior mean. While the proposed adaptive discretization scheme (sampling from posterior optima) is a sensible heuristic used in prior work (e.g., Entropy Search), it is still an approximation. The paper does not analyze the quality of this approximation or its potential impact on performance. For instance, if the posterior mean is very sharp, a fixed number of samples `M` might fail to capture the true minimum.
3.  **Limited Scalability in Batch Size `q`:** The experiments are conducted with a batch size of `q=4`. The method involves inverting a `q x q` matrix (via Cholesky decomposition) both in the GP update and in the gradient calculation. While feasible for small `q`, the `O(q^3)` scaling could become a bottleneck for larger batch sizes (e.g., `q` > 16), which are increasingly relevant in modern distributed computing environments. A discussion of this limitation would be appropriate.

### **Detailed Review**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the q-KG factor is a natural and correct extension of the sequential KG concept. The use of IPA to differentiate through the expectation and the `min` operator is a solid and well-established technique in stochastic optimization, and its application here is novel and well-executed. The experimental methodology is rigorous, using multiple random initializations and reporting both mean and standard deviation, which provides confidence in the results. The claims of superior performance, especially in noisy settings, are well-supported by the empirical evidence provided.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation and review of related work, introduces the necessary background on Gaussian Processes, and then logically develops the q-KG acquisition function and the computational machinery to optimize it. The description of the algorithm and the experimental setup is sufficient for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work. The inclusion of a link to the code further enhances clarity and reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the knowledge gradient concept existed for the sequential setting, its generalization to the batch setting and, crucially, the development of a tractable gradient-based optimization scheme is a novel and significant contribution. This work clearly distinguishes itself from previous parallel BO methods. Unlike heuristic extensions of EI or UCB, q-KG is derived from first principles to value the batch as a whole. It also advances upon prior work on parallel KG [26] by handling continuous domains and providing a much more efficient gradient-based optimization procedure.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of high practical and theoretical importance: efficient parallel black-box optimization. As parallel computing resources become ubiquitous, methods that can effectively leverage them for expensive optimization tasks like hyperparameter tuning are increasingly critical. q-KG provides a new state-of-the-art method that is both principled and practical. Its demonstrated strength in noisy settings is particularly valuable, as many real-world evaluations are inherently stochastic. Researchers and practitioners working on hyperparameter tuning, automated machine learning, and experimental design are likely to use these ideas and build upon them. This work represents a demonstrable advance in the state of the art for batch Bayesian optimization.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This is a strong paper with a novel, well-motivated method, a significant technical contribution to make it practical, and compelling experimental results.
