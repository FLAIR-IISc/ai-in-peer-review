

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a new batch Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG), which extends the sequential knowledge gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors develop a computationally efficient method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize the q-KG acquisition function and claim superior performance over existing batch BO methods like parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration.

## Strengths
The paper provides a clear and well-organized presentation of the theoretical foundations of Bayesian optimization and the knowledge gradient method. The derivation of q-KG is logically sound and builds appropriately on prior work. The experimental evaluation includes both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, which is relevant to the NIPS community. The authors provide a reasonable implementation of their method and compare against several established baselines.

## Weaknesses
The main weakness of this paper is insufficient evidence to support its core claim of consistent superiority over existing methods. While the results show that q-KG performs well on the tested problems, the paper lacks rigorous statistical validation of these results. The paper reports "mean and standard deviation" of results across multiple runs but does not provide statistical significance testing to confirm that the observed improvements are meaningful.

The experimental section is particularly underwhelming. The authors test on only four synthetic functions (with varying dimensions) and two practical ML tuning problems (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR10). This is insufficient to establish "consistent" superiority as claimed in the abstract and introduction. The paper also fails to compare against the most recent state-of-the-art batch BO methods, such as Parallel Predictive Entropy Search [18], which was presented at NIPS 2016 and would be highly relevant to this work.

The paper makes a strong claim that "q-KG provides especially large value when function evaluations are noisy," but the evidence for this is limited to one noisy synthetic function example and one noisy logistic regression case. This is insufficient to support such a specific claim about noise robustness.

The computational complexity analysis is lacking. While the authors claim efficiency through IPA, they provide no detailed comparison of computational costs with existing methods, nor do they demonstrate how the method scales with batch size q. This is a critical omission for a batch optimization method.

The paper also fails to thoroughly discuss the limitations of the proposed method. For instance, it does not address how the discretization approach (Section 5.3) might affect performance or whether there are specific problem types where q-KG would perform poorly.

## Originality and Significance
While extending the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting is a logical progression, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from previous work. The related work section adequately covers prior batch BO methods but doesn't clearly articulate the specific novelty of q-KG beyond "considering the batch collectively." The paper cites relevant work but doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this approach represents a meaningful advance over existing methods.

The significance of this work is limited by the lack of comprehensive validation and the relatively modest improvements claimed over existing methods. For a field like Bayesian optimization where small improvements can be meaningful, the paper doesn't provide enough evidence to justify the novelty of the approach.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound extension of knowledge gradient to batch settings, the evidence supporting its claimed advantages is insufficient. The experimental validation is limited, lacks statistical rigor, and doesn't compare against the most relevant recent work. The paper makes strong claims without sufficient evidence to back them up, which is particularly concerning for a skeptical reviewer who values thorough validation.

The paper would require substantially more experimental evidence, including statistical significance testing, comparison against the most recent state-of-the-art methods, and a more thorough analysis of computational complexity and limitations, to warrant acceptance at NIPS. As it stands, the paper does not provide enough evidence to support its claims of consistent superiority over existing batch BO methods.
