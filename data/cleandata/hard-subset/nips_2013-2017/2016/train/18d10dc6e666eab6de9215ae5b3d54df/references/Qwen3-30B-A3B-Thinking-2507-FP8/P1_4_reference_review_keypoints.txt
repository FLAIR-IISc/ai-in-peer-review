- Introduces q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition function that extends sequential KG to jointly optimize batches of q points.  
- Uses infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate gradients of q-KG, making batch optimization computationally feasible.  
- Empirical results on synthetic benchmarks and ML hyperparameter tuning show q-KG outperforms or matches state-of-the-art, especially in noisy settings.  

- Theoretical derivation is sound and novel, framing q-KG as a decision-theoretic extension of sequential KG.  
- IPA-based gradient computation is a clever, practical computational innovation that addresses evaluation cost.  
- Experiments are comprehensive, covering multiple synthetic functions and real ML tuning tasks with consistent improvements.  
- Demonstrates clear practical relevance by improving real-world hyperparameter tuning results (e.g., CIFAR10 CNN).  
- Paper is well-written, with clear notation, reproducible algorithm description, and effective figures.  

- Lacks a detailed analysis of how computational complexity scales with batch size q.  
- Experiments are limited to low-dimensional problems (d ≤ 6), leaving high-dimensional scalability unaddressed.  
- Does not sufficiently compare to some recent parallel BO methods (e.g., parallel ES) in experiments.  
- No theoretical convergence guarantees provided for the batch q-KG method.  

- Overall assessment: technically strong, practically relevant, and empirically validated contribution to batch BO.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  

- Pros for acceptance: novel theoretical contribution, efficient IPA-based computation, comprehensive experiments, practical ML relevance, clear presentation.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited complexity analysis w.r.t. q, low-dimensional experiments, incomplete comparisons with recent methods, missing theoretical guarantees.  

- Suggest adding complexity scaling analysis or empirical runtime measurements with q.  
- Suggest extending experiments to higher-dimensional problems (d > 10) to demonstrate scalability.  
- Recommend a more detailed empirical/theoretical comparison with recent parallel BO methods like parallel ES.  
- Suggest discussing theoretical properties of q-KG and how sequential KG guarantees might extend.  
- Recommend clarifying practical limitations and computational resource requirements for large batch sizes.
