This paper proposes a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) policy, which is a decision-theoretic approach that seeks to maximize the expected value of information gained from an additional batch of function evaluations. As the direct optimization of the q-KG acquisition function is intractable, the authors derive an unbiased gradient estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), enabling the use of gradient-based optimizers. The proposed method is evaluated on a set of synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, in both noise-free and noisy settings, and compared against several established parallel BO baselines, including parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) variants.

The paper's primary strengths lie in its principled, decision-theoretic derivation and its promising empirical results in noisy environments. The generalization of the well-established KG framework to the batch setting is a logical and valuable research direction. Unlike many heuristic approaches to batch construction, q-KG is derived from a clear objective: maximizing the expected improvement in the final recommended solution after the next batch of evaluations. The technical contribution of applying IPA to derive a stochastic gradient is non-trivial and provides a viable, if complex, path to implementation. Furthermore, the experimental results, particularly on the noisy synthetic functions and the logistic regression tuning task, suggest that q-KG can outperform standard methods. The ability to handle noise effectively is a significant advantage, as KG's formulation does not restrict the final solution to be one of the evaluated points, making it naturally robust to observation noise.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several significant weaknesses that temper my enthusiasm for the proposed method. My primary concerns are related to methodological soundness, computational complexity, and the thoroughness of the empirical validation.

First, the method's practicality is critically dependent on a heuristic for discretizing the continuous domain `A` when computing the q-KG factor. The authors propose using a set of `M` samples drawn from the optima of the GP posterior, a technique borrowed from prior work. This introduces a crucial approximation step whose impact is not analyzed. The performance of the entire q-KG algorithm may be highly sensitive to the size `M` and the quality of this discrete set `An`. A rigorous evaluation would require a sensitivity analysis of `M` and a discussion of the potential failure modes of this heuristic. Without this, it is difficult to assess whether the reported performance gains are due to the principled nature of q-KG or the specific behavior of this discretization scheme. This reliance on an unevaluated approximation undermines the method's claims of methodological rigor.

Second, the paper fails to adequately address the computational cost of the proposed method. Optimizing the q-KG acquisition function requires Monte Carlo sampling to estimate both the function value and its gradient at every step of the gradient ascent procedure. This is almost certainly far more computationally expensive than the baseline methods, particularly the UCB variants which do not require Monte Carlo sampling. The authors acknowledge that naive maximization would be "extremely computationally intensive" but provide no analysis or empirical comparison of the wall-clock time required to select a batch compared to the baselines. For a method to be considered a practical advancement, its performance benefits must be weighed against its computational overhead. This omission is a serious flaw, making it impossible to judge the method's utility in real-world scenarios where the time to select the next batch is a relevant factor.

Third, the empirical evaluation, while demonstrating some positive results, is not extensive enough to convincingly establish the superiority of this new, complex method.
*   The comparison set is incomplete. While parallel EI and UCB variants are included, a comparison to other collective batch methods like parallel Entropy Search [18], which is cited in the related work, is notably absent.
*   The number of independent runs for the most complex experiment (CNN tuning) is only five. Given the high variance inherent in such experiments, this is insufficient to draw strong conclusions.
*   The performance gains in the noise-free setting are modest. On the Branin2 function, q-KG is slightly outperformed by baselines, and on other functions, the improvement is not always dramatic. A new, computationally demanding method should ideally demonstrate a clear and consistent advantage over simpler, established techniques. The current results do not fully meet this standard.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically appealing extension of the knowledge gradient method to batch Bayesian optimization. However, its practical implementation relies on a critical but unanalyzed discretization heuristic, and its computational cost is likely prohibitive but is not discussed or measured. The empirical evidence, while showing promise in noisy settings, is not comprehensive enough to justify the adoption of such a complex method over existing, well-understood baselines. The work represents a potentially interesting research direction, but in its current form, it lacks the methodological rigor and extensive validation required to be considered a significant and reliable contribution to the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The method is derived from sound decision-theoretic principles, generalizing the established KG method.
2.  The derivation of the gradient estimator via IPA is a solid technical contribution.
3.  The method shows strong empirical performance in noisy optimization settings, which are common in practice.

**Arguments for rejection:**
1.  The method's performance depends on a heuristic discretization of the domain, and the impact of this approximation is not analyzed.
2.  The computational cost is likely very high, yet it is not quantified or compared to baselines, limiting the assessment of the method's practical utility.
3.  The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently thorough; it lacks comparisons to some key baselines (e.g., parallel ES) and has too few runs on the most complex problem to be conclusive. The performance advantage over established methods is not consistently demonstrated across all experiments.
