## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) method, the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), aiming to improve upon existing parallel BO techniques. The core idea is to derive an acquisition function based on a decision-theoretic analysis that maximizes the expected incremental value of a batch of evaluations, considering the entire batch collectively rather than greedily adding points. To address the computational complexity of maximizing this acquisition function, the authors develop an efficient gradient estimation technique using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA).  The paper demonstrates empirically that q-KG outperforms other parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings, on both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The q-KG acquisition function represents a genuine contribution to the field of parallel BO.  Unlike methods that iteratively build batches with single-point evaluations, it directly optimizes for the batch as a whole, aligning with a more theoretically sound approach.  The connection to the knowledge gradient in the sequential case is clearly articulated.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of q-KG based on decision theory provides a strong theoretical justification for the method. This contrasts with many heuristic approaches in parallel BO.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The use of IPA for gradient estimation is crucial for making q-KG practical.  The authors acknowledge and successfully address the significant computational challenges involved in optimizing the acquisition function.
* **Empirical Performance:** The extensive experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of q-KG.  It consistently outperforms benchmark algorithms on a variety of tasks, including those with noisy evaluations. The results on practical machine learning tasks (logistic regression and CNN tuning) are particularly compelling.
* **Code Availability:** Providing publicly available code (https://github.com/wujian16/qKG) is a significant strength, facilitating reproducibility and further research.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are reasonably accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization of A:** While the authors address the challenge of maximizing q-KG over a continuous domain *A* through discretization, the method used (based on the global optima of the posterior distribution) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more thorough discussion of the impact of the number of samples *M* on performance, and perhaps an exploration of alternative discretization strategies, would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t extensively discuss the sensitivity of q-KG’s performance to its own hyperparameters.  While they state *M* = 1000, a sensitivity analysis with different values of *M* or the batch size *q* would be informative.
* **Limited Comparison:**  The comparison to other methods, while thorough, is largely focused on a specific set of algorithms (parallel EI, batch UCB, GP-UCB-PE). Including more recent or specialized parallel BO algorithms (e.g., those based on diversity promotion or active learning) could strengthen the empirical analysis.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the paper acknowledges the complexity of the approach, a more detailed discussion of its limitations and potential areas for future work would be beneficial. For instance, the scalability of the IPA-based gradient estimation to very high-dimensional problems is not fully explored.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are well-supported.  The method appears complete and the authors are careful in their evaluation, acknowledging the benefits and limitations. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized. However, some sections (e.g., the detailed derivation of the IPA-based gradient) could benefit from further simplification or more illustrative examples. **(Good)**
* **Originality:** The q-KG method is original and represents a significant advance in parallel BO.  The combination of decision-theoretic principles and efficient gradient estimation is novel. **(Strong)**
* **Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact the field of Bayesian optimization, particularly in scenarios where parallel function evaluations are available. The demonstrated improvements in noisy environments are particularly valuable. **(Strong)**




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian optimization.
* Strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world problems.
* Efficient gradient estimation technique makes the method practical.
* Publicly available code promotes reproducibility and further research.
* Addresses a challenging and important problem in optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The discretization strategy for continuous domains could be more robust.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Could benefit from a broader comparison to more recent parallel BO methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The q-KG method is innovative, well-motivated, and empirically validated. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:** **Strong Accept.**  I believe this paper is suitable for presentation at NIPS 2016.
