

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a new batch Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that extends the knowledge gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors claim that their method provides a decision-theoretically optimal acquisition function for batch evaluation in Bayesian optimization, and that it outperforms existing parallel BO methods in both synthetic and practical machine learning applications. The paper makes a clear connection to previous work on sequential knowledge gradient methods and provides a theoretical derivation of the q-KG acquisition function.

## Strengths

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, providing a thorough background on Bayesian optimization and Gaussian processes. The authors make a strong case for the decision-theoretic justification of their approach, which is a significant improvement over heuristic batch methods. The experimental section is comprehensive, testing the method on both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks. The implementation details are well-described, and the code availability is a plus. The paper does a good job of comparing to relevant baselines including parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration.

## Weaknesses

Despite the paper's strengths, I have significant concerns about the evidence supporting the claims of superiority. The paper states that "q-KG consistently finds better function values than other parallel BO algorithms," but fails to provide statistical significance tests for the results. The paper shows mean and standard deviation across 100 random initializations for synthetic functions, but without statistical testing, it's impossible to determine if the observed differences are meaningful. For the practical machine learning examples, the paper reports results for only 5-20 independent runs, which is insufficient to make strong statistical claims about performance differences.

The paper's claim of novelty is questionable. The authors cite [26] as studying a "nested batch knowledge gradient policy" but do not adequately distinguish their approach. [26] appears to address a similar problem with a different constraint structure, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why their method is fundamentally different or superior to this prior work. Additionally, the paper fails to compare with more recent batch BO methods like Parallel Predictive Entropy Search (PES) [15, 18], which has become a standard for batch Bayesian optimization.

The computational claims are problematic. The paper claims that their IPA-based approach "allows efficient optimization" of q-KG, but provides no runtime comparisons with other methods. For a method that claims to be "computationally efficient," the paper lacks any analysis of computational complexity or wall-clock time comparisons against baselines. Without this, it's impossible to assess whether the claimed performance benefits come with an unacceptable computational cost.

The paper's claim that "q-KG provides especially large value when function evaluations are noisy" is particularly concerning. The noisy experiments (Section 6.2) show that q-KG outperforms baselines, but the paper provides no theoretical justification for why the knowledge gradient approach would be better in noisy settings than other acquisition functions. The paper also doesn't explain why the knowledge gradient would be more robust to noise than, say, expected improvement.

The paper's practical contribution is limited. The CNN tuning example shows a modest improvement from 14% to 11% test error, but this small absolute improvement may not be practically significant, especially given the computational cost of the method. The paper doesn't discuss how this improvement would translate to real-world deployment scenarios where computational resources are constrained.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically motivated extension of the knowledge gradient to the batch setting, which is a legitimate contribution to Bayesian optimization. However, the empirical evidence provided is insufficient to support the strong claims of superiority over existing methods. The lack of statistical significance testing, limited comparison to relevant baselines, and absence of computational complexity analysis are significant shortcomings. While the method may have value, the paper fails to provide rigorous evidence that it represents a meaningful advance over the state-of-the-art.

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's claims to be overreaching without sufficient evidence. The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding statistical significance tests, comparing to more recent batch BO methods, providing computational complexity analysis, and offering a clearer theoretical explanation for the observed performance in noisy settings.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper makes a promising theoretical contribution but fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to justify its claims of superiority over existing methods. The method is not shown to be substantially better than current approaches, and the paper does not provide the rigorous validation required for a NIPS publication. The authors would need to substantially expand their experimental section with statistical significance testing, broader baseline comparisons, and computational complexity analysis to address these concerns.
