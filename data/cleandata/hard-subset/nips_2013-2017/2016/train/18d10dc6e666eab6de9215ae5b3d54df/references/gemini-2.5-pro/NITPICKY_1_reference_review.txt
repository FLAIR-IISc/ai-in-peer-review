This paper proposes a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, aiming to select a batch of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final objective value after one more parallel evaluation step. To optimize this new acquisition function, which lacks a closed-form expression, the authors propose an efficient gradient estimation technique based on Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). This allows the use of gradient-based optimizers to find the optimal batch of points to evaluate. The method is evaluated on a suite of synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, demonstrating competitive or superior performance against several other parallel BO algorithms, with particularly strong results in noisy settings. This work extends the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework to the parallel setting and situates itself among other batch BO methods that jointly optimize the entire batch, such as parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and parallel Entropy Search (ES).

### Strengths

1.  **Principled Derivation:** The acquisition function is derived from a clear and well-founded decision-theoretic principle: maximizing the one-step-ahead expected value of information. This provides a solid theoretical motivation for the method, extending the elegant logic of the sequential KG to the more complex batch setting.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling, particularly in the noisy evaluation setting. The proposed q-KG method consistently performs as well as or better than several state-of-the-art benchmarks on both synthetic functions and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks. The experiment on noisy logistic regression, which realistically simulates hyperparameter tuning with small validation sets, is a well-designed and convincing demonstration of the method's utility.

3.  **Novel Computational Approach:** The use of IPA to derive a stochastic gradient estimator for the q-KG acquisition function is a key technical contribution. This enables an efficient, gradient-based search for the optimal batch, which is a non-trivial optimization problem.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core idea and empirical results, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor and major flaws in its technical presentation, methodology, and experimental reporting.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript exhibits a lack of careful preparation.
    *   There is a formatting error in the introduction, where a conference submission footnote appears mid-paragraph ("th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...").
    *   Algorithm 1 contains a typo ("Main Stange" instead of "Main Stage").
    *   The reference list is poorly formatted and incomplete. For example, [16] (Rasmussen) and [1] are missing key bibliographic details, and formatting is inconsistent across entries. This carelessness undermines the reader's confidence in the work.
    *   The explanation in the preamble of Section 5 regarding the two different uses of the domain `A` (one for computing the KG factor, one for the optimization search space) is confusing and could be presented more clearly with distinct notation.

2.  **Methodological and Technical Gaps:** The paper glosses over several critical technical details, raising concerns about its soundness.
    *   **Gradient Estimator Validity:** The derivation of the gradient in Section 5.2 is the paper's core technical method, yet its validity is not rigorously established. The interchange of the gradient and expectation operators in (5.3) relies on the differentiability of the inner function `g`. However, `g` contains a `min` operator over `A`, which depends on `x*(after) = argmin(...)`. The `argmin` function is not generally differentiable. The authors seem to implicitly use an envelope theorem-style argument, but the conditions for its application (e.g., uniqueness of the minimizer, behavior at the boundary) are not discussed. Stating that details are in the supplementary material is insufficient for such a critical component of the methodology.
    *   **Discretization Strategy:** The proposed discretization scheme in Section 5.3 is problematic. The discrete set `An` is defined as `AMn ∪ x(1:n) ∪ z(1:q)`. Since the optimization variables `z(1:q)` are part of the set over which the `min` in the acquisition function is taken, the domain of the `min` operation changes as the optimizer explores different `z`. This can introduce discontinuities in the objective function landscape, which would invalidate the use of a gradient-based optimizer. This significant issue is not acknowledged or addressed.
    *   **Comparison to EI:** The argument in Section 4 that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under specific conditions is presented in a hand-wavy manner. The steps in the derivation, such as equating `min µ(n)(x)` with `min y(1:n)`, lack formal justification and seem to rely on unstated assumptions.

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Analysis:**
    *   **Computational Cost:** A major omission is the lack of any discussion or measurement of the computational time of the q-KG method. The proposed approach involves Monte Carlo estimation for both the function and its gradient at each step of a gradient-ascent optimization, which is likely significantly more expensive than the benchmark methods. Without this analysis, the practical utility of the method is unclear, as the improved sample efficiency might come at an unacceptable computational cost.
    *   **Limited Runs:** For the CNN tuning experiment, results are averaged over only 5 independent runs. This number is too small to provide statistically robust conclusions, especially for a high-variance problem like neural network training.
    *   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The batch size is fixed at `q=4` for all experiments. An analysis of how the algorithm's performance and computational cost scale with `q` would have significantly strengthened the paper.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, theoretically motivated acquisition function for an important problem (batch BO).
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance, especially in noisy environments, which are common in practice.
*   Introduces a sophisticated gradient-based optimization technique for the acquisition function.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Significant methodological concerns regarding the validity of the gradient estimator and the discretization strategy are left unaddressed.
*   The paper shows signs of carelessness, including typos, major formatting errors, and an incomplete/inconsistent reference list.
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete, critically omitting any analysis of computational cost and providing insufficient runs for the main deep learning experiment.

### Recommendation

While the core contribution of this paper is interesting and the empirical results appear promising, the significant number of technical and methodological gaps, combined with a general lack of polish, prevent a recommendation for acceptance. The issues with the gradient estimator's validity and the problematic discretization scheme are particularly concerning and strike at the heart of the paper's proposed methodology. Furthermore, the absence of any computational cost analysis makes it impossible to assess the practical trade-offs of the proposed method. I therefore recommend **rejection**. The authors have a strong starting point, but the work requires substantial revision to address the technical flaws and to provide a more complete and carefully presented analysis before it meets the standards for publication at NIPS.
