

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "Parallel Knowledge Gradient" (q-KG), extending the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting where q evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor when only one batch remains. They address the computational challenges of maximizing q-KG by developing an efficient gradient estimation technique using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The paper compares q-KG against several parallel BO methods including parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration, demonstrating consistent improvements, especially in noisy settings.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for q-KG, clearly deriving the acquisition function from a decision-theoretic perspective. The extension from sequential to parallel KG is well-motivated and technically sound.

2. **Computational Innovation**: The authors' use of IPA for efficient gradient estimation of q-KG is a significant contribution that addresses a major computational bottleneck in parallel BO. This technique enables practical implementation of the theoretically appealing q-KG acquisition function.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The empirical evaluation is thorough, testing on both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR10). The comparison against multiple relevant baselines provides strong evidence of q-KG's superiority, particularly in noisy settings.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The paper demonstrates clear practical value, showing that q-KG can improve machine learning model performance (e.g., reducing CNN test error from 14% to 11% on CIFAR10).

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper presents an efficient optimization method for q-KG, it lacks a detailed computational complexity analysis. The authors should provide a theoretical analysis of the time complexity of their IPA-based optimization approach compared to existing methods, especially as q increases.

2. **Discretization Sensitivity**: The paper uses M=1000 for discretization (Section 5.3), but doesn't analyze how q-KG performance varies with M. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the methodological contribution.

3. **Comparison Against Recent Work**: The paper cites [18] and [25] as related work, but doesn't compare against their methods in the experiments. Given that these papers also address parallel BO with gradient-based optimization, including them would provide a more complete evaluation.

4. **Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper lacks theoretical analysis of regret bounds for q-KG. While the sequential KG has known regret guarantees, the paper doesn't establish similar guarantees for the parallel extension, which would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution.

5. **Limitations Discussion**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss limitations of the proposed method, particularly regarding high-dimensional domains where discretization becomes computationally prohibitive.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a detailed computational complexity analysis of the IPA-based optimization method, comparing its time complexity to existing parallel BO approaches as q and dimension d increase.

2. Conduct a sensitivity analysis of q-KG performance with respect to M (the discretization parameter) and include this in the experiments.

3. Extend the experimental comparison to include the most recent parallel BO methods such as those in [18] and [25] to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

4. Add theoretical analysis of regret bounds for the q-KG method to establish stronger theoretical guarantees.

5. Discuss the limitations of the discretization approach in high-dimensional settings and suggest potential extensions to handle such cases.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, with a clear theoretical extension of knowledge gradient to parallel settings, a novel computational technique for efficient optimization, and compelling empirical results. The paper makes a significant practical contribution to hyperparameter tuning in machine learning.

The main weaknesses relate to the lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis and a more thorough evaluation against the most recent methods. Addressing these would substantially strengthen the paper.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to batch Bayesian optimization with a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and practically valuable method. The empirical results are convincing, and the method has clear applications in machine learning hyperparameter tuning. While the paper could be strengthened with additional theoretical analysis and more comprehensive comparisons, it represents a solid contribution to the field that meets NeurIPS standards. The reviewers should consider that the paper provides both theoretical insights and practical implementation details that are valuable for the community.
