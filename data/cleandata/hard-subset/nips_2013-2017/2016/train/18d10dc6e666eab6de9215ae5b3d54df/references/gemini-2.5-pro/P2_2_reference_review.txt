### **Summary**

This paper introduces a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea is to extend the decision-theoretic framework of the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) to the parallel setting. The q-KG acquisition function measures the expected improvement in the final recommended solution (the minimum of the posterior mean) after observing the results from a batch of `q` points. This contrasts with methods like parallel Expected Improvement (EI), which is optimal only under the constraint that the final solution must be one of the evaluated points.

Recognizing that the direct optimization of q-KG is computationally intractable, the authors' main technical contribution is a method to efficiently maximize it. They propose a gradient-based approach, deriving an unbiased stochastic estimator for the gradient of q-KG using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). To handle continuous optimization domains, they employ a dynamic discretization strategy that focuses on promising regions of the space. The paper presents a strong set of empirical results on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression and CNNs), demonstrating that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms several state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, with a particularly notable advantage in noisy settings.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function from a one-step Bayes-optimal decision-theoretic principle is sound and provides a strong theoretical motivation for the method.
*   The use of Infinitesesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased gradient estimator for a complex, nested acquisition function is a sophisticated and powerful technique. This is the core technical innovation that makes the maximization of q-KG feasible and is a significant contribution.
*   The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare against a strong and relevant set of baselines, including different implementations of parallel EI and parallel UCB variants. The experiments cover both synthetic and real-world problems, and crucially, explore both noise-free and noisy settings. The results strongly support the central claim that q-KG is particularly effective for noisy functions, which is a common and important scenario in practice (e.g., when evaluating models on a subset of data).

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized.

*   The introduction clearly situates the work within the context of Bayesian Optimization and motivates the need for principled parallel methods.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of existing parallel BO techniques, clearly differentiating the proposed method from greedy/sequential batch construction (like GP-BUCB) and other joint-optimization approaches (like parallel EI/ES).
*   The description of the q-KG factor and the computational strategy for maximizing it is presented logically. The separation of the finite-domain case (for which the gradient is derived) and the discretization strategy for the infinite-domain case makes the approach easy to follow.
*   The inclusion of a link to the open-source code is a major plus, enhancing reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   While sequential KG is an established concept, its generalization to the parallel setting for continuous domains, coupled with a practical optimization algorithm, is a novel contribution. Previous work on batch KG focused on discrete domains where gradient-based methods are not applicable.
*   The application of IPA for gradient estimation of a BO acquisition function is novel and represents a significant methodological advance over finite-difference or other less efficient gradient estimation techniques. This approach allows for joint optimization over the entire batch of `q` points in a continuous space, which is a key advantage over many prior methods.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is significant and likely to have a lasting impact.

*   Parallelism is essential for making BO practical for expensive, real-world problems like large-scale hyperparameter tuning. This paper provides a new, principled, and high-performing algorithm for this crucial setting.
*   The demonstrated strength in noisy environments is particularly important. Many real-world optimization problems involve stochastic evaluations (e.g., ML model performance on a data fold, physical experiments), and q-KG appears to be a state-of-the-art method for this domain.
*   The technical approach of using IPA for acquisition function optimization may inspire similar techniques for other complex, expectation-based acquisition functions in BO and beyond.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The primary weakness is the computational cost. The method requires Monte Carlo sampling to estimate both the q-KG value and its gradient at each step of the optimization. Furthermore, this is nested within a discretization scheme that itself involves sampling from the GP posterior. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of how the computational cost scales with the batch size `q`, the dimension `d`, or the number of discretization points `M`. This makes it difficult to assess the practical limits of the method. For instance, is it feasible for `q > 10` or high-dimensional `d`?

2.  **Sensitivity to Discretization:** The performance relies on the quality of the discretization `An`. While the proposed strategy of sampling from the posterior optima is clever and well-motivated, it is still an approximation. It would be beneficial to include an analysis or discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm to the number of discretization points `M`. Is `M=1000` sufficient across different problems, or does it need to be tuned?

3.  **One-Step Horizon:** The method is, by construction, one-step Bayes-optimal. While this is standard in most of the BO literature, it is a known limitation. The policy does not account for the information that will be gained for *future* batches of evaluations beyond the immediate next one. This is a minor point, as multi-step lookahead is generally intractable, but it is a theoretical limitation worth acknowledging.

### **Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance**

**Pros:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled acquisition function (q-KG) for the important problem of batch Bayesian Optimization.
*   Introduces a sophisticated and novel technical contribution by using IPA to enable efficient gradient-based optimization of the acquisition function.
*   Optimizes the batch of points jointly, which is theoretically superior to greedy approaches.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art empirical performance across a range of benchmarks, with a particularly strong and valuable advantage in noisy settings.
*   The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and provides open-source code.

**Cons:**
*   The computational cost appears high, and the paper lacks a clear analysis of its scaling properties with respect to key parameters like batch size and dimensionality.
*   The method's performance depends on a discretization heuristic, and its sensitivity to the parameters of this heuristic (e.g., `M`) is not explored.

**Recommendation:**
Despite the concerns about computational cost, the strengths of this paper are overwhelming. It addresses a critical problem with a novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated approach. The technical contribution of the IPA-based gradient estimator is significant and could influence future work. This is a high-quality paper that represents a clear advance in the state of the art for Bayesian Optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
