- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that generalizes the knowledge gradient to select q points by maximizing expected reduction in the GP posterior minimum.  
- q-KG evaluation requires Monte Carlo; authors derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based batch optimization.  
- For continuous domains, the method approximates inner minimizations by discretizing the domain using a dynamically updated candidate set.  
- Experiments on four synthetic test functions and two real hyperparameter tuning tasks show q-KG is competitive or superior to parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with strong gains under observation noise.  
- Code for the method is released, improving reproducibility.  
- Extends classic single-step KG to the batch setting and clarifies that under noiseless evaluations and restricted recommendations q-KG reduces to parallel EI.  
- Targets continuous domains and provides an efficient stochastic gradient estimator, addressing scalability limitations of prior discrete nested batch KG work.  
- Uses standard GP modeling (ARD Matérn-5/2) with hyperparameters fitted by MLE and re-estimated after each batch.  
- Strength: principled decision-theoretic formulation consistent with KG optimality for one remaining step.  
- Strength: IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator is a meaningful technical contribution enabling joint batch optimization without greedy heuristics.  
- Strength: empirical evaluation is reasonably broad and demonstrates practical benefits, especially in noisy settings.  
- Strength: open-source implementation aids reproducibility.  
- Weakness: theoretical analysis stops at one-step optimality and gradient unbiasedness; lacks discretization error bounds, finite-sample properties, regret bounds, or consistency guarantees.  
- Weakness: discretization strategy is underspecified—how An is constructed and how samples of posterior optima are drawn is unclear and may affect performance.  
- Weakness: computational complexity and wall-clock runtimes are not reported; scaling with larger q and higher dimensions is not evaluated.  
- Weakness: potential implementation asymmetries (e.g., hyperparameter re-estimation frequency) may bias comparisons and need clarification.  
- Clarity strength: paper is generally well organized; q-KG definition and Monte Carlo construction are clearly presented.  
- Clarity issues: discretization section needs more precise description and intuition; report MC sample sizes and discuss variance control.  
- Minor issues: typos and notational slips should be corrected.  
- Originality: batch extension of KG with IPA gradient estimation in continuous domains appears novel; link to EI under special cases is insightful.  
- Significance: relevant to practical batch BO, especially under noisy evaluations; open-source code aids adoption, but missing runtime/scaling results temper immediate impact.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled acquisition, technically interesting gradient estimator, strong empirical performance under noise, and public code.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited theoretical analysis, missing runtime/scaling/sensitivity studies, and ambiguity in discretization procedures.  
- Suggested improvements: add complexity and wall-clock time comparisons across q and d; clarify and ablate discretization scheme and sensitivity to M; report MC sample sizes and gradient-variance control techniques; ensure and state fair hyperparameter handling; discuss asynchronous extensions.  
- Overall: solid, well-motivated contribution with strong empirical results and methodological novelty, meriting acceptance despite gaps in theoretical and scalability analysis.
