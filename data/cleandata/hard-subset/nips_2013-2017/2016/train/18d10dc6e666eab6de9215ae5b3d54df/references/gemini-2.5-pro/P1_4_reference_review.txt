This paper proposes a new acquisition function for parallel (batch) Bayesian Optimization (BO), called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea is to generalize the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy, which is known to be Bayes-optimal for a single remaining evaluation step. The proposed q-KG acquisition function values a batch of `q` candidate points by the expected improvement they provide to the final recommended solution, where the final solution is taken to be the minimum of the posterior mean function. This contrasts with greedy or heuristic approaches that build a batch sequentially and provides a principled, decision-theoretic foundation for jointly selecting the batch of points.

Recognizing that the direct optimization of q-KG is computationally intractable, the main technical contribution of the paper is a method to maximize it efficiently. The authors express the q-KG factor as an expectation over a standard normal random vector and propose a Monte Carlo estimator. Crucially, they use Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic gradient of the q-KG factor. This enables the use of efficient gradient-based methods to find the optimal batch of points. For continuous domains, the authors propose a dynamic discretization scheme to approximate the `argmin` operation within the q-KG definition. The method is evaluated on a suite of synthetic benchmark functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression and CNNs), showing strong performance against other state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, especially in the presence of noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled Derivation:** The acquisition function is derived from a solid decision-theoretic foundation, extending the one-step optimal KG policy to the parallel setting. This provides a clear and well-motivated alternative to more heuristic parallel BO strategies like the "constant liar" or greedy UCB-based methods. The joint optimization of the entire batch is a significant advantage over methods that construct the batch sequentially.

2.  **Novel and Significant Technical Contribution:** The development of an efficient optimization procedure for this complex acquisition function is a major strength. The use of Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to compute an unbiased stochastic gradient is clever and enables the practical application of the q-KG method. This moves beyond simply proposing an acquisition function to providing a viable algorithm for its use.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance, Especially in Noisy Settings:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments on both synthetic functions and real-world machine learning applications. The proposed q-KG method is consistently competitive with or superior to established baselines. Its performance advantage is particularly pronounced in the noisy setting, which is a very common and practical scenario (e.g., when evaluating models on a subset of data or dealing with stochastic algorithms). This is a key result that highlights the practical value of the proposed method.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for the method, its mathematical formulation, and the computational strategy are explained logically. The authors have also made their code available, which is commendable and significantly helps with reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Discussion of Computational Cost:** A notable omission is a thorough discussion of the computational complexity and wall-clock time required to optimize the q-KG acquisition function. While the IPA-based gradient is efficient, the overall procedure involves multi-start stochastic gradient ascent over a `q*d` dimensional space, which can be expensive. A comparison of the batch selection time for q-KG versus the baselines would be very valuable. For BO to be practical, the time spent selecting points must be significantly less than the time for function evaluations. It would be useful to understand the trade-offs and in what regimes (e.g., very expensive `f`) this method is most suitable.

2.  **Sensitivity of the Discretization Scheme:** The dynamic discretization of the domain `A` is a critical component for making the method work in continuous spaces. The paper follows prior work but could be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of this approximation. For example, how sensitive is the performance of q-KG to the number of discretization points `M`? A brief ablation study or discussion on this point would help practitioners understand how to set this hyperparameter.

3.  **Scalability with Batch Size `q`:** The experiments are conducted with a fixed batch size of `q=4`. The paper would be more complete with a discussion or experiment on how the method scales with `q`. The optimization problem dimension (`q*d`) and the cost of Cholesky decomposition (`O(q^3)`) within the GP update suggest that the computational cost could grow rapidly. Understanding these scaling properties is important for users who might want to use larger batch sizes.

4.  **Comparison to Parallel EI:** The paper mentions that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under specific conditions (noise-free, final recommendation restricted to sampled points). However, the experiments show q-KG outperforming parallel EI even in the noise-free setting. A deeper intuition or analysis of why this occurs would be insightful. It is likely due to the flexibility of recommending any point `x` in the domain as the optimum, rather than just previously evaluated points, but explicitly discussing this connection in the results section would strengthen the paper's narrative.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, with a well-founded theoretical motivation and strong empirical support for its claims.
*   **Clarity:** Good. The paper is clearly written and structured. Minor improvements could be made by adding discussions on computational cost and parameter sensitivity.
*   **Originality:** High. The formulation of a joint KG acquisition function for batch BO and the development of an efficient gradient-based optimization method using IPA are novel and significant contributions to the field.
*   **Significance:** High. Parallel BO is a problem of great practical importance. This paper presents a new state-of-the-art algorithm that performs particularly well on noisy problems. The work is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers in the field.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily areas for additional discussion. I recommend acceptance.
