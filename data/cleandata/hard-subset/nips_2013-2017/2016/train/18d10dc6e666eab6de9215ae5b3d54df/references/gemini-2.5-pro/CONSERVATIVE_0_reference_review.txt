This paper proposes a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The method generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) to the parallel setting, where `q` points are to be selected for simultaneous evaluation. The q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, aiming to maximize the expected value of information from a batch of evaluations, assuming it is the final batch. As direct optimization of q-KG is computationally challenging, the authors propose a gradient-based optimization approach. The main technical contribution is the use of Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased Monte Carlo estimator for the gradient of the q-KG factor. The method is evaluated on a set of synthetic benchmark functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks, showing competitive or superior performance against several established parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled Derivation:** The proposed q-KG acquisition function is a methodologically sound and natural extension of the established Knowledge Gradient policy to the batch setting. The one-step Bayes-optimality provides a solid decision-theoretic foundation, which is preferable to more heuristic or greedy approaches for batch construction.
2.  **Novel Computational Approach:** The application of IPA to derive an unbiased gradient estimator for a batch BO acquisition function is a notable contribution. This enables the use of efficient gradient-based optimizers for the non-trivial task of jointly optimizing the locations of `q` points, moving beyond greedy or sequential construction heuristics.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance in Noisy Settings:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage for q-KG in noisy environments. This is an important and practical setting, and the method’s ability to outperform strong baselines like parallel EI is a key strength. The results on noisy synthetic functions and the logistic regression tuning task with noisy labels (small test sets) are compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Cost and Practicality:** The primary concern with the proposed method is its computational complexity. While the paper claims the gradient estimation is "efficient," this claim is not substantiated with empirical evidence. The method relies on Monte Carlo sampling to estimate both the q-KG factor and its gradient. The paper does not report the number of Monte Carlo samples required for stable estimates, nor does it provide any wall-clock time comparisons against the baseline methods. For BO to be practical, the time spent selecting the next batch must be significantly smaller than the time spent evaluating the objective function. It is unclear if this condition holds for q-KG, especially as the batch size `q` or dimensionality `d` increases.
2.  **Reliance on Discretization:** The method's implementation for continuous domains relies on a discretization of the search space `A` to compute the inner minimization term `min_{x \in A} \mu^{(n+q)}(x)`. The authors propose a dynamic discretization scheme, but the performance of the overall algorithm may be highly sensitive to the quality and size (`M`) of this discrete set. The paper uses `M=1000` but provides no sensitivity analysis. A poor discretization could lead to a significant underestimation of the true q-KG value and result in suboptimal decisions. Furthermore, the paper states the discrete set is `An = AMn ∪ x(1:n) ∪ z(1:q)`. This implies the set over which the minimum is taken changes as the candidate points `z(1:q)` are optimized. This seems extraordinarily expensive, as it would require re-evaluating many terms within the gradient ascent loop. This crucial implementation detail is not sufficiently explained or justified.
3.  **Limited Scalability Analysis:** The experiments are conducted on problems with low to moderate dimensionality (up to d=8) and a fixed batch size of `q=4`. The scalability of the method with respect to both `d` and `q` is not investigated. The gradient computation involves operations on `q x q` covariance matrices (including a Cholesky decomposition), which scales as O(q³). The overall optimization cost is likely to grow substantially with `q` and `d`, potentially limiting its applicability to the small-batch, low-dimensional settings presented.
4.  **Inconclusive Performance in Noise-Free Settings:** While the method excels in noisy problems, its advantage in the standard noise-free setting is less pronounced. It performs slightly worse than baselines on the Branin function and offers modest improvements on others. Given the likely increase in computational overhead compared to simpler methods like parallel EI, the trade-off may not be favorable in noise-free scenarios. An established method should demonstrate a clearer, more consistent advantage to justify its added complexity.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

The paper introduces a theoretically well-motivated extension of the KG method to batch Bayesian optimization. The use of IPA for gradient estimation is a solid technical contribution. The empirical results in noisy settings are impressive and represent a clear strength.

However, the work is let down by a lack of critical evaluation regarding its practical viability. The computational cost, a known challenge for KG-based methods, is not addressed empirically. The reliance on a heuristic discretization scheme without a sensitivity analysis raises concerns about the method's robustness. Without timing comparisons and a more thorough analysis of scalability and the impact of the discretization parameter `M`, the presented approach remains an interesting but unproven innovation. The benefits over well-established baselines are not sufficiently demonstrated to outweigh these significant practical concerns, especially in the standard noise-free setting.

Therefore, while the paper presents valuable ideas, it does not currently meet the high standards for rigor and thorough validation expected for a NIPS publication. I would recommend major revisions to address the computational analysis and the role of the discretization before it could be considered for acceptance.
