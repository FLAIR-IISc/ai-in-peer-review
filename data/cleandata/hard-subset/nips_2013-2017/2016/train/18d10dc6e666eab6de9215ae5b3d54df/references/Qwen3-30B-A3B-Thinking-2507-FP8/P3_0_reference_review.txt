

# Peer Review: "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors develop a decision-theoretic acquisition function that jointly optimizes a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously, rather than sequentially selecting points one by one. The key technical contribution is an efficient computational method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize the q-KG acquisition function. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that q-KG outperforms existing batch BO methods (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), especially in noisy settings, on both synthetic functions and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems.

## Strengths

1. **Solid Theoretical Foundation**: The paper builds on the well-established knowledge gradient method for sequential BO and provides a rigorous extension to the batch setting. The decision-theoretic justification for q-KG is sound and clearly explained.

2. **Novel Computational Approach**: The use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute gradients of q-KG is a significant technical contribution. This addresses a major practical challenge in batch BO where naive approaches would be computationally intractable.

3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments on both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and real-world applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The comparison with multiple relevant baselines (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) is appropriate.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The paper demonstrates clear practical value by showing that q-KG can improve hyperparameter tuning for machine learning models, with a notable 3% improvement in test error on CIFAR10 for CNNs.

5. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-written, logically organized, and provides sufficient mathematical details for the technical contributions.

## Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1. **Theoretical Gap**: The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the q-KG method. While the sequential KG has known theoretical guarantees, the paper doesn't establish how these extend to the batch setting, which is a significant omission for a NIPS paper. The authors should discuss whether q-KG maintains the theoretical properties of sequential KG in the batch setting.

2. **Limited Baseline Comparison**: The paper doesn't compare against several relevant recent batch BO methods that also optimize the batch collectively, such as the gradient-based parallel EI method in [15] and the parallel entropy search in [18] that are mentioned in the related work. The comparison is limited to parallel EI (iterative), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, which are less directly comparable to the authors' approach.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Computational Complexity**: The paper claims that IPA makes the method "efficient" but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity compared to alternatives. For instance, how does the time complexity scale with q (the batch size) compared to the iterative parallel EI approach? This is crucial for understanding when q-KG would be preferable to other methods.

4. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The experimental results present mean and standard deviation, but the paper doesn't provide statistical tests to determine whether the observed improvements are statistically significant. This is especially important given the variability in hyperparameter tuning experiments.

5. **Ambiguity in the Relationship to Sequential KG**: The paper states that "q-KG will reduce to the parallel EI algorithm if function evaluations are noise-free and the final recommendation is restricted to the previous sampling decisions," but doesn't provide the mathematical derivation. A brief derivation would strengthen the connection between the two methods.

6. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of the proposed method. For instance, how does the method scale with large batch sizes (q)? How sensitive is it to the choice of discretization set (M=1000)? What are the specific scenarios where q-KG might not perform well?

## Specific Technical Comments

1. **Equation (4.1)**: The definition of q-KG as the expected improvement in the minimum of the GP predictor is well-motivated. However, the paper should clarify why this particular quantity is the appropriate objective for minimizing the function value, rather than alternative objectives like expected improvement of the batch.

2. **Section 5.2**: The IPA approach for gradient estimation is novel and well-explained, but the paper doesn't address the potential bias in the gradient estimate. The authors should discuss whether the IPA estimator is unbiased and why this matters for optimization.

3. **Section 6.2.2**: The experiment on noisy logistic regression with small test sets is interesting and practically relevant, but the paper doesn't explain why the test error on a small subset is a "noisy approximation" rather than simply an estimate with higher variance. This distinction is important for understanding the noise model.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper correctly identifies the key distinction between their approach and previous work: most batch BO methods use iterative greedy selection of points (e.g., parallel EI), while q-KG optimizes the batch collectively. This is a valid distinction, but the paper could better position itself against recent work that also optimizes the batch collectively. The authors cite [2, 15, 25] as using gradient-based methods for parallel EI, but don't directly compare with these methods. A comparison with [15] would be particularly valuable since it also uses gradient-based optimization for batch acquisition functions.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important practical problem in Bayesian optimization: how to efficiently utilize parallel evaluations. This is particularly relevant for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning, where multiple models can be trained simultaneously on different machines. The demonstrated improvements, especially in noisy settings, are significant for practitioners. However, the significance could be strengthened by providing more concrete examples of time or resource savings that would be achieved by using q-KG instead of existing methods.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with solid technical contributions, thorough experiments, and clear practical relevance. The proposed q-KG method represents a meaningful advancement in batch Bayesian optimization, particularly for noisy settings. However, the paper has several important weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be considered for publication at NIPS.

The most critical gaps are the lack of theoretical analysis of the batch method, limited baseline comparison against relevant recent work, and insufficient discussion of computational complexity and limitations. These are significant issues for a NIPS paper, which typically expects strong theoretical grounding alongside empirical validation.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with a novel method for batch selection. The technical approach is sound, and the experimental results are compelling. However, the paper would benefit from addressing the theoretical gaps, expanding the comparison to include relevant recent work, and providing a more comprehensive discussion of the method's limitations and computational complexity.

With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS. As it stands, it is a good paper that makes a useful contribution, but the theoretical weaknesses prevent it from being a truly outstanding contribution for NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Revision

1. Add a theoretical section analyzing the convergence properties of q-KG in the batch setting, comparing with the sequential KG case.

2. Expand the comparison to include recent methods that optimize the batch collectively, particularly [15] and [18].

3. Provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of q-KG compared to other methods, especially as a function of batch size q.

4. Include statistical significance tests for the experimental results.

5. Add a discussion of the limitations of the method, including sensitivity to discretization parameters and potential failure cases.

6. Clarify the relationship between q-KG and parallel EI with a brief mathematical derivation.

7. Discuss the practical implications of the computational complexity results for real-world applications.
