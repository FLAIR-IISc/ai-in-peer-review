Summary
The paper proposes the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), an acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization (BO) that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to the parallel setting. The acquisition value of a batch z(1:q) is the expected decrease in the minimum posterior mean minx∈A μ(n)(x) after observing the batch, i.e., En[minx μ(n)(x) − minx μ(n+q)(x)], which is Bayes-optimal in the one-remaining-batch setting. The authors show that q-KG reduces to parallel expected improvement (q-EI) when observations are noise-free and the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points. Because evaluating q-KG and optimizing over z is computationally challenging, they develop a Monte Carlo estimator and an unbiased stochastic gradient via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization. To handle continuous domains in the inner minimizations, they discretize A adaptively by sampling candidate minimizers of the GP posterior (augmented by previously sampled points and the current candidate batch). Experiments on synthetic functions and on hyperparameter tuning (MNIST logistic regression; CIFAR-10 CNN) show that q-KG outperforms or matches q-EI (including MOE), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with especially strong gains under observation noise.

Relation to prior work: The method builds on sequential KG [7,17], parallel EI [2,15,25], UCB-based batch methods [3,5], and entropy-search-based batch BO [11,18]. It is also related to nested batch KG on discrete sets [26], but differs by addressing continuous domains and by developing differentiable estimators enabling efficient optimization. The reduction to q-EI in the noiseless/restricted-solution setting connects to the known Bayes-optimality distinctions between EI and KG.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The acquisition is well-defined and derives directly from decision-theoretic principles. The Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG is standard given the linear-Gaussian update form. The IPA-based stochastic gradient is sensible; exchanging expectation and differentiation is justified under smoothness assumptions and almost-sure uniqueness of minimizers (ties are measure-zero). However, these conditions and the resulting unbiasedness are deferred to the supplement; a brief statement of the required regularity (e.g., differentiability of kernel, handling of non-differentiability from argmin) would strengthen the main text. The discretization of A introduces approximation bias; the paper acknowledges this but does not analyze its impact or provide error controls.
- Algorithmic and computational aspects: The approach requires repeated inner minimizations over a discretized set for each Monte Carlo draw and gradient step. While the use of Cholesky factors and vectorized forms is appropriate, the paper lacks a complexity analysis in terms of q, M (discretization size), and the number of Monte Carlo samples for function and gradient estimates. Derivatives of Cholesky factors are used; implementation details and numerical stability considerations are not discussed. Convergence of the stochastic gradient ascent (e.g., step sizes, stopping criteria) is not described.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are generally well-executed, with consistent improvements under noise. Hyperparameters of the GP, including noise variance, are learned via MLE for fairness across methods. However, comparisons do not include entropy-search-based batch methods (PPES [18]), which are strong baselines. The batch size is fixed at q=4; sensitivity to q and to discretization size M is not reported. Runtime and scalability are not evaluated, which is important for batch methods optimized via Monte Carlo.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. The decision-theoretic motivation is concise, and the reduction to q-EI is informative. Some aspects would benefit from clarification:
- The construction “draw M samples from the global optima of the posterior” (Section 5.3) is vague; it likely refers to sampling from the posterior over the minimizer (as in ES/PES), but the mechanism and computational cost should be stated or referenced precisely ([11,18]).
- Minor typos (e.g., “Main Stange”), and some notation could be tightened (consistent use of A vs. discretized An; explicit statement that z is optimized over the continuous A while the inner min uses An).
- Details needed for reproducibility: number of Monte Carlo samples per gradient step, learning rate schedule, number of restarts, and stopping criteria.

Originality
Extending KG to parallel batch selection with an efficient, unbiased stochastic gradient via IPA for continuous domains appears novel. The connection to q-EI in a limiting setting is known in spirit but here is usefully articulated. While batch BO with parallel EI and entropy-based methods is well studied, the decision-theoretic q-KG with practical gradient estimation and optimization represents a distinct contribution.

Significance
The method is practically relevant for batch BO, particularly under noise, and the open-source code increases impact. The improvements over strong baselines (q-EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic and tuning tasks suggest utility. The absence of comparisons to PPES [18], lack of runtime/scaling analysis, and limited sensitivity studies temper the claims. Theoretical guarantees beyond one-step Bayes optimality (e.g., regret bounds) are not provided, which is typical for KG but limits formal significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear decision-theoretic formulation of batch acquisition (q-KG); reduction to q-EI in a special case.
- Novel and principled stochastic gradient estimator via IPA enabling efficient joint optimization of batch points.
- Strong empirical results, especially in noisy settings relevant to ML hyperparameter tuning.
- Public implementation and thorough comparisons to widely used baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing comparisons to entropy-search-based batch methods (PPES), which may be competitive.
- No complexity or runtime analysis; scalability with q, M, and MC samples is not characterized.
- Discretization scheme for inner minimization is under-specified; no sensitivity or error analysis.
- Limited ablations (e.g., effect of q, MC sample size, discretization size); only q=4 is studied.
- Theoretical properties (beyond one-step optimality) and convergence of the optimization algorithm are not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a complexity analysis and runtime benchmarks versus q-EI and PPES across q and dimension.
- Clarify and justify the discretization strategy (how samples from the posterior over minimizers are obtained; sensitivity to M; impact on bias/variance).
- Include comparisons to PPES [18] and, if feasible, to recent scalable batch BO methods; report wall-clock time.
- Report sensitivity to batch size q and to noise level; add ablations on the number of Monte Carlo samples and their impact on convergence/stability.
- Provide implementation details for stochastic gradient ascent (step sizes, restarts) and numerical stability for differentiating Cholesky factors.
- Minor editorial fixes and clearer notation around A vs. An.

Overall evaluation
A technically solid and practically meaningful contribution that extends KG to parallel BO with an effective optimization strategy, demonstrating strong empirical performance, particularly under noise. Addressing the missing baselines, scalability, and clarity on discretization would further strengthen the work.
