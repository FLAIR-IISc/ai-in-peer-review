This paper proposes a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The method is derived from a principled decision-theoretic framework, choosing a batch of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final recommended solution value after one more parallel evaluation step. This one-step Bayes-optimal approach generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to the parallel setting. The main technical contribution is a method to efficiently optimize this acquisition function, which involves a Monte Carlo expectation over a minimum of posterior means. The authors develop an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), enabling the use of gradient-based optimizers. The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation on synthetic benchmarks and two machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, comparing q-KG against several state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, including parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and parallel Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) variants. The results demonstrate that q-KG is highly competitive and often outperforms baselines, with a particularly significant advantage in noisy settings.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled Derivation:** The q-KG acquisition function is not a heuristic. It is derived from a solid decision-theoretic foundation, representing the Bayes-optimal choice for a single remaining batch of evaluations. This provides a strong theoretical justification for the method, which is a significant advantage over more heuristic or greedy approaches to parallel BO.

2.  **Novel and Efficient Optimization Strategy:** The primary challenge with a lookahead acquisition function like q-KG is its computational complexity. The authors' use of Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator is a key contribution. This allows them to jointly optimize the locations of all `q` points in the batch efficiently, moving the derivative inside the Monte Carlo expectation. This is a technically sophisticated and powerful approach that makes the principled q-KG acquisition function practical.

3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is thorough. The authors compare against multiple strong, open-source baselines on a variety of synthetic test functions of varying dimensionality. Crucially, they also demonstrate performance on relevant, real-world machine learning problems (tuning Logistic Regression and a CNN). The inclusion of both noise-free and noisy settings is a major strength, and the results convincingly show the method's particular aptitude for noisy optimization problems—a very common and practical scenario. The improvement on the CIFAR-10 task from a human-tuned baseline is particularly impressive.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the technical details of the gradient estimation are laid out logically, and the experimental setup is well-described. The authors' decision to make their code publicly available is commendable and significantly increases the potential impact and reproducibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Cost Analysis:** While the paper presents an *efficient* method for gradient estimation, the overall computational cost of optimizing the q-KG acquisition function is still likely to be high compared to baselines like greedy EI or parallel UCB. The optimization requires multiple Monte Carlo samples and iterations of a stochastic gradient ascent algorithm. The paper lacks a discussion of the wall-clock time trade-off. For expensive objective functions, this overhead may be negligible, but for moderately expensive functions, it could be a significant factor. A comparison of the time required to select one batch for q-KG versus the baselines would make the paper more complete.

2.  **Dependence on Discretization:** The computation of the q-KG factor requires finding the minimum of the posterior mean over the domain A. To make this tractable, the authors rely on a discretization of A. The quality of this discretization (controlled by parameter M and the sampling strategy) is critical to the performance of the algorithm. While the chosen strategy is sensible and builds on prior work, the paper does not analyze the sensitivity of q-KG to the quality of this approximation (e.g., how performance changes with M).

3.  **Scalability with Batch Size `q`:** The experiments are conducted with a fixed batch size of `q=4`. The computational method involves a Cholesky decomposition of a `q x q` matrix and optimizing `q*d` variables. It is not clear how the method's performance and computational cost scale as `q` grows to larger values (e.g., 16, 32, 64), which are relevant in modern large-scale parallel computing environments. A discussion on the scalability limitations with respect to `q` would be a valuable addition.

**Summary of evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound. The decision-theoretic derivation is solid, the application of IPA for gradient estimation is correct and novel in this context, and the claims are very well-supported by extensive experiments.
*   **Clarity:** High. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the methodology.
*   **Originality:** High. The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the KG framework to the batch setting and, most importantly, providing a practical, gradient-based algorithm for its optimization. This approach of jointly optimizing a one-step optimal acquisition function is a clear advance over greedy or heuristic parallelization strategies.
*   **Significance:** High. Batch Bayesian optimization is an important and active area of research. This paper introduces a new state-of-the-art method that is likely to be widely used and built upon, especially for problems with noisy evaluations. The availability of open-source code further enhances its potential impact.

**Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a principled, novel, and effective method for a significant problem. The strengths—a strong theoretical foundation, a clever computational solution, and compelling empirical results—far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily related to clarifying computational trade-offs and limitations. I strongly recommend acceptance.
