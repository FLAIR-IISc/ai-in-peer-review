- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that selects q points by expected reduction in the GP posterior mean minimum.
- Shows q-KG reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless setting when final recommendation is constrained to sampled points.
- Acquisition is intractable for q>1; authors use Monte Carlo reparameterization with standard normal Zq to estimate it.
- Derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based optimization.
- For continuous domains, inner minimization is discretized adaptively using samples near posterior optima and previous evaluations.
- Experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning (MNIST, CIFAR-10) show q-KG is competitive or superior to q-EI variants and batch-UCB, especially under observation noise.
- Situates work among KG, q-EI, PPES, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE; novelty is a principled batch KG with tractable unbiased gradient optimization.
- Technical soundness: reparameterization and IPA derivation are plausible, but key differentiability/argmin justifications are deferred to the supplement.
- Computational method: using Cholesky to propagate derivatives is reasonable, but numerical stability and complexity as q and M grow need more discussion.
- Discretization of the inner minimization introduces bias; adaptive scheme helps but no formal analysis of bias or sensitivity is provided.
- Empirics are promising under noise, but lack wall-clock/runtime comparisons, scaling analysis with q/d/M, and ablation studies (e.g., finite-difference vs IPA).
- Theory is limited to myopic Bayes-optimality for one remaining batch; no regret or convergence guarantees are given.
- Clarity: generally well-written with helpful algorithm summary, but some notation/clarity issues, minor typos, incomplete references, and sparse optimization/reproducibility details.
- Originality: extending KG to joint batch selection with an unbiased stochastic gradient appears novel; the reduction to q-EI is a neat unifying result.
- Significance: practically important and likely impactful if it scales well, though missing baselines and runtime analyses limit demonstrated impact.
- Pros: principled decision-theoretic objective, unbiased gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization, strong noisy-setting performance, code released.
- Cons: reliance on discretization without sensitivity/ bias analysis, limited theoretical guarantees, incomplete empirical baselines and reproducibility details.
- Suggestions: add wall-clock and scaling experiments vs q-EI/PPES, sensitivity analyses for M/discretization, clarify optimizer/MC settings, include PPES/local penalization/Thompson baselines, and discuss asynchronous extensions.
- Overall: novel and useful contribution that likely merits acceptance pending stronger empirical validation and minor clarifications.
