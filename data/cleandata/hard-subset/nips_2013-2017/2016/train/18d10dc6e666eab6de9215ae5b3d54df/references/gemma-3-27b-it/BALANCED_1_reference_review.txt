This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient approach to parallel evaluations. The core idea is to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected incremental value of information, considering the entire batch collectively rather than iteratively. The authors derive an acquisition function and address its computational challenges via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate gradients. Empirical results on synthetic functions and practical machine learning tasks demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms other parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper is well-motivated, building on established concepts in BO and addressing a critical need for efficient parallel optimization. The problem setting – parallel BO with a focus on noisy evaluations – is relevant and practical.  The theoretical derivation of q-KG, linking it to Bayes-optimality under specific conditions (one remaining batch), is a significant strength.  The IPA-based gradient estimation is a clever solution to the computational bottleneck of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function. The experimental validation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic benchmarks and real-world applications (logistic regression and CNN tuning) in both noise-free and noisy scenarios. The code availability is commendable.  The paper clearly situates itself within the existing BO literature, appropriately referencing relevant work such as parallel EI, UCB, and other KG variants.

However, the paper isn’t without its weaknesses. While the derivation is sound, the connection to practical applicability could be strengthened. The assumptions made (e.g., normally distributed noise) are stated, but a discussion of the method's robustness to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.  Although IPA is used for efficient gradient calculation, the computational complexity of the method is still not fully addressed.  For example, the discretization of the domain `A` in section 5.3 relies on a fixed parameter `M` and doesn’t seem to be adaptively adjusted. This could lead to suboptimal performance if the GP posterior is highly concentrated in a small region of the search space.  The description of the experimental setup lacks some detail. For example, the specific hyperparameter ranges for the CNN tuning are only briefly mentioned. A more thorough description would enhance reproducibility.  Additionally, while the paper claims significant improvements, the error bars presented in the figures sometimes overlap considerably, making it difficult to discern statistically significant differences. A more rigorous statistical analysis (e.g., using paired t-tests or ANOVA) would bolster the claims of superiority.  The writing, while generally clear, could benefit from more concise phrasing in certain sections, particularly those detailing the mathematical derivations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The q-KG method represents a novel approach to parallel BO, grounded in decision-theoretic principles.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the acquisition function and the use of IPA are mathematically well-justified.
*   **Empirical Performance:**  The experimental results consistently demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods, especially in noisy settings.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Parallel BO is highly relevant to many real-world applications, and the method is readily applicable.
*   **Code Availability:** This promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The computational cost, while reduced by IPA, could still be a limitation for high-dimensional problems.
*   **Assumptions:** The method relies on certain assumptions that may not always hold in practice.
*   **Statistical Rigor:** The presented results lack robust statistical analysis to definitively demonstrate superiority.
*   **Discretization:** The static discretization strategy could limit performance in certain scenarios.



**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The proposed q-KG method is theoretically sound, empirically effective, and addresses a significant challenge in parallel optimization. While some weaknesses exist, the strengths outweigh the limitations.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the method and potential strategies for further optimization.
2.  Include a more comprehensive statistical analysis of the experimental results, with appropriate statistical tests and significance levels.
3.  Explore adaptive discretization strategies to improve performance in scenarios with highly concentrated posterior distributions.
4.  Elaborate on the sensitivity of the method to violations of the underlying assumptions (e.g., normality of noise).
5.  Provide a more detailed description of the experimental setup, including specific hyperparameter ranges and optimization parameters.
