

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), extending the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function from sequential to batch settings. The authors derive q-KG as a decision-theoretically optimal acquisition function for the parallel setting, where q points can be evaluated simultaneously. They address the computational challenge of maximizing q-KG by developing an efficient gradient estimation method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The paper compares q-KG against several state-of-the-art batch BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration) on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, demonstrating consistent improvements, especially in noisy settings.

The work builds on the knowledge gradient framework for sequential BO [17] and extends the parallel BO literature [2, 3, 5, 18, 25]. While previous parallel BO methods typically optimize acquisition functions in an iterative greedy manner, the authors' approach jointly optimizes the acquisition function over the entire batch, which they argue is more theoretically sound.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a clear and significant theoretical contribution by extending the knowledge gradient to the batch setting and providing a decision-theoretic justification for the q-KG acquisition function. This is a well-motivated extension with a sound theoretical foundation.

2. **Computational Innovation:** The use of IPA for efficient gradient estimation of q-KG is a practical and well-justified technical contribution. The computational approach addresses a major bottleneck in batch BO and enables the method to scale better than naive implementations.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering both noise-free and noisy synthetic functions, as well as two real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently demonstrate q-KG's superiority, particularly in noisy settings.

4. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide code availability, which is a significant plus for the community and enhances reproducibility.

5. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and provides sufficient mathematical detail for a technical audience. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scalability Analysis:** While the paper acknowledges that naive maximization of q-KG is computationally intensive for large q, it lacks a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the IPA-based method. The authors should provide concrete complexity analysis (e.g., how runtime scales with q and dimensionality d) and empirical evidence of scalability for larger q values.

2. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison:** The paper compares against several batch BO methods but omits direct comparison with recent gradient-based parallel EI methods [15, 25], which could provide a more complete picture of q-KG's relative advantages. Including these comparisons would strengthen the empirical claims.

3. **Limited Dimensionality Analysis:** The paper doesn't thoroughly address how the method scales with dimensionality (d), which is a critical factor in Bayesian optimization. A more detailed analysis of performance as d increases would be valuable.

4. **Theoretical Justification Gap:** While the paper establishes q-KG as optimal for the one-iteration remaining case, it doesn't provide a stronger theoretical justification for why q-KG outperforms other batch methods in the general case beyond this specific scenario. A more comprehensive theoretical comparison would strengthen the paper.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Add a section analyzing the computational complexity of the IPA-based method, including runtime scaling with q and d. Provide empirical evidence of scalability for larger batch sizes (e.g., q=10, q=20).

2. Include direct comparisons with gradient-based parallel EI methods [15, 25] to provide a more complete empirical evaluation.

3. Expand the experimental section to include a more detailed analysis of performance as dimensionality d increases, particularly for high-dimensional problems.

4. Strengthen the theoretical discussion by providing a more comprehensive comparison of q-KG's optimality properties with other batch acquisition functions.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of the method's limitations, such as cases where the Gaussian process approximation may be inadequate or when the domain A is highly non-convex.

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the batch Bayesian optimization literature. The theoretical extension of knowledge gradient to the batch setting is well-motivated and technically sound, and the computational approach using IPA is practical and innovative. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate consistent improvements over state-of-the-art baselines, especially in noisy settings.

While the paper has minor weaknesses in scalability analysis and baseline comparison, these are relatively minor issues that could be addressed in a revised version. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

The paper is well-positioned to make a significant impact in the field, particularly given the increasing importance of batch optimization for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning. With the suggested improvements, the paper would be even stronger, but as it stands, it meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission.
