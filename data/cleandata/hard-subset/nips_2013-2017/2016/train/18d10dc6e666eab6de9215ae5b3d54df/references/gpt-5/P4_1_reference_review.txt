Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to selecting q points jointly. The acquisition maximizes the expected reduction in the GP posterior predictive minimum after one additional batch of evaluations. It is Bayes-optimal in the myopic setting with a single remaining batch, and reduces to parallel EI under noise-free observations when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points. Because the acquisition requires expectations over the posterior conditioned on yet-unseen batch outcomes, the paper develops a Monte Carlo estimator and an efficient stochastic gradient based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the q-point set. For continuous domains, the inner minimization in the q-KG definition is approximated by discretizing A using samples of posterior optima, augmented by previously sampled points and the candidate batch.

The method is positioned relative to: (i) sequential acquisitions (EI, PI, UCB, ES, KG), (ii) batch extensions that greedily build a batch (constant liar, heuristic parallel EI), (iii) joint batch acquisitions requiring Monte Carlo (q-EI with gradients; PPES), and (iv) KG variants in discrete domains and nested-batch settings. The paper’s main distinctions are a decision-theoretic batch KG objective for continuous domains and an unbiased IPA gradient estimator to optimize it. Empirically, q-KG is compared to parallel EI (closed-form and heuristic variants) and batch UCB policies (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic test functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR10 CNN), with results indicating consistent gains, especially under noise. Code is released.

Strengths
- Decision-theoretic objective: q-KG directly optimizes expected reduction in posterior predictive minimum for one remaining batch, a principled criterion distinct from regret-focused UCB and improvement-based EI.
- Handles noise naturally: the formulation and GP posterior updates incorporate observation noise; q-KG exhibits strong performance in noisy experiments, which is consistent with KG’s strengths.
- Gradient-based optimization via IPA: provides an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG under smoothness assumptions, enabling practical joint optimization of q locations. Using Cholesky factors and reusing GP routines is sensible.
- Empirical evidence: across multiple synthetic functions and two realistic ML tuning tasks, q-KG achieves lower regret/test error and maintains advantages in noisy settings. Open-source implementation supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Discretization of A for the inner minimization: The approach relies on approximating the minimum over A by a finite set An formed by “drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior” plus previously sampled points and the candidate batch. The procedure for sampling these optima is only sketched (referencing ES/PPES techniques), and its effect on bias/variance of the q-KG estimate is not analyzed. No sensitivity analysis w.r.t. M, nor guarantees on the discretization error, are provided.
- Baseline coverage: The comparisons omit strong joint batch methods such as parallel predictive entropy search (PPES) and batch Thompson sampling, which are natural competitors for parallel BO with Monte Carlo optimization. Including only EI variants and batch UCB provides an incomplete picture.
- Computational analysis: There is no explicit complexity characterization for evaluating q-KG and its gradient as functions of n, q, and M. Empirical wall-clock comparisons and scalability with larger q are not reported, yet these are central for batch BO in practice.
- IPA justification details: Exchanging derivative and expectation with a nonsmooth objective involving argmin over An is delicate. The paper states assumptions (µ, K differentiable) and defers proofs to the supplement; however, the inner min creates non-differentiability on measure-zero sets, and robustness of the estimator under ties/changing minimizers should be discussed in the main text. For the continuous case, gradients are taken after discretization; clarifying the implications would help.
- Experimental details: Only q=4 is shown; behavior for different batch sizes, asynchronous settings, and ablations (e.g., number of Monte Carlo samples for Zq, step sizes/initialization for gradient ascent) are missing. No discussion of GP hyperparameter priors, noise variance learning stability, or the effect of refitting hyperparameters at each iteration on performance.
- Presentation issues: A few typos (e.g., “Main Stange”), minor notation inconsistencies in the EI equivalence derivation, and incomplete references (missing years/pages/venues for several entries).

Quality
- The formulation of q-KG is sound and aligns with KG’s decision-theoretic foundation. The Monte Carlo estimator and IPA-based gradient are standard tools adapted appropriately. However, theoretical guarantees are limited to the myopic optimality statement; there is no convergence or regret analysis, which is common for BO but worth noting. The computational approach seems correct but would benefit from complexity analysis and variance reduction details.
- Empirical evaluation supports the claims on test functions and ML tuning; however, stronger baselines and wall-clock measurements would substantiate the practical impact.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The algorithm and gradient estimator are described concretely for finite An. The discretization strategy and its practical implementation require more detail. Some notation and reference formatting should be polished.

Originality
- Extending KG to batch selections with a joint objective for continuous domains and providing an IPA gradient estimator is a meaningful, though incremental, contribution relative to prior KG in discrete sets and existing joint batch acquisitions (q-EI, PPES). The decision-theoretic perspective for batches is less explored and constitutes novelty.

Significance
- The method addresses an important use case: parallel BO with noise. If computational overhead is acceptable, practitioners could benefit, particularly in noisy settings. Impact would be higher with broader baseline coverage, scalability results, and clarifications on discretization.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled batch acquisition with clear myopic optimality interpretation.
- Effective, implementable gradient estimator for joint batch optimization.
- Consistent empirical gains, especially with noise; open-source code.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited baseline comparisons (omits PPES and batch Thompson sampling) and lack of wall-clock/scalability analysis.
- Discretization and IPA details insufficiently justified in the main paper; no sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Minor clarity and reference issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Describe precisely how AMn is constructed (e.g., sampling posterior functions via random features and minimizing them) and analyze sensitivity to M and the sampling procedure.
- Add complexity analysis per gradient evaluation and per optimization step; report wall-clock times and scaling in q.
- Include PPES and batch Thompson sampling baselines; explore larger q and possibly asynchronous batches.
- Provide details on Monte Carlo sample sizes for Zq, optimizer settings, and variance-reduction techniques.
- Fix typos, standardize notation in the EI equivalence, and complete references.
