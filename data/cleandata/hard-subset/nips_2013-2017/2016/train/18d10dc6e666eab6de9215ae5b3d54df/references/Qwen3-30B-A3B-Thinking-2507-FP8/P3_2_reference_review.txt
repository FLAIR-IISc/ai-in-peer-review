

# Peer Review: "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) that extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors propose a decision-theoretic approach to selecting batches of q points to evaluate simultaneously, which is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor when only one batch remains. To address the computational challenge of maximizing q-KG, they develop an efficient gradient estimation technique based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over existing parallel BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, parallel UCB with exploration) on synthetic functions and practical machine learning tuning tasks, with particularly strong performance in noisy settings.

The work builds on the knowledge gradient framework [7, 17], which differs from the more common expected improvement (EI) approach by not restricting the final solution to previously evaluated points. The authors position their work as the first decision-theoretic batch BO method that considers the batch collectively rather than iteratively, distinguishing it from prior work that extends EI or UCB to batch settings.

## Quality

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical foundation for the q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated by decision theory, and the IPA-based gradient estimation technique is clever and well-executed. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10).

However, the theoretical justification for the Bayes-optimality claim requires strengthening. The paper states that "the parallel knowledge gradient policy is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the predictor of the GP if only one decision is remaining" but provides no formal proof or detailed justification for this claim. The connection between the sequential KG (which is Bayes-optimal for the sequential setting) and the batch version needs to be more rigorously established.

The computational complexity analysis is limited. While the authors claim efficiency through IPA, they don't provide concrete complexity bounds or compare computational times between q-KG and other methods. This is particularly important for a paper claiming to provide an efficient method for batch optimization.

The experimental section would benefit from more thorough analysis of the noise handling in the noisy settings. The paper states they assume "additive independent normally distributed noise" but doesn't explain how the noise variance is handled in the q-KG acquisition function during optimization, which is crucial for the noisy setting results.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement, methodology, and experimental design are presented in a logical flow with consistent notation. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and easy to follow. The figures (Figures 3-4) effectively illustrate the performance comparisons.

One minor clarity issue is in Section 5.3, where the authors describe their discretization strategy: "we suggest drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior distribution of the Gaussian process." This description is somewhat vague. The paper would benefit from more detail on how exactly these samples are drawn (e.g., via optimization, random sampling, or other techniques) and why this approach is preferable to standard discretization methods.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution to the batch Bayesian optimization literature. While several papers have extended sequential BO to batch settings (e.g., [2, 15, 18, 25, 26]), this is the first to apply the knowledge gradient framework to the batch setting. The authors correctly distinguish their work from prior art by noting that most existing parallel methods construct batches iteratively by maximizing a single-point acquisition function, whereas q-KG optimizes the batch collectively.

The IPA-based gradient estimation technique for q-KG appears novel. The paper effectively contrasts with [26] (which studies a nested batch knowledge gradient policy) by noting that their method is more efficient due to the continuous nature of the domain (where gradients exist) versus the discrete set considered in [26].

However, the paper could better contextualize its contribution within the broader landscape of parallel BO. While they cite relevant work, a more detailed comparison of how q-KG differs from the parallel EI methods in [2, 15, 25] would strengthen the originality claim.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high. Batch Bayesian optimization is increasingly important for real-world applications where multiple evaluations can be run in parallel (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines), and the paper demonstrates that q-KG provides consistent improvements over existing methods, especially in noisy settings where real-world evaluations often occur.

The practical impact is substantial: the paper shows that q-KG can improve CNN tuning on CIFAR10 from 14% test error to 11%, which is a meaningful improvement in a practical machine learning task. The consistent performance across multiple synthetic functions and real-world applications demonstrates the method's generalizability.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the state of the art in Bayesian optimization, advancing the field beyond the common parallel EI and UCB extensions.

## Overall Assessment and Recommendations

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to Bayesian optimization. The q-KG method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing methods. The IPA-based computational approach is clever and addresses a key practical challenge.

**Strengths**:
- Well-motivated extension of knowledge gradient to batch setting
- Novel IPA-based gradient estimation technique
- Comprehensive experimental validation across diverse test functions and real-world applications
- Consistent outperformance over benchmarks, especially in noisy settings
- Clear and well-organized presentation

**Weaknesses**:
- Theoretical justification for Bayes-optimality in batch setting is insufficient
- Limited computational complexity analysis and comparison
- Vague description of discretization strategy in Section 5.3
- Limited discussion of scalability with larger batch sizes (q)
- Incomplete explanation of noise handling in the acquisition function

**Recommendations for Revision**:
1. Provide a formal proof or detailed justification for the Bayes-optimality claim in the batch setting.
2. Add a computational complexity analysis comparing q-KG to other batch BO methods.
3. Clarify the discretization strategy in Section 5.3 with more technical detail.
4. Include a discussion of how the method scales with increasing batch size (q).
5. Provide more detail on how the noise variance is handled within the q-KG acquisition function during optimization.

## Final Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions. This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is theoretically well-founded, computationally efficient, and empirically validated across a range of problems. The minor weaknesses identified are easily addressable with revisions that would strengthen an already strong paper.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound, well-written, original, and significant. The work advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization and has clear practical applications in machine learning hyperparameter tuning. The authors have provided a valuable contribution to the field that will be of interest to both researchers and practitioners.

I would be happy to recommend this paper for acceptance at NIPS after addressing the minor revisions outlined above.
