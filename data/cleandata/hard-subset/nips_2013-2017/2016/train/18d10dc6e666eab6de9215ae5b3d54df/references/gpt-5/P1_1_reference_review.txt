Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes q-KG, an acquisition function and associated algorithm for batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO). It extends the knowledge gradient (KG) to select a set of q points jointly by maximizing the expected one-step reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean. The formulation is decision-theoretically motivated: with one batch remaining, q-KG is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the posterior mean minimum over the (continuous) domain, without restricting the final recommendation to evaluated points. The authors show that in the noiseless case, and if the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG in continuous spaces is intractable in closed form, they (i) reparameterize the posterior update using a standard normal Zq and (ii) derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch. To handle continuous domains inside the objective, they discretize the inner minimizations by drawing M candidates from samples of the posterior minimum (as used in PES/PPES) augmented with previously sampled points and the proposed batch. Empirically, q-KG outperforms parallel EI (closed-form and heuristic), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic benchmarks and in hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), with especially strong gains under observation noise.

The work builds on and extends: KG for sequential BO (Frazier et al., Scott et al.), batch EI and its optimization via closed form or gradients (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.; Wang et al.), batch UCB (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.), and batch entropy-based methods (Hernandez-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani). The closest prior KG variant for batches (Wang et al., nested batch KG) treats discrete domains; here the domain is continuous, the gradient exists, and the computation is more scalable.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The acquisition is well-defined and principled. The reparameterization µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z(1:q)) Zq is standard and makes Monte Carlo estimation straightforward. The IPA/pathwise derivative argument for unbiased stochastic gradients of q-KG is appropriate under differentiability and uniqueness-of-argmin conditions (invoking Danskin’s theorem-type arguments). The statement that q-KG is Bayes-optimal for one remaining batch mirrors classical KG results and is plausible; a more explicit proof sketch would be welcome.
- A potential notational/derivational issue: In Sec. 5.2, the gradient expression includes ∂µ(n)(x*)/∂zij terms. Since µ(n) does not depend on z, these terms should vanish; the derivative of the inner minima should flow through the argmin (via Danskin) and the σ̃ term, not through µ(n) directly. Clarifying this would avoid confusion.
- Computation: Differentiating through the Cholesky factor of the q×q covariance is nontrivial but feasible (there are established formulas and autodiff support); more details on numerical stability and cost would be useful. Overall complexity per gradient step seems O(|An| q d + q^3), but an explicit complexity analysis and wall-clock overheads relative to baselines are missing.
- Use of discretization: The algorithm optimizes a discretized surrogate q-KG(z, An). This introduces bias relative to the continuous objective. The chosen An construction (posterior-minimum samples plus previously sampled and candidate points) is reasonable and mirrors ES/PPES practice, but the paper does not analyze sensitivity to M or this choice. An ablation on M and an alternative discretization (e.g., Sobol/space-filling) would strengthen the case.
- Experiments: The empirical study is solid: strong baselines, many repetitions on standard test functions, and two realistic hyperparameter tuning tasks. Gains under noise are consistent with KG’s noise-awareness. It would be helpful to report acquisition optimization time and total wall-clock (optimizer overhead can matter when q is small or f is modestly expensive), and to show performance vs batch size q.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with clear motivation, algorithm description, and experiments. A few clarity issues:
- Some notation is overloaded (A as the true domain and the inner domain in Eq. 4.1). The authors highlight this but the exposition could be streamlined.
- Minor typos/formatting (e.g., “Main Stange”, subsection numbering “.1”, incomplete references).
- More explicit discussion of the one-step optimality claim and the gradient derivation details would help expert readers reproduce results.

Originality
The paper makes a novel and timely contribution by extending KG to the batch/continuous/noisy regime and by providing a practical and efficient stochastic gradient estimator tailored to this setting. While q-EI and batch ES variants treat the joint batch selection, q-KG’s decision-theoretic objective and noise-aware formulation are distinct. The closest KG prior for batches works over discrete sets; this work’s continuous-domain, gradient-based approach is new.

Significance
Batch BO is of high practical interest. A method that is (i) principled, (ii) handles noise well, and (iii) is computationally feasible, is valuable. The empirical gains—particularly in noisy settings—are meaningful for ML hyperparameter tuning. Open-source code increases impact. The lack of theoretical convergence/regret guarantees is common in BO but still a missed opportunity.

Pros
- Principled acquisition with clear decision-theoretic meaning; reduces to q-EI in the appropriate limit.
- Efficient stochastic gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise; realistic ML tuning tasks included.
- Open-source implementation and use of established GP toolchains.

Cons
- No complexity/runtime analysis; missing wall-clock comparisons and scaling with q and M.
- Limited analysis of discretization bias and sensitivity to M/An construction.
- Minor derivational clarity issues (gradient expression) and some presentation typos.
- No theoretical convergence or regret guarantees.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Please clarify the gradient derivation in Sec. 5.2 (do ∂µ(n)/∂z terms vanish by construction? Provide a precise Danskin-based statement).
- Report acquisition optimization/runtime overheads vs. baselines, and complexity per iteration as functions of q, |An|, and d.
- Provide sensitivity to M and to the An construction; how robust is performance to these choices?
- Show results across different batch sizes q and discuss scalability.
- Briefly outline conditions under which q-KG for continuous A is consistent as M→∞ and iterations grow.

Recommendation: Accept (strong)
This is a well-motivated, technically solid, and empirically convincing contribution to batch BO. The method is novel, practical, and shows clear advantages in noisy settings. Addressing the minor clarity and evaluation suggestions would further strengthen the paper.
