- Proposes q-KG, a decision-theoretic batch BO acquisition that generalizes Knowledge Gradient to select a batch maximizing expected reduction in the GP posterior mean minimum.
- Implements Monte Carlo estimation and an unbiased stochastic gradient via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize z(1:q) with a Cholesky-based reparameterization.
- Uses discretization of the domain A (samples from posterior minimizers plus evaluated/candidate points) to compute q-KG for continuous domains.
- Empirically matches or outperforms strong batch BO baselines on synthetic 2–6D functions and ML hyperparameter tuning (MNIST, CIFAR-10), with notable gains under noisy evaluations.
- Extends prior KG work from sequential to batch; differs from nested batch KG and complements multipoint EI and information-theoretic batch methods like PPES.
- Targets simple regret (final recommendation) rather than cumulative regret, unlike UCB-based batch extensions.
- Follows differentiable parallel acquisition optimization literature but contributes novel IPA-derived stochastic gradients for q-KG.
- Decision-theoretic derivation is sound and reduction to q-EI in the noiseless sampled-recommendation case is well-argued.
- IPA-based gradient through a min over a finite set is plausible but requires explicit regularity conditions and a precise theorem; nondifferentiabilities at argmin changes need measure-zero justification.
- Reparameterization and Monte Carlo approach are standard and effective for gradient estimation.
- Computational complexity: Cholesky costs O(q^3), traversal over discretization M costs O(Mq^2 + Md); variance and runtime considerations need discussion.
- Discretization scheme is reasonable but sensitivity to discretization size M and to dimension is not reported.
- Experiments are broad and convincing for low/moderate dimensions and noise; however, missing wall-clock/runtime comparisons to lighter baselines is a gap.
- Lacks comparison to PPES/q-PES (or PES sequential), a notable omission among information-theoretic baselines.
- ML tuning experiments conflate tuning and evaluation by optimizing test error; a separate validation set is preferable.
- Figures report variance across runs but should include confidence intervals and explicitly state GP hyperparameter learning protocols (MLE details).
- Clarity is generally good; recommend renaming discretized evaluation set earlier (e.g., A_eval) to avoid confusion.
- Some typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., “Main Stange”, equation numbering, undefined symbols like D(n)) need fixing.
- Originality: substantive contribution in extending KG to parallel setting with practical differentiable estimator; discretization strategy draws from ES/PES but combination is novel.
- Significance: impactful for batch BO in low- to moderate-dimensions; code release aids adoption but scalability (>10D) and asynchronous extensions are needed.
- Pros: principled Bayes-optimal one-step batch motivation, unbiased stochastic gradients, strong empirical results under noise, open-source implementation.
- Cons: reliance on discretization without sensitivity/theoretical analysis, missing PPES comparisons, no runtime analysis, limited theoretical guarantees beyond one-step optimality, some IPA technical details deferred.
- Questions for authors: provide a theorem/assumptions for IPA gradient unbiasedness through the min; sensitivity to M and sampling scheme; wall-clock times and engineering tricks; experiments for q>4 and >10D; reasons for omitting PPES or comparisons.
- Minor suggestions: use separate validation sets for ML tuning, include ablations for noise magnitude/q/M, discuss asynchronous BO and constrained extensions.
- Recommendation: leaning accept, contingent on addressing missing comparisons and clarifying theoretical justification for the stochastic gradient.
