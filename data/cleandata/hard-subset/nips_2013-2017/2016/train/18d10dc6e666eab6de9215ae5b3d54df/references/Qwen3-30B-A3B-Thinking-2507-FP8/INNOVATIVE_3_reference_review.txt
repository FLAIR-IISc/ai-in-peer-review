

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces a novel acquisition function, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), for batch Bayesian optimization. The authors extend the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting, where multiple evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The key insight is to choose a batch of points that jointly maximizes the expected information gain in the solution quality, rather than using iterative greedy approaches that optimize one point at a time while holding others fixed. The authors develop an efficient computational method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients of the q-KG acquisition function, enabling practical optimization.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) a decision-theoretic foundation for batch Bayesian optimization that generalizes the sequential KG method; (2) an efficient computational technique using IPA to overcome the computational challenges of optimizing q-KG; and (3) thorough experimental validation showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

**Originality:** This work significantly extends the knowledge gradient framework to the parallel setting, which is a notable gap in the literature. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from existing methods, which typically use iterative greedy batch construction (e.g., parallel EI, GP-UCB-PE). The IPA-based gradient estimation technique is particularly novel and addresses a key computational bottleneck. While the paper builds on Frazier et al.'s KG work, the extension to parallel batch optimization represents a meaningful conceptual advance.

**Significance:** Batch Bayesian optimization is increasingly important for practical applications where function evaluations can be performed in parallel (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines). The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over benchmarks across both synthetic functions and real-world ML tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The most compelling results are in noisy settings, where q-KG provides significantly larger gains than other methods, addressing a critical challenge in practical BO applications.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with clear mathematical derivations. The IPA-based computational approach is well-justified and effectively addresses the main computational challenge. The experimental design is thorough, covering multiple synthetic functions and practical ML applications. The authors provide open-source code, enhancing reproducibility. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., computational complexity for large q) and compares against relevant baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear, and the experimental results are effectively presented with appropriate figures and tables. The algorithm description is precise and implementable.

**Strengths:**
- Novel acquisition function that fundamentally differs from existing parallel BO methods
- Strong theoretical foundation with decision-theoretic justification
- Elegant computational solution using IPA
- Consistent empirical improvements across diverse benchmarks
- Practical relevance to real-world hyperparameter tuning
- Clear writing and organization

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't fully explore theoretical guarantees beyond the one-batch optimization
- Limited comparison with very recent parallel BO methods (e.g., parallel predictive entropy search)
- Some computational complexity analysis could be more detailed for large q

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in batch Bayesian optimization. The authors successfully bridge a critical gap between sequential knowledge gradient methods and practical parallel optimization scenarios. While the method is not without computational costs, the IPA-based approach provides an elegant and efficient solution that enables practical implementation.

The paper excels in novelty and significance, which aligns strongly with the reviewer's priorities. The experimental results are compelling, particularly the consistent advantages in noisy settings where batch optimization is most valuable. The method is clearly differentiated from previous work, and the computational technique is a noteworthy contribution in its own right.

The minor weaknesses noted above don't detract from the paper's substantial contribution. The authors have made a clear, well-executed, and impactful advance in Bayesian optimization that will be of interest to the NIPS community. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a bold new approach that significantly advances the state of the art.

Given the emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, and the paper's clear demonstration of a significant conceptual advance with strong empirical validation, I recommend acceptance. This work provides a valuable new direction for batch Bayesian optimization research with clear practical implications.
