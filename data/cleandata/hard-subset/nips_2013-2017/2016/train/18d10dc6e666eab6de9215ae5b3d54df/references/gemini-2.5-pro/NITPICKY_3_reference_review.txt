This paper introduces a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, selecting a batch of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality after observing their outcomes, assuming it is the last batch to be evaluated. This generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient to the parallel setting. A key contribution is an efficient method for maximizing this new acquisition function; the authors derive an unbiased gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling the use of gradient-based optimizers to jointly find the optimal batch of points. The proposed q-KG method is evaluated on a suite of synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, demonstrating competitive or superior performance against several existing parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy environments.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled Derivation:** The primary strength of this work is its well-founded, decision-theoretic motivation. Unlike many parallel BO methods that are heuristic extensions of sequential acquisition functions (e.g., greedy selection using EI or "constant liar" strategies), q-KG is derived as a Bayes-optimal policy for a single remaining batch. This provides a strong theoretical underpinning for the method.
2.  **Joint Batch Optimization:** The use of an IPA-based gradient estimator allows for the joint optimization of all `q` candidate points simultaneously. This is a significant advantage over methods that construct the batch greedily, as it can capture synergistic effects between points in the batch.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling. The method is shown to be competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art baselines like parallel EI and parallel UCB variants across several benchmarks. Its particularly strong performance in the noisy setting is a notable and valuable result, as many real-world optimization problems involve noisy evaluations.
4.  **Contribution to Methodology:** The development and application of an efficient gradient-based optimization scheme for a complex, Monte Carlo-based acquisition function is a solid technical contribution that could be useful beyond this specific application.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the strong core contribution, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor flaws and a lack of precision in key details, which collectively detract from its quality and clarity.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   There are several typographical and formatting errors. For instance, "Main Stange" instead of "Main Stage" in Algorithm 1. The section numbering in Section 5 (".1", ".2") is unconventional. The citation format in the references is inconsistent (e.g., compare [6], [16], and [24]). The conference information at the bottom of the first page is inappropriate for a submission.
    *   **Crucially, the figures (Figure 3 and Figure 4) referenced in the experimental section are not included in the paper.** This is a major omission that prevents a full assessment of the empirical claims. The review must rely solely on the authors' textual descriptions of the results.
    *   The explanation for swapping the expectation and derivative in Equation (5.3) is relegated to the supplementary material. While common, a brief justification in the main text (e.g., appealing to the dominated convergence theorem) would improve the paper's self-containedness and rigor.

2.  **Methodological Details and Reproducibility:**
    *   **Domain Discretization:** The method's performance hinges on the discretization of the domain `A` into `An` to compute the q-KG factor. The strategy—sampling `M` points from the posterior optima—is a critical heuristic. The paper sets `M=1000` without any justification or sensitivity analysis. How does the choice of `M` affect performance and computational cost? This approximation could be a significant source of error. Furthermore, since `An` depends on the candidate points `z(1:q)`, the optimization problem becomes somewhat circular, and it is not entirely clear how this is handled in the implementation.
    *   **Inner-loop Optimization:** The paper states that q-KG is maximized using "multi-start stochastic gradient ascent". This description is too vague. Key details for reproducibility are missing: How many random starts? What optimizer was used (e.g., Adam, SGD with momentum)? What learning rate schedule was employed? The success of the entire method depends on reliably solving this non-convex optimization problem, making these details essential.
    *   **Hyperparameter Fitting:** Algorithm 1 states that GP hyperparameters are re-trained via MLE at each iteration. This can be computationally prohibitive, especially for the experiments on tuning a CNN. The paper provides no discussion of the computational cost of the overall q-KG algorithm compared to the baselines, which is a key practical consideration.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The set of baseline algorithms is reasonable but not exhaustive. The "constant liar" heuristic is mentioned in the related work but not included in the experiments. More importantly, batch Thompson Sampling approaches are a notable omission from the comparison.
    *   In the noisy setting, the observation noise `σ^2` is estimated via MLE. The reliability of this estimate, especially with few data points, is not discussed. Given that the method's superior performance in the noisy setting is a key claim, the robustness of this component warrants further investigation.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Pro:** Principled, decision-theoretic derivation for batch BO.
*   **Pro:** Enables joint, gradient-based optimization of the entire batch.
*   **Pro:** Strong empirical results, especially in the important noisy setting.
*   **Con:** The paper lacks the level of polish expected for a top-tier publication, with numerous formatting and typographical errors.
*   **Con:** The experimental results cannot be verified as the figures are missing from the manuscript.
*   **Con:** Critical implementation details regarding the domain discretization (`M`), the gradient-based optimizer, and computational cost are underspecified, harming clarity and reproducibility.
*   **Con:** The set of experimental baselines could be more comprehensive.

**Recommendation:**

While the core idea of this paper is novel and significant, the execution and presentation are flawed. The missing figures represent a critical failure in the submission, making it impossible to properly evaluate the central claims of the paper. This, combined with the lack of precision on key methodological details and numerous minor presentation issues, prevents me from recommending acceptance. The work has the potential to be a strong contribution, but the manuscript requires a major revision to address these shortcomings. I recommend **rejection**.
