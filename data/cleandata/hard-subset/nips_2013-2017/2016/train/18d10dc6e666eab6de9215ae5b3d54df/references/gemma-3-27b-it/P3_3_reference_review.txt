## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) method, the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), designed to efficiently leverage parallel function evaluations. The core idea is to maximize the expected incremental value of a batch of samples, extending the single-point Knowledge Gradient approach to the parallel setting. The authors address the computational challenge of maximizing this acquisition function by employing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate its gradient.  The method is empirically evaluated on synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, demonstrating superior performance compared to several existing parallel BO algorithms, especially in noisy evaluation scenarios.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on the established field of Bayesian Optimization, specifically addressing the growing need for parallelization. It correctly positions itself within the landscape of existing parallel BO methods like parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) approaches. The authors clearly distinguish their method from iterative greedy batch construction strategies (like parallel UCB) and collective batch optimization approaches (like parallel EI with gradient-based optimization).  Importantly, the connection to the original Knowledge Gradient method is well-explained, highlighting the generalization to the parallel case and the added complexity of efficient computation. The comparison to the nested batch knowledge gradient policy [26] is also insightful, and rightly points out the computational differences due to the smooth gradient used in the current work.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is well-motivated and provides a clear justification for the proposed acquisition function.  The goal of optimizing for the Bayes-optimal solution given a final batch is a sensible and compelling objective.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The use of IPA to efficiently compute the gradient of q-KG is a significant contribution.  This addresses a major challenge of maximizing batch acquisition functions and allows the method to scale to larger batch sizes and higher-dimensional problems.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superiority of q-KG over competing methods, both in noise-free and noisy settings. The performance gains are particularly pronounced when function evaluations are noisy, a realistic scenario in many applications.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. The algorithm is clearly outlined, and the notation is consistent.
* **Code Availability:** Providing publicly available code is a crucial factor for reproducibility and adoption and significantly strengthens the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization of A:** The approach to discretizing the continuous domain A, while practical, relies on sampling from the posterior’s global optima. This could potentially miss important regions of the search space, especially in early iterations or with poorly informed priors. The sensitivity of the results to the number of samples (M=1000) in this discretization is not thoroughly investigated. 
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t extensively analyze the sensitivity of q-KG to its own hyperparameters, such as the parameters controlling the IPA step size or the discretization strategy.  A more detailed discussion of these parameters and their impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Comparison Depth:** While the comparison to other methods is solid, further investigation into *why* q-KG outperforms them would be valuable. For example, does it explore more effectively, exploit better, or strike a more optimal balance?  A visualization of the exploration patterns of q-KG compared to parallel EI would be insightful.
* **Computational Cost:** While IPA improves efficiency, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the computational cost of q-KG compared to simpler methods, particularly as the batch size 'q' increases.  Providing some complexity analysis would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization.
* The computational efficiency achieved through IPA is a significant contribution.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear performance advantage over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings.
* The code availability promotes reproducibility and wider adoption.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The discretization strategy for continuous domains could be a potential limitation.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters is not thoroughly investigated.
*  A deeper analysis of *why* q-KG outperforms competing methods is lacking.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While the discretization approach and hyperparameter sensitivity warrant further investigation, the benefits of q-KG, particularly in noisy settings, outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Rating:**  **Accept (Strongly)**

**Recommendations:**

* **Address Discretization:**  Discuss the potential limitations of the discretization strategy and perhaps explore alternative discretization methods.
* **Hyperparameter Analysis:**  Include a section discussing the sensitivity of q-KG to its hyperparameters.
* **Deeper Comparison:**  Provide a more in-depth analysis of why q-KG outperforms competing methods, perhaps through visualizations of exploration patterns.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:** Briefly discuss the computational complexity of q-KG as a function of batch size and dimensionality.
