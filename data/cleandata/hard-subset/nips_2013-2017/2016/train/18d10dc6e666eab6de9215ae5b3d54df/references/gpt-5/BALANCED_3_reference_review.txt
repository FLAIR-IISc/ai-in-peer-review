Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to selecting q points jointly. q-KG measures the expected reduction in the posterior GP predictor’s minimum if one additional batch of q evaluations were obtained, and it is Bayes-optimal in the one-batch-remaining setting. For computation, the paper uses Monte Carlo to estimate q-KG and develops an efficient stochastic gradient estimator based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch. To handle continuous domains, the acquisition evaluation uses a dynamic discretization built from samples drawn from approximate posterior minimizers, augmented with previously evaluated points and the candidate batch.

Empirically, q-KG is compared against multi-point EI (both heuristic and exact implementations), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, on synthetic benchmarks (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and two ML hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR10), in both noise-free and noisy settings. q-KG typically outperforms baselines, with especially clear gains under noise.

This work sits within the BO literature using GPs with acquisition functions such as EI, UCB, entropy search (ES/PES/PPES), and KG. It extends sequential KG [Frazier et al.; Scott et al.] to the batch setting. For batch BO, it contrasts with: q-EI and its differentiable formulations [Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.], PPES [Shah & Ghahramani], GP-BUCB [Desautels et al.], and heuristic constructions such as constant liar and fantasizing. The use of IPA for q-KG gradients and the decision-theoretic interpretation distinguish it from these.

Strengths
- Principled objective: q-KG directly optimizes expected improvement in final decision quality (min of posterior mean) over a batch, with a clear decision-theoretic justification and correct reduction to q-EI in the noise-free, “recommend from sampled points” setting.
- Noise robustness: KG naturally models information value under noise; the reported empirical gains in noisy scenarios are plausible and meaningful for practical tuning tasks.
- Computational contribution: IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradients for q-KG enable joint optimization over the batch, which is nontrivial given the min over A and the dependence on pending outcomes.
- Practicality: Open-source implementation; comparisons on standard synthetic functions and realistic ML tuning with batch q=4; re-fitting hyperparameters by MLE as commonly done.
- Clarity of core idea: The construction of q-KG, its Bayes-optimal interpretation, and the gradient estimator are explained concisely.

Weaknesses and points to clarify
Quality
- Approximation layers: The Bayes-optimality claim holds only in the exact, finite-A setting. In practice, there are three approximations (dynamic discretization of A, Monte Carlo estimation, frequent re-fitting of GP hyperparameters). The paper should discuss error sources and their impact on the objective (e.g., sensitivity to M and number of MC samples).
- Computational complexity and wall-clock: There is no thorough runtime/scaling analysis vs. q, dimension d, discretization size M, and number of MC samples. Given that exact q-EI now has efficient implementations and UCB-based methods are fast, wall-clock efficiency is important. Reporting runtime and sample efficiency vs. baselines would strengthen the case.
- Limited ablations: No study of how performance varies with q (>4), with higher dimensions (>6), or with discretization strategies. Also missing variance-reduction details (e.g., common random numbers across iterations) that are often important for stochastic gradients.
- Statistical reporting: For CNN tuning, only 5 runs are reported; confidence is limited. It would be helpful to include seed-wise variance comparisons and significance tests.
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond the unbiasedness of the gradient estimator, there are no convergence guarantees for the acquisition optimization under stochastic gradients, nor regret/consistency results for the overall BO procedure (even in simplified settings).

Clarity
- The discretization “samples from global optima of the posterior” deserves a more explicit description, as in PES/PPES; the current text assumes familiarity and may leave readers unsure how AM_n is constructed in practice.
- Some derivative expressions (e.g., derivatives through Cholesky) are only sketched; a pointer to precise formulas or supplement pages would help reproducibility.
- Experimental details could be more complete: number of MC samples for q-KG and its gradient, step sizes and stopping criteria for gradient ascent, parallelization details, and how constraints (if any) are handled.

Originality
- Extending KG to batch with a workable, differentiable estimator over continuous domains is a meaningful contribution distinct from both q-EI and ES/PPES. While nested batch KG for discrete spaces exists, the IPA-based approach here appears novel in the continuous, joint batch selection setting.

Significance
- If computational overhead is acceptable, a method that is robust to noise and consistently outperforms strong baselines would see adoption in hyperparameter tuning. Demonstration with moderate q and low-to-moderate d is useful. However, the lack of runtime/scaling data and comparisons on larger-q/d setups limit claims about broad practicality.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a detailed complexity and runtime study vs. q, d, M, and MC samples; compare wall-clock to MOE’s q-EI, PPES, and GP-BUCB. Include GPU/CPU resources and parallelization.
- Add ablations: impact of M and discretization strategy; number of MC samples; batch size q∈{2,4,8}; higher-dimensional benchmarks (e.g., d≈10–20).
- Include additional baselines: local penalization batch BO (González et al., 2016), batch Thompson sampling variants, and fantasized EI variants beyond constant liar if available.
- Expand on the gradient estimator: variance-reduction techniques (common random numbers), autodiff vs. manual differentiation through Cholesky, and convergence behavior of the stochastic optimization (e.g., learning rate schedules).
- Clarify the construction of AM_n and how it relates to PES/PPES sampling of minima; discuss when including x(1:n) and z(1:q) is necessary/sufficient.
- Strengthen empirical reporting: more runs on CNN, standard errors/confidence intervals, and statistical tests where appropriate.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear, principled acquisition with a strong decision-theoretic basis.
- Novel and practical stochastic gradient estimator enabling joint batch selection.
- Solid empirical performance, especially under noise, on synthetic and real tasks.
- Open-source implementation.

Cons:
- Insufficient analysis of computational cost and scalability; no wall-clock comparisons.
- Limited experimental breadth (q mostly 4; d up to 6).
- Approximation effects (discretization, MC) not quantified; limited theoretical guarantees.
- Missing some relevant baselines (e.g., local penalization, Thompson sampling).

Recommendation
Weak accept. The methodological contribution is sound and original, the decision-theoretic perspective is valuable, and the empirical results are promising, particularly in noisy settings. Addressing runtime/scalability, adding broader baselines, and clarifying discretization/gradient details would significantly strengthen the paper.
