Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes q-KG, an acquisition function for batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization that generalizes the (sequential) knowledge gradient (KG) to q simultaneous evaluations. The key idea is to select a batch z(1:q) that maximizes the expected decrease in the posterior mean minimum, i.e., minx µ(n)(x) − E[minx µ(n+q)(x) | y(z(1:q))], making q-KG Bayes-optimal in the one-step lookahead sense when only one batch remains. Evaluating and optimizing q-KG exactly is intractable; the authors propose a Monte Carlo estimator of q-KG and an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling stochastic gradient ascent over the batch. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner “solution-return” set A by sampling M candidates from posterior mode distributions (as in entropy-search style discretization), augmented with previously sampled and candidate points. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning (MNIST logistic regression and CIFAR-10 CNN) show that q-KG outperforms or matches parallel EI (both exact and heuristic variants), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with especially strong gains in noisy settings.

This work builds on: (i) classical BO with GP models and sequential acquisition functions (EI, PI, UCB, ES, KG); (ii) batch BO via q-EI (closed-form or gradient-based optimization; Chevalier & Ginsbourger 2013; Wang et al. 2016; Marmin et al. 2015), GP-BUCB and GP-UCB-PE (Desautels et al. 2014; Contal et al. 2013), and PPES (Shah & Ghahramani 2015). It extends prior KG work (Frazier et al. 2009; Scott et al. 2011) from sequential to batch, and from discrete to continuous domains with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: A principled, Bayes-optimal (one-step) batch generalization of KG that values batches jointly rather than greedily.
- Technical contribution: An IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG that avoids nested Monte Carlo or finite-difference approximations and appears scalable in q relative to naive approaches.
- Practical relevance: Strong empirical gains, particularly under observation noise where EI-like criteria are known to struggle; code release is a plus.
- Clear connection to existing theory: Shows reduction to q-EI in the noiseless case with the solution restricted to sampled points, clarifying the relation between KG and EI.

Weaknesses:
Quality/technical soundness:
- Differentiability and exchange of expectation/gradient rely on regularity and on handling non-differentiability in the min/argmin; the paper states conditions and defers proofs to the supplement. It would help to more explicitly discuss subgradient issues and the measure-zero argument for switches in the argmin.
- The discretization of A used to compute q-KG (and its gradient) can bias the acquisition; performance depends on M and the sampling scheme for AMn. There is no ablation showing sensitivity to M, discretization strategy, or how often AMn is refreshed.
- The Bayes-optimal justification assumes fixed GP hyperparameters and noise; in practice hyperparameters (including noise variance) are re-fit via MLE each iteration, which breaks formal optimality. A discussion or empirical check of robustness to hyperparameter uncertainty would strengthen the claims.
- No theoretical guarantees (e.g., asymptotic convergence or regret bounds) are provided, unlike UCB-style methods; even a discussion of known obstacles and possible directions would be useful.

Clarity:
- Generally clear, but Section 5’s dual roles of A (domain vs. discretized set for computing q-KG) may confuse readers; the paper notes this but could more cleanly separate notation and give a brief complexity discussion.
- Some implementation details crucial to reproducibility are light: number of Monte Carlo samples for estimating q-KG and its gradient; variance reduction (e.g., common random numbers, control variates); optimizer settings; wall-clock cost and scalability in q, M, and d.

Originality:
- Extending KG to batch with a continuous domain and an efficient gradient estimator appears novel relative to prior KG or q-EI/PPES work. The relation to nested batch KG on discrete sets is clearly stated.

Significance:
- Batch BO is practically important; improved robustness to noise is valuable for ML hyperparameter tuning and other stochastic objectives.
- Empirical evaluations are promising but limited in scale (dimensions up to 6 for synthetic, 8 hyperparameters for CNN) and number of runs on CNN (n=5). Missing comparisons to some contemporaneous batch BO baselines (e.g., batch Thompson sampling, local penalization) leave open questions about relative performance and speed.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a complexity and scalability analysis in terms of q, M, n, and d, including the costs of Cholesky updates, Monte Carlo samples, and gradient evaluations. Include wall-clock comparisons to q-EI and PPES on shared hardware.
- Add ablations on M, the construction of AMn, and the number of Monte Carlo samples, including gradient variance and convergence behavior of the stochastic optimizer; consider simple variance reduction techniques.
- Evaluate sensitivity to GP hyperparameter estimation (e.g., fully Bayesian marginalization vs MLE) and noise mis-specification.
- Compare against additional batch baselines (e.g., batch Thompson sampling, local penalization) and report batch size sweeps (q).
- Clarify and, if possible, formalize the IPA justification (subgradients, almost-sure differentiability). If proofs are in the supplement, summarize key lemmas in the main text.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled acquisition design with clear conceptual advance (batch KG).
- Technically interesting and potentially broadly useful stochastic gradient estimator for batch acquisitions.
- Strong empirical performance, particularly in noisy settings; code availability.

Cons for acceptance:
- Lack of theoretical convergence/regret guarantees.
- Dependence on discretization without sensitivity analysis.
- Limited experimental breadth (missing some relevant baselines, limited ablations, modest dimensionality and number of runs in real tasks).

Decision: Weak Accept
This is a solid and timely contribution to batch BO with a principled acquisition, a nontrivial gradient estimator, and compelling empirical gains in noisy regimes. Addressing the noted clarity and evaluation gaps would further strengthen the paper.
