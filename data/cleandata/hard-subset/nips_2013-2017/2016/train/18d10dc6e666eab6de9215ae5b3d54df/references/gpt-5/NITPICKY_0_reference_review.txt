Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes q-KG, a batch/parallel extension of the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization with Gaussian processes. The idea is to choose a batch z(1:q) by maximizing the expected decrement in the minimum posterior mean achieved after assimilating the q outcomes, i.e., minx µ(n)(x) − E[minx µ(n+q)(x) | y(z(1:q))]. The authors derive a Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG and an IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator to optimize it efficiently. They argue that, in the noise-free case and if the final recommendation is restricted to previously sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. For continuous domains, they approximate the inner minimizations by discretizing A using samples from the posterior distribution of the global optimum (citing PES/PPES). Experiments on synthetic test functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks compare q-KG to parallel EI, two UCB-based batch policies, and show stronger gains especially under observation noise.

The work relates to KG (Frazier et al.), multi-point EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Wang et al./MOE), GP-BUCB and GP-UCB-PE, and PES/PPES for batch acquisition via entropy. The contribution is the first practical gradient-based optimization of a genuinely batch KG objective over continuous domains.

Strengths
- Conceptually solid decision-theoretic objective that is Bayes-optimal in a one-batch setting (for fixed hyperparameters and exact inner minimization).
- Clear Monte Carlo construction using µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z(1:q))Zq with a standard normal vector, enabling stochastic gradient optimization.
- IPA-based gradient estimator should be effective in practice; using C++ implementation is a plus.
- Empirical results suggest notable gains in noisy settings, where EI/UCB can struggle.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological
- Discretization/gradient mismatch: Sections 5.1–5.2 derive gradients assuming A is finite and independent of z. In Section 5.3, A is discretized as An = AMn ∪ x(1:n) ∪ z(1:q), so the set over which the inner min is taken depends on z. The presented gradient derivation does not address this dependence. This can bias the IPA gradient estimator and affects convergence guarantees of the optimizer. Please either fix An to be independent of z when taking gradients (common in q-EI implementations) or provide a correct derivative accounting for the envelope theorem with parameter-dependent feasible sets (and discuss non-smoothness when the argmin switches).
- Discretization scheme is underspecified. “Drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior distribution” is ambiguous. PES/PPES approximate p(x*) using EP/importance sampling; please specify the exact sampler used, its parameters, and how it scales with dimension. Without this, reproducibility is limited and the accuracy of the q-KG estimator is unclear.
- Complexity analysis is missing. There is no discussion of computational cost per acquisition evaluation/gradient step as a function of n, q, M, and number of Monte Carlo samples S. The gradient requires derivatives through the Cholesky factor; practical complexity and numerical stability should be discussed. Runtime comparisons to q-EI/PPES are important for batch BO.
- Bayes-optimality claim is qualified. The statement “By construction, … Bayes-optimal … if only one decision is remaining” holds only for fixed hyperparameters and exact evaluation of (4.1). In Algorithm 1, hyperparameters (including noise) are re-fit by MLE at every iteration; this breaks the Bayes-optimality claim. Please soften/qualify this.
- Reduction to parallel EI needs more careful justification. The algebra assumes the final recommendation is restricted to observed points; however, even in the noise-free case µ(n+q)(x) changes for all x, so the step to (·)+ requires explicit restriction to the discrete set of observed points. Please make assumptions explicit and correct the derivation.
- Noise model for classification experiments: treating test error on a small test set as Gaussian with constant variance is a mis-specification (Bernoulli/binomial noise, heteroscedastic). Please discuss the impact, or use an appropriate likelihood or variance model.

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous typographical/formatting issues that reduce clarity:
  - Section headers inconsistent (e.g., “Parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG)” missing section number; subsections labeled “.1”, “.2”).
  - “Main Stange” → “Main Stage”. “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)” appears mid-text.
  - Equation text: “minx µ(n+q)(x) is itself a random vector” should be “random variable”.
  - Notation: clarify that σ̃n(x, z(1:q)) is a 1×q vector; define D(n) as the Cholesky factor with orientation (upper/lower); be consistent about transposes/inverses.
  - Algorithm 1: define N (number of batches), q explicitly; clarify stopping budget; use consistent pseudocode formatting.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (missing years/venues/pages; malformed URLs; “E.C. etal”; “D. Bingham, , ,”; “http://yelp, … github.io/MOE/”). Please fix to standard NeurIPS style.
- Experimental details are insufficient to reproduce:
  - No reporting of optimization budgets (N, total evaluations), number of Monte Carlo samples, gradient step sizes, number of restarts, or M’s effect.
  - No wall-clock times for acquisition optimization.
  - CNN tuning setup lacks training details (optimizer, schedule, data augmentation, early stopping). The claimed 14%→11% improvement vs a “tutorial” baseline is hard to interpret.

Evaluation/positioning
- Missing comparisons to PPES (cited) and to modern q-EI optimizers beyond MOE, as well as no ablation on the benefit of IPA vs zeroth-order optimization.
- Only q=4 is tested; sensitivity to q and to increasing dimension is not explored.

Quality: The core idea is sound and potentially impactful, but there are important methodological gaps (discretization-dependent gradient), insufficient algorithmic details/complexity analysis, and under-specified experiments.

Clarity: Currently below NeurIPS standards due to numerous typos/formatting issues and missing details.

Originality: Extending KG to batch with a practical gradient estimator over continuous domains is novel relative to existing batch EI/ES work.

Significance: Potentially significant for noisy batch BO; however, claims would be stronger with rigorous treatment of the gradient/discretization, broader baselines, and runtime/ablation studies.

Pros
- Principled acquisition function with clear one-batch optimality interpretation.
- Practical stochastic gradient estimator.
- Empirical gains in noisy settings.

Cons
- Gradient derivation does not account for z-dependent discretization.
- Underspecified discretization/sampling of x*; no complexity/runtime analysis.
- Many presentation and reference issues; reproducibility gaps.
- Limited baselines/sensitivity analyses.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The contribution is promising, but the paper, as written, has too many methodological and presentation issues. Addressing the discretization/gradient mismatch, adding full algorithmic and runtime details, fixing the references/typos, and expanding experiments (q sensitivity, PPES comparison, runtime and ablations) would make this a strong submission.
