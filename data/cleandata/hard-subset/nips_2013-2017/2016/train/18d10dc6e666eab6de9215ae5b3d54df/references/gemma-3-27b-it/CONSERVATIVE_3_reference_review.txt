This paper proposes a new batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), aiming to improve upon existing parallel BO techniques. The core idea is to maximize the expected incremental value of a batch of evaluations, leveraging a decision-theoretic analysis. While the motivation is sound – seeking a more principled approach to batch selection than iterative greedy methods – I find the presentation and justification lacking in rigor, and the empirical validation, while extensive, doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a significant and robust advantage over simpler, well-established methods.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to extend the sequential Knowledge Gradient to the parallel setting. The theoretical derivation, while briefly summarized, points toward a more holistic batch selection criterion. However, the practical implementation relies heavily on approximations (discretization of the search space, Monte Carlo sampling) which are acknowledged but not thoroughly analyzed for their impact on the overall performance and theoretical guarantees. The claim of deriving q-KG “using a decision-theoretic analysis” feels somewhat overstated; it's essentially an extension of the KG principle, and the parallelization introduces significant computational challenges addressed through pragmatic, rather than theoretically grounded, approximations.

My primary concern is the lack of a strong baseline comparison. The authors compare against several parallel BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, etc.), but these are often implemented from different sources (Metrics Optimization Engine, Gpoptimization, Spearmint), making a fair comparison difficult. More importantly, they don't compare against a robust and well-tuned sequential BO method with a modest increase in sequential evaluations to achieve the same batch size. This is crucial. If q-KG requires significant computational overhead to approximate the gradient, the gains need to be substantial enough to justify that cost, *especially* compared to simply running a sequential BO method with the same total number of function evaluations. The figures show improvement, but the error bars are often large, and the gains aren’t consistently dramatic across all experiments. Furthermore, the claim of "especially large value when function evaluations are noisy" needs more convincing evidence – the synthetic noisy experiments show improvement, but it’s not overwhelming.

The computational complexity discussion is also somewhat hand-wavy. While the authors acknowledge the difficulty of maximizing q-KG and present IPA for gradient estimation, the scalability of this approach isn't thoroughly investigated, particularly for high-dimensional problems or large batch sizes. The discretization strategy (using samples from the GP posterior) is reasonable but could be a bottleneck, and its sensitivity to the number of samples (M=1000) isn't explored.

Clarity is decent, but the notation is dense, and the mathematical derivations could benefit from more detailed explanations. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is concise but lacks sufficient detail regarding parameter tuning and stopping criteria. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the practical considerations for implementing q-KG.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Attempts a principled approach to parallel BO, moving beyond greedy strategies.
*   Demonstrates improvement over several existing parallel BO methods on a range of synthetic and real-world problems.
*   Provides a computationally efficient method (IPA) for gradient estimation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks a strong baseline comparison with well-tuned sequential BO methods.
*   Empirical gains are not consistently substantial or statistically significant.
*   The theoretical justification and analysis of the approximations are limited.
*   Scalability concerns regarding the gradient estimation and discretization strategy.
*   The complexity of the method does not appear to be sufficiently justified by the demonstrated performance gains.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the idea is interesting, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant advancement over existing techniques. The empirical results are encouraging but not definitive, and the lack of a strong baseline comparison weakens the claims of superiority. The methodological details and analysis of the approximations need further refinement. The authors should consider revisiting the paper with a more thorough comparative analysis, a more rigorous theoretical foundation, and a clearer justification for the computational complexity of the proposed method. It currently feels like a promising direction, but not a fully realized contribution.
