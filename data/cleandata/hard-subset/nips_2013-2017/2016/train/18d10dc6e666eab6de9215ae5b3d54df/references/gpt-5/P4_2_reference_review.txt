Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to selecting q points simultaneously. The core idea is to select a batch z(1:q) that maximizes the expected one-step improvement in the minimum of the GP posterior mean, i.e., minx∈A μ(n)(x) − E[minx∈A μ(n+q)(x)|y(z(1:q))]. This is a decision-theoretic criterion that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the posterior-mean minimum when only one batch remains. The authors show that in the noise-free case, and when the final recommendation is restricted to previously sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel expected improvement (q-EI).

Computationally, evaluating q-KG and its gradient requires Monte Carlo (MC) over a reparameterization of the posterior mean update using a standard normal Zq and the Cholesky factor of the batch covariance. The paper derives an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling stochastic gradient ascent to optimize the batch jointly. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization set A by combining previously sampled locations, the candidate batch, and M samples of posterior function minimizers (in the spirit of entropy search/Thompson sampling approaches [11, 18]).

Empirically, q-KG is compared against parallel EI (both MOE’s exact q-EI and Spearmint’s heuristic), GP-BUCB [5], and GP-UCB-PE [3] on synthetic benchmarks (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and on hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), in both noise-free and noisy settings. q-KG generally matches or outperforms competitors, with a larger gap under noise.

This work builds on KG for sequential BO [7, 17], extends batch BO methods such as q-EI [2, 15, 25], and relates to batch entropy search [18] and UCB-based batch policies [3, 5]. The use of IPA for optimizing a collective batch acquisition parallels gradient-based optimization of q-EI [15, 25], but here for a decision-theoretic KG objective.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic grounding: q-KG directly targets the expected posterior-mean improvement with one batch remaining, a principled batch generalization of KG.
- Sound reparameterization and gradient estimation: the Zq-based expression and IPA provide a plausible, unbiased stochastic gradient estimator under stated smoothness assumptions, enabling efficient batch optimization compared to naïve MC search.
- Empirical performance: consistent gains over q-EI and UCB-based baselines, especially in noisy settings where KG-type criteria are expected to be advantageous.
- Practical implementation: integration with existing MOE components, code availability, and application to realistic hyperparameter tuning tasks.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on discretization: The inner minimization over A is approximated via a dynamic discrete set An. While standard in ES-style methods [11, 18], this introduces approximation bias. The choice “draw M samples from the global optima of the posterior” is not fully specified; more detail is needed on how these candidates are generated and how sensitive results are to M.
- Limited theoretical guarantees: Beyond an unbiased gradient estimator (under conditions relegated to the supplement), there is no convergence analysis for the batch policy (e.g., consistency as evaluations grow, or guarantees as M and MC samples increase). No regret or simple-regret rates are provided.
- Differentiation through argmins: The IPA argument implicitly differentiates through argmin operations that are piecewise constant. The sufficient conditions ensuring unbiasedness (e.g., uniqueness of minimizers almost surely, measure-zero tie sets) should be clearly stated and discussed in the main text.
- Scalability and wall-clock cost: The paper does not report computational time or scaling with q, |An|, and MC samples. Since each evaluation requires O(q^3) for Cholesky and O(|An| q) operations per MC draw, a wall-clock comparison versus q-EI and GP-BUCB would clarify practical trade-offs. Empirics use q=4; larger q would strengthen claims.
- Experimental details for fairness: Performance of UCB-based methods depends on βt schedules and implementation choices. The paper should specify these settings and confirm comparable hyperparameter tuning across methods. The CNN study uses only 5 runs; more repetitions would improve statistical confidence.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The acquisition definition is correct, the reduction to q-EI in the stated special case is reasonable, and the reparameterization is standard. The IPA-based gradient is plausible; however, key regularity conditions and a concise proof sketch would improve confidence.
- Empirical validation is broad and aligns with the claimed advantages under noise. Missing are ablations on M, MC sample size, q, and wall-clock time.

Clarity
- Well organized, with clear notation for x(1:n), z(1:q), and the GP posterior updates. A few minor typos and notational inconsistencies exist (e.g., “Main Stange”; “gaussian”). The description of the discretization procedure should be more explicit (how are “global optima of the posterior” sampled; is this Thompson sampling from GP draws?).
- The Bayes-optimality claim should clearly state the objective (posterior-mean minimum) and the single-batch horizon assumption in the main text.

Originality
- Extending KG to batch selection with an efficient gradient estimator is a meaningful and novel contribution relative to prior sequential KG [7, 17] and batch EI/ES/UCB methods [2, 3, 5, 15, 18, 25]. Related nested batch KG [26] focuses on discrete domains without gradient-based optimization; this work advances continuous-domain batch KG with differentiable computation.

Significance
- Practically relevant for parallel experiment settings, with observed advantages under noise. If computational costs are competitive, q-KG could be adopted in hyperparameter tuning workflows. The impact would be higher with demonstrated scalability to larger q, higher-dimensional spaces, and asynchronous settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide concrete details and ablations for the discretization An construction (method, M sensitivity, effect of including z(1:q) and x(1:n)).
- Report wall-clock times and scaling with q and |An|, and compare to q-EI and BUCB/PE baselines.
- Summarize the conditions under which the IPA estimator is unbiased and stable, including handling of non-unique minimizers.
- Explore larger batch sizes and include asynchronous or heterogeneous-evaluation-time variants.
- Clarify UCB baseline settings (βt) and ensure fair hyperparameter tuning across methods.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled batch generalization of KG with a workable stochastic gradient estimator.
- Strong empirical performance, particularly in noisy settings where many practitioners struggle.
- Bridges a gap between decision-theoretic BO and practical batch optimization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of theoretical guarantees beyond gradient unbiasedness; no convergence/regret analyses.
- Reliance on discretization without a thorough study of its impact; limited guidance on choosing M.
- Missing computational cost/scalability evidence; experiments limited to relatively small q and modest run counts for some tasks.
