Summary
The paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization (BO) that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to the setting where q points are evaluated in parallel. The acquisition is derived from a decision-theoretic one-step lookahead: it maximizes the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after adding a batch. In the noiseless case and when the final recommendation is restricted to previously sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Because exact evaluation is intractable for q > 1, the authors estimate q-KG via Monte Carlo over a Gaussian draw Zq and optimize it using a stochastic gradient computed by infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). For continuous domains, they approximate the inner minimization over A via a dynamically updated discretization based on samples from likely posterior minimizers (as in ES/PPES-style support sets). Experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks show that q-KG is competitive or superior to parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with especially strong gains under observation noise. The implementation is released in C++.

Relation to prior work
- Extends classic KG for sequential BO (Frazier et al., Scott et al.) to the batch setting with noisy evaluations and continuous domains.
- Contrasts with batch EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.) and PPES (Shah & Ghahramani), which jointly value sets but with different objectives and optimizers.
- Improves on nested-batch KG for discrete domains (Wang et al.) by providing differentiable objectives and scalable gradient estimators for continuous spaces.
- Compared with UCB-based batch methods (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), q-KG directly optimizes for simple regret in a one-step Bayes-optimal sense.

Quality and technical soundness
Strengths:
- The decision-theoretic derivation is principled: q-KG is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the posterior mean minimum with one batch remaining.
- The IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator is a key technical contribution. Exchanging differentiation and expectation is justified under smoothness and measure-zero tie assumptions; the structure of the gradient (including derivatives through the Cholesky factor) is carefully laid out.
- The reduction to parallel EI in the noiseless/restricted-recommendation setting is correct and provides intuition and a sanity check.

Weaknesses / questions:
- Discretization of A: The paper adopts a dynamic support set via “samples from the global optima of the posterior,” referencing ES/PPES, but this is under-specified. How are these samples generated in practice? Thompson-like sampling of minimizers? The impact of M and the sampling method on bias/variance and performance should be quantified (ablation on M, sensitivity to support-set quality).
- Computational complexity and scalability: q-KG requires (i) repeatedly solving an inner min over |An| points per MC draw and (ii) Cholesky of the q×q covariance per candidate batch; then optimizing a qd-dimensional nonconvex objective with stochastic gradients. The paper lacks runtime comparisons, scaling studies in q and d, or gradient variance diagnostics. Without wall-clock plots, it is hard to judge practical efficiency versus q-EI or PPES.
- Differentiability at argmin switches: The gradient relies on IPA; while ties have probability zero under continuous noise, the discrete approximate inner min can be nondifferentiable with respect to z(1:q). A discussion and empirical evidence (e.g., stability with common random numbers, smoothing) would strengthen the argument.
- Hyperparameter uncertainty: The method re-fits GP hyperparameters by MLE between batches and treats them as fixed within acquisition computation. This is standard but undermines the strict Bayes-optimality claim. A brief discussion or empirical comparison to marginalizing hyperparameters (e.g., via MCMC or ensembles) would be valuable.
- Baselines: It would be important to include the “fast multipoint EI” (Chevalier & Ginsbourger) and/or gradient-based q-EI (Marmin et al.) as strong batch-EI baselines (the MOE/Spearmint implementations do not necessarily reflect the state-of-the-art). PPES is a natural comparator for joint set valuation but appears absent in experiments.

Empirical evaluation
- The benchmarks (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann; q=4) and ML tuning tasks are appropriate. The noisy setting results are compelling and consistent with KG’s strength under noise.
- However, experiments are relatively small-scale (d ≤ 8, q=4). Including higher q (e.g., 8, 16), higher-d problems (e.g., d ≥ 10–20), and wall-clock comparisons would better establish practical advantages.
- Reproducibility is aided by released code. Reporting MC sample counts, number of gradient steps/restarts, learning-rate schedules, and the computational overhead relative to EI/UCB would improve reproducibility.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The derivation and estimator are explained at a high level; some details are deferred to the supplement.
- The discretization procedure should be described more concretely. Also, replace explicit inverses with triangular solves for numerical stability; clarify how derivatives of the Cholesky are computed efficiently.

Originality
- The batch extension of KG with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator for continuous domains appears novel and nontrivial relative to prior KG and batch-EI/PPES work circa 2016.

Significance
- Addresses an important use case (parallel evaluation, noisy observations). The method advances the toolbox of batch BO with a principled one-step Bayes-optimal criterion and shows gains in realistic scenarios. If computational concerns are addressed, it is likely to be adopted.

Pros
- Principled decision-theoretic acquisition; reduces to known acquisition in a special case.
- Joint batch selection (not greedy), with an efficient stochastic gradient.
- Strong empirical results under noise; released code.

Cons
- Missing strong baselines (fast q-EI, PPES) and runtime analyses.
- Under-specified discretization; no ablation on M or MC budget.
- Limited scaling evidence in q and d; no discussion of gradient variance/stability.

Questions for authors
1. How are “samples from the global optima of the posterior” generated exactly? Can you provide an ablation on M and the sampling method?
2. What are typical MC sample sizes and optimizer settings? How sensitive is performance to these?
3. Can you include wall-clock time comparisons and results for larger q and d?
4. How does q-KG compare empirically to fast q-EI and PPES?

Overall recommendation: Accept (weak). The paper is technically solid, novel, and addresses a relevant problem with promising empirical gains, especially under noise. Strengthening baselines and computational analyses would make it even more convincing.

Reviewer confidence: High.
