### Summary

This paper introduces a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The goal is to select a batch of `q` points to evaluate in parallel to find the minimum of an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function. The proposed q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective: it quantifies the expected value of information gained from a batch of `q` evaluations, measured by the expected improvement in the final recommended solution (i.e., the minimum of the posterior GP mean). This provides a principled generalization of the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy, which is known to be one-step Bayes-optimal.

A key challenge is that jointly optimizing the `q` points to maximize this acquisition function is computationally intensive. The paper's main technical contribution is a method to make this optimization tractable. The authors propose a Monte Carlo estimator for the q-KG factor and, critically, derive an unbiased stochastic gradient of this estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). This enables the use of efficient gradient-based methods to find the optimal batch of points. The method is evaluated on several synthetic benchmark functions and two real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), in both noise-free and noisy settings. The results demonstrate that q-KG consistently performs as well or better than existing state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, with a particularly strong advantage in noisy environments.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is a principled and well-motivated extension of existing theory. The use of IPA to derive a stochastic gradient is a clever and non-trivial technical contribution that successfully addresses the primary computational bottleneck of the proposed method. The experimental methodology is rigorous, involving multiple random initializations and comparisons against a strong and relevant set of baselines from recent literature (including different parallel EI and UCB variants). The claims of superior performance, especially in the noisy setting, are well-supported by the empirical results.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the motivation for their work, articulate the definition of the q-KG factor, and outline the computational strategy for its optimization. The relationship to and distinction from prior work in both sequential KG and parallel BO is clearly established. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the overall procedure. The authors are also honest about the approximations made, such as the discretization of the domain for calculating the posterior minimum.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While sequential KG and other parallel BO methods exist, this paper is the first to successfully generalize the decision-theoretic KG framework to the batch setting with a computationally feasible optimization strategy for continuous domains. The core technical idea of applying IPA to derive an efficient gradient estimator for the parallel KG acquisition function is novel and significant. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, citing relevant work on parallel EI, UCB, and ES methods, and clearly highlighting its unique contributions.

**Significance:** The contributions of this paper are significant and likely to have a strong impact. Batch BO is of great practical importance for accelerating hyperparameter optimization and other expensive optimization tasks. The proposed q-KG method provides a new state-of-the-art algorithm that is not only highly effective but also grounded in strong decision-theoretic principles. Its superior performance in noisy settings is particularly valuable, as many real-world evaluation functions (e.g., model validation accuracy on a subsample of data) are inherently noisy. The provision of open-source code further increases the likelihood that this method will be adopted by both researchers and practitioners.

### Arguments for Acceptance (Pros)

1.  **Principled Method:** The q-KG acquisition function is derived from a clear, decision-theoretic objective—maximizing the expected improvement in the final solution quality—making it a very well-motivated approach for batch BO.
2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The development of an efficient stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG via IPA is a significant technical achievement that makes the principled approach computationally viable.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method demonstrates state-of-the-art or superior performance against a comprehensive set of strong baselines across multiple synthetic and real-world problems.
4.  **Excellent Performance in Noisy Settings:** The method shows a particularly large performance gain in noisy environments, a common and challenging scenario in practice (e.g., tuning models on data subsets). This aligns well with the theoretical motivation of KG, which decouples the final recommendation from the set of evaluated points.
5.  **High Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the authors have made their code available, facilitating reproducibility and adoption.

### Arguments for Rejection (Cons)

1.  **Lack of Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper claims the method is "computationally efficient" but provides no analysis of its wall-clock time. The optimization of the acquisition function involves Monte Carlo estimation and stochastic gradient ascent, which is likely more computationally expensive than some of the baselines. A discussion of the trade-off between optimization time and function evaluation cost is missing but crucial for practitioners.
2.  **Details on Discretization are Sparse:** The quality of the q-KG approximation depends heavily on the discretization scheme (controlled by parameter M). The paper sets M=1000 but provides no justification or sensitivity analysis, leaving the reader to wonder about the robustness of the method to this choice.

---

### Suggestions for Improvement

Overall, this is a very strong paper. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contributions. The following suggestions could further strengthen the manuscript:

*   **Computational Cost:** It would be highly beneficial to include a comparison of the wall-clock time required to select one batch of points for q-KG versus the baseline methods. This would give practitioners a clearer picture of the computational trade-offs involved and in what scenarios (i.e., how expensive `f(x)` needs to be) q-KG is most practical. This could be added to the appendix if space is a concern.
*   **Sensitivity to Discretization (M):** A brief discussion or an experiment in the appendix showing the method's sensitivity to the number of discretization points `M` would enhance the paper's completeness and provide practical guidance for users.
*   **Clarification on Hyperparameter Updates:** Algorithm 1 mentions re-training GP hyperparameters. It would be helpful to clarify precisely when this occurs (e.g., after each batch is evaluated) and how it is integrated into the optimization loop, as this can be a subtle but important implementation detail.
*   **Notation in Eq (4.1):** The notation `En[...|y(z(1:q))]` is slightly ambiguous. The expectation is over the distribution of the future observations `y(z(1:q))`. A clearer notation might be `E_{y(z) ∼ P(·|z(1:q), D_n)} [ ... ]` where `D_n` represents the data from the first `n` evaluations. This is a minor point, as the text explains the concept clearly.
