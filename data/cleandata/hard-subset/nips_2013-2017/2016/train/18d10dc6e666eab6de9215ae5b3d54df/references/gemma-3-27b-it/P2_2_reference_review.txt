## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) method called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea is to extend the sequential Knowledge Gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting, aiming to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected information gain.  A key contribution is the development of an efficient gradient-based optimization method (using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis - IPA) to tackle the computational complexity of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function.  The authors demonstrate empirically that q-KG consistently outperforms existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings, on both synthetic benchmarks and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The q-KG method presents a principled approach to parallel BO, extending the well-established Knowledge Gradient concept.  Unlike many existing parallel BO methods that rely on greedy iterative selection, q-KG considers the batch as a whole, aiming for a more globally optimal solution.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is a strong point. The paper clearly motivates the method based on maximizing expected information gain.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors recognize the computational challenges of optimizing q-KG and provide a significant contribution by developing an efficient IPA-based gradient estimation technique. This allows for practical application of the method, particularly in higher dimensions or with larger batch sizes.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments are thorough, covering both synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems (logistic regression and CNNs).  The consistent outperformance of q-KG compared to strong baselines (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) is compelling. The demonstration of improved performance in noisy settings is particularly valuable.
* **Code Availability:**  The authors provide code, which promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization of the Search Space:** The method relies on discretizing the search space 'A' for gradient computation, which, while addressed in Section 5.3, introduces an approximation and potential sensitivity to the discretization scheme (number of samples M). The impact of varying M isn't fully explored in the experiments.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Like many BO methods, q-KG likely has its own hyperparameters that need tuning. The paper doesn’t explicitly discuss the sensitivity of q-KG's performance to its own hyperparameters or provide guidance on how to choose appropriate values.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** Although the authors describe the IPA-based gradient estimation, implementing q-KG still appears to be considerably more complex than some of the baseline methods.  This could be a barrier to adoption.
* **Limited Comparison to Nested Batch KG:** While the paper acknowledges [26], the comparison is brief and highlights differences in computation rather than a detailed performance comparison. A more direct comparison could strengthen the argument for the authors’ approach.



**Relevance to NIPS & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper is highly relevant to NIPS. Bayesian optimization and its extensions are active research areas within the machine learning community, and parallel BO is crucial for scaling BO to complex problems. The work builds upon established BO principles (Gaussian Processes, Knowledge Gradient) while introducing a novel approach to batch optimization.  The paper acknowledges and correctly positions itself within the existing literature, contrasting q-KG with greedy methods (parallel EI, UCB) and other collective-batch approaches. The reference list is comprehensive.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical derivation is clear, and the experimental results provide strong support for the claims. The use of IPA is a clever and effective solution to the computational challenge.  The paper is a complete piece of work and demonstrates careful evaluation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanation of the q-KG acquisition function and the IPA-based gradient estimation could benefit from more illustrative examples, but overall, the ideas are presented clearly.
* **Originality:** The method is original in its extension of the Knowledge Gradient to the parallel setting and its development of an efficient gradient-based optimization approach.
* **Significance:**  The results are significant. q-KG offers a promising improvement over existing parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy settings. The potential for wider adoption in complex optimization problems, such as hyperparameter tuning of deep learning models, is substantial.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and a novel approach to parallel BO.
* Compelling empirical results demonstrating superior performance over existing methods.
* Efficient gradient-based optimization technique enabling practical application.
* Code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on discretization of the search space, which introduces an approximation.
* Implementation complexity may hinder adoption.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a **strong paper** that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The authors have presented a technically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated method that addresses a significant challenge in parallel BO.  The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance** to NIPS.  Minor revisions to address the points regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and the impact of discretization would further strengthen the paper.
