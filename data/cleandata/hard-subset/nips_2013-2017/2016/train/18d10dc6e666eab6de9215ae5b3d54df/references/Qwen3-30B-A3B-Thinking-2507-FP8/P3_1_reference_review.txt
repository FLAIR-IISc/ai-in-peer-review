

# Peer Review: "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, extending the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting where q points can be evaluated simultaneously. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that jointly optimizes a batch of points rather than sequentially adding points as in previous parallel BO methods. The key contribution is a computationally efficient method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function, enabling practical optimization. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments that q-KG outperforms existing parallel BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, GP-UCB-PE) on both synthetic functions and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly excelling in noisy settings.

The paper builds upon the foundational work of knowledge gradient (KG) [7, 17] and extends it to the parallel setting, addressing a gap in the literature where most parallel BO methods construct batches iteratively rather than jointly optimizing them. The work is highly relevant to the NeurIPS community, as batch Bayesian optimization is increasingly important for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning where parallel evaluations are feasible.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting with a decision-theoretic foundation. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is sound and well-motivated, providing a principled alternative to the iterative approaches used in previous work.

2. **Computational Innovation**: The use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function is a notable computational contribution. This addresses a major practical barrier to implementing parallel KG, as the authors correctly note that naive maximization would be prohibitively expensive.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The experimental section is thorough and well-designed. The authors test on a variety of synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and real-world machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), with appropriate statistical analysis (mean and standard deviation across multiple runs). The demonstration of superior performance in noisy settings is particularly valuable.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The paper directly addresses a significant practical challenge in machine learning: leveraging parallel computing resources for hyperparameter tuning. The demonstrated improvements in hyperparameter tuning for real machine learning tasks (e.g., reducing test error from 14% to 11% on CIFAR10) provide concrete evidence of the method's value.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and a logical flow from problem statement to method to experiments. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is concise and easy to follow.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Formal Optimality Proof**: While the authors claim that q-KG is "Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the predictor of the GP if only one decision is remaining," they provide no formal proof of this property for the parallel setting. The sequential KG is proven to be Bayes-optimal in [7], but the extension to the parallel case requires careful justification that is missing here.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper discusses the computational challenges of maximizing q-KG but lacks a thorough complexity analysis of the IPA-based approach. It would be valuable to understand how the computational cost scales with q (batch size) and d (dimensionality), which is crucial for understanding the method's practical applicability.

3. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The authors cite [25] as related work, but their experimental comparison does not include the parallel EI method that uses gradient-based optimization (as described in [2] and [15]). This omission is significant because these methods also directly optimize parallel EI and could provide a more direct comparison to the authors' approach.

4. **Discretization Strategy Justification**: The discretization strategy in Section 5.3 (using samples from the global optima of the posterior) is not well-justified. It's unclear why this particular strategy is superior to other discretization approaches (e.g., grid-based or random sampling), and the paper doesn't discuss potential limitations of this approach for different problem types.

5. **No Discussion of Dimensionality**: The paper does not address how the method scales with the dimensionality of the problem (d). This is a critical aspect of Bayesian optimization that is often challenging for GP-based methods. The experiments are limited to low-dimensional problems (d=2,3,5,6), but the method would need to be evaluated on higher-dimensional problems to demonstrate its practical utility for real-world machine learning applications.

6. **Lack of Theoretical Explanation for Noisy Setting Performance**: The paper claims that q-KG "provides especially large value when function evaluations are noisy," but doesn't provide a theoretical explanation for this phenomenon. A brief discussion of why the decision-theoretic approach might be more robust to noise than other methods would strengthen the paper.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add a formal proof** of the Bayes-optimality claim for the parallel setting, or at least clarify the conditions under which this holds.

2. **Include a computational complexity analysis** comparing the proposed IPA-based method with naive maximization and other existing parallel BO methods.

3. **Expand the experimental comparison** to include gradient-based parallel EI methods [2, 15] and consider adding a comparison with the parallel ES method [18].

4. **Justify the discretization strategy** in Section 5.3 with either theoretical arguments or additional experimental results comparing different discretization approaches.

5. **Add experiments on higher-dimensional problems** to demonstrate the method's scalability, at least for d=10-20, which would be more representative of modern machine learning applications.

6. **Provide a brief theoretical discussion** of why the decision-theoretic approach might be more robust to noise than other acquisition functions.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization by providing a principled method for batch optimization that outperforms existing approaches, particularly in noisy settings. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the computational approach using IPA is innovative and well-executed. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates clear practical benefits.

The main weaknesses are the lack of formal optimality proof for the parallel case, the missing computational complexity analysis, and the limited comparison with recent gradient-based parallel BO methods. These are important but fixable issues that would strengthen the paper significantly.

## Recommendation

**Accept** (Strong Accept)

This paper makes a substantial contribution to Bayesian optimization, with clear theoretical insights and strong empirical results. The method is novel, well-motivated, and addresses an important practical problem. The weaknesses identified are primarily in the presentation and completeness of the evaluation rather than in the core contribution. With the suggested revisions (particularly the formal optimality proof and expanded comparison), this paper would be an excellent contribution to NeurIPS.

The paper is well-suited to NeurIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning (hyperparameter optimization) with a methodologically sound approach that has direct practical applications. The significance of the work is high, as batch Bayesian optimization is increasingly important for leveraging parallel computing resources in machine learning.
