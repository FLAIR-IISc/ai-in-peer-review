Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. It defines the value of a batch as the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after observing that batch, i.e., the one-step Bayes-optimal policy for minimizing the posterior mean (“immediate simple regret”). The authors show that in the noiseless case, and when the final recommendation is restricted to previously evaluated points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Because q-KG has no closed form for q > 1, the paper develops a Monte Carlo estimator and an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch. For continuous domains, the paper discretizes the inner minimization (over A) using sampled candidate sets akin to techniques used in entropy search and parallel PES. Experiments on standard synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR-10) show that q-KG is competitive or superior to parallel EI (two variants), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with especially strong gains under observation noise.

This work relates closely to: KG for sequential BO [Frazier et al.; Scott et al.], parallel EI and its optimizers/gradients [Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.; Wang et al.], parallel UCB variants [Desautels et al.; Contal et al.], and information-theoretic approaches [PES, PPES]. It fills a gap by providing a principled joint batch selection rule targeting simple regret in the noisy setting, with a practical gradient-based optimizer.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a principled extension of KG to the batch setting with a clean decision-theoretic objective (expected reduction in min posterior mean).
- Practical contribution: an unbiased IPA-based stochastic gradient to optimize a joint q-point acquisition, avoiding the greedy approximations common in batch BO.
- Empirical performance: strong and consistent improvements over competitive baselines, particularly under noise—an important use case for BO in ML tuning.
- Sensible engineering: uses reparameterization via standard normals and Cholesky factors; leverages existing tools (MOE) and releases code.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to one-step optimality (by construction) and an unbiased gradient estimator. There are no consistency/regret results or convergence guarantees for the stochastic optimization of the acquisition (step-size schedules, variance reduction, convergence to stationary points).
- The interchange of gradient and expectation/min requires care because of the non-differentiability of the min and dependence on z(1:q). The paper states assumptions and defers proofs to the supplement; please clarify the conditions ensuring almost sure uniqueness of the argmin and that ties occur with probability zero. Also clarify how the dependence of the discrete set An on z(1:q) (An = AMn ∪ x(1:n) ∪ z(1:q)) is handled in the gradient; does the estimator treat An as fixed during differentiation? If so, discuss the bias introduced by ignoring the z-dependence of the set.
- Hyperparameter learning is plug-in MLE per batch. Since KG is sensitive to posterior uncertainty, marginalizing over hyperparameters (as in Snoek et al. 2012) could materially change results, especially in early iterations/noisy regimes. Please discuss this choice and its impact; an ablation or sensitivity analysis would be valuable.
- Computational complexity and runtime are not reported. For each acquisition evaluation, complexity scales roughly with O(T(q^3 + |An|q^2)) for T Monte Carlo samples, plus computing µ(n+q)(x) over |An|. Report T, |An| (M=1000 plus existing points), number of gradient steps and restarts, and wall-clock against baselines, at least on the synthetic problems where acquisition overhead can dominate.
- The discretization strategy for An is described as sampling from “global optima of the posterior” referencing PES/PPES. Please clarify the procedure (e.g., Thompson sampling of posterior function draws and taking their minimizers, or sampling high-probability minima via ES-style representer points). The quality of this discretization drives both accuracy and cost; an ablation over M would help.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but several notational and editorial issues: section numbering glitches, minor typos, and some references incomplete (e.g., URLs, missing details). The description of D(n)(·) and its derivatives through Cholesky deserves more operational detail (e.g., using established Cholesky differentiation routines) for reproducibility.
- Algorithm 1 mixes the two roles of A (the feasible domain of z and the set used for inner minimization). The text notes the distinction; it would help to keep different symbols (e.g., A for domain, S for discretization set) to avoid confusion.

Originality
- Extending KG to the parallel case with a tractable and unbiased gradient estimator is novel and nontrivial. While batch acquisition gradients have been explored for q-EI and information-based methods, applying IPA to q-KG with a full joint batch optimizer is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
- Batch BO with noise arises frequently in ML hyperparameter tuning. A method that directly targets simple regret and handles noise well is practically important. The empirical advantages, especially under noise, suggest q-KG will be useful. Providing code increases potential impact.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Provide details on the Monte Carlo budget, optimization hyperparameters, number of restarts, and wall-clock runtime vs. baselines.
- Clarify the discretization procedure and analyze sensitivity to M and to the choice of sampling method for AMn.
- Discuss and, if possible, evaluate marginalization over GP hyperparameters.
- Consider variance reduction for the stochastic gradient (e.g., common random numbers across z, antithetics), and report its effect.
- Analyze behavior as q grows (scaling of optimization difficulty and variance of gradient).
- Expand to higher-dimensional problems or discuss strategies (e.g., input warping, additive kernels) for d > 10.
- Minor edits: clean up references; fix section numbering; define all symbols upon first use; clarify derivative of Cholesky.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled batch acquisition targeting simple regret, with a practical and novel optimization scheme.
- Strong empirical gains, especially in noisy settings.
- Clear connection to and improvement over widely used baselines; released code.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited theoretical analysis beyond one-step optimality and unbiasedness; no runtime analysis.
- Dependence on discretization with limited guidance on its quality and impact.
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps (optimization details, hyperparameter treatment).

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a solid, principled contribution to batch BO with convincing empirical benefits, especially under noise. Addressing the clarity and experimental detail issues would strengthen it further.
