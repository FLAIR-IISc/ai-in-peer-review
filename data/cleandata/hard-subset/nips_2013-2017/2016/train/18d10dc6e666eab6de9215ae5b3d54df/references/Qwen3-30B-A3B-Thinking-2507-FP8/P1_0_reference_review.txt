

# Peer Review: "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization (BO) method called "Parallel Knowledge Gradient" (q-KG) that extends the knowledge gradient acquisition function from sequential to parallel settings. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function for selecting a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously, which is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor when only one batch remains. To address the computational challenges of optimizing this acquisition function, the authors develop an efficient method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for gradient estimation. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments on synthetic functions and practical machine learning problems that q-KG consistently outperforms existing batch BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper builds on the knowledge gradient (KG) method for sequential BO [7, 17], which has been previously applied to single-point selection. The authors properly situate their work within the context of existing parallel BO approaches, including parallel EI [2, 10, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and GP-UCB-PE [3]. They provide a clear distinction between their collective batch selection approach and the iterative greedy approaches of previous methods.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a theoretically well-motivated acquisition function with a solid decision-theoretic foundation. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is clear and properly justified. The computational approach using IPA for gradient estimation is technically sound and addresses a critical challenge in batch BO.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments across multiple synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). They evaluate both noise-free and noisy settings, which is crucial for real-world applications. The comparison against multiple relevant baselines (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) is comprehensive.

3. **Practical Significance**: The paper demonstrates clear practical value by showing that q-KG achieves meaningful improvements in hyperparameter tuning for machine learning algorithms. The results on CNN tuning (improving test error from ~14% to ~11%) are particularly compelling.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is straightforward. The experimental results are presented with appropriate visualizations and statistical analysis (mean and standard deviation across multiple runs).

5. **Reproducibility**: The authors provide code availability (https://github.com/wujian16/qKG), which significantly enhances the reproducibility of their work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees**: While the paper provides a decision-theoretic justification for q-KG, it lacks theoretical performance guarantees (e.g., regret bounds). For a NIPS paper, some theoretical analysis of the convergence properties or regret bounds would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of q-KG compared to other batch BO methods. It's unclear how the IPA-based gradient estimation scales with q (batch size) and d (dimensionality), which is critical for practical applications.

3. **Scalability to High Dimensions**: The experiments focus on relatively low-dimensional problems (d ≤ 6 for synthetic functions, d = 4-8 for practical problems). The paper doesn't address how well q-KG scales to higher-dimensional problems (d > 10), which is common in modern machine learning applications.

4. **Comparison with More Recent Work**: The related work section references [18] as a parallel ES method, but doesn't mention more recent parallel BO methods that have emerged since 2016 (e.g., [27, 28]). This weakens the claim of being "state-of-the-art" compared to the current literature.

5. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of q-KG. For example, it doesn't address how the method performs when the batch size q is very large (e.g., q > 10) or when the problem has strong constraints.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The experimental results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate the advantages of q-KG, particularly in noisy settings. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths and weaknesses by comparing against multiple baselines and including both noise-free and noisy experiments. The paper represents a complete piece of work rather than a position paper. However, the lack of theoretical guarantees and limited scalability discussion are notable weaknesses.

## Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithm description is straightforward. The experimental setup and results are presented with sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results. Some minor improvements could be made to the explanation of IPA in Section 5.2, but this is not a major issue.

## Originality

The paper makes a clear contribution by extending the knowledge gradient approach to batch selection. While there are previous works on parallel BO (e.g., [2, 5, 10, 18, 25]), the authors' focus on the knowledge gradient for collective batch selection is novel. The use of IPA for efficient gradient estimation of q-KG is also novel. The paper adequately differentiates its approach from related work in Section 2.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important practical problem in machine learning: batch hyperparameter optimization. The results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings, which are common in real-world applications. The practical impact is reinforced by the successful application to logistic regression and CNN tuning. The code availability further enhances the significance and potential impact of the work.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with a well-motivated method that is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. The results are compelling, the writing is clear, and the code availability enhances reproducibility.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief theoretical discussion of the regret bounds or convergence properties of q-KG, even if limited.

2. Include a discussion of computational complexity and scalability to higher dimensions (d > 10).

3. Update the related work to include more recent parallel BO methods published after 2016.

4. Expand the discussion of limitations to include how the method scales with large batch sizes (q) and high dimensions (d).

5. Consider adding a brief comparison to more recent batch BO methods like [27, 28] in the experimental section.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
- Novel extension of knowledge gradient to batch setting with a decision-theoretic foundation
- Efficient computational approach using IPA for gradient estimation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple synthetic and real-world problems
- Significant performance gains, especially in noisy settings
- Practical relevance to machine learning hyperparameter tuning
- Code availability enhances reproducibility

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical guarantees (regret bounds)
- Limited discussion of scalability to high dimensions
- No comparison to some more recent parallel BO methods
- Limited analysis of computational complexity

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and it represents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization that would be of interest to the NIPS community.
