

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a new batch Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that extends the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors claim their method outperforms existing batch BO approaches like parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration, particularly in noisy settings. The method derives from decision-theoretic principles, choosing a batch of points that maximizes the expected incremental value of the solution quality, and employs infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute gradients for optimization.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a solid connection to existing Bayesian optimization literature. The experimental section provides comprehensive comparisons across multiple synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The method demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines, particularly in noisy settings where the gains are more pronounced. The implementation details are thorough, and the code availability is a plus. The computational approach using IPA to approximate gradients is technically sound and addresses a genuine bottleneck in batch BO.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite these strengths, I have significant concerns about the paper's novelty and theoretical grounding. The core idea is a straightforward extension of sequential KG to the batch setting, which has been previously explored in [7, 17] and more recently in [26]. The authors acknowledge this connection but don't sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing work. The "novelty" claim is overstated given that similar batch acquisition functions have been proposed in [2, 15, 18, 25].

The most concerning aspect is the lack of rigorous theoretical justification for the q-KG acquisition function. The paper claims q-KG is "Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the predictor of the GP if only one decision is remaining," but this is a well-known property of the sequential KG acquisition function, not a new theoretical contribution. The extension to the batch setting doesn't provide a clear theoretical justification for why this particular extension is better than other possible extensions.

The experimental validation is also insufficiently thorough. While the paper shows improvements on specific synthetic functions and machine learning tasks, it fails to provide:
1) Statistical significance testing for the claimed improvements
2) A comparison with more recent batch BO methods (e.g., [18] on parallel predictive entropy search)
3) Analysis of computational complexity for larger batch sizes (q=4 is used throughout, but scalability to q>10 isn't addressed)
4) A clear explanation of why the marginal gains over baselines are worth the additional computational complexity

The paper's most compelling claim is that q-KG "provides especially large value when function evaluations are noisy." However, the noise experiments use only a single noise level (σ=0.5), and the paper doesn't explore how the method might behave under varying noise levels or with different noise structures.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents a technically sound method that appears to work well in practice, I find the novelty claim overstated and the theoretical justification insufficient. The improvements over baselines are generally modest, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the method represents a significant advance over existing approaches. The method is a natural extension of established knowledge gradient principles to the batch setting, but it lacks the rigorous theoretical grounding and substantial empirical validation required for NIPS acceptance.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) A more thorough comparison with recent batch BO methods
2) Statistical significance testing of performance gains
3) A clearer theoretical justification for the batch extension
4) Analysis of computational complexity for larger batch sizes
5) Exploration of multiple noise levels to better validate the noisy setting claims

As a reviewer who values established methods and rigorous validation, I am skeptical that this work represents a substantial contribution to the field. The method is an incremental improvement rather than a significant advance, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify its novelty or significance. While the implementation is sound, the lack of compelling theoretical justification and the modest empirical gains make me question whether this work meets NIPS's high standards for novelty and significance.

I recommend rejection, but would consider a strong revision if the authors could address the theoretical gaps and provide more comprehensive experimental validation.
