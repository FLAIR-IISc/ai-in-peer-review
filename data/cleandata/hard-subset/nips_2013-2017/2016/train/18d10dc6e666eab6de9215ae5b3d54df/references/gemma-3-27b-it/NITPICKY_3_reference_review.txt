This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization, extending the sequential KG approach to the parallel setting. The core idea is to maximize the expected incremental value of a batch of evaluations, considering the entire batch collectively rather than greedily adding points. The authors address the computational challenges of maximizing q-KG by employing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate its gradient. They demonstrate empirically that q-KG outperforms existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings, on both synthetic and real-world problems.

While the paper presents a conceptually sound and potentially valuable extension to parallel Bayesian Optimization, and demonstrates strong empirical performance, the execution and presentation contain a concerning number of minor, yet persistent, flaws. These issues, while individually small, accumulate to create an impression of insufficient rigor and attention to detail. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending the Knowledge Gradient to a parallel batch setting is a meaningful contribution. The proposed q-KG acquisition function represents a genuine advancement in the field.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is well-motivated and provides a solid grounding for the method.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate that q-KG outperforms existing methods, particularly in noisy environments, which is a crucial practical scenario.
* **IPA for Gradient Estimation:** Utilizing IPA to address the computational complexity of gradient computation is a clever and effective approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Inconsistency:** Throughout the paper, the notation is slightly inconsistent. For instance, `z(1:q)` is used to denote a set of points, yet sometimes it’s treated as a single vector. A more consistent and rigorous notation would significantly improve readability.
* **Lack of Detail in Algorithm 1:** Algorithm 1, the core algorithm outline, lacks sufficient detail. What constitutes a "Latin hypercube design?" How are the hyperparameters of the GP re-trained? What specific MLE objective is used? These details are glossed over, requiring the reader to infer from other parts of the paper.
* **Discretization of A:** Section 5.3 describes discretizing the domain A, but the rationale for using M=1000 is not provided. A sensitivity analysis or justification for this choice is missing.  Furthermore, the statement "This sample set… is then extended by the locations of previously sampled points" is awkwardly worded and potentially misleading; it suggests the previously sampled points are *added* to the discrete set, not considered already within the search space.
* **Hyperparameter Settings in Experiments:** The reported experimental results lack sufficient details about the hyperparameter settings for *all* compared methods.  Specifying learning rates, kernel parameters, and other relevant settings is crucial for reproducibility. A full parameter sweep table would be ideal.
* **Presentation of Results:** While the plots are generally clear, the error bars in Figure 3 appear excessively small, potentially obscuring important variance information. Also, the legends could be improved for clarity.
* **Citation Errors/Omissions:** The citation of [16] (Gaussian Processes) is premature in Section 2 before the concept is fully introduced. Several other citations appear to be missing or inaccurate when cross-referenced. For example, the claim that "[10, 19] suggests parallelizing EI" seems inaccurate given the context of [19].
* **Minor Grammatical Errors:** Numerous minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasing occur throughout the paper. While not critical, they detract from the overall polish and readability. ("...making better (more aggressive) progress..." in Section 6.2.2 is an example.)
* **Overstatement of Significance:** The claim that q-KG improves a CNN’s test error "to around 11%" when an "expert" achieved 14% requires significant qualification. Was this consistently achieved? What was the variance? This level of detail is missing.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The core contribution (q-KG) is novel and addresses an important problem.
* The empirical results are strong and convincing.
* The use of IPA is a clever and effective solution to a computational bottleneck.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from numerous minor flaws that demonstrate a lack of meticulousness and attention to detail.
* The lack of complete details regarding algorithm implementation and experimental setup hinders reproducibility.
* The overstatement of certain results undermines the credibility of the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential significance of the proposed method, the numerous minor flaws and lack of complete details prevent me from recommending acceptance. While the empirical results are promising, I am concerned about the overall rigor and reproducibility of the work. The paper requires substantial revision to address these concerns. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to resubmit after carefully addressing the points raised in this review.  A more thorough and detailed presentation, combined with a rigorous attention to detail, could transform this paper into a strong contribution to the field.
