

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces a novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization by extending the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors propose a decision-theoretic acquisition function called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that jointly optimizes a batch of q points rather than sequentially selecting them. The key insight is that q-KG measures the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor after evaluating a batch of q points, providing a theoretically grounded alternative to existing parallel BO methods that typically construct batches iteratively.

The paper's main contribution lies in its conceptual novelty: generalizing KG from sequential to batch settings, which is a significant theoretical advance. While previous work on parallel BO (e.g., parallel EI, batch UCB) constructs batches iteratively by optimizing single-point acquisition functions, the authors' approach considers the batch collectively. The paper also develops an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients of q-KG, enabling practical optimization of the acquisition function.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across both synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The q-KG method consistently outperforms benchmarks, with particularly notable improvements in noisy settings where existing methods struggle. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across multiple problem domains, including noise-free and noisy evaluations, and make their code publicly available.

The paper excels in originality and significance. The parallel KG approach represents a genuinely new direction in Bayesian optimization, moving beyond incremental improvements to existing methods. The decision-theoretic foundation of q-KG provides a principled alternative to heuristic batch construction methods, and the empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages. The paper is well-motivated by the growing need for efficient batch optimization in machine learning hyperparameter tuning, where parallel evaluations are increasingly common.

The paper is clearly written, with well-organized sections that logically build the theoretical foundation before presenting empirical results. The mathematical derivations are sound, and the experimental methodology is thorough. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the computational complexity for large q, and provide a clear explanation of how their IPA-based approach addresses this challenge.

The main weaknesses are somewhat minor. The paper could have provided more discussion on the scalability of their approach for very large q (e.g., q=100+), though this is a common limitation in batch BO. The comparison with the recently proposed nested batch knowledge gradient policy [26] is somewhat brief, though the authors do correctly note that their approach is more efficient for continuous domains.

However, these are minor points in the context of the paper's strong novelty and significance. The work represents a substantial conceptual advance over existing batch BO methods, and the empirical results provide convincing evidence of its practical value. The paper's theoretical foundation is solid, and the computational approach is clever and well-executed.

This paper makes a compelling case for a new direction in Bayesian optimization that moves beyond iterative batch construction. The parallel knowledge gradient method is conceptually fresh, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. It addresses a practical problem (batch optimization) with a novel solution that outperforms existing methods, especially in challenging noisy settings.

The paper's significance is particularly strong given the widespread use of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning. The availability of code will facilitate adoption by the community, and the consistent performance gains across diverse problem domains demonstrate broad applicability.

In summary, this paper introduces a genuinely novel concept that advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization. While the methodological details are sound, the true value lies in the bold conceptual leap of generalizing knowledge gradient to the parallel setting. The empirical results provide strong validation of the approach, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented.

The paper's novelty and significance far outweigh the minor methodological limitations. It represents a significant contribution to the field that deserves publication at NIPS. I recommend acceptance for its strong conceptual contribution and clear demonstration of practical value.
