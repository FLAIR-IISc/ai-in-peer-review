This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), extending the knowledge gradient concept to parallel evaluation settings. The method aims to maximize information gain by jointly considering a batch of points, derived through a decision-theoretic analysis. A computationally efficient approach based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) is developed to optimize the acquisition function. The authors demonstrate improved performance on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy environments.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the q-KG method. It effectively builds upon existing work in Bayesian optimization and specifically addresses the challenges of parallelization. The connection to the knowledge gradient method is clearly explained, as is the motivation for developing a new acquisition function suitable for batch evaluation. The introduction of IPA for gradient computation is a significant contribution, making the method computationally tractable. The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating improvements over several existing parallel BO algorithms on both synthetic and real-world problems. The availability of the code further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

A key strength lies in the theoretical justification of q-KG, stemming from a decision-theoretic analysis aiming for Bayes-optimality in the final iteration. The use of IPA to enable efficient gradient-based optimization is also a significant achievement, addressing a major practical hurdle for optimizing the proposed acquisition function. The thorough empirical validation, including experiments with noisy function evaluations, adds credibility to the claims. The comparison against a range of existing algorithms (parallel EI, batch UCB, etc.) allows for a clear assessment of the method's strengths and weaknesses.

However, the paper has some areas that could be improved. While the authors mention the discretization of the domain A in Section 5.3, a more detailed discussion on the impact of the discretization level (M = 1000) on performance would be beneficial. A sensitivity analysis regarding M would add insight. Furthermore, the explanation of why q-KG outperforms other methods in noisy scenarios, while mentioned, could be elaborated on with more in-depth theoretical insights.  The connection between the KG's sensitivity to noise and the proposed formulation would be valuable. The algorithm description in Algorithm 1 could be more precise, specifically regarding the initial sampling strategy and the hyperparameter tuning process. While the code is available, a more detailed description of implementation choices might be useful for other researchers seeking to build upon this work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The q-KG method presents a novel approach to parallel Bayesian optimization by extending the knowledge gradient concept and introducing an efficient optimization technique.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is grounded in decision theory and employs a sound mathematical framework. The IPA technique is effectively used to address computational challenges.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over existing methods on both synthetic and real-world problems, particularly in noisy environments.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making the proposed method easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Discretization Details:** The impact of the discretization parameter (M) on performance is not thoroughly explored.
*   **Noisy Performance Explanation:** A more detailed theoretical explanation of the improved performance in noisy settings would be beneficial.
*   **Algorithm Specificity:** Certain aspects of the algorithm description (initial sampling, hyperparameter tuning) could be more precise.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, offering improvements over existing parallel optimization algorithms. While some minor improvements could be made regarding the discussion of discretization and the explanation of performance in noisy scenarios, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend acceptance.
