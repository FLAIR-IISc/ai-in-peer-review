Summary
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. q-KG is defined as the expected one-step reduction in the minimum posterior mean after taking a batch of q evaluations. It reduces to parallel EI in the noise-free case when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points. Because exact evaluation/optimization of q-KG is intractable for q>1, the authors use Monte Carlo to estimate the acquisition and develop an IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator to optimize it with gradient ascent. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization set using samples from the posterior’s minimum (as in ES/PES). Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks (MNIST logistic regression and a CIFAR-10 CNN) suggest q-KG outperforms parallel EI variants and batch UCB methods, particularly under observation noise. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
The method builds on classic KG (Frazier et al.) and is positioned against batch EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.), batch UCB variants (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.), and batch ES (Shah & Ghahramani). The main novelty is extending KG to the batch setting with a practical stochastic gradient optimization scheme, and showing reduction to q-EI in a special case. The paper cites relevant work, including nested batch KG on discrete sets.

Strengths
- Decision-theoretic foundation: q-KG is the Bayes-optimal one-step policy for reducing min posterior mean (when only one batch remains).
- Practical optimization: An IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator is derived, enabling joint optimization of all q points rather than greedy construction.
- Noise robustness: Empirical results show larger gains in noisy settings, where EI often degrades.
- Reproducibility: Code release and use of established GP toolchains; multiple runs reported on benchmarks.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Reliance on discretization: The continuous-domain acquisition is approximated by discretizing A to An via M=1000 samples from “global optima of the posterior,” plus previously sampled and candidate points. This is a strong approximation that can bias the value-of-information calculation and the selected batch. There is no sensitivity analysis to M, to the sampling scheme, or to the composition of An. The procedure to sample from “global optima” is only sketched, and can itself be costly/unreliable in higher dimensions.
- Limited theoretical guarantees: Beyond one-step Bayes optimality and claims of an unbiased gradient under smoothness conditions, there are no convergence or regret guarantees for repeated batch application. This is common in BO, but UCB-based baselines do have such guarantees; the paper should be explicit about limitations and assumptions (e.g., ties in the inner min and nonsmoothness).
- Methodological details missing: The IPA derivation depends on differentiating through the Cholesky factor and a min over a discrete set; while ties are of measure zero under continuous noise, the unbiasedness and regularity conditions are deferred to the supplement. Computational complexity per iteration (as a function of q, M, number of MC samples) is not analyzed; this makes it hard to assess scalability.
- Scalability untested: Experiments use q=4 and d up to 6 (synthetic) or 8 (hyperparameters). There is no study of larger q or higher-dimensional settings, despite claiming efficiency when q is large. Wall-clock comparisons and overhead versus baselines are missing.
- Baseline coverage and fairness: Comparisons omit batch ES/PPES (cited) and other competitive batch BO strategies available at the time (e.g., local penalization, batch Thompson sampling). Implementations come from different codebases (MOE, Spearmint, gpoptimization) with potentially different kernels, priors, and hyperparameter learning; while the authors state Matérn-5/2 is used, it is unclear that all baselines were matched and re-fit fairly (including noise variance).
- Noise modeling: The noisy experiments assume homoscedastic Gaussian noise estimated by MLE, but the “small test set” logistic regression setting is likely heteroscedastic and non-Gaussian. It is unclear whether q-KG’s apparent advantage stems from better handling of noise or from modeling/implementation choices.
- Reproducibility of the discretization strategy: Sampling “global optima of the posterior” (per ES/PES) is nontrivial and under-specified in the main text. Since q-KG’s value depends heavily on An, more detail (and ablations) are needed.

Quality
The core idea is sound and well-motivated, and the reduction to q-EI in a special case is reassuring. However, the paper lacks a thorough complexity analysis, careful justification of the stochastic gradient estimator in the presence of nonsmoothness (beyond a pointer to the supplement), and rigorous study of sensitivity to discretization. Experimental evidence is promising but not comprehensive; missing baselines and fairness clarifications weaken the empirical support.

Clarity
Generally clear and readable, with sensible notation and background. Some algorithmic details are deferred or vague (construction of An; differentiating the Cholesky; computational costs). Minor typos remain (e.g., “Main Stange”).

Originality
Extending KG to batch with a computable stochastic gradient is a useful contribution. The components (KG, Monte Carlo, IPA, discretization) are known, and the novelty lies in their integration for batch BO. This is an incremental but meaningful advance.

Significance
If robust and scalable, q-KG could be valuable for noisy batch BO. The reported gains are encouraging, but given the methodological approximations and evaluation gaps, the practical impact is not yet fully demonstrated.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to M and to the specific method used to construct An? Please provide ablations and wall-clock trade-offs.
- What is the computational complexity per acquisition optimization step (in q, M, d, and number of MC samples)? How does runtime compare to q-EI and PPES?
- Can you include PPES and batch Thompson sampling/local penalization baselines, and ensure all methods share the same GP prior/kernel and hyperparameter learning?
- How many MC samples are used to estimate q-KG and its gradient? Any variance-reduction techniques?
- Do you observe failures due to ties/nonsmoothness in the inner min? How is the gradient estimator stabilized in practice?
- Can the method handle heteroscedastic noise or non-Gaussian likelihoods?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear decision-theoretic formulation extending a well-known acquisition.
- Practical algorithm with an unbiased gradient estimator.
- Strong empirical gains in noisy settings; code available.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on discretization with no sensitivity analysis.
- Missing baselines and unclear fairness across implementations.
- No scalability study or complexity analysis; limited dimensionality/q.
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond one-step optimality.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the current empirical and methodological evidence falls short of the rigor needed to endorse a new acquisition function over established batch BO methods. Strengthening the evaluation (baselines, sensitivity, runtime) and clarifying theory/complexity would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
