## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel approach to batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea is to extend the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting, aiming to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected information gain. Unlike existing parallel BO methods which often rely on greedy selection, q-KG considers the batch collectively.  The authors address the computational challenges of maximizing q-KG by developing an efficient gradient estimation technique based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA).  Empirical results on both synthetic functions and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing literature on parallel BO. It acknowledges and differentiates itself from approaches like parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) methods, highlighting their greedy nature. It also distinguishes itself from recent work focusing on nested batch KG policies, emphasizing the more efficient gradient computation enabled by IPA. The authors thoroughly cite relevant literature, showcasing a solid understanding of the field.  The connection to the sequential KG method is clearly articulated, establishing a strong foundation for the proposed extension.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The q-KG method presents a conceptually sound and novel approach to parallel BO by extending the KG principle to batch selection.  Considering the batch collectively, rather than greedily, is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is a strong point, providing a clear rationale for the method.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The development of IPA-based gradient estimation is crucial for making q-KG practical, especially for larger batch sizes. This addresses a significant challenge in maximizing the acquisition function.
* **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experiments consistently demonstrate that q-KG outperforms existing methods, both on synthetic benchmarks and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks. The performance gains are particularly notable in noisy environments.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and generally clearly written, making it relatively easy to follow the core ideas and technical details.
* **Code Availability:** Providing code is a valuable contribution to the community and facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization Strategy:** While the paper mentions discretizing the search space for practical implementation (Section 5.3), the details regarding the choice of M (1000) and the method for generating the AMn samples are somewhat limited. A more in-depth discussion of the impact of this discretization on performance would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t thoroughly analyze the sensitivity of q-KG’s performance to its own hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate for the gradient-based optimization).  Understanding these sensitivities is important for practical application.
* **Computational Cost (Relative):**  While IPA improves efficiency, the paper doesn’t provide a detailed comparison of the computational cost of q-KG versus other methods like parallel EI.  Quantifying this cost difference would be helpful.
* **Limited Exploration of Noise Levels:** The noise experiments use a fixed noise level (σ = 0.5).  Exploring a wider range of noise levels would provide a more comprehensive understanding of q-KG’s robustness.
* **Experiment Scope:** While the real-world experiments are good, expanding the scope to include more diverse and challenging hyperparameter tuning problems would further strengthen the empirical validation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* q-KG presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to parallel BO with demonstrable improvements over existing methods.
* The efficient gradient estimation technique makes the method practical for realistic applications.
* The empirical results are compelling and support the claims made by the authors.
* The paper is well-written and contributes valuable insights to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed discussion regarding the discretization strategy and hyperparameter sensitivity are minor weaknesses.
* While the empirical results are strong, expanding the scope of experiments would further solidify the contributions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The proposed q-KG method is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The authors have addressed a key challenge in parallel BO – efficient batch selection – and have developed a practical and effective solution.  While there are a few areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  I believe this paper is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.  I would encourage the authors to address the minor weaknesses identified above in a revised version of the manuscript.  Specifically, more details on the discretization strategy and hyperparameter sensitivity would be valuable additions.
