Summary
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The method defines the expected one-step reduction in the GP posterior mean’s minimum after observing a batch, and optimizes this quantity over batches. Because exact evaluation/optimization is intractable for q > 1, the authors estimate q-KG via Monte Carlo by sampling the latent Gaussian Zq and use infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient, enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch locations. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization over A using candidates sampled from likely posterior minima (à la PES), augmented with evaluated points and the candidate batch. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression and a CNN suggest q-KG outperforms or matches parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with larger gains under noise.

Relation to prior work
- Extends KG (sequential) to the batch setting, where EI, UCB variants, and entropy-based methods (q-EI, PPES) already exist. The paper connects q-KG to q-EI in the noise-free, “final recommendation restricted to sampled points” limit, and uses gradient-estimation machinery similar to recent work on differentiable q-EI.
- Compared to PPES and related entropy-based batch methods, q-KG adopts a decision-theoretic one-step lookahead objective on solution quality (not mutual information), arguing better performance under noise.
- Related to nested batch KG on discrete domains; this work targets continuous domains and leverages IPA for efficiency.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic motivation: Bayes-optimal for one remaining batch under the GP posterior.
- Sound use of IPA to obtain an unbiased gradient estimator, which is practically important for optimizing a collective batch acquisition.
- The reduction to q-EI in the specific noise-free/restricted recommendation case provides a useful sanity check and situates the method among established baselines.
- Empirical results indicate consistent gains, especially with observation noise—an important regime for ML hyperparameter tuning.
- Public code and a reasonably careful engineering effort (C++ implementation).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited baselines: No comparison to parallel entropy search (PPES) or recent entropy-based batch methods that consider batches jointly, despite discussing them. At minimum, PPES is a key baseline for the collective-batch setting. The two EI baselines include a heuristic (constant liar) and MOE’s q-EI, but omitting PPES weakens the empirical case.
- Discretization sensitivity: The inner minimization over A is approximated via discretization (M = 1000). There is no ablation on M, sampling strategy, or stability of results to discretization quality. The guarantees and practical impact of this approximation are unclear.
- Computational analysis is underdeveloped: While IPA reduces complexity relative to naive enumeration, the paper lacks wall-clock comparisons, scalability with q and dimension, MC sample sizes, variance of the gradient estimator, and optimization settings (e.g., number of restarts). Empirical runtime and scaling are crucial for batch BO in practice.
- Theoretical caveats: The Bayes-optimality claim is strictly for one-step under a fixed GP posterior. In practice, hyperparameters are re-fit by MLE after each batch; there is no discussion of how this violates assumptions or affects the objective. Also, uniqueness/differentiability issues in argmins for IPA are pushed to the supplement; the main text should more clearly state and justify regularity assumptions and how ties/non-differentiabilities are handled.
- Experimental breadth: Results are mostly with q=4 and moderate dimensionality. No exploration of larger batches (e.g., q≥8) or higher-dimensional problems, where computational costs and discretization issues may be more severe.
- Statistical rigor: Some experiments use few runs (e.g., CNN with 5 runs), no statistical tests, and minimal reporting of uncertainty due to MC/discretization. The very large improvement on CIFAR-10 with a basic architecture warrants more careful controls.

Quality
- The core derivations are standard for GP posteriors and one-step lookahead, and the IPA-based gradient follows known techniques. However, important technical details (assumptions for unbiasedness, handling of non-smooth minima) are deferred and not critically discussed. The empirical section demonstrates promise but lacks key baselines, ablations, and runtime analysis needed to substantiate practical claims.

Clarity
- Generally readable and well organized. Some typos and minor issues (e.g., “Main Stange”). The algorithm and notation are clear. The discretization strategy could be described more concretely (how posterior minima samples are drawn; parameters; sensitivity). Provide explicit pseudo-code for the stochastic gradient ascent and optimization hyperparameters.

Originality
- The idea of extending KG to batch and optimizing a collective acquisition via IPA is a natural but nontrivial step. The connection to q-EI and the gradient estimator are not entirely novel in spirit but are meaningful in this context. Relative to PPES and q-EI, the objective is distinct and potentially advantageous under noise.

Significance
- Batch BO is important for ML hyperparameter tuning. If the method scales and consistently outperforms strong baselines, it could be adopted. However, the missing PPES baseline, absent runtime/scaling evidence, and limited ablations reduce the strength of the claim at this stage.

Questions and suggestions
- Include PPES as a baseline, or justify convincingly why it is infeasible here; report runtime and performance trade-offs.
- Report wall-clock time, number of MC samples for q-KG and its gradient, number of optimizer restarts, and gradient variance; provide scaling plots versus q and dimension.
- Ablate M and the discretization strategy; discuss convergence as M→∞ and practical guidance.
- Clarify assumptions for IPA unbiasedness when argmins are non-unique; describe tie-breaking.
- Evaluate larger q (e.g., 8, 16) and higher-dimensional problems.
- Discuss impact of re-fitting GP hyperparameters on the objective’s interpretation; consider marginalizing hyperparameters or at least evaluating robustness.
- Provide statistical tests and more repetitions for real tasks; control for randomness in training pipelines.

Pros
- Well-motivated acquisition; principled and potentially strong under noise.
- Joint batch optimization with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator.
- Consistent empirical gains over EI/UCB in reported settings; code available.

Cons
- Missing strong collective-batch baselines (PPES).
- Insufficient runtime/scaling characterization.
- Sensitivity to discretization not addressed.
- Limited evaluation breadth and statistical rigor.

Recommendation
- Score: 5 (borderline/weak reject)
- Confidence: 0.7

Rationale: The idea is solid and plausibly useful, but the empirical evidence is incomplete (missing PPES), and practical/scalability aspects are insufficiently analyzed. With stronger baselines, runtime/scaling evidence, and ablations, this could be a compelling contribution.
