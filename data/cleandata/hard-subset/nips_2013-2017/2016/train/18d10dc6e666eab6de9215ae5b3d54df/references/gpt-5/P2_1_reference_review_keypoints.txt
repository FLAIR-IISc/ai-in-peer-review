- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that generalizes Knowledge Gradient to simultaneously evaluate q points, defined as expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean.  
- q-KG is one-step Bayes-optimal when only one batch remains and reduces to parallel EI (q-EI) in the noiseless/sampled-point-restricted setting.  
- Main technical contribution: an efficient stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) enabling joint (non-greedy) batch optimization.  
- Inner minimization is discretized over a resampled set concentrated near posterior optima, unioned with past samples and the candidate batch.  
- Empirical comparisons against MOE’s q-EI, a Spearmint heuristic, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic benchmarks and two hyperparameter tuning tasks (q=4).  
- Results show q-KG is competitive or better, with especially strong gains under observation noise; code is open-source.  
- Acquisition is well-defined and grounded in decision theory; reduction to q-EI is correct and informative.  
- IPA-based gradient estimator is reasonable, but nondifferentiabilities from argmin switches require explicit technical conditions (e.g., uniqueness a.s.).  
- Differentiation through the Cholesky factor needs a reference or implementation detail for stability/efficiency.  
- Experimental details missing: Monte Carlo sample counts for q-KG and its gradient, optimizer settings, step sizes, number of restarts, and discretization sizing/scheme.  
- No wall-clock or runtime comparisons; q-KG is likely more computationally intensive and scalability to larger q/d is unclear.  
- Reproducibility is helped by code release and standard GP/kernel choices but paper should include MC budgets, stopping criteria, and discretization algorithms.  
- Clarity is generally good with a helpful algorithm box, but the two uses of A (discretized inner set vs continuous domain) could be clarified further.  
- A complexity discussion (per-evaluation/gradient cost as function of q, M, n) is missing and would aid practitioners.  
- Originality: novel and nontrivial extension of KG to batch with a tractable unbiased stochastic gradient estimator; distinct from q-EI, UCB-based, and entropy-based methods.  
- Paper does not compare to some relevant baselines (PPES, batch Thompson sampling) and omits more information-theoretic comparisons.  
- Significance: batch BO impact is high and empirical gains under noise are valuable, but significance would be higher with runtime and broader baseline/ablation studies.  
- Strength: principled decision-theoretic acquisition with a clear connection to q-EI and an efficient gradient estimator enabling joint optimization.  
- Strength: solid empirical improvements, especially in noisy settings, and open-source implementation using standard GP machinery.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on discretization for the inner min without sensitivity analysis on M or sampling strategy.  
- Weakness: missing baselines (PPES, batch Thompson sampling) and no runtime/complexity evaluation to assess practical scalability.  
- Weakness: theoretical analysis limited to one-step optimality; lacks regret or asymptotic guarantees.  
- Weakness: important technical details deferred to supplement and practical hyperparameters omitted.  
- Suggestions: add wall-clock comparisons and per-evaluation complexity, include PPES/batch-Thompson baselines, report MC/optimizer/discretization details, state IPA conditions, and reference Cholesky differentiation.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — meaningful novel contribution with empirical gains, but needs more baselines and practical/runtime details to strengthen the case.
