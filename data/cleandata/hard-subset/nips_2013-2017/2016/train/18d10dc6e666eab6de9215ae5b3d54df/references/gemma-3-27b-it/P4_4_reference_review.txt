This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization, extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient approach to the parallel setting. The core idea is to maximize the expected incremental value of a batch of evaluations, choosing points that are optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior distribution if only one batch remains. To address the computational challenges of maximizing this function, the authors leverage infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for efficient gradient estimation. The method is evaluated on synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, demonstrating improved performance compared to existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, method, and experimental results. It builds logically upon existing work in Bayesian Optimization, specifically highlighting the limitations of extending sequential methods directly to the parallel setting. The connection to existing parallel methods like parallel EI and UCB is clearly articulated, and the paper emphasizes the key difference: q-KG considers the batch collectively rather than greedily adding points. The mathematical formulation is generally sound, and the derivation of q-KG, while potentially dense for some readers, appears rigorous.  The use of IPA for gradient estimation is a significant contribution, enabling optimization of the acquisition function in higher dimensions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The q-KG acquisition function represents a novel approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization, moving beyond iterative single-point optimization.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is grounded in decision-theoretic principles and the Knowledge Gradient framework. The IPA-based gradient estimation is a sound and practical optimization technique.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over existing parallel BO algorithms on both synthetic and real-world problems, particularly when dealing with noisy evaluations.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the key ideas and contributions.
*   **Availability of Code:** The authors provide code, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While IPA improves gradient estimation, the computational cost of maximizing q-KG, especially for large batch sizes and high-dimensional problems, remains a concern. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the scalability limitations and potential further optimizations.
*   **Discretization of A:** The method for discretizing the feasible domain (A) introduces an approximation. The impact of the discretization on the performance is not fully explored. A more in-depth sensitivity analysis of the parameter M (number of samples) would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method might be sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters within the Gaussian process (e.g., kernel parameters). This aspect is not thoroughly investigated in the experiments.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is mostly limited to a few existing parallel BO algorithms. A more comprehensive comparison against recent advancements in parallel optimization, potentially including methods that utilize different acquisition functions or optimization strategies, would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Detailed Explanation of IPA:** While the use of IPA is mentioned, a slightly more detailed explanation of how it is applied to this specific context would benefit readers less familiar with the technique.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements over existing methods.
*   The code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.
*   The method addresses a practical challenge – efficient parallel optimization of expensive black-box functions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the method remains a concern for large-scale problems.
*   The discretization of the search space introduces an approximation that requires further investigation.
*   The comparison to other state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The proposed q-KG method is novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically effective. While the computational complexity and discretization issues are worth noting, they do not overshadow the overall value of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly focusing on a more detailed discussion of scalability and sensitivity analysis.
