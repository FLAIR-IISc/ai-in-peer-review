

# Peer Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

## Summary and Context
This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that extends the knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that jointly optimizes a batch of q points, rather than selecting points sequentially. The key innovation is the use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate the gradient of the acquisition function, enabling practical implementation. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over state-of-the-art batch BO methods on both synthetic functions and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, with particularly strong performance in noisy settings.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper builds on a solid decision-theoretic framework, clearly establishing q-KG as Bayes-optimal for minimizing the GP predictor's minimum when only one batch remains. This is a significant conceptual advancement over iterative batch methods that optimize single-point acquisition functions.

2. **Computational Innovation**: The IPA-based gradient estimation approach is well-motivated and effectively addresses the computational challenges of optimizing the q-KG acquisition function. This is a major practical contribution that enables the method to scale to larger batch sizes.

3. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed, covering both noise-free and noisy settings on multiple synthetic functions and two practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN tuning on CIFAR10). The results consistently demonstrate q-KG's superiority, particularly in noisy settings where the advantage over competitors is most pronounced.

4. **Clear Differentiation from Related Work**: The paper effectively distinguishes its approach from prior parallel BO methods, especially by highlighting how q-KG optimizes the batch collectively rather than iteratively (as in parallel EI or GP-BUCB).

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Limitations**: While the paper establishes q-KG as Bayes-optimal for the one-batch case, it lacks theoretical guarantees for the multi-batch setting. The authors should clarify whether the sequential application of q-KG (as described in Algorithm 1) maintains any theoretical optimality properties, or whether it's primarily a practical heuristic.

2. **Discretization Justification**: Section 5.3's discretization approach (using samples from the posterior's global optima) lacks sufficient theoretical justification. The paper doesn't explain why this specific discretization strategy is optimal or how it affects the convergence properties of the method. A more detailed analysis of the discretization error would strengthen the paper.

3. **Comparison with [26]**: The authors briefly mention [26] (nested batch knowledge gradient) but don't provide a thorough comparison. Given that [26] also addresses batch knowledge gradient, the paper should clarify how their approach differs fundamentally from this related work, particularly regarding the handling of continuous vs. discrete domains.

4. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis of the IPA-based gradient estimation. It would be helpful to understand how the computational cost scales with q (batch size) and d (dimensionality), as this is crucial for practical implementation.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to batch Bayesian optimization. The q-KG method is theoretically well-founded, computationally efficient, and empirically superior to existing approaches, particularly in noisy settings where many practical applications operate. The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by moving beyond iterative batch construction to joint optimization.

The main weaknesses are theoretical in nature - the lack of multi-batch theoretical guarantees and insufficient justification for the discretization approach. However, these are relatively minor given the paper's strong experimental results and practical contributions. The computational innovation using IPA is particularly valuable for making batch Bayesian optimization feasible at scale.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. While there are some theoretical gaps to address, the empirical results are compelling and the method is likely to be widely adopted in practice. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and makes a clear contribution that advances the state of the art in batch Bayesian optimization.

The authors should consider addressing the theoretical limitations in the final version, particularly regarding the multi-batch setting and discretization error. However, these would be valuable additions rather than prerequisites for publication. The paper is already a substantial contribution that will be of interest to the NIPS community and practitioners working with expensive optimization problems.
