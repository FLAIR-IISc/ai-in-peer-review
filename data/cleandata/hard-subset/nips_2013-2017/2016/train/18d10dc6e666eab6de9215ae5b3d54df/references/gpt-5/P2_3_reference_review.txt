Summary
The paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to selecting q points simultaneously. The core idea is decision-theoretic: choose the batch that maximizes the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after observing the batch. Evaluating q-KG requires Monte Carlo because the future posterior mean depends on random observations; the authors derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling efficient gradient-based optimization of the batch. For continuous domains, they approximate the inner minimizations by discretization using a dynamically updated candidate set. Experiments on synthetic functions and two ML hyperparameter tuning tasks show that q-KG is competitive or superior to parallel EI (both analytic and heuristic variants), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with especially strong gains under observation noise. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Extends classic sequential KG to the batch setting; contrasts with parallel EI (both analytic multi-point EI and greedy heuristics), batch UCB variants (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), and batch entropy search (PES).
- Shows that, under noiseless evaluations and if the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI, clarifying the relationship to EI.
- Compared to prior “nested batch KG” on discrete domains, this work targets continuous domains and provides an efficient stochastic gradient estimator, addressing scalability.
- Uses standard GP modeling with ARD Matérn-5/2, hyperparameters via MLE, and re-estimation after each batch.

Quality
Strengths:
- The decision-theoretic formulation is sound and mirrors the well-established optimality of KG for a single remaining step.
- The IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator is a technically meaningful contribution, making joint batch optimization practical without resorting to greedy selection.
- Empirical results are broad enough to be convincing within the typical BO scope: four standard test functions (2–6D) and two real hyperparameter tuning tasks (MNIST logistic regression and CIFAR-10 CNN), with multiple independent runs and both noise-free and noisy settings. Gains are particularly clear in noisy evaluations, which is an important practical regime.
- Implementation details and open-source code improve reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
- Theory stops at the one-step Bayes-optimality claim and unbiased gradient derivation. There is no analysis of discretization error (continuous-to-discrete approximation), finite-sample properties, regret bounds, or consistency. While not mandatory for BO papers, some guidance on approximation error or convergence would strengthen the work.
- The discretization strategy is underspecified: “draw M samples from the global optima of the posterior distribution” is vague. How are these samples produced (e.g., Thompson sampling over function minimizers, PES methodology)? How sensitive are results to M and the sampling method?
- Computational complexity and runtime are not reported. Since q-KG requires MC sampling, Cholesky factorizations for the batch covariance, and inner minimizations over a discretized set, a wall-clock comparison to parallel EI and PES would be informative. The experiments use q = 4; it would be helpful to see scaling with larger q and higher dimensions.
- Some implementation asymmetries could bias comparisons (e.g., hyperparameter re-estimation every batch for q-KG—do baselines do the same?); this should be clarified.

Clarity
Strengths:
- The paper is generally well organized. The definition of q-KG is clear, the relation to EI under special conditions is helpful, and the algorithmic outline is understandable.
- The Monte Carlo construction via expressing the updated posterior mean as µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z(1:q))Zq is clear and standard.

Areas to improve:
- The discretization section should more precisely describe how An is constructed and provide intuition, possibly with ablations or references detailing the sampling of posterior optima.
- Provide the number of Monte Carlo samples used for acquisition and gradient estimation, and discuss variance control.
- Minor typos and notational slips (e.g., “Main Stange”) can be polished.

Originality
- The batch extension of KG with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator appears novel in the continuous-domain setting. The link to EI under noiseless/restricted recommendation is a nice insight.
- While Monte Carlo optimization of batch acquisitions is not new, the derivation and use of IPA for q-KG gradients is a meaningful contribution beyond prior batch EI/PES work.

Significance
- Batch BO is widely used in practice, and robustness under noise is especially valuable. If q-KG consistently outperforms commonly used parallel EI/UCB methods, this is a significant practical advance.
- The open-source implementation lowers the barrier to adoption.
- The absence of runtime and larger-scale studies somewhat tempers immediate impact; nonetheless, the methodological contribution is likely to influence subsequent work on batch BO and decision-theoretic acquisitions.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and principled acquisition design with a solid connection to KG.
- Technically interesting gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise.
- Public code.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical analysis beyond one-step optimality and gradient correctness; no discretization error guarantees.
- Missing runtime/scaling evaluations and sensitivity analyses (M, MC budget).
- Some ambiguity in discretization procedure and potential reproducibility gaps.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Add a brief complexity analysis and report wall-clock times versus baselines for varying q and d.
- Clarify and justify the discretization scheme; include sensitivity to M and an ablation comparing alternative candidate set constructions.
- Report MC sample sizes and discuss gradient variance control; consider using common random numbers to reduce variance.
- Ensure fair hyperparameter handling across methods and state these details explicitly.
- Consider an asynchronous extension discussion, as it is common in practical batch BO.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid, well-motivated contribution that advances batch Bayesian optimization with a principled acquisition and an efficient optimization scheme. Despite some gaps in theoretical and scalability analysis, the empirical gains and methodological clarity make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
