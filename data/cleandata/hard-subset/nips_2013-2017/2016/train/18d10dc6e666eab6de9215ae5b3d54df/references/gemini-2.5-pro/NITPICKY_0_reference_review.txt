This paper proposes a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, selecting a batch of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the posterior mean's minimum value after one additional batch evaluation. This extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) framework to the parallel setting. To overcome the computational challenge of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function, the authors develop an efficient, gradient-based optimization approach using an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator derived via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method is evaluated on several synthetic benchmark functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), in both noise-free and noisy settings. The results demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods, such as parallel Expected Improvement and parallel UCB variants, with particularly strong performance in noisy environments.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The extension of the knowledge gradient criterion to the batch setting is a significant and well-motivated contribution. Unlike many heuristic approaches to parallel Bayesian optimization that construct batches greedily, q-KG provides a principled, decision-theoretic framework for jointly selecting the optimal set of points. The formulation is technically sound.
2.  **Efficient Optimization Strategy:** A key contribution is the development of a computationally tractable method for optimizing the q-KG acquisition function. The use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to obtain an unbiased estimator of the gradient is elegant and enables the use of standard gradient-based optimizers. This is a non-trivial technical accomplishment that makes the proposed method practical.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance, Especially in Noisy Settings:** The experimental evaluation is reasonably thorough, covering both synthetic and real-world problems. The method shows compelling performance, consistently matching or exceeding state-of-the-art baselines. The reported advantage in noisy settings is particularly noteworthy, as this is a common and challenging scenario in practice (e.g., when evaluating models on a subset of a validation set).
4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors state that code is available, which is a commendable practice that facilitates verification and adoption by the community.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, methodological detail, and experimental reporting. These issues, in aggregate, detract significantly from the quality of the submission.

1.  **Clarity and Formatting:** The paper exhibits a lack of meticulous preparation.
    *   The bibliography is in a state of extreme disrepair. Nearly every single reference is incomplete, missing critical information such as the conference or journal name, year, and page numbers. This is not a minor issue; it makes it difficult for a reader to consult the cited work and reflects a profound lack of care.
    *   There are formatting artifacts, such as the floating line "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain." in the introduction.
    *   Subsection numbering in Section 5 is incorrect (e.g., ".1" instead of "5.1").
    *   There are consistent grammatical errors, such as the repeated use of "state-of-art" instead of "state-of-the-art".

2.  **Missing Methodological Details:** The paper omits several crucial details required for a full understanding and reproduction of the work.
    *   The discretization of the domain `A` in Section 5.3 is a critical component of the algorithm. The choice of `M = 1000` samples from the posterior optima is presented without any justification or sensitivity analysis. The performance of the method could be highly dependent on this hyperparameter, but this is not explored.
    *   While the authors claim their gradient estimation method is "efficient," they provide no computational complexity analysis. What is the cost of computing the stochastic gradient as a function of the batch size `q`, dimension `d`, and discretization size `M`? How does this compare to the cost of optimizing the baselines? This omission makes it impossible to assess the practical cost-benefit trade-off of the proposed method.
    *   The optimization procedure for the acquisition function is underspecified. The authors mention "multi-start stochastic gradient ascent," but do not state the number of restarts, the learning rate schedule, or the number of gradient steps used. These details are essential for reproducibility.
    *   The claim in Section 4 that q-KG reduces to parallel EI is imprecise. The provided derivation is a simplification and its exact relationship to the parallel EI formulations in the cited works [2, 25] is not clearly established.

3.  **Experimental Deficiencies:**
    *   The description of the baselines is confusing. The paper compares against two different implementations of parallel EI (one from MOE, one from Spearmint) without clearly justifying why both are needed or which one is represented in the plots.
    *   The number of independent runs for the expensive CNN experiment is very low (n=5). While the cost is high, this small sample size limits the statistical significance of the conclusions drawn from this experiment.
    *   The paper refers to Figures 3 and 4, but neither the figures nor their captions are included in the provided text, making it impossible to fully evaluate the experimental results.
    *   The claim of improving a human-tuned CNN from 14% to 11% error is strong, but the "human expert" baseline is not properly cited or described, making the comparison difficult to contextualize.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces a well-motivated and technically interesting approach to batch Bayesian optimization. The core idea of a parallel knowledge gradient is sound, and the use of IPA for its optimization is a valuable contribution. However, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. The large number of minor flaws—ranging from a deeply flawed bibliography and formatting errors to a significant lack of methodological detail and experimental clarity—collectively undermine the paper's scientific quality. While the core research contribution is potentially significant, the execution of the paper itself does not meet the high standards of the conference. I recommend rejection, but I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the paper to address the issues raised and resubmit to a future venue.
