This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), designed for parallel function evaluations. The core idea is to extend the Knowledge Gradient concept to the batch setting, choosing a set of points that maximize the expected incremental value of information gained from evaluating them simultaneously. To address the computational complexity of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function, the authors develop an efficient gradient-based optimization method utilizing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method is empirically evaluated on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios.

The paper tackles a relevant and important problem in Bayesian Optimization – efficiently utilizing parallel evaluations. The extension of the Knowledge Gradient to the batch setting is a natural and promising direction, and the authors successfully develop a computationally tractable approach for optimizing the resulting acquisition function. The experiments, while not exhaustive, do demonstrate a consistent improvement over established baselines, particularly in noisy settings, highlighting a key strength of the proposed method. The inclusion of code is also a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility.

However, several weaknesses and areas for improvement exist. Firstly, while the paper positions q-KG as a significant advancement, the performance gains, while consistent, are not always dramatic. A more thorough statistical analysis, including confidence intervals and effect sizes, is needed to convincingly demonstrate the significance of the improvements, especially on the more complex real-world problems. 

Secondly, the description of the experimental setup lacks crucial details. For example, the specific CNN architecture used for the CIFAR10 tuning task isn’t fully specified, making it difficult to reproduce the results. Furthermore, the justification for the choice of hyperparameters used in the benchmark algorithms isn't provided. Were they tuned or taken from previous studies? This information is critical for a fair comparison.

Thirdly, the connection to related work could be strengthened. The paper mentions [26] but dismisses it rather quickly, focusing on the differing application domain. However, the core idea of optimizing a batch acquisition function is relevant, and a more nuanced comparison would be beneficial. Also, while the paper highlights the advantages of q-KG over UCB-based methods due to minimizing simple regret, this point isn't fully elaborated and could be clarified with more detailed theoretical discussion.

Fourthly, the discretization strategy for the continuous domain A, using samples from the posterior distribution, is a crucial aspect of the IPA-based gradient estimation. However, the paper provides limited justification for the choice of M = 1000. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of M on the performance and computational cost would be valuable.

Finally, the paper is somewhat dense and could benefit from improved clarity in certain sections. For example, the derivation of the gradient in Section 5.2 is quite involved and might be difficult for readers unfamiliar with IPA to follow. More intuitive explanations and intermediate steps could improve readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of the Knowledge Gradient to the parallel setting.
*   Development of an efficient gradient-based optimization method.
*   Empirically demonstrates improved performance over existing methods, especially in noisy scenarios.
*   Code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Performance gains, while consistent, aren't always substantial.
*   Insufficient detail in the experimental setup, hindering reproducibility.
*   Limited discussion of related work and a potentially too-quick dismissal of relevant contributions.
*   Lack of sensitivity analysis regarding the discretization parameter M.
*   Dense writing and complex derivations may hinder readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization, offering a novel and theoretically sound method for parallel evaluation. However, the limited statistical analysis, lack of experimental details, and areas for improved clarity raise concerns about the robustness and reproducibility of the results. While the improvements demonstrated are promising, further work is needed to solidify the method’s advantages and make it more accessible to the broader research community.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses in a revised version. Specifically, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation with statistical analysis, increased clarity in the experimental setup and derivations, and a more thorough discussion of related work would significantly strengthen the paper.
