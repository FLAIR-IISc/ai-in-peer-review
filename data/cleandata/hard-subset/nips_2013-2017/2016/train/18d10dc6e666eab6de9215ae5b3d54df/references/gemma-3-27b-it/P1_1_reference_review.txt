## Peer Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient policy to the parallel setting. The core idea is to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected incremental improvement in the solution quality, considering the entire batch collectively, rather than iteratively adding points based on single-evaluation acquisition functions. The authors address the computational challenges of maximizing q-KG by utilizing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate its gradient.  Empirical results on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning demonstrate q-KG’s superior performance, especially in noisy evaluation scenarios, compared to existing parallel BO algorithms like parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing body of work on parallel BO. It acknowledges the iterative greedy approaches of parallel EI and UCB methods ([19, 5, 3]) and highlights the advantage of their method’s batch-wise optimization. The connection to the original Knowledge Gradient method ([17]) is well explained, and the authors differentiate their work by addressing the computational complexity introduced by parallel evaluation and proposing an efficient gradient estimation technique. They also appropriately acknowledge related work leveraging gradients for parallel EI ([15, 25]) and nested batch knowledge gradients ([26]), pointing out the limitations of the latter in terms of gradient existence and computational efficiency. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The q-KG acquisition function represents a principled approach to batch BO, directly optimizing for information gain across the entire batch. This contrasts with the prevalent iterative approaches.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is solid and well-explained.  The Bayes-optimality argument for the single-batch case is compelling.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The use of IPA to efficiently estimate the gradient of q-KG is a significant contribution, enabling the optimization of a potentially complex acquisition function.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate q-KG’s superiority over competing methods, particularly in noisy settings.  The consistent improvement across both synthetic and real-world problems is encouraging.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The algorithm is clearly described, and the notation is consistent.
* **Code Availability:** Providing the code is a huge plus for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization Strategy:**  While the authors mention discretizing the domain A, the reliance on sampling from the global optima of the posterior (using M=1000) may be sensitive to the accuracy of the GP model and the choice of M.  A more detailed discussion of the impact of this discretization on performance, and potential alternative discretization strategies, would be valuable.  It’s not entirely clear *why* this discretization scheme is expected to work well.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t explicitly discuss the sensitivity of the performance to hyperparameters of the q-KG method itself (beyond M).  Understanding the robustness of the method to its own parameters is important for practical application.
* **Scalability Discussion:** While IPA addresses the gradient computation, the scalability of the method to very high-dimensional problems (e.g., tuning very large neural networks) isn't thoroughly discussed.  The computational cost of inverting the covariance matrix in the GP, even with efficient methods, remains a potential bottleneck.
* **Limited Comparison to Recent Methods:** The landscape of parallel BO is rapidly evolving. Comparing to more recent and potentially competitive methods (beyond those cited) would strengthen the empirical evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The authors present a theoretically sound and empirically validated method for parallel BO that addresses a key limitation of existing approaches. The computational contribution of using IPA to efficiently estimate the gradient is particularly valuable.  While some minor weaknesses exist regarding discretization strategy and scalability, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and principled approach to parallel BO.
* Strong theoretical foundation.
* Effective computational technique (IPA) for gradient estimation.
* Compelling empirical results demonstrating superior performance.
* Clear writing and organization.
* Code availability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Discretization strategy could be more thoroughly justified and investigated.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Scalability to very high-dimensional problems is not fully addressed.




**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions.  The authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the discretization strategy used to approximate q-KG, including justification for the choice of sampling from the posterior optima and a sensitivity analysis of the parameter M.
2.  Include a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the method’s performance to its own hyperparameters.
3.  Expand the discussion on the scalability of q-KG to high-dimensional problems, acknowledging potential limitations and suggesting possible mitigation strategies.
4. Consider including comparison to more recent parallel BO algorithms if possible.
