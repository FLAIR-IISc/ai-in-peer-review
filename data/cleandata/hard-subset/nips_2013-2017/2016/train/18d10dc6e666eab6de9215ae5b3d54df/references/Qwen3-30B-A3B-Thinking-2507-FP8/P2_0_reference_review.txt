

# NIPS Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG), which extends the sequential knowledge gradient approach to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that chooses a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously, rather than sequentially, by maximizing the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) the theoretical development of the q-KG acquisition function, and (2) a computationally efficient approach to optimize q-KG using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on both synthetic functions and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks that q-KG consistently outperforms existing batch Bayesian optimization methods, with particularly significant improvements in noisy settings.

The paper builds on previous work in Bayesian optimization, particularly the knowledge gradient method [17] for sequential optimization, and extends it to the parallel setting. It also relates to recent work on parallelizing expected improvement [2, 19, 25] and UCB [5, 3]. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, positioning their contribution clearly within the existing literature.

## Evaluation of Review Criteria

### Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated and correctly extends the sequential knowledge gradient approach to the parallel setting.
- The computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute the gradient of q-KG is clever and well-executed. The authors provide sufficient mathematical details to verify the correctness of their approach.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and well-designed, covering both synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and real-world applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10).
- The authors carefully compare q-KG against relevant baselines (parallel EI, batch UCB, parallel UCB with exploration) and provide statistically significant results with appropriate error bars and multiple independent runs.
- The paper acknowledges the computational complexity of q-KG while demonstrating that their IPA-based approach makes it practical to implement.

**Weaknesses:**
- The computational complexity analysis is somewhat superficial. The authors state that "naively maximizing q-KG would be extremely computationally intensive," but they don't provide a detailed complexity comparison with other methods (e.g., how does q-KG scale with q compared to parallel EI or batch UCB?).
- The choice of M = 1000 for discretization (Section 5.3) is not justified. It's unclear whether this value was determined through empirical testing or is a standard choice in the literature.
- The paper doesn't thoroughly address the limitations of q-KG in high-dimensional settings, which is a significant practical concern for Bayesian optimization.

### Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The structure follows a logical flow from problem statement to theory, computational method, and experimental results.
- The mathematical notation is consistently defined and used throughout the paper, making it easy to follow the theoretical developments.
- The explanation of the q-KG acquisition function is particularly clear, with a good balance between mathematical rigor and intuitive explanation.
- The experimental section is well-organized, with clear presentation of results for both noise-free and noisy settings.
- The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation details and experimental parameters.

**Weaknesses:**
- The connection between the theoretical development and the practical implementation could be slightly strengthened. For example, the relationship between the discrete set An used in the computation and the continuous domain A could be explained more thoroughly.

### Originality

**Strengths:**
- The extension of knowledge gradient to the parallel setting is novel. While there are other parallel BO methods, q-KG is unique in how it considers the batch of points collectively rather than iteratively.
- The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous contributions, particularly in Section 2 (Related Work) and the comparison to parallel EI in Section 4.2.
- The computational technique using IPA to efficiently compute the gradient of q-KG is also novel and well-executed.
- The paper is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers on Bayesian optimization, as evidenced by the thorough related work section that cites recent NIPS proceedings.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't provide a sufficiently detailed comparison with some of the most recent batch BO methods (e.g., [15, 18, 25] mentioned in the related work but not fully compared in experiments).

### Significance

**Strengths:**
- The problem addressed (batch Bayesian optimization) is highly relevant to practical machine learning applications where parallel evaluations are common (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines).
- The results demonstrate clear and consistent improvements over existing methods, especially in noisy settings, which is particularly valuable for real-world applications where function evaluations are often noisy.
- The method has clear potential for adoption in machine learning practice, as demonstrated by the successful application to tuning logistic regression and CNNs.
- The paper advances the state of the art in batch Bayesian optimization by providing a theoretically grounded approach that outperforms existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't fully explore the potential impact of the method in other domains beyond machine learning hyperparameter tuning.
- The significance of the improvements in noisy settings could be more explicitly discussed in terms of practical implications.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization by introducing a novel parallel acquisition function (q-KG) that is theoretically grounded and empirically superior to existing methods. The theoretical development is sound, the computational approach is efficient and well-executed, and the experimental results are comprehensive and compelling.

The main weakness is the lack of detailed computational complexity analysis and justification for the discretization parameter M=1000. However, these are minor issues that could be addressed in a revision.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper is a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, with significant theoretical and practical impact. The work is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS, particularly in terms of quality, clarity, originality, and significance.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. Consider adding a more detailed computational complexity analysis comparing q-KG with parallel EI and batch UCB, especially with respect to the batch size q.

2. Justify the choice of M=1000 for discretization. Was this determined empirically? How does the performance vary with different values of M?

3. Expand the discussion of limitations to include the scalability of q-KG to high-dimensional problems (d > 10), which is a significant challenge for Bayesian optimization.

4. Consider comparing against more recent batch BO methods, such as those mentioned in [15, 18, 25], to strengthen the empirical comparison.

5. In the conclusion, briefly discuss potential future directions, such as extending q-KG to handle constraints or multi-objective optimization.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization and is well-suited for publication at NIPS.
