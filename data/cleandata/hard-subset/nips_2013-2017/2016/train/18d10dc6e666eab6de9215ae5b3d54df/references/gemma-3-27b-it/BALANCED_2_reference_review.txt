This paper introduces a novel approach to batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea is to extend the knowledge gradient concept, traditionally used in sequential BO, to the parallel setting, aiming to select a batch of points that maximize information gain. The authors derive the q-KG acquisition function and, importantly, address the computational challenges of its optimization using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method is evaluated on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, demonstrating competitive performance, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents its contributions. The motivation for parallel BO is well established, and the connection to the existing literature on both sequential and parallel BO is thorough. The authors accurately position their work relative to alternatives like parallel EI and UCB, highlighting the key difference of considering the batch collectively rather than iteratively. The derivation of the q-KG function, while requiring some familiarity with GPs and decision theory, is reasonably clear and justified. The use of IPA to address the computational burden is a clever and significant contribution, and the explanation is accessible. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results are presented comprehensively with comparisons to several baselines. The availability of code is also a significant positive aspect.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the IPA technique is explained, the computational complexity analysis could be more detailed. Specifically, a more explicit discussion of how the computational cost scales with q and the dimensionality of the problem would be valuable. The paper mentions that discretizing the search space (A) is important, but the discussion of the discretization strategy (using global optima of the posterior) feels somewhat cursory. It would be beneficial to include a more in-depth exploration of the sensitivity of the results to the discretization parameters (e.g., the number of samples, M).  The empirical evaluation, while thorough, primarily focuses on comparing q-KG to existing methods. Investigating *why* q-KG performs better, perhaps through ablation studies or visualizations of the posterior distributions, would strengthen the analysis.  Furthermore, the advantages of q-KG are presented as particularly strong in noisy settings, but the level of noise in the experiments seems relatively mild.  Exploring more extreme noise levels could further highlight the robustness of the method.  The phrasing regarding the "optimality" of q-KG being limited to the "average case" is a bit vague and could be clarified. It's essential to emphasize that this optimality relates to the final iteration given the constraints.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The q-KG acquisition function is a novel extension of the KG concept to the parallel setting.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation is mathematically sound, and the use of IPA is a clever solution to the computational complexity.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The method consistently performs competitively with, and often outperforms, existing parallel BO algorithms on both synthetic and real-world problems.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts and methodology.
*   **Reproducibility:** Code availability facilitates reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Computational Complexity Analysis:** Lacks detailed discussion of computational cost scaling.
*   **Discretization Strategy:** Discretization strategy is not explored in sufficient depth.
*   **Limited Insight into Performance:**  The empirical analysis lacks in-depth investigation of *why* q-KG performs well.
*   **Noise Level:** Experiments with noise could be more extensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The q-KG method is a promising new approach to parallel BO, and the authors have successfully addressed the associated computational challenges. While some areas could be strengthened through further analysis and experimentation, the paper is of high quality and warrants acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above regarding computational complexity, discretization, and insights into performance. Specifically, the authors should consider adding a section to discuss the runtime complexity in more detail and potentially include visualizations or ablation studies to support their claims about q-KG's advantages.
