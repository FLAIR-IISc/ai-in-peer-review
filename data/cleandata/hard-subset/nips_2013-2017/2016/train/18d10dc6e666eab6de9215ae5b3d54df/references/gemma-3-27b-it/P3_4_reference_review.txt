## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of parallel Bayesian Optimization (BO). The proposed Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method offers a principled, decision-theoretic approach to batch acquisition, demonstrably outperforming existing methods, particularly in noisy evaluation settings. The efficient gradient estimation via Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) is a clever and crucial addition, making the method practical. While the presentation could be streamlined, the technical depth and empirical results strongly support acceptance to NIPS.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper tackles the important problem of efficiently optimizing expensive black-box functions using BO in a parallel setting. Existing parallel BO methods largely rely on iteratively selecting points based on single-evaluation acquisition functions, a greedy approach. This paper moves beyond this by proposing q-KG, which jointly optimizes the acquisition function for a batch of *q* points, directly leveraging the decision-theoretic benefits of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) in a parallel context. 

The authors clearly position their work within the broader context of BO and parallel optimization. They rightly highlight the limitations of previous parallel approaches like parallel EI and UCB, which often prioritize immediate regret minimization rather than the broader exploration benefit captured by KG. The related work section is well-structured and covers relevant literature, including a good discussion of the challenges in extending KG to the parallel setting. They correctly point out the differences from nested-batch approaches like [26], emphasizing the computational advantages of their IPA-based gradient estimation.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Principled Approach:** Deriving the acquisition function from a decision-theoretic framework (Bayes-optimality for the final iteration) is a strong foundation. This contrasts with many heuristic approaches in parallel BO.
* **Superior Performance:** The empirical results are compelling. q-KG consistently outperforms competing methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, parallel UCB with exploration) across both synthetic functions and real-world problems (logistic regression, CNN tuning). The advantage is particularly pronounced in noisy scenarios, which is a practical and crucial setting.
* **Efficient Gradient Estimation:** The use of IPA to efficiently compute the gradient of q-KG is a key innovation. This makes the method scalable to larger batch sizes, addressing a major computational bottleneck. 
* **Clear Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 1 provides a concise and understandable outline of the q-KG procedure.
* **Open Source Code:** The availability of code is excellent for reproducibility and encourages further research.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The connection to the sequential KG method and the Bayes-optimality argument provide a strong theoretical foundation.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Notation:** The paper suffers from a density of notation, particularly in Section 5. The multiple uses of 'A' (feasible domain vs. discretization points) can be confusing.  More careful notation and explanation would significantly improve readability.
* **Discretization Strategy:** While the dynamic discretization of the search space (using samples from the posterior) is a reasonable approach, the justification for M=1000 feels somewhat arbitrary. A more detailed discussion of the impact of M on performance, and perhaps a sensitivity analysis, would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments do not thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of q-KG to its hyperparameters (e.g., parameters controlling the IPA optimization).  A more robust analysis would strengthen the claim of generalizability.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** While IPA addresses a significant bottleneck, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of q-KG compared to other methods (especially for larger batch sizes and higher-dimensional problems) would be valuable. A complexity analysis would be helpful.
* **Presentation:** While technically strong, the writing style can be dense and lacks a narrative flow. Rewriting certain sections with a more pedagogical approach could improve understanding.



**4. Originality:**

The work is highly original. While parallel BO has been explored, the application of the KG framework to the batch setting, coupled with the efficient IPA-based gradient computation, represents a novel and significant advance. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous efforts by focusing on joint optimization of the acquisition function and addressing the computational challenges.

**5. Significance:**

The potential impact of this work is substantial. Efficient parallel BO is critical for accelerating the optimization of expensive, real-world applications, particularly in areas like hyperparameter tuning of deep learning models. q-KG's superior performance, especially in noisy environments, makes it a valuable tool for practitioners. The theoretical insights and computational techniques presented in the paper are likely to inspire further research in this area.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Significant performance improvement over existing parallel BO methods.
* Principled derivation from decision theory.
* Innovative and effective gradient estimation technique (IPA).
* Thorough empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world problems.
* Open source implementation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Dense notation and occasionally unclear writing.
* Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Lack of detailed computational cost analysis.



**Final Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** The paper presents a strong technical contribution with significant potential impact. The primary recommendation is to improve the clarity of the presentation, particularly in Section 5, and to address the minor weaknesses regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost. Addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and enhance its accessibility to a wider audience. This work is worthy of presentation at NIPS.
