This paper presents a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), designed for scenarios with parallel function evaluations. The core idea is to leverage a decision-theoretic analysis to select a batch of points that maximizes information gain, going beyond simple extensions of sequential acquisition functions to the parallel setting. The authors also address the computational challenges of optimizing q-KG through infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). While the presented approach demonstrates promising empirical results, outperforming several baselines on both synthetic and practical problems, a number of methodological and presentation issues significantly detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The q-KG acquisition function offers a theoretically grounded approach to parallel BO, moving beyond greedy iterative batch construction. The decision-theoretic foundation is appealing.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over existing parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy settings, showcasing the method’s practical potential.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The introduction of IPA to efficiently estimate q-KG's gradient is a valuable contribution, addressing a significant hurdle in practical application.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** While comparisons are made to several baselines, the parameter tuning for these baselines isn’t thoroughly detailed. The claim of "consistent" outperformance feels overstated without a more rigorous comparison – hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for competing methods is missing.
*   **Discretization Strategy:** The discretization of the search space A using samples from the posterior (Section 5.3) is vaguely described. The selection of M=1000 seems arbitrary. A justification or sensitivity analysis regarding the choice of M is lacking. Its impact on performance is not discussed.
*   **Implementation Details:** Several details about the implementation are missing. For example, what optimization algorithm is used to maximize q-KG? What are the specific stopping criteria? These omissions hinder reproducibility.
*   **Overly Dense Writing:** The paper is incredibly dense. Several sentences could be broken down for improved readability. The mathematical notation is often presented without sufficient explanation, requiring the reader to repeatedly refer back to earlier sections.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:**
    *   Equation (3.1) lacks proper formatting; the diagonal matrix definition is not visually clear.
    *   In Section 5.1, the definition of σ̃n(x, z(1:q)) relies on the Cholesky factor D(n)(z(1:q)). It's not explicitly stated whether this is computed at each iteration, which impacts computational cost.
    *   The justification for the application of IPA in Section 5.2 feels superficial. A more detailed explanation of why the differentiability assumptions hold for practical GP kernels would strengthen the argument.
*   **Related Work Gaps:** The discussion of related work, while extensive, misses crucial recent developments in parallel BO, specifically those focusing on diversity promotion and acquisition function design beyond EI and UCB. The claim that UCB-based methods "underperform in BO" is too strong and requires more nuance.
*   **Figure Captions:** The figure captions (e.g., Figure 3 and 4) are inadequate. They lack sufficient detail regarding the specific experiment setup and the meaning of the plotted curves.

**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

*   **Section 4:** The connection between q-KG and parallel EI under noise-free conditions and the restriction to previously sampled points is not sufficiently clear. Elaborate on the precise conditions under which this reduction occurs.
*   **Section 5.2:** The claim of an "unbiased estimator" for the gradient requires careful justification, especially considering the potential for numerical instability in the IPA calculation.
*   **Section 6:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. How was the initial Latin Hypercube design constructed? What are the specific hyperparameter settings for each algorithm? Providing a table summarizing these details would significantly improve reproducibility.
*   **Figure 3 & 4:** The standard deviation bars are quite small. Report confidence intervals or perform statistical significance tests to demonstrate the robustness of the observed improvements.
*   **Algorithm 1:** The algorithm description is concise but lacks details. Include pseudocode for the IPA gradient estimation and the discretization process.

**Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

*   The q-KG method represents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian optimization.
*   The empirical results demonstrate promising performance gains over existing methods.
*   The introduction of IPA enhances the practical applicability of the method.

**Con Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from several methodological weaknesses, including a lack of rigorous comparison, vague implementation details, and an insufficiently justified discretization strategy.
*   The writing is dense and the mathematical notation is sometimes unclear.
*   The related work discussion is incomplete.
*   The lack of detailed experimental setup hinders reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea behind q-KG is promising and the empirical results are encouraging, the paper’s numerous flaws prevent a positive recommendation for acceptance. The lack of methodological rigor, combined with the writing style and implementation details, create a significant barrier to understanding and replicating the results. A major revision addressing these concerns is necessary before this work could be considered for publication at NIPS. Specifically, the authors need to provide a much more detailed and rigorous experimental evaluation, clarify the implementation details, and improve the clarity and organization of the paper. I recommend **rejection** at this time.
