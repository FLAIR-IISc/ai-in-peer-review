## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO). The authors address the challenge of efficiently selecting a batch of points to evaluate in parallel, extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) approach to the parallel setting. The key idea is to maximize the expected incremental value of a batch of evaluations, formulating this as a novel acquisition function (q-KG).  Crucially, they tackle the computational complexity of optimizing q-KG by developing an efficient gradient estimation technique based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The authors demonstrate through synthetic and real-world experiments that q-KG consistently outperforms existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

**Relationship to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself well within the existing literature on parallel BO. It correctly identifies limitations of earlier approaches like parallel EI and UCB which often rely on greedy iterative selection of points. The authors emphasize the distinction of q-KG – considering the entire batch collectively and optimizing a joint acquisition function.  The connection to the sequential KG method is clearly explained, highlighting the generalization to the parallel case and the added complexity of Monte Carlo sampling. The comparison to recent work like [26] (nested batch KG) is fair and points out important differences in computational efficiency and problem setting.  The referencing is generally strong, covering relevant BO and parallel optimization papers.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The q-KG acquisition function represents a principled and theoretically grounded approach to parallel BO, extending the well-established KG method.  The focus on maximizing the *expected incremental value* is a strong conceptual contribution.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The development of the IPA-based gradient estimation is a significant achievement. Maximizing q-KG directly would be intractable for even moderate batch sizes, and this technique makes the method practical.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling. Consistent improvements over existing methods on both synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in the presence of noise, demonstrate the effectiveness of q-KG. The inclusion of both noise-free and noisy settings is valuable.
* **Clarity of Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The derivation of q-KG and the explanation of IPA are relatively clear, although some mathematical details are delegated to the supplementary materials (which is acceptable).
* **Code Availability:**  Providing code is a major strength, promoting reproducibility and allowing others to build upon this work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization of A:** While the method for discretizing the search space `A` using samples from the posterior is reasonable, the sensitivity of the results to the number of samples (M) is not thoroughly investigated.  A more rigorous analysis of the impact of this discretization would be beneficial.  How does performance scale with dimensionality and what are guidelines for selecting M?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t explicitly discuss the sensitivity of q-KG to its own hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate of the gradient ascent optimizer).  A discussion of hyperparameter tuning strategies for q-KG itself would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** While IPA improves efficiency, the computational cost of gradient estimation for q-KG remains significant, especially for high-dimensional problems and large batch sizes. A detailed analysis of the computational complexity (e.g., scaling with problem dimension and batch size) compared to other parallel BO methods would be useful.
* **Limited Comparison:**  The comparison to other methods is solid, but it would be interesting to see a comparison to more recent parallel BO algorithms that have emerged since the NIPS 2016 submission date.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound method for parallel BO.
* The computational challenges are addressed effectively through a clever use of IPA.
* The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of q-KG over existing methods.
* The code availability facilitates reproducibility and further research.
* The paper fills a gap in the literature by extending the KG method to the parallel setting.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The sensitivity of the results to the discretization of the search space is not adequately addressed.
* The computational cost of q-KG, while improved by IPA, remains a concern for large-scale problems.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of its own hyperparameter sensitivity.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The q-KG method is a well-motivated and effective approach to parallel BO, and the authors have successfully addressed the key computational challenges. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh them.  I believe this paper is a good fit for NIPS and recommend **acceptance**.
