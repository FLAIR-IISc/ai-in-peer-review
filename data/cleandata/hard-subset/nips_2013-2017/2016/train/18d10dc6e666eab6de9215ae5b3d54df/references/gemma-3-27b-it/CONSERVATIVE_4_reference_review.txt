This paper introduces a novel parallel Bayesian optimization method, q-KG, motivated by a decision-theoretic analysis. The core idea – extending the knowledge gradient to the batch setting – is a reasonable direction, building on existing work in sequential BO. However, while the presented methodology appears sound, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence that q-KG represents a significant advancement over existing parallel BO methods, particularly given its added complexity.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical derivations are presented reasonably clearly, and the algorithm is described in a straightforward manner. The related work section provides a decent overview of the field, although it could benefit from more critical assessment of the limitations of existing parallel BO approaches, framing the motivation for q-KG more effectively. The inclusion of code is a positive aspect.

However, the key weakness lies in the empirical evaluation. While the results demonstrate q-KG often outperforms existing methods, the improvements are not consistently substantial across all experiments. The figures showing the mean regret curves often exhibit overlapping confidence intervals, making it difficult to definitively claim superiority. The gains appear more pronounced in the noisy settings, which is noted by the authors, but the justification for *why* q-KG is better suited to noisy evaluations is somewhat lacking. A more in-depth analysis of the behavior of q-KG in the presence of noise would be beneficial. Furthermore, the experiments, while covering both synthetic and real-world problems, feel somewhat limited in scope. More challenging, high-dimensional problems, or comparisons to more recent parallel BO algorithms, would strengthen the claims. The justification for choosing the discretization parameter M=1000 also feels somewhat arbitrary.

The authors’ use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute the gradient of q-KG is a solid contribution, addressing a key computational bottleneck. However, the explanation of IPA is dense and could be made more accessible to readers unfamiliar with the technique. The computational complexity analysis, while implicitly present, could be made more explicit.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The method is theoretically sound and builds upon established Bayesian optimization principles.
*   The IPA-based gradient computation is a novel and valuable contribution.
*   The code is publicly available, promoting reproducibility.
*   The method shows promise, particularly in noisy environments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical improvements over existing methods are not consistently large enough to justify the increased complexity.
*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope and lacks a more thorough analysis of the results.
*   The explanation of IPA could be clearer.
*   The performance gains in the noisy settings require more theoretical or empirical justification.

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. However, the empirical evidence provided is not compelling enough to demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods. I believe the paper, as it stands, falls short of NIPS standards. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit in the future. Specifically, a more extensive and rigorous experimental evaluation, a clearer explanation of IPA, and a more thorough analysis of the performance in noisy settings are needed to strengthen the claims made in the paper. The authors should consider including comparisons to more recent and competitive parallel BO algorithms.
