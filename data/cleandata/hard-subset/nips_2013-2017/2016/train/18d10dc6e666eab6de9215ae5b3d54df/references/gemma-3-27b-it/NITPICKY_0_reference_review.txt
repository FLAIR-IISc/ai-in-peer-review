This paper presents a novel parallel Bayesian optimization method, q-KG, based on a decision-theoretic analysis of information gain. The core idea of maximizing expected incremental value in the parallel setting is appealing and builds upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient framework. However, despite the novelty of the approach and promising empirical results, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet cumulatively significant, flaws in presentation, technical detail, and rigor.

The introduction is reasonably well-written, but lacks sufficient motivation for the specific choice of q-KG over existing parallel BO methods. The claim of outperforming “other” parallel BO algorithms is vague and lacks quantifiable context. While the authors mention noisy evaluations, the emphasis feels somewhat secondary, given its prominence in the experimental results. 

Section 2, "Related Work," is overly verbose and suffers from a lack of critical assessment. Several papers are *listed* rather than *discussed* in relation to the authors’ contribution. For instance, the distinction between methods constructing batches greedily and the proposed joint optimization isn't clearly highlighted. The explanation of existing methods is sometimes imprecise – for example, describing GP-BUCB as not requiring Monte Carlo sampling is technically true, but it doesn't highlight the trade-off with potentially suboptimal exploration. The paper also fails to adequately acknowledge [26] in the context of nested batch knowledge gradients, dismissing it largely due to the application domain, which feels arbitrary.

Section 3 provides a standard review of Gaussian Processes, but includes an unnecessary equation numbering (3.1) that does not appear to be referenced anywhere else. The notation is generally consistent, but the repeated use of potentially ambiguous symbols like 'q' (batch size, also used as an index) could be improved.

Section 4, the core of the paper, contains several issues. The derivation of the q-KG factor (4.1) is presented without sufficient explanation of the underlying assumptions. The connection to the Bayes-optimal sequential KG is stated, but the reasoning behind its extension to the parallel case isn't fully articulated. The claim that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under certain conditions is valid but could be presented more concisely. More importantly, the jump from the conceptual definition of q-KG to the computational challenge feels abrupt.

Section 5, detailing the computational approach, is where the paper truly falters. While IPA is a reasonable technique, its application is presented with a level of hand-waving that is unacceptable for NIPS. The derivation of the gradient (5.3) is merely stated, with a promise of details in the supplementary materials – this is insufficient. The discretization strategy (5.4) is also described superficially, and the choice of M = 1000 feels arbitrary without justification. The discussion lacks an analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method.  The repeated mention of "infinitesimal perturbation analysis" (IPA) without a proper citation within the section is a noticeable omission.

Section 6, the experimental evaluation, provides some evidence of q-KG’s effectiveness, but it’s marred by inconsistencies and a lack of detailed analysis. The experimental setup is described briefly. The choice of hyperparameters for the competing methods isn’t discussed, raising concerns about fair comparison. While the authors state q-KG “performs especially well” in noisy settings, the magnitude of the improvement isn’t always compelling.  The figures are of relatively low resolution and some labels are small and difficult to read. The claim that the automatic algorithm (q-KG) improves the test error on the CIFAR10 dataset to around 11% while the “human expert” achieved 14% is problematic; the baseline should be clearly established and referenced.

The acknowledgement section is standard. The conclusion is adequate but could be more concise.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to parallel Bayesian optimization.
*   Promising empirical results, particularly in noisy settings.
*   Potential for significant impact if the computational challenges can be addressed.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor technical flaws and inconsistencies.
*   Lack of rigor in the derivation and presentation of the computational method.
*   Insufficiently detailed experimental analysis.
*   Overly verbose and lacking critical assessment in the related work section.
*   Poor figure quality and resolution.
*   Unjustified claims about improvements compared to "human experts".

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, the numerous flaws in its presentation and technical detail significantly detract from its impact. The lack of rigor, combined with the superficial treatment of the computational aspects, raises serious concerns about the reproducibility and generalizability of the proposed method. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should address the aforementioned issues thoroughly and resubmit to a future conference. A significantly revised version with a more rigorous derivation, detailed experimental analysis, and improved clarity is needed to meet the standards of NIPS.
