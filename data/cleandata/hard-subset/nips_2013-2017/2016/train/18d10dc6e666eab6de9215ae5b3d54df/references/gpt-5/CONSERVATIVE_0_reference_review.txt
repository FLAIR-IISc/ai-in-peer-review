Summary
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The acquisition is defined as the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after observing a batch, i.e., a Bayes-optimal one-step lookahead for minimizing minx µ(n)(x). Because closed-form evaluation is intractable for q > 1, the authors estimate q-KG via Monte Carlo using a reparameterization (µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z(1:q))Zq) and optimize it by stochastic gradients derived via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). For continuous A, they discretize the inner minimization set by repeatedly sampling candidate minimizers from the GP posterior (as in PES) and augmenting with previously sampled points and the current batch. Empirically, q-KG is compared to parallel EI variants (MOE’s implementation, a Spearmint heuristic) and UCB-based parallel methods (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic test functions and on hyperparameter tuning for MNIST logistic regression and a CIFAR-10 CNN. The authors report consistent improvements, particularly under noisy observations. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
- Extends sequential KG (Frazier et al., 2008; Scott et al., 2011) to batch selection and connects to parallel EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger, 2013; Marmin et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2016).
- Relates to parallel ES/PES (Hernández-Lobato et al., 2014; Shah & Ghahramani, 2015).
- Distinguishes from UCB-based batch methods (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), which target cumulative regret.

Strengths
- Decision-theoretic acquisition: q-KG is a principled one-step Bayes-optimal criterion for a batch when the final decision is min of the posterior mean, and it reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless “recommend-from-evaluated set” case.
- Computational contribution: a clean reparameterization and an IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator (for finite A) enabling joint batch optimization.
- Noise-aware behavior: the formulation naturally handles observation noise; experiments suggest notable gains in noisy settings.
- Practicality: open-source implementation; empirical validation on both synthetic functions and nontrivial ML tuning tasks.
- Clear connection to established baselines and literature.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical guarantees are limited. The Bayes-optimality is strictly one-step and for the inner finite A. There are no convergence results, regret bounds, or analysis of discretization bias when using evolving finite sets An for continuous A. The gradient estimator is shown unbiased only for finite A; in the proposed algorithm, An depends on z(1:q) (it includes z and re-sampled candidates), which can break differentiability and unbiasedness. This coupling and any impact on optimization are not analyzed.
- Discretization details are underspecified. “Sampling from the global optima of the posterior” is nontrivial (as in PES) and implementation choices (sampler, number of samples, initialization) matter a lot. Using M = 1000 points in up to 6D problems may be coarse; sensitivity to M and to the number of Monte Carlo samples for Zq is not explored.
- Computational cost not quantified. There is no wall-clock comparison or complexity analysis (as a function of q, M, number of MC samples). IPA requires differentiating through Cholesky and kernel matrices; per-iteration overhead could be substantial vs. parallel EI/PES, especially in higher d or larger q. Claims of “efficient optimization” are not supported by runtime profiles or scaling studies.
- Experimental validation is somewhat limited and potentially unfair:
  - Missing key baselines: no comparison to parallel PES (Shah & Ghahramani, 2015) or to more recent parallel Thompson sampling variants; only UCB and EI are included.
  - Heterogeneous codebases (MOE, Spearmint, Gpoptimization) can lead to different kernel choices, hyperparameter learning, constraints handling, and numerical tolerances. It’s not clear the comparisons use identical GP priors, ARD settings, and MLE routines across methods.
  - Scale and dimensionality are modest (d ≤ 6 for synthetic tasks; 4–8 hyperparameters in tuning). No study of larger d, larger batch sizes, or asynchronous settings.
  - No ablation studies (e.g., effect of q, M, Monte Carlo budget, IPA step sizes/optimizers) or robustness to misspecified noise.
- Clarity issues: several technical steps are deferred to the supplement (e.g., conditions for IPA, derivatives of Cholesky), but the main text leaves key pieces vague (how An is formed “by sampling from posterior optima,” how many MC samples are used, how ties in the argmin are handled, etc.). The double use of A (inner objective vs. outer feasible region) is a potential source of confusion despite a brief note; a more explicit notation would help.

Quality
Technically solid at a high level, but important gaps remain: (i) justification of the discretization-and-optimize scheme and its gradient properties; (ii) rigorous runtime/scaling characterization; (iii) stronger experimental controls and breadth of baselines. As presented, the method is plausible and well-motivated, but the empirical evidence is not yet sufficient to justify the broad performance claims.

Clarity
Generally readable, but the algorithmic details needed for reproduction (An sampling, MC budgets, optimizer settings) are incomplete. Several editorial issues/typos exist. A clearer separation of the inner discretized minimization and the outer continuous optimization would aid understanding.

Originality
Moderate. Extending KG to batch with an IPA gradient is a natural and useful step, and the reduction to parallel EI in the noiseless restricted setting is a nice connection. However, the core idea is a straightforward generalization of known principles; novelty lies mostly in the gradient estimator and practical implementation.

Significance
Potentially significant if the empirical advantages under noise hold broadly. Batch BO is widely used. However, the lack of comprehensive comparisons, scaling evidence, and ablations tempers confidence in real-world impact.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide runtime comparisons and complexity analysis versus q-EI and q-PES across q and d.
- Include parallel PES (and, if possible, parallel Thompson sampling) as baselines.
- Detail the construction of An: how are posterior minimizers sampled? How many samples? Sensitivity to M? How is the inclusion of z handled to avoid bias/non-differentiability?
- Report MC sample sizes for Zq and gradient estimation; include variance reduction techniques if used.
- Analyze sensitivity to noise variance estimation and to kernel hyperparameters (MLE vs. priors).
- Unify implementations to ensure identical GP settings across methods or reimplement baselines on your framework.
- Provide ablations on q, M, and optimizer choices; study higher-dimensional problems and larger batches.

Pros
- Principled acquisition; coherent with KG theory.
- Practical gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Code release; promising empirical results, especially with noise.

Cons
- Limited theory beyond one-step and finite A; unclear properties under discretization.
- Missing key baselines and ablations; unclear fairness of comparisons.
- No runtime/scalability evidence; modest problem sizes.
- Some clarity/reproducibility gaps.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is sound and promising, but the current evidence and analysis are insufficiently thorough for publication at NIPS. Strengthening the experimental evaluation (especially baselines and runtime), clarifying the discretization/gradient methodology, and adding sensitivity/scaling studies would substantially improve the paper.
