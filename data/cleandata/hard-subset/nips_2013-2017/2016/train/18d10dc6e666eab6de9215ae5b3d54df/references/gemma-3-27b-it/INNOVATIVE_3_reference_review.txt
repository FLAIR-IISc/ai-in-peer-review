This paper presents a novel approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization (BO) based on the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, termed the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea—extending the single-iteration Bayes-optimality of the KG to a batch setting—is a strong and compelling one. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing parallel BO methods (often greedy or focusing on immediate regret) and propose a method explicitly considering the collective benefit of a batch of evaluations. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and, crucially, the development of an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) are significant technical contributions. The empirical results, demonstrating consistent improvements over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings, are promising.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The q-KG acquisition function represents a genuinely new approach to parallel BO, moving beyond iterative single-point optimization. The decision-theoretic justification and extension of KG to the parallel setting are conceptually sound and offer a refreshing perspective.
*   **Technical Contribution:** The IPA-based gradient estimation is a crucial enabling factor for practical implementation. This allows scaling to larger batch sizes and complex problems. The connection to existing KG and EI frameworks is clearly explained.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The consistent outperformance of q-KG across both synthetic and real-world problems (logistic regression and CNN tuning) demonstrates its effectiveness. The particular advantage in noisy evaluations is a valuable characteristic.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical derivations, while complex, are presented in a reasonably accessible manner.
*   **Code Availability:** Providing code is a definite plus, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Discretization Dependence:** The performance of the method is linked to the choice of how to discretize the search space *A* (using AMn). While the proposed approach of sampling from the posterior is sensible, the sensitivity of the results to the size and generation method of AMn is not thoroughly explored.
*   **Computational Cost:** Despite the IPA improvement, the method is likely still computationally demanding, especially in high-dimensional spaces and for large batch sizes. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability limitations would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the derivation of the q-KG is sound, a more in-depth theoretical analysis of its convergence properties or regret bounds would strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** There is very little discussion of hyperparameter selection, particularly for the GP kernel and the optimization procedure of q-KG itself. Providing insights into how these parameters were chosen would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to parallel BO.
*   The technical contributions (IPA-based gradient estimation) are significant.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The method’s sensitivity to the discretization of the search space is a potential concern.
*   The computational cost could limit its applicability to certain problems.
*   The lack of extensive theoretical analysis weakens the paper's overall rigor.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The novelty of the approach, combined with the technical ingenuity and promising empirical results, outweighs the minor weaknesses. While a more thorough investigation of the discretization scheme and computational cost would be valuable, the current work is sufficient to warrant acceptance. I would rate this as a **strong accept**. The paper prioritizes a bold and innovative idea over absolute methodological perfection, which aligns perfectly with NIPS’s values. The authors have successfully introduced a new direction for research in parallel BO, and I anticipate that this work will inspire further advancements in the field.
