Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch/parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to selecting q points jointly. The acquisition is defined as the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after observing a batch of q evaluations. The authors show that, in the noiseless setting with the final recommendation constrained to sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG is intractable in closed form for q > 1, they (i) estimate the acquisition via Monte Carlo by reparameterizing the posterior update with a standard normal Zq, and (ii) derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable efficient gradient-based optimization. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization set A adaptively using samples near posterior optima and previously sampled points. Experiments on synthetic test functions and hyperparameter tuning (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR-10 CNN) show q-KG is competitive or superior to parallel EI variants and batch-UCB methods, with especially large gains under observation noise.

This work sits at the intersection of BO with batch selection. It is closely related to KG in the sequential setting (Frazier et al.; Scott et al.) and to parallel EI (including gradient-based q-EI optimization; Wang et al.), as well as batch extensions of UCB and entropy search (e.g., GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE, PPES). The main novelty is a principled, Bayes-optimal (for the one-batch remaining myopic objective) batch extension of KG with a tractable stochastic gradient optimization strategy.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The definition of q-KG as an expected decrement of the posterior mean’s minimum is standard KG logic extended to batches. The reparameterization µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z(1:q)) Zq is correct under GP posteriors, enabling MC estimation. The derivation of an unbiased IPA gradient estimator is plausible and valuable; full details are deferred to the supplement, which is appropriate but means key justifications (e.g., differentiability conditions, handling argmin non-differentiabilities) cannot be fully verified from the main paper.
- Computational method: Using the Cholesky factorization to propagate derivatives is reasonable, but numerical stability and complexity with increasing q and M deserve more discussion. The proposed discretization for the inner minimization is standard in BO but introduces bias; the paper partially mitigates this with adaptive sets.
- Empirics: The experiments demonstrate solid improvements, particularly under noise, which aligns with KG’s strengths. However, several aspects would strengthen the claims: runtime/wall-clock comparisons versus q-EI and PPES, scaling with q and dimension, sensitivity to M and the discretization strategy, and ablations (e.g., finite-difference vs IPA gradients).
- Theory: Beyond the myopic Bayes-optimality for one remaining batch, there are no regret or convergence guarantees, which is common for batch BO but still a limitation.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured, with a helpful algorithm summary and explicit notation. The distinction between the discretized set used to compute q-KG and the continuous feasible set used to optimize z(1:q) is acknowledged but could be clarified further. Minor typos (e.g., “Main Stange,” “th Conference”) and several incomplete references should be fixed. Details on the stochastic optimization procedure (optimizer, step sizes, stopping) are sparse; adding them would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Extending KG to parallel selection with a jointly optimized acquisition and providing a scalable, unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via IPA appears novel. Prior KG work was sequential or restricted to discrete/batch settings without efficient gradients. Related batch acquisitions (q-EI, PPES, batch UCB) differ in objective and often in optimization approach. The reduction to parallel EI under noiseless/restricted recommendation assumptions is a nice unifying insight.

Significance
Batch BO is practically important. A principled, noise-robust batch method that outperforms strong baselines on synthetic and real tasks is valuable. If the computational approach scales well, practitioners could benefit. The lack of comparisons to some contemporary batch methods (e.g., local penalization, Thompson sampling with fantasizing, PPES in practice) and absence of runtime/scaling analyses somewhat limit the demonstrated impact, but the ideas are likely to be adopted and built upon.

Pros
- Principled acquisition with a clear decision-theoretic foundation; elegant connection to EI in a special case.
- Unbiased stochastic gradient estimator that enables efficient joint optimization over a batch.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise, matching KG’s strengths.
- Practical implementation details and released code.

Cons
- Inner minimization relies on discretization; no analysis of discretization bias/sensitivity or alternatives.
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond myopic optimality; no regret or convergence results.
- Empirical evaluation lacks runtime/scaling comparisons and some relevant baselines (e.g., PPES, local penalization, batch Thompson sampling).
- Reproducibility details on stochastic optimization and hyperparameter settings are thin.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide wall-clock and scaling results versus q-EI and PPES as q, d, and M increase.
- Add sensitivity analyses to M and the discretization scheme, and discuss principled choices of M.
- Clarify the stochastic optimizer used for q-KG (learning rates, number of MC samples per gradient, restarts).
- Include additional baselines where feasible (PPES, local penalization, batch Thompson sampling with fantasizing).
- Discuss asynchronous settings and whether the approach extends naturally.

Overall assessment
The paper meets NeurIPS standards on originality and (practical) significance, with generally solid technical quality and clear exposition. The main weaknesses are in the breadth/depth of empirical evaluation and missing theoretical guarantees typical for this area. I lean toward acceptance: the contribution is novel, well-motivated, and useful, and the empirical results are compelling, especially in noisy settings. A stronger empirical section and minor clarifications would make this a very strong paper.
