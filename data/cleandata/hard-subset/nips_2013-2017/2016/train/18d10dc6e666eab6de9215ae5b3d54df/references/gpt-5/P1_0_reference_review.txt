Summary
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization (BO) that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. q-KG is framed as the expected one-step Bayes-optimal improvement in the minimum posterior mean attainable after observing a batch of q noisy evaluations. The authors show that in the noise-free case with the final recommendation restricted to evaluated points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Because q-KG has no closed form for q > 1, the paper develops a Monte Carlo estimator and an efficient gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based optimization of the batch locations. For continuous domains, they approximate the inner minimizations by discretizing A using samples from the GP posterior’s optima (as in entropy search). Empirically, q-KG outperforms or matches strong baselines (parallel EI variants, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic functions and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR-10), with especially marked gains under observation noise. An open-source implementation is provided.

Relation to prior work
The work is squarely within BO with GP surrogates. It builds on KG for sequential BO (Frazier et al.; Scott et al.) and relates to batch BO methods: parallel EI (Ginsbourger et al.; Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.), “constant liar” (Ginsbourger et al.), UCB-based batch policies (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), and (parallel) predictive entropy search (Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani). A recent nested-batch KG for discrete spaces (Wang et al.) is discussed. The main novelty is a practical, differentiable stochastic estimator for batch KG over continuous spaces with an efficient gradient via IPA.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The one-step Bayes-optimal derivation is standard for KG; the reduction to parallel EI in the stated special case is correct and reassuring. The Monte Carlo estimator and IPA-based gradient estimator are plausible and well-motivated; assuming differentiable mean/kernel, the interchange of expectation and differentiation is standard but should be justified (the paper points to the supplement). The use of Cholesky factors to parameterize the conditional update is numerically stable.
- A few technical clarifications are needed:
  - In the derivative expression for g, the first term ∂/∂zij µ(n)(x*(before)) should be zero since µ(n) does not depend on z—please clarify if this is a typo or if µ(n) is being redefined contextually.
  - The gradient through the “min over A” is only a.s. differentiable (ties). Please state how non-differentiable cases are handled (subgradient, tie-breaking) and the measure-zero assumptions.
  - Complexity: provide explicit per-iteration cost in terms of q, M (discretization size), and number of Monte Carlo samples S. For example, computing σ̃ and its gradient scales with O(q^3) (Cholesky) and O(M q^2) per Z sample; this helps practitioners choose q and M.
  - Discretization: sampling “global optima of the posterior distribution of the GP” references ES/PES. Please detail how AM_n is constructed (draw function samples from the GP posterior, optimize each, etc.), choice of M, and its effect on bias/variance of q-KG.
- Empirics: The experiments are broad and suggest strong performance, especially with noise. However, wall-clock costs are not reported—this is important given the increased computational burden versus EI/UCB. Also, an ablation on q, M, and the number of Monte Carlo samples would illuminate robustness/performance trade-offs. A comparison to (parallel) ES/PES would strengthen the case further.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is heavy but standard for GP BO. Good effort to distinguish the two roles of A (optimization over z versus inner discretization), though this section could be tightened. Minor issues:
- Fix typos (e.g., “Main Stange,” “gaussian” capitalization).
- Clarify the final recommendation: argmin over µ on continuous A—how is this solved in practice (multi-start local search)?
- Explicitly state IPA conditions and where proofs can be found.

Originality
Moderately high. KG in sequential settings is known, and batch EI/ES and UCB-based batch policies exist. The contribution is a new collective batch acquisition grounded in KG together with an efficient stochastic gradient approach to optimize it over continuous domains. Prior “batch KG” work has focused on discrete sets or nested structures; this paper advances practicality and scalability in continuous spaces.

Significance
High for the BO community. Batch BO is important in modern ML settings with parallel resources. q-KG’s principled handling of noise and collective optimization of batches is compelling. If the computational overhead is acceptable (and this should be quantified), practitioners are likely to adopt it. The empirical gains in noisy settings are particularly meaningful.

Pros
- Clear decision-theoretic grounding; connection to EI in special cases.
- Practical gradient-based optimization via IPA, avoiding inner greedy heuristics.
- Strong empirical results, including realistic hyperparameter tuning tasks.
- Open-source implementation.

Cons
- Missing runtime comparisons and scalability analysis versus baselines.
- No comparison to (parallel) ES/PES, a key information-theoretic baseline.
- Some technical clarity gaps (derivative of the “before” term; handling non-differentiability; details of discretization).
- No theoretical regret/convergence analysis (not required, but a brief discussion would be valuable).

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please clarify the derivative of µ(n)(x*(before)) w.r.t. z and how non-differentiable cases are treated.
- Provide per-iteration wall-clock times for all methods and scaling with q and M.
- Detail the construction of AM_n and sensitivity to M; provide an ablation on M and Monte Carlo samples.
- Compare against (parallel) ES/PES, or justify its omission.
- Report sensitivity to kernel choice and hyperparameter refitting frequency; does refitting each iteration materially affect performance/cost?

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a principled and practically useful batch BO method with strong empirical evidence and a sound computational approach. Addressing the clarity points and adding runtime/scalability comparisons (and ideally a PES comparison) would make it even stronger.
