Summary
This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to selecting q points jointly. The acquisition equals the expected reduction in the GP posterior predictive minimum after one batch. The authors derive a Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG and propose to optimize it via stochastic gradients using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization set adaptively using samples of putative optima of posterior draws. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and on hyperparameter tuning (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10) indicate that q-KG is competitive or better than parallel EI and UCB-based batch policies, with particularly strong gains under noise.

Relation to prior work: The work is closely related to (i) sequential KG [Frazier et al.], (ii) multipoint EI and its gradient-based optimizers [Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.; Wang et al. (MOE)], and (iii) batch ES/PES [Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani], and UCB-based batch policies [Desautels et al.; Contal et al.]. The main novelty is a batch KG objective with an IPA-based estimator enabling joint optimization over q points in continuous domains.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: extending KG to the batch setting with a clear decision-theoretic interpretation (Bayes-optimal with one batch remaining).
- Computational contribution: stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG via IPA, avoiding heavy nested MC optimizations and enabling gradient-based optimizers.
- Practical relevance: batch BO is important; the empirical results suggest robustness under noisy evaluations where q-KG is particularly strong.
- Code availability is a plus for reproducibility.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Theoretical guarantees are limited. While KG is myopically Bayes-optimal with one step remaining, the paper provides no regret, consistency, or asymptotic guarantees for repeated use or for the batch variant. This is not necessarily fatal, but the paper should at least discuss limitations and provide a complexity and convergence analysis for the stochastic optimization (e.g., conditions for unbiasedness of the gradient estimator and convergence of multi-start stochastic gradient ascent).
- IPA through a min over a discretized set: g involves argmin over A_n; differentiation through argmin is non-smooth. The text briefly asserts differentiability “under the condition that µ and K are continuously differentiable,” which is insufficient because the min introduces non-differentiability (ties are measure-zero but need explicit handling). A precise statement (almost sure differentiability, tie-breaking, dominated convergence conditions) and proof sketch should be included, not only relegated to the supplement.
- Discretization of A: The method “draws M samples from the global optima of the posterior distribution” but the procedure is not specified sufficiently to reproduce (e.g., do you sample functions from the GP and minimize each to get a sample of argmins; if so, how are those minima computed efficiently; how do you scale with d?). This is a critical component and needs algorithmic detail and complexity analysis. Also, the impact of M on quality and compute should be studied (ablation).
- Computational complexity: No cost model is provided. Evaluating q-KG requires O(M q^2) to form K^n(x, z) for M candidates per gradient step plus Cholesky of q×q matrices, nested within MC samples. Scaling in n, d, M, q, and the number of MC samples and gradient steps should be made explicit, with wall-clock comparisons to baselines.
- Reduction to q-EI: The reduction shown in the noise-free case is intuitive but not rigorous as written. The derivation mixes µ(n) at sampled points with y and uses a minimum over y(1:n). Please formalize the argument (conditions under which µ(n)=y at sampled points, handling of ties, and the restriction of final recommendation).
- Hyperparameter learning: The paper re-estimates GP hyperparameters via MLE after each batch. In noisy and noise-free cases, this can be unstable; no details on priors, optimization, restarts, or integration over hyperparameters are provided. Results may be sensitive to these choices; a discussion or sensitivity analysis is needed.
- Experimental rigor: 
  - No comparison to batch ES/PES [18], which is a highly relevant information-theoretic baseline considering batches jointly.
  - Limited exploration of q (results only for q=4); no study of performance vs q or robustness to larger batches.
  - No ablations on M, number of MC samples, step sizes, number of multistarts, or the discretization strategy; no wall-clock or iteration-time measurements.
  - Statistical reporting is minimal; while means and standard deviations are mentioned, there is no detail on seeds, stopping budgets, or significance testing.
  - The CNN tuning description lacks architectural and training specifics (optimizer, data augmentation, early stopping), making reproducibility hard; “improves from 14% to around 11%” is striking but needs careful controls and fairness in compute budgets.

Clarity
- The manuscript contains numerous typographical and formatting issues that materially impede comprehension:
  - Section headers and numbering are inconsistent (e.g., “Parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG)” appears as a section without a number).
  - Algorithm 1: “Main Stange” → “Main Stage”.
  - The “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems…” footer appears mid-text.
  - Equations are sometimes unnumbered or misnumbered; references to (4.1) are followed by “.1” subsections (“.1 Estimating q-KG…”).
  - Notation: Z_q is defined; but phrases like “µ(n+q)(x) is itself a random vector” are imprecise (it is a random function of x; the min over A is a random variable).
  - Citations within the text are sometimes awkward (“[10] also proposes an algorithm called ‘constant liar’, which iteratively constructs a batch of points to sample by maximizing singleevaluation…”).
- The references section is incomplete and inconsistent: many entries lack authors, venues, years, or have broken URLs (e.g., MOE link), inconsistent capitalization, and missing details. This must be fixed for a camera-ready.

Originality
- Extending KG to batches and providing an efficient gradient estimator is a meaningful and, to my knowledge, novel contribution in 2016. However, some methodological components (Monte Carlo evaluation over discretized sets, joint optimization via stochastic gradients) are shared with parallel EI literature; clear differentiation is mostly provided but could be strengthened by including parallel ES baselines.

Significance
- Batch BO is impactful, and the empirical improvements under noise are promising. However, the absence of comparisons to PES and the limited experimental analysis reduce confidence in broad significance. With stronger experiments and clearer computational analysis, the contribution could be substantial.

Pros
- Clear decision-theoretic objective; recovers known methods in special cases.
- Practical stochastic gradient estimator for joint batch selection.
- Strong empirical results under noise; code released.

Cons
- Insufficient theoretical and computational analysis (unbiasedness conditions, complexity, convergence).
- Ambiguous and under-specified discretization strategy; no ablations.
- Missing key baseline (batch ES/PES); limited exploration of q and scaling.
- Numerous clarity, formatting, and reference issues; some derivations are informal.

Questions for the authors
1. Precisely define and analyze the discretization of A: how are “samples from the global optima of the posterior” obtained? What are the per-iteration costs and failure modes?
2. Provide conditions under which the IPA estimator is unbiased; how do you handle non-differentiability due to argmin? Any variance reduction?
3. How many MC samples and gradient steps are used per batch? What is the wall-clock overhead vs parallel EI and PES?
4. Can you include comparisons to parallel ES/PES? 
5. How does performance vary with q and M? Any results in higher dimensions (d>6)?

Recommendation
At present, I recommend rejection. The core idea is promising and likely impactful, but the paper requires substantial improvements in rigor (both theory and computation), experimental completeness (key baselines, ablations, scaling), and presentation (clarity and references). With these addressed, this work would be a strong candidate for publication.
