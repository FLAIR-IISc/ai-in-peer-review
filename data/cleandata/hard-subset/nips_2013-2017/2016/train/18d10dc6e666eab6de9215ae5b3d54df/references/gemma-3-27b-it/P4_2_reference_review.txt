This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization, extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient approach to parallel evaluations. The core idea is to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected incremental value of a measurement, considering the entire batch collectively rather than greedily. The authors derive an acquisition function and address the computational challenge of its optimization using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method is evaluated on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, demonstrating improved performance, particularly in noisy scenarios.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental results. The connection to existing work in Bayesian Optimization, particularly parallel BO algorithms like parallel EI and UCB, is well-established. The authors correctly identify the limitations of iterative greedy approaches and position q-KG as a method that considers the batch as a whole. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function appears theoretically sound, building upon the sequential Knowledge Gradient framework. The use of IPA to efficiently compute gradients is a significant contribution, making the method practical for larger batch sizes. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including both synthetic benchmarks and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, covering both noise-free and noisy evaluation scenarios. The comparison against several existing parallel BO methods is thorough and highlights the advantages of q-KG. The code availability is a positive aspect.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the explanation of the core idea is clear, the mathematical details, especially surrounding the IPA-based gradient computation, are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The notation, particularly in Section 5, is complex and requires careful reading. While the authors mention the discretization of the domain A in Section 5.3, the impact of the discretization level (M=1000) on the performance of q-KG isn't thoroughly investigated or discussed.  A more detailed ablation study exploring the sensitivity to this parameter would be valuable.  The discussion of the limitations of q-KG is relatively brief.  For example, the computational cost, even with IPA, could become prohibitive in very high-dimensional spaces, and a discussion of scalability would be helpful.  Furthermore, while the empirical results demonstrate improvements, the magnitude of those improvements isn't always dramatic, and a more detailed statistical analysis of the results (e.g., confidence intervals, p-values) would strengthen the claims. The reference list is extensive but lacks citations for some key concepts; for instance, the discussion of IPA could benefit from citing more foundational papers on stochastic gradient estimation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** q-KG presents a novel approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization by considering the entire batch collectively, unlike greedy methods.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is based on a well-established decision-theoretic framework.
* **Technical Contribution:** The use of IPA to efficiently compute gradients addresses a significant computational challenge.
* **Empirical Performance:**  q-KG demonstrates superior performance compared to existing methods on a variety of benchmarks, particularly in noisy settings.
* **Code Availability:** Increases reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Complexity:** The mathematical details are dense and may be difficult for some readers to follow.
* **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the scalability and sensitivity of q-KG to key parameters.
* **Statistical Rigor:** The presentation of empirical results could be strengthened with more detailed statistical analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The q-KG method offers a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel BO, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of clarity and statistical rigor, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and provide a more thorough discussion of the method's limitations and scalability.  Specifically, adding more intuition to the IPA explanation and performing a sensitivity analysis of the discretization parameter would be beneficial.
