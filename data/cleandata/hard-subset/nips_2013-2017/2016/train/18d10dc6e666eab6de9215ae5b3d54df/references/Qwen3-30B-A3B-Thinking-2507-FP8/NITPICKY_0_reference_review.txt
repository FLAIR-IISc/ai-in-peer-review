

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that generalizes the knowledge gradient acquisition function to the batch setting. The authors claim to derive q-KG using decision-theoretic analysis and propose an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). While the paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian optimization, I identify several significant technical flaws and presentation issues that undermine the paper's contribution and prevent it from being accepted at NIPS.

## Major Technical Issues

1. **Incorrect Derivation of q-KG**: The paper claims that q-KG reduces to parallel EI when evaluations are noise-free and the final recommendation is restricted to previous sampling decisions. However, their derivation (equation 4.1) is mathematically incorrect. The expression "min x∈x(1:n) µ(n)(x)− min x∈x(1:n)∪z(1:q) µ(n+q)(x)" is ambiguous and does not correctly represent the parallel EI acquisition function. The paper uses "min y(1:n)" which is not defined, and the final step "min y(1:n) − min x∈z(1:q) µ(n+q)(x)" is not equivalent to the parallel EI acquisition function. This fundamental error casts serious doubt on the paper's theoretical foundation.

2. **Mischaracterization of Knowledge Gradient**: The paper claims q-KG is "Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the predictor of the GP if only one decision is remaining." However, the standard knowledge gradient (KG) is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the expected simple regret, not the minimum of the predictor. The paper conflates these two concepts, leading to confusion about the theoretical properties of the proposed method.

3. **Computational Complexity Claims**: The paper states that "Naively maximizing q-KG would be extremely computationally intensive, especially when q is large, and so, in this paper, we develop a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to evaluate q-KG's gradient efficiently." However, the paper does not provide any complexity analysis of the proposed IPA method or how it compares to alternative methods for computing gradients of parallel acquisition functions. Without this analysis, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed method is truly efficient.

## Methodological and Presentation Issues

1. **Ambiguous Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout, particularly with respect to the indexing of points (e.g., "z(1:q)" vs "z(1:q)"). This makes it difficult to follow the mathematical derivations. For example, in equation (5.1), the notation "z(1:q)" is used inconsistently with "z(1:q)".

2. **Lack of Statistical Significance**: The experimental results (Section 6) claim that "q-KG is significantly better on Rosenbrock3, Ackley5 and Hartmann6" but provide no statistical tests to support this claim. The paper reports mean and standard deviation, but without significance testing, it's impossible to determine if the observed differences are statistically meaningful.

3. **Incomplete Experimental Setup**: The paper states "We set M = 1000 to discretize the domain following the strategy in Section 5.3" but does not justify why M=1000 was chosen or how this choice affects the results. It's unclear whether this discretization is sufficient or if larger M would yield better results.

4. **Inconsistent Reference to Prior Work**: The paper cites [19] as a reference for Bayesian optimization, but [19] is the paper itself (NIPS 2016 proceedings), which is confusing and unprofessional. The paper also fails to adequately cite related work on parallel Bayesian optimization, particularly the work by Wang et al. (2016) on parallel Bayesian global optimization.

## Minor but Significant Issues

1. **Formatting Inconsistencies**: The paper has inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., in equation 3.1), which makes it difficult to read. Some equations are poorly formatted, with inconsistent use of parentheses and brackets.

2. **Missing Theoretical Justification**: The paper claims "q-KG provides especially large value when function evaluations are noisy" but provides no theoretical justification for this claim. It's unclear why the method would be particularly effective in noisy settings.

3. **Lack of Computational Cost Analysis**: The paper does not provide a comparison of the computational cost of q-KG versus other methods, which is essential for a paper claiming to propose an efficient method.

## Strengths

The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian optimization (batch acquisition functions) and provides a reasonable experimental comparison with several baselines. The idea of generalizing knowledge gradient to the batch setting is conceptually interesting, and the paper does present some empirical results showing the method's effectiveness.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses an interesting problem in Bayesian optimization, the technical errors in the theoretical foundation, the lack of statistical significance in the experiments, and the numerous presentation issues significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The incorrect derivation of the relationship between q-KG and parallel EI is particularly problematic, as it suggests the authors may not fully understand their own method.

The paper is not technically sound, and the claims made are not well-supported by the analysis or experiments. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper due to these substantial technical flaws, even though the problem addressed is of interest to the community.

I recommend rejection, as the paper's fundamental theoretical errors prevent it from making a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors would need to completely revise the theoretical foundation and provide rigorous mathematical justification for their claims before the paper could be considered for publication.
