

# Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "Parallel Knowledge Gradient" (q-KG) that generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient approach to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that measures the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor after evaluating a batch of q points simultaneously. To address the computational challenges of maximizing q-KG, they develop an efficient gradient estimation approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments on synthetic functions and practical machine learning tuning tasks that q-KG consistently outperforms existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods, particularly in noisy settings.

The work builds on prior knowledge gradient methods [7, 17] and extends them to the parallel setting, distinguishing itself from previous approaches that construct batches iteratively by optimizing single-point acquisition functions while holding other points fixed [2, 3, 5, 10, 18, 25]. The authors provide a thorough comparison with relevant literature, including parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration.

## Strengths

**Quality (Strong):** The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated and follows logically from the decision-theoretic perspective. The computational approach using IPA to efficiently estimate the gradient is novel and well-justified, addressing a critical bottleneck in applying the knowledge gradient to batch settings. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show q-KG outperforming baselines, with particularly significant gains in noisy settings. The authors provide code at https://github.com/wujian16/qKG, enhancing reproducibility.

**Clarity (Strong):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly states the problem, contributions, and significance. The background on Gaussian processes is concise yet sufficient for understanding the main results. The methodological sections (q-KG definition, computational approach) are logically structured and well-explained, with appropriate mathematical notation. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for replication, including implementation specifics (M = 1000 for discretization, batch size q = 4, hyperparameter settings).

**Originality (Strong):** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature by generalizing the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting. While prior work has explored parallel Bayesian optimization (e.g., [2, 3, 5, 10, 18, 25]), the authors' approach of optimizing the batch collectively rather than iteratively represents a meaningful advancement. The paper clearly articulates how their method differs from existing approaches (e.g., "The acquisition function we propose considers the batch of points collectively, and we choose the batch to jointly optimize this acquisition function"). The theoretical and computational developments are novel, with the IPA-based gradient estimation technique being particularly innovative.

**Significance (Strong):** Batch Bayesian optimization is a practically important problem, especially for hyperparameter tuning of machine learning models where parallel evaluations can significantly reduce optimization time. The paper demonstrates that q-KG provides consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods, with the most significant gains occurring in noisy settings—where many real-world applications operate. The results on practical machine learning tasks (logistic regression and CNN tuning) demonstrate tangible benefits over existing methods, with q-KG improving test error from 14% to 11% on CIFAR10, a substantial gain. This work advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization and provides practitioners with a more effective tool for parallel optimization.

## Weaknesses

The paper is strong overall, but there are a few minor weaknesses. First, while the authors acknowledge that q-KG requires Monte Carlo sampling and IPA-based gradient estimation (making it computationally more intensive than methods like GP-BUCB or GP-UCB-PE), they could provide more detailed analysis of the computational trade-offs. A comparison of wall-clock time or number of function evaluations per iteration would help practitioners assess whether the performance gains justify the computational cost.

Second, the choice of M = 1000 for discretization in Section 5.3 is not thoroughly justified. While the authors mention this is a standard practice [11, 18], a more detailed discussion of how this parameter scales with problem dimensionality or how it might be tuned for different applications would strengthen the paper.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization by providing a theoretically grounded and computationally efficient method for batch optimization. The quality of the theoretical development, the thorough experimental validation, and the clear presentation make it a strong candidate for NeurIPS. The results are compelling, particularly the consistent outperformance of baselines in noisy settings, which is especially relevant for real-world applications.

The paper meets all four review criteria at a high level:
- **Quality:** Excellent technical foundation, well-supported claims, complete work
- **Clarity:** Exceptionally well-written and organized
- **Originality:** Clear advancement over existing work with novel theoretical and computational contributions
- **Significance:** Addresses a practically important problem with demonstrable improvements over state-of-the-art

The minor weaknesses noted above do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution. I recommend acceptance.

## Pro and Con for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel extension of knowledge gradient to parallel setting with strong theoretical foundation
- Efficient computational approach using IPA to overcome computational bottleneck
- Comprehensive experimental validation on both synthetic and practical problems
- Significant performance gains, especially in noisy settings where real-world applications often operate
- Code availability enhances reproducibility

**Con:**
- Slightly more detailed analysis of computational trade-offs would be beneficial
- Justification for discretization parameter M = 1000 could be strengthened

Overall, this is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to Bayesian optimization and would be valuable to the NeurIPS community.
