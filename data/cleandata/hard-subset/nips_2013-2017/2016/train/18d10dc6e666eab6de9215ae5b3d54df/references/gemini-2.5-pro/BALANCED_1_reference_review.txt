**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), termed the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to the parallel setting, where `q` function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, selecting the batch of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final recommended solution, defined as the minimum of the posterior mean function. This one-step Bayes-optimal formulation distinguishes it from more heuristic approaches to parallel BO. A key technical contribution is an efficient method for maximizing this acquisition function. As direct optimization is computationally intractable, the authors derive an unbiased estimator for the gradient of the q-KG factor using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), enabling the use of standard gradient-based optimizers. The authors evaluate their method on a suite of synthetic benchmark functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), comparing it against several state-of-the-art parallel BO algorithms. The experiments show that q-KG is highly competitive and often outperforms baselines, with a particularly significant advantage in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The paper's primary strength is its principled, decision-theoretic derivation of the q-KG acquisition function. It is a natural and well-motivated extension of the strong sequential KG framework. Unlike many parallel BO methods that rely on greedy heuristics (e.g., iteratively selecting points) or simple extensions of sequential criteria, q-KG values the batch of points collectively based on its expected impact on the final solution quality. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.

2.  **Significant Technical Contribution:** The development of an efficient optimization strategy for q-KG is a crucial and novel contribution. The acquisition function involves an expectation over the posterior predictive distribution, making its direct evaluation and optimization difficult. The use of Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient is an elegant solution that makes the approach practical. This technique of interchanging the gradient and expectation operators is powerful and well-applied here.

3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough empirical study across a variety of domains, including standard synthetic functions and relevant machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks. The results compellingly demonstrate that q-KG performs as well as or better than several strong baselines, including variants of parallel Expected Improvement and parallel UCB.

4.  **Demonstrated Advantage in Noisy Settings:** A standout result is the superior performance of q-KG in the presence of observation noise. This is a critical and common scenario in real-world applications (e.g., tuning models on subsampled data, A/B testing). The paper provides convincing evidence that the performance gap between q-KG and other methods widens in the noisy setting. This aligns with the theoretical advantage of KG-style methods, which decouple the points to be evaluated from the final recommended solution, a property that is especially valuable when observations are noisy.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions**

1.  **Computational Overhead Analysis:** The primary concern with the proposed method is its computational complexity. The optimization of the q-KG acquisition function requires Monte Carlo sampling to estimate the function and its gradient, followed by a gradient-based optimization procedure (e.g., multi-start stochastic gradient ascent) in a `q*d`-dimensional space. This is likely to be significantly more computationally expensive than the baseline methods. The paper does not provide any analysis or reporting of the wall-clock time required to select a batch of points. This is a critical practical consideration for users of BO.
    *   **Suggestion:** I strongly recommend the authors add a section to the appendix (and a brief summary in the main text) discussing the computational complexity. An empirical comparison of the wall-clock time per iteration against the baselines on one of the test problems would be invaluable for understanding the trade-offs involved. How does the optimization time scale with batch size `q` and the number of Monte Carlo samples?

2.  **Sensitivity to Discretization:** The method for handling the inner optimization (`min_x µ(x)`) over a continuous domain `A` relies on a dynamic discretization `An`. The quality of this finite set of points is crucial for the accuracy of the q-KG factor and its gradient. The paper proposes a reasonable strategy for constructing `An` but does not analyze its potential limitations or sensitivity. For instance, how does performance change with the number of sampled points `M`? Is there a risk that this discretization fails to capture important regions of the search space?
    *   **Suggestion:** A sensitivity analysis on the size of the discretization set (`M`) would strengthen the paper's claims. Even a small experiment in the appendix would provide useful insight. Additionally, a brief discussion of the potential failure modes of this discretization approach would be welcome.

3.  **Missing Baseline Comparison:** The related work section mentions the "constant liar" heuristic for parallelizing EI, which is a simple, fast, and often surprisingly effective baseline. Given that the authors compare against a Spearmint implementation of parallel EI, it would have been natural and informative to also include a comparison to the constant liar strategy, which is also implemented in that package.
    *   **Suggestion:** If feasible, adding a comparison to the constant liar heuristic would make the experimental section more complete and further contextualize the performance gains of q-KG.

**Decision**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is theoretically well-founded, the technical approach for optimizing it is novel and elegant, and the empirical results are strong and convincing, particularly in the important noisy setting. The weaknesses identified, primarily concerning computational cost and sensitivity to discretization, are valid points for discussion and future work but do not undermine the core contribution of the paper. The work clearly advances the state of the art in batch Bayesian optimization.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and theoretically sound acquisition function for batch BO.
*   Introduces a sophisticated and efficient optimization method based on IPA to make the approach practical.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance against strong baselines through extensive experiments.
*   Highlights a particularly strong advantage in noisy optimization problems, a key practical setting.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Lack of analysis on the computational overhead, which is likely to be a significant practical limitation.
*   The performance relies on a discretization of the objective's domain, and the sensitivity to this choice is not explored.

I believe the strengths substantially outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
