### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), termed the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The goal is to select a batch of `q` points to evaluate in parallel. The proposed q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic framework and quantifies the expected improvement in the final optimal solution value after observing the outcomes of the `q` parallel evaluations. This extends the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy, which is known to be Bayes-optimal for a single remaining evaluation, to the parallel setting.

A key challenge is that naively optimizing the q-KG acquisition function is computationally intractable. The authors' main technical contribution is a computationally efficient method to maximize q-KG. They formulate the q-KG factor as a Monte Carlo expectation and then derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). This allows for the use of efficient gradient-based methods to find the optimal batch of points. The authors evaluate their method on a suite of synthetic benchmark functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), considering both noise-free and noisy evaluation settings. The empirical results demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms several state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, with particularly strong performance in the presence of observation noise.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Novel Approach:** The generalization of the Knowledge Gradient to the batch setting is a natural and well-motivated extension. Unlike many heuristic approaches to parallel BO that greedily build a batch, q-KG is derived from a decision-theoretic principle of maximizing the expected value of information for the final solution, considering the joint informational value of the entire batch. This provides a strong theoretical foundation for the method.

2.  **Significant Technical Contribution:** The paper goes beyond simply proposing the acquisition function. The development of an efficient optimization strategy using IPA to obtain unbiased stochastic gradients is a non-trivial and crucial contribution that makes the q-KG method practical. This technique for differentiating through the expectation of a minimum of random functions is elegant and could be of broader interest.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering a range of synthetic functions with varying dimensionalities and two relevant, real-world hyperparameter tuning problems. The comparison against multiple strong baselines, including different implementations of parallel Expected Improvement and parallel UCB variants, provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.

4.  **Demonstrated Strength in Noisy Settings:** The paper compellingly shows that the performance advantage of q-KG is particularly pronounced in settings with noisy function evaluations. This is a critical and common scenario in practice, for example, when evaluating machine learning models on a validation subset. The KG framework's focus on the posterior mean (`µ(x)`) rather than just observed function values provides a natural robustness to noise, and the experiments validate this intuition.

### **Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions**

1.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** While the IPA-based optimization is a significant improvement over naive approaches, the method is still computationally intensive, relying on Monte Carlo sampling within a gradient ascent loop. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the computational complexity as a function of batch size `q`, problem dimension `d`, and the number of discretization points `M`. A comparison of wall-clock time against the baselines (especially the non-Monte Carlo methods like GP-UCB-PE) in the experiments would provide valuable practical insight for potential users. How does the optimization time for finding a batch compare to the function evaluation time, which is the motivating bottleneck for BO?

2.  **Dependence on Discretization:** The method's performance relies on the quality of the domain discretization (`An`) used to approximate the `min` operator over the continuous space `A`. The proposed strategy of sampling from the posterior optima is sensible, but the choice of `M=1000` seems somewhat arbitrary. It would be beneficial to include a sensitivity analysis or discussion regarding the impact of `M` on performance. Is `M=1000` sufficient for higher-dimensional problems (e.g., the 8D CNN tuning problem), and how does the required `M` scale with dimension?

3.  **Missing Baselines:** The comparison is strong, but the inclusion of parallel Predictive Entropy Search (PES) [18] would have been a valuable addition. Like q-KG, PES is an information-theoretic method that jointly optimizes the batch of points. Comparing q-KG against PES would better position it among the class of non-greedy, information-based batch acquisition functions.

4.  **Clarity on Gradient Derivation:** Section 5.2, which details the gradient estimation, is very dense. While the supplement likely contains the full derivation, the main text could benefit from more intuition. For instance, a brief explanation of why the interchange of derivative and expectation is valid (a key assumption for IPA) and how the derivative propagates through the `argmin` operation (which is handled implicitly by the structure of `g`) would improve the clarity and accessibility of this core technical contribution.

### **Decision Rationale**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is novel, theoretically well-grounded, and supported by a strong technical contribution that makes it practical. The empirical results are convincing and highlight an important practical advantage in noisy settings. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to areas for potential improvement and further analysis, rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed method or evaluation. The work advances the state of the art in parallel BO and is likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
