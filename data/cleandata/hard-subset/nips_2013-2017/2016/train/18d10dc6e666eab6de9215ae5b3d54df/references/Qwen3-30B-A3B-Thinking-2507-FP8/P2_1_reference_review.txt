

# Review: "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which extends the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors derive q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective that selects a batch of q points to evaluate next that maximizes the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor. They address the computational challenge of optimizing q-KG by developing an efficient method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute its gradient. The paper compares q-KG against several batch BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, GP-UCB-PE) on both synthetic functions and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms other batch BO algorithms, especially in noisy settings.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and provides strong theoretical and empirical support for its claims. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated and correctly extends the sequential KG method to the parallel setting. The use of IPA to efficiently compute the gradient of q-KG is a reasonable and well-justified approach to address the computational challenges. The experimental design is comprehensive, with both synthetic test functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and real-world applications (hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression and CNNs). The results are statistically significant, with multiple runs and appropriate error bars.

The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and provide a clear comparison with relevant baselines. They correctly note that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under specific conditions (noise-free evaluations with final recommendation restricted to sampled points), which helps contextualize their contribution. The implementation details are sufficiently clear to allow for reproducibility, and they provide a code repository.

## Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and motivation, and the related work section provides a thorough comparison with existing methods. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, and the notation is consistent throughout. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear and concise.

The experimental results are presented in a straightforward manner, with appropriate figures showing mean and standard deviation across multiple runs. The discussion of results is well-structured, highlighting both the strengths and limitations of the method.

## Originality

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization by extending the knowledge gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting. While there are previous works on batch BO (e.g., parallel EI, batch UCB), the q-KG approach is distinct in that it considers the batch of points collectively rather than iteratively. This is a significant departure from previous methods that optimize a single point at a time while holding others fixed.

The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous contributions, especially in the "Related work" section. They correctly note that while previous methods (like [2, 5, 3, 18]) have addressed batch BO, they do so by iteratively constructing batches, whereas q-KG optimizes the entire batch jointly. This is a substantive methodological difference.

The paper's approach to discretizing the domain for infinite search spaces (Section 5.3) is also novel in its dynamic adaptation of the candidate set based on the posterior distribution.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high. Batch Bayesian optimization is an increasingly important problem as machine learning applications move toward distributed computing environments where parallel evaluations are common. The paper demonstrates that q-KG consistently outperforms existing batch BO methods, especially in noisy settings where many practical applications (like hyperparameter tuning) operate.

The real-world applications on MNIST and CIFAR10 demonstrate the practical utility of the method. The paper shows that q-KG can improve CNN performance from 14% test error to around 11%, which is a meaningful improvement in a practical setting. The results in noisy settings (Section 6.2) are particularly compelling, showing that q-KG's advantages are amplified when dealing with noisy function evaluations, which is common in many applications.

## Strengths

1. The theoretical foundation of q-KG is strong and well-motivated from a decision-theoretic perspective.
2. The computational approach using IPA to efficiently optimize q-KG is clever and well-justified.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic benchmarks and practical machine learning applications.
4. The results consistently show that q-KG outperforms existing methods, especially in noisy settings.
5. The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the technical contributions.

## Weaknesses

1. The computational complexity of q-KG, while improved by IPA, is still high for large batch sizes (q). The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the computational cost scales with q, which would be important for practical applications where q might be large.
2. The sensitivity of the method to the choice of M (the number of samples for discretization) is not thoroughly explored. The paper sets M=1000, but it's not clear how this choice affects performance.
3. The paper doesn't explore the limitations of the Gaussian process assumption in the context of the real-world applications (e.g., how well does the GP model capture the underlying function for CNN hyperparameter tuning?).
4. While the comparison with [26] (nested batch knowledge gradient) is mentioned, a more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper. The contribution is significant, the methodology is sound, and the experimental results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The paper makes a clear and meaningful advancement in the field of batch Bayesian optimization, which is an important problem with practical relevance to machine learning applications.

The weaknesses identified are important but do not undermine the core contribution of the paper. The authors could address some of these in a revised version (if accepted), but they are not severe enough to warrant rejection.

## Summary of Arguments for Acceptance

* **Strong theoretical foundation** - The q-KG acquisition function is derived from a solid decision-theoretic perspective.
* **Novel approach** - The method differs fundamentally from previous batch BO approaches by optimizing the entire batch jointly.
* **Comprehensive evaluation** - The paper provides thorough empirical results across multiple synthetic functions and practical machine learning applications.
* **Practical impact** - The method shows significant improvements in real-world applications, especially in noisy settings.
* **Well-written** - The paper is clearly organized and presented.

## Summary of Arguments Against Acceptance

* **Computational complexity** - While IPA improves efficiency, the method still has high computational cost for large q.
* **Limited sensitivity analysis** - The paper doesn't thoroughly explore how the method depends on hyperparameters like M.
* **GP assumptions** - The paper doesn't discuss the limitations of the Gaussian process model in practical applications.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and it represents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.
