Summary
The paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization under Gaussian process priors. q-KG is derived from a decision-theoretic viewpoint: for a candidate batch z(1:q), it measures the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after observing that batch, without restricting the final recommendation to be among evaluated points. This generalizes the sequential KG to the parallel setting and coincides with parallel EI in the noiseless case if the recommendation is restricted to sampled points.

Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG is computationally challenging, the authors propose an efficient stochastic gradient-based optimizer using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). They compute q-KG via Monte Carlo over a standard normal Zq using the linear update of the GP posterior mean after a batch, and derive an unbiased estimator of the gradient with respect to the batch locations. To handle continuous domains inside the inner min defining q-KG, they discretize A adaptively by sampling from approximate posterior minimizers (as in PES), augmented with previously sampled points and the current candidate batch.

Empirically, q-KG is compared against parallel EI (two implementations), GP-BUCB and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10), both in noiseless and noisy settings. q-KG is consistently competitive or superior, with particularly strong gains under noise. Code is released.

Quality
- Soundness: The decision-theoretic derivation is clear, and the reduction to parallel EI in the stated special case is correct. The IPA-based gradient estimator is well-motivated; under smoothness of mean/covariance, the interchange of derivative and expectation is standard, though key regularity proofs are deferred to the supplement.
- Methodological completeness: The computational recipe is coherent: MC evaluation, IPA gradient, multi-start stochastic gradient ascent, and adaptive discretization for the inner min. The discretization introduces approximation error not analyzed theoretically but is a common and pragmatic choice in BO.
- Empirical validation: The experiments cover multiple dimensions and both synthetic and practical ML tasks, and include both noiseless and noisy regimes. q-KG shows consistent improvements, especially with noise. However, the study lacks ablations (e.g., sensitivity to discretization size M, number of MC samples, batch size q, and GP hyperparameter learning), detailed wall-clock comparisons, and higher-dimensional cases.
- Reproducibility: Important implementation choices are described, code is available, and GP hyperparameters are fit by MLE. Some details that would aid reproduction (MC sample counts, step sizes, stopping criteria, handling of numerical degeneracy in Cholesky) should be clarified.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. The decision-theoretic framing, the connection to EI, and the computational strategy are presented clearly.
- Some notation and the two uses of “A” (feasible domain vs. discretized set used inside q-KG) can confuse; the text acknowledges this and could streamline by using distinct symbols.
- Important derivations (e.g., formal conditions for unbiasedness of the IPA estimator, gradients through Cholesky) are relegated to the supplement; brief intuition in the main text would help.

Originality
- The paper offers a substantial extension of KG to parallel BO with joint batch optimization. This is more than an incremental tweak: it brings a Bayes-optimal one-step lookahead criterion to batch settings and handles noise natively, without the “lie” heuristics often used in batch construction.
- The IPA-based stochastic gradient for a collective batch acquisition is a useful computational contribution distinct from the existing work on q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.) and parallel ES.
- The relation to nested batch KG on discrete sets is clearly distinguished; here the focus is continuous domains with differentiability enabling efficient gradients.

Significance
- Batch BO is central in practice; a principled and effective acquisition that naturally handles noise is valuable. If adopted, q-KG could influence both practice and follow-up research on batch decision-theoretic acquisitions.
- The empirical gains under noise are especially important for realistic ML tuning scenarios with stochastic training and limited validation sets.
- The method’s reliance on discretization for the inner min may limit theoretical guarantees, but practically it is competitive, and the released code increases impact.

Strengths
- Clear, principled extension of KG to batch setting; reduction to q-EI in the noiseless, restricted case is elegant.
- Joint batch optimization with an unbiased IPA gradient estimator is technically interesting and practical.
- Strong and consistent empirical performance, with notable advantages in noisy regimes.
- Open-source implementation; sensible experimental choices.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scalability and complexity: Provide explicit complexity in terms of q, M, n, and number of MC samples; include wall-clock comparisons and robustness to larger q (e.g., q≥8) and higher d.
- Discretization: Analyze sensitivity to M and the adaptive sampling strategy; consider variance-reduction (e.g., quasi-MC) and alternative inner-approximation strategies; discuss the impact on Bayes-optimality.
- Theoretical aspects: While not essential for acceptance, a short consistency argument (as M→∞ and MC samples→∞) or regret discussion would strengthen the paper.
- Baselines: Clarify precisely which q-EI implementations you compare to and whether gradient-based joint q-EI optimization is included; include parallel ES if feasible.
- Practicalities: Detail MC sample counts, learning rates, stopping criteria, and numerical stabilization in Cholesky-based gradients; discuss asynchronous settings and heteroscedastic noise.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled acquisition for batch BO with solid computational innovation.
- Clear advantage in noisy settings, which are practically important.
- Good clarity and open-source code; likely to be used and extended.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of computational scaling and sensitivity to key hyperparameters (M, MC samples).
- Theoretical guarantees are light, and the discretization may bias the objective.
- Some missing baselines or ablations and limited exploration of higher-dimensional regimes.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a meaningful and original direction in batch BO—bringing a decision-theoretic KG criterion to parallel settings with an efficient gradient-based optimizer—and demonstrates compelling empirical benefits, especially under noise. Minor additions on scalability, sensitivity, and practical details would further strengthen it.
