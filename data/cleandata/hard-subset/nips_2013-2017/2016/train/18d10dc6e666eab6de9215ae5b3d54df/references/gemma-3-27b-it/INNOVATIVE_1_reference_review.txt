This paper presents a novel approach to parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea – extending the single-iteration Bayes-optimal Knowledge Gradient to the batch setting – is compelling and represents a genuinely new direction in parallel BO. While the paper relies on established tools like Gaussian Processes, the theoretical grounding and the proposed solution for efficient gradient computation via IPA are substantial contributions. The focus on decision-theoretic analysis, aiming for optimality in the final iteration, distinguishes it from many existing greedy or UCB-based parallel BO methods.

The strengths of this work are clear: novelty, a strong theoretical foundation, and promising empirical results. The q-KG consistently outperforms existing parallel BO algorithms across synthetic and real-world problems, especially in noisy settings. The IPA-based gradient computation is a clever solution to a significant practical hurdle, enabling optimization of the acquisition function even for relatively large batch sizes. The code release is a huge plus, fostering reproducibility and further research. The connection to, and improvement upon, previous Knowledge Gradient work is well-articulated.

However, the paper isn’t without limitations. While the empirical results are strong, the experiments, while comprehensive, could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of *why* q-KG performs better. What characteristics of the objective function make q-KG particularly effective? The hyperparameter sensitivity of q-KG isn’t thoroughly explored. Additionally, the discretization strategy for continuous domains, while reasonable, feels somewhat ad-hoc; a more rigorous justification or sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. The comparisons to other methods, while numerous, are primarily focused on established algorithms.  Exploring the performance against more recent parallel BO techniques would be useful. The discussion on the computational complexity of q-KG versus other methods, while present, could be more detailed.  Specifically, a more quantitative comparison of scaling with batch size *q* would be helpful. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The core idea of generalizing the knowledge gradient to a parallel setting is a significant contribution.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation from decision-theoretic principles provides a solid justification for the proposed method.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Consistent outperformance over existing methods on diverse problems, especially in noisy settings.
*   **Practicality:** The development of an efficient gradient computation technique makes the method applicable to real-world problems.
*   **Code Availability:**  Facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Analysis of Why it Works:** A deeper understanding of the conditions under which q-KG excels would strengthen the paper.
*   **Ad-hoc Discretization:** The discretization strategy for continuous domains could benefit from further justification.
*   **Missing Comparisons:** Lack of comparisons to the *most* recent parallel BO algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor shortcomings, this paper presents a highly promising and original contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The novelty of the approach, the strong theoretical grounding, and the compelling empirical results outweigh the limitations. This paper represents a bold step forward and has the potential to significantly impact how parallel BO is approached. I believe the paper meets the NIPS criteria for acceptance. I lean strongly towards acceptance, recognizing that the minor points raised could be addressed in future work.
