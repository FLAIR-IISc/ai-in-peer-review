### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea is to select a batch of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality, defined as the minimum of the posterior mean function. This approach is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, making it Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch of evaluations.

The main challenge addressed is the computational complexity of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function. The authors develop a practical optimization strategy based on two key components. First, they show how to express the q-KG factor as an expectation over a standard normal random vector, which can be estimated via Monte Carlo sampling. Second, and more critically, they derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for the q-KG factor using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). This allows for efficient gradient-based optimization of the batch of candidate points. To handle continuous optimization domains, the method relies on a dynamic discretization scheme, where the solution space is approximated by a set of points sampled from the posterior distribution of the function's global optimum.

The authors compare q-KG against several state-of-the-art parallel Bayesian optimization methods, including parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and parallel Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) variants. Through experiments on synthetic benchmark functions and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), they demonstrate that q-KG is highly competitive and often outperforms existing methods, with a particularly notable advantage in noisy settings.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The theoretical foundation of the q-KG acquisition function is sound and principled. Grounding the method in a one-step Bayes-optimal decision framework provides a strong justification that is often lacking in more heuristic parallel BO strategies.
    *   The derivation and application of IPA to compute a stochastic gradient is a significant technical contribution that makes the proposed method practical. Without this, optimizing q-KG would be prohibitively expensive.
    *   The experimental evaluation is thorough. It includes a good mix of synthetic and real-world problems, considers both noise-free and noisy settings, and compares against multiple relevant and strong baselines. The results convincingly support the paper's claims, especially the superior performance in the presence of noise.
    *   The authors are careful to connect their method to prior work, explaining how q-KG generalizes the sequential KG and under what conditions it relates to parallel EI.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The computational cost is a potential concern that is not fully addressed. While the IPA-based gradient is "efficient" relative to finite differences, the overall optimization process (involving Monte Carlo sampling and gradient ascent) is likely much more expensive than baselines like GP-BUCB or greedy EI variants. A discussion of the wall-clock time trade-offs would have strengthened the paper.
    *   The performance of the method depends on the quality of the domain discretization `An`. While the chosen strategy is reasonable and follows prior work, the sensitivity to the number of discretization points `M` is not explored.
    *   The paper does not investigate the scalability of the method with respect to the batch size `q`. The Cholesky decomposition involved is O(q³), which might become a bottleneck for larger `q`. The experiments are limited to `q=4`.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The motivation for a principled batch acquisition function is clearly established in the introduction.
    *   The mathematical derivations are presented logically, starting from the definition of q-KG (Eq. 4.1) and systematically building up the computational machinery for its optimization.
    *   The distinction between the two uses of the domain `A` (one for discretization in the acquisition function, the other for optimization over candidate points) is handled clearly.
    *   The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, with informative plots that effectively communicate the performance differences between methods.
    *   The provision of a link to the source code is a major plus for clarity and reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   While sequential KG and other parallel BO methods exist, this paper is the first to successfully combine these ideas into a practical and principled parallel KG method for continuous domains.
    *   The primary novelty lies in the formulation of the batch acquisition function and, crucially, the development of the IPA-based gradient estimation technique to optimize it. This moves beyond the greedy or heuristic construction of batches common in other methods (e.g., "constant liar" EI or sequential UCB selection).
    *   The work clearly distinguishes itself from prior art, such as [26], by tackling continuous domains and developing a much more efficient gradient-based optimization scheme.

**Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   Parallel BO is an increasingly important problem as parallel computing resources become ubiquitous. This paper provides a new, powerful, and state-of-the-art tool for this setting.
    *   The method's strong performance in noisy environments is particularly important. Many real-world optimization problems involve stochastic objectives (e.g., training a neural network with random initialization, or evaluating performance on a data subsample), and q-KG appears uniquely well-suited for these cases.
    *   The principled, decision-theoretic approach is likely to inspire further research into non-myopic and theoretically grounded acquisition functions for batch BO. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by other researchers.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**

1.  **Principled and Novel Method:** Proposes a well-founded, decision-theoretic acquisition function (q-KG) for the important problem of batch Bayesian optimization, moving beyond common heuristics.
2.  **Key Technical Innovation:** Introduces a novel and effective method based on IPA to compute stochastic gradients of q-KG, making an otherwise intractable optimization practical.
3.  **Strong Empirical Evidence:** Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance across a range of synthetic and real-world problems, with a clear and significant advantage in noisy settings.
4.  **High Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, and the authors provide code, maximizing its potential impact on the community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

1.  **Unaddressed Computational Cost:** The paper lacks a direct comparison of the computational overhead of optimizing q-KG versus the baseline methods, which may be a critical factor for practitioners.
2.  **Limited Scalability Analysis:** The scalability of the method with respect to the batch size `q` and the discretization size `M` is not explored, leaving open questions about its applicability in settings with larger batches.

### Final Recommendation

The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. The paper presents a novel, principled, and high-performing method for a significant problem in machine learning. The technical contribution of using IPA for efficient optimization is substantial, and the empirical results are compelling. While a more detailed analysis of computational costs would have been welcome, its absence does not detract from the core contributions of the paper. This is a high-quality submission that represents a clear advance in the state of the art for Bayesian optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
