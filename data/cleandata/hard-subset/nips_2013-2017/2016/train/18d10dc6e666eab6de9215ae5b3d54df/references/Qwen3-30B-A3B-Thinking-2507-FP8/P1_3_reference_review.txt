

# Peer Review: "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "Parallel Knowledge Gradient" (q-KG), extending the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting where multiple evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors provide a decision-theoretic justification for their acquisition function, which measures the expected incremental value of evaluating a batch of q points. They also develop an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize q-KG, addressing the significant computational challenge of directly maximizing this acquisition function. The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness through extensive experiments on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, showing consistent improvements over existing parallel BO methods, especially in noisy settings.

This work builds upon the knowledge gradient method (Frazier et al., 2009) and extends previous parallel BO approaches (e.g., parallel EI, parallel UCB) that typically construct batches iteratively by optimizing a single-evaluation acquisition function while holding other points fixed. The authors clearly distinguish their method from these approaches by optimizing the batch collectively rather than sequentially.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound extension of knowledge gradient to the parallel setting with a solid theoretical foundation. The authors provide a clear decision-theoretic justification for their acquisition function, showing how it is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor when only one batch remains. The computational approach using IPA is well-motivated and effectively addresses the main bottleneck in optimizing the parallel acquisition function. The experimental design is comprehensive, including both synthetic functions and real-world ML tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), with careful attention to noise conditions. The results consistently demonstrate q-KG's superiority, particularly in noisy settings, with appropriate statistical analysis (mean and standard deviation over multiple runs).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, with clear explanations of the key concepts. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is straightforward, and the computational approach is explained with sufficient detail. The experimental setup is clearly described, making it easy for readers to understand the methodology and results.

**Originality:** The parallel knowledge gradient acquisition function represents a meaningful extension of the sequential KG method to the batch setting. Unlike previous parallel BO methods that construct batches iteratively (e.g., parallel EI, GP-BUCB), the q-KG method optimizes the batch collectively, which is a theoretically sound approach. The authors' use of IPA for efficient gradient computation is novel in this context and represents a significant contribution to computational efficiency. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work, particularly in Section 2 (Related Work), where they comprehensively review and contrast with previous methods.

**Significance:** Batch Bayesian optimization is increasingly important for real-world applications where multiple evaluations can be performed in parallel (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines). The paper's demonstration of consistent improvements over state-of-the-art baselines, especially in noisy settings where many practical applications operate, makes this work highly significant. The publicly available code (github.com/wujian16/qKG) further enhances the practical value of the contribution. The method has clear potential for immediate application in hyperparameter tuning for machine learning systems.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a strong theoretical justification for the acquisition function, it lacks a formal analysis of regret bounds or theoretical guarantees for the q-KG method. A theoretical analysis of how q-KG's regret compares to other batch BO methods would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation.

**Computational Complexity:** The paper demonstrates the efficiency of their IPA-based approach, but it doesn't provide a detailed computational complexity analysis of how the method scales with batch size q. This is particularly important since the paper focuses on q=4 in experiments, but practitioners often need to handle larger batch sizes.

**Comparison with More Recent Methods:** As a 2016 NIPS paper, the authors couldn't include methods published after 2016, but the paper could have better acknowledged the subsequent literature that emerged after 2016 (e.g., more recent batch BO methods). The comparison with existing methods is strong, but could be enhanced by explicitly discussing how q-KG compares to the most relevant methods published after 2016.

**Explanation of Noisy Setting Performance:** The paper notes that q-KG performs particularly well in noisy settings, but doesn't provide a detailed explanation for why this is the case. A deeper analysis of why the collective batch optimization approach is more robust to noise would strengthen the paper.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper. The contribution is significant, the technical approach is sound, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a clear distinction from related work. The method has immediate practical value for practitioners in machine learning who need to perform batch hyperparameter tuning.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, particularly in the important parallel setting. While the theoretical analysis could be expanded, the practical significance of the work, combined with the strong experimental results, makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. The authors have provided a solid foundation for future work in batch Bayesian optimization.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a brief discussion of the theoretical regret bounds for q-KG in the conclusion or as future work.
2. Add a computational complexity analysis showing how the IPA-based approach scales with batch size q.
3. In the experimental section, consider including a brief discussion of why q-KG performs particularly well in noisy settings.
4. Consider adding a comparison with more recent batch BO methods (post-2016) in the related work section, even if only in a brief discussion.

This paper represents a high-quality contribution to Bayesian optimization that will be of interest to both researchers and practitioners working in the field. The method is well-motivated, the implementation is efficient, and the results are compelling.
