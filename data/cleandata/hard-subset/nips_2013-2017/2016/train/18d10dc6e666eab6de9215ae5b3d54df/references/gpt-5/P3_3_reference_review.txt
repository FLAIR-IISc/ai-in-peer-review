Summary
This paper proposes q-KG, a principled acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization (BO) that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to the parallel setting. Given a GP posterior after n observations, q-KG values a candidate batch z(1:q) by the expected decrease in the posterior mean minimum, i.e., minx µ(n)(x) − En[minx µ(n+q)(x)], which is Bayes-optimal if only one batch remains and the final decision is the argmin of the posterior mean. The authors derive an efficient Monte Carlo estimator using a reparameterization µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z)Z, and develop a stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based batch selection. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner argmin over a dynamically chosen finite set An combining samples from the GP posterior minimum, previously sampled points, and the candidate batch. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks show q-KG outperforms batch EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, especially under noise.

Relation to prior work
- Extends classical KG (Frazier et al. 2008; Scott et al. 2011) to q > 1, whereas prior KG work was sequential.
- Contrasts with batch EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Wang et al.) and shows the reduction of q-KG to q-EI in the noiseless, restricted-recommendation setting.
- Related to batch ES/PPES (Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani), and batch UCB variants (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.), but with a distinct one-step Bayes-optimal objective.
- Uses gradient estimators similar in spirit to those used for optimizing q-EI, but here for the q-KG functional.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean, decision-theoretic derivation. The reduction to q-EI clarifies the relationship to common practice and situates q-KG as a strict generalization that handles noise and unrestricted final recommendations.
- Sound reparameterization and IPA-based gradient estimator that allow practical optimization of a difficult acquisition. The pathwise gradient under Gaussian reparameterization is natural; exchanging derivative and expectation is justified under smoothness and uniqueness conditions.
- Empirical results are compelling and consistent: q-KG is especially strong in noisy settings, where q-EI and UCB-based heuristics often degrade.
- Implementation details and code availability increase reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Discretization of A in the inner minimizations: While standard in KG, performance depends on the quality of An. The proposed construction (AMn drawn from posterior minimizer sampling + past points + the current candidate z) is reasonable but raises issues:
  - An depends on z, which makes the objective piecewise constant in the non-z components and can complicate gradient flow. The paper sidesteps derivatives through the changing index set; a discussion of whether An is held fixed during line searches/gradient steps (to preserve unbiasedness) would help.
  - No ablation on M (size of An), nor sensitivity to the posterior-minimizer sampler, which is nontrivial to implement robustly. Practical guidance on M vs. accuracy vs. runtime is missing.
- The Bayes-optimality guarantee is strictly for the myopic objective of minimizing minx µ(n+q)(x) after one batch. This diverges from the true BO objective (minimizing simple regret in f with multiple remaining rounds). Some discussion of horizon effects or regret bounds would strengthen the theoretical framing.
- Scalability: Complexity scales at least O(q^3) per batch (Cholesky) and O(Mq) per Monte Carlo sample for the min over An. The paper would benefit from a concrete complexity analysis and wall-clock comparisons vs. strong baselines (e.g., PPES, state-of-the-art q-EI implementations like MOE) at larger q and d.
- Comparisons: The experiments omit PPES and other information-theoretic batch methods, despite citing them. Since q-KG is also an information-directed criterion, a direct comparison would be illuminating. Also, clarifying whether the “parallel EI in [24]” is the fast multi-point EI and whether gradient-based q-EI (e.g., Marmin et al.) was used would help assess fairness.
- Gradient estimator details: IPA requires conditions (differentiability, unique argmin almost surely). At minimizer ties, g is nonsmooth; the supplement likely covers this, but a brief statement of assumptions and variance-reduction strategies (common random numbers across candidate batches, antithetic sampling) would aid clarity.
- Hyperparameter learning: The GP hyperparameters and noise variance are re-fit by MLE each iteration. This is common, but can be unstable in low data and noisy settings. A brief note on priors/MAP, or sensitivity analyses, would be useful.

Clarity
Overall well written, with a clear exposition of the model, acquisition, and estimator. Some section numbering appears off, and Section 5.3’s description of posterior minimizer sampling could benefit from a concise algorithmic sketch or a citation with precise implementation details. Adding complexity notes and a short paragraph on implementation choices for An during optimization would improve reproducibility.

Significance and impact
Batch BO is practically important, and robust performance under noise is especially valuable for ML hyperparameter tuning. q-KG provides a principled alternative to q-EI and batch ES/UCB, with a usable gradient estimator and open-source code. I expect practitioners to adopt this when evaluations are noisy and batching is available. The work advances the state of the art.

Pros
- Principled derivation; reduction to q-EI clarifies novelty.
- Effective stochastic gradient estimator; practical and elegant.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise.
- Code availability.

Cons
- Reliance on discretization; limited analysis/sensitivity to M and sampling scheme.
- No direct comparison to PPES or other information-theoretic batch methods.
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond one-step optimality; no regret analysis.
- Scalability and runtime analysis could be more thorough.

Questions/suggestions for the authors
- Clarify whether An is held fixed during gradient steps to ensure an unbiased gradient and a well-defined objective.
- Include sensitivity to M and an ablation comparing different An construction strategies.
- Add comparisons to PPES and a state-of-the-art q-EI gradient optimizer, with wall-clock times.
- Discuss variance-reduction techniques for the Monte Carlo gradient and provide guidance on the number of samples.
- Provide complexity per iteration in terms of q, M, and n.
