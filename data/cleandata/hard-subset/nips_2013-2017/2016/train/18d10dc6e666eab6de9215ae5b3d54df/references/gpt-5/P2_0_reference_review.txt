Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes q-KG, an acquisition function for batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to selecting q points jointly. The objective is the expected decrease in the minimum posterior mean after acquiring a batch, i.e., minx µn(x) − E[minx µn+q(x)], which is Bayes-optimal if only one batch remains and the final recommendation need not be among the sampled points (contrasting EI). Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG is intractable in closed form, the authors (i) estimate q-KG via Monte Carlo using a linear-Gaussian update parameterized by a standard normal Zq, and (ii) derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based maximization. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization domain adaptively by sampling candidate locations from a distribution intended to concentrate near posterior optima (as in entropy search), augmented with past samples and the candidate batch. Experiments on standard test functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10), in both noise-free and noisy settings, show q-KG outperforming or matching parallel EI (exact and heuristic) and parallel UCB variants, with especially large gains under noise. The work builds on sequential KG [Frazier et al., 2008], connects to multi-point EI [Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.], and to batch entropy search [Shah & Ghahramani], and contrasts with greedy batch construction heuristics (constant liar, kriging believer) and GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The acquisition is well-defined and justified decision-theoretically (one-step Bayes optimality). The Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG via the conditional linear-Gaussian posterior is standard. The IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator is a substantive technical contribution; while the min operator complicates differentiability, the paper asserts unbiasedness under smoothness with details in the supplement, which is plausible given uniqueness a.s. under Gaussian perturbations. However:
  - There are no regret or asymptotic guarantees (common in BO work, but worth noting), nor analysis of the impact of GP hyperparameter re-estimation on optimality.
  - The discretization of the inner min is critical; while the adaptive scheme is reasonable, its accuracy and impact on bias/variance are not analyzed, and the method for sampling from the “global optima” distribution is only referenced, not described.
  - Computational complexity and scaling with q, d, and discretization size M are not analyzed; no wall-clock comparisons are reported.
- Empirical support: Benchmarks are sensible, include both synthetic and real problems, and demonstrate clear gains—most notably under noise, where KG’s value is expected. Results are averaged over multiple runs with variability reported. Limitations: q is fixed to 4; there is no study of performance vs q, M, or noise level, and no ablation on the discretization strategy or gradient estimator parameters.

Clarity
- Generally readable and well-organized, with clear motivation and relation to prior work. The mathematical setup is concise.
- Some presentation issues should be corrected: inconsistent section numbering; minor typos; notation in Eq. (4.1) mixes En[·] with a conditional on y(z(1:q))—please clarify the conditioning and the definition of En; the discretization procedure (how samples from the posterior-optimum distribution are obtained) should be explained more concretely for reproducibility.
- The computational details of differentiating through the Cholesky factor and handling non-differentiability of min deserve a bit more space in the main text (even if full proofs remain in the supplement).

Originality
- Extends KG from sequential to batch selection over continuous domains with a practical, unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via IPA. To my knowledge, this specific combination (joint batch KG + IPA gradient + adaptive discretization) is novel relative to nested-batch KG on discrete sets and to batch EI/ES work.
- The reduction to parallel EI in the noise-free, “recommend-from-evaluated-set” case is a nice conceptual link.
- That said, the general theme of joint batch acquisition optimization has prior art (q-EI, PPES), and the discretization approach is inspired by ES; novelty is solid but incremental within the BO literature.

Significance
- Batch BO is important in practice; methods that handle noise well are especially valuable. The empirical improvements, particularly under noise, suggest q-KG could be adopted by practitioners.
- The IPA gradient estimator may be of independent interest for other expectation-of-min acquisitions.
- Practical significance would be strengthened by scaling and runtime analyses, and by comparisons on higher-dimensional or larger-q settings.

Specific suggestions for improvement
- Add wall-clock time and complexity discussion (per-iteration complexity in terms of q, M, d, Monte Carlo samples; practical runtimes vs q-EI/PPES).
- Include PPES in the empirical comparison, as it is the most directly comparable joint batch method you cite.
- Vary q (e.g., 2, 4, 8) and report performance vs q; include at least one higher-dimensional synthetic task (e.g., d ≥ 10).
- Clarify the adaptive discretization: precisely how are samples drawn from the posterior-optimum distribution? If you use PES machinery, summarize the approximation used.
- Discuss the effect of re-estimating GP hyperparameters on the Bayes-optimality claim and on stability; consider a brief experiment comparing fixed vs re-fitted hyperparameters.
- Clean up section numbering/notation and make the conditioning in Eq. (4.1) precise.

Pros
- Principled acquisition with clear decision-theoretic motivation.
- Novel, practical stochastic gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical performance, notably under noise; code released.
- Connects and contrasts well with EI/UCB/ES-based batch methods.

Cons
- Missing runtime/scaling analysis; experiments limited to q=4 and low-to-moderate d.
- Key baseline (PPES) absent from experiments.
- Discretization and IPA details are under-specified in the main text; no sensitivity analysis.
- No theoretical guarantees beyond one-step optimality under idealized assumptions.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a sound and useful contribution to batch BO, with a principled acquisition, a technically interesting gradient estimator, and convincing empirical gains in settings where noise matters. Addressing clarity and experimental scope (especially runtime/scaling and PPES comparison) would further strengthen the case.
