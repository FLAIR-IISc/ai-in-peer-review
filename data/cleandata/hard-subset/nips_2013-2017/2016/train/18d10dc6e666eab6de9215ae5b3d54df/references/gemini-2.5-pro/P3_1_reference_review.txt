### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO). The goal of batch BO is to select a set of `q` points to evaluate simultaneously to find the minimum of an expensive-to-evaluate function. The proposed q-KG method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework. Specifically, it quantifies the value of a batch of `q` candidate points by the expected improvement in the final solution quality after observing their outcomes. The final solution is defined as the minimum of the posterior mean function, which is a key distinction from Expected Improvement (EI), where the solution is typically restricted to previously evaluated points.

The main technical challenge is the optimization of the q-KG acquisition function, which involves a nested expectation and a minimization over the domain. The authors' key contribution is an efficient method to optimize q-KG using a stochastic gradient-based approach. They derive an unbiased estimator for the gradient of the q-KG factor using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), allowing them to use standard gradient ascent algorithms. To handle the continuous optimization domain within the acquisition function, they propose an adaptive discretization scheme based on sampling from the posterior optima.

This work builds directly on the sequential KG literature [7, 17] and provides a non-trivial generalization to the parallel setting. It contrasts with many existing parallel BO methods like parallel EI heuristics [10, 19] or batch UCB methods [3, 5], which often build the batch greedily or sequentially. The q-KG approach values the batch of points collectively, similar to joint optimization of parallel EI [2, 15, 25] and parallel Entropy Search (ES) [18], but is based on a different decision-theoretic principle (simple regret vs. information gain).

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Principled Derivation:** The q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated and principled. It is derived as a one-step Bayes-optimal policy for minimizing the value of the final recommended solution (`argmin_x mu(x)`). This directly aligns with the primary goal of global optimization (finding the best point), which is a conceptual advantage over methods like UCB that are often designed to minimize cumulative regret.

2.  **Novel and Significant Technical Contribution:** The development of an efficient, unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG via IPA is a significant technical achievement. Naively optimizing the Monte Carlo estimate of q-KG would be computationally prohibitive. This gradient-based approach makes the method practical and is a valuable contribution to the BO toolbox.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance, Especially in Noisy Settings:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering a range of synthetic benchmarks and two practical hyperparameter tuning problems. The results convincingly demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or superior to several state-of-the-art parallel BO methods. The performance advantage is particularly pronounced in the noisy evaluation setting. This is intuitively sensible, as KG's focus on the expected value of the posterior minimum (rather than just the observed values) should make it more robust to observation noise than methods like EI.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and structured. The motivation is clear, the methodology is explained step-by-step from the definition of q-KG to its practical computation, and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently. The inclusion of open-source code is also a major plus for reproducibility and community adoption.

### Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection / Areas for Improvement)

1.  **Computational Cost:** The primary weakness of the proposed method is its computational complexity. Optimizing the acquisition function requires a multi-start stochastic gradient ascent procedure. Each gradient evaluation itself requires a Monte Carlo average, and within each sample, one must compute derivatives of the GP kernel and its Cholesky factor. The paper does not provide any analysis of this computational overhead (e.g., wall-clock time comparisons). For problems where function evaluations are only moderately expensive, the time spent selecting the next batch via q-KG could dominate the total optimization time, making it less practical than faster heuristics.

2.  **Reliance on Discretization:** The method for handling the continuous domain `A` inside the q-KG formulation relies on a discretization `An`. While the proposed adaptive scheme is sensible, the quality of the q-KG approximation is fundamentally tied to the quality of this discrete set. The paper does not analyze the sensitivity of the algorithm to the size of this set (`M=1000` is used without justification). It is possible that for high-dimensional or highly multi-modal functions, this discretization could be a significant source of error.

3.  **Missing Comparison to Key Baseline:** The paper compares against parallel EI and UCB variants. However, it omits a comparison to Parallel Predictive Entropy Search (PES) [18], which is cited and is perhaps the most closely related work in terms of jointly optimizing a batch based on an information-theoretic principle. Like q-KG, PES values points collectively and uses Monte Carlo methods. A comparison against PES would be necessary for a complete picture of the state of the art.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **On IPA Justification:** The paper states that the exchange of derivative and expectation in Eq. (5.3) is justified in the supplementary material. It would be helpful to briefly state the required conditions (e.g., differentiability of the mean and kernel functions) in the main text for completeness.
*   **On Implementation Details:** How many Monte Carlo samples are used to estimate the q-KG gradient at each step of the optimization? And how many steps of stochastic gradient ascent are performed to maximize the acquisition function? These are crucial parameters that affect both performance and runtime.
*   **On Hyperparameter Fitting:** The algorithm re-trains GP hyperparameters via MLE after each batch. This is standard practice but can be a major computational bottleneck. It would be beneficial to mention this as part of the overall cost of the algorithm.

### Overall Evaluation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.

*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The derivation is rigorous, and the claims are well-supported by a strong set of experiments.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making a complex topic accessible.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The extension of the knowledge gradient to the batch setting is novel, and the use of IPA for its optimization is a clever and effective contribution.
*   **Significance:** The results are important. Batch BO is of great practical interest, and q-KG appears to advance the state of the art, particularly for noisy optimization problems. The method and its underlying principles are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.

The weaknesses, primarily related to computational cost and the details of the discretization, are limitations to be aware of but do not undermine the core contribution. They represent avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws.

**Recommendation: Accept**
