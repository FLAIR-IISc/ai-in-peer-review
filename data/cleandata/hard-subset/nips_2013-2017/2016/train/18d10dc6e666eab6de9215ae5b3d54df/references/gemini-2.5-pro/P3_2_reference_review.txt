### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO). The core idea is to extend the decision-theoretic foundation of the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) to the parallel setting. The q-KG acquisition function quantifies the expected improvement in the final recommended solution value, gained from evaluating a batch of `q` points simultaneously. This contrasts with methods like Expected Improvement (EI), which measure the expected improvement over the best-observed value so far.

A key challenge is that optimizing the q-KG acquisition function over a `q*d`-dimensional space is computationally demanding. The paper's main technical contribution is a practical method to maximize q-KG. This is achieved by deriving an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). This allows the use of efficient gradient-based optimizers. To handle continuous domains, the authors propose a dynamic discretization scheme, where the final recommendation is chosen from a finite set of points sampled from the posterior distribution over the function's global optima, combined with previously evaluated points. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks, demonstrating that q-KG often outperforms other state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, with a particularly strong advantage in noisy environments.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled and Novel Acquisition Function:** The derivation of q-KG from a one-step Bayes-optimal decision-theoretic perspective is elegant and well-founded. It provides a principled way to value a batch of points jointly, capturing their synergistic effect on reducing uncertainty about the true optimum's location and value. This is a significant conceptual advance over methods that construct batches greedily (e.g., constant liar heuristics, GP-BUCB) and offers a compelling alternative to information-theoretic approaches like parallel Entropy Search.

2.  **Efficient Optimization via IPA:** The paper's most significant technical contribution is the development of a practical optimization strategy for q-KG. Naively maximizing a Monte Carlo-based acquisition function is often intractable. The use of IPA to obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient is a sophisticated and effective solution that makes the entire method feasible. This is a non-trivial extension of gradient-based optimization for acquisition functions.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance, Especially in Noisy Settings:** The experimental results are thorough and convincing. q-KG is shown to be competitive with or superior to several strong baselines (parallel EI, GP-BUCB) across multiple synthetic and real-world problems. The reported advantage in noisy settings is particularly noteworthy and aligns well with the theoretical motivation of KG. By decoupling the evaluation points from the final recommendation point (`argmin E[f(x)]`), KG-based methods are inherently more robust to observation noise than EI-based methods, which are constrained to recommend a previously evaluated point. The experiment on tuning logistic regression with a noisy (subsampled) test set is a very practical and compelling demonstration of this benefit.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly explained. The authors have also made their code publicly available, which is a major asset for reproducibility and will encourage adoption and further research in the community.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Cost Analysis:** While the IPA-based optimization is efficient relative to naive approaches, the overall method remains computationally intensive. It involves Monte Carlo sampling for the objective and gradient, followed by a multi-start stochastic gradient ascent procedure at each BO iteration. The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the computational complexity and wall-clock time compared to the baselines. For practitioners, understanding the trade-off between the improved sample efficiency and the increased computational cost of selecting the next batch is crucial.

2.  **Dependence on Discretization:** The method's performance relies on the quality of the discrete set `An` used to approximate the `argmin` over the continuous domain `A`. The paper follows previous work in constructing this set but does not analyze the sensitivity of q-KG's performance to the size of this set (`M`). A larger `M` provides a better approximation but increases the cost of computing the inner minimization in the q-KG formula. Some analysis or discussion on this trade-off would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Potentially Problematic Optimization Objective:** As defined in Section 5.3, the discretization set `An` includes the candidate points `z(1:q)`. This means the domain of the inner minimization in the acquisition function (Eq. 5.4) depends on the variable `z(1:q)` that is being optimized. This can create discontinuities in the objective function and its gradient as `z(1:q)` changes, which could pose challenges for gradient-based optimizers. While the method seems to work well in practice, this theoretical subtlety should be acknowledged and discussed.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

-   In the comparison against parallel EI, the paper mentions two implementations: one from MOE (which also uses joint gradient-based optimization) and one from Spearmint (a heuristic). This is a good choice of baselines. A comparison with Parallel Entropy Search [18] would have been a valuable addition, as it is another prominent method that jointly values a batch of points.

-   The experiments for tuning the CNN on CIFAR-10 use only 5 independent runs. While understandable given the computational expense, this small sample size leads to large error bars, making it difficult to draw statistically significant conclusions about the performance difference between methods on this complex task.

### Summary of evaluation

-   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the claims are well-supported by extensive experiments.
-   **Clarity:** High. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making a complex topic accessible.
-   **Originality:** High. The paper presents a novel acquisition function for batch BO and a non-trivial, efficient method for optimizing it. It is a significant and original extension of the knowledge gradient framework.
-   **Significance:** High. This work provides a new state-of-the-art algorithm for batch BO, a problem of great practical importance in areas like hyperparameter tuning. Its excellent performance in noisy settings makes it particularly valuable. The methodology is likely to inspire further work on decision-theoretic acquisition functions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and powerful acquisition function (q-KG) for batch BO.
*   Develops a sophisticated and practical gradient-based method to optimize q-KG.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art empirical performance, especially in noisy settings, which are common in practice.
*   The work is a significant contribution to the BO literature and is likely to have a high impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The computational cost of the method is high and not thoroughly analyzed against baselines.
*   Potential theoretical issues with the optimization objective due to the dynamic discretization are not discussed.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

The strengths of this paper—a novel and principled method, a strong technical contribution to make it practical, and compelling empirical results—far outweigh its minor weaknesses. This is a high-quality paper that clearly advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization.
