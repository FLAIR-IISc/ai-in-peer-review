

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) that generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors derive q-KG using decision-theoretic analysis and propose an efficient computational method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize it. The paper claims that q-KG outperforms existing batch BO methods on synthetic and real-world problems, especially in noisy settings.

## Major Technical Issues

1. **Critical Conceptual Error**: The paper states in Section 4 that "minx∈A µ(n+q)(x) is itself a random vector due to its dependence on the outcome of the samples." This is fundamentally incorrect. minx∈A µ(n+q)(x) is a scalar random variable, not a vector. This error appears to undermine the core theoretical justification for the q-KG acquisition function.

2. **Citation Mismatch**: The paper cites [10] as suggesting "parallelizing EI by iteratively constructing a batch" but [10] is listed as "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (a paper about kriging, not EI). This citation mismatch is severe and suggests the authors may not have properly reviewed the literature.

3. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout, particularly with the posterior mean. The authors write µ(n)(x) when standard notation would use μ_n(x) or μ(x|D_n). This inconsistency appears in equations (3.1), (4.1), (5.1), and elsewhere, making the mathematics difficult to follow.

4. **Missing Theoretical Justification**: The paper claims q-KG is "Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the predictor of the GP if only one decision is remaining" but provides no proof or reference for this claim. This is a significant theoretical gap in the paper.

5. **Equation Error**: In equation (3.1), the parentheses do not match: "µ(n)(x) = µ(x) +K(x,x(1:n)) ( K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) + diag{σ2(x(1)), · · · , σ2(x(n))} )−1 (y(1:n) − µ(x(1:n))),". The closing parenthesis for the inverse is missing before the final right parenthesis.

## Minor Technical and Formatting Issues

1. **Typographical Errors**: "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" should be "The Conference". Also, a missing period appears after "min x∈A f(x)," in the first paragraph.

2. **Equation Notation**: In equation (4.1), the notation "En" should be "E_n" to indicate the posterior expectation. In equation (5.2), "EZq" should be "E_{Z_q}" for proper mathematical notation.

3. **Reference Inconsistency**: The reference for [24] is poorly formatted with "http://yelp, J. Wang, S.C. Clark, E. Liu, P.I. Frazier, github.io/MOE/. Last accessed on 2016-01-21" - this should be standardized.

4. **Missing Reference**: The paper states "We set M = 1000 to discretize the domain following the strategy in Section 5.3" but Section 5.3 does not specify M=1000, only the general strategy.

5. **Vague Description**: The CNN architecture description ("It consists of 2 convolutional layers, 2 fully connected layers...") is too vague for reproducibility. Specific architectural details would be helpful.

## Strengths

The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian optimization (parallel evaluation) and makes a reasonable extension of the knowledge gradient to the batch setting. The computational approach using IPA for gradient estimation is interesting and potentially useful. The empirical results show promising performance, particularly in noisy settings.

## Weaknesses

The paper's theoretical foundation is undermined by the critical conceptual error regarding the random nature of minx∈A µ(n+q)(x). The citation mismatch for [10] suggests inadequate literature review. The inconsistent notation throughout makes the paper difficult to follow and raises questions about the authors' attention to detail. The lack of proof for the Bayes-optimality claim is a significant theoretical weakness.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting method with promising empirical results, the technical errors and inconsistencies are too severe to recommend acceptance. The critical conceptual error regarding a scalar being a vector fundamentally undermines the theoretical justification of the acquisition function. The citation mismatch and inconsistent notation further erode confidence in the paper's rigor. These issues are not merely minor typographical errors but represent significant technical flaws that would confuse readers and potentially mislead researchers.

The paper would benefit from substantial revision to correct these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication. As it stands, the technical inconsistencies outweigh the empirical contributions.

**Recommendation**: Reject
