- Paper introduces q-KG, a novel batch Bayesian optimization acquisition function extending the sequential knowledge gradient to parallel evaluations.
- Authors derive q-KG decision-theoretically and propose an IPA-based method to efficiently compute its gradient for optimization.
- Experiments on synthetic functions and real ML tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10) show q-KG consistently outperforms baselines, especially in noisy settings.
- Theoretical derivation of q-KG is sound and well-motivated, correctly extending the sequential KG approach to batches.
- The IPA computational technique is clever, mathematically detailed, and makes q-KG practical to implement.
- Experimental design is comprehensive, comparing against parallel EI and batch UCB with multiple runs and error bars.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and uses consistent, easy-to-follow mathematical notation.
- Explanations balance rigor and intuition, and the paper provides sufficient implementation details for reproducibility.
- Computational complexity discussion is superficial; no detailed comparison of scaling with batch size q versus other methods.
- The choice of discretization parameter M=1000 is unexplained and lacks empirical justification.
- The paper does not adequately address q-KG’s limitations or scalability in high-dimensional problems (d > 10).
- Comparisons to some recent batch BO methods ([15, 18, 25]) are insufficient or missing in experiments.
- Contribution is original: parallelizing KG and using IPA for gradient computation are novel and distinct from prior BO work.
- The method is significant and practically relevant for parallel hyperparameter tuning, with clear empirical benefits in noisy evaluations.
- Paper could better discuss broader domain impacts and practical implications of noisy-setting improvements.
- Overall assessment: significant, well-executed contribution that merits acceptance.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Suggested revisions: add detailed computational complexity analysis comparing scaling with q, justify M=1000 and study sensitivity, discuss high-dimensional scalability, compare to more recent batch BO methods, and mention future extensions (constraints, multi-objective).
