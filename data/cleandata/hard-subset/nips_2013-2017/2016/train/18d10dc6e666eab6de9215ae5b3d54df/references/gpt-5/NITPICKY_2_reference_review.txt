Summary
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. q-KG is defined as the expected reduction in the posterior mean’s minimum after one synchronous batch, i.e., the Bayes-optimal policy when only one batch remains. Since exact evaluation is intractable for q > 1, the authors estimate q-KG via Monte Carlo by reparameterizing the posterior update with a standard normal Zq, and optimize it with a stochastic gradient computed using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). For continuous domains, q-KG is approximated by discretizing the search space using representer points drawn from the GP’s posterior minimizer distribution (as in ES/PES). Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10) suggest q-KG is competitive or better than parallel EI variants and GP-UCB-based baselines, with larger wins in noisy settings. Code is released.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic grounding: q-KG is the Bayes-optimal one-batch policy for minimizing the posterior mean’s minimum, aligning with KG’s strengths in noisy settings.
- Joint (non-greedy) batch selection with a differentiable stochastic objective; leveraging IPA to obtain gradients is sensible and practically useful.
- Conceptual connection to q-EI in the noise-free, evaluate-and-return-a-sampled-point setting.
- Empirical results indicate consistent advantages over strong baselines, particularly under noise, where KG-type methods often shine.
- Open-source implementation and use of established toolkits for baselines.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical correctness and clarity
- The gradient expression in Section 5.2 appears inconsistent: ∂/∂zij µ(n)(x*(before)) should be zero because µ(n) does not depend on z(1:q). Including this term suggests a misunderstanding or a typographical error. Please clarify the application of the envelope theorem/IPA and ensure the “before” term is omitted or correctly justified.
- The IPA unbiasedness and regularity conditions are relegated to the supplement. Given the non-smooth min over a discrete set and dependence on z through Cholesky factors, the main text should at least state the assumptions (e.g., uniqueness of argmin almost surely, differentiability of kernel/mean, treatment of ties, use of common random numbers) under which Eq. (5.3) holds.
- The derivation of the equivalence to q-EI under noise-free evaluations and restricted final decision is informal. A precise statement and proof (or a citation) would strengthen the claim, especially since q-EI depends on the joint distribution of future evaluations and q-KG is written via µ(n+q), which itself depends on the yet-unknown y(z).
- The discretization of A via “drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior distribution of the GP” is underspecified. Sampling from the minimizer distribution is non-trivial; ES/PES rely on specific constructions. Please specify the exact sampler used, its parameters, and how z is included without biasing the objective (since An depends on z).
- Computing ∂D/∂zij for the Cholesky factor can be numerically delicate and costly. No details are provided on how this is implemented (e.g., using standard reverse-mode rules for Cholesky) and whether the derivative is formed via implicit differentiation (to avoid explicit differentiation of the factor). Citing standard references (e.g., Giles, 2008, or recent autodiff literature) and providing complexity would help.
- Notation issues impede comprehension: En[ min µ(n+q)(x) | y(z(1:q)) ] reads oddly because conditioning on y makes µ(n+q) deterministic; the intent is an outer expectation over y(z), later rewritten via Zq. Please fix.
- No theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence as M→∞, consistency of the discretization scheme, or regret bounds) are provided. While not necessary for acceptance, some discussion of limitations and known results would improve the paper.

Experimental methodology
- Runtime/scalability is not reported. q-KG involves repeated MC inner loops, Cholesky factorizations and gradients; comparisons to q-EI (MOE), GP-UCB variants, and PES/q-PES should include wall-clock time and CPU/GPU budget to assess practical trade-offs.
- Sensitivity/ablation is missing: number of MC samples for q-KG and its gradient, impact of M (discretization size), batch size q (only q=4 is shown), and dimensionality beyond d=6. It’s unclear how q-KG scales with q and d.
- Fairness of comparisons: q-KG uses an adaptive discretization. Are baselines optimized to the same tolerance and with comparable budgets? Are hyperparameters re-estimated identically across methods (e.g., MLE frequency, priors)?
- The “noisy” MNIST experiment estimates the objective using 1,000 test points; please quantify the noise variance empirically and discuss whether the homoskedastic constant-noise assumption is reasonable. Similarly, specify the noise model used in the GP (prior on σ^2, MLE procedure).
- The CNN experiment lacks details: exact architecture, training pipeline, data augmentation, early stopping, and computational budget per evaluation. A reported improvement from 14% to ~11% error is significant; more detail is needed to assess reproducibility and fairness.
- Missing baselines: parallel ES/PES (e.g., Shah & Ghahramani 2015) are closely related and should be included if feasible.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous typos/formatting issues: “Main Stange,” inconsistent capitalization of Gaussian, “th Conference,” missing punctuation, inconsistent superscripts/subscripts, and several notational slips.
- References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing years/venues, malformed URLs, e.g., MOE link “http://yelp, … github.io/MOE/”). Several key works lack full bibliographic details.
- Algorithm 1 formatting is broken (line numbering, colons).
- Please define all symbols on first use and maintain consistent notation (e.g., z(1:q) vs. z, A vs. An).

Relation to prior work
- The relation to (and differences from) q-EI with gradient-based optimization (e.g., Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.; Wang et al. MOE) is discussed, but a more systematic comparison, especially under noise, would be valuable.
- The discretization approach borrows from ES/PES; implementation details and differences should be clarified.

Quality/Clarity/Originality/Significance
- Quality: Promising method, but several technical and clarity issues reduce confidence and reproducibility. Missing runtime/scaling analysis.
- Clarity: Needs substantial polishing; notational and formatting problems, incomplete methodological details.
- Originality: Extends KG to batch with an IPA-based optimizer; the general idea is known in sequential KG, but the batch gradient-based optimization is a meaningful contribution.
- Significance: Batch BO is important; gains in noisy settings are useful. With stronger experiments and clearer methods, impact could be solid.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, decision-theoretic acquisition for batch setting.
- Practical gradient-based optimization strategy for joint selection.
- Consistent empirical improvements, especially with noise.
- Open-source code.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical inconsistency in gradient expression; insufficient detail on IPA assumptions and Cholesky differentiation.
- Vague discretization description; missing ablations and runtime/scalability.
- Presentation quality (typos, references) below NIPS standards.
- Missing strong baselines and detailed experimental protocols.

Questions for the authors
- How many MC samples are used for q-KG and for its gradient per optimization step? Do you use common random numbers?
- How exactly do you sample representer points from the minimizer distribution? Please specify the algorithm and parameters.
- How is ∂D/∂z computed in practice? Any stability tricks or autodiff backends?
- Can you report wall-clock time vs performance for q=4,8 and d up to 10–20?
- Why is ∂/∂zij µ(n)(x*(before)) included in the gradient?

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The core idea is strong and likely impactful, but the paper has too many technical, methodological, and presentation issues for NIPS as is. With corrected derivations, fuller experimental details (including runtime/scaling and stronger baselines), and polished writing/references, this could become a solid contribution.
