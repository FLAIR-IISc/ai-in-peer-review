### Paper Summary

This paper introduces a new acquisition function for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), termed the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The goal of batch BO is to select a set of `q` points to evaluate in parallel at each iteration to find the minimum of an expensive-to-evaluate function. The proposed q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, selecting the batch of points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality, under the assumption that this is the last batch of evaluations to be performed. The final solution is defined as the point that minimizes the posterior mean of the Gaussian Process (GP) model. This approach generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy, which is known to be Bayes-optimal for the one-step-remaining setting, to the parallel case.

A key challenge is that the q-KG acquisition function, which involves an expectation over the posterior distribution of future observations, is computationally intensive to optimize. The main technical contribution of the paper is an efficient method to maximize q-KG. The authors propose using a Monte Carlo estimator for the acquisition function and derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). This enables the use of gradient-based optimization algorithms to find the optimal batch of points. For continuous domains, the method relies on a dynamic discretization scheme where the optimum of the posterior mean is sought over a finite set of points, which is updated at each iteration.

The paper provides an empirical evaluation of q-KG against several established parallel BO methods, including variants of parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and parallel Upper Confidence Bound (UCB). Experiments are conducted on synthetic benchmark functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), considering both noise-free and noisy evaluation settings. The results demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms the baseline methods, showing a particularly significant performance advantage when the function evaluations are noisy.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is a principled extension of the established KG framework to the batch setting. The core technical contribution—the application of IPA to derive an unbiased gradient estimator for the Monte Carlo approximation of q-KG—is sophisticated and well-justified, providing a viable path to optimizing what would otherwise be an intractable acquisition function. The experimental methodology is solid, involving relevant baselines, standard benchmark problems, and both noise-free and noisy settings. The claims of superior performance, especially in the noisy case, are well-supported by the empirical results, which include means and standard deviations over multiple independent runs.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a good overview of Bayesian optimization and situates the work within the context of parallel BO. The formulation of the q-KG acquisition function in Section 4 is clear. The description of the computational approach in Section 5, particularly the use of Monte Carlo sampling and the IPA-based gradient, is presented logically. However, certain aspects could be improved for clarity. The dynamic discretization strategy in Section 5.3 is a critical component for practical application, but its description is brief. Specifically, the discretized domain `An` depends on the candidate points `z(1:q)`, which are the optimization variables. This means the objective function landscape changes during the optimization of `z(1:q)`, which could have non-trivial implications for a gradient-based solver. A more detailed discussion of this aspect would be beneficial. Furthermore, a comparison of the computational overhead (e.g., wall-clock time to select a batch) with the baseline methods is absent, which would be valuable information for a practitioner.

**Originality:** The work is original. While the concept of the Knowledge Gradient exists for the sequential setting, its generalization to the batch setting and the development of a scalable, gradient-based optimization algorithm are novel contributions. Unlike many existing parallel BO methods that build a batch greedily, q-KG optimizes the batch of points jointly, considering their collective information gain. While other methods also perform joint optimization (e.g., for parallel EI), the decision-theoretic principle behind q-KG is distinct and focuses on the quality of the final recommended point rather than the improvement over the best-observed value. The related work is adequately cited and the paper clearly distinguishes its contributions from prior art.

**Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of accelerating optimization through parallel function evaluations. The proposed q-KG method presents a strong new contender in the space of batch BO algorithms. The empirical results suggest that it advances the state of the art, particularly for problems with noisy evaluations, which are common in machine learning applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning where model training is stochastic or evaluation is done on a validation subset). The provision of open-source code further enhances the potential impact and allows other researchers and practitioners to use and build upon this work.

### Arguments for acceptance
*   **Pro:** Proposes a novel and principled acquisition function (q-KG) for batch Bayesian optimization, derived from a clear decision-theoretic standpoint.
*   **Pro:** The development of an efficient optimization procedure using IPA to estimate the gradient of the q-KG factor is a strong and non-trivial technical contribution.
*   **Pro:** The comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, with a particularly notable advantage in the challenging and highly relevant noisy setting.
*   **Pro:** The problem of parallelizing expensive black-box optimization is of high interest to the NIPS community.

### Arguments against acceptance
*   **Con:** The description of the dynamic discretization scheme is brief, and a deeper analysis of its implications for the gradient-based optimization of the acquisition function is missing.
*   **Con:** The paper lacks a discussion of the computational complexity and wall-clock time of the proposed method relative to its competitors, which is an important practical consideration.
