- Proposes q-KG, a parallel extension of the knowledge gradient acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization.  
- Defines q-KG decision-theoretically as the expected improvement in the GP predictor minimum after adding a batch of q points.  
- Uses infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an efficient gradient estimator for optimizing q-KG.  
- Empirically outperforms parallel EI, batch UCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic functions and ML hyperparameter tuning tasks.  
- Shows particularly strong robustness and performance in noisy evaluation settings.  
- Theoretical strength: provides a Bayes-optimal justification for a single-batch setting, contrasting with expected improvement assumptions.  
- Computational innovation: IPA-based gradient estimation addresses a key bottleneck and reduces Monte Carlo expense.  
- Comprehensive evaluation: tests on multiple synthetic benchmarks and real tasks (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR10 CNN).  
- Demonstrates practical impact, e.g., reducing CIFAR10 CNN test error from 14% to 11%.  
- Scalability concern: paper lacks analysis of scalability to large batch sizes (q) and high dimensions (d); discretization (M=1000) may be prohibitive.  
- Theoretical gap: unclear practical implications of Bayes-optimality and how q-KG translates to long-term regret minimization.  
- Comparative analysis shortfall: insufficient differentiation from other gradient-based batch acquisition methods and unclear theoretical distinctions.  
- Discretization choice (M=1000) appears arbitrary; paper does not study sensitivity or provide principled selection of M.  
- Noise assumption limitation: experiments assume constant noise variance; paper does not address heteroscedastic noise scenarios.  
- Revision requests: add computational complexity/scalability analysis, clarify theoretical distinctions, investigate M sensitivity, and discuss non-constant noise handling.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—methodologically significant and empirically strong but needs clarification on scalability and theory.
