This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization (BO) method, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), aiming to improve upon existing parallel BO approaches. The core idea is to leverage a decision-theoretic analysis to jointly optimize a batch of evaluations, rather than iteratively adding points based on single-evaluation acquisition functions. The authors also present a computationally efficient method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function, enabling its optimization.

While the motivation for improving parallel BO is sound – enabling efficient exploration of expensive objective functions – this paper falls short of convincing me of the significance of the proposed method. My primary concern revolves around the incremental nature of the contribution relative to the complexity introduced. The paper builds on existing knowledge gradient methods and extends them to the parallel setting, but the gains demonstrated, particularly in the synthetic experiments, are not substantial enough to justify the added methodological burden.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts. The mathematical derivations appear correct, and the IPA-based gradient estimation is a reasonable approach to address the computational challenges. The related work section is comprehensive, acknowledging relevant contributions in both sequential and parallel BO. The authors also provide code, which is commendable. 

However, the originality of the work feels limited. The core idea of extending the knowledge gradient to a batch setting is logical, but not groundbreaking. The authors acknowledge this, noting the connection to parallel EI and other batch methods. More critically, the performance improvements over established baselines, like parallel EI and UCB, are not consistently dramatic. The gains appear more pronounced in noisy settings, which is a positive aspect, but the noisy experiments lack sufficient detail regarding the noise levels used. The claim of “consistently finds better function values” is an overstatement given the experimental results. While q-KG performs well, the improvements are often marginal, and sometimes outperformed by simpler methods. 

The experimental setup also raises some concerns. The discretization of the search space (A) using samples from the posterior is a practical approach, but its sensitivity to the number of samples (M) is not thoroughly investigated. Furthermore, the comparison with existing methods lacks statistical rigor. Reporting only mean and standard deviation of the regret is insufficient; statistical significance tests are needed to definitively demonstrate the superiority of q-KG. The experiments also rely heavily on relatively simple synthetic functions. While the application to logistic regression and CNN tuning is valuable, the hyperparameter search spaces are not fully described, making it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear and well-written presentation of a logical extension of existing BO methods.
*   Addresses the computational challenges of parallel acquisition function optimization with IPA.
*   Demonstrates some performance improvements, particularly in noisy settings.
*   Code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution that does not significantly advance the state of the art.
*   Modest performance improvements that are not consistently observed across all experiments.
*   Lack of statistical significance testing in the experimental evaluation.
*   Limited description of hyperparameter search spaces in the practical applications.
*   The reliance on a discretization scheme without a thorough sensitivity analysis.



Overall, I believe this paper is a solid but not exceptional contribution. The technical soundness is good, and the paper is clearly written. However, the limited originality and modest performance gains, coupled with the lack of rigorous statistical analysis, make it difficult to justify acceptance to NIPS. I recommend **rejection** with the encouragement to conduct more extensive experiments, including statistical significance tests, and to thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of the method to key parameters like the discretization size (M). A clearer articulation of the theoretical advantages of q-KG over existing methods would also strengthen the paper.
