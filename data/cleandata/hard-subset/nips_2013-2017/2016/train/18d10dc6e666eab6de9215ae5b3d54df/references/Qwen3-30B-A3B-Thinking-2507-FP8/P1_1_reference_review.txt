

# Peer Review: "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that extends the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function from sequential to parallel settings. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that measures the information gain of evaluating q points simultaneously, rather than sequentially. They develop an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of q-KG, enabling its optimization. The method is evaluated on synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

The work builds upon the knowledge gradient method [17] and extends it to the parallel setting, differentiating itself from previous parallel BO approaches that typically use iterative greedy methods (e.g., parallel EI [2, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and parallel UCB with exploration [3]). The paper also relates to recent NIPS work on batch Bayesian optimization, including [18], [25], and [10], but makes a novel contribution by optimizing the batch collectively rather than iteratively.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for the q-KG acquisition function, deriving it from a decision-theoretic perspective. The extension of knowledge gradient to the parallel setting is well-motivated and represents a significant conceptual advance over previous parallel BO methods. The use of IPA for gradient estimation is both novel and well-justified.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The paper conducts thorough experiments across multiple dimensions (synthetic functions with 2-6 dimensions, real-world hyperparameter tuning with 4-8 parameters). The comparison with multiple state-of-the-art baselines (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) is well-designed, and the results consistently demonstrate the superiority of q-KG, especially in noisy settings.

3. **Practical Impact**: The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on real-world machine learning problems (logistic regression and CNN tuning), showing tangible improvements over existing methods. The practical relevance to hyperparameter tuning in machine learning is strong, as evidenced by the CNN improvement from 14% to 11% test error.

4. **Clarity and Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear, the algorithm is well-described (Algorithm 1), and the experimental setup is thoroughly explained. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

5. **Code Availability**: The authors have made their code publicly available on GitHub, which enhances reproducibility and adoption by the research community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Scalability Concerns**: While the paper acknowledges that naive maximization of q-KG would be computationally intensive, it does not provide a thorough analysis of how the computational complexity scales with q (the batch size) and d (the dimensionality). The discretization approach (M=1000 samples) may become prohibitively expensive for larger batch sizes or higher-dimensional problems, which is common in hyperparameter tuning (e.g., 10+ dimensions).

2. **Discretization Method Justification**: The discretization strategy (Section 5.3) is somewhat ad-hoc. The authors draw M=1000 samples from the global optima of the posterior GP, but don't provide a rigorous justification for this choice or explore how different discretization strategies might affect performance.

3. **Comparison with Recent Methods**: The paper could benefit from comparing against more recent batch BO methods. For example, [18] (Parallel Predictive Entropy Search) and [15] (Differentiating the multipoint expected improvement) have been published since the cited work. A comparison with these would strengthen the paper's claim of being state-of-the-art.

4. **High-Dimensional Limitations**: The empirical results are limited to problems with up to 6 dimensions. Hyperparameter tuning problems often involve higher dimensions (e.g., 10+), and it's unclear how well q-KG would perform in such settings. The authors should address this limitation more explicitly.

5. **Theoretical Guarantees**: While the paper claims q-KG is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the GP predictor with one batch remaining, it doesn't provide theoretical guarantees on the regret bounds or convergence properties of the full algorithm.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations and well-supported experimental results. The claims are well-justified by both theory and experiments. The work is complete, not merely a position paper, and the authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., computational complexity, discretization approach).

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, the algorithms are well-described, and the experimental setup is thoroughly explained. The writing is accessible to researchers in the field of Bayesian optimization.

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The extension of knowledge gradient to the parallel setting is novel and well-differentiated from previous work. The use of IPA for gradient estimation in this context is also novel. While there has been recent work on batch BO (e.g., [3, 5, 10, 18, 25]), the q-KG approach represents a distinct methodological advance by optimizing the batch collectively rather than iteratively.

## Significance Assessment

The results are significant for both theoretical and practical reasons. The method provides a demonstrable improvement over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in noisy settings where parallel evaluation is common. The practical applications to hyperparameter tuning in machine learning are compelling, with the CNN example showing a meaningful improvement in test error. The code availability further enhances the significance of the work.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with clear theoretical and empirical advantages over existing methods. The writing is excellent, the experiments are comprehensive, and the practical impact is significant.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel extension of knowledge gradient to parallel settings with clear theoretical foundation
- Efficient computational approach using IPA for gradient estimation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods
- Demonstrated practical impact on real machine learning problems
- Well-written, clear, and reproducible with code available

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Limited scalability analysis for high batch sizes or high-dimensional problems
- Discretization strategy lacks rigorous justification
- Could include comparisons with more recent batch BO methods
- Theoretical guarantees beyond the one-batch case are not provided

## Additional Comments for Authors

1. Consider adding a discussion on how the method scales with the batch size q and dimensionality d. A table or figure showing the computational time for different values of q and d would be valuable.

2. Justify the choice of M=1000 in Section 5.3. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance changes with different M values would strengthen the paper.

3. Compare against more recent batch BO methods like Parallel Predictive Entropy Search [18] and the gradient-based parallel EI method [15].

4. In the experiments, consider including a higher-dimensional problem (e.g., 10+ dimensions) to better reflect real-world hyperparameter tuning challenges.

5. The paper states that q-KG reduces to parallel EI in the noise-free case, but the derivation could be made more explicit to help readers understand this relationship.

This paper represents a solid contribution to Bayesian optimization that would be of interest to the NIPS community. The authors have done an excellent job of presenting their work clearly and thoroughly.
