- Proposal: compute Bayes-optimal parallel batch via the knowledge gradient (q-KG) and use infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients for acquisition maximization.  
- Experiments: three tests show q-KG is competitive on noiseless problems and outperforms others on noisy problems; includes artificial benchmarks and CNN/logistic regression on CIFAR10/MNIST.  
- Positive: parallel hyperparameter optimization is a useful idea for multi-core environments.  
- Concern: reviewer finds aspects unclear and is not fully convinced by the experimental evidence.  
- Technical critique: paper lacks wall-clock time measurements and comparisons to random search or sequential BO to show practical speedups.  
- Technical critique: no justification given for fixing batch size q = 4 across all experiments.  
- Novelty critique: using KG and IPA is not novel; similar IPA-based parallel selection (parallel EI/q-EI) exists (e.g., Wang et al. 2015).  
- Impact: parallel HPO methods are highly relevant to many application domains.  
- Clarity: background and technical details are well described and experiments are understandable.  
- Major question 1: Have you compared to Wang et al. (2015) / parallel EI (q-EI), and can you clearly state differences?  
- Major question 2: How does the method scale to larger q (e.g., tens/hundreds) and what benefits are expected for more parallel runs?  
- Major question 3: Can the synchronous method be extended to asynchronous parallel evaluations?  
- Major question 4: What is the wall-clock performance vs random search or sequential BO, and what is the overhead to compute each new batch?  
- Major question 5: Clarify the x-axis “iterations” in plots — batches or function evaluations, does it include initial design, and why do starting values differ across methods?  
- Minor comments: possible missing z^(1:n) in Eq.(4.1); grammar typo “achieve”→“achieves”; fix citation phrasing; Wang et al. (2015) should not be credited with introducing IPA.
