

The paper introduces a batch-optimization variant of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) algorithm for Bayesian optimization over finite domains, enabling parallel evaluation of multiple sampling points per iteration. The core contribution lies in extending KG to a batch setting where the algorithm selects multiple points simultaneously, rather than sequentially. To address the computational intractability of optimizing over candidate point sets, the authors propose an efficient gradient estimation method using Monte Carlo sampling and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), which approximates the gradient of the batch KG criterion. This allows scalable optimization of the information gain objective. The approach is evaluated on multiple benchmark problems, demonstrating competitive or superior performance against state-of-the-art methods based on Confidence Bounds.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents a practical method for batch Bayesian optimization. However, the novelty appears incremental rather than transformative. The extension of KG to batch settings is a natural progression from prior work (e.g., [citation]), and while the computational challenge of optimizing over multiple points necessitates a more sophisticated criterion, the core framework builds directly on established KG principles. The primary technical novelty resides in the IPA-based gradient estimation for the q-KG criterion—a technique that, while effectively implemented, aligns with standard approaches in gradient-based optimization for Bayesian methods. This does not constitute a fundamental advance but rather a competent adaptation of existing tools to a new setting.

A specific notational issue requires clarification: in Equation (3.1), the notation $x^{(1:n)}$ denotes a sequence of vectors, yet $\mu(\cdot)$ and $K(\cdot,\cdot)$ are defined as functions of individual vectors (or pairs of vectors), not sequences. This creates ambiguity—$\mu(x^{(1:n)})$ and $K(x^{(1:n)},x^{(1:n)})$ are undefined under standard interpretations of these functions. The authors should explicitly clarify whether $x^{(1:n)}$ represents a set (where $\mu$ and $K$ would operate on the set elements) or a sequence (where the functions require vector inputs), and adjust the notation accordingly to avoid confusion. For instance, if $\mu$ and $K$ are applied per point, the expressions should reference individual $x^{(i)}$ rather than the sequence. This is critical for reproducibility and technical accuracy.

Overall, the work provides a useful and well-executed extension to batch Bayesian optimization, with strong empirical results. The method is likely to be valuable for practitioners seeking efficient parallel evaluation strategies. Yet, the contribution’s theoretical novelty is modest, as it primarily leverages established KG theory and standard optimization techniques (IPA) for a new application context. The paper would benefit from a more precise discussion of how this batch KG variant differs fundamentally from prior extensions (e.g., those using Thompson sampling or expected improvement in batch settings) and a clearer justification for why IPA is preferable to alternative gradient estimation methods (e.g., finite differences) in this specific case. Without such distinctions, the incremental nature of the contribution is underemphasized.
