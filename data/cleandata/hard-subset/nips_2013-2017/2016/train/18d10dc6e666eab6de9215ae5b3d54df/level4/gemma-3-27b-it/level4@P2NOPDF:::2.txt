This paper addresses the important problem of parallelizing Bayesian optimization (BO), a technique increasingly popular for tasks like hyperparameter optimization. While standard BO methods are inherently sequential, this work proposes a novel algorithm for constructing Bayes-optimal batches, and demonstrates performance improvements over existing parallel BO approaches. The paper is clearly written and easy to understand. The concept of directly determining a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising, and the proposed method appears more elegant than many current parallelization strategies.

The authors appropriately leverage the common Gaussian process assumption of independent, normally distributed errors. However, it’s crucial to acknowledge the potential limitations of this assumption within the context of hyperparameter optimization. In this domain, "errors" typically represent the discrepancy between true generalization performance and empirical estimates obtained via methods like cross-validation, where dependencies and structural biases are frequently present, particularly with limited sample sizes. A discussion of this potential mismatch would strengthen the paper.

The theoretical contributions are strong; however, the experimental evaluation raises some concerns. First, the use of test set error as the sole optimization criterion is problematic. It’s well-established that test error can be a suboptimal objective for hyperparameter tuning, leading to misleading results [1]. Metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss would provide a more robust and informative evaluation.

Second, while MNIST and CIFAR-10 are common benchmark datasets, evaluating performance within the broader hyperparameter optimization landscape would be beneficial. Utilizing benchmarks from HPOlib [2], a library specifically designed for this purpose, would facilitate a more direct comparison with other state-of-the-art algorithms.

Finally, the limited scope of the experimental benchmark – only two datasets, a single task (classification), and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN) – restricts the generalizability of the findings. The relative performance of optimization algorithms can vary substantially depending on the learning algorithm and the characteristics of the search space. A more diverse benchmark would provide a more convincing assessment of the proposed method’s robustness and wider applicability.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution with a strong theoretical foundation. The proposed parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm represents an interesting and promising advance. However, the current experimental evaluation is too narrow to fully support the claims of superiority. Expanding the benchmark with more diverse datasets, tasks, learning algorithms, and evaluation metrics would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.

[1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." *ICML*. Vol. 98. 1998.

[2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." *NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice*. 2013.
