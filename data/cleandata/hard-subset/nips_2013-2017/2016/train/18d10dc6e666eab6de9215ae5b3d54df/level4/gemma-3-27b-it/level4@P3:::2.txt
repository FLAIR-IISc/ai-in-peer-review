This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm designed to efficiently select batches of points for parallel evaluation. The key contribution lies in deriving a method to compute a Bayes-optimal batch, offering a theoretically grounded approach to parallel BO. The method is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in practical hyperparameter optimization where simultaneous evaluations are readily available. The paper is clearly written and logically organized, making the core ideas accessible.

The work builds on a strong foundation in Bayesian optimization and knowledge gradient methods. While parallel BO has seen increasing attention (e.g., [3, 5, 18, 25]), the proposed approach distinguishes itself by explicitly aiming for Bayes optimality within a batch setting, rather than iteratively extending single-point acquisition functions. The efficient gradient-based optimization strategy developed to address the computational complexity of q-KG is a noteworthy contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is grounded in decision theory, offering a principled approach to batch selection.
*   **Bayes Optimality:** The pursuit of a Bayes-optimal batch is a compelling objective and sets this work apart from many existing parallel BO algorithms.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The development of an efficient gradient estimation technique (IPA) is crucial for practical application.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand, clearly explaining the method and its derivation.
*   **Strong Baselines:** The comparison against several relevant existing parallel BO methods is appropriate and demonstrates competitive performance.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Gaussian Process Assumption:** The assumption of independent, normally distributed noise in function evaluations may be limiting. As noted by the authors, this assumption is often unrealistic in hyperparameter optimization where noise arises from finite data estimates (e.g., cross-validation). Structural biases and correlations in these estimates are not accounted for.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate performance gains, the benchmark is somewhat limited in scope. The use of test error as the sole optimization criterion is potentially problematic, as highlighted by Provost et al. [1]. Metrics like area under the ROC curve or log-loss may provide a more robust evaluation signal.
*   **Benchmark Diversity:** The evaluation focuses on only two datasets (MNIST and CIFAR10) and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). The broader HPOlib benchmark suite [2] would offer a more comprehensive assessment of the method’s generalization ability across different problem characteristics.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study:** It would be useful to see an ablation study to understand the contribution of the different components of the q-KG method, particularly the IPA-based gradient estimation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian optimization.
*   The method addresses a practically relevant problem and offers a potentially significant improvement over existing techniques.
*   The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate competitive performance on the tested problems.
*   The code is publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The Gaussian process noise assumption may limit the applicability of the method in certain scenarios.
*   The empirical evaluation lacks diversity and relies on a potentially suboptimal performance metric.
*   The practical benefit over more easily implemented methods isn’t definitively established by the current experiments.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a valuable theoretical contribution. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened, the novelty and potential impact of the q-KG method warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the limitations in the empirical evaluation by (1) considering alternative performance metrics and (2) expanding the benchmark to include a wider range of datasets and machine learning algorithms, preferably using the HPOlib benchmark suite.  It would also be beneficial to briefly discuss the implications of violating the Gaussian process noise assumption and potential extensions to address it.
