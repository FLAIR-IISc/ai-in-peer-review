This paper proposes a new method for batch Bayesian Optimization, introducing a parallel version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) criterion, named q-KG. The paper begins with a review of related work and background on Gaussian Processes. It then formally defines the q-KG acquisition function, which is motivated from a decision-theoretic perspective as the one-step Bayes-optimal choice of points. A computationally efficient, gradient-based method for maximizing this new acquisition function is detailed. The paper concludes with a comprehensive set of numerical experiments on synthetic and real-world problems, demonstrating that q-KG generally outperforms several state-of-the-art batch Bayesian Optimization algorithms, particularly when function evaluations are noisy.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of parallelizing Bayesian optimization. The generalization of the Knowledge Gradient criterion to the batch setting is a novel and well-motivated contribution. The decision-theoretic grounding of q-KG is elegant and provides a strong theoretical basis for the method.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically solid. The derivation of an efficient, gradient-based optimization strategy using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to maximize the q-KG criterion is a key contribution that makes the method practical.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are thorough and convincing. The method is benchmarked against several relevant and state-of-the-art parallel Bayesian optimization algorithms across a range of synthetic functions and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks. The consistent and sometimes significant performance gains, especially in the noisy setting, strongly support the paper's claims.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation, methodology, and experimental setup are clearly explained.

Overall, this is a very strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The work is technically sound, novel, and addresses a problem of significant practical interest, making it an excellent candidate for publication at NIPS.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Missing Baseline for Speed-up:** The experimental evaluation provides a strong comparison of q-KG against other *batch* methods. However, it does not analyze the speed-up or performance gain relative to a strong *sequential* baseline (e.g., sequential KG run for `q` times as many iterations). Including such a comparison would help to better quantify the practical benefits and efficiency gains of the proposed parallelization scheme.
*   **Precision of Literature Review:** A few statements in the literature review could be more precise.
    *   The idea of averaging the Expected Improvement (EI) acquisition function with respect to the posterior distribution of previously selected points in the batch was discussed in Ginsbourger et al. (2010) in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization," alongside the "Constant Liar" heuristic.
    *   In the discussion of Chevalier and Ginsbourger [2], it might be worth mentioning their proposed CL-mix heuristic.
    *   The work of Marmin et al. (2015), "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design," also studies the maximization of q-EI using gradient-based methods, which is closely related to the optimization approach taken here.

**Questions and Suggestions for the Authors**

1.  **Theoretical Assumptions:** Could you please clarify the theoretical assumptions on the domain A (e.g., must it be compact?) and the objective function f (e.g., continuity)?
2.  **Initial Design:** The initial stage of the algorithm uses a Latin hypercube design. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to this specific choice compared to other space-filling designs?
3.  **Hyperparameter Estimation:** Algorithm 1, line 5, mentions re-training GP hyperparameters via MLE in each iteration. Is this optimization performed from scratch at each step, or are the hyperparameter values from the previous iteration used as a warm start? A bit more detail on this procedure would be helpful.
4.  **Definition of `g`:** In Section 5.2, the function `g` is defined as the difference between two minima. Could you please confirm this formulation is correct? It seems plausible that one of the terms might involve a maximization.
5.  **Differentiability of `g`:** The derivation of the gradient in equation (5.3) relies on interchanging the derivative and expectation operators, which requires `g` to be sufficiently smooth. Since `g` is defined via a `min` operator, its differentiability is not immediately obvious, particularly at points where the `argmin` is not unique. Could you comment on this or provide a reference to justify this step?
6.  **Cholesky Factor Derivative:** The gradient calculation involves the derivative of a Cholesky factor. As this is a non-trivial computation, could you please provide a reference or briefly outline how it is computed?
7.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experiments compare methods implemented in different open-source packages (MOE, Spearmint, Gpoptimization). To what extent were the underlying GP models (e.g., mean function, kernel, and hyperparameter fitting procedures) standardized across these different platforms to ensure the fairest possible comparison?
