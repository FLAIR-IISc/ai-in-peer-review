This paper develops a batch version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function. To select an optimal batch of design points, the authors propose a Monte Carlo–based gradient estimation method. Experiments suggest the proposed approach outperforms other batch Bayesian optimization (BO) methods.

1. Novelty and contribution relative to prior work. To my knowledge, batch KG has been studied previously; this paper is not the first to propose it. Please clarify the specific contribution of this work relative to prior batch KG papers such as [1] and [2]. My understanding is that your main contribution is the gradient estimation approach for optimizing the batch. If that is correct, the paper should explicitly compare the efficiency and effectiveness of your method with prior batch KG approaches—either empirically (head-to-head experiments under matched settings) or, where possible, via mathematical analysis (e.g., computational complexity, sample efficiency, or variance properties of the estimator).

2. Exposition of Section 5.2. Section 5.2 appears to be the core technical contribution, but it is too terse. Please expand this section to include clearer derivations, algorithmic steps (possibly pseudocode), assumptions required for the gradient estimator, and a discussion of computational complexity and convergence/stability. This added detail is essential for reproducibility and for understanding when and why the estimator performs well.

3. Fairness of comparisons under observation noise. Expected Improvement (EI) is typically defined for noiseless observations. As a result, the comparison in the noisy setting may be unfair if a noise-aware EI variant was not used. Please clarify which EI implementation was employed in your experiments. If plain EI was used, the results in Section 6.2 should be interpreted and discussed with the caveat that EI does not account for observation noise, and this limitation should be made explicit.

4. Notational consistency. In line 162, the abbreviation “IPA” is redefined. Please avoid redefining acronyms; define them once and use them consistently throughout.
