

The paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient algorithm integrated with Bayesian modeling to optimize multivariate objective functions over finite domains. The core innovation lies in its iterative identification of multiple points for objective evaluation, enabling parallel computation. The authors frame the point selection problem within the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, which quantifies the expected objective improvement from adding a single sample. This is generalized to batch selection, where multiple points are chosen simultaneously. To address computational intractability, the authors propose an algorithm leveraging Monte Carlo methods and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients for maximizing information gain across candidate sampling sets. Experimental results demonstrate the method either outperforms or significantly competes with existing Confidence Bounds-based approaches across benchmark problems. The paper is well-written and holds practical relevance. However, the novelty appears limited as the primary contribution is extending the established KG framework to a batch setting, requiring a more sophisticated optimization strategy for the batch criterion. The key technical novelty, in my view, resides in the IPA-based estimation of the q-KG gradient, which remains relatively standard. A specific comment: Equation (3.1) uses x^(1:n) to denote a finite sequence of vectors, but the definitions of μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n),x^(1:n)) are unclear, as μ and K are typically defined for single or pairs of vectors, not sequences.
