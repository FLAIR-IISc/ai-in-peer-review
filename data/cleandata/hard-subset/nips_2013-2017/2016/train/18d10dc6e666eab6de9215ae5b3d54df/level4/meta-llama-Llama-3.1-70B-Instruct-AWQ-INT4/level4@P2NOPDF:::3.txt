The paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient algorithm combined with Bayesian modeling to optimize a multivariate objective function over a finite domain. The key innovation of the algorithm lies in its ability to identify multiple points for objective function computation in each iteration, allowing for parallelization. The challenge is to determine the optimal sampling points, which the authors address by adopting the Knowledge Gradient framework. This approach estimates the expected improvement in the objective value with an additional sampling point and decides whether and which point to use. The authors extend this framework to make decisions on multiple sampling points in batch, necessitating the development of a computationally efficient algorithm. To achieve this, they employ Monte Carlo and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis to estimate relevant gradients, maximizing information gain over candidate sampling sets.

The algorithm is evaluated on several test problems, demonstrating competitive performance with existing approaches, primarily those based on Confidence Bounds. The paper is well-written and has potential practical applications. However, the level of novelty may not be substantial, as the authors build upon the previously studied Knowledge Gradient (KG) approach by expanding it to a batch setting. This extension requires a more sophisticated method for optimizing the criterion over multiple points. In my opinion, the primary novelty lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient using an Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) approach, which, although valuable, is a relatively standard technique.

One specific comment I have is regarding equation (3.1). Since x^(1:n) is a finite sequence of vectors, the definitions of mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n),x^(1:n)) are unclear. The functions mu and K are defined for one or two vectors, not sequences. Clarification on this point would be beneficial.
