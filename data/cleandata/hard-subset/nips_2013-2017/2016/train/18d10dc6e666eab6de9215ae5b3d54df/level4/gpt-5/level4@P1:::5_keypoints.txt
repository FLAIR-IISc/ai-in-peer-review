- Paper proposes a batch (q-)Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) for Bayesian optimization with an IPA-based Monte Carlo gradient estimator to jointly optimize continuous batches.  
- Strength: Clear decision-theoretic motivation—q-KG directly targets expected reduction in posterior minimum after one batch.  
- Strength: Practical optimization strategy—unbiased stochastic gradient via IPA makes joint batch selection computationally feasible.  
- Strength: Empirical results show competitive or superior performance to strong baselines, with especially large gains under noisy evaluations.  
- Main concern: Novelty unclear because batch KG exists in prior work; authors should clarify contributions relative to prior batch KG papers [1],[2].  
- Request: Empirical or theoretical comparison to prior batch KG methods (sample efficiency, wall-clock time, scalability with q and dimension) to establish incremental contribution.  
- Section 5.2 needs substantial elaboration—it's the core technical contribution and currently lacks detail.  
- Request: Provide full derivation of the unbiased gradient estimator and precise regularity conditions (differentiability of µ and K, argmin handling, and conditions for interchanging expectation and differentiation).  
- Request: Explain handling of non-differentiability from the min over a discrete set (argmin uniqueness, ties, almost-sure considerations).  
- Request: Report variance and stability of the gradient estimator, any variance-reduction techniques, and computational complexity per gradient evaluation.  
- Request: Describe convergence behavior of stochastic gradient ascent (step-size policy, multi-start strategy), sensitivity to initialization, and practical optimization details.  
- Fairness concern: EI used in noisy experiments must be specified; if standard (noiseless) EI was used, comparisons in noisy settings are unfair.  
- Request: If plain EI was used, discuss Section 6.2 results in light of EI's mismatch to noise and explain how q-KG handles noise differently.  
- Minor issue: IPA abbreviation is redefined—define it once and use consistently.  
- NIPS criteria: Quality—well-motivated and strong empirical results but Section 5.2 lacks technical detail for correctness and reproducibility.  
- NIPS criteria: Clarity—generally organized but Section 5.2 and noisy EI baseline specification need improvement.  
- NIPS criteria: Originality—batch KG prior art exists; main novelty appears to be the IPA-based computational strategy but needs direct comparison to prior work.  
- NIPS criteria: Significance—empirical noise-robust gains suggest impact if computational advantages over prior batch KG are demonstrated.  
- Pros for acceptance: decision-theoretic foundation, strong noisy-setting performance, open-source code and multiple-baseline comparisons.  
- Cons for acceptance: incomplete positioning vs prior batch KG, insufficient technical detail in gradient estimation, and potentially unfair noisy-baseline comparisons.
