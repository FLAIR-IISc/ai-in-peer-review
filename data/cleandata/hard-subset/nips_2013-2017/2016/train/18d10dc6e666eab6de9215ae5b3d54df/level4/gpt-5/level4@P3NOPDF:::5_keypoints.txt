Summary: Proposes a batch Knowledge Gradient (KG) with a Monte Carlo gradient estimator for optimizing batches; empirical results show competitive or superior performance.

Strength: Clear motivation for batch BO and gradient-based batch optimization.

Strength: The Monte Carlo gradient estimator appears practical, scalable, and enables direct optimization of batch-KG.

Strength: Empirical results are promising against existing batch BO baselines.

Concern 1 — Novelty/positioning: Explicitly delineate novelty relative to prior batch-KG work and add a focused related-work subsection.

Concern 1 — Comparisons in related work: Compare modeling assumptions, objective definitions, and optimization strategies to prior works [1],[2].

Concern 1 — Estimator novelty: Highlight which estimator properties (unbiasedness, variance control, reparameterization vs LR vs IPA) are new.

Concern 1 — Complexity: Discuss computational complexity per iteration and wall-clock implications.

Concern 2 — Empirical comparisons: Directly compare to prior batch-KG implementations on final objective vs evaluations, wall-clock time, and sensitivity to batch size and noise.

Concern 2 — Controlled baseline: If prior implementations aren’t available, compare same KG objective optimized with alternatives (random restarts, local search, greedy) to isolate estimator benefits.

Concern 3 — Section 5.2 detail: Provide precise estimator statement, assumptions, and justification for interchange of differentiation and expectation.

Concern 3 — Estimator properties: Clarify unbiasedness (or asymptotic), characterize variance, and describe variance-reduction techniques and their impact.

Concern 3 — Optimization details: Specify step-size schedules, stopping criteria, constraint handling, initialization, and provide convergence/smoothness/regularity discussion.

Concern 3 — Complexity scaling: Give per-iteration computational complexity as function of batch size, Monte Carlo samples, and dimension.

Concern 4 — Noisy comparisons fairness: Clarify whether noise-aware EI variants were used; if plain EI was used, discuss its limitations and add a noise-aware EI baseline or ablation.

Concern 5 — Terminology/notation: Define IPA once and consistently, introduce all acronyms at first use, and unify notation for gradients, expectations, and sampling.

Suggestion — Ablations and robustness: Include ablations over Monte Carlo sample count, batch size, dimension, kernel hyperparameters, noise variance, and report variability across seeds with confidence intervals.

Suggestion — Practical guidance: Provide heuristics for sample counts, step sizes, initialization, and discuss failure modes (high-dimensions, multimodality).

Suggestion — Comparators and clarity: Consider adding UCB/q-EI baselines or justify exclusions and include pseudo-code/schematic for reproducibility.

Minor — References and proofreading: Ensure complete/stable citations (especially [2]/thesis), fix grammatical issues, and state explicitly if common-random-number coupling is used.

Overall assessment: Paper addresses an important problem with a useful Monte Carlo estimator and promising results, but needs clearer positioning vs prior batch KG, expanded Section 5.2, and fair/noise-aware empirical comparisons for a strong contribution.
