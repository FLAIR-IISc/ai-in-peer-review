Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization method that selects a set of q points by maximizing a parallel knowledge gradient acquisition function. The decision-theoretic motivation mirrors the sequential KG criterion: when only one batch remains, q-KG selects the Bayes-optimal batch for minimizing the posterior predictive minimum. Because exact evaluation and optimization of q-KG are intractable for continuous domains, the authors (i) discretize the search space for acquisition evaluation, and (ii) develop a stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize the batch jointly. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10) show that q-KG is competitive in the noise-free case and provides larger gains in the presence of observation noise, compared to parallel EI (both MOE and Spearmint variants), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE.

The work builds on a rich literature: sequential KG for continuous domains (e.g., Frazier et al., Scott et al.), parallel EI with closed-form expressions or gradient-based optimization (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Wang et al.), parallel PES (Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani), and UCB-based batch policies (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.). The key conceptual step here is extending KG to the parallel setting with efficient gradient estimation.

Quality
- The decision-theoretic foundation is sound: q-KG is optimal for a single remaining batch under the GP posterior. The reduction to parallel EI in the noiseless, “final solution from sampled points” case is correct.
- The computational approach (Monte Carlo evaluation with IPA-based stochastic gradients) is plausible and aligns with prior work on q-EI optimization.
- However, the empirical evaluation omits wall-clock measurements and an analysis of computational overhead, which is essential for batch BO. It also fixes q=4 without discussing scalability of computation or performance for larger batches.
- The discretization strategy for computing q-KG is reasonable but may introduce bias; there is no sensitivity analysis w.r.t. M or the sampling scheme.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with clear background and method sections. Algorithmic steps are easy to follow, and the empirical setup is described sufficiently to reproduce the comparisons.
- A few notational and stylistic issues remain (see Minor comments).

Originality
- The use of KG as a batch acquisition is a natural extension of sequential KG; novelty lies in adapting the criterion to parallel decisions and enabling efficient joint optimization using IPA.
- IPA for optimizing batch acquisitions has been used in prior work (e.g., for q-EI); thus the computational contribution feels incremental rather than entirely new.

Significance
- Batch BO is practically important, particularly for hyperparameter tuning in parallel computing environments and in noisy settings. Demonstrated robustness to noise is a valuable contribution.
- The lack of wall-clock comparisons and limited exploration of larger batch sizes temper the practical impact.

Strengths
- Principled, Bayes-optimal acquisition for a single-step batch decision.
- Solid treatment of noisy evaluations; empirical results show clear benefits under noise.
- Joint (non-greedy) batch selection with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator.
- Comparisons against multiple strong baselines and release of code.

Weaknesses
- No wall-clock or overhead analysis; unclear tradeoff between improved sample efficiency and acquisition optimization time.
- Scalability to large q is not explored; all experiments use q=4.
- Limited discussion of asynchronous execution and of integration into real parallel environments.
- Incremental novelty relative to q-EI with IPA; relationship to closely related methods could be clarified.
- Dependence on discretization for acquisition evaluation without sensitivity analysis.

Arguments for acceptance
- Well-motivated, principled extension of KG to batch settings.
- Strong performance in noisy scenarios, which are common in practice.
- Methodological clarity and potential for adoption in hyperparameter tuning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation lacks wall-clock analysis and scalability studies for larger batches.
- Computational novelty is moderate given existing IPA-based optimization of batch acquisitions.
- Some ambiguity in comparisons to prior q-EI implementations may confuse readers.

Questions for the authors
1) Baselines and nomenclature: Did you compare directly to the IPA-optimized q-EI of Wang et al. (MOE)? If so, please use consistent terminology (e.g., “q-EI (MOE)” vs “Spearmint’s parallel EI”) and clearly distinguish these implementations and their optimization strategies.
2) Scalability in batch size: Modern systems routinely support >10 parallel workers (e.g., 25–800 in prior studies). How does q-KG’s computational cost and performance scale with q? Can you report results and runtimes for larger q?
3) Asynchronous settings: Can q-KG be extended to asynchronous batch BO (e.g., filling available workers as they free up)? If so, what modifications are required for the acquisition and optimization?
4) Wall-clock evaluation: How does q-KG compare to random search and sequential BO in terms of end-to-end time to reach a target performance? What is the overhead per batch for optimizing q-KG versus q-EI?
5) Plot axes: In your figures, does “iterations” count batches or total function evaluations? Does it include the initial design? If included, why do curves not start from the same value?

Minor comments
- Eq. (4.1): Please double-check the conditioning; is there missing notation (e.g., conditioning on z(1:q) or x(1:n), y(1:n))?
- “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” → “achieves.”
- Use author-year/named citations in prose: e.g., “Snoek et al. [16] suggest…” rather than “[16] suggests…”.
- Cite foundational IPA references (e.g., L’Ecuyer) when introducing IPA; Wang et al. (2015) apply IPA but do not introduce it.
- Clarify the discretization scheme (size M, resampling per iteration) and provide a brief sensitivity analysis if possible.

Overall evaluation
A solid and well-presented contribution with clear advantages in noisy settings and a principled acquisition. The main shortcomings are limited scalability evidence and missing wall-clock and asynchronous evaluations. With clarifications and additional experiments addressing these points, the paper would be significantly strengthened.
