Bayesian optimization is widely used for tasks such as hyperparameter tuning, yet most existing methods are inherently sequential. This paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that selects Bayes-optimal batches. The authors demonstrate that their approach surpasses existing BO methods. The manuscript is clear and well organized. Parallel BO is practically important for hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed method is appealing and more principled than many alternatives I know. The ability to choose a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly compelling.

The method assumes independent, normally distributed observation noise, a standard assumption in GP-based BO. However, in hyperparameter optimization this can be problematic because observation noise reflects discrepancies between true generalization performance and its empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation). Systematic bias can arise in portions of the hyperparameter space, especially with small sample sizes. It would be worth acknowledging that the independence assumption may be violated in such settings.

While the theoretical development is strong, the experimental evaluation is less convincing for three primary reasons:
1. Using test set error as the optimization target is problematic; it is well known that such threshold-based metrics are suboptimal for model selection and hyperparameter optimization (e.g., [1]). Metrics such as AUC or log loss would be more appropriate and informative.
2. Although MNIST and CIFAR10 are common benchmarks, I would have preferred to see results on HPOlib [2], which is a widely used suite specifically designed for benchmarking hyperparameter optimization algorithms.
3. The benchmark includes only two datasets and focuses solely on a single task (classification) with two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). Greater diversity would strengthen the evaluation, as different optimizers can excel on different model families and tasks.

Overall, I like the paper; the theoretical contribution is substantial and valuable. However, the current empirical study is too limited to be fully persuasive.

[1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. “The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms.” ICML, 1998.
[2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. “Towards an empirical foundation for assessing Bayesian optimization of hyperparameters.” NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice, 2013.
