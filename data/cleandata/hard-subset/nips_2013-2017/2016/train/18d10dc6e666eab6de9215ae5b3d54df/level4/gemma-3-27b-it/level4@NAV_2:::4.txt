This paper considers the parallel knowledge gradient method for batch Bayesian Optimization. The authors address the scenario where function evaluations are time-consuming, proposing a novel acquisition function, q-KG, capable of suggesting multiple points for parallel evaluation. They also present an efficient approach for evaluating this q-KG function. Empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed method. The problem addressed is important; enabling distributed computation is crucial for improving the performance of data-driven applications. Instead of sequential evaluation, the algorithm leverages q knowledge gradients to propose q points for parallel evaluation, significantly enhancing efficiency. This is an interesting and worthwhile direction for exploration. However, the assumption of a Gaussian process may be questionable, particularly when applied to neural network architectures with high-dimensional input spaces. It would be beneficial if the authors could provide theoretical results regarding the error bounds of their method and a quantitative analysis of its computational complexity with respect to dimensionality. Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The method is sound and the experimental validation is thorough. The paper is generally well-written. Specific suggestions include: line 36, rephrase “the set of points to evaluate next that is” for conciseness; line 95, clarify the definition of A; line 104, specify the input of µ<sup>n</sup>; line 128, briefly discuss the parallel EI algorithm to provide context; and line 140, consider using distinct notations for the different instances of A to avoid ambiguity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important practical problem in Bayesian Optimization: parallel evaluation.
*   Proposes a novel and theoretically grounded acquisition function (q-KG).
*   Provides an efficient computational method for optimizing q-KG.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world problems, especially in noisy settings.
*   The paper is well-written and technically sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The assumption of a Gaussian process may limit applicability to high-dimensional problems.
*   Lack of theoretical analysis regarding error bounds and computational complexity.
