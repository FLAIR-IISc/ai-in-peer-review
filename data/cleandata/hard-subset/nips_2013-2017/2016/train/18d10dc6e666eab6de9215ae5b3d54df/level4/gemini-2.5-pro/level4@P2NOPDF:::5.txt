This paper presents a batch Bayesian optimization method based on the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function. The core technical contribution is a gradient estimation technique, which uses Monte-Carlo simulation, to optimize the selection of a batch of points to evaluate in parallel. The experimental results suggest that the proposed method outperforms several other batch Bayesian optimization algorithms on the tested problems.

While the approach is interesting and the results appear promising, I have several major concerns regarding the novelty, clarity of the methodology, and fairness of the experimental evaluation.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Novelty and Comparison to Prior Work:** A primary concern is the positioning of this work with respect to existing literature on batch Knowledge Gradient methods. The concept of a batch KG policy is not new, with prior work such as [1, 2] exploring this area. The paper should more clearly articulate its specific contributions in this context. It appears the main novelty lies in the proposed Monte-Carlo-based gradient estimation for optimizing the batch selection. If this is the case, it is crucial to benchmark the proposed optimization approach against the methods used in previous batch KG papers. Without a direct comparison—either through experimental results or theoretical analysis—it is difficult to assess the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed gradient estimation technique relative to prior art in batch KG optimization.

2.  **Insufficient Detail in Core Methodology:** The core methodology is presented in Section 5.2, but this section lacks sufficient detail. As this gradient estimation procedure is the central contribution of the paper, it warrants a more thorough explanation. To improve the clarity and reproducibility of the work, the authors should elaborate on the derivation, provide more details about the implementation, and discuss any underlying assumptions of the proposed estimator.

3.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** The experimental comparison with Expected Improvement (EI) in the noisy setting (Section 6.2) may be inappropriate. The standard EI acquisition function is derived under the assumption of noiseless observations and is not designed to handle observation noise. The authors should clarify which variant of EI was used as a baseline. If the standard, noiseless EI was used, the comparison is potentially misleading, as the baseline is being applied in a setting for which it was not designed. In this case, the authors should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and discuss how EI's inability to account for observation noise might explain its performance. A more appropriate comparison would involve a noise-aware variant of EI.
