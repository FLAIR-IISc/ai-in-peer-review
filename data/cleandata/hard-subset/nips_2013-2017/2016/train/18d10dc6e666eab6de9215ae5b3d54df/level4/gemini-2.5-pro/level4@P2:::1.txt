This paper introduces a novel method for batch Bayesian Optimization, termed the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The work extends the decision-theoretic Knowledge Gradient (KG) criterion to the parallel setting, where a batch of `q` points can be evaluated simultaneously. The paper provides a formulation for the q-KG acquisition function, which aims to select a batch that is one-step Bayes-optimal. To make the maximization of this acquisition function computationally feasible, the authors propose an efficient gradient estimation technique based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through extensive numerical experiments on synthetic benchmark functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks for machine learning models, showing superior performance compared to existing state-of-the-art batch Bayesian Optimization algorithms, especially in noisy environments.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles the highly relevant and practical problem of accelerating Bayesian Optimization through parallelization. The proposed q-KG method is a principled and well-motivated extension of the sequential Knowledge Gradient criterion. Unlike many heuristic batch methods, q-KG is derived from a clear decision-theoretic framework, aiming to maximize the expected value of information from a batch of evaluations. This represents a significant and novel contribution to the batch Bayesian Optimization literature.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical contributions are sound. The derivation of the q-KG factor as the expected improvement in the posterior mean's minimum is clear and logical. A key strength is the development of a computationally tractable method for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function. The use of IPA to obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator is a sophisticated and effective approach that enables the use of standard gradient-based optimizers for a problem that would otherwise be computationally prohibitive. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple strong baselines on a variety of problems, including both synthetic and real-world applications in both noise-free and noisy settings. The strong performance, particularly in the noisy case, convincingly supports the authors' claims.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide sufficient background on Gaussian Processes and Bayesian Optimization, making the paper accessible. The motivation for the q-KG method is well-explained, and the algorithmic details are laid out systematically.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Context in Related Work:** The literature review is generally good but could be slightly more precise in acknowledging prior work. For instance, the idea of integrating the Expected Improvement (EI) acquisition function with respect to the posterior distribution of previously selected points in a batch was already a central theme in Ginsbourger et al. ("Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization," 2010), which also introduced the "Constant Liar" heuristic. Mentioning this would provide a richer context for the development of parallel EI methods. Furthermore, the discussion could benefit from citing Marmin et al. ("Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design," 2015), which also explores gradient-based optimization for a parallel EI criterion.

2.  **Experimental Analysis:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the sample efficiency (i.e., immediate regret vs. number of evaluations) of q-KG. However, the analysis would be more complete with a discussion of the computational cost. Optimizing the q-KG acquisition function via stochastic gradients is more complex than the greedy procedures used by some baselines. A comparison of the wall-clock time required per iteration or an analysis of the trade-off between computational overhead and sample efficiency would be very valuable for practitioners. Additionally, the experiments focus on comparing batch algorithms but do not include a study of the speed-up achieved by the batch approach (q-KG with q>1) compared to its sequential counterpart (KG with q=1).

3.  **Clarity of Technical Assumptions:** Some technical assumptions underlying the gradient computation could be stated more explicitly in the main text. The application of IPA to the function `g` in Section 5.2 relies on its smoothness, which is not self-evident due to the `argmin` operator. While details may be in the supplementary material, a brief justification in the main paper regarding the conditions under which this interchange of expectation and differentiation is valid would strengthen the presentation. Similarly, the differentiability of the Cholesky factor is a non-trivial detail that could be briefly addressed.

**Detailed Questions and Minor Remarks:**

*   What are the formal assumptions on the objective function `f` and the domain `A` (e.g., continuity of `f`, compactness of `A`)?
*   In Algorithm 1, the initial design is a Latin Hypercube. Is there a particular reason for this choice over other space-filling designs or simple random sampling?
*   It is good that Algorithm 1 mentions re-training the GP hyperparameters, as this is a crucial step.
*   In the definition of `g` in Section 5.2, the expression is `min(mu) - min(mu_new)`. Is this correct? Typically, improvement is framed as `current_best - new_best`, which might involve a `max` operation if dealing with maximization, or if `new_best` can be worse than `current_best`. Please double-check the formulation.
*   There appears to be a typo in Section 6: the kernel used is likely the Matérn 5/2, not 2/5.
*   Regarding the experiments, were the underlying GP models and hyperparameter fitting procedures implemented consistently across all benchmark methods? Since different open-source packages were used, clarifying this would help ascertain that performance differences are attributable to the acquisition functions themselves rather than implementation artifacts.

**Conclusion:**

This is a very strong paper presenting a novel, principled, and effective method for batch Bayesian Optimization. The work is technically solid and the empirical results are compelling. The weaknesses are minor and primarily relate to providing additional context and experimental analysis. I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. The proposed q-KG method is likely to be impactful and widely used by researchers and practitioners working on expensive black-box optimization problems.
