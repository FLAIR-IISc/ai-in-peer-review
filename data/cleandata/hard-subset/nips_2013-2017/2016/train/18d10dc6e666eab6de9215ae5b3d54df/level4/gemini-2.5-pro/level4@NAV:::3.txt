This paper introduces a novel variant of a stochastic gradient algorithm, integrated with Bayesian modeling, to optimize a multi-dimensional objective function over a discrete search space. The key contribution of the algorithm is its capacity to identify a batch of points for parallel objective function evaluation in each iteration. The central challenge lies in identifying these sampling points according to an optimality criterion. For this, the authors leverage the Knowledge Gradient framework, which estimates the anticipated gain in the objective value from an additional sample to inform the selection process. This work extends this concept to a batch setting, where the decision is made concurrently for multiple sampling points.

Given that this problem is computationally intractable, the authors propose a method that utilizes Monte Carlo simulation and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis to estimate the relevant gradients, thereby maximizing the information gain over the set of candidate sampling batches. The algorithm's performance is demonstrated on several benchmark problems, where it is shown to be superior to or highly competitive with other methods in the literature, especially those employing Confidence Bounds.

The manuscript is well-presented and the proposed method appears potentially valuable for practical applications. However, I am not persuaded that the novelty is substantial, as the authors primarily extend a previously-established approach (KG) to a batch processing context. This extension necessitates a more sophisticated method for optimizing the selection criterion over a set of multiple points. In my opinion, the main novelty lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient using an IPA approach, which is itself a fairly standard technique.

I have one specific suggestion for clarification: In Equation (3.1), the notation `x^(1:n)` denotes a finite sequence of vectors. It is ambiguous how `mu(x^(1:n))` and `K(x(1:n),x(1:n))` are defined, given that `mu` and `K` are presented as functions that take one or two vectors as input, not sequences.
