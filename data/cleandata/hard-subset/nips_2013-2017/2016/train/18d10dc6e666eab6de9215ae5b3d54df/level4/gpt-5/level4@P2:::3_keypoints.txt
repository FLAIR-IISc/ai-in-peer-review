- Introduces q-KG: a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that selects q points to maximize expected reduction in GP posterior predictor’s minimum after one batch.  
- Shows q-KG is one-step Bayes-optimal (one batch remaining) and reduces to parallel EI in the noise-free sampled-point recommendation limit.  
- Uses Monte Carlo estimation and IPA-based stochastic gradients for acquisition evaluation and batch optimization; discretizes inner minimization adaptively for continuous domains.  
- Empirical results: outperforms or matches parallel EI and UCB-based batch methods on synthetic and hyperparameter tuning tasks, with strong gains under noisy evaluations; code released.  
- Well positioned relative to prior work: extends KG from sequential to batch and provides a differentiable gradient-based optimizer for continuous domains, building on reparameterization/IPA ideas from batch EI.  
- Strength: principled decision-theoretic formulation directly targeting posterior-minimum improvement.  
- Strength: naturally handles noisy evaluations with pronounced empirical advantages.  
- Strength: practical IPA-based optimization enabling non-greedy batch selection.  
- Strength: solid empirical evaluation against strong baselines and publicly available code.  
- Strength: clarifies relation to parallel EI via reduction result.  
- Weakness: incremental novelty—main advance is extending KG to batches and standard IPA-style gradient estimator.  
- Weakness: discretization of inner minimization set A may introduce approximation bias; sensitivity to discretization size M not quantified.  
- Weakness: computational cost and scalability (dependence on q, MC samples, dimension, Cholesky calls) not fully characterized; no wall-clock comparisons.  
- Weakness: theoretical guarantees limited to one-step optimality; no multi-batch regret or convergence analysis.  
- Weakness: some implementation details (MC sample counts, gradient variance control, step sizes/restarts) are underspecified, risking reproducibility issues.  
- Quality: technically sound derivations; IPA/unbiased gradients reasonable under smoothness; discretization practical but error unanalyzed; experiments support claims.  
- Clarity: generally well written and organized, but minor notational issues and typos need fixing.  
- Originality: moderate—useful evolutionary step rather than a major conceptual breakthrough.  
- Significance: high practical relevance for parallel hyperparameter tuning and noisy expensive evaluations.  
- Suggestions: add complexity/runtime analysis and wall-clock benchmarks; include sensitivity/ablation studies for MC samples, q, M, and variance reduction; discuss numerical stability and Cholesky differentiation; clarify noise-variance estimation and effects of misspecification.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled batch acquisition, strong empirical performance under noise, practical optimization method with released code.  
- Cons for acceptance: incremental novelty, discretization approximation without sensitivity analysis, missing runtime and some reproducibility details.  
- Overall: solid, practically useful contribution with clear empirical benefits in noisy settings, though novelty is mainly methodological and some analyses/details are missing.
