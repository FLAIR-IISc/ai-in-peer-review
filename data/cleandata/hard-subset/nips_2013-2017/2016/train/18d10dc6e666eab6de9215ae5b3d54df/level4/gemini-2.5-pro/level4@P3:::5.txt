This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization. The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, aiming to select a batch of points that provides the one-step Bayes-optimal improvement in the final solution quality, defined as the minimum of the posterior mean function. A key contribution of the paper is the development of an efficient computational strategy to maximize this acquisition function. The authors propose using a Monte Carlo approach to estimate the q-KG value and, crucially, derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling the use of gradient-based optimization. This work extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) framework to the parallel setting. The proposed method is evaluated on a suite of synthetic functions and two hyperparameter tuning tasks, demonstrating strong empirical performance, particularly in settings with noisy function evaluations, where it often outperforms other batch Bayesian optimization methods like parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and parallel Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) variants.

**Strengths:**

*   **Principled Formulation:** The q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated and derived from a clear decision-theoretic principle (one-step Bayes-optimality). This provides a strong theoretical foundation, distinguishing it from more heuristic or greedy approaches to batch construction.
*   **Novel Computational Approach:** The main technical contribution—the application of IPA to derive an efficient and unbiased gradient estimator for the q-KG function—is significant. This makes the optimization of a complex, non-greedy batch acquisition function over continuous domains tractable, which is a notable advance.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering both synthetic and practical machine learning problems in noise-free and noisy settings. The results convincingly demonstrate that q-KG is a state-of-the-art method, showing substantial improvements over existing benchmarks, especially when evaluations are noisy. This highlights the practical significance of the proposed method.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code available, which is commendable and greatly benefits the community by facilitating reproducibility and future research.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Positioning with Respect to Prior Work:** The paper's novelty concerning previous work on batch knowledge gradient methods could be stated more clearly. The authors cite Wang et al. (2015), which also investigates a batch KG policy. While the paper mentions that Wang et al. focus on a discrete feasible set, the distinction and the specific contributions of this work (e.g., the gradient-based optimization for continuous domains) should be more prominently articulated in the related work section. An explicit discussion comparing the computational efficiency or applicability of the proposed method against that of Wang et al. would strengthen the paper's claim to originality.

2.  **Clarity of the Gradient Estimation Section:** Section 5.2, which describes the gradient estimation via IPA, is the technical core of the paper but is presented quite concisely. While supplementary material may contain the full derivation, expanding this section with more intermediate steps or intuition would improve the paper's self-containedness and help readers better appreciate this key contribution.

3.  **Fairness of Experimental Baselines in Noisy Settings:** The comparison against Expected Improvement (EI) in the noisy evaluation settings (Section 6.2) may not be entirely fair. The standard EI acquisition function is derived under the assumption of noise-free observations. When applied to a noisy problem, its performance can degrade as it does not explicitly model the observation noise in its improvement calculation. It would be more convincing to compare q-KG against a variant of EI specifically designed for noisy functions (e.g., by integrating out the noise in the improvement calculation). The authors should clarify which version of parallel EI was used and, if it was the standard noise-free version, they should discuss this limitation and how it might affect the interpretation of the results.

4.  **Minor Issue:** The acronym "IPA" is defined in the introduction and then defined again in Section 5.2 (line 162). This is a minor point that can be easily corrected for improved readability.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a theoretically sound and practically effective method for batch Bayesian optimization. The core contributions are the principled formulation of the q-KG acquisition function and the novel, efficient method for its optimization. While the paper is strong, its impact could be further enhanced by clarifying its positioning relative to prior batch KG work and by ensuring the experimental comparisons in the noisy setting are made against the most appropriate baselines.
