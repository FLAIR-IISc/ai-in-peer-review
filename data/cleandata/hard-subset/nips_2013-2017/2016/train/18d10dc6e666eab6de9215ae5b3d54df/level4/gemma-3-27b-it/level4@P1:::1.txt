This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) criterion, extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient approach to parallel evaluations. The authors provide a theoretical foundation for q-KG, detail its computation, and demonstrate its effectiveness through a series of numerical experiments where it outperforms several state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization algorithms. This is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field, given the importance of parallelizing Bayesian optimization for practical applications.

The core idea – adapting the decision-theoretic principles of the Knowledge Gradient to a batch setting – is well-motivated and clearly presented. The proposed computational strategy, utilizing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for efficient gradient estimation, is a key contribution enabling practical application of the method. The empirical results on both synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems consistently demonstrate q-KG’s superior performance, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios.

A minor point is that the paper does not explicitly analyze the speed-ups achieved by utilizing a batch-sequential approach compared to purely sequential optimization. Furthermore, while the literature review is comprehensive, some statements could be more precise. For example, the iterative construction of batches by averaging single-evaluation Expected Improvement (EI) over the posterior was presented in Ginsbourger et al. ("Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization") alongside the Constant Liar algorithm, and Marmin et al. ("Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design") explores q-EI maximization using natural gradients. 

I have several specific remarks and questions:

*   Is the feasible domain A assumed to be compact, and the objective function f continuous? Explicitly stating these assumptions would strengthen the theoretical foundation.
*   Why is a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) specifically chosen for the initial stage sampling? A brief justification would be helpful.
*   In Algorithm 1, the process of hyperparameter re-estimation (or Bayesian updating of the GP) is mentioned but not detailed. Providing more information about this step would improve reproducibility.
*   In Section 5.2, the definition of *g* involves a minimization; please confirm this is correct.
*   Regarding Section 5.2, the smoothness of *g* isn’t immediately obvious and should be justified more thoroughly. Similarly, the differentiability of the Cholesky factor may require further explanation.
*   The kernel used is Matérn 5/2, not 2/5, as stated in Section 6.
*   Concerning Figure 3 and the corresponding experiments: Was it possible to verify the results by fitting the same GPs using different software implementations to ensure consistency?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to parallel Bayesian optimization. The use of IPA for efficient gradient estimation is a significant contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the method and its implementation.
*   **Originality:** The extension of the Knowledge Gradient criterion to the parallel setting is a novel contribution.
*   **Significance:** The potential impact of efficient parallel Bayesian optimization for tuning complex machine learning models is substantial. The results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Minor Imprecisions:** A few statements in the literature review could be more precise.
*   **Lack of Speed-up Analysis:** The paper does not quantify the speed-ups gained by using a batch approach.
*   **Details in Implementation:** Some details regarding hyperparameter re-estimation and the justification for initial sampling method are lacking.



Overall, I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The contributions are significant, the method is sound, and the experimental results are compelling. Addressing the minor points raised above would further strengthen the paper.
