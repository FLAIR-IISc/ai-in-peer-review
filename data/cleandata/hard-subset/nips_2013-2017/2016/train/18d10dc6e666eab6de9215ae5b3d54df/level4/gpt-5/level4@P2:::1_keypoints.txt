- Paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) Knowledge Gradient acquisition function for Bayesian optimization.  
- q-KG is derived as the Bayes-optimal one-step batch policy when the final recommendation need not be one of the sampled points.  
- q-KG is computed via Monte Carlo and optimized using an IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator.  
- Continuous-domain optimization uses discretization of the domain A to approximate the posterior-mean minimum.  
- Experiments on synthetic problems and hyperparameter tuning (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR-10 CNN) show q-KG matches or outperforms strong batch BO baselines, especially under observation noise.  
- Public code and a detailed algorithmic description are provided, aiding reproducibility.  
- Builds on sequential KG literature and various parallel BO methods (parallel EI/CL, q-EI gradients, GP-BUCB, PPES).  
- Main novelty is a principled batch extension of KG with an efficient stochastic gradient computation for continuous domains.  
- Related prior work (integrating EI over pending outcomes, Constant Liar variants, gradient-based q-EI) should be more fully acknowledged.  
- Strength: clear decision-theoretic foundation; q-KG reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless, incumbent-restricted case.  
- Strength: computational contribution of an IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator enabling joint optimization over q points.  
- Strength: sensible discretization approach consistent with prior BO practice.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance and notable robustness to noise.  
- Weakness: no quantification of wall-clock or sample-efficiency speedups from batching (time-to-target, acquisition optimization overhead vs. evaluation time).  
- Weakness: derivations rely on differentiability and expectation–differentiation interchange—assumptions (compactness, continuity, uniqueness of argmins) need clarification.  
- Weakness: possible confusion in the definition of g (min vs. max); signs and optimization direction should be double-checked.  
- Weakness: gradient expressions involve Cholesky derivatives—cite or state the formulas used and discuss numerical stability.  
- Weakness: initial design constrained to Latin hypercube—sensitivity to other designs (Sobol, adaptive) should be discussed.  
- Weakness: GP hyperparameter learning by MLE each iteration—consider Bayesian marginalization/MAP or stabilizing strategies and assess sensitivity to misspecification.  
- Weakness: discretization scheme for A (sampling candidate points from “global optima of the posterior”) needs clarification and analysis of bias/variance impact.  
- Weakness: comparative fairness—different baseline implementations (MOE, Spearmint, Gpoptimization) may differ in GP fitting, kernels, and optimization routines; consistency should be ensured.  
- Minor issues: Matérn kernel typo (5/2, not 2/5); clarify compactness of A and continuity of f for minima existence; expand related-work acknowledgments.  
- Pros for acceptance: solid theoretical grounding, novel practical gradient estimator, strong empirical results under noise, high practical impact potential.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited analysis of computational cost and scalability with q, derivational clarity and attribution issues, and reliance on discretization without sensitivity analysis.  
- Overall assessment: strong and timely contribution with a lean toward acceptance pending clearer assumptions, attribution fixes, and brief computational/sensitivity analyses.
