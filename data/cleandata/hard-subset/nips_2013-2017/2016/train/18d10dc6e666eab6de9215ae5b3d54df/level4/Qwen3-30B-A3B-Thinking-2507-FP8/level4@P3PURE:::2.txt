

The paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, addressing a significant limitation of existing sequential approaches. The theoretical contribution is strong, and the approach is indeed elegant compared to alternatives. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms state-of-the-art sequential Bayesian optimization techniques. The paper is well-written and accessible, making a compelling case for the value of parallelization in practical hyperparameter optimization.

However, the assumption of independent normally distributed errors warrants critical discussion. While common in Gaussian process-based BO, this assumption is questionable in hyperparameter optimization, where measurement errors reflect the discrepancy between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation). Structural bias is prevalent in hyperparameter space, particularly with small sample sizes, and the independence assumption may not hold in such scenarios. The authors should explicitly address this limitation.

The experimental evaluation, while supporting the theoretical claims, is limited in its ability to convincingly validate the method's practical advantages. Three key issues undermine the benchmark's robustness:  
1. **Optimization criterion**: Using test set error as the objective is suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning, as established in prior work [1]. Metrics such as area under the ROC curve or log loss are more appropriate, as test set error introduces significant issues for model selection.  
2. **Benchmark datasets**: The reliance on MNIST and CIFAR10—though common—misses an opportunity to leverage HPOlib [2], the standard benchmark library specifically designed for hyperparameter optimization algorithms.  
3. **Benchmark scope**: The evaluation uses only two datasets and focuses on a single learning task (classification) with two algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). This narrow scope fails to capture the diversity of problems where optimization performance can vary significantly across algorithm types.  

While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the current experimental validation is insufficient to establish the method's general superiority. A more comprehensive benchmark—including diverse metrics, datasets, and learning algorithms—would strengthen the paper's impact.
