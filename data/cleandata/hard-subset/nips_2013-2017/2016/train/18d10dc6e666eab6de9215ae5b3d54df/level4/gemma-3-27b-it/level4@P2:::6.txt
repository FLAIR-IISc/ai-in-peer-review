This work introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization by computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate. The proposed method, q-KG, measures the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations using a knowledge gradient-based acquisition function. To efficiently maximize this function, the authors present a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate its gradient. Experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning (CNN, logistic regression) demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing methods in noise-free settings and outperforms them in noisy scenarios. The idea of efficiently utilizing multi-core environments for hyperparameter optimization is appealing, though some aspects require clarification and the experimental results need further scrutiny.

**Technical Quality:**

The paper employs standard benchmark functions to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. Crucially, it also presents results for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR10/MNIST, moving beyond trivially cheap-to-evaluate problems. However, the evaluation lacks a critical comparison: wall-clock time or an analysis of the speedup achieved by this parallel method compared to simple random search or sequential optimization. Understanding the practical gains relative to established baselines is essential. Furthermore, the consistent choice of *q*=4 across all experiments isn’t justified. The sensitivity of performance to this batch size parameter warrants investigation.

**Novelty/Originality:**

The paper proposes leveraging the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function and utilizes IPA for maximizing q-KG. While the individual techniques aren’t novel, their combination within this specific framework is a contribution. However, the relevance of “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions” (Wang et al., 2015) is significant, as it already demonstrates the application of IPA to batch selection using expected improvement. The paper needs to clearly delineate its advancements over this prior work.

**Impact/Usefulness:**

Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for real-world applications across diverse disciplines, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics. The potential to accelerate computationally expensive optimization processes is substantial.

**Clarity/Presentation:**

The technical details and background sections are well-described. The experiment section provides sufficient information to understand the comparisons. However, a more in-depth discussion of computational cost and scalability would enhance clarity.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant. Have you compared against this method? If so, where? If not, why? (Clarifying the relationship to “parallel EI in MOE” is also important, and using a distinct name like “q-EI” would avoid confusion with Spearmint’s parallel EI.)
2.  Modern multi-core systems often support significantly more than four parallel runs (e.g., 800 in Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks, ICML’15; 25 in Parallel Algorithm Configuration, LION’12; 10 in Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms, NIPS’12). What are the limitations of your method in scaling to larger batch sizes, and what gains can be expected from increased parallelism?
3.  Currently, your method appears to operate synchronously. Could it be extended to an asynchronous approach, similar to Spearmint?
4.  Ultimately, wall-clock time is critical. How does your method compare to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead associated with calculating each new batch?
5.  The x-axis label “iterations” in your plots is ambiguous. Does it represent the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do the methods not start at the same value?

**Minor Comments:**

*   There appears to be a missing z^(1:n) in equation (4.1).
*   “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” should be “achieves.”
*   Parenthetical references should not be used as nouns; for example, “[16] suggests parallelizing” should be “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing.”
*   Wang et al. (2015) does not *introduce* IPA, but rather applies it; this should be clarified.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising approach to parallel Bayesian optimization. However, the evaluation lacks a crucial time-based comparison to existing methods and doesn’t fully justify the choice of parameters. Addressing the questions above and strengthening the experimental analysis would significantly improve the paper. Currently, I lean towards a **weak accept** pending the authors’ response to these concerns and potential revisions. The technical soundness and potential impact warrant publication, but a more compelling and comprehensive evaluation is necessary.
