This paper develops a batch (parallel) version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization, and proposes a Monte Carlo–based gradient estimator (via infinitesimal perturbation analysis, IPA) to efficiently optimize the batch acquisition over continuous domains. The method aims to provide the one-step Bayes-optimal batch and is evaluated against existing batch BO methods (e.g., parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks, with especially strong performance under noisy evaluations.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic motivation: the proposed q-KG directly targets the expected improvement in the posterior minimum after one batch.
- Practical optimization strategy: the use of IPA to obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient makes joint batch selection computationally feasible.
- Empirical results: across several functions and real tuning tasks, the method is competitive or superior to strong baselines, with larger gains in the noisy setting.

Main concerns and requests
1) Novelty relative to prior batch KG work. As far as I know, batch KG itself is not new. Please clarify the paper’s contribution in relation to prior batch KG papers such as [1] and [2]. My current reading is that the primary contribution is the efficient gradient estimation and optimization procedure for finding the optimal batch. If that is correct, the paper should compare the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed method against these prior batch KG approaches, either empirically (e.g., sample efficiency, wall-clock time, scalability with q and dimension) or via mathematical analysis. Without this comparison, it is hard to assess the incremental contribution.

2) Section 5.2 needs elaboration. This section appears to be the core technical contribution. Please provide more detail on:
- The derivation of the unbiased gradient estimator, including the precise regularity conditions (differentiability of µ and K, handling of the argmin, and conditions under which interchange of expectation and differentiation holds).
- How non-differentiability induced by the min over a discrete set is handled (e.g., almost-sure uniqueness of argmin, ties).
- Variance and stability of the gradient estimator, any variance-reduction techniques, and the computational complexity per gradient evaluation.
- Convergence behavior of the stochastic gradient ascent used to maximize q-KG (step-size policy, multi-start strategy), and sensitivity to initialization.

3) Fairness of comparisons in the noisy setting. EI in its standard form assumes noiseless observations. Please clarify which EI variant is used in the noisy experiments. If a noise-aware EI (or a standard approach to handle noisy observations within EI/q-EI) is not used, the comparison is unfair. If the plain EI is used, please discuss the results in Section 6.2 explicitly in light of EI’s mismatch to noisy observations, explaining why EI underperforms and how q-KG’s design addresses noise.

4) Minor terminology issue. The abbreviation “IPA” appears to be redefined at line 162. Please define it once and use it consistently thereafter.

Evaluation by NIPS criteria
- Quality: The approach is well-motivated and the empirical results are strong. However, the paper currently lacks sufficient technical detail in Section 5.2 to fully assess correctness (unbiasedness, differentiability, tie-handling) and to reproduce the gradient estimator and optimizer.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well organized, but Section 5.2 should be expanded as noted. In addition, the noisy-setting EI baseline must be clearly specified to avoid confusion about fairness.
- Originality: Batch KG has prior art. The contribution seems to lie in the computational strategy (IPA-based gradient estimation and joint optimization). A direct comparison to prior batch KG work [1, 2] is needed to establish novelty.
- Significance: Empirical gains, especially under noise, suggest practical impact. If the proposed optimization approach is indeed substantially more efficient and scalable than previous batch KG methods, the significance would be high; this needs to be demonstrated explicitly.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong decision-theoretic foundation and a practical algorithm for joint batch selection.
- Consistently competitive or superior empirical performance, notably in noisy settings relevant to real-world tuning.
- Open-source implementation and comparisons to multiple baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete positioning relative to prior batch KG work; lack of direct empirical or theoretical comparisons to [1], [2].
- Insufficient technical detail in the key gradient-estimation section, affecting reproducibility and assessment of rigor.
- Potentially unfair baseline in noisy experiments if EI is used in its noiseless form without discussion.
