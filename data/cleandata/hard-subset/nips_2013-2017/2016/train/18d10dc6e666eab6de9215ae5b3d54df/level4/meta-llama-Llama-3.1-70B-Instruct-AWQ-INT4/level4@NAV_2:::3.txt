The paper proposes a new version of a Bayesian optimization algorithm for optimizing a multivariate objective function over a finite domain. The novel part of the algorithm is that in every iteration it identifies a number of points on which to compute the objective and this computation can be parallelized. The problem is to identify the sampling points in some optimal sense. For this purpose the authors adopt the framework of Knowledge Gradient, which estimates the expected improvement in the objective value if one more sampling point is used and based on that make a decision on whether and which sampling point to use. This approach is generalized here so that the decision is made on multiple sampling points in batch. Since this problem is computationally intractable, the authors develop an algorithm based on Monte Carlo and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis to estimate the relevant gradients so as to maximize the information gain over the set of candidate sampling sets. The algorithm is applied on several test problems and is shown to either outperform or seriously compete with other approaches in the literature, mainly those based on Confidence Bounds. The paper is well written and potentially useful for practical applications.

The main strength of the paper is its ability to effectively generalize the Knowledge Gradient approach to a batch setting, and to develop a computationally efficient algorithm for estimating the relevant gradients. The paper also provides a thorough evaluation of the algorithm on several test problems, demonstrating its effectiveness in practice.

However, I am not convinced that the level of novelty is very high, since the authors expand a previously studied approach (KG) to a batch setting. This requires a more sophisticated method for optimizing the criterion over a set of multiple points. The main novelty of the paper in my opinion lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient based on an IPA approach, which is also rather standard.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound and well written. The authors provide a clear and concise description of the algorithm and its underlying principles. The paper is also well organized and easy to follow.

One specific comment I have is regarding equation (3.1). Since x^(1:n) is a finite sequence of vectors, it is not clear how mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) are defined, since mu and K are functions of (one or two) vectors and not sequences. The authors should provide a clear explanation of how these quantities are defined and computed.

In terms of significance, the paper makes a useful contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The algorithm proposed in the paper has the potential to be widely applicable in practice, particularly in situations where the objective function is expensive to evaluate and parallelization is possible.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with some revisions to address the issues mentioned above.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel algorithm for Bayesian optimization that is potentially widely applicable in practice.
* The algorithm is well motivated and effectively generalizes the Knowledge Gradient approach to a batch setting.
* The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the algorithm on several test problems, demonstrating its effectiveness in practice.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The level of novelty is not very high, since the authors expand a previously studied approach (KG) to a batch setting.
* The main novelty of the paper lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient based on an IPA approach, which is also rather standard.
* The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of how certain quantities are defined and computed, such as mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)).
