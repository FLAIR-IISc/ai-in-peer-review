This work proposes a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization by computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next. The proposed method measures the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations using the knowledge gradient (q-KG). To efficiently maximize the acquisition function, the authors present a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the gradient of q-KG. In three experiments, they demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on problems without observation noise and outperforms these methods on problems with observation noise.

While I appreciate the idea of performing parallel hyperparameter optimization to enable the efficient use of multi-core environments, some aspects remain unclear, and I am not convinced by the experimental results. Below, I provide feedback on each of the above points, along with some questions.

Technical quality: The paper utilizes common artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. In addition to these cheap-to-evaluate problems, the paper also presents results for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression for Cifar10/MNIST. However, I would like to see an experiment in the experiment section that shows wall-clock time or an analysis of the potential gains from using this parallel method compared to simple random search or sequential optimization. Furthermore, it is unclear why q is set to 4 for all experiments.

Novelty/Originality: The paper proposes using the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize q-KG. Although both methods themselves are not new, the paper refers to "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions," which already demonstrates how to use IPA to select a batch of configurations using expected improvement.

Impact/Usefulness: Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for real-world applications across various disciplines, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

Clarity/Presentation: The technical details and background section are well-described, and the experiment section contains all the necessary information to comprehend the performed comparisons.

Questions to the authors:

1. As Wang et al. (2015) seems to be highly relevant to this work, and code appears to be available online, have you compared your method to this approach? (If this is indeed the "parallel EI in MOE," it would be beneficial to clearly distinguish it from Spearmint's parallel method by referring to it as q-EI.) To avoid confusion, it would be helpful to explicitly state the differences in the paper.

2. Nowadays, multi-core systems allow for significantly more than 4 parallel runs. For instance, "Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks" (ICML'15) used up to 800 parallel runs of DNGO, "Parallel Algorithm Configuration" (LION'12) used 25 SMAC runs in parallel, and "Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms" (NIPS'12) used up to 10 parallel runs of Spearmint. Do you have any insights into the scalability of your method and the potential gains from more parallel runs?

3. Currently, your method is designed to run synchronously. Would it be possible to extend it to run asynchronously, similar to Spearmint?

4. Ultimately, wall-clock time is a crucial factor. Have you evaluated how your method compares to random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the overhead for computing a new batch?

5. What does "iterations" as the x-label in your plots represent? Is it the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do not all methods start from the same value?

Minor comments:

- Is there a missing z^(1:n) in equation (4.1)?

- Parenthetical references are not nouns; for example, "[16] suggests parallelizing" should be "Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing."
