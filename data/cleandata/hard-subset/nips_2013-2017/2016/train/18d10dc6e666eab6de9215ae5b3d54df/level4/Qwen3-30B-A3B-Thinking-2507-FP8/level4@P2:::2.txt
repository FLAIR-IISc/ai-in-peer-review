

This paper proposes a novel parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm called the "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) method, which computes Bayes-optimal batches of points to evaluate simultaneously. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that selects a batch of q points to maximize the expected incremental value of the solution quality, providing a theoretically grounded approach to batch BO. This extends the sequential knowledge gradient method to the parallel setting, addressing a significant gap in the literature where most prior work assumes sequential evaluation.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by generalizing the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting and developing an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize the acquisition function. The authors demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms existing parallel BO methods (such as parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration) on both synthetic test functions and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios. The writing is clear, well-organized, and the technical details are thoroughly explained.

**Strengths:**
- **Quality:** The theoretical foundation is sound, with careful derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and its efficient computation. The experimental design is rigorous, with comprehensive comparisons against multiple state-of-the-art baselines across diverse test functions and real-world machine learning tasks.
- **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of both theoretical concepts and practical implementation details. The algorithm description is precise, and the figures effectively illustrate performance comparisons.
- **Originality:** While parallel BO has been studied before, the authors' decision-theoretic approach to batch selection (as opposed to iterative greedy methods) represents a novel perspective. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work in Section 2 and provides a thorough literature review.
- **Significance:** The parallel BO problem is highly relevant for practical machine learning applications where multiple evaluations can be run simultaneously (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines). The demonstrated performance gains, especially in noisy settings, make this method immediately applicable to real-world optimization challenges.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Experimental design limitations:** The benchmarking is insufficiently comprehensive. The authors use only two datasets (MNIST and CIFAR10) and two machine learning tasks (logistic regression and CNN classification), which limits the generalizability of their conclusions. For hyperparameter optimization, it would be preferable to use established benchmark libraries like HPOlib, which provides a wider variety of tasks and more rigorous evaluation protocols.
- **Evaluation metric concerns:** The paper optimizes using test set error, which is known to be suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning (as noted by Provost et al., ICML 1998). Metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss would provide more meaningful optimization objectives that better align with standard machine learning practice.
- **Noise assumption limitations:** While the paper correctly assumes independent normally distributed noise (common in GP-based BO), this assumption may not hold in hyperparameter optimization contexts where "noise" represents the difference between empirical performance and generalization (e.g., through cross-validation). Structural biases in hyperparameter space, particularly with small sample sizes, may render the independence assumption problematic.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Theoretical contribution is strong and novel, providing a Bayes-optimal approach to batch BO
- Clear, well-organized presentation with thorough experimental validation
- Demonstrates significant practical improvements over existing methods, especially in noisy settings
- The efficient computational method via IPA makes the approach feasible for practical use

*Con:*
- Benchmarking is limited in scope, using only two datasets and two learning algorithms
- Optimization metric (test set error) is suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning
- The noise assumption may not be realistic for hyperparameter optimization applications

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a technically strong contribution to Bayesian optimization with clear theoretical and practical value. While the theoretical foundation is compelling and the experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, the current benchmarking is insufficient to fully convince the community of the method's broad applicability. I recommend acceptance with the following conditions: (1) The authors should expand their benchmarking to include more diverse tasks from standard HPO libraries like HPOlib, and (2) They should justify their choice of evaluation metric or consider using more appropriate metrics for hyperparameter optimization.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, and with minor revisions to the experimental evaluation, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The theoretical novelty combined with practical performance improvements makes this work highly significant for both researchers and practitioners in machine learning.
