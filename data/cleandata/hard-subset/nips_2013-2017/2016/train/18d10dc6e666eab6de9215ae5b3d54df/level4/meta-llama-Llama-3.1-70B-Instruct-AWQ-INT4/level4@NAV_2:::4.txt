This paper considers the parallel knowledge gradient method for batch Bayesian Optimization, addressing the problem of optimizing expensive-to-evaluate functions in a parallel setting. The authors propose a novel acquisition function, q-KG, which can output multiple points for parallel evaluation, and provide an efficient approach to evaluate this function. The method is an extension of the knowledge gradient policy to the parallel setting, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness through empirical results on synthetic functions and real-world problems.

The main idea of the paper is to develop a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that can evaluate multiple points simultaneously, which is useful in applications where function evaluations are time-consuming. The authors achieve this by proposing a new acquisition function, q-KG, which measures the information gain of evaluating q points collectively. They also develop a computationally efficient approach to evaluate this function using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA).

The strengths of the paper include the novelty of the proposed method, the thoroughness of the experimental evaluation, and the clarity of the writing. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method, including the mathematical formulation and the computational approach. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in comparison to state-of-the-art benchmark algorithms.

The weaknesses of the paper include the lack of theoretical results on the error bound of the method and the quantitative analysis of the complexity with regard to dimension. While the authors provide some discussion on the computational complexity of the method, it would be helpful to have a more detailed analysis of the complexity with respect to the dimension of the input space. Additionally, the authors assume a Gaussian process prior, which may not be suitable for all applications, particularly those with high-dimensional input spaces.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed method is novel and effective, and the experimental evaluation is thorough. However, the lack of theoretical results and the assumption of a Gaussian process prior are limitations that should be addressed in future work.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and effective method for batch Bayesian optimization in a parallel setting.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The writing is clear and well-organized.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks theoretical results on the error bound of the method and the quantitative analysis of the complexity with regard to dimension.
* The assumption of a Gaussian process prior may not be suitable for all applications, particularly those with high-dimensional input spaces.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the complexity with respect to the dimension of the input space.
