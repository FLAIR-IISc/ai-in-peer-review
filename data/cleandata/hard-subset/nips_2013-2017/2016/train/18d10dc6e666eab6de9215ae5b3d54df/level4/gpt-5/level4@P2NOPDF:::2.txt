This paper tackles the growing need for parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) in machine learning, particularly for hyperparameter tuning, where most current methods remain inherently sequential. The authors propose a parallel BO algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, and they demonstrate improvements over existing approaches. The paper is clearly written, easy to follow, and the method is conceptually elegant relative to prior work. Determining a Bayes-optimal batch is especially appealing.

The method assumes independent, normally distributed observation noise, which is standard in GP-based BO. However, in hyperparameter optimization this assumption can be problematic: the “noise” often reflects discrepancies between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation), and structural biases can arise in certain regions of hyperparameter space, particularly with small sample sizes. It would be valuable to explicitly discuss the realism of the independence assumption and its potential impact.

While the theory is strong, the experimental evaluation is less compelling for three reasons:

1) The use of test-set error as the optimization criterion is problematic and is known to be suboptimal for hyperparameter selection [1]. Threshold-based measures such as classification error or accuracy can obscure model differences and lead to subpar model selection. Metrics such as AUC or log loss are typically more informative for optimization and comparison.

2) The experiments rely on MNIST and CIFAR-10, which are common benchmarks, but I would have preferred a broader and more standardized suite such as HPOlib [2], which was created specifically for benchmarking hyperparameter optimization algorithms and facilitates fair, reproducible comparisons.

3) The benchmark scope is narrow: only two datasets, a single task (classification), and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). Given that different optimizers can excel on different model classes and tasks, a more diverse evaluation—spanning additional datasets, tasks (e.g., regression), and algorithms—would make the empirical claims more convincing.

Overall, I like the paper. The theoretical contribution is valuable and the approach to Bayes-optimal batch selection is promising. However, the current experimental evidence is too limited to fully substantiate the practical advantages. Strengthening the empirical section with more appropriate metrics, broader benchmarks (e.g., HPOlib), and a wider variety of tasks and models would substantially improve the paper.

[1] Provost, F. J., Fawcett, T., & Kohavi, R. (1998). The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms. ICML.

[2] Eggensperger, K., et al. (2013). Towards an empirical foundation for assessing Bayesian optimization of hyperparameters. NIPS Workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice.
