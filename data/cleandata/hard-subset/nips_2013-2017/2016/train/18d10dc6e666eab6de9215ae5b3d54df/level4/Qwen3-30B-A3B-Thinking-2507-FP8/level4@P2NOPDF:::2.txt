

Bayesian optimization (BO) has gained significant traction for hyperparameter optimization in machine learning, yet most existing methods remain inherently sequential. This paper proposes a parallel BO algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, demonstrating superior performance over established approaches. The work is well-written, theoretically sound, and the determination of Bayes-optimal batches represents a particularly promising advancement. The authors assume independent, normally distributed errors—a common simplification in GP-based BO—but this assumption may not hold in hyperparameter optimization, where measurement errors reflect the discrepancy between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation). Structural bias is prevalent in hyperparameter space, especially with small sample sizes, and the independence assumption should be acknowledged as potentially unrealistic.  

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the experimental evaluation is limited in three critical aspects. First, the use of test set error as the optimization criterion is suboptimal. Threshold-based metrics like test set error introduce well-documented issues for hyperparameter tuning (Provost et al., 1998), and metrics such as AUC or log loss would provide more reliable optimization signals. Second, the benchmark relies solely on MNIST and CIFAR-10, whereas a rigorous evaluation should leverage HPOlib (Eggensperger et al., 2013), the standard library for hyperparameter optimization benchmarking. Third, the benchmark uses only two datasets and two algorithms (logistic regression and CNN) across a single task (classification), lacking diversity in both learning tasks and model types. Given that optimizer performance often varies significantly across algorithm families, this narrow scope undermines claims of broad practical superiority.  

The paper’s theoretical insights are valuable, but the experimental validation falls short of demonstrating robust, generalizable advantages. A more comprehensive benchmark—including diverse datasets, learning tasks, and performance metrics—would be essential to substantiate the method’s practical impact. As it stands, the current evaluation does not sufficiently convince readers of the approach’s real-world applicability.
