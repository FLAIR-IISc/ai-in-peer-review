This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) knowledge-gradient acquisition function for Bayesian optimization when function evaluations are expensive. The method selects a set of q points to evaluate in parallel at each iteration and includes an efficient procedure for computing (or approximating) the value of the q-KG objective. Empirical results support the approach.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Efficiently leveraging parallel resources is crucial in modern experimental design and hyperparameter optimization, where wall-clock time is often the primary bottleneck.
- Methodological contribution: Extending the knowledge gradient to the batch setting is a natural and principled idea. The use of q-KG aims to capture the value of information from jointly evaluating multiple points, which can be superior to greedy or myopic batch selection.
- Computational approach: The paper provides an efficient method to evaluate or approximate the q-KG objective, which is essential given the combinatorial nature of batch selection. The algorithmic development appears solid.
- Empirical validation: Experiments indicate that q-KG performs well in practice and can improve efficiency relative to sequential methods.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling assumptions in high dimensions: The approach relies on Gaussian process (GP) modeling. While standard in Bayesian optimization, GP assumptions can be strained in high-dimensional settings common in neural architecture or hyperparameter search. It would be helpful to discuss the implications and potential mitigations (e.g., additive structure, embeddings, or feature learning) and to clarify the limits under which the method is expected to work well.
- Theoretical guarantees: The paper would be strengthened by providing formal results on the quality of the q-KG policy, such as bounds on simple or cumulative regret, consistency, or approximation error of the estimator used to compute q-KG. Even partial results (e.g., under specific kernels or noise models) or asymptotic guarantees would add credibility.
- Complexity analysis: Please quantify the computational complexity of evaluating and optimizing the q-KG acquisition as a function of batch size q, the number of observations n, and the input dimension d. In particular, specify the cost drivers (e.g., Cholesky updates, Monte Carlo sampling, gradient estimation) and how they scale with q and d. This would help readers understand practical limits and compare against baselines like parallel EI/UCB/TS.
- Comparison to related batch methods: While the experiments are valuable, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of existing batch acquisition strategies (e.g., q-EI, local penalization, constant liar/fantasizing, Thompson sampling) and when q-KG is expected to have an advantage or disadvantage.

Clarity and presentation
- Overall the paper is clearly written, but certain definitions and notations need clarification (see minor comments below).
- It would be useful to include an algorithm box summarizing the q-KG batch selection procedure, including how candidate sets are optimized and how gradients (if any) are computed.
- Please make explicit whether evaluations are assumed to be noiseless or noisy, and how observation noise is incorporated into the q-KG objective and GP posterior.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is q-KG to kernel choice and hyperparameter estimation? Do you re-estimate GP hyperparameters during optimization, and if so, how does this interact with the batch selection?
- Does the proposed efficient evaluation method rely on reparameterization or common random numbers to reduce variance? Any guidance on the number of Monte Carlo samples needed for stable optimization would be helpful.
- Can the method handle constraints or multiple fidelities? If not, could you comment on possible extensions?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concise but explicit definition of the q-KG objective, including the underlying one-step lookahead criterion and how the value of information is computed for a set of q points.
- Add a complexity and scalability subsection reporting empirical wall-clock times versus q and n, and, if possible, ablation results showing the contribution of the proposed efficiency improvements.
- Expand the related work to briefly describe parallel EI and other batch methods, and position q-KG theoretically and empirically relative to them.
- Include experiments in a moderately high-dimensional setting or with dimensionality reduction to illustrate practical behavior when d is large.
- If full regret bounds are out of scope, consider providing at least an error analysis of the q-KG estimator (e.g., Monte Carlo bias/variance, convergence of stochastic gradients), and discuss approximation quality.

Minor comments and typos
- l.36: The phrase “the set of points to evaluate next that is” reads incomplete; please rephrase for clarity.
- l.95: The set A is referenced without definition. Please define A at first use and keep notation consistent.
- l.104: Clarify the input and meaning of μ^n (e.g., posterior mean function after n evaluations, μ^n(x)).
- l.128: It would help to briefly discuss the parallel EI algorithm and related batch methods for context.
- l.140: Consider using distinct symbols if A denotes multiple concepts; overloaded notation is confusing.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution to batch Bayesian optimization. The core idea—extending the knowledge gradient to parallel selection—is well motivated, and the proposed evaluation scheme appears practical. The empirical results are promising. The paper would be further strengthened by clearer notation, a more thorough comparison to existing batch methods, explicit complexity analysis, and at least partial theoretical guarantees or estimator error analysis. With these improvements, the work should be of interest to the Bayesian optimization and sequential decision-making communities.
