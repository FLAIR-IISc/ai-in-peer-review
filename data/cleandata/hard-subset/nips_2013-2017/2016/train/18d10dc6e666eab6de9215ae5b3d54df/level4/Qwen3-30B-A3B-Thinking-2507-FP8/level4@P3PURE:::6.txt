

This work proposes a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization by computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next. The approach employs the knowledge gradient (q-KG) as the utility measure for batch evaluation and introduces an efficient gradient estimation technique for maximizing the acquisition function, based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The authors demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noiseless problems and outperforms them on problems with observation noise. While I appreciate the motivation for parallel hyperparameter optimization to leverage multi-core environments, several aspects remain unclear, and the experimental results do not fully convince me. Below are my detailed comments:

**Technical Quality**  
The paper uses standard artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the method's superiority. However, the experimental section lacks wall-clock time comparisons or an analysis of the practical gains from parallelization versus random search or sequential optimization. Additionally, the choice of q=4 for all experiments is not justified.  

**Novelty/Originality**  
The paper proposes using the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function and IPA to maximize q-KG. Both components are not novel; the authors reference "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions" (which demonstrates IPA for batch selection using expected improvement). The paper should clearly differentiate its approach from this prior work (e.g., by explicitly comparing to q-EI).  

**Impact/Usefulness**  
Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for real-world applications across machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.  

**Clarity/Presentation**  
The technical details and background are well-explained, and the experiment section provides sufficient information for reproducibility.  

**Questions to the Authors**  
1. Wang et al. [2015] is highly relevant to this work (with publicly available code). Have you compared against this method? If it is "parallel EI in MOE," the paper should use "q-EI" to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel method. Clarify the key differences.  
2. Modern multi-core systems support far more than 4 parallel runs (e.g., ICML’15 used 800 parallel DNGO runs; LION’12 used 25 SMAC runs; NIPS’12 used up to 10 parallel Spearmint runs). What is the scalability of your method, and how does performance change with increased parallelism (q > 4)?  
3. The method currently runs synchronously. Can it be extended to asynchronous execution (like Spearmint)?  
4. Wall-clock time is critical. How does your method compare to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead for generating a new batch?  
5. What does "iterations" on the x-axis of your plots represent? Is it the number of function evaluations or batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do methods not start at the same value?  

**Minor Comments**  
- Equation (4.1) appears to be missing a \( z^{(1:n)} \) term.  
- "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" → "achieves a test error."  
- Parenthetical references should not be nouns: e.g., "[16] suggests" → "Snoek et al. [16] suggest."  
- Wang et al. [2015] does not introduce IPA; it applies it. The reference to IPA should be corrected.
