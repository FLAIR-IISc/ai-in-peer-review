

This paper introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization using a Bayes-optimal batch selection criterion based on the knowledge gradient (q-KG). The authors propose efficiently maximizing the q-KG acquisition function via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), a technique previously applied to expected improvement (EI) in parallel Bayesian optimization. The method demonstrates competitive performance on noiseless benchmark functions and superior results on noisy problems compared to existing parallel methods. While the idea of parallel hyperparameter optimization for multi-core environments is compelling and relevant, several technical and experimental aspects require clarification, and the experimental validation is insufficient to fully support the claims.

**Technical Quality**  
The experimental evaluation relies on standard artificial benchmarks and two machine learning tasks (CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR-10/MNIST). However, the paper lacks critical comparisons to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of *wall-clock time*—a decisive factor for parallel methods. Without this, it remains unclear whether the proposed method delivers practical speedups. Additionally, the rationale for fixing the batch size *q* at 4 across all experiments is not justified. Given that modern multi-core systems support far more parallel evaluations (e.g., 800 in [ICML'15], 25 in [LION'12], 10 in [NIPS'12]), the choice of *q*=4 appears arbitrary without analysis of scalability. The paper should clarify whether performance degrades or improves with larger *q*.

**Novelty/Originality**  
The core components—q-KG as an acquisition function and IPA for maximization—are not novel. The paper cites Wang et al. (2015) for IPA-based batch selection using EI, yet fails to distinguish its q-KG approach from existing work. If the method builds directly on Wang et al., it should explicitly clarify the differences (e.g., *q*-KG vs. *q*-EI) and cite the foundational IPA work (e.g., [Glynn & Whitt, 1989] for IPA, not Wang et al.). The claim of novelty is weakened by this lack of differentiation.

**Impact/Usefulness**  
Parallel hyperparameter optimization is indeed impactful for real-world applications. However, the paper does not demonstrate a *practical* advantage over simpler baselines (e.g., random search or sequential BO) in terms of *time-to-solution*, which is paramount for resource-constrained settings. Without wall-clock time analysis, the method’s utility remains theoretical.

**Clarity**  
The technical background and methodology are well-explained. The experiment section is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, but the lack of time-based metrics undermines the conclusions.

**Questions for Authors:**  
1. **Comparison to Wang et al. (2015):** The paper references Wang et al. (2015) for IPA-based batch selection, but Wang uses *q*-EI, not *q*-KG. Have you compared to Wang’s *q*-EI method? If so, why is it omitted? If not, clarify how *q*-KG differs from *q*-EI in the paper (e.g., "Unlike Wang et al.’s *q*-EI, our *q*-KG...").  
2. **Scalability of *q*:** The paper uses *q*=4, but modern systems support >100 parallel runs. What is the scalability of your method? How does performance change with *q*=10, 50, or 100?  
3. **Asynchronous Execution:** The method operates synchronously. Could it be extended to asynchronous execution (like Spearmint), and what would be the benefits?  
4. **Wall-clock Time Analysis:** How does your method compare to random search or sequential BO in *wall-clock time*? What is the overhead for computing a new batch?  
5. **Plot Axes:** In Figure X, does the *x*-axis ("iterations") represent function evaluations or batches? Does it include the initial design? Why do methods start at different points?  

**Minor Comments:**  
- Equation (4.1) likely omits *z^(1:n)*.  
- "achieve" should be "achieves" ("...carefully tuned... and *achieves* a test error").  
- "Wang et al. (2015)" incorrectly attributes IPA to them; IPA was introduced earlier (e.g., [Glynn & Whitt, 1989]).  
- Replace parenthetical references (e.g., "[16] suggests") with full author citations (e.g., "Snoek et al. [16] suggest").  

**Summary:**  
The paper addresses a relevant problem with a technically sound approach, but the experimental evaluation fails to demonstrate *practical* advantages. Key gaps—wall-clock time comparisons, scalability analysis, and differentiation from prior work—must be addressed. The method’s real-world utility hinges on these clarifications. Without them, the contribution remains incremental rather than transformative. The paper would benefit from additional experiments and a sharper focus on time efficiency.
