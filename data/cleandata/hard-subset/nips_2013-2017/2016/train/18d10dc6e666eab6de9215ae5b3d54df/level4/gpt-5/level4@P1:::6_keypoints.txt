- Summary: Proposes q-KG, a Bayes-optimal batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that jointly values a final batch and uses an IPA-based gradient estimator for efficient maximization; shows competitive/noisy-setting gains.  
- Relation to prior work: Extends sequential KG to parallel setting; related to q-EI, constant-lie heuristics, GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE, and parallel entropy search.  
- Strength: Principled acquisition with decision-theoretic Bayes-optimality for one remaining batch.  
- Strength: IPA-based gradient estimator enables efficient optimization of a Monte Carlo–defined acquisition.  
- Strength: Consistently strong empirical performance under observation noise.  
- Strength: Clear technical exposition and released code.  
- Weakness: Experiments fix batch size q = 4, so statistical and computational scaling to larger q is unknown.  
- Weakness: No wall-clock/time-to-solution analysis or per-batch overhead measurements.  
- Weakness: Unclear baseline labeling—need to distinguish MOE’s q-EI from Spearmint’s heuristic parallelization for fair comparison.  
- Weakness: Method is synchronous; no discussion or experiments on asynchronous operation.  
- Weakness: Plot x-axis labeled “iterations” is ambiguous (batches vs evaluations, inclusion of initial design) and methods do not start aligned.  
- Quality assessment: Derivation and IPA use are technically sound but empirical validation is incomplete regarding practical efficiency.  
- Clarity assessment: Paper is generally clear and organized, with minor notational and language issues.  
- Originality assessment: Natural but nontrivial extension of KG to batches; IPA application is not new but novel in this context.  
- Significance assessment: Potentially high for parallel HPO if gains persist and method scales/asynchronizes; experimental support not yet sufficient.  
- Pros for acceptance: Principled decision-theoretic approach, strong noisy performance, solid technical development and released code.  
- Cons for acceptance: Lack of time-to-solution data, limited q scalability evidence, incomplete baseline clarity, synchronous-only evaluation.  
- Question 1: Did you directly compare to MOE’s q-EI and can you label it explicitly to distinguish from Spearmint’s heuristic?  
- Question 2: How does q-KG’s statistical performance and computational cost scale with larger q (e.g., >10 or hundreds)?  
- Question 3: Can q-KG be adapted to asynchronous settings and what changes would be required?  
- Question 4: How does q-KG compare in wall-clock time-to-target vs random search and sequential BO; what is per-batch overhead as a function of q, M, and dimensionality?  
- Question 5: Does “iterations” denote batches or evaluations and does it include the initial design; why don’t methods start from the same value?  
- Minor: Possible missing conditional notation in Eq. (4.1) for z;  
- Minor: Grammar fix (“achieve” → “achieves”);  
- Minor: Use citation-aware phrasing (e.g., “Snoek et al. [16] suggest …”);  
- Minor: Cite IPA foundations (e.g., L’Ecuyer) rather than implying Wang et al. (2015) introduced IPA.
