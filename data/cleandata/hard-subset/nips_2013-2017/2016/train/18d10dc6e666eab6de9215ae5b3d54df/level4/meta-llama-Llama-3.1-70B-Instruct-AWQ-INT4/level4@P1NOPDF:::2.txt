Bayesian optimization has gained popularity for machine learning tasks, particularly hyperparameter optimization. However, most existing approaches are inherently sequential. This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, showing that the proposed method outperforms existing Bayesian optimization approaches. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The parallelization of Bayesian optimization is a crucial subject for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed approach is interesting and more elegant than most existing approaches I am aware of. The fact that a Bayes-optimal batch is determined is very promising.

The authors assume independent normally distributed errors, which is a common assumption in most Bayesian optimization methods based on Gaussian processes. However, in hyperparameter optimization, this assumption can be problematic, as measurement errors represent the difference between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., through cross-validation). Structural bias is common in certain regions of hyperparameter space, especially with small sample sizes. It is worth noting that assuming independence is not always realistic.

The theoretical aspect of the paper is strong, but the experiments are somewhat disappointing for three main reasons. Firstly, using test set error as an optimization criterion is problematic, as it is well-known that such score functions are suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Threshold-based metrics like test set error introduce several problems for model selection and hyperparameter optimization, making it preferable to use metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss. Secondly, the authors used the MNIST and CIFAR10 data sets as the basis for their experiments, which are commonly used in benchmarks. However, I would have preferred to see benchmarks using HPOlib [2], a widely-used library specifically designed to benchmark hyperparameter optimization algorithms. Thirdly, the number of data sets (2) used in the benchmark is very low, and the entire benchmark is based on a single machine learning task (classification) with only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). I would have liked to see more variety in the benchmark, as it is known that some optimizers perform well for certain types of learning algorithms but poorly for others.

Overall, I appreciate the paper and the theoretical contribution is definitely valuable. However, the current benchmark is too limited to be truly convincing. To strengthen the paper, I recommend considering more diverse benchmarks, including different data sets, learning tasks, and algorithms.
