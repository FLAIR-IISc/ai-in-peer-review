

This paper introduces a batch-sequential variant of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization, termed q-KG. After establishing necessary background on Gaussian processes and related work, the authors formally define the q-KG criterion and detail its computation, following the structure of the standard sequential KG approach. The experimental section demonstrates that q-KG achieves superior performance compared to several state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization methods across multiple benchmarks. The paper presents a strong, technically sound contribution with significant potential for practical impact in accelerating optimization tasks through parallelization.

The work is well-motivated and addresses an important gap in the literature. The proposed q-KG criterion is clearly articulated, and the numerical results provide compelling evidence of its effectiveness. The paper merits publication in NIPS due to its technical rigor and relevance to a core challenge in optimization.

A few minor points for clarification and strengthening would enhance the paper:
1.  The analysis does not quantify the speedup gains achieved by parallelizing the optimization (i.e., the ratio of sequential to batch runtime). Including this would strengthen the practical argument for q-KG.
2.  The literature review contains a few imprecisions. Specifically, the integration of Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was indeed explored earlier in the work "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (alongside the Constant Liar method), and Chevalier et al. notably introduced CL-mix in [2]. Additionally, the maximization of q-EI using natural gradients was investigated in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design" (a reference that should be cited).
3.  The following technical clarifications are requested:
    *   Are the domain `A` and objective function `f` assumed to be compact and continuous, respectively? (This is critical for the theoretical guarantees.)
    *   Why is the initial design restricted to a Latin hypercube sample (LHS)? Is this a necessary assumption or merely a practical choice?
    *   Algorithm 1 does not address hyperparameter re-estimation (or Bayesian updating) during the batch optimization process. How are model hyperparameters updated after each batch?
    *   In Section 5.2, the definition of `g` uses "max" in the text but "min" in the equation. Please confirm the correct optimization direction.
    *   The smoothness of `g` (as claimed in Section 5.2) requires justification; it does not follow immediately from the stated assumptions.
    *   The derivative of the Cholesky factor with respect to parameters is a non-trivial step; a brief mention of the key technique used (e.g., the formula from [reference]) would be helpful.
    *   The Matérn kernel is specified as 5/2 (not 2/5), which is standard for this parameterization; please correct this typo.
4.  Regarding Figure 1 and the associated experiments: Were the Gaussian process models fitted using different software packages (e.g., GPy, GPflow, scikit-learn) to ensure consistency and rule out implementation-specific artifacts? A brief note on this would strengthen the experimental reproducibility.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper making a valuable contribution to batch-sequential Bayesian optimization. The core technical arguments are sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the potential impact of enabling efficient parallel optimization is significant. Addressing the minor points above would further solidify the paper's impact and clarity. I recommend acceptance.
