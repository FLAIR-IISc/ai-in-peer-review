

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, which extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) approach to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the expected minimum of the Gaussian process predictor when only one batch of evaluations remains. To address the computational challenge of maximizing this acquisition function, they propose an efficient gradient estimation method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling practical implementation of the q-KG algorithm.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the growing literature on batch Bayesian optimization. While previous work has extended common acquisition functions like expected improvement (EI) and upper confidence bound (UCB) to the parallel setting [3, 5, 18, 25], the q-KG approach differs fundamentally by considering the batch of points collectively rather than through iterative greedy selection. This distinction is important because it avoids the suboptimality inherent in sequential construction methods. The authors correctly position their work as extending the sequential KG method [7, 17], which itself is a more principled approach than EI for Bayesian optimization.

The technical contribution of the IPA-based gradient estimation method is the paper's most significant innovation. The authors provide a clear derivation of the gradient estimator and demonstrate its computational efficiency. However, the draft review rightly points out that Section 5.2 (on gradient estimation) could be more thoroughly elaborated, particularly regarding the mathematical justification for the IPA approach and its advantages over alternative gradient estimation techniques. A more detailed explanation of the computational complexity would strengthen this key section.

The experimental results demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms state-of-the-art batch BO methods, including parallel EI [2, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and parallel UCB with exploration [3], especially in noisy settings. The authors evaluate their method on both synthetic test functions and practical hyperparameter tuning problems (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR10), providing comprehensive comparisons across multiple metrics. The results are particularly compelling for noisy problems, where the performance advantage of q-KG is more pronounced than in noise-free settings.

Regarding the draft review's concerns: The paper's contribution is indeed distinct from previous batch KG approaches. While Wang et al. [26] study a nested batch knowledge gradient policy, their work focuses on discrete feasible sets where gradient-based optimization isn't applicable, making their computation significantly less efficient. The authors' claim of novelty is supported by this distinction. The paper also correctly addresses the noise setting by using a GP model with noise, which is standard practice in Bayesian optimization literature.

The draft review's point about EI being designed for noiseless observations is well-taken. However, the authors correctly note that they use a GP model with noise in their experiments, making the comparison fair. The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of how their implementation of parallel EI handles noise, but the experimental setup appears appropriate.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The theoretical development is well-motivated, the algorithm description is precise, and the experimental design is thorough. The authors provide a clear implementation of their method, with code available on GitHub, which enhances reproducibility.

Strengths:
1. Clear extension of the sequential KG method to the parallel setting with a decision-theoretic foundation
2. Novel IPA-based computational approach for efficiently maximizing the batch acquisition function
3. Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple synthetic and real-world problems
4. Strong performance, especially in noisy settings, with consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods
5. Well-organized, clearly written manuscript with accessible mathematical exposition

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim could be more precisely articulated to distinguish from previous batch KG work
2. Section 5.2 on gradient estimation would benefit from more detailed explanation of the mathematical derivation
3. The comparison with EI in noisy settings could be more explicitly justified in the text
4. The paper could better discuss how their method relates to recent batch optimization approaches [15, 25]

The paper makes a valuable contribution to Bayesian optimization literature. The q-KG method provides a principled approach to batch Bayesian optimization that is both theoretically grounded and practically effective. The computational technique using IPA to efficiently estimate the acquisition function's gradient is particularly noteworthy. The results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, especially in noisy settings where many practical applications occur.

Significance: This work addresses an important practical challenge in Bayesian optimization—parallel evaluation of expensive functions—which has become increasingly relevant with the rise of distributed computing. The method has direct applicability to hyperparameter tuning of machine learning models, where parallel evaluation of different configurations is common. The consistent performance improvements across multiple problem types suggest that q-KG could become a standard approach for batch Bayesian optimization.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field. The technical content is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the writing is clear. With minor revisions to clarify the novelty claim and enhance the explanation of the IPA-based gradient estimation, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Novel extension of knowledge gradient to parallel setting with decision-theoretic foundation
- Efficient computational method using IPA for gradient estimation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements
- Strong significance for practical Bayesian optimization applications

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Minor clarification needed on novelty relative to previous batch KG work
- Section 5.2 could be more detailed
- Slight ambiguity in the comparison with EI in noisy settings

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity concerns regarding novelty and Section 5.2. The paper makes a valuable contribution to Bayesian optimization that is well-suited for NIPS.
