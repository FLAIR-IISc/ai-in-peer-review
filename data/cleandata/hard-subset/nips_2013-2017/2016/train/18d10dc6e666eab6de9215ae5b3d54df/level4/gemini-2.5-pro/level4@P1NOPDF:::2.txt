This paper addresses the inherently sequential nature of most Bayesian optimization (BO) methods, which have become popular for tasks like hyperparameter optimization. The authors propose a novel parallel BO algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches of points to evaluate, and they demonstrate its superior performance against existing approaches. The paper is well-written and the proposed method is clearly explained.

The problem of parallelizing BO is highly relevant for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed approach is notably elegant compared to many existing methods. The ability to determine a Bayes-optimal batch is a particularly promising contribution.

The authors assume independent, normally distributed measurement errors, a common convention for Gaussian process-based BO. However, this assumption can be problematic in the context of hyperparameter optimization. Here, measurement error often stems from the discrepancy between true generalization performance and its empirical estimate (e.g., via cross-validation). This can lead to structural biases in certain regions of the hyperparameter space, especially when evaluation budgets are small. It would strengthen the paper to acknowledge this limitation and discuss its potential implications.

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the experimental evaluation is somewhat disappointing for three main reasons:

1.  The choice of test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic. It is well-documented [1] that threshold-based metrics like accuracy or error rate can be suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning and model selection. Using more robust metrics such as area under the ROC curve or log-loss would provide a more reliable assessment of performance.

2.  While MNIST and CIFAR-10 are standard datasets, the evaluation would be more compelling if it included benchmarks from established suites like HPOlib [2]. HPOlib is specifically designed for benchmarking hyperparameter optimization algorithms and would allow for more direct and standardized comparisons to other methods.

3.  The diversity of the experimental setup is low. The evaluation is limited to only two datasets, a single machine learning task (classification), and two model types (logistic regression, CNN). It is known that the performance of optimizers can vary significantly across different tasks and model families. A broader benchmark including more datasets, tasks (e.g., regression), and learning algorithms would be necessary to convincingly demonstrate the general applicability of the proposed method.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and elegant theoretical contribution to parallel Bayesian optimization. The core idea is strong and promising. However, the empirical evidence presented is not yet sufficient to be fully convincing due to the limitations in the experimental design. I would encourage the authors to strengthen the experimental section to better support their theoretical claims.

[1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998.
[2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
