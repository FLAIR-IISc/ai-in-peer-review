- Introduces q-KG, a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm extending knowledge gradient to parallel evaluations.  
- Derives a decision-theoretic acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for selecting a batch to minimize the expected posterior minimum when one batch remains.  
- Proposes an efficient optimization strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients of the acquisition function.  
- Empirical evaluation on synthetic functions and ML hyperparameter tuning shows consistent improvements over existing batch BO methods, especially in noisy settings.  
- Key contributions: generalization of KG to batch setting, IPA-based computational method, and strong empirical performance.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, with concise Gaussian process background and precise algorithm description.  
- Originality is solid but novelty claims should better differentiate from prior batch KG work (Wang et al. 2015, Peng’s thesis).  
- Authors should explicitly compare or contrast their approach with Wang et al. and Peng, and show whether the IPA approach is more efficient (empirically or theoretically).  
- Comparisons involving standard expected improvement (EI) may be misleading because standard EI assumes noiseless observations.  
- Authors must clarify whether a noise-aware EI was used or acknowledge and discuss the limitation if standard EI was used in noisy experiments.  
- Section 5.2 (gradient estimation) is the technical core but needs more detail on practical implementation challenges and computational complexity.  
- Paper should compare IPA-based gradient estimation to alternative methods in terms of computational cost and accuracy.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, covering both synthetic and practical ML tasks.  
- Implementation details and comparisons with open-source baselines (parallel EI from Metrics Optimization Engine, GP-UCB variants) are thorough and transparent.  
- Significance: method is practically important for accelerating hyperparameter tuning and handling noisy parallel evaluations.  
- Strengths: theoretically grounded batch acquisition, efficient IPA computation, comprehensive experiments, strong noisy-setting performance.  
- Weaknesses: insufficient differentiation from prior batch KG, inadequate discussion of EI’s noise assumption, Section 5.2 needs more detail, IPA redefined at line 162 without enough context.  
- Overall assessment: meets NIPS standards for quality and significance; with clarifications on novelty and noise assumptions, it would be an excellent contribution.
