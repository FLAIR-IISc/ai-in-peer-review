The paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient algorithm, grounded in Bayesian modeling, for optimizing multivariate objective functions over finite domains. The key innovation lies in its parallelizable computation of the objective function; in each iteration, the algorithm identifies a set of points for evaluation, allowing for simultaneous computation. A core challenge is optimally selecting these sampling points. To address this, the authors leverage the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, which estimates the expected improvement in the objective value from acquiring an additional sample, and uses this to guide sampling decisions. This work generalizes the KG approach to operate on batches of sampling points. Recognizing the computational intractability of this generalization, the authors develop an algorithm based on Monte Carlo estimation and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate the necessary gradients for maximizing information gain over candidate sampling sets. The algorithm is evaluated on several test problems, demonstrating performance comparable to or exceeding that of existing methods, particularly those based on Confidence Bounds. 

The paper is well-written and presents a potentially valuable tool for practical applications. However, I am not entirely convinced by the magnitude of the novelty. The authors essentially extend a previously established approach (KG) to a batch setting, which necessitates a more complex optimization procedure for the selection criterion. In my view, the primary novelty resides in the estimation of the q-KG gradient using the IPA approach—though this technique is itself relatively standard.

I have one specific comment/suggestion: In equation (3.1), the definitions of  μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n),x^(1:n)) are unclear. Given that x^(1:n) represents a finite sequence of vectors, it is not obvious how these functions, which typically operate on single vectors or pairs of vectors, are being applied to the sequence.
