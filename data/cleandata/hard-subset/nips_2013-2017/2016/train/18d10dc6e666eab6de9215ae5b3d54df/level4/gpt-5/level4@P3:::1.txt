Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a batch (parallel) extension of the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization, termed q-KG. Under a Gaussian process (GP) prior with Gaussian observation noise, q-KG selects a batch of q points that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the posterior mean predictor if only one batch remains. The acquisition value is the expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean after observing the batch. Because exact evaluation/optimization is intractable for q > 1, the paper proposes a Monte Carlo estimator of q-KG and an efficient stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch.

The authors position q-KG among prior batch BO methods: parallel EI (including constant liar and variants), batch UCB methods (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), and batch entropy search (PES/PPES). They also connect to the sequential KG literature (Frazier et al.; Scott et al.) and to recent work on multipoint EI with closed-form/more efficient estimators and gradients (Ginsbourger et al.; Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.). The paper distinguishes q-KG from greedy batch construction and emphasizes its strength under observation noise. Experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10) show q-KG outperforming or matching strong baselines, especially in noisy settings.

Quality
- Theory: The decision-theoretic formulation of q-KG is sound and reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless case when the final recommendation is constrained to sampled points. The Bayes-optimality claim for one remaining batch is well-motivated. The IPA-based gradient estimator is appealing, but relies on regularity conditions (differentiability of mean/kernel and of the mapped objective involving a min). Please make explicit the conditions under which the unbiasedness holds given the non-differentiability of the min (e.g., almost sure differentiability, measure-zero tie sets) and discuss subgradient handling. A brief sketch or lemma in the main text would increase confidence.
- Computation: The estimator leverages Cholesky factors of the q×q posterior covariance at the batch and triangular solves to compute σ̃n. The paper alludes to differentiating the Cholesky factor; please either cite a standard result for differentiating Cholesky or include a short derivation to clarify stability and conditioning concerns.
- Empirics: The experiments are broad and consistent with claims. However, reporting wall-clock time per iteration, scaling with q, and sensitivity to the discretization size M and the number of Monte Carlo samples would strengthen the case for practicality and scalability.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a helpful background and algorithm summary. A few places would benefit from clarification:
- Notation: There are two uses of A (feasible domain, and its discretization inside the q-KG definition); the paper notes this, but an explicit renaming (e.g., A for domain, Ân for the discretized set used inside the min) would avoid confusion.
- Algorithm 1: Hyperparameter re-estimation is included (MLE) after each batch; consider stating whether you also explored Bayesian hyperparameter treatment or marginalization, and whether all baselines use the same GP fitting pipeline.
- Section 5.2: In the definition of g, confirm that “min” is intended in both terms (it appears consistent with (5.2) and the preceding text). Also discuss the smoothness of g; while the min of smooth functions is non-smooth at ties, these events have measure zero under a GP prior, which supports IPA—please state this explicitly.

Originality
Extending KG to parallel batch selection with a practical and efficient stochastic gradient estimator is novel and fills a gap relative to parallel EI, UCB-based approaches, and PPES. The connection showing reduction to parallel EI in the noiseless, “recommend-from-samples” setting is insightful. Please refine the related work:
- Parallel EI with integration over fantasized outcomes was already explored in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization,” along with constant liar and CL-mix, and Chevalier & Ginsbourger provided fast q-EI computation; Marmin et al. derived gradients for q-EI to enable joint batch optimization.
- Distinguish your continuous-domain, gradient-based approach from nested-batch KG on finite sets (Wang et al.); the differences are clear but could be stated more crisply.

Significance
The contribution is important for practitioners who can evaluate in parallel, particularly under noise where greedy EI tends to struggle. The empirical gains on both synthetic and ML tuning tasks are meaningful. With added clarity on computational scaling and robustness, this work is likely to be influential and adopted.

Questions and suggestions
- Assumptions: Do you require A to be compact and f to be continuous? Compactness typically ensures the minima in q-KG are attained; stating assumptions would be helpful.
- Initialization: You use a Latin hypercube (LHS) design for the initial I samples. Is there a specific reason to prefer LHS over, e.g., Sobol sequences or GP-based space-filling criteria? Any sensitivity analysis to I?
- Discretization of A: You draw M points from approximate posterior minimizers and augment with past samples and the candidate batch. How sensitive is performance to M and to the sampling heuristic? A brief ablation would be valuable.
- Computational scaling: Please report q-KG optimization time vs. batch size q and dimension d, including the cost of Monte Carlo samples and the size of Ân.
- Software and fairness: Different baselines are sourced from different packages (MOE, Spearmint, Gpoptimization). Were the same GP priors, kernels, and hyperparameter fitting procedures used across methods? If not, please harmonize or discuss the impact. Relatedly, in Figure 1 and related experiments, could the same GP implementation be used for all methods?
- Noisy settings: You estimate σ2 by MLE. Do you re-estimate at every batch? Are uncertainty estimates for σ2 fed back consistently into all baselines?
- Asynchrony: The method is synchronous. Please comment on extending q-KG to asynchronous settings (e.g., fantasy outcomes for pending evaluations).
- Minor: “Matérn 5/2” (not 2/5). Typos: “Knowldege,” “Main Stange,” “gaussian” → “Gaussian.”

Pros
- Clear, principled derivation of a Bayes-optimal batch acquisition function.
- Practical stochastic gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical results, particularly under observation noise.
- Public code and reproducibility.

Cons
- Limited analysis of computational scaling and wall-clock performance.
- Some related-work statements are imprecise; missing discussion of prior q-EI gradient/optimization work.
- Dependence on discretization of A; sensitivity and accuracy not fully explored.
- Non-smoothness/IPA justification and Cholesky differentiation details deferred to the supplement.

Overall recommendation
A strong and timely contribution to batch Bayesian optimization. With modest clarifications (assumptions, IPA smoothness conditions), tighter related work, and added discussion of computational scaling and software fairness, I recommend acceptance.
