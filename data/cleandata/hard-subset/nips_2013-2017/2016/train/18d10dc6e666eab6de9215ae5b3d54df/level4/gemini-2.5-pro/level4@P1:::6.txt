This paper introduces a novel method for batch Bayesian optimization, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The proposed acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective and represents the one-step Bayes-optimal batch of points to evaluate. To make the maximization of this acquisition function computationally tractable, the authors propose an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The empirical evaluation on synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems shows that q-KG is competitive with existing parallel BO methods in noise-free settings and outperforms them when function evaluations are noisy.

**Strengths**

*   The paper addresses the important and highly relevant problem of parallelizing Bayesian optimization, which is crucial for leveraging modern multi-core computing environments.
*   The proposed q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated from a decision-theoretic standpoint, extending the sequential knowledge gradient to the batch setting.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the technical details of the method, the necessary background, and the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses**

The main weaknesses of the paper relate to the experimental evaluation and a lack of discussion regarding practical applicability and scalability.

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the paper includes experiments on both synthetic and real-world problems, the evaluation is not entirely convincing and lacks some crucial comparisons.
    *   A key missing component is an analysis of **wall-clock time**. For a method focused on parallelization, it is essential to compare its real-world runtime against simpler baselines like sequential Bayesian optimization and parallel random search. The computational overhead of selecting each batch is not discussed, making it difficult to assess the method's practical speed-up.
    *   The choice of batch size **q=4** is used consistently across all experiments without justification. It would be insightful to understand how the method's performance and computational overhead scale with larger `q`, especially since related work has demonstrated scalability to much larger batch sizes (e.g., Snoek et al., NIPS'12 used up to 10 parallel runs; Hutter et al., LION'12 used 25).
    *   The plots use **"iterations" on the x-axis**. It is unclear whether this refers to the number of batches or the total number of function evaluations. Additionally, it is unclear if the initial design points are included in this count, and if so, why the performance curves do not all start from the same value.

2.  **Novelty and Comparison to Prior Work:**
    *   The paper's novelty appears to be somewhat incremental. The knowledge gradient is a known acquisition function, and using IPA for gradient-based optimization of a batch acquisition function has been previously demonstrated by Wang et al. (2015) for Expected Improvement. The primary contribution is the specific application of these techniques to the knowledge gradient.
    *   The relationship to the work of **Wang et al. (2015)** needs clarification. The paper compares against "parallel EI in MOE," but it is not specified if this is the method from Wang et al. (2015). If so, it would be beneficial to use a more specific name (e.g., q-EI) and to more clearly articulate the key differences between the two approaches to avoid confusion.

3.  **Practical Considerations:**
    *   The paper focuses on a **synchronous batch setting**. In many practical applications, an asynchronous approach where workers can start new evaluations as soon as they become free is more efficient. It is unclear if the proposed q-KG method could be extended to such a setting. A discussion of this limitation and potential extensions would strengthen the paper.

**Minor Comments**

*   In the text, "...this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error..." should be "...achieves a test error."
*   Parenthetical citations are sometimes used as nouns (e.g., in Section 2, "...[10, 19] suggests..."). This should be corrected to, for example, "Ginsbourger et al. [10] and Snoek et al. [19] suggest...".
*   In Equation (4.1), the notation `En[·]` is defined as an expectation conditional on past data. To improve clarity, it could be made more explicit that the expectation is taken with respect to the distribution of the upcoming measurement `y(z(1:q))`.
