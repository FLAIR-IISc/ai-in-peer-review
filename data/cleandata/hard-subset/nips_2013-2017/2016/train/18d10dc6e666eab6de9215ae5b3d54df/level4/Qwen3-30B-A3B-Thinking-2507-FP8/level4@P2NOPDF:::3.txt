

The paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient algorithm integrating Bayesian modeling for optimizing multivariate objective functions over finite domains. Its core innovation lies in selecting multiple points for parallel evaluation in each iteration, rather than sequentially, with the selection strategy derived from a batch extension of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework. Specifically, the authors generalize KG to identify an optimal set of points to sample simultaneously, maximizing expected information gain. To address the computational intractability of batch optimization, they propose an efficient method leveraging Monte Carlo sampling and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients of the q-KG criterion. This enables scalable optimization over candidate point sets without exhaustive enumeration.

The approach is evaluated on multiple benchmark problems, demonstrating competitive or superior performance against established methods, particularly confidence-bound techniques like GP-UCB. The paper is well-structured, clearly articulates the technical contributions, and presents compelling empirical results supporting its practical utility. The implementation details and experimental setup are thorough, enhancing reproducibility.

However, the novelty claim requires careful assessment. While the batch extension of KG is a meaningful contribution, it builds directly on a well-studied framework. The primary technical advance—efficient gradient estimation via IPA for the q-KG objective—is a well-established technique in Bayesian optimization literature. The core challenge of batch optimization is addressed through standard computational methods rather than a fundamentally new theoretical insight. Consequently, the novelty is modest, lying more in the effective adaptation of existing tools to the batch setting than in introducing a transformative concept. The authors convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of their method, but the theoretical contribution is incremental.

A specific technical concern arises in Equation (3.1). The notation μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n),x^(1:n)) is ambiguous, as μ and K are defined for vectors (or pairs of vectors), not sequences. Since x^(1:n) denotes a finite sequence of points, the expressions require clarification: Are these functions applied element-wise to the sequence, or do they represent aggregated statistics (e.g., mean over the sequence)? The authors should explicitly define how μ and K operate on the sequence to avoid confusion. For instance, clarifying whether μ(x^(1:n)) refers to a vector of means at each point or a single mean over the set would resolve this ambiguity.

In summary, the paper presents a practical and well-executed extension of KG to batch optimization, with strong empirical validation. The method offers a valuable tool for parallel Bayesian optimization tasks, particularly where sequential sampling is prohibitive. While the theoretical novelty is limited, the efficient gradient estimation via IPA and the resulting performance gains constitute a useful contribution to the field. The work is suitable for publication with minor revisions addressing the notation concern in Equation (3.1).
