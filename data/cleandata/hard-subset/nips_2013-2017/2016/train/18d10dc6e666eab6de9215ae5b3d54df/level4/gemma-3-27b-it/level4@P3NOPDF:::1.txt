## Review of Batch-Sequential Bayesian Optimization via the Knowledge Gradient

This paper introduces a batch-sequential variant of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) criterion, termed q-KG, for efficient global optimization. The authors provide a clear definition of the parallel criterion, detailing its computation in a manner closely aligned with the established KG framework, and demonstrate its superior performance through a comprehensive set of numerical experiments against existing state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization (BO) algorithms. Overall, this is a strong contribution with significant potential impact, and I believe it merits publication in NIPS. The ability to parallelize Bayesian optimization effectively is crucial for tackling increasingly complex and computationally demanding real-world problems.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a concise overview of related work and a necessary refresher on Gaussian Processes (GPs). The presentation of q-KG is logical and the derivation is relatively easy to follow for those familiar with the KG criterion. The empirical evaluation is robust, showcasing consistent improvements over competing methods. 

However, while the paper is strong, there are a few points for consideration. The presented experiments focus on performance gains in terms of objective function evaluations, but do not explicitly analyze the speed-up achieved through parallelization. Quantifying this speed-up, even with caveats regarding hardware and implementation details, would further strengthen the paper’s impact. 

Additionally, several statements within the related work section could benefit from greater precision. The idea of integrating Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was explored earlier than cited, notably in the work by Lizotte et al. ("Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization," 2008), alongside the Constant Liar strategy. Furthermore, Chevalier et al. introduced the CL-mix approach in [2] (2013), and the maximization of q-EI using natural gradients was investigated in “Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design” (2014).  Acknowledging these prior works more accurately would enhance the thoroughness of the literature review.

I have several specific questions and remarks regarding the technical details:

* **Assumptions & Constraints:** Is the set *A* assumed to be compact, and is the objective function *f* assumed to be continuous on *A*? Furthermore, the choice of using a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) for the initial design points is somewhat arbitrary. What motivated this specific restriction?
* **Algorithm 1:** The algorithm description does not explicitly mention hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating of the GP model. Is this intentional, or is it assumed to be a standard practice performed outside of the presented framework? Clarification on this point is needed.
* **Section 5.2:**  The definition of *g* in Section 5.2 requires clarification. Is the expression a maximization or a minimization problem?
* **Smoothness & Derivatives:** The smoothness of the function *g* in Section 5.2 does not appear self-evident.  Similarly, the differentiability of the Cholesky factor, crucial for the computations, is not straightforward and warrants further discussion.
* **Typographical Error:** The Matérn kernel parameter is typically denoted as ν = 5/2, not 2/5, as stated in the paper.
* **Reproducibility:** Regarding Figure 1 and the corresponding experiments, was it possible to fit the GPs using different software packages? This would provide valuable insight into the robustness and reproducibility of the results.  Differences in GP fitting implementations could potentially impact the observed performance.



Despite these minor concerns, the paper presents a novel and valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. Addressing the points raised above would further strengthen the manuscript and solidify its position as a significant advancement in parallel BO. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions.
