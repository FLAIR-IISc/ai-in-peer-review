This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient algorithm leveraging Bayesian modeling to optimize multivariate objective functions defined over a finite domain. The core contribution lies in its iterative identification of points for objective function evaluation, designed for parallel computation. The central challenge addressed is the optimal selection of these sampling points, tackled through an extension of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework. Specifically, the authors generalize KG to a batch setting, determining multiple sampling points simultaneously. Recognizing the computational intractability of this extension, they develop an algorithm utilizing Monte Carlo methods and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate gradients for maximizing information gain across candidate sampling sets. Empirical evaluation across several test problems demonstrates the algorithm’s competitive performance, often exceeding or matching existing methods—particularly those based on Confidence Bounds. The manuscript is clearly written and presents work with potential practical relevance. 

While the approach is promising, the degree of novelty appears incremental. The work builds upon a pre-existing methodology (KG) by extending it to a batch framework, necessitating a more complex optimization process. The primary novelty, in my assessment, resides in the IPA-based estimation of the q-KG gradient, although this technique itself represents a standard approach. 

I have one specific comment regarding the notation: In equation (3.1), the definitions of  μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) are unclear, given that μ and K are defined as functions accepting single vectors or vector pairs, not sequences of vectors as presented.
