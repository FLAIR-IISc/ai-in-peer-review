This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), which generalizes the knowledge gradient criterion to the parallel setting. The authors provide a thorough background on related work, Gaussian processes, and the definition of the q-KG criterion. They also detail the computation of q-KG and present numerical experiments demonstrating its performance compared to state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization algorithms.

The paper is well-written, and the authors have made a significant effort to provide a clear and concise explanation of their work. The potential impact of parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms on society is substantial, and this paper makes a valuable contribution to this area.

However, there are a few minor criticisms and questions that need to be addressed. Firstly, the authors do not study the speed-ups achieved by parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms, which would be an essential aspect to investigate. Additionally, some statements in the literature review are slightly imprecise. For instance, the idea of integrating expected improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was already present in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (along with Constant Liar), and Chevalier et al. presented notably CL-mix in [2]. Furthermore, q-EI maximization using natural gradient is studied in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design".

Regarding the technical aspects of the paper, the authors should clarify the following points:

1. Is the feasible domain A compact, and is the function f continuous?
2. Why is the feasible domain A restricted to be an Latin hypercube design (LHS) at the initial stage?
3. In Algorithm 1, what about hyperparameter re-estimation (or Bayesian updating)?
4. In Section 5.2, is it a max or a min in the definition of g?
5. The smoothness of g does not seem to be guaranteed, and the derivative of the Cholesky factor might be non-obvious.
6. The Matérn kernel is referred to as 5/2, but it is mentioned as 2/5 in the text.
7. Regarding Figure 1 and the corresponding experiments, was it possible to fit the same Gaussian processes using different software?

Overall, this is a good paper that has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of Bayesian optimization. With some minor revisions to address the above points, it would be even stronger.

Strengths:

* The paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that generalizes the knowledge gradient criterion to the parallel setting.
* The authors provide a thorough background on related work, Gaussian processes, and the definition of the q-KG criterion.
* The numerical experiments demonstrate the performance of q-KG compared to state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization algorithms.

Weaknesses:

* The paper does not study the speed-ups achieved by parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms.
* Some statements in the literature review are slightly imprecise.
* There are some technical points that need to be clarified, such as the compactness and continuity of the feasible domain A, the restriction of A to be an LHS at the initial stage, and the hyperparameter re-estimation in Algorithm 1.

Acceptance:

* I would recommend accepting this paper, pending minor revisions to address the above points.
* The paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of Bayesian optimization, and with some revisions, it would be even stronger.
