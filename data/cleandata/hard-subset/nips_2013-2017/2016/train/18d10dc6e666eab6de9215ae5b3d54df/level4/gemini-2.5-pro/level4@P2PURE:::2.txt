This paper addresses the sequential nature of most Bayesian optimization methods by proposing a parallel algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches of points to evaluate. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms existing parallel Bayesian optimization techniques.

The paper is well-written and its contributions are clearly presented. The parallelization of Bayesian optimization is a critical problem for its practical application in areas like hyperparameter tuning, and the proposed method is notably elegant compared to many existing alternatives. The ability to determine a Bayes-optimal batch is a particularly promising contribution.

One minor concern is the assumption of independent, normally distributed errors, which is standard for Gaussian process-based methods. This assumption can be problematic in the context of hyperparameter tuning, where measurement error arises from the discrepancy between true generalization performance and its empirical estimate (e.g., via cross-validation). In such scenarios, structural bias can occur in certain regions of the hyperparameter space, particularly with small sample sizes. It would be worth acknowledging that this assumption of independence may not always hold in practice.

While the theoretical contributions of the paper are strong, the experimental evaluation is somewhat disappointing for three main reasons:

1.  The use of test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic. As established in prior work [1], threshold-based metrics are suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning and can introduce biases in model selection. More appropriate metrics for this task would include the area under the ROC curve or log-likelihood.

2.  Although MNIST and CIFAR-10 are standard datasets, the evaluation would be more compelling if conducted on established benchmark suites for hyperparameter optimization, such as HPOlib [2]. This would facilitate a more direct and rigorous comparison against a wider range of state-of-the-art methods.

3.  The experimental scope is limited. The evaluation relies on only two datasets, a single machine learning task (classification), and two model types (logistic regression and CNN). Given that an optimizer's performance can vary significantly across different problem types, a more comprehensive benchmark across a greater diversity of tasks and learning algorithms is needed to validate the proposed method's general applicability.

In summary, the paper presents a valuable and elegant theoretical contribution to parallel Bayesian optimization. However, the empirical validation is currently too limited to be fully convincing. The work would be significantly strengthened by a more robust experimental evaluation.
