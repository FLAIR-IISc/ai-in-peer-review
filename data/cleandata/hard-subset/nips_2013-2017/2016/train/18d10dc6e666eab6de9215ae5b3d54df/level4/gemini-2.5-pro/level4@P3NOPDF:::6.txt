This paper proposes a method for parallel (batch) Bayesian Optimization by selecting a Bayes-optimal set of configurations to evaluate concurrently. The core of the method is the use of the knowledge gradient (q-KG) as the acquisition function to measure the utility of evaluating a batch of `q` points. To make the maximization of this acquisition function tractable, the authors employ an efficient stochastic gradient-based optimization method leveraging infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of q-KG.

The empirical evaluation on several synthetic and two machine learning benchmark problems demonstrates that the proposed q-KG method is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian optimization strategies on noiseless problems and outperforms them in the presence of observation noise. While the idea of principled parallel hyperparameter optimization is highly relevant and promising, several aspects of the evaluation remain unclear, and I am not fully convinced by the experimental results in their current form.

**Strengths**

*   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of accelerating optimization by leveraging parallel computing resources.
*   The proposed approach is well-motivated and principled, aiming to find a Bayes-optimal batch of points rather than using common heuristics.
*   The technical details of the method and the background material are described clearly.
*   The demonstration of strong performance in noisy environments is a notable and valuable result.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Experimental Evaluation and Wall-Clock Time:** The primary motivation for parallel optimization is to reduce the total time to find a good solution. The current experiments compare methods based on the number of function evaluations, but they critically omit any analysis of wall-clock time. The overhead of computing the next batch, especially with a sophisticated method like gradient-based maximization of q-KG, can be substantial. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an experiment that compares the wall-clock time of q-KG against relevant baselines like parallel random search and standard sequential Bayesian optimization. This would provide a clear picture of the practical speedups offered by the proposed method.

2.  **Novelty and Relation to Prior Work:** The novelty of this work lies in the specific combination of q-KG and IPA for batch Bayesian optimization. However, both components have been established in prior work. In particular, the paper "Parallel Bayesian Global Optimization of Expensive Functions" by Wang et al. (2016) also uses IPA to select a batch of points, but with the Expected Improvement criterion (q-EI). The paper would benefit from a more direct comparison to this highly related method and a clearer discussion of the specific advantages of using q-KG over q-EI.

3.  **Scalability and Practicality:** The experiments are limited to a small batch size (`q=4`). In practice, modern computing environments allow for much larger-scale parallelism. For instance, Snoek et al. (2015, "Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks") used up to 800 parallel workers. It is unclear how the proposed method scales, both in terms of optimization performance and computational overhead, as `q` increases. An analysis of how the cost of computing the batch scales with `q` would be a valuable addition. Furthermore, the proposed method is synchronous, which can lead to inefficient resource utilization if function evaluations have variable completion times. A discussion on the potential for extending the method to an asynchronous setting would improve its practical relevance.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Wang et al. (2016) seems highly relevant to this work, and code appears to be available online. Have you compared against their q-EI method? If the "parallel EI in MOE" baseline is this method, I suggest you explicitly name it q-EI to distinguish it from other parallel EI heuristics (e.g., Spearmint's) and clearly state the differences in the paper.

2.  Modern multi-core systems often allow for much more than four parallel runs. Do you have any insights into how your method scales to larger values of `q`? How does the overhead for computing a new batch grow as `q` increases?

3.  The current method is synchronous. Could you comment on the feasibility of extending it to an asynchronous setting, such as the one used by Spearmint, to improve wall-clock efficiency?

4.  As mentioned above, wall-clock time is a critical metric for parallel methods. Have you evaluated how your method compares to parallel random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of total time? How large is the computational overhead for selecting a new batch?

5.  Could you please clarify the meaning of "iterations" on the x-axis of your plots? Does it represent the number of batches evaluated or the cumulative number of individual function evaluations? Additionally, does the x-axis include the initial design points? If so, it is unclear why the performance curves for the different methods do not start from the same value.

*   Grammar: "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" should be "... and achieves a test error".
*   Citation style: Parenthetical references should not be used as nouns. For example, "[16] suggests parallelizing" should be rewritten as "Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing".
*   The reference provided for Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) appears to be a paper that applies the method rather than introducing it. A more foundational reference may be more appropriate.
