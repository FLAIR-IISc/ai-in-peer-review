Summary
This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, enabling principled and efficient parallel evaluation. The method is clearly presented, theoretically well grounded, and empirically shown to outperform several existing BO approaches. Parallelization of BO is important for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed approach is more elegant than many existing batch strategies.

Strengths
- Clear, well-written exposition with a strong theoretical foundation.
- Conceptually appealing contribution: selecting Bayes-optimal batches rather than relying on heuristics.
- Addresses a highly relevant practical need (parallelism) in hyperparameter optimization.
- Reported improvements over standard BO baselines suggest strong potential.

Concerns and suggestions
1) Noise model and independence assumption
- The method assumes independent, normally distributed observation noise, which is standard for GP-based BO. However, in hyperparameter optimization the noise typically arises from estimating generalization performance via finite-sample procedures (e.g., cross-validation), which often induces heteroscedasticity and correlations across configurations evaluated on shared folds or data splits. Moreover, structural biases may appear in certain regions of hyperparameter space (e.g., strong regularization, early stopping), especially with small datasets.
- Suggestion: Discuss the robustness of the proposed batch-selection rule to violations of independence and Gaussianity, and, if feasible, outline extensions (e.g., heteroscedastic GPs, Student-t likelihoods, modeling correlations across folds or shared data splits). Empirically, consider repeated CV or nested CV to reduce variance and bias, or at least report sensitivity to different evaluation protocols.

2) Choice of optimization metric and protocol
- The use of test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic. First, test data should be held out strictly for final evaluation to avoid optimistic bias. Second, threshold-based metrics like accuracy or 0–1 loss can be suboptimal for model selection, are insensitive to calibrated probabilities, and can be misleading under class imbalance [1].
- Suggestions:
  - Optimize a validation or cross-validated metric (e.g., log loss or AUC) and report final performance on an untouched test set.
  - Prefer proper scoring rules (e.g., negative log likelihood) or rank-based metrics (AUC) over accuracy/error, and analyze robustness to class imbalance.
  - Clarify the full evaluation protocol (data splits, any repeated CV, early stopping criteria) to ensure fair and reproducible comparisons.

3) Breadth and external validity of experiments
- The experimental scope is narrow: two datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10), one task family (image classification), and two learners (logistic regression, CNNs). BO performance can vary substantially across tasks, learners, search-space structures, and noise regimes.
- Suggestions:
  - Include benchmarks from HPOlib [2] (or its successors) to provide a standardized, widely used testbed and to facilitate comparison against prior work.
  - Broaden the set of tasks (e.g., regression and classification; balanced and imbalanced datasets), models (e.g., SVMs, random forests, gradient boosting, Bayesian neural nets), and search spaces (mixed discrete–continuous, conditional).
  - Report performance across multiple datasets to assess stability and generalization, not just best-case outcomes.

4) Baselines, ablations, and practical considerations
- While the paper reports improvements over some BO methods, it would be useful to compare against strong batch/parallel baselines representative of the state of the art (e.g., q-EI, local penalization, Thompson sampling-based batching, and knowledge-gradient variants) where applicable.
- Provide ablations to disentangle where performance gains come from:
  - Sensitivity to batch size and parallel worker count; wall-clock speedups versus sequential BO; overhead of computing Bayes-optimal batches.
  - Scaling with dimensionality and categorical/conditional variables.
  - Impact of the noise model (Gaussian vs. heavy-tailed or heteroscedastic).
- Clarify whether the method supports asynchronous evaluations (stragglers, varying training times) and, if not, discuss how it could be adapted.

5) Reproducibility
- Please include sufficient details to reproduce results (search space definitions, priors, kernel choices, acquisition optimization settings, random seeds, compute budgets). Releasing code or pseudocode would help adoption.

Minor comments
- Precisely state the computational complexity per batch selection and any approximations used to make Bayes-optimal batch computation tractable.
- If the method relies on particular utility assumptions (e.g., myopic expected improvement or knowledge gradient), state these explicitly and discuss limitations.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and elegant theoretical contribution addressing an important practical problem in Bayesian optimization. The idea of computing Bayes-optimal batches is appealing and, as presented, appears to offer meaningful improvements. However, the current empirical evaluation is too limited to convincingly establish broad practical impact, and the evaluation protocol (optimizing test error and reliance on accuracy) is concerning. I would be enthusiastic about this work with a strengthened experimental section that (i) adopts appropriate validation/test protocols and metrics; (ii) broadens benchmarks, ideally including HPOlib [2]; and (iii) provides more comprehensive comparisons and ablations. In its current form, I lean toward rejection, but I view the paper as promising and potentially impactful with expanded empirical evidence.
