Summary and contributions
The paper introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization method that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly per iteration. The acquisition function is derived from a one-step Bayes-optimal decision-theoretic criterion, i.e., it chooses the batch that maximizes the expected reduction in the GP posterior’s predictive minimum. Because exact optimization is computationally intensive, the authors propose an efficient gradient-based scheme using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) with Monte Carlo estimation, along with a discretization strategy for continuous domains. Empirically, q-KG is compared against parallel EI (both MOE and Spearmint variants), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on standard synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and CNNs (CIFAR-10). q-KG is particularly strong when evaluations are noisy. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Sequential KG has been studied for discrete and continuous settings; q-KG extends the Bayes-optimal one-step idea to batch selection.
- Prior batch acquisition functions include parallel EI (greedy and joint optimization), GP-BUCB and GP-UCB-PE, and parallel entropy search (PES). The paper’s novelty lies in a collective batch selection rule with a principled one-step Bayes-optimal objective and an efficient gradient estimator for joint optimization.
- The paper clarifies that in the noiseless case with the final recommendation restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI, correctly situating the method among existing approaches.

Evaluation against review criteria
- Quality: The decision-theoretic derivation is sound, and the IPA-based gradient estimator is appropriate under smoothness assumptions. The discretization of the domain and Monte Carlo estimation are standard in BO. Experiments are thorough and include both synthetic and practical tasks, with noise-free and noisy settings. However, theoretical guarantees (e.g., regret bounds or convergence under discretization and Monte Carlo error) are not provided.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear motivation, method, and experiments. Some notational ambiguities and minor typos remain (see comments below), and a brief, self-contained discussion of competing batch EI variants would aid readability.
- Originality: Extending KG to the batch setting with a tractable gradient estimator is novel and timely. The connection to parallel EI in special cases is insightful.
- Significance: The method is practically relevant where parallel resources and noisy evaluations are common (e.g., hyperparameter tuning). Empirical gains over strong baselines, especially under noise, suggest real value to practitioners.

Strengths
- Principled, Bayes-optimal one-step derivation for batch selection.
- Efficient gradient estimation via IPA enabling joint optimization of the batch.
- Strong empirical results across diverse benchmarks; robust advantage in noisy settings.
- Open-source implementation and comparison to widely used baselines.
- Clear articulation of the relationship to EI in the noiseless, restricted-recommendation case.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scalability and high dimensionality: While GPs are standard in BO, their performance and computational cost can degrade in high-dimensional hyperparameter spaces. Discussion or experiments on higher-dimensional problems, use of embeddings/input warping, or sparse/structured kernels would strengthen the paper.
- Theoretical guarantees: Provide at least partial theory—e.g., bounds on the bias/variance of the IPA gradient, convergence properties of stochastic gradient ascent, or conditions under which discretization yields a controlled approximation error. Any insight on how q and dimension d affect compute and sample complexity would be valuable.
- Discretization of A: The evolving discrete set An built from posterior samples and previously evaluated points is reasonable, but the impact of M and the sampling scheme on solution quality and runtime should be discussed and, if possible, ablated.
- Hyperparameter learning: Re-estimating GP hyperparameters by MLE each iteration is practical but can be unstable under high noise. Consider discussing marginalization, priors, or regularization, and provide sensitivity analyses to kernel choice and noise variance estimation.
- Computational considerations: Include complexity estimates per q-KG evaluation/gradient step (as a function of n, q, and M), and wall-clock comparisons to baselines for representative settings.
- Baselines: If not already included, consider comparisons to parallel entropy search (PES/PPES) on at least one benchmark, and report performance for larger batch sizes.

Minor comments and typos
- l.36: “the set of points to evaluate next that is” → “the set of points to evaluate next that are” or rephrase as “the next set of points to evaluate.”
- l.95: Define A explicitly upon first use (domain, constraints, and any assumptions such as easy membership checking).
- l.104: Clarify the input and notation for µ(n)(·), e.g., µ(n)(x) with x ∈ A.
- l.128: Include a brief description of parallel EI variants (greedy/constant liar and joint optimization) to contextualize your approach.
- l.140: Consider distinct notation for the two uses of A in Eq. (4.1)/(4.2) to avoid confusion between the discretized set used for evaluating q-KG and the continuous feasible domain for optimizing z(1:q).
- Algorithm 1: “Main Stange” → “Main Stage.”
- Use consistent capitalization: “Gaussian,” “Monte Carlo,” “Cholesky,” etc.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled batch acquisition function with efficient optimization.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise.
- Clear presentation, practical implementation, and open-source code.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical analysis of approximation and optimization errors.
- Scalability and high-dimensional performance not fully addressed.
- Some clarity issues in notation and minor editorial problems.

Overall recommendation
A solid and timely contribution to batch Bayesian optimization. The method is well motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates meaningful empirical gains, particularly in noisy parallel settings. Addressing theoretical guarantees, scalability, and minor clarity issues would further strengthen the paper.
