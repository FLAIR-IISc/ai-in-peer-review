The paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient algorithm leveraging Bayesian modeling to optimize multivariate objective functions over finite domains. The key innovation lies in its parallelizable selection of sampling points for objective function evaluation at each iteration. The core challenge is optimally identifying these points, which the authors address using the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework. They extend KG to a batch setting, deciding on multiple sampling points simultaneously. Recognizing the computational intractability of this extension, they develop a Monte Carlo-based algorithm, coupled with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), to estimate gradients and maximize information gain over candidate sampling sets. Experimental results on several test problems demonstrate the algorithm’s competitive performance, either surpassing or matching existing methods, particularly those utilizing Confidence Bounds. The paper is well-written and appears potentially valuable for practical applications. However, the degree of novelty is not entirely compelling, as the work primarily expands a pre-existing framework (KG) to a batch context, necessitating a more complex optimization procedure. In my assessment, the primary novelty resides in the IPA-based estimation of the q-KG gradient, although this technique is relatively standard. I have one specific comment: In equation (3.1), the definitions of  mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n),x^(1:n)) are unclear, given that mu and K are functions accepting one or two vectors, while x^(1:n) represents a finite sequence of vectors.
