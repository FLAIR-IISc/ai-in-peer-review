This paper is about batch-sequential Bayesian optimization. A batch-sequential version of the knowledge gradient criterion is presented. After some background on related work and Gaussian processes, the parallel (q-KG) criterion is defined. Following the path of the standard KG criterion, a detailed description of its calculation is provided, and several numerical experiments are presented where the q-KG appears to outperform some state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization algorithms. It is a very good paper and I think it has important qualities that justify its publication in NIPS. The potential impact of parallel Bayesian optimization algorithms on society is paramount. A minor criticism is that the speed-up (from sequential to batch-sequential) is not studied, and some statements in the literature review are also slightly ambiguous; for example, the idea of integrating EI with respect to the posterior distribution already existed in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (with stationary linear), while Chevalier et al. specifically presented CL-mixing [2]. Also, the study of q-EI maximization using natural gradients is done in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design". Finally, I have the following comments and questions: Is A compact, f continuous? Why limit A to the LHS in the initial stage? In algorithm 1: What about hyperparameter re-estimation (or Bayesian updating)? In 5.2: Is it max or min in the definition of g? Again, about 5.2: There is no need to say that g is smooth. Similarly, the derivative of the Cholesky factor may be non-obvious. Matérn is 5/2 and not 2/5, I think. About figure 1 and related experiments: Was it possible to fit the same GPs using different software?
