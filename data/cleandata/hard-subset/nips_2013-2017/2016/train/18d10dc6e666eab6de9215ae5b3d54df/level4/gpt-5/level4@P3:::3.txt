Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), a batch Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that selects a set of q points jointly by maximizing the expected reduction in the posterior minimum of the GP predictor after one batch of evaluations. The method is Bayes-optimal in the one-step setting (for minimizing the posterior mean minimum), and reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless setting when the final recommendation is restricted to evaluated points. Because exact evaluation and optimization of q-KG is intractable for q > 1, the authors derive a Monte Carlo estimator and an efficient stochastic gradient via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch.

The work builds on the knowledge gradient literature for sequential optimization (Frazier et al., 2008; Scott et al., 2011), and contrasts with batch extensions of EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger, 2013; Marmin et al., 2015), UCB-based batch methods (Desautels et al., 2014; Contal et al., 2013), and entropy-based batch methods (Shah & Ghahramani, 2015). A nested batch KG for discrete domains was studied by Wang et al. (2016). The contribution here is a continuous-domain batch KG that is jointly optimized via an unbiased gradient estimator, along with an adaptive discretization of the inner minimization. Empirically, q-KG outperforms or matches state-of-the-art batch BO methods on standard synthetic functions and on tuning logistic regression (MNIST) and CNNs (CIFAR-10), with especially strong gains under noise.

Strengths
- Conceptual: Clear decision-theoretic formulation of batch KG that retains the one-step Bayes-optimality property (in terms of the posterior mean minimum).
- Methodological: Efficient stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG using IPA, enabling practical joint optimization of the batch; careful handling of the GP update via a Cholesky-based parameterization.
- Practical relevance: Consistent empirical gains over competitive baselines (parallel EI implementations, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), particularly under noisy evaluations—an important regime for ML hyperparameter tuning.
- Reproducibility: Public code and detailed baseline implementations; sensible kernel and hyperparameter estimation choices.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Novelty: The main conceptual advance is extending KG to a batch setting and providing an IPA-based gradient estimator. While the extension is valuable, much of the machinery (Monte Carlo, IPA for stochastic gradients, adaptive discretization) is standard; the paper’s novelty is moderate rather than high.
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond the one-step Bayes-optimality interpretation (for the discretized inner problem), there are no convergence or regret guarantees, nor analysis of bias introduced by discretization. Stating assumptions under which the IPA interchange of expectation and differentiation holds and discussing non-differentiability due to the min operator would strengthen the theoretical grounding.
- Scalability and runtime: The paper lacks a wall-clock comparison and complexity analysis in n, q, and the discretization size M. Since q-KG requires repeated Cholesky factorizations and Monte Carlo sampling, runtime and scaling with q (beyond q = 4) should be reported. Sensitivity to M and the number of Monte Carlo samples for both value and gradient estimation would be helpful.
- Discretization details: The adaptive construction of An by sampling “from the global optima of the posterior” is underspecified. Please clarify the sampling procedure (e.g., approximate Thompson sampling for the argmin of the posterior mean or posterior over minimizers), and discuss how including z(1:q) in An affects bias/variance of the KG estimate.
- Experimental scope: Most synthetic problems are low to moderate dimensional (d ≤ 6). A brief study on higher-dimensional hyperparameter tuning (e.g., >10 dimensions) or using dimensionality reduction/ARD effects would clarify practical limits.
- Minor notational issue: In Eq. (3.1), please define µ(x(1:n)) as the n-vector [µ(x(1)), …, µ(x(n))] and K(x(1:n), x(1:n)) as the n×n Gram matrix with entries K(x(i), x(j)). Similarly clarify K(x, x(1:n)) and its transpose. This will avoid ambiguity since µ and K are functions of vectors, not sequences.

Quality
- The algorithmic development is sound, and the IPA-based gradient estimator is appropriate and efficiently implementable given the Cholesky parameterization. However, the paper would benefit from a clear statement of the regularity conditions ensuring unbiasedness and from an analysis of estimator variance and any variance-reduction techniques used.
- Empirical evaluations are thorough on the selected benchmarks and real tasks, with multiple random initializations. Including runtime plots or per-iteration costs would round out the assessment of practical utility.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and well organized. Suggested improvements:
  - Provide explicit definitions for vector/matrix-valued quantities in Eq. (3.1), and dimensions of D(n)(z(1:q)).
  - Summarize the conditions under which the interchange of expectation and differentiation in Eq. (5.3) is valid, given the min operator.
  - Detail the discretization strategy for An, including M, sampling method, and its impact on bias/variance and final performance.
  - Fix minor typos (e.g., “Main Stange” → “Main Stage”).

Originality
- Extending KG to batch selection and optimizing it via an IPA-based stochastic gradient is a nontrivial engineering contribution, but closely related to existing batch EI/ES optimization techniques and prior KG work. Novelty is moderate.

Significance
- Given the prevalence of parallel resources and noisy evaluation in ML hyperparameter tuning, a batch method that is robust to noise and performs well empirically is valuable. If accompanied by runtime analyses and slightly expanded experiments, this could see immediate adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled formulation of batch KG with practical optimization.
- Strong empirical results, especially in noisy settings relevant to ML.
- Public code and careful baselines.

Cons against acceptance
- Moderate novelty relative to existing batch acquisition methods and prior KG.
- Missing theoretical analysis of convergence/regret and estimator properties.
- No runtime/scalability assessment; limited dimensionality exploration.
- Some ambiguity in discretization and notation.

Questions for the authors
1. What are the exact assumptions ensuring Eq. (5.3) (unbiased gradient via IPA) holds despite the nondifferentiability of the min? How is non-uniqueness of minimizers handled?
2. How do runtime and memory scale with n, q, and M? Can you report wall-clock times and scaling experiments for larger q (e.g., 8–16)?
3. How sensitive are results to M and the number of Monte Carlo samples? Any variance reduction used?
4. Please clarify the procedure for constructing An (“samples from the global optima of the posterior”) and its practical impact.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and practically relevant contribution that brings the knowledge gradient principle to batch BO with an efficient optimization strategy. While the conceptual novelty is moderate and theoretical analysis limited, the empirical gains—particularly under noise—are compelling. With clarifications on notation/discretization and additional runtime/scalability evidence, this would be a strong addition to the BO literature. I lean toward acceptance.
