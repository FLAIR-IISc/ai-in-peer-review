This paper presents a batch extension of a stochastic gradient algorithm within a Bayesian optimization framework for maximizing a multivariate objective over a finite domain. In each iteration, the method selects multiple evaluation points, enabling parallel computation. The core challenge is to choose these sampling points in a principled way. To address this, the authors adopt the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, which quantifies the expected improvement from additional samples, and generalize it to select batches of points (q-KG).

Because optimizing the q-KG objective over all candidate batches is computationally intractable, the authors propose a Monte Carlo–based procedure combined with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients of the q-KG objective. They then use these gradient estimates to search over candidate batches and maximize expected information gain. Experiments on several benchmarks indicate that the method typically outperforms, or is at least competitive with, approaches based on confidence bounds.

The paper is clearly written and the proposed method appears useful in practical parallel evaluation settings.

Novelty and significance:
- The batch generalization of KG is a natural and valuable extension for parallel optimization. However, the conceptual novelty relative to prior KG work seems modest: the main difference lies in optimizing over sets of points rather than single points.
- The principal technical contribution is the IPA-based estimation of the q-KG gradient. While this is well motivated and executed, IPA itself is a fairly standard tool in stochastic optimization.
- Overall, the work offers a solid engineering and algorithmic contribution, but does not appear to introduce fundamentally new concepts beyond adapting KG to the batch setting and devising a suitable gradient estimator.

Specific comment/suggestion:
- Equation (3.1): Let x^(1:n) denote a finite sequence of input vectors. It is unclear how mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n)) are defined, since mu and K are functions of one and two vectors, respectively. Please clarify the notation explicitly. For example, define mu(x^(1:n)) as the n-dimensional vector [mu(x^1), …, mu(x^n)], and K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n)) as the n×n Gram matrix with entries K(x^i, x^j). Stating the dimensions would further improve clarity.

In summary, this is a well-presented and practically relevant paper with strong empirical results. The main advancement is an effective implementation of batch KG via Monte Carlo and IPA-based gradient estimation. While I find the contribution valuable for applications, the level of novelty appears limited since it primarily extends established KG methodology to the batch setting using standard stochastic gradient tools.
