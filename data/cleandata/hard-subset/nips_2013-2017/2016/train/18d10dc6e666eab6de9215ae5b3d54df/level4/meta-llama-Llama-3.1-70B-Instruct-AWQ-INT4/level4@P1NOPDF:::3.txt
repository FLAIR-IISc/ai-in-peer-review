The paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient algorithm that combines Bayesian modeling to optimize a multivariate objective function over a finite domain. The key innovation of this algorithm lies in its ability to identify a set of points for objective function computation in each iteration, allowing for parallelization. The challenge is to determine the optimal sampling points. To address this, the authors adopt the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, which estimates the expected improvement in the objective value when an additional sampling point is used, and makes decisions on whether and which sampling point to use based on this estimate. This approach is generalized to make decisions on multiple sampling points in batches. However, this problem is computationally intractable, prompting the authors to develop an algorithm that leverages Monte Carlo and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis to estimate the relevant gradients, thereby maximizing information gain over the set of candidate sampling sets.

The algorithm is applied to several test problems, demonstrating its ability to either outperform or closely compete with existing approaches in the literature, primarily those based on Confidence Bounds. The paper is well-written and has the potential to be useful in practical applications. However, I am not entirely convinced that the level of novelty is exceptionally high, as the authors essentially expand a previously studied approach (KG) to a batch setting. This expansion requires a more sophisticated method for optimizing the criterion over a set of multiple points. In my opinion, the main novelty of the paper lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient using an Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) approach, which, while innovative in this context, is a relatively standard technique.

I have one specific comment/suggestion: In equation (3.1), since x^(1:n) is a finite sequence of vectors, it is unclear how μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n),x^(1:n)) are defined, as μ and K are functions of one or two vectors, not sequences.
