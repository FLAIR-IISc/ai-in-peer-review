This manuscript focuses on batch-sequential Bayesian optimization. It introduces a batch-sequential variant of the Knowledge Gradient criterion. After providing background on related work and Gaussian processes, the parallel (q-KG) criterion is defined. Its computation is laid out by closely following the standard KG approach, and the paper reports several numerical experiments in which q-KG appears to outperform certain state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization methods. Overall this is a strong paper, and I believe it has important qualities that warrant publication in NIPS. The societal impact of enabling parallel Bayesian optimization is significant. A minor critique is that speed-ups (from sequential to batch-sequential) are not analyzed, and a few statements in the literature review are somewhat imprecise; for example, integrating EI with respect to posterior distributions was already discussed in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization” (together with Constant Liar), and Chevalier et al. notably introduced CL-mix in [2]. Also, the maximization of q-EI using a natural gradient is studied in “Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design.” Finally, I have the following remarks and questions: Is A compact, and is f continuous? Why is A restricted to an LHS at the initialization stage? In Algorithm 1, what is done about hyperparameter re-estimation (or Bayesian updating)? In 5.2, is it a max or a min in the definition of g? Related to 5.2, the smoothness of g does not seem immediate. Similarly, the derivative of the Cholesky factor may not be obvious. I assume the Matérn is 5/2 and not 2/5. Regarding Figure 1 and the corresponding experiments: was it possible to fit the same GPs using different software?
