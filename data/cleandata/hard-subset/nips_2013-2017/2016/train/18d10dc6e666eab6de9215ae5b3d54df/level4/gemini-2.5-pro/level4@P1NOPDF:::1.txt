This paper addresses the problem of batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization by introducing a parallel version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) criterion, which the authors term q-KG. The paper begins with a review of related work and Gaussian processes before formally defining the proposed q-KG acquisition function. The authors then detail its computation, which largely follows the methodology of the standard KG criterion. Finally, they present a series of numerical experiments demonstrating that q-KG outperforms several state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization algorithms.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions that merit publication at NIPS. The development of effective parallel Bayesian optimization algorithms is of considerable practical importance, and this work represents a valuable contribution to the field.

While the paper is well-executed, I have a few minor criticisms and questions. My main critique is that the paper does not study or report the computational speed-ups achieved by the batch-sequential approach compared to its fully sequential counterpart. Additionally, the literature review contains a few minor imprecisions that should be corrected:

*   The idea of integrating the Expected Improvement (EI) over the posterior distribution of the optimum was previously introduced in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization," which also proposed the Constant Liar heuristic.
*   The review should acknowledge that Chevalier and Ginsbourger [2] introduced the CL-mix heuristic.
*   The maximization of q-EI using natural gradients was studied in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design."

Finally, I have the following specific remarks and questions for the authors:

1.  **Assumptions:** Are there any assumptions on the domain A and the objective function f, such as A being compact and f being continuous? These are often required for the theoretical guarantees of Bayesian optimization.

2.  **Initial Design:** Could the authors elaborate on the decision to restrict the initial design to a Latin Hypercube Sample?

3.  **Hyperparameters:** Regarding Algorithm 1, is there a step for re-estimating or performing a Bayesian update of the Gaussian Process hyperparameters within the optimization loop? This is often a crucial component for performance.

4.  **Definition of g:** In Section 5.2, could the authors clarify whether the definition of the function g involves a maximization or a minimization?

5.  **Smoothness of g:** Also in Section 5.2, the smoothness of the function g appears to be implicitly assumed. Could the authors provide a justification for this assumption?

6.  **Cholesky Derivative:** Similarly, the method seems to rely on the derivative of the Cholesky factor. As this computation is not straightforward, a brief discussion or citation would be helpful.

7.  **Matérn Kernel:** There appears to be a typo related to the kernel function. I believe it should be the Matérn-5/2 kernel, not 2/5.

8.  **Reproducibility:** Regarding the experiments shown in Figure 1, were the Gaussian Process models implemented using a standard, publicly available software package? It would be useful to know if the results are sensitive to the specific GP implementation or if they could be reproduced using other standard libraries.
