This paper proposes a batch version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) for Bayesian Optimization. The core contribution is a method for finding an optimal batch of design points by optimizing the batch KG acquisition function using a gradient estimator based on Monte-Carlo simulation and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The experiments conducted on synthetic and real-world problems suggest that the proposed method outperforms other batch Bayesian optimization baselines.

While the paper addresses an important problem and the proposed method shows promising empirical results, there are several major points that need to be addressed to strengthen the submission.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Novelty and Relation to Prior Work:** A primary concern is the paper's positioning with respect to existing literature on batch Knowledge Gradient methods. The concept of a batch KG policy is not new and has been explored in prior work, for instance, by Wang et al. [1] and Peng [2]. The authors should clarify the specific contributions of this paper in the context of these previous works.

    The main contribution appears to be the specific gradient-based optimization procedure for maximizing the batch KG acquisition function. If this is the case, the authors should explicitly state this and provide a more thorough discussion of how their approach differs from, and improves upon, the optimization strategies used in previous batch KG frameworks. Crucially, a direct comparison is needed. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an experimental or analytical comparison of the proposed gradient estimation method against the optimization techniques used in [1] and [2] in terms of both computational efficiency and the quality of the selected batches. Without such a comparison, the practical advantage of the proposed optimization scheme is not well-established.

2.  **Insufficient Detail on the Core Method:** Section 5.2, which details the gradient estimation, is central to the paper's contribution but lacks sufficient detail. As this section is the technical core, it should be expanded to provide the reader with a clearer understanding of the derivation and implementation. The current presentation is too brief, making it difficult to fully appreciate the technical nuances and replicate the method. The authors should consider including a more step-by-step derivation, providing more intuition behind the use of IPA in this context, and perhaps including pseudocode for the overall batch selection algorithm.

3.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** The experimental comparison with Expected Improvement (EI) in the noisy observation setting (Section 6.2) may be unfair. The standard EI acquisition function is derived under the assumption of noiseless observations. Applying it directly to a noisy setting without modification can lead to suboptimal performance, as it does not account for the posterior uncertainty at previously observed points. Please clarify if a noise-aware variant of EI (e.g., EI with observational noise) was used for the comparison. If the standard, noiseless version of EI was used, this choice must be justified, and the results should be discussed in light of this known limitation. A fair comparison would require benchmarking against baselines that are also designed for, or adapted to, the noisy setting.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In line 162, the abbreviation "IPA" is redefined. Please check for consistency and define acronyms only at their first use.
