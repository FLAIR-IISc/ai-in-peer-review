

The paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, addressing the inherent sequentiality of most existing BO methods. The theoretical contribution is strong, and the approach—deriving Bayes-optimal batches rather than relying on heuristic parallelization—represents a significant advancement in elegance over prior work. The paper is clearly written and accessible, making a compelling case for parallelization as a critical direction for practical hyperparameter optimization.  

However, the assumption of independent Gaussian errors warrants scrutiny. While common in GP-based BO, this assumption is questionable for hyperparameter optimization, where measurement errors reflect discrepancies between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., cross-validation scores). Structural bias frequently arises in hyperparameter space, particularly with small sample sizes, making independence a simplification that may not hold in practice. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this limitation.  

The experimental evaluation, while supporting the theoretical claims, suffers from three key shortcomings that undermine its persuasiveness:  
1. **Optimization criterion**: The use of test set error as the objective metric is suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning. As established in literature (e.g., Provost et al., 1998), threshold-based metrics like test accuracy introduce significant issues for model selection, including sensitivity to class imbalance and noisy evaluations. Metrics such as AUC or log loss would provide a more robust basis for comparison.  
2. **Benchmark scope**: The experiments rely solely on MNIST and CIFAR-10, which, while standard, do not leverage HPOlib [2]—a widely adopted benchmark library specifically designed for rigorous hyperparameter optimization comparisons. Including HPOlib would have strengthened the empirical validity.  
3. **Benchmark diversity**: The evaluation uses only two datasets and focuses exclusively on classification with two algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). Given that optimizer performance varies significantly across learning algorithms and task types (e.g., regression, clustering, or deep learning architectures), this narrow scope limits the generalizability of the results. A more diverse benchmark would better demonstrate the method’s applicability.  

The theoretical contribution is valuable and merits publication, but the current experimental section fails to convincingly establish the method’s practical advantages. The limited benchmark design—particularly the reliance on test set error and insufficient task diversity—prevents a robust conclusion about the method’s real-world utility. To address this, the authors should re-evaluate their experiments using more appropriate metrics and a broader set of benchmarks, ideally within HPOlib.  

In summary, the paper offers a theoretically sound and elegant solution to parallel BO, but the experimental validation requires substantial expansion to support the claim of superiority over existing approaches. With stronger empirical evidence, this work would make a significant contribution to the field.
