Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a batch (parallel) version of the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization with Gaussian processes. The method selects a set of q points by maximizing the expected one-step reduction in the GP posterior minimum (q-KG), which is Bayes-optimal when only one batch remains and the final recommendation need not be among the queried points. Because direct maximization of q-KG is computationally demanding, the paper introduces an efficient stochastic gradient estimator based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and Monte Carlo sampling, enabling joint optimization of the batch. The algorithm also discretizes the domain for evaluating the inner minimizations, and demonstrates strong empirical performance on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning, particularly under observation noise. Comparisons include parallel EI (q-EI and heuristic variants), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE.

The work builds on prior sequential KG [Frazier et al. 2008; Scott et al. 2011], batch BO via EI (closed-form and gradient-based q-EI), UCB-based batch methods, and batch entropy-search variants. Closely related are batch/nested KG formulations over discrete sets (e.g., Wang et al. 2015) and earlier batch-KG ideas (e.g., Peng 2010 thesis). The present paper differs by treating continuous domains, providing an unbiased IPA gradient estimator for q-KG, and handling noisy observations within the KG framework.

Strengths
- Decision-theoretic objective: q-KG is a principled, Bayes-optimal one-step criterion for batches.
- Efficient optimization: an IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator makes joint batch optimization practical.
- Noisy evaluations: the approach naturally accounts for observation noise and shows substantial gains in noisy settings.
- Empirical breadth: results span multiple synthetic functions and real hyperparameter tuning; code availability is a plus.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications
1) Novelty vs prior batch KG. Batch KG is not entirely new. Please clarify the precise contribution relative to:
- Wang et al. (2015) “Nested-Batch-Mode Learning …” [1], which uses batch KG over finite discrete sets (and does not exploit gradients).
- Peng (2010) thesis [2], which discusses batch KG ideas.
A side-by-side comparison (conceptual and empirical) would strengthen the claims: What is new in the formulation (e.g., continuous domains, domain discretization strategy), in the computation (unbiased IPA gradient vs prior enumeration/MC), and in theoretical guarantees? If possible, include runtime and regret comparisons against [1] on finite/discrete scenarios, or a complexity analysis contrasting your estimator with their computational strategy.

2) Section 5.2 (gradient estimator). This section is central and should be expanded:
- State conditions under which differentiation under the expectation is valid; provide a clear argument for unbiasedness (beyond the supplement).
- Discuss the variance of the gradient estimator, any variance-reduction techniques, and practical choices for the number of Monte Carlo samples per gradient step.
- Provide computational complexity per iteration in terms of q, d, and |A_n| (e.g., Cholesky costs), and contrast with q-EI optimizers.
- Detail the optimizer (step sizes, restarts, stopping criteria) and sensitivity to these choices.

3) Fair comparison under noise. Classical EI assumes noiseless evaluations; using it directly in noisy settings disadvantages EI. Please clarify:
- Which EI variants were used under noise (e.g., noise-aware EI that integrates over latent f, or q-EI with “fantasies” accounting for noise)? If a noiseless EI was used, explicitly note this and discuss the expected impact.
- Consider adding a noise-aware EI baseline (or citing why it is impractical here), and/or include PPES (if not already) as it is naturally noise-aware.

4) Notation and acronym consistency. The acronym “IPA” appears to be redefined around line 162; please define once and ensure consistent usage. Also clarify the two roles of A in Eq. (4.1) vs the optimization domain, and ensure symbols (e.g., A vs A_n) are clearly distinguished.

Additional suggestions
- Discretization sensitivity: Report how performance depends on M and on the sampling strategy for A_n. Is q-KG robust to different discretizations? Provide ablations or sensitivity plots.
- Scaling with q and d: Include wall-clock timings and iteration-level costs for varying batch sizes and dimensions; compare with q-EI and PPES.
- Reproducibility: Specify the number of MC samples for q-KG and its gradient, number of restarts, hardware, and average runtime per iteration.
- Minor edits: fix typos (e.g., “Main Stange”), standardize capitalization (Gaussian), and ensure consistent notation for vectors and sets.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a principled objective and a plausible unbiased IPA gradient estimator. The main gaps are in the detail and rigor of Section 5.2 (assumptions, variance/complexity), and in ensuring fair noisy baselines.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. Section 5.2 needs more exposition. Clarify the discretization procedure and its computational impact. Clean up minor notation and acronym issues.

Originality
Moderate to high. While sequential KG and some batch KG ideas exist, the combination of a continuous-domain q-KG with an efficient unbiased gradient estimator appears novel. Please better situate against [1,2].

Significance
High practical relevance for batch BO with noise. If computational advantages are substantiated and fair noisy baselines are included, the method is likely to be adopted.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled batch acquisition with strong noisy-regime performance
- Efficient gradient-based optimization enabling practical batch selection
- Solid empirical results and open-source implementation

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity about novelty vs prior batch KG work
- Incomplete details on the gradient estimator’s assumptions, variance, and complexity
- Potentially unfair noisy comparisons if EI is not noise-aware
- Dependence on discretization not fully analyzed
