- Paper introduces q-KG, a batch extension of the knowledge gradient for Bayesian optimization, selecting q points to minimize expected posterior-mean minimum when one batch remains.  
- q-KG value is the expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean; exact evaluation is intractable for q>1, so the paper uses Monte Carlo and an IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator.  
- Positions q-KG relative to parallel EI (including constant liar), batch UCB methods, PPES, and sequential KG literature, and contrasts with recent multipoint EI/gradient work.  
- Claims novelty in extending KG to continuous-domain, gradient-based joint batch optimization and shows reduction to parallel EI in the noiseless recommend-from-samples case.  
- The decision-theoretic formulation is sound and Bayes-optimal for the one-batch-left setting; IPA-based gradient estimator is appealing but depends on differentiability conditions.  
- Reviewer requests explicit conditions/lemma for unbiasedness of the IPA gradient given min-induced non-differentiability and measure-zero tie arguments.  
- Computational approach uses Cholesky factors and triangular solves; paper should cite/derive Cholesky differentiation and discuss numerical stability/conditioning.  
- Experiments are broad and support claims, with q-KG outperforming baselines, especially under observation noise.  
- Reviewer asks for wall-clock time per iteration, scaling with q, and sensitivity analyses for discretization size M and Monte Carlo sample count.  
- Notational clarity needed: avoid dual use of A (domain vs. discretization), e.g., rename discretized set to Ân.  
- Algorithm 1: clarify hyperparameter treatment (MLE after each batch) and whether Bayesian hyperparameter marginalization was considered and whether baselines used the same GP pipeline.  
- Section 5.2/g definition: confirm both terms use “min” and explicitly state smoothness argument for g under GP priors (measure-zero ties supporting IPA).  
- Related work should better acknowledge prior parallel EI/fantasy outcome work and recent q-EI gradient/optimization contributions (Chevalier & Ginsbourger, Marmin et al.).  
- Distinguish continuous-domain q-KG from nested-batch KG on finite sets (Wang et al.) more crisply.  
- Contribution is significant for parallel evaluation, particularly in noisy settings, and likely influential if computational and robustness concerns are addressed.  
- Request explicit assumptions (e.g., compact domain A and continuous f) to ensure attainment of minima in q-KG.  
- Ask for rationale and sensitivity for initialization choice (LHS vs. Sobol or other space-filling designs) and number of initial samples I.  
- Ask for ablation on discretization of A: sensitivity to M and the heuristic for drawing candidate minimizers.  
- Request reporting of q-KG optimization time vs. batch size q and input dimension d, including MC sample cost and Ân size.  
- Request harmonization of GP implementations across baselines or discussion of impact if different packages/kernels/hyperparameter fitting were used.  
- Clarify noisy setting procedures: whether σ^2 is re-estimated each batch and whether baselines receive consistent uncertainty estimates.  
- Ask for comments on extending q-KG to asynchronous evaluation settings (e.g., using fantasy outcomes).  
- Minor corrections: Matérn 5/2 naming and typos (“Knowldege,” “Main Stange,” “gaussian”).  
- Pros: principled Bayes-optimal batch acquisition, practical stochastic gradient estimator, strong empirical results under noise, public code.  
- Cons: limited analysis of computational scaling/wall-clock time, imprecise related-work statements, dependence on discretization not fully explored, IPA/Cholesky differentiation details deferred.  
- Overall recommendation: strong and timely contribution recommended for acceptance pending modest clarifications on assumptions, IPA smoothness, related work, and computational scaling.
