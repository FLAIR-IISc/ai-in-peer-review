

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), extending the knowledge gradient criterion to the parallel setting where multiple function evaluations can occur simultaneously. The authors develop a decision-theoretic approach that selects batches of points to evaluate by maximizing the expected incremental value of a measurement, rather than using greedy iterative methods common in prior work. The q-KG method is derived as the one-step Bayes-optimal batch selection strategy when only one batch remains, and the authors provide an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis to optimize the acquisition function.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature by addressing an important gap: most prior work on parallel BO either constructs batches greedily (e.g., parallel EI, GP-UCB) or focuses on discrete settings, whereas q-KG considers the batch collectively as a single decision. This is a meaningful extension of the sequential knowledge gradient method [7, 17], which has been shown to outperform other acquisition functions in sequential settings. The authors also correctly note that q-KG reduces to parallel EI under specific conditions (noise-free evaluations with final recommendation restricted to sampled points), providing an important theoretical connection to existing work.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance across multiple synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show q-KG outperforms or matches state-of-the-art batch BO methods, with particularly notable improvements in noisy evaluation settings. The authors provide thorough experimental validation with appropriate baselines, clear presentation of results, and thoughtful discussion of practical implications. The code is publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations. The authors carefully derive the acquisition function, provide a detailed computational approach with justifications, and conduct comprehensive experiments with appropriate statistical analysis (including multiple random initializations). The experimental design effectively compares against relevant baselines, and the authors appropriately acknowledge limitations (e.g., computational complexity for large q).

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and generally clear, with good use of notation. The background on Gaussian processes is concise yet sufficient. Some sections (particularly 5.2 on gradient estimation) could benefit from more intuitive explanations, but the overall exposition is accessible to the intended audience. The algorithm description is precise and well-organized.

Originality: The parallel knowledge gradient method represents a novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization that differs significantly from prior work. While some prior papers (e.g., [2, 3, 5, 10, 18, 25]) have addressed parallel BO, they either use iterative greedy methods or focus on different acquisition functions. The q-KG approach is distinct in its decision-theoretic foundation and collective batch optimization. The authors properly contextualize their work against recent NIPS papers (e.g., [19, 22] for BO in ML, [3, 5, 18, 25] for parallel BO), showing clear differentiation from prior contributions.

Significance: The work addresses a critical practical need in machine learning—efficient hyperparameter tuning in parallel computing environments. The ability to handle noisy evaluations effectively (where q-KG shows particular advantage) is especially valuable for real-world applications. The method has clear potential for impact in hyperparameter optimization for deep learning and other computationally intensive ML tasks, where parallelization is increasingly important.

The paper is well-written and makes a strong contribution to the field. While the draft review raised some minor points, they can be addressed through minor clarifications in the final version:

1. The compactness of domain A and continuity of f are standard assumptions in BO literature and not critical to the method's validity.
2. Initial Latin hypercube sampling is a common and effective approach for initializing BO, though the authors might briefly justify this choice.
3. Hyperparameter re-estimation is properly addressed in Algorithm 1 (step 5).
4. The definition of g in Section 5.2 correctly uses a minimization (not maximization).
5. The smoothness assumptions for the derivative calculation are standard in BO contexts, though the authors could provide a brief justification.
6. The Matérn 5/2 kernel is correctly referenced, not 2/5.

The paper is a strong contribution to the field, with clear theoretical foundations, rigorous experimental validation, and significant practical implications. It addresses a meaningful gap in the literature with a well-motivated approach that consistently outperforms existing methods, particularly in noisy settings. The work is of high quality, clear, original, and significant—making it an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
