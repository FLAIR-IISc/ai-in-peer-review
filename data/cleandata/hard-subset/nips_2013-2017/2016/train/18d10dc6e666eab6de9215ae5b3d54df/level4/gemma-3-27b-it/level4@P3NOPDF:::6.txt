This work introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization, focusing on the efficient selection of configurations to evaluate in a parallel setting. The approach utilizes the knowledge gradient (q-KG) as an acquisition function to quantify the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations. To facilitate optimization of this acquisition function, the authors present a technique based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for estimating its gradient. Through three experiments, the paper demonstrates that q-KG achieves competitive performance compared to existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noise-free problems and outperforms them in the presence of observation noise. While the core idea of leveraging parallelization for efficient hyperparameter optimization is appealing, several aspects of the paper require further clarification, and the experimental validation does not fully convince.

**Technical Quality:**

The paper employs standard artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. Importantly, it also extends the evaluation to more complex, practical problems – optimizing a CNN and logistic regression for CIFAR10/MNIST – which is commendable. However, the presented results lack a crucial element: a comparison against the wall-clock time performance of other methods, including simple random search and sequential Bayesian optimization. Including such analysis would significantly strengthen the justification for the proposed approach, demonstrating the tangible benefits of parallelization. Furthermore, the choice of *q* = 4 across all experiments remains unexplained. A sensitivity analysis or justification for this specific value is needed.

**Novelty/Originality:**

The paper builds on established techniques – the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) – to address the problem of parallel Bayesian optimization. While the combination is not entirely novel, the application of IPA to maximize q-KG is a reasonable contribution. However, the related work section should more thoroughly address "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions" (Wang et al., 2015), which already utilizes IPA with expected improvement for batch selection. A clearer distinction between the proposed method and existing approaches (e.g., labeling Wang et al.’s method as “parallel EI in MOE” and the presented method as “q-EI” to avoid confusion) would strengthen the novelty claim.

**Impact/Usefulness:**

Parallel hyperparameter tuning techniques are undeniably relevant and valuable across numerous disciplines, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics. The potential for accelerating the optimization process is significant, making this research potentially impactful if the practical benefits can be convincingly demonstrated.

**Clarity/Presentation:**

The technical details and background information are presented clearly and comprehensively. The experiment section provides sufficient details to understand the performed comparisons. However, the definition of “iterations” on the x-axis of the plots is ambiguous. Is it the number of function evaluations, the number of batches, or a combination of both? Furthermore, if the x-axis includes the initial design, why do the different methods not begin at the same value? Addressing these points would enhance the clarity of the results.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.) Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant to this work, and their code is publicly available. Have you compared your method to theirs? If the work you cite is "parallel EI in MOE", it would be beneficial to clearly label it as q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel method. A clearer articulation of the differences between these approaches is crucial.

2.) Modern multi-core systems routinely support a significantly larger number of parallel runs than the four used in your experiments. For example, DNGO (ICML’15) utilized up to 800 parallel runs, SMAC (LION’12) used 25, and Spearmint (NIPS’12) used up to 10. Do you have any insights into the scalability of your method and the potential gains achievable with a higher degree of parallelism?

3.) Your method currently operates synchronously. Is it possible to extend it to support asynchronous execution, similar to Spearmint?

4.) Ultimately, wall-clock time is the key performance indicator. Have you evaluated how your method compares to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization regarding wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead associated with computing a new batch of configurations?

5.) Please clarify the meaning of “iterations” as used on the x-axis of your plots. Is it the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design, and if so, why do different methods not start from the same value?

**Minor Comments:**
