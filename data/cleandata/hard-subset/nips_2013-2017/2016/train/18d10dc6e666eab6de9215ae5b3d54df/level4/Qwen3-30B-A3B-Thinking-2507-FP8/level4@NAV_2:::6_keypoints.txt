- Introduces q-KG: a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that generalizes the knowledge gradient to select q points by maximizing a decision-theoretic acquisition function.  
- Derives q-KG and proposes efficient gradient estimation using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for maximizing the acquisition.  
- Builds on prior IPA work for parallel expected improvement (Wang et al., 2015) but adapts it for the knowledge gradient.  
- Empirically evaluated on synthetic benchmarks (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and real hyperparameter tuning (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR10 CNN).  
- Shows strong performance gains, especially in noisy evaluation settings.  
- Technical approach is sound with clear theoretical grounding in Bayesian optimization and decision theory.  
- Experimental design reports mean and standard deviation across runs, and presents comprehensive experiment choices.  
- Lacks wall-clock time comparisons against baselines, limiting assessment of practical efficiency.  
- Uses batch size q=4 in all experiments without justification, raising questions about applicability to larger parallel environments.  
- Does not demonstrate how q-KG scales with larger batch sizes or the computational trade-offs involved.  
- Insufficient direct comparison and differentiation from Wang et al. (2015) regarding computational efficiency and performance.  
- Ambiguity in experimental plots: x-axis labeling (iterations vs. function evaluations) is unclear and initial design point handling is inconsistent.  
- Originality: meaningful advance by extending KG to parallel setting, but core KG idea is not new.  
- Significance: addresses practical hyperparameter tuning needs and is especially relevant for noisy real-world evaluations.  
- Needs evidence of concrete wall-clock time savings versus sequential optimization and random search to strengthen impact claims.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically sound, consistent noisy-setting improvements, computationally efficient batch selection via IPA, practical relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing wall-clock comparisons, unjustified batch size choice, insufficient comparison to prior IPA/q-EI work, and ambiguous experimental details.  
- Actionable questions for authors: justify q=4, show scaling with q, adaptibility to asynchronous execution, provide wall-clock timings, and clarify x-axis/initial design in plots.  
- Minor corrections requested: include missing z^(1:n) in equation (4.1) and fix grammatical error ("achieve" → "achieves").
