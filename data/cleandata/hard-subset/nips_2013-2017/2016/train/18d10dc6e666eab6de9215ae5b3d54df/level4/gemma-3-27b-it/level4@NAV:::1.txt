This paper presents a batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization method utilizing a parallelized version of the Knowledge Gradient criterion, termed q-KG. The authors begin with a concise overview of relevant background material, including Gaussian processes, before formally defining the q-KG criterion and detailing its computational aspects, mirroring the established approach for the standard KG criterion. The empirical evaluation demonstrates q-KG’s competitive performance, exceeding that of several current state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization algorithms across a range of experiments. Overall, this is a strong contribution with significant potential, and I believe it is well-suited for publication at NIPS. The ability to efficiently parallelize Bayesian optimization techniques has considerable implications for a broad spectrum of applications. 

A minor point for consideration is the lack of analysis regarding the achieved speed-ups through batch-sequential processing. Furthermore, several statements within the related work discussion could benefit from greater precision. For example, the integration of Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was previously explored in the work by Gelbart et al. ("Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization,” alongside Constant Liar), while Chevalier et al. introduced CL-mix in [2]. Additionally, the use of natural gradient for q-EI maximization is discussed in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design."

I also have the following specific questions and requests for clarification:

*   Is the set A assumed to be compact, and is the function f continuous on A?
*   What is the rationale for restricting the initial design points to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS)?
*   Regarding Algorithm 1, does the process incorporate hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating?
*   In Section 5.2, is the definition of *g* a maximization or minimization problem?
*   Also in Section 5.2, the smoothness of *g* is not immediately apparent and requires further justification. Similarly, the differentiability of the Cholesky factor could benefit from additional explanation.
*   There appears to be a typographical error: the Matérn kernel parameter is 5/2, not 2/5.
*   Concerning Figure 1 and the associated experiments, was it feasible to reproduce the results by fitting the same Gaussian Processes using alternative software implementations?
