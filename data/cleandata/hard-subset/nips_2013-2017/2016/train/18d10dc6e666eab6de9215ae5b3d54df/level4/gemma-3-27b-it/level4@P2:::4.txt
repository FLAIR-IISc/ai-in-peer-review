This paper introduces a novel approach to batch Bayesian Optimization, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG). The authors address the common scenario where function evaluations are computationally expensive, and leverage the potential for parallelization by proposing a new acquisition function, q-KG, capable of suggesting a batch of *q* points for simultaneous evaluation. They also detail an efficient strategy for optimizing this acquisition function. Empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both synthetic test functions and in hyperparameter tuning for machine learning algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

The problem addressed is highly relevant. Efficiently utilizing parallel computing resources is crucial for scaling data-driven applications, and batch Bayesian Optimization offers a promising pathway. The core idea of extending the knowledge gradient framework to a parallel setting is insightful, allowing for more informed decisions when multiple function evaluations can be performed concurrently. The paper builds upon prior work in Bayesian Optimization, particularly sequential knowledge gradient methods [7, 17], and acknowledges related approaches to parallel BO like parallel Expected Improvement (EI) [2, 19, 25] and parallel Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) [3, 5].

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The q-KG method represents a valuable contribution to the field of parallel Bayesian Optimization by framing the problem as a decision-theoretic optimization over a batch of points.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function from a Bayes-optimal perspective is sound and well-motivated.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) technique for gradient estimation is a key strength, enabling practical optimization of the q-KG function, especially for larger batch sizes.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic benchmarks and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks demonstrate the superior performance of q-KG, especially in noisy environments. The inclusion of comparisons to several relevant baseline methods strengthens these conclusions.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making the concepts accessible. The code availability is also a significant plus.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Gaussian Process Assumption:** While widely used, the reliance on Gaussian Processes may be limiting, particularly when dealing with high-dimensional input spaces common in modern machine learning applications (e.g., neural network architectures). It would be valuable to discuss the potential limitations of this assumption and explore alternative modeling approaches.
*   **Theoretical Analysis Depth:** While the paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the method, a more in-depth analysis of the error bounds and computational complexity, particularly with respect to input dimension, would further strengthen the contribution.
*   **Notation Clarity:** Minor inconsistencies in notation could be addressed for improved readability. Specifically, the definition of *A* (l.95) and the input of *µ<sup>n</sup>* (l.104) could be clarified.
*   **Related Work Discussion:** A brief discussion of the parallel EI algorithm within the main text (l.128) would provide better context for the comparisons.
*   **Ambiguous A:** The use of 'A' for both the feasible domain and within equations (like A in Section 5.3) could create confusion. Employing distinct notation could enhance clarity.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian Optimization with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   The theoretical contributions are solid, and the proposed method is computationally efficient.
*   The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of q-KG over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings.
*   The paper is well-written and clear, with readily available code.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on Gaussian Processes could limit the applicability of the method to certain problem domains.
*   A more thorough theoretical analysis of error bounds and computational complexity would be beneficial.
*   Minor clarity issues with notation and related work discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. While some areas could be further strengthened with additional theoretical analysis and clarification, the novelty, theoretical foundation, computational efficiency, and empirical validation collectively support its acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the identified weaknesses.
