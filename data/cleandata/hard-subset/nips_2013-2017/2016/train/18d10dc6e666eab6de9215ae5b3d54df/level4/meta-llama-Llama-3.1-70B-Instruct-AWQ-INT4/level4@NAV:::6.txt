This work presents a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization by identifying the Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next. The proposed method utilizes the knowledge gradient (q-KG) to measure the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations. To efficiently maximize the acquisition function, the authors employ infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the gradient of q-KG. The experimental results demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noise-free problems and outperforms them on problems with observation noise in three experiments. While the concept of parallel hyperparameter optimization is appealing, allowing for efficient utilization of multi-core environments, some aspects of the work remain unclear, and the experimental results are not entirely convincing.

Technical Quality:
The paper uses common artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method, as well as real-world problems such as optimizing a CNN and logistic regression for Cifar10/MNIST. However, to further strengthen the experimental section, it would be beneficial to include an experiment comparing wall-clock time or analyzing the potential gains of using this parallel method over simple random search or sequential optimization. Additionally, the choice of q = 4 for all experiments requires further justification.

Novelty/Originality:
The paper proposes using the knowledge gradient as an acquisition function and infinitesimal perturbation analysis to maximize q-KG. Although these methods themselves are not new, the application of q-KG in this context is novel. However, the paper's connection to "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions" is notable, as it previously demonstrated the use of IPA to select a batch of configurations using expected improvement.

Impact/Usefulness:
Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant to various real-world applications, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

Clarity/Presentation:
The technical details and background section are well-described, and the experiment section provides sufficient information to comprehend the performed comparisons.

Questions to the authors:

1. Given the relevance of Wang et al. (2015) to this work and the availability of their code online, have you compared your method to theirs? If so, it would be beneficial to clearly state the differences in the paper to avoid confusion.

2. With modern multi-core systems allowing for more than 4 parallel runs, do you have any insights into the scalability of your method and the potential gains from increasing the number of parallel runs?

3. Would it be possible to extend your method to run asynchronously, similar to Spearmint?

4. Have you evaluated the wall-clock time comparison between your method and random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization? What is the overhead for computing a new batch?

5. Could you clarify the meaning of "iterations" as the x-label in your plots? Does it represent the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design, and if so, why do not all methods start from the same value?

Minor comments:

* Is there a missing z^(1:n) in equation (4.1)?
* "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" should be "achieves".
* Parenthetical references should be rephrased, e.g., "[16] suggests parallelizing" should be "Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing".
* The reference to "Wang et al. (2015)" for IPA is incorrect, as they did not introduce the method but rather applied it.
