This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel algorithm for batch Bayesian Optimization. The work addresses the setting where function evaluations are expensive, and multiple evaluations can be performed in parallel. The core contribution is a new acquisition function, q-KG, which is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective to select the one-step Bayes-optimal batch of `q` points to evaluate. To overcome the computational challenge of maximizing this acquisition function, the authors develop an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through empirical comparisons against several state-of-the-art batch Bayesian Optimization algorithms on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks for machine learning models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The problem of parallelizing Bayesian Optimization is highly relevant, as parallel computing resources are increasingly available. The proposed q-KG method is a principled and novel extension of the Knowledge Gradient to the batch setting. Unlike many existing methods that construct batches greedily or heuristically, q-KG provides a fully Bayesian approach that jointly optimizes the selection of all points in the batch, maximizing the expected improvement in the final solution quality.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is sound and well-motivated from a decision-theoretic standpoint. The development of an efficient, gradient-based method for maximizing q-KG is a significant technical contribution, making the approach practical for continuous domains.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments on both synthetic and real-world problems (tuning logistic regression and CNNs). The comparison with multiple strong baselines, including different versions of parallel EI and UCB, is thorough. The results convincingly show that q-KG is competitive and often outperforms existing methods, with particularly strong performance in the more realistic noisy setting.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Scalability and Assumptions:** The method relies on a Gaussian Process (GP) model, which is standard in Bayesian Optimization. However, the scalability of GPs to high-dimensional problems is a well-known challenge. The experiments involve tuning up to 8 hyperparameters, but it remains unclear how the method would perform in much higher-dimensional search spaces, such as those encountered in neural architecture search. A discussion of the limitations of the GP assumption and potential challenges in high-dimensional settings would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks theoretical analysis. It would be beneficial for the authors to provide theoretical results concerning their algorithm, such as convergence guarantees or an analysis of the computational complexity with respect to the input dimension `d` and the batch size `q`. This would provide deeper insight into the method's properties and limitations.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the Bayesian Optimization literature. The proposed q-KG method is principled, technically sound, and empirically shown to be effective. While the paper could be improved by a discussion on scalability and the inclusion of theoretical analysis, the work is a significant step forward for batch Bayesian Optimization. The method is sound, the experiments are convincing, and the paper is generally well-written. I recommend acceptance.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **l.36:** The phrasing "the set of points to evaluate next that is" is slightly awkward. Consider rephrasing for clarity.
*   **l.95, l.140:** The notation `A` is used to denote both the overall feasible domain for the optimization problem (in eq. 4.2) and the domain for the inner minimization problem (in eq. 4.1), which is later discretized. This can be confusing. Using distinct notation for the discretization of the domain (e.g., `A_d` instead of `An`) and clarifying its role might improve readability.
*   **l.104:** In `min_{x∈A} µ^(n)(x)`, the notation is standard, but a brief reminder that `µ^(n)` is the posterior mean after `n` observations could be helpful for readers less familiar with GP notation.
*   **l.128:** The paper states that q-KG reduces to the parallel EI algorithm under certain conditions. It would be helpful to briefly define or describe the parallel EI algorithm at this point for better context and to make the paper more self-contained.
