This paper addresses the important problem of parallel Bayesian optimization (BO), which is highly relevant for tasks like hyperparameter tuning where multiple evaluations can be run simultaneously. The authors propose a novel algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, which selects a batch of points to evaluate by identifying the one-step Bayes-optimal batch. This decision-theoretic approach is more principled than many existing heuristics for parallel BO. The authors derive an efficient gradient-based method for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function and demonstrate empirically that their method outperforms several state-of-the-art parallel BO algorithms, particularly in settings with noisy function evaluations.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The parallelization of BO is a significant challenge for making these methods practical, and this work presents an elegant and theoretically grounded approach. The core contribution—determining a Bayes-optimal batch rather than constructing it greedily—is a strong one and represents a valuable advance in the field.

However, while the theoretical contribution is strong, the experimental validation has several limitations that temper the conclusions.

First, a key assumption of the model is that measurement errors are independent and normally distributed. While common in Gaussian Process literature, this assumption can be problematic in the context of hyperparameter optimization. In this setting, "measurement error" often stems from using a finite validation set to estimate generalization performance. This can introduce structural biases, particularly in certain regions of the hyperparameter space or with smaller datasets, where the assumption of independent noise may not hold. A brief discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper.

Second, the experiments could be more convincing. I have three main concerns:

1.  **Choice of Optimization Metric:** The experiments on logistic regression and CNNs use test set error as the optimization criterion. It is well-established that threshold-based metrics like accuracy or error rate can be suboptimal for model selection and hyperparameter tuning, as they are less sensitive than metrics like AUC or log-likelihood [1]. The evaluation would be more robust if it relied on these alternative metrics.

2.  **Lack of Standardized Benchmarks:** While MNIST and CIFAR-10 are standard datasets, the hyperparameter optimization community has developed dedicated benchmark suites like HPOlib [2]. Using such a suite would have made the results more comparable to a wider body of literature and provided a more comprehensive assessment of the algorithm's performance across diverse and challenging optimization landscapes.

3.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is confined to two datasets for a single machine learning task (classification) using two specific models (logistic regression and a CNN). The performance of optimization algorithms can vary significantly across different model types and tasks. To make a more compelling case for the general superiority of q-KG, the benchmark should include a greater variety of learning algorithms (e.g., SVMs, GBDTs) and tasks (e.g., regression).

In summary, the paper presents a valuable and elegant theoretical contribution to parallel Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method is well-motivated and principled. However, the current experimental benchmark is too limited to be fully convincing of the method's practical, widespread advantages. I would be more confident in the paper's impact if the empirical evaluation were broadened and strengthened as suggested above.

[2] Eggensperger, K., et al. (2013). Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters. In *NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice*.
