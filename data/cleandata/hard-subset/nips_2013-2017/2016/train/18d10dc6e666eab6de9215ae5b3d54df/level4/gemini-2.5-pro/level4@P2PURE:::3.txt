This paper presents a Bayesian optimization algorithm designed for optimizing multivariate functions over a finite domain. The algorithm's key novelty is its ability to select a batch of points for parallel evaluation in each iteration. To select these points optimally, the authors extend the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework. Whereas standard KG estimates the expected improvement from evaluating a single additional point, this work generalizes the approach to simultaneously select a batch of multiple points.

As maximizing this batch KG criterion is computationally intractable, the authors propose an algorithm that uses Monte Carlo estimation and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to compute the gradients needed to optimize the point selection. Experimental results on several test problems show that the proposed algorithm outperforms or is highly competitive with existing methods, particularly those based on Confidence Bound criteria. The paper is well-written and the method appears promising for practical applications.

However, I have some reservations about the level of novelty. The core idea is to extend the existing KG policy to a batch setting, which is a natural next step. While this extension requires a more sophisticated optimization procedure, the main technical contribution—using an IPA-based gradient estimator—is an application of a fairly standard technique. Consequently, the primary innovation appears to be the application of a known method to a straightforward extension of an existing framework.

I have one specific comment regarding notation: In Equation (3.1), it is unclear how μ(x¹:ⁿ) and K(x¹:ⁿ, x¹:ⁿ) are defined, as x¹:ⁿ is a finite sequence of vectors, whereas the functions μ and K are defined on single vectors and pairs of vectors, respectively. The authors should clarify this notation.
