Summary
The paper presents a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), for optimizing expensive black-box functions when multiple evaluations can be run in parallel. Extending the knowledge gradient (KG) framework, which quantifies the expected improvement from an additional evaluation, the authors define an acquisition function that evaluates the expected gain from a batch of q points and selects the batch that maximizes this gain. Because optimizing this objective directly is intractable, they estimate the objective and its gradient using Monte Carlo sampling and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). They report results on standard synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning tasks, showing that q-KG typically outperforms or is competitive with parallel EI and UCB-based methods, with particularly strong gains when evaluations are noisy.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on the well-established KG framework for sequential Bayesian optimization and relates to prior batch/parallel methods including parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE. The paper generalizes KG to the batch setting and proposes a computational strategy for optimizing the batch acquisition via Monte Carlo and IPA. This positions the work among decision-theoretic BO approaches, in contrast to UCB-style methods designed primarily for cumulative regret.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic motivation: q-KG directly targets the expected one-step reduction in the posterior minimum when selecting a batch, a principled criterion aligned with BO’s simple regret objective.
- Practical optimization strategy: the IPA-based gradient estimator facilitates joint optimization over q points, avoiding purely greedy constructions.
- Empirical performance: consistent improvements over strong baselines on both synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks; the advantage appears larger in noisy settings, which is important in practice.
- Implementation and reproducibility: integration with existing BO toolkits and release of code increase potential impact.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty: the core idea extends a known acquisition (KG) to batches. While the batch generalization and gradient estimation are useful, the conceptual advance beyond existing KG and parallel EI/UCB methods seems moderate. The principal novelty is the IPA-based gradient estimation for q-KG, which itself is a standard technique.
- Computational aspects: while the paper argues efficiency, the approach still relies on Monte Carlo estimation and discretization for acquisition optimization; the trade-offs versus closed-form approximations (e.g., for parallel EI) could be discussed more thoroughly.

Quality
- The methodology is sound, and the decision-theoretic derivation is appropriate. The use of Monte Carlo and IPA for gradient estimation is standard and justified. Experiments are well chosen and indicate meaningful improvements.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The decision-theoretic framing, algorithm description, and experimental setup are understandable. One notation issue remains (see specific comment below).

Originality
- Moderate. The batch generalization of KG and the IPA-based gradient estimation are useful but incremental relative to prior KG and batch BO literature.

Significance
- Practical significance is solid: batch BO is common in modern ML workflows, and improved performance under noise is valuable. The method should be of interest to practitioners tuning models at scale.

Specific comment/suggestion
- Equation (3.1): Please clarify the notation for µ(x(1:n)) and K(x(1:n), x(1:n)). Since x(1:n) denotes a finite sequence of input vectors, it would help to state explicitly that µ(x(1:n)) denotes the vector [µ(x(1)), …, µ(x(n))] and K(x(1:n), x(1:n)) denotes the n×n Gram matrix with entries K(x(i), x(j)). This will avoid confusion given that µ and K are functions of one or two vectors, not sequences.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled batch acquisition aligned with simple regret.
- Effective and broadly applicable gradient-based optimization of a batch criterion.
- Strong empirical results, especially with noisy evaluations.
- Released implementation likely to aid adoption.

Cons for acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is limited to a natural extension of KG plus standard gradient estimation.
- Computational details (MC/IPA/discretization) may be heavier than some alternatives; scalability trade-offs could be better quantified.

Overall
A well-executed and practically useful extension of KG to the batch setting with a workable optimization strategy and strong empirical results. While the novelty is incremental, the contribution is solid and relevant to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance if the authors clarify the notation around Equation (3.1) and, if space permits, discuss computational trade-offs more explicitly.
