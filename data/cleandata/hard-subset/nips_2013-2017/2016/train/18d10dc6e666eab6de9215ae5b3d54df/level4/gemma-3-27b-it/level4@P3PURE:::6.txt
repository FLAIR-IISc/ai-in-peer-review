This work introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization, focused on computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations for evaluation. The proposed approach utilizes the knowledge gradient (q-KG) to measure the utility of evaluating a batch, and employs infinitesimal perturbation analysis to efficiently maximize this acquisition function. The authors demonstrate through three experiments that q-KG performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on problems without observation noise, and outperforms them on problems *with* observation noise. While the idea of parallel hyperparameter optimization to leverage multi-core environments is appealing, several aspects of the work remain unclear, and the experimental results are not fully convincing. Specific feedback and questions are provided below.

**Technical Quality:** The paper evaluates the proposed method using common artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate its superiority. Importantly, the authors also include results for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR10/MNIST. However, the experiment section would be significantly strengthened by the inclusion of wall-clock time measurements, or an analysis quantifying the gains achieved through this parallel method compared to simpler approaches like random search or sequential optimization. Furthermore, the rationale for setting *q* to 4 across all experiments is not provided and requires justification.

**Novelty/Originality:** The paper proposes leveraging the knowledge gradient (a generalization of Expected Improvement) as an acquisition function, combined with infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for maximizing q-KG. While both methods are established, the combination itself presents a contribution. However, the paper acknowledges “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions,” which already demonstrates the application of IPA to batch selection using Expected Improvement. It is therefore important to clearly delineate the differences between this work and prior art. 

**Impact/Usefulness:** Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant to a wide range of applications across disciplines including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics. 

**Clarity/Presentation:** The technical details are well-described, and the background section provides sufficient context. The experiment section is comprehensive, containing all necessary information to understand the reported comparisons.

**Questions to the Authors:**

1.) Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant to this work, and code is publicly available. Have you compared your method to theirs? (If the reference is to “parallel EI in MOE”, clarifying this and using the notation q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel method would be beneficial to avoid confusion.) A clearer articulation of the distinctions between these approaches is needed.

2.) Modern multi-core systems routinely support far more than 4 parallel runs. For example, "Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks" (ICML’15) used up to 800 parallel runs of DNGO, "Parallel Algorithm Configuration" (LION’12) used 25 SMAC runs, and "Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms" (NIPS’12) used up to 10 parallel runs of Spearmint. Do you have any insights into the scalability of your method and the potential benefits of increasing the number of parallel runs?

3.) Currently, your method appears to operate synchronously. Would it be feasible to extend it to asynchronous operation, as implemented in Spearmint?

4.) Ultimately, wall-clock time is a critical factor. Have you evaluated how your method compares to random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead associated with computing a new batch?

5.) The x-axis label "iterations" in your plots is ambiguous. Does it represent the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Furthermore, does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do the methods not all begin from the same value?

**Minor Comments:**

* There appears to be a missing z^(1:n) in equation (4.1).
* “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” should read “achieves a test error.”
* Parenthetical references are not nouns; for example, “[16] suggests parallelizing” should be “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing.”
* The reference to “Wang et al. (2015)” may be inaccurate; they do not introduce IPA but rather apply it.
