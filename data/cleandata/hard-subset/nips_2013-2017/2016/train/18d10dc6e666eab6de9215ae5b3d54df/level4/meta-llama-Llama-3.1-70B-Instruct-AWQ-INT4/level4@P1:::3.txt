The paper proposes a new version of a stochastic gradient algorithm combined with Bayesian modeling for optimizing a multivariate objective function over a finite domain. The novel part of the algorithm is that in every iteration it identifies a number of points on which to compute the objective, and this computation can be parallelized. The problem is to identify the sampling points in some optimal sense. For this purpose, the authors adopt the framework of Knowledge Gradient, which estimates the expected improvement in the objective value if one more sampling point is used and, based on that, makes a decision on whether and which sampling point to use. This approach is generalized here so that the decision is made on multiple sampling points in batch.

Since this problem is computationally intractable, the authors develop an algorithm based on Monte Carlo and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis to estimate the relevant gradients, thereby maximizing the information gain over the set of candidate sampling sets. The algorithm is applied to several test problems and is shown to either outperform or seriously compete with other approaches in the literature, mainly those based on Confidence Bounds.

The paper is well-written and potentially useful for practical applications. However, I am not convinced that the level of novelty is very high, since the authors expand a previously studied approach (KG) to a batch setting. This requires a more sophisticated method for optimizing the criterion over a set of multiple points. The main novelty of the paper, in my opinion, lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient based on an IPA approach, which is also rather standard.

One specific comment I have is regarding equation (3.1). Since x^(1:n) is a finite sequence of vectors, it is not clear how mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) are defined, as mu and K are functions of (one or two) vectors and not sequences. It would be beneficial to clarify this point.

Strengths:

- The paper proposes a new algorithm for parallel Bayesian optimization, which has the potential to be useful in practical applications.
- The algorithm is well-motivated and based on a clear theoretical framework.
- The paper presents several experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of the algorithm.

Weaknesses:

- The level of novelty is not very high, as the authors build upon a previously studied approach (KG).
- The main novelty of the paper lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient based on an IPA approach, which is also rather standard.
- There is a lack of clarity in the definition of mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) in equation (3.1).
