This paper proposes a method for parallel batch Bayesian optimization that selects a Bayes-optimal set of configurations to evaluate next by maximizing the knowledge gradient for batches (q-KG). The authors develop an estimator of the gradient of q-KG via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable efficient optimization of the acquisition. Across three experiments, q-KG is competitive with existing parallel BO methods on noiseless problems and outperforms them when observations are noisy. I find the goal of efficient parallel hyperparameter optimization compelling, but several aspects remain unclear, and I am not fully convinced by the experimental evidence.

Technical quality
- The paper evaluates on standard synthetic benchmarks and on two ML tasks (CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR-10/MNIST), which is appropriate.
- However, the experimental section lacks an analysis of wall-clock performance. Please quantify the practical speedups from parallelization versus baselines such as random search and sequential BO. Without wall-clock results, it is hard to assess real-world utility.
- The choice to fix q=4 in all experiments is not justified. Please explain this design choice and provide ablations or sensitivity analysis over q.

Novelty/originality
- The paper uses the knowledge gradient as the acquisition function and IPA to optimize q-KG. While this combination is interesting, both components are established. Prior work (e.g., “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions”) already uses IPA to optimize batch expected improvement (q-EI).
- The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of what is novel relative to prior batch BO methods (e.g., why q-KG provides advantages over q-EI in theory and practice, and what is new in the gradient estimator or optimization scheme).

Impact/usefulness
- Parallel hyperparameter tuning is highly relevant across ML, vision, biology, and robotics. If the method scales and delivers measurable wall-clock gains, it could be impactful in practice.

Clarity/presentation
- The background and technical sections are well-written, and the experimental setup is generally clear.
- Nevertheless, key practical details are missing: computational overhead per batch, scaling with q, and synchronous vs. asynchronous operation.

Questions for the authors
1) Comparison to Wang et al. (2015): This appears closely related, and code is available. Did you compare against that method? If by “parallel EI in MOE” you mean q-EI, please name it as such to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallelization. It would help to explicitly list methodological differences and clarify how your approach relates to both q-EI (as in MOE) and Spearmint’s strategy.
2) Scaling with the level of parallelism: Modern systems often allow many more than 4 parallel evaluations (e.g., DNGO used up to 800, SMAC up to 25, and Spearmint up to 10). How does your method scale in computation and performance as q increases? What practical gains should users expect from larger q?
3) Asynchrony: The method is presented as synchronous. Can it be adapted to asynchronous settings (as in Spearmint), and if so, how would you maintain the benefits of the q-KG formulation?
4) Wall-clock efficiency: How does your approach compare to random search and sequential BO in actual runtime to reach a given accuracy? What is the overhead of computing each batch (as a function of n, q, and dimensionality)?
5) Plot axes: What does “iterations” on the x-axis denote—number of function evaluations or number of batches? Does it include the initial design? If yes, why do the methods not start from the same value?

Minor comments
- Equation (4.1) may be missing z^(1:n).
- “This architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieves a test error …”
- Parenthetical references are not nouns; e.g., use “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing” rather than “[16] suggests parallelizing.”
- Wang et al. (2015) is not the original source of IPA; please cite an appropriate foundational reference for IPA in addition to that work.

Overall, the method is promising and well-presented, but stronger empirical validation—particularly wall-clock metrics, scaling with q, and comparisons to closely related batch BO methods—would substantially improve the paper.
