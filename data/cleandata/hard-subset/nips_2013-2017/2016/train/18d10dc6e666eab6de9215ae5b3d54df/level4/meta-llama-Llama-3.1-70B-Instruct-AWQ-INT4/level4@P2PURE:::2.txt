Bayesian optimization has gained significant popularity in machine learning for tasks such as hyperparameter optimization, but most existing methods are inherently sequential. This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, demonstrating superior performance to existing Bayesian optimization approaches. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. Parallelizing Bayesian optimization is a crucial subject for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed approach is interesting and more elegant than most existing methods. The determination of a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising. The authors assume independent normally distributed errors, a common assumption in most Bayesian optimization methods based on Gaussian processes. However, in hyperparameter optimization, this assumption can be problematic, as measurement errors represent the difference between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., through cross-validation). Structural bias is common in certain regions of the hyperparameter space, especially with small sample sizes. It is worth noting that assuming independence may not always be realistic.

The theoretical aspect of the paper is strong, but the experimental evaluation is somewhat disappointing for three main reasons. Firstly, using test set error as an optimization criterion is problematic, as it is well known that such score functions are suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Threshold-based metrics like test set error introduce several problems for model selection and hyperparameter optimization, making it preferable to use metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss. Secondly, the authors use the MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets as the basis of their experiments, which are commonly used in benchmarks. However, it would be more beneficial to use benchmarks from HPOlib [2], a widely-used library specifically designed to benchmark hyperparameter optimization algorithms. Thirdly, the number of datasets (2) used in the benchmark is very low, and the entire benchmark is based on a single machine learning task (classification) with only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). It would be desirable to see more variety in the benchmark, as it is known that some optimizers perform well for certain types of learning algorithms but poorly for others.

Overall, I appreciate the paper and the theoretical contribution, which is valuable. However, the current benchmark is too limited to be truly convincing. [1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998. [2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
