This paper studies batch-sequential Bayesian optimization and introduces a parallel version of the Knowledge Gradient, denoted q-KG. After a concise review of related work and Gaussian process preliminaries, the authors define q-KG, derive its computation by extending the standard KG framework, and present numerical experiments in which q-KG outperforms several state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization methods. The paper is well-written, technically solid, and addresses an important problem; the practical impact of enabling effective parallel Bayesian optimization is substantial. I believe the work meets the bar for publication at NIPS.

A few minor issues remain. First, the paper does not analyze wall-clock speedups from batching (sequential vs. batch), which would strengthen the empirical story. Second, parts of the literature review could be more precise:
- Integrating EI with respect to posterior distributions, alongside Constant Liar, was already discussed in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization,” and Chevalier et al. notably proposed CL-mix in [2].
- The maximization of q-EI using natural gradients was studied in “Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design.”

Additional remarks and questions:
- Assumptions: Is the domain A compact, and is the objective f assumed continuous?
- Initialization: Why restrict A to a Latin hypercube sample at the initial stage? Could other designs be used, and does this choice affect performance?
- Algorithm 1: How are GP hyperparameters handled—periodic re-estimation (e.g., MLE/MAP) between batches or full Bayesian updating? Please clarify the schedule and its computational cost.
- Section 5.2: In the definition of g, should it be a maximization or a minimization? Please verify the sign convention.
- Also in Section 5.2: The smoothness of g is not immediate; a brief justification or set of conditions would help.
- Gradients through the Cholesky factorization are nontrivial; a citation or short derivation would be valuable.
- The Matérn kernel order appears to be 5/2, not 2/5.
- Figure 1 and related experiments: Were the same GP modeling choices and software used across methods to ensure a fair comparison? If not, can you comment on potential discrepancies?

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution that extends the Knowledge Gradient to parallel evaluation settings with careful derivations and promising results. Addressing the points above—especially clarifying assumptions, hyperparameter treatment, and empirical fairness—would further improve the paper’s clarity and impact.
