This paper proposes a novel method for batch Bayesian optimization, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient by selecting a batch of points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality, assuming it is the last batch to be evaluated. This decision-theoretic framing makes it, by construction, the one-step Bayes-optimal policy. Recognizing that naively maximizing the q-KG acquisition function is computationally prohibitive, the authors derive an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and Monte Carlo sampling. The proposed method is evaluated on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks for machine learning models, demonstrating superior performance compared to several state-of-the-art parallel Bayesian optimization algorithms, particularly in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper is well-motivated. The proposed q-KG acquisition function is derived from a principled, decision-theoretic perspective, which provides a strong theoretical foundation for the method.
*   The primary technical contribution—an efficient method for computing the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function—is significant. It addresses the key computational challenge of optimizing a complex, Monte Carlo-estimated acquisition function over a batch of points, making the approach practical.
*   The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic and real-world problems in noise-free and noisy settings. The results convincingly demonstrate that q-KG is a high-performing algorithm, often outperforming established baselines. The particularly strong performance in the noisy setting is a notable advantage, as many practical optimization problems involve noise.
*   The authors have made their code available, which is commendable and greatly aids in the reproducibility of the work.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
1.  **Positioning with Respect to Prior Work:** The paper's main contribution appears to be the efficient gradient-based optimization of the batch KG policy. However, the concept of a batch knowledge gradient policy has been explored before (e.g., Wang et al., 2015, cited as [26] in the paper). The paper would be strengthened by a more direct discussion of this prior work and an explicit comparison, either experimentally or analytically. Without this, it is difficult to fully assess the novelty and performance improvement of the proposed optimization technique over existing methods for optimizing batch KG.

2.  **Clarity of the Core Technical Section:** Section 5.2, which describes the gradient estimation via IPA, is the technical core of the paper. This section is quite condensed and would benefit from further elaboration. Providing more intermediate steps in the derivation of the gradient estimator would improve the paper's clarity and make it easier for readers to understand and reproduce the central contribution.

3.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparisons:** The comparison to parallel Expected Improvement (EI) in the noisy setting (Section 6.2) could be made more robust. Standard EI is formulated for noise-free objective functions and is known to perform poorly under noise. The paper should clarify which specific variant of parallel EI was used in the experiments. If a standard noise-agnostic version was used, the reported performance gap may be partially explained by this known limitation of EI. Discussing this nuance or comparing against a noise-aware variant of EI would strengthen the experimental claims.

4.  **Minor Issue:** The abbreviation "IPA" is redefined in line 162, despite having been introduced earlier.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper presents a well-founded, Bayes-optimal acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization. It makes a strong technical contribution by developing an efficient gradient-based method to optimize this function, overcoming a major computational hurdle. The experimental results are strong and show a clear advantage over existing methods, especially on important noisy problems.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The discussion and comparison to prior work on batch KG are insufficient, making the precise novelty of the contribution unclear. The core technical section lacks some detail, and the experimental comparison against EI in the noisy setting could be perceived as unfair without further clarification.
