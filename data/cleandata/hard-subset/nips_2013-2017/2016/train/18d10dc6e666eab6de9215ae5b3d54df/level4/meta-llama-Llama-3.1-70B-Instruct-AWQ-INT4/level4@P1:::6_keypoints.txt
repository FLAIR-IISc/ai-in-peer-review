- Proposes parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization using the knowledge gradient (q-KG) and IPA-based gradient estimation to select Bayes-optimal batches.
- Empirical results: q-KG is competitive with existing parallel BO on noiseless problems and outperforms them on noisy problems.

- Technical quality: uses standard artificial benchmarks plus CNN/logistic on CIFAR10/MNIST.
- Technical concern: no wall-clock time measurements or comparisons to random search/sequential BO to quantify practical gains.
- Experimental choice concern: unclear why batch size q is fixed to 4 across experiments.

- Novelty: combines known methods (KG and IPA); prior work (parallel EI using IPA) appears closely related and needs clearer differentiation.

- Impact: parallel hyperparameter tuning is broadly relevant across many application domains.

- Clarity: technical background and experiment descriptions are well written and reproducible.

- Question: Have you compared to Wang et al. (2015) / parallel EI (q‑EI)? If so, please clarify and name it q‑EI to avoid confusion.
- Question: How does the method scale to larger parallelism (e.g., >4, up to tens/hundreds)? Any empirical or complexity insights?
- Question: Can the synchronous method be extended to asynchronous evaluations (like Spearmint)?
- Question: What is the wall-clock overhead for computing batches and how does overall runtime compare to random/sequential methods?
- Question: What does “iterations” on the x-axis mean (evaluations or batches), does it include the initial design, and why don't all methods start at the same value?

- Minor: possible missing z^(1:n) in Eq. (4.1).
- Minor: grammar correction (“achieve” → “achieves”).
- Minor: parenthetical references should be phrased as nouns (e.g., “Snoek et al. [16] suggest…”).
- Minor: Wang et al. (2015) likely do not introduce IPA and the citation may be incorrect for IPA origin.

- Strength: introduces a new parallel BO algorithm (parallel knowledge gradient).
- Strength: decision-theoretic derivation provides solid theoretical foundation.
- Strength: provides an efficient strategy to compute Bayes-optimal batches, making the method tractable.
- Strength: demonstrates competitive or superior empirical performance, particularly with observation noise.

- Weakness: lacks thorough comparison to other parallel BO methods (e.g., parallel EI).
- Weakness: experiments are limited to a few synthetic functions and two practical problems; generalization to more complex tasks is unclear.
- Weakness: no detailed analysis of computational complexity or scaling with number of parallel runs.
- Weakness: paper does not discuss limitations or directions for future work.

- For acceptance: novel algorithm with theoretical grounding, strong noisy-case performance, and clear reproducibility.
- Against acceptance: insufficient comparisons, limited experimental breadth, and missing scalability/complexity analysis.
