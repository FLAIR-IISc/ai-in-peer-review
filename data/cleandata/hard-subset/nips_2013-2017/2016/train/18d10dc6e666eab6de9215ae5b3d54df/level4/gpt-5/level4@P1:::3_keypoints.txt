- Presents q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that generalizes Knowledge Gradient to select a batch of q points by maximizing expected one-step reduction in the posterior minimum.  
- Uses Monte Carlo sampling and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the q-KG objective and its gradient for joint optimization of the batch.  
- Evaluated on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks, showing q-KG outperforms or matches parallel EI/UCB methods, especially under noisy evaluations.  
- Builds on established KG framework and relates to parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE; positions as decision-theoretic BO rather than cumulative-regret UCB methods.  
- Strength: principled decision-theoretic objective aligned with simple regret.  
- Strength: practical IPA-based gradient estimator enabling joint (non-greedy) batch optimization.  
- Strength: strong empirical gains on benchmarks and noisy settings.  
- Strength: code release and integration with BO toolkits improve reproducibility and impact.  
- Weakness: conceptual novelty is moderate—natural batch extension of KG and use of standard IPA are incremental.  
- Weakness: computational cost concerns remain due to Monte Carlo estimation and discretization; trade-offs versus closed-form approximations need more discussion.  
- Methodology and experiments are sound and well chosen.  
- Paper is generally well written and organized, but notation around Equation (3.1) is ambiguous.  
- Originality assessed as moderate—useful but incremental relative to prior KG and batch BO work.  
- Practical significance is solid for practitioners needing batch BO, particularly with noisy evaluations.  
- Specific suggestion: explicitly define µ(x(1:n)) as the vector [µ(x(1)),…,µ(x(n))] and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) as the n×n Gram matrix to avoid confusion.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify notation and, if possible, expand discussion of computational trade-offs.
