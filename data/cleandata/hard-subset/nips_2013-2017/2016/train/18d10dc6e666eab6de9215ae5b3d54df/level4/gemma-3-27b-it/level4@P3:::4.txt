This paper proposes the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization, addressing the common scenario where function evaluations are computationally expensive and can be parallelized. The authors develop a novel acquisition function, q-KG, designed to select a batch of *q* points for simultaneous evaluation, aiming for Bayes-optimal performance with a single batch remaining. A key contribution is an efficient computational strategy, based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), to optimize the q-KG function, particularly crucial when *q* is large. The method’s effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning, showcasing superior performance, especially in noisy evaluation settings.

This work tackles an important problem: accelerating Bayesian Optimization through parallelization. The ability to leverage parallel computing resources is critical for real-world applications, particularly when evaluating complex models like deep neural networks. The q-KG method represents a theoretically sound approach to batch selection, moving beyond iterative greedy strategies employed in previous work (e.g., parallel EI, UCB-based methods) by considering the batch as a whole. The IPA-based optimization is a clever and necessary contribution, addressing the computational challenges inherent in maximizing the q-KG acquisition function. The empirical validation is well-executed, showing consistent gains over established benchmarks, and the provision of code further strengthens the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The q-KG method is grounded in decision theory, aiming for Bayes-optimal batch selection.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The IPA-based gradient estimation provides a practical solution for optimizing the acquisition function.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The method demonstrates consistent improvements over existing parallel Bayesian Optimization algorithms on both synthetic and real-world problems. The advantage is particularly pronounced in noisy evaluation scenarios.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured.
*   **Code Availability:** Providing the code promotes reproducibility and facilitates adoption by the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Gaussian Process Assumption:** The reliance on a Gaussian Process prior might be limiting in high-dimensional input spaces, such as those commonly encountered in neural network architecture search. Exploring the robustness of the method to deviations from the GP assumption or considering alternative surrogate models would be valuable.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a clear motivation and algorithm description, a more detailed theoretical analysis of the convergence properties and error bounds would strengthen the contribution.  Specifically, quantifying the impact of the discretization of the search space (Section 5.3) on the optimality of the solution would be helpful.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experimental results are compelling, expanding the evaluation to a wider range of hyperparameter tuning problems and datasets would further demonstrate the generalizability of the q-KG method.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically motivated approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization.
*   The proposed method demonstrably outperforms existing state-of-the-art algorithms in challenging scenarios (noisy evaluations).
*   The efficient optimization strategy makes the method practically applicable.
*   The code release fosters reproducibility and community adoption.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The underlying Gaussian Process assumption may limit the method’s applicability to high-dimensional problems.
*   The lack of rigorous theoretical guarantees regarding convergence and error bounds.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The proposed q-KG method offers a principled and efficient approach to parallel batch evaluation, and the empirical results are convincing. While the limitations regarding the Gaussian Process assumption and theoretical analysis warrant consideration, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** of this paper.
