This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) criterion, designed for scenarios where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors derive q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective and develop an efficient computational approach for its implementation. Through extensive numerical experiments on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems, they demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings. This is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field by enabling more efficient optimization of expensive black-box functions. The ability to parallelize Bayesian optimization algorithms is critically important for many real-world applications.

The paper begins with a concise introduction to Bayesian optimization and the motivation for parallel evaluation. It then provides necessary background on Gaussian processes and related work. The core contribution, the q-KG criterion, is clearly defined and its computation is detailed, building upon existing techniques for the standard Knowledge Gradient criterion. The experimental results are well-presented and convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of q-KG is well-motivated and appears mathematically sound. The proposed computational approach, utilizing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), addresses a significant challenge in optimizing this criterion efficiently.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow from introduction to experiments. The notation is consistent, and the key concepts are explained clearly.
*   **Originality:** The extension of the Knowledge Gradient to the parallel batch setting is a novel contribution. While building on previous work, the q-KG criterion offers a distinct approach to batch acquisition function design.
*   **Significance:** The potential impact of improved batch Bayesian optimization algorithms is substantial, particularly in areas like hyperparameter tuning for machine learning models, where function evaluations are costly. The demonstrated performance gains over existing methods suggest a significant advancement in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Analysis of Speed-ups:** The paper focuses on performance (finding better function values) but does not explicitly analyze the speed-up achieved through parallelization compared to sequential Bayesian optimization. Reporting speed-up metrics would strengthen the paper.
*   **Imprecision in Literature Review:** The literature review could be more precise in attributing related work. The iterative construction of batches using Expected Improvement averaged over the posterior was presented in Ginsbourger et al. ("Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization") alongside the Constant Liar algorithm, and Marmin et al. ("Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design") explored q-EI maximization using natural gradients.
*   **Assumptions & Details:** Certain assumptions (e.g., regarding the domain *A* being compact and the function *f* being continuous) are not explicitly stated. Additionally, some details regarding the implementation choices (e.g., using a Latin Hypercube Sample at the initial stage) could benefit from further justification.
*   **Technical Details:** While the IPA approach is described, a deeper discussion of the smoothness assumptions required for the gradient estimates in Section 5.2 would be beneficial. Specifically, clarifying the justification for the differentiability of *g* and the Cholesky factor would add to the rigor. A minor typographical error regarding the Matérn kernel (5/2 instead of 2/5) was noted.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the superiority of q-KG over existing methods.
*   The potential impact of the work is significant, particularly in areas where function evaluations are expensive.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the key ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of analysis of parallel speed-ups is a notable omission.
*   Minor imprecisions in the literature review detract slightly from the overall quality of the paper.
*   Some technical details could benefit from further clarification.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a discussion of the computational speed-up achieved by q-KG compared to sequential Bayesian optimization?
*   Would you be willing to clarify the imprecisions in the literature review regarding related work on parallel Bayesian optimization?
*   Can you elaborate on the assumptions regarding the continuity of *f* and compactness of *A*?
*   What motivated the choice of a Latin Hypercube Sample for the initial stage in Algorithm 1?
*   Could you provide more details on the smoothness assumptions required for the gradient estimates in Section 5.2?
*   Was it feasible to verify the results by fitting the GPs using different software packages?



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2016. Addressing the questions and suggestions above would further strengthen the paper.
