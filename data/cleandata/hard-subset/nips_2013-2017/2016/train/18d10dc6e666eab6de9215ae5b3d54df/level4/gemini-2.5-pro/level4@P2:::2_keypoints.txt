- Introduces q-KG, a novel parallel extension of the knowledge gradient for batch Bayesian optimization to select q points for simultaneous evaluation.  
- q-KG is derived decision-theoretically and is one-step Bayes-optimal, targeting maximal expected improvement in the final solution after one batch.  
- To make q-KG tractable, the authors develop an efficient gradient estimator using Monte Carlo sampling and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA).  
- Empirical evaluation on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression and CNNs (MNIST, CIFAR-10) shows favorable performance versus other parallel BO methods, especially under noise.  
- Distinguishes itself from heuristic batch methods (e.g., constant liar, greedy EI) by providing a principled, formal optimization criterion.  
- Originality/significance: the parallel generalization of the knowledge gradient is novel and potentially influential for parallel hyperparameter tuning.  
- Technical quality: theoretical derivation is sound and the practical IPA-based optimization strategy addresses intractability concerns.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and situates the contribution within related work.  
- Experimental scope is limited: evaluations cover only two model types and two datasets, which may not generalize across response surface types.  
- Recommends broader experiments across more tasks (e.g., regression, RL) and model types to strengthen empirical claims.  
- Suggests using standardized HPO benchmark suites (e.g., HPOlib) for fairer, wider comparisons against state-of-the-art optimizers.  
- Critiques choice of optimization metric: test accuracy/error can be flat and noisy; smoother metrics like log-loss or AUC could better reflect optimizer performance.  
- Notes a limiting assumption: independent Gaussian observation noise may not hold in HPO settings (validation noise can be structured), and this should be discussed.  
- Recommendation: accept for strong theoretical contribution, but request expanded empirical validation and discussion of noise-model limitations in the final version.
