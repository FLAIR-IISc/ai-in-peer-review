- Proposes q-KG, a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm extending the sequential knowledge gradient to the parallel setting with a Bayes-optimal decision-theoretic acquisition for the final batch.  
- Introduces an IPA-based gradient estimator to efficiently maximize the batch acquisition function, making q-KG practical.  
- Differs fundamentally from parallel EI/UCB and iterative greedy batch construction by optimizing the batch jointly, avoiding greedy suboptimality.  
- Technically significant contribution is the IPA gradient estimation; derivation is clear but Section 5.2 needs more mathematical justification and complexity analysis.  
- Demonstrates consistent empirical superiority over state-of-the-art batch BO methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, etc.), especially in noisy settings.  
- Evaluations include synthetic benchmarks and real hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), showing strong practical performance.  
- Comparison fairness: uses a GP model with noise so EI comparisons are reasonable, but paper should more explicitly explain how parallel EI handles noise.  
- Correctly distinguishes from prior batch KG work (e.g., Wang et al.) which targets discrete sets and is less amenable to gradient-based optimization.  
- Manuscript is well-organized and clearly written, with precise algorithm description and available code for reproducibility.  
- Strengths: principled parallel KG extension, novel efficient IPA computation, comprehensive experiments, strong noisy-performance gains, clear exposition.  
- Weaknesses: novelty claim needs clearer articulation relative to prior batch KG, Section 5.2 needs expanded derivation detail, EI/noise comparison should be more explicit, relation to recent batch methods [15,25] should be discussed.  
- Significance: addresses an important practical problem (parallel evaluations) with direct applicability to hyperparameter tuning and distributed evaluation scenarios.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify novelty and strengthen the IPA/gradient estimation exposition.  
- Pro-accept: principled extension, efficient computation, thorough experiments, practical significance.  
- Con-accept: minor clarifications needed on novelty, gradient-section detail, and EI comparison in noisy settings.
