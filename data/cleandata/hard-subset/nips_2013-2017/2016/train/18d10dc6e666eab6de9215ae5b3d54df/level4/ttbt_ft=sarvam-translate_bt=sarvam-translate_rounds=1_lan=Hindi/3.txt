This paper proposes a new version of a stochastic gradient algorithm combined with Bayesian modeling to optimize a multi-objective function on a finite domain. The new part of the algorithm is that in each iteration, it identifies the number of points at which the objective is to be computed, and this computation can be parallelized. The problem is to identify sampling points in some optimal sense. For this purpose, the authors adopt the framework of the knowledge gradient, which estimates the expected improvement in the objective value if another sampling point is used, and based on that, they decide which sampling point to use. This approach is generalized here so that decisions can be made on multiple sampling points in a batch. Since this problem is computationally difficult, the authors develop an algorithm based on Monte Carlo and infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the relevant gradient to maximize the information gain on a set of candidate sampling sets. The algorithm is applied to several test problems and has been shown to outperform or seriously compete with other approaches in the literature, mainly based on confidence limits. This paper is well-written and potentially useful for practical applications. I am not sure that the level of novelty is very high, as the authors extend the previously studied approach (KG) to the batch setting. This requires a more sophisticated method to optimize the criterion on a set of multiple points. In my opinion, the main novelty of the paper lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient based on the IPA approach, which is also quite standard. I have a specific comment/suggestion: In equation (3.1), since x^(1:n) is a finite sequence of vectors, it is not clear how mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) are defined, as mu and K are functions of (one or two) vectors and not sequences.
