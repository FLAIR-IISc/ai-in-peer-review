Bayesian optimization has gained significant traction in machine learning, particularly for hyperparameter optimization, but many existing methods are inherently sequential. This paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm designed to compute Bayes-optimal batches, and demonstrates its superior performance compared to existing approaches. The paper is well-written and clear. Parallelizing Bayesian optimization is a crucial area for practical hyperparameter tuning, and this approach is both interesting and more elegant than many current methods. The determination of a Bayes-optimal batch is a particularly promising aspect. The authors employ the common assumption of independent, normally distributed errors, typical in Gaussian process-based Bayesian optimization. However, this assumption can be problematic in the context of hyperparameter optimization, where measurement errors stem from the difference between generalization performance and empirical estimates obtained through techniques like cross-validation. Structural bias is often present in hyperparameter spaces, especially with limited sample sizes, and it's worth noting that the independence assumption may not always hold. The paper’s theoretical foundation is strong, but the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited for three primary reasons: First, using test set error as the optimization criterion is questionable, as it is known to be suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Test set error, being a threshold-based metric, introduces difficulties in model selection and hyperparameter optimization; metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss would be preferable. Second, while MNIST and CIFAR10 are common benchmark datasets, I would have preferred to see results reported using HPOlib [2], a library specifically designed for benchmarking hyperparameter optimization algorithms. Third, the benchmark’s limited scope – only two datasets and a single machine learning task (classification) evaluated with two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN) – is a concern. Given that optimizer performance can vary significantly depending on the learning algorithm, a more diverse benchmark would be more compelling. Overall, this is a valuable paper with a strong theoretical contribution. However, the current experimental results are not entirely convincing due to the limited nature of the benchmark. [1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998. [2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
