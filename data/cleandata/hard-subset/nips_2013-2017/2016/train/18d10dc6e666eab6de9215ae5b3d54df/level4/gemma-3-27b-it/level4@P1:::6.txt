This work introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization by computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next. The proposed method measures the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations using the knowledge gradient (q-KG). To efficiently maximize the acquisition function, the authors present a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the gradient of q-KG. In three experiments, they demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on problems without observation noise and outperforms these methods on problems with observation noise. I appreciate the idea of performing parallel hyperparameter optimization to allow the efficient use of multi-core environments, but some aspects remain unclear, and I am not fully convinced by the experimental results. Please find feedback for each of the points below, along with some questions at the end.

**Technical Quality**

The paper utilizes common artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. In addition to these computationally inexpensive problems, the paper also presents results for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression for CIFAR10/MNIST. In the experiment section, I would like to see an experiment showing wall-clock time or an analysis of how much can be gained from using this parallel method compared to simple random search or sequential optimization. Also, the rationale for setting the batch size *q* to 4 for all experiments is not clear.

**Novelty/Originality**

The paper proposes using the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize q-KG. While these methods themselves are not new, the paper refers to “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions,” which already demonstrates how to use IPA to select a batch of configurations using expected improvement.

**Impact/Usefulness**

Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for applications in many disciplines, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

**Clarity/Presentation**

The technical details and background sections are well-described, and the experiment section contains all the necessary information to understand the performed comparisons.

**Questions to the Authors:**

1.) Wang et al. (2015) seems highly relevant to this work, and code appears to be publicly available. Have you compared to this method? (Or is this the “parallel EI in MOE” implementation, in which case, clarifying this by naming it q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel method would be beneficial). To avoid confusion, it would be helpful to explicitly state the differences in the paper.

2.) Modern multi-core systems allow for more than 4 parallel runs. For example, “Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks” (ICML’15) used up to 800 parallel runs of DNGO; “Parallel Algorithm Configuration” (LION’12) used 25 SMAC runs in parallel; and “Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms” (NIPS’12) used up to 10 parallel runs of Spearmint. Do you have any insights into how well your method scales and how much benefit can be gained from increasing the number of parallel runs?

3.) Currently, your method is designed to run synchronously. Would it be possible to extend it to run asynchronously, as Spearmint does?

4.) Ultimately, wall-clock time is crucial. Have you evaluated how your method compares to random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization with respect to wall-clock time? How significant is the overhead for computing a new batch?

5.) What does “iterations” on the x-axis of your plots represent? Is it the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? And does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do all methods not start from the same value?

**Minor Comments:**

*   Is there a z^(1:n) missing in Eq. (4.1)?
*   “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” should be “achieves a test error.”
*   Parenthetical references are not nouns; for example, "[16] suggests parallelizing" should be "Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing."
*   I do not believe “Wang et al. (2015)” is the correct reference for IPA, as they do not introduce the method but use and apply it.
