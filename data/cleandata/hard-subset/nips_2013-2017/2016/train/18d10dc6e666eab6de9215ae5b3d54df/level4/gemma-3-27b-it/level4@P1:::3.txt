The paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), for optimizing expensive, black-box functions. The core idea is to extend the knowledge gradient (KG) framework – which aims to select the next point(s) to evaluate by maximizing the expected improvement in the objective value – to a parallel setting, where multiple points can be evaluated simultaneously. This involves finding a batch of points that jointly optimizes an acquisition function. Recognizing the computational intractability of directly maximizing this function, the authors develop an efficient strategy based on Monte Carlo sampling and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients and facilitate optimization. The algorithm is demonstrated to outperform or match the performance of existing batch Bayesian optimization methods on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It effectively summarizes related work, provides necessary background on Gaussian processes, and clearly explains the proposed method. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating the benefits of q-KG across a range of problems. This work is likely to be useful to practitioners interested in efficient optimization of complex systems.

While the approach builds upon existing work in Bayesian optimization and knowledge gradients, the extension to the parallel batch setting is a valuable contribution. The main novelty lies in the development of the efficient gradient estimation technique using IPA, which enables scalable optimization of the parallel KG acquisition function. However, the novelty is incremental rather than revolutionary, as it adapts established techniques to a new context. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Sound Technical Foundation:** The method is grounded in a solid decision-theoretic framework and leverages well-established techniques like Gaussian processes and IPA.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The algorithm consistently outperforms or matches state-of-the-art methods on a diverse set of problems, including both synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The parallel nature of the algorithm makes it particularly well-suited for applications where function evaluations are costly and can be parallelized, such as in machine learning.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the proposed method accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** The approach represents an extension of existing work rather than a fundamentally new paradigm. The core idea of extending KG to a batch setting is intuitive.
*   **Reliance on Established Techniques:** The IPA-based gradient estimation, while effectively applied, is a standard technique in the optimization literature.
