- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that jointly selects q points by maximizing expected reduction in the GP posterior mean minimum.
- q-KG is Bayes-optimal for the single remaining-batch setting and reduces to parallel EI in the noise-free constrained-return case.
- Develops an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and optimizes via stochastic gradient ascent.
- Uses a dynamic discretization of the domain for inner minimization based on posterior samples plus past points and candidates.
- Empirically outperforms or matches parallel EI variants and UCB-based batch policies on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning, with strong gains under noise.
- Code for the method is released.

- Strength: strong decision-theoretic grounding and clear explanation of the reduction to parallel EI.
- Strength: meaningful methodological contribution in enabling efficient joint optimization of batches (non-greedy).
- Strength: practical relevance for parallel resources and robust empirical performance in noisy settings.
- Strength: sound empirical comparisons to multiple baselines and real ML tuning tasks.

- Weakness: computational complexity not fully characterized—missing per-evaluation/gradient cost analysis w.r.t. Monte Carlo samples, O(q^3) Cholesky, discretization size M, and dimensionality d.
- Weakness: adaptive inner discretization depends on z(1:q); potential bias/variance and convergence/sensitivity to M are not analyzed.
- Weakness: “Bayes-optimal” claim only applies to one-step; repeated-myopic use needs clarification or theoretical guarantees (e.g., asymptotic consistency).
- Weakness: gradient estimator relies on differentiability and unique inner minima; regularity conditions and tie-handling are not emphasized.
- Weakness: assumes homoscedastic noise estimated by MLE; extensions to heteroscedastic noise and robustness to misspecification are not discussed.

- Clarity issue: A is overloaded (continuous feasible set vs discrete inner set); distinct symbols should be used early.
- Clarity issue: Algorithm 1 should state inner minimizations are over the discretized set and specify MC sample counts and gradient steps.
- Clarity issue: relationship to parallel entropy search is mentioned but not empirically evaluated; omission should be justified.

- Originality: extending KG to batch with an IPA-based stochastic gradient optimizer for continuous domains is novel and nontrivial compared to prior discrete/nested work.
- Significance: empirical improvements in noisy regimes are practically important, though experiments are limited to modest dimensions (up to 6–8).

- Suggestion: provide runtime comparisons per iteration and scaling with q (e.g., q=4,8,16), plus ablations on discretization size M and MC sample counts.
- Suggestion: report optimization details (step sizes, multi-starts, convergence criteria) and fraction of BO wall-time spent optimizing q-KG.
- Suggestion: include sensitivity analyses across kernels and noise levels, and show learned noise variance trajectories.
- Suggestion: add or discuss parallel ES / batch Thompson sampling baselines or explain computational barriers to inclusion.
- Suggestion: formally clarify the precise meaning and constraints of “Bayes-optimal” and the conditions under which parallel EI is recovered.

- Minor fixes: several typos and notation clarifications (define A earlier, fix “Main Stange,” capitalization consistency, clarify μ(n)(·) input, etc.).

- Pros for acceptance: principled extension of KG to batch setting with efficient optimization, strong noisy-regime results, and open-source code.
- Cons for acceptance: limited theoretical guarantees beyond one-step optimality, unclear scaling/high-d behavior, and unquantified approximation from inner discretization.

- Overall: well-executed and timely contribution with compelling empirical performance—acceptance recommended if computational scaling, discretization effects, and practical optimization details are strengthened.
