The paper proposes a new version of a stochastic gradient algorithm combined with Bayesian modeling, for optimizing a multivariate objective function over a finite domain. The novel part of the algorithm is that in every iteration it identifies a number of points on which to compute the objective and this computation can be parallelized. The problem is to identify the sampling points in some optimal sense. For this purpose the authors adopt the framework of Knowledge Gradient, which estimates the expected improvement in the objective value if one more sampling point is used and based on that make a decision on whether and which sampling point to use. This approach is generalized here so that the decision is made on multiple sampling points in batch. Since this problem is computationally intractable, the authors develop an algorithm based on Monte Carlo and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis to estimate the relevant gradients so as to maximize the information gain over the set of candidate sampling sets. 

The algorithm is applied on several test problems and is shown to either outperform or seriously compete with other approaches in the literature, mainly those based on Confidence Bounds. The paper is well written and potentially useful for practical applications. However, I have some reservations regarding the novelty of the approach. The authors expand a previously studied approach (KG) to a batch setting. This requires a more sophisticated method for optimizing the criterion over a set of multiple points. While the main contribution of the paper lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient based on an IPA approach, this method is also rather standard.

I have one specific comment/suggestion: In equation (3.1), since x^(1:n) is a finite sequence of vectors, it is not clear how mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) are defined, since mu and K are functions of (one or two) vectors and not sequences. The authors should clarify this point to improve the readability of the paper.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the proposed algorithm appears to be correct. However, I am not entirely convinced that the level of novelty is very high. The authors build upon existing work and generalize it to a batch setting. While the IPA approach for estimating the q-KG gradient is a significant contribution, it is not entirely new.

Clarity: The paper is well written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed algorithm. However, there are some minor points that need clarification, such as the definition of mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) in equation (3.1).

Originality: The paper builds upon existing work and generalizes it to a batch setting. While the authors make a significant contribution by developing an IPA approach for estimating the q-KG gradient, the overall novelty of the paper is not extremely high.

Significance: The paper has the potential to be useful for practical applications, especially in the context of Bayesian optimization. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm on several test problems, and the results are promising. However, the significance of the paper could be further enhanced by providing more insights into the theoretical properties of the algorithm and its limitations.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper is well written and easy to follow.
* The proposed algorithm has the potential to be useful for practical applications.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm on several test problems.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The level of novelty is not extremely high.
* The paper builds upon existing work and generalizes it to a batch setting.
* The IPA approach for estimating the q-KG gradient, while significant, is not entirely new.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should clarify the definition of mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) in equation (3.1) and provide more insights into the theoretical properties of the algorithm and its limitations.
