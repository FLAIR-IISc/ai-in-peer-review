This paper proposes a batch/parallel extension of the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization (BO). The authors define the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which, by construction, selects the Bayes-optimal batch of q points when a single batch remains. They provide an efficient Monte Carlo–based estimator of q-KG and, crucially, an IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator that allows joint optimization of the batch via gradient-based methods. Empirically, q-KG outperforms or matches state-of-the-art batch BO algorithms (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10), with especially strong gains under observation noise. Code is released.

Relation to prior work: The paper clearly builds on the classical KG literature for sequential BO and on recent work on parallel acquisition functions (e.g., q-EI, UCB variants, predictive entropy search). A few statements in the related work would benefit from tightening. The idea of integrating EI with respect to posterior distributions in a batch setting was already discussed in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization” (together with Constant Liar), and Chevalier et al. also introduced CL-mix [2]. In addition, q-EI maximization using natural gradient is studied in “Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design.” The present paper’s main novelty is the decision-theoretic derivation of a Bayes-optimal one-step batch rule and the development of an efficient gradient estimator via IPA for continuous domains.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic foundation: q-KG is Bayes-optimal (one-step) for minimizing the GP posterior predictor, and reduces to parallel EI in the noise-free, “recommend-from-evaluated” setting.
- Solid computational contribution: unbiased stochastic gradients via IPA enable joint batch optimization without greedy construction; the treatment of the Cholesky factor and Monte Carlo approximation is practical.
- Strong empirical results: consistent improvements over baselines, particularly with noise; inclusion of real ML tuning tasks; open-source code.
- Potential impact: Parallelizing BO to leverage modern compute is important in practice.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- No study of wall-clock speed-ups or throughput: Since the paper’s motivation is parallel evaluation, it would be valuable to quantify practical speed-ups vs. sequential KG/EI (e.g., time to reach a target regret) and discuss the overhead of optimizing q-KG as q grows.
- Literature precision: as noted above, please credit early batch-EI integration ideas (Ginsbourger et al.) and CL-mix; and acknowledge gradient-based q-EI optimization (“Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement...”). This helps position the contribution more crisply.
- Assumptions: Is A assumed compact and f continuous? These conditions often underlie existence/continuity arguments for min operators; please state assumptions explicitly.
- Initial design: Why restrict the initial design to a Latin hypercube? Could other space-filling or adaptive initializations change performance?
- Algorithm 1: Please clarify your hyperparameter strategy. You state re-training by MLE each iteration; do you also consider Bayesian hyperparameter averaging? How sensitive is q-KG to hyperparameter uncertainty?
- Section 5.2: In the definition of g, is it a max or a min? Please double-check the sign/convention (the text suggests a min).
- Smoothness of g: The interchange of derivative and expectation under IPA requires conditions (e.g., uniqueness of minimizers a.s., differentiability of µ and K); please specify precise regularity assumptions and point to proofs.
- Differentiating the Cholesky factor: The derivative of D(n)(z(1:q)) (the Cholesky factor) is non-trivial; please add a citation or brief derivation to justify the expression used.
- Kernel: Matérn is 5/2, not 2/5.
- Experimental comparability: For Figure 1 and related experiments, were the same GP model classes, kernels, and hyperparameter fitting procedures used across software packages? Differences in GP implementations can materially affect results; please clarify to ensure fair comparisons.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with a principled derivation and a reasonable stochastic gradient scheme. The empirical section is thorough. The theoretical assumptions behind unbiasedness and differentiability should be stated more explicitly.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. Small issues: fix the Matérn order; clarify min/max in g; expand the discussion of differentiability and Cholesky derivatives; and refine related-work attributions.

Originality
- Extends KG to the parallel setting with a decision-theoretic justification and contributes an efficient gradient estimator for joint batch selection. While parallel acquisition functions and gradient-based optimization of q-EI exist, the Bayes-optimal q-KG framing and IPA-based computation are novel and valuable.

Significance
- High practical relevance given the prevalence of parallel compute. Empirical gains, especially under noise, are compelling. With code released, adoption is likely.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, principled contribution; efficient implementation; excellent empirical performance; important practical impact; open-source code.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing wall-clock speed-up analysis; a few imprecisions in related work; technical assumptions and derivative details need clearer exposition.

Questions for the authors
- Are A compact and f continuous? What assumptions ensure existence/continuity of the inner minima and justify IPA?
- Why choose an LHS for the initial design specifically?
- Do you re-estimate GP hyperparameters by MLE at every iteration only, or consider Bayesian treatment?
- In Section 5.2, is g defined with a min or a max?
- Please justify the smoothness of g and the derivative of the Cholesky factor with references or derivations.
- Matérn: please confirm you used 5/2.
- In Figure 1 and other experiments, were identical GP kernels and hyperparameter strategies used across software implementations to ensure fair comparisons?

Overall, this is a strong paper that, with minor clarifications and literature corrections, would make a valuable contribution to NIPS.
