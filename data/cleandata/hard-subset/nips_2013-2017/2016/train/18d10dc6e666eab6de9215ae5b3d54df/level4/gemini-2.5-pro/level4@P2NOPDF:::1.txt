This paper introduces a batch-sequential version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization, termed q-KG. The authors first provide relevant background on Bayesian optimization, Gaussian processes, and related work. They then formally define the parallel q-KG criterion and detail its computation, which largely follows the methodology of the standard sequential KG criterion. The effectiveness of the proposed method is demonstrated through a series of numerical experiments where q-KG is shown to outperform several state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization algorithms on benchmark problems.

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG criterion is a natural and well-motivated extension of the KG principle to the batch setting. The derivations are clear, and the empirical results are compelling, suggesting that q-KG is a competitive, and often superior, method for parallel optimization. The problem of parallelizing Bayesian optimization is of significant practical importance, and this work represents a notable advancement. I believe the paper possesses the quality and potential impact that would justify its publication at NIPS.

Despite the overall high quality of the work, there are a few areas that could be improved:

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments effectively demonstrate the sample efficiency of q-KG compared to other batch methods, one aspect that is not explored is the computational speed-up. A discussion or analysis of the wall-clock time improvements gained by moving from a sequential KG approach to the batch-sequential q-KG would strengthen the paper's practical claims.

2.  **Literature Review Precision:** The literature review is generally thorough but could be sharpened with a few minor corrections for precision. For instance:
    *   The authors might consider acknowledging that the concept of integrating Expected Improvement (EI) over the posterior distribution of the maximum was previously explored in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" by Ginsbourger et al., which also introduced a variant of the Constant Liar heuristic.
    *   When discussing the work of Chevalier et al. [2], it would be more precise to mention their notable contribution, the CL-mix heuristic.
    *   The discussion of q-EI could also mention that its maximization via natural gradient methods was studied in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design" by Marmin et al.

I also have several specific questions and remarks for the authors:

*   **Assumptions:** Could the authors clarify the theoretical assumptions on the domain A (e.g., compactness) and the objective function f (e.g., continuity)?
*   **Initialization:** The paper initializes the design with a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS). Is there a specific reason for this choice over other space-filling designs, or is it primarily for consistency in the experiments?
*   **Algorithm 1:** The algorithm description does not explicitly mention the re-estimation of the Gaussian Process hyperparameters (e.g., length-scale, variance) at each iteration. Are the hyperparameters fixed after initialization, or are they updated (e.g., via marginal likelihood maximization) as new data is acquired? Clarifying this would be helpful.
*   **Section 5.2 (Function g):** Could the authors confirm if the definition of the function g should involve a 'max' or a 'min' operator? Please double-check the formulation.
*   **Section 5.2 (Smoothness):** The paper seems to assume the smoothness of g. Given that g is defined via a maximization operator, its smoothness is not immediately obvious. A brief justification or reference for this property would be beneficial.
*   **Cholesky Derivative:** The computation requires the derivative of a Cholesky factor. While this is a known result, it can be non-trivial. It might be helpful to include a reference or a brief note in an appendix for the sake of completeness.
*   **Figure 1 and Experiments:** Regarding the experiments shown in Figure 1, the results may be sensitive to the specific Gaussian Process implementation used. Did the authors consider or attempt to replicate the results for competing methods using a single, unified GP software framework to ensure the comparison is not confounded by implementation details?
*   **Typo:** There appears to be a minor typo regarding the Matérn kernel; it is likely Matérn-5/2 rather than Matérn-2/5. Please verify.

In summary, this is an excellent paper proposing a novel and effective method for parallel Bayesian optimization. Addressing the points above would further improve its clarity and impact. I recommend acceptance.
