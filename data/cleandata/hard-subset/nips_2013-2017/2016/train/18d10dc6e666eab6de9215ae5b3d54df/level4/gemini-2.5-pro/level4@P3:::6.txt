This paper proposes a novel method for parallel (batch) Bayesian Optimization called the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The method extends the decision-theoretic motivation of the sequential Knowledge Gradient to the batch setting, aiming to select a batch of points that is Bayes-optimal under a one-step lookahead model. To make the maximization of the q-KG acquisition function computationally feasible, the authors employ infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an efficient gradient estimator, enabling the use of gradient-based optimization. The experimental evaluation on synthetic functions and machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks shows that q-KG is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian Optimization methods in noise-free settings and demonstrates superior performance in the presence of observation noise.

This work addresses the highly relevant problem of accelerating expensive black-box optimization by leveraging parallel computation, which is a significant practical concern for the machine learning community. The core ideas are clearly presented, and the paper is well-structured. However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened to better demonstrate the method's practical utility, and the relationship to very similar prior work needs to be clarified.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled Approach:** The q-KG acquisition function is derived from a clear decision-theoretic principle: selecting the one-step Bayes-optimal batch of points to evaluate. This provides a strong theoretical motivation compared to more heuristic or greedy methods for batch construction.
2.  **Performance in Noisy Settings:** The empirical results strongly suggest that q-KG offers a significant advantage over other methods when function evaluations are noisy. This is an important and common scenario in practice (e.g., when evaluating models on a subset of data), and the paper makes a convincing case for its method's robustness in this setting.
3.  **Significance:** Methods for parallel hyperparameter tuning are of high practical importance across many disciplines that use machine learning. An effective, well-motivated batch Bayesian optimization algorithm would be a valuable contribution to the field.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the technical details of the proposed method are described clearly. The background on Gaussian Processes and the formulation of the q-KG factor are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

The primary weaknesses of the paper lie in the experimental evaluation and the positioning with respect to closely related work.

1.  **Relationship to Prior Work:** The paper cites "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions" by Wang et al. (2015), which also uses IPA to efficiently optimize a batch acquisition function (Expected Improvement, which could be called q-EI). Given the strong similarity in the optimization approach, this work feels like a more direct and crucial baseline than the methods currently included.
    *   Could you please clarify the precise contribution of your work over Wang et al. (2015)? Is the primary novelty the application of this IPA-based optimization technique to the Knowledge Gradient acquisition function instead of Expected Improvement?
    *   The paper compares against "parallel EI in MOE". Is this an implementation of the q-EI method from Wang et al. (2015)? If so, this should be stated explicitly to avoid confusion with heuristic parallel EI methods like the one in Spearmint. A direct and clear comparison highlighting the differences would greatly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scalability and Practicality:** The experiments are limited to a batch size of `q=4`. However, modern multi-core systems often allow for many more parallel evaluations. For instance, Snoek et al. (NIPS 2012) used up to 10 parallel evaluations, and other work on parallel algorithm configuration has explored even larger scales.
    *   How does the computational overhead of selecting a batch with q-KG scale as `q` increases?
    *   How does the optimization performance change with larger batch sizes (e.g., `q` = 8, 16, 32)? A study on scalability is essential for understanding the practical limits and utility of the proposed method.

3.  **Missing Baselines and Wall-Clock Time:** The ultimate goal of parallel optimization is to find better solutions faster. The current evaluation, which uses "iterations" (number of batches) on the x-axis, does not capture the computational overhead of the acquisition function.
    *   How does q-KG compare to simple, non-parallel sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? A parallel method is only useful if the speed-up from parallel evaluations outweighs the overhead of selecting the batch.
    *   A comparison against parallel random search would also be a valuable baseline to quantify the benefit of the intelligent batch selection strategy. Could you provide experiments showing performance versus wall-clock time?

4.  **Asynchronous Execution:** The proposed method appears to be synchronous, requiring all `q` function evaluations from a batch to complete before the next batch can be selected. In many real-world settings, evaluations may finish at different times.
    *   Have you considered how q-KG might be extended to an asynchronous setting, where new points are chosen as soon as computational resources become available?

5.  **Experimental Clarity:**
    *   What does "iterations" on the x-axis of the plots represent—the number of batches evaluated or the total number of function evaluations? This should be clarified.
    *   The x-axis appears to include the initial design. If all methods start with the same initial design, why do their performance curves not originate from the same point at the first iteration?

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper addresses an important practical problem with a well-motivated, decision-theoretic approach. The proposed q-KG method shows particularly strong performance on noisy problems, which is a significant advantage. The presentation is clear and the technical contribution of applying IPA to optimize q-KG is sound.
*   **Arguments against Acceptance:** The experimental evaluation is not fully convincing. It lacks crucial wall-clock time comparisons, a study on scalability with the batch size `q`, and a direct comparison to the most relevant prior work (q-EI from Wang et al., 2015). Without these elements, it is difficult to assess the true practical value and novelty of the contribution.

I believe this is a promising paper that could be a strong contribution to the conference. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the experimental comparison and scalability, would be necessary to make a compelling case for acceptance.
