This paper studies Bayesian optimization of a multivariate objective over a finite set with parallel (batch) evaluations. The authors extend the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework from single-point to batch selection, proposing a q-point KG acquisition and an accompanying stochastic gradient ascent scheme based on Monte Carlo estimation with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). At each iteration, the method identifies a set of q points for simultaneous evaluation, thereby leveraging parallel resources. Because the exact optimization of the batch KG criterion is intractable, the paper develops a Monte Carlo estimator of its gradient and uses it to navigate the search over candidate batches. Experiments on several benchmarks suggest the approach often outperforms, or is competitive with, confidence-bound–based baselines.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation and motivation for parallel evaluation in Bayesian optimization over a finite domain.
- Sound extension of the KG decision-theoretic framework to batch selection, with a principled objective (expected value of information) rather than heuristic parallelization.
- Use of Monte Carlo with IPA to obtain unbiased (or low-bias) gradient estimates for the batch KG objective is sensible and practically implementable.
- Empirical results indicate strong performance relative to widely used UCB-type methods, supporting the method’s practical utility.
- The paper is generally well written and organized.

Weaknesses and novelty:
- The conceptual novelty appears incremental. The work primarily generalizes a known acquisition function (KG) to the batch setting. While this is useful, batch extensions of acquisition functions (e.g., for EI/UCB) are by now common, and the need for more sophisticated optimization of a batch criterion is expected.
- The methodological novelty centers on the IPA-based gradient estimator for the q-KG objective. IPA is a standard tool in stochastic optimization; its application here is appropriate but not, in my view, a substantial theoretical advance.
- The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of computational complexity and scalability: per-iteration cost as a function of q, the domain size, the GP state (including matrix updates), and the number of Monte Carlo samples. This would help position the approach against competing batch BO methods in terms of wall-clock efficiency.

Clarity and technical comments:
- Notation in equation (3.1): x^(1:n) denotes a finite sequence of points, but μ(·) and K(·,·) are functions of single inputs. Please define μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n)) explicitly. For example, clarify that μ(x^(1:n)) is the vector [μ(x^(1)), …, μ(x^(n))] and K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n)) is the n-by-n matrix with entries K(x^(i), x^(j)). Making this explicit will prevent confusion about dimensions.
- It would help to state assumptions under which the IPA estimator is unbiased (or characterize any bias) and to provide guidance on variance control (e.g., use of common random numbers, antithetic variates), since optimization of a noisy acquisition can be sensitive to estimator variance.
- Please clarify how candidate batches are represented and optimized. For example, do you search in the continuous relaxation of indices with a subsequent projection to discrete points, or directly over discrete sets using stochastic search? How do you prevent duplicate points within a batch?
- Some practical details that would improve reproducibility and interpretation of results:
  - Initialization strategy and the number of initial evaluations.
  - The number of Monte Carlo samples used for gradient estimation and any schedules for adapting it.
  - Stopping criteria and step-size/tuning choices for the stochastic gradient ascent.
  - Hyperparameter learning for the GP model and whether hyperparameter uncertainty is integrated or point-estimated.
- Since the motivation is parallel resources, a wall-clock analysis (time to a given regret threshold) versus q and versus baselines would strengthen the empirical claims. Even a rough comparison would be valuable.

Overall assessment:
This is a solid and carefully executed extension of the Knowledge Gradient to batch selection, with a practical stochastic gradient optimization scheme based on IPA. The empirical results are promising and the paper is clearly written. However, the novelty is moderate: the primary contribution is adapting an existing acquisition to batch mode and applying a fairly standard gradient-estimation technique. I view the work as a useful addition for practitioners who favor value-of-information principles, but not a major conceptual advance.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clearer notation (especially around equation (3.1)), a more explicit discussion of computational complexity and estimator properties, and additional implementation details to aid reproducibility.
