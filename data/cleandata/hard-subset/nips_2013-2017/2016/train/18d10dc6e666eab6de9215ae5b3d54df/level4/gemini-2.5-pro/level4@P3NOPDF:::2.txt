This paper addresses the critical challenge of parallelizing Bayesian Optimization (BO), a popular technique for tasks like hyperparameter tuning that is often limited by its sequential nature. The authors propose a novel algorithm for selecting Bayes-optimal batches of points to evaluate in parallel. The core contribution is an elegant and principled approach to parallel BO that, according to the authors' experiments, outperforms existing methods. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow.

The paper's primary strength lies in its strong theoretical contribution to an important practical problem. As the authors correctly point out, making BO practical for real-world hyperparameter optimization requires effective parallelization. The proposed method is particularly compelling because it seeks to find a truly Bayes-optimal batch, a more principled solution than many existing ad-hoc heuristics for parallel BO.

A minor point of discussion concerns the assumption of independent, normally distributed measurement errors. While this is a standard assumption in Gaussian Process-based BO, its validity in hyperparameter optimization can be questionable. The "error" in this context often represents the difference between a model's true generalization performance and an empirical estimate (e.g., from cross-validation). These estimates can exhibit structural biases, particularly in certain regions of the hyperparameter space or when working with small datasets. A brief acknowledgment of this potential mismatch between the model's assumption and the practical reality of HPO would strengthen the paper.

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the experimental validation is unfortunately not comprehensive enough to be fully convincing. My main concerns are threefold:

1.  **Choice of Optimization Criterion:** The experiments use test set error as the primary metric for optimization. It is well-established in the literature [1] that threshold-based metrics like accuracy or error rate can be misleading and suboptimal for model selection and hyperparameter tuning. Using more informative metrics, such as log-loss or Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC), would provide a more robust and reliable evaluation of the optimizer's performance.

2.  **Benchmark Environment:** The experiments are conducted on two standard image classification datasets (MNIST and CIFAR-10). While these are common, the hyperparameter optimization community has developed specialized benchmark suites, such as HPOlib [2], specifically for the purpose of rigorously comparing HPO algorithms. Evaluating the proposed method within such a standardized framework would significantly increase the credibility and comparability of the results.

3.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is confined to only two datasets, a single machine learning task (classification), and two model architectures (Logistic Regression and a CNN). The performance of optimizers can vary significantly across different problem types, dataset characteristics, and model families. A more compelling benchmark would include a wider variety of tasks (e.g., regression), learning algorithms (e.g., Gradient Boosting Machines, SVMs), and datasets to demonstrate the general applicability and robustness of the proposed method.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and elegant theoretical contribution to the important field of parallel Bayesian Optimization. The proposed method for finding Bayes-optimal batches is well-motivated and promising. However, the empirical evaluation in its current form is too limited to fully substantiate the claims of superiority over other methods. While I am positive about the core idea, I would strongly encourage the authors to strengthen the experimental section by addressing the concerns regarding the optimization metric, benchmark suite, and the diversity of tasks and models.

[1] Provost, F. J., Fawcett, T., & Kohavi, R. (1998). The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms. In *ICML* (Vol. 98).
[2] Eggensperger, K., et al. (2013). Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters. In *NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice*.
