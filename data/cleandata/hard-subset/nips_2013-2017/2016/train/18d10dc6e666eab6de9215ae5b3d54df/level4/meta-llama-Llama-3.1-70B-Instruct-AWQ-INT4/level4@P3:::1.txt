This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, which is derived from a decision-theoretic analysis. The authors provide a thorough background on related work and Gaussian processes, and clearly define the q-KG criterion. The computation of q-KG is detailed, and the authors present several numerical experiments where q-KG appears to outperform some state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization algorithms.

The paper is well-written, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying concepts. The q-KG method is a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, and its potential impact on society is substantial. The authors' use of Gaussian processes as the statistical model is well-justified, and their choice of the ARD Matérn 5/2 kernel is appropriate.

The numerical experiments presented in the paper are thorough and well-designed. The authors test their algorithm on both synthetic functions and real-world problems, and compare its performance to several state-of-the-art algorithms. The results show that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms the other algorithms in most cases, especially in the noisy setting.

However, there are a few minor criticisms that can be made. Firstly, the authors do not study the speed-ups obtained by parallelizing the Bayesian optimization algorithm, which would be an interesting addition to the paper. Secondly, some statements in the literature review are slightly imprecise. For instance, the idea of integrating EI with respect to posterior distributions was already present in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (along with Constant Liar), and Chevalier et al. presented notably CL-mix in [2]. Additionally, q-EI maximization using natural gradient is studied in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design".

The authors could also clarify a few points in the paper. For instance, it is not clear whether A is compact and f is continuous. The restriction of A to be an LHS at the initial stage could also be justified. In Algorithm 1, the authors do not mention hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating. In Section 5.2, it is not clear whether the definition of g is a max or a min. The smoothness of g and the derivative of the Cholesky factor could also be clarified.

Finally, the authors could provide more details about the experiments presented in Figure 1. For instance, was it possible to fit the same GPs using different software? This would help to increase the reproducibility of the results.

In conclusion, this is a very good paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying concepts, and the q-KG method is a substantial improvement over existing algorithms. With a few minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, this paper would be a strong candidate for publication in NIPS.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.
* The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying concepts and provide a thorough background on related work and Gaussian processes.
* The numerical experiments are thorough and well-designed, and the results show that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms state-of-the-art algorithms in most cases.
* The potential impact of parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms on society is substantial.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper does not study the speed-ups obtained by parallelizing the Bayesian optimization algorithm.
* Some statements in the literature review are slightly imprecise.
* A few points in the paper could be clarified, such as the compactness and continuity of A and f, the restriction of A to be an LHS at the initial stage, and the smoothness of g and the derivative of the Cholesky factor.

Overall, I believe that the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
