

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which extends the sequential knowledge gradient approach to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the expected minimum of the Gaussian process predictor when only one batch of evaluations remains. The paper builds on previous work in Bayesian optimization, particularly the knowledge gradient method [7, 17] and recent parallel BO approaches [2, 3, 5, 18, 25]. Unlike prior batch methods that optimize points iteratively while holding others fixed, q-KG considers the batch collectively, providing a more theoretically grounded approach to parallel evaluation. The authors develop an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis to optimize the q-KG acquisition function, which addresses a key limitation of previous parallel methods that required Monte Carlo sampling for evaluation.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance, showing that q-KG consistently outperforms state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization algorithms on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems, particularly when function evaluations are noisy. The experimental design is thorough, with appropriate comparisons against parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration on multiple benchmark functions and real-world applications (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN tuning on CIFAR10).

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical foundation for the q-KG acquisition function. The authors provide a detailed computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis that efficiently handles the gradient computation required for optimization. The experimental results are well-designed, with appropriate statistical analysis (mean and standard deviation across multiple runs) and thorough comparison against relevant baselines. The paper acknowledges the computational complexity of the approach while demonstrating its practical effectiveness. The authors are appropriately careful in discussing the limitations of their method, particularly noting that q-KG is most advantageous in noisy settings.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical formulations are presented clearly, with sufficient detail to reproduce the results. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem and the contributions, and the experimental section provides comprehensive results. The algorithm description is precise, though some details about the discretization strategy (Section 5.3) could be elaborated for greater clarity. The figures effectively illustrate the comparative performance of the methods.

Originality: The parallel knowledge gradient method represents a significant extension of the sequential knowledge gradient approach to the batch setting, providing a theoretically grounded alternative to the iterative greedy approaches used in previous parallel BO methods. While previous work has explored parallel EI [2, 10, 18, 25] and parallel UCB [3, 5], the authors' decision-theoretic foundation for the batch acquisition function distinguishes their approach from the literature. The paper properly references related work, including Chevalier et al.'s work on differentiating the multipoint expected improvement [18] and the "Constant Liar" approach [10], though it could more explicitly acknowledge that the idea of integrating acquisition functions with respect to posterior distributions was present in Ginsbourger et al. [10] and Chevalier et al. [2].

Significance: The method has clear practical significance for applications where parallel function evaluations are possible, such as hyperparameter tuning of machine learning models on distributed computing environments. The paper demonstrates substantial improvements over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings where q-KG provides more substantial gains than in noise-free scenarios. The potential impact on reducing the computational cost of hyperparameter tuning for complex machine learning models is considerable, as it enables more efficient exploration of the hyperparameter space when parallel resources are available.

Pro: The paper introduces a theoretically grounded batch Bayesian optimization method that outperforms existing approaches, particularly in noisy settings. The computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis is novel and well-executed. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.

Con: The paper does not explicitly analyze the speed-up achieved through parallelization (beyond the number of function evaluations), which would be valuable for practitioners. Some technical details in the gradient computation (Section 5.2) could be more thoroughly justified, particularly regarding the smoothness assumptions. The initial Latin hypercube design (Algorithm 1) could be better motivated, as the authors do not explain why this specific initialization method is preferable to others.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical foundation is sound, the computational approach is efficient, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-suited for publication at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the minor points regarding technical justification and literature context.
