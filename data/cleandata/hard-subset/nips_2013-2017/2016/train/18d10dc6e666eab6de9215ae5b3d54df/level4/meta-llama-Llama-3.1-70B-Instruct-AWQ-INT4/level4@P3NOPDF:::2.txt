The paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, addressing a significant limitation of existing approaches that are inherently sequential. Bayesian optimization has become increasingly popular for machine learning tasks, particularly hyperparameter optimization, and the authors' contribution to this field is noteworthy.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience. The parallelization of Bayesian optimization is an important subject for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed approach is both interesting and more elegant than most existing methods. The determination of a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising and has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of hyperparameter optimization.

The authors assume independent normally distributed errors, which is a common assumption in most Bayesian optimization methods based on Gaussian processes. However, in the context of hyperparameter optimization, this assumption can be problematic. Measurement errors in hyperparameter optimization often represent the difference between generalization performance and empirical estimates, such as those obtained through cross-validation. Structural bias can be present in certain regions of the hyperparameter space, particularly when working with small sample sizes. It is worth noting that assuming independence is not always realistic and may lead to suboptimal results in certain scenarios.

The theoretical aspect of the paper is strong, and the authors provide a solid foundation for their proposed method. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat disappointing, and there are several areas that could be improved. Firstly, the use of test set error as an optimization criterion is problematic, as it is well-known that such score functions are suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning. Threshold-based metrics like test set error can introduce a number of problems for model selection and hyperparameter optimization, and it would be more informative to use metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss.

Secondly, while the authors use the MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets as the basis for their experiments, which are commonly used in benchmarks, it would be more valuable to see benchmarks using the HPOlib library. HPOlib is a widely-used library specifically designed to benchmark hyperparameter optimization algorithms, and it would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

Thirdly, the experimental evaluation is limited to only two datasets and a single machine learning task (classification) with only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). It would be beneficial to see a more varied benchmark, as it is known that some optimizers perform well for certain types of learning algorithms but poorly for others.

In conclusion, while the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, the experimental evaluation is too limited to be truly convincing. With some additional experiments and a more comprehensive evaluation, the authors could further demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed method and make a stronger case for its adoption in practice.
