Bayesian optimization has become increasingly popular for machine learning tasks, particularly hyperparameter optimization, but many existing approaches are inherently sequential. This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches of points to evaluate. The authors demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms existing batch Bayesian optimization algorithms on both synthetic test functions and when tuning hyperparameters of practical machine learning algorithms, especially in noisy evaluation scenarios. The paper is well-written and clearly presented. Parallelizing Bayesian optimization is a crucial area for practical hyperparameter tuning, and the proposed approach is both interesting and more theoretically grounded than many existing methods. The derivation of a Bayes-optimal batch selection strategy is particularly promising. The authors appropriately assume independent, normally distributed errors, a common simplification in Gaussian process-based Bayesian optimization. However, it’s worth noting that this assumption can be problematic in hyperparameter optimization, where evaluation noise often stems from the variance between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation). Structural biases can be present in certain regions of hyperparameter space, particularly with limited data. Acknowledging this limitation would strengthen the discussion. The theoretical contribution of this work is strong; however, the empirical evaluation has some limitations. Firstly, using test set error as the optimization criterion is a known issue, as such metrics can be suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Test set error introduces challenges for model selection and optimization, and alternative metrics like area under the ROC curve or log-loss might be more appropriate. Secondly, while MNIST and CIFAR10 are commonly used benchmarks, evaluating against a more comprehensive suite of problems, such as those available in HPOlib [2], would provide a more robust assessment. Finally, the benchmark relies on a limited number of datasets (two) and focuses solely on classification tasks with logistic regression and CNNs. A more diverse benchmark, reflecting the varying performance characteristics of different optimizers across different learning algorithms and problem types, would be beneficial. Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable theoretical contribution. **Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian optimization, deriving a Bayes-optimal batch selection strategy.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand, even for those not deeply familiar with the field.
*   **Promising Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing algorithms on the tested problems, particularly in noisy scenarios.
*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** Parallelizing Bayesian optimization is crucial for real-world applications, and this paper offers a significant step forward.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The benchmark is relatively small and lacks diversity in terms of datasets and learning algorithms.
*   **Suboptimal Evaluation Metric:** Using test set error as the optimization criterion can be misleading and may not accurately reflect the algorithm's performance.
*   **Simplifying Assumption:** The assumption of independent Gaussian errors may not always hold in practical hyperparameter optimization scenarios.
