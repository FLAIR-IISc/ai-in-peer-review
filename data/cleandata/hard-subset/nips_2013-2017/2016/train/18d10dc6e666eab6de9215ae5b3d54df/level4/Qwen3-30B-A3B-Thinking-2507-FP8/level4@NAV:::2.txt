

Bayesian optimization has gained significant traction for machine learning tasks such as hyperparameter optimization, yet most existing methods remain inherently sequential. This paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, demonstrating superior performance over current approaches. The manuscript is clearly written and accessible. Parallelization of BO is a critical advancement for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed method presents a compelling and elegant alternative to existing techniques. The derivation of a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising. While the authors assume independent normally distributed errors—a standard simplification in Gaussian process-based BO—their assumption is questionable for hyperparameter optimization, where measurement errors reflect discrepancies between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation). Structural bias often arises in hyperparameter space, especially under small sample sizes, warranting explicit acknowledgment that independence is not universally realistic. The theoretical foundation is robust, but the experimental evaluation is underwhelming for three key reasons:  
1. Optimization relies on test set error, a suboptimal criterion for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Metrics such as area under the ROC curve or log loss would be more appropriate, as test set error introduces well-documented issues for model selection.  
2. The benchmarks use MNIST and CIFAR10, common but narrow choices. Inclusion of HPOlib [2], a widely adopted library for hyperparameter optimization benchmarking, would have strengthened the evaluation.  
3. The benchmark employs only two datasets and focuses exclusively on classification with two algorithms (logistic regression and CNN), lacking diversity. Given that optimizer performance varies significantly across learning algorithms and tasks, a broader benchmark is essential for convincing validation.  
Overall, the theoretical contribution is valuable, but the limited experimental scope undermines the paper’s practical impact.
