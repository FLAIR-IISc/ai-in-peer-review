- Summary: Proposes a principled parallel BO algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, with solid theory and empirical gains over several baselines.

- Strength: Clear, well-written, theoretically grounded, and conceptually appealing approach to batch selection that addresses practical parallelism in HPO.

- Concern 1 (Noise model): Assumes independent Gaussian observation noise, which may be violated in HPO (heteroscedasticity, correlations from shared CV folds, structural biases); robustness and extensions (heteroscedastic GPs, Student-t, modeling shared splits) should be discussed and tested.

- Concern 2 (Metric/protocol): Optimizing test error is inappropriate—use validation or cross-validated proper scoring rules (e.g., NLL, AUC), hold out test sets, and clarify data splits, CV/repeats, and early stopping to avoid optimistic bias.

- Concern 3 (Experimental breadth): Experiments are narrow (MNIST, CIFAR-10; logistic regression, CNNs); need broader tasks, models, search spaces, and standardized benchmarks (e.g., HPOlib) to demonstrate external validity.

- Concern 4 (Baselines & ablations): Compare to strong batch baselines (q-EI, local penalization, Thompson batching, KG variants), provide ablations (batch size, wall-clock speedups, dimensionality, noise model), and address asynchronous evaluation/stragglers.

- Concern 5 (Reproducibility): Provide full reproducibility details (search spaces, priors, kernels, acquisition settings, seeds, budgets) and release code or pseudocode.

- Minor: State computational complexity per batch selection and approximations used; explicitly state any utility assumptions and their limitations.

- Overall: Strong and elegant theoretical contribution with promising empirical results, but insufficient empirical validation and problematic evaluation protocol—would favor acceptance after expanded experiments and stricter evaluation.
