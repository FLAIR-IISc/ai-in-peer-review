

This paper introduces a novel parallel Bayesian optimization method called the "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that selects batches of points for simultaneous evaluation, addressing an important practical challenge in black-box optimization where function evaluations can be performed in parallel. The authors extend the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function from sequential to batch settings, providing a theoretically grounded method that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor when only one batch remains. The key innovation lies in their efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to evaluate the q-KG acquisition function and its gradient, which enables practical implementation of this theoretically optimal batch selection approach.

The paper builds upon prior work in Bayesian optimization (BO), particularly the knowledge gradient method [7, 17] and existing parallel BO approaches [3, 5, 18, 25]. While previous parallel methods (e.g., parallel EI, batch UCB) construct batches iteratively by optimizing single-point acquisition functions while holding other points fixed, the authors' approach jointly optimizes the batch, providing a more theoretically sound solution. The paper effectively compares with relevant prior work, including parallel EI [2, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and parallel UCB with exploration [3], demonstrating clear advantages of q-KG in both synthetic and practical settings.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. Theoretical soundness: The paper provides a clear decision-theoretic foundation for q-KG, establishing its Bayes-optimality in the parallel setting.
2. Computational efficiency: The IPA-based approach for evaluating q-KG and its gradient is well-motivated and appears practical, with the authors demonstrating significant speedups compared to naive approaches.
3. Comprehensive empirical validation: The experiments cover both synthetic test functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical ML hyperparameter tuning problems (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), showing consistent improvements over benchmarks, especially in noisy settings.
4. Well-structured presentation: The paper is clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement through method development to experimental validation.

The paper has several minor weaknesses that could be addressed:
1. The Gaussian process assumption is not thoroughly justified for high-dimensional problems like neural network hyperparameter tuning, where the input dimension can be large. The authors should discuss how the GP assumption scales with dimension and whether they have tested the method on higher-dimensional problems.
2. The theoretical analysis is somewhat limited; the authors do not provide error bounds for their method or a rigorous complexity analysis with respect to input dimension. A brief discussion of how q-KG scales with dimension would strengthen the paper.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with parallel EI, particularly explaining why q-KG outperforms it (as the authors note, q-KG reduces to parallel EI under specific conditions).
4. Notational inconsistencies exist (e.g., multiple uses of "A" without clear distinction), which could be clarified with better notation.

The paper is well-written overall, with only minor issues like the inconsistent use of "A" (l.95, l.140) and unclear definition of input to μⁿ (l.104). The authors should also clarify the relationship between q-KG and parallel EI in Section 4, as suggested by the reviewer.

The significance of this work is substantial. As machine learning applications increasingly require hyperparameter tuning for complex models (e.g., deep neural networks), the ability to parallelize this process becomes critical for efficiency. The paper demonstrates that q-KG consistently finds better solutions than existing batch BO methods, with especially pronounced benefits in noisy evaluation settings. This has direct implications for practitioners seeking to optimize expensive machine learning pipelines.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims backed by comprehensive experiments. The method is complete and the authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses. The clarity is good, though some notational improvements would enhance readability. The originality is strong, as the paper extends the knowledge gradient method to the parallel setting in a theoretically grounded way, distinct from existing batch BO approaches.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important practical problem in Bayesian optimization
- Provides a theoretically sound batch acquisition function (q-KG)
- Demonstrates clear empirical improvements over existing methods
- Offers an efficient computational approach via IPA
- Well-executed experiments on both synthetic and real-world problems

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical analysis of error bounds and dimensionality scaling
- Insufficient discussion of GP assumptions for high-dimensional problems
- Minor notational inconsistencies

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, with clear practical value for practitioners. The paper makes a significant advance in batch Bayesian optimization, providing a theoretically grounded and empirically superior method for parallel function evaluation. While some theoretical analysis could be strengthened, the paper's empirical results and computational approach are compelling. I recommend acceptance as a solid contribution to NIPS.
