## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title]

This paper presents a novel stochastic gradient optimization algorithm combining Bayesian modeling with a batch Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework for optimizing multivariate objective functions over finite domains. The core idea is to intelligently select a batch of points at each iteration for objective function evaluation, leveraging the KG framework to estimate the expected improvement in the objective value gained by these evaluations. This batch selection process is designed for parallelization, potentially offering significant computational benefits.

The authors address the intractability of optimal batch selection via a Monte Carlo approach coupled with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate the necessary gradients for maximizing information gain. This allows them to navigate the combinatorial space of potential sampling sets and prioritize those expected to yield the greatest reduction in uncertainty regarding the optimal solution. Experimental results on several test problems demonstrate that the proposed algorithm either outperforms or achieves competitive performance compared to existing methods, particularly those based on Confidence Bounds.

Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly presents a technically sound approach with potential for practical application. The motivation for efficiently exploring finite domains, especially when objective function evaluations are expensive, is well-established. The proposed algorithm builds upon a solid foundation – the Knowledge Gradient framework – and extends it to a batch setting, a natural progression given the benefits of parallel computation. 

However, the level of novelty, while present, feels incremental. The extension of KG to a batch setting necessitates a more complex optimization strategy for the selection criterion, and the authors appropriately address this challenge.  In my assessment, the primary contribution lies in the specific implementation of the q-KG gradient estimation using IPA. While the application of IPA is effective, it represents a relatively standard technique within the broader field of stochastic optimization and Bayesian optimization.  Therefore, while technically proficient, the paper’s innovative leap isn't substantial.

I do have one specific comment regarding the notation. In equation (3.1), the definitions of  μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n),x^(1:n)) are unclear. Since x^(1:n) represents a finite sequence of vectors, it is not immediately apparent how these functions, which are defined for single vectors or vector pairs, are applied to a sequence. Clarifying this point—perhaps by specifying an aggregation function or a vectorized operation—would improve the paper's readability and rigor.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and well-structured presentation.
*   Technically sound approach building upon a recognized framework (KG).
*   Demonstrated empirical performance competitive with existing methods.
*   Potential for parallelization, enhancing practical applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental novelty. The core idea of batch KG is a natural extension, and the IPA-based gradient estimation, while well-executed, is a standard technique.
*   Notational ambiguity in equation (3.1) regarding the handling of vector sequences.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper, but with a leaning towards a weaker acceptance (e.g., oral presentation not prioritized). The work is solid, well-executed, and offers a potentially useful extension to the KG framework. However, the incremental nature of the novelty should be considered. Addressing the notational issue in equation (3.1) is crucial for clarity.
