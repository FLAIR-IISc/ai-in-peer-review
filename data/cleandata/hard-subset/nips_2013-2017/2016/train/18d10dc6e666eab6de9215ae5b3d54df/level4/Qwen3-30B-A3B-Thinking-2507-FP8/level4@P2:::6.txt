

The paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization method, q-KG, which computes a Bayes-optimal batch of points to evaluate by extending the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors derive a computationally efficient strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize q-KG, demonstrating superior performance over existing parallel BO methods (e.g., parallel EI, GP-UCB) on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly under noisy evaluations. This work builds on NIPS 2012–2015 BO literature, including Snoek et al. (2012) on Bayesian hyperparameter optimization and Wang et al. (2015) on parallel EI, but advances by jointly optimizing the batch via decision-theoretic principles rather than iterative greedy selection.

**Strengths**  
The paper is technically sound and well-motivated. The q-KG acquisition function is rigorously derived from decision theory, providing a theoretically grounded alternative to heuristic batch selection. The IPA-based gradient estimation efficiently addresses computational challenges in maximizing q-KG, avoiding the need for Monte Carlo approximations used in prior work (e.g., Shah & Ghahramani, 2015). The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering diverse synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical ML tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10). Results consistently show q-KG outperforms benchmarks in noisy settings—a critical advantage for real-world applications where evaluations are often noisy. The clarity of exposition is excellent, with well-structured background, precise notation, and accessible algorithm description. Code availability further enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**  
The experimental validation has notable gaps. While the paper compares q-KG to state-of-the-art parallel methods, it omits critical baselines:  
1. **Wang et al. (2015)** (the MOE implementation of parallel EI) is not directly compared, despite being the canonical parallel EI method. The draft review correctly notes this omission; the paper should clarify whether their "parallel EI" benchmark is Spearmint’s heuristic or MOE’s IPA-based method, and explicitly contrast them.  
2. **Wall-clock time analysis** is missing. For parallel optimization to be practically useful, the paper should quantify speedup over sequential BO and random search (e.g., time to reach target accuracy with q=4 vs. q=1). Without this, the claimed "efficiency gains" remain theoretical.  
3. **Scalability to large q** is unaddressed. The paper uses q=4 consistently, but modern systems support >100 parallel evaluations (e.g., ICML 2015: 800 DNGO runs). The authors should discuss computational complexity scaling with q and provide insights into optimal batch sizes for large-scale deployments.  
4. **Asynchronous execution** (e.g., like Spearmint) is not considered, though it is crucial for distributed systems with variable evaluation times.  

The significance is strong for hyperparameter tuning in ML, but the paper underemphasizes *why* q-KG’s noise robustness matters beyond synthetic tests. For instance, it should explicitly link noisy evaluation performance to real-world constraints (e.g., limited GPU hours for noisy neural network training).

**Recommendation**  
*Pro acceptance:*  
- Novel integration of KG and IPA for *joint* batch optimization (vs. iterative methods).  
- Demonstrated superiority in noisy settings—a gap in prior parallel BO work.  
- High clarity, reproducibility, and relevance to ML practitioners.  

*Con acceptance:*  
- Critical omission of Wang et al. (2015) comparison.  
- Lack of wall-clock time analysis for practical adoption.  
- Insufficient discussion of scalability to large q.  

**Final Assessment**  
This paper makes a solid contribution to parallel Bayesian optimization, advancing the state-of-the-art with a theoretically grounded, efficient method that excels in noisy environments. However, the experimental section requires strengthening to address scalability, practical runtime, and direct comparison to the MOE parallel EI baseline. With these revisions, the work would be highly impactful. As currently presented, it is *borderline acceptable* but merits *weak acceptance* pending minor clarifications. The novelty is clear (q-KG extends KG to parallel decision theory), the quality is high, and significance is strong for ML hyperparameter tuning. The main gap lies in experimental rigor for real-world deployment scenarios.
