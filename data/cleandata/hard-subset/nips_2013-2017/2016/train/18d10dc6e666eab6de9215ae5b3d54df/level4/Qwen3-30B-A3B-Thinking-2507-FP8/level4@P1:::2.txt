

The paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which computes Bayes-optimal batches of points for parallel evaluation. The method generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient to the parallel setting, providing a decision-theoretic acquisition function that jointly optimizes q points rather than iteratively selecting them. The authors derive an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis to maximize q-KG, and demonstrate superior performance over existing parallel methods (e.g., parallel EI, batch UCB) on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly under noisy evaluations. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the implementation is well-documented with code released publicly.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by formalizing a Bayes-optimal batch acquisition function for parallel Bayesian optimization—a significant advancement over greedy iterative methods. The computational approach via infinitesimal perturbation analysis is elegant and efficiently addresses the scalability challenge of maximizing q-KG. The empirical results are compelling: q-KG consistently outperforms benchmarks on synthetic functions (Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), with notable gains in noisy settings. The clarity of exposition is excellent, with well-structured sections that guide the reader through the technical development. The code availability further enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**  
While the theoretical contribution is robust, the experimental evaluation has critical limitations. First, the use of test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic: as established in the literature (Provost et al., ICML 1998), accuracy-based metrics are suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning due to high variance and bias, especially with small test sets. Metrics like log loss or AUC would provide more reliable optimization signals. Second, the benchmark lacks diversity: experiments are limited to two classification tasks (logistic regression and CNN) on MNIST/CIFAR10, omitting standard HPOlib benchmarks (Eggensperger et al., NIPS Workshop 2013), which are designed to rigorously evaluate hyperparameter optimization algorithms across varied tasks and model types. Third, the scope is narrow—only two datasets and two learning algorithms are tested, whereas prior work demonstrates that optimizer performance varies significantly across tasks (e.g., tree-based models vs. neural networks). This limits the generalizability of the claims.

**Additional Notes:**  
The assumption of independent normally distributed noise is standard in GP-based BO but merits discussion in the context of hyperparameter optimization. Here, measurement errors reflect the discrepancy between empirical and generalization performance (e.g., via cross-validation), which often exhibits structural bias—particularly in high-dimensional hyperparameter spaces with small samples. While the paper acknowledges this assumption, it does not address whether it impacts the practical utility of q-KG in real-world settings.

**Recommendation:**  
The theoretical novelty and computational efficiency of q-KG are strong merits. However, the experimental evaluation fails to sufficiently validate the method’s practical utility due to the limited benchmark scope and suboptimal optimization criterion. To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) replace test set error with more robust metrics (e.g., log loss), (2) incorporate HPOlib benchmarks across diverse tasks, and (3) expand the empirical validation to include non-classification tasks (e.g., regression, reinforcement learning). With these revisions, the paper would make a significant contribution to the field. As presented, the results are suggestive but not fully convincing for NIPS acceptance.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Theoretical novelty (Bayes-optimal batch), efficient computation, clear writing, strong synthetic results.  
*Con:* Limited benchmark scope, suboptimal optimization metric, insufficient validation across tasks.
