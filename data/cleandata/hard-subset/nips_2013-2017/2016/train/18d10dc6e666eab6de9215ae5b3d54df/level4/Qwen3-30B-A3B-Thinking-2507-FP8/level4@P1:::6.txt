

This paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization method—parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG)—that selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously by maximizing a decision-theoretic acquisition function. The authors derive q-KG as a Bayes-optimal batch acquisition function for minimizing the expected minimum of the Gaussian process predictor, and develop an efficient gradient-based optimization approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute its gradient. Experiments demonstrate that q-KG outperforms state-of-the-art parallel methods (e.g., parallel EI, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, with particularly strong gains under noisy evaluations. The technical presentation is clear, and the problem is well-motivated for real-world applications like hyperparameter optimization in machine learning.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The method is technically sound, with a solid theoretical foundation rooted in decision theory. The IPA-based gradient estimation is well-justified and addresses a key computational bottleneck. The experimental design is thorough, covering both noise-free and noisy synthetic benchmarks alongside practical ML tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show q-KG’s superiority in noisy settings, which aligns with the paper’s theoretical focus on noisy evaluations.  
*Clarity*: The paper is well-organized, with clear background, precise definitions (e.g., q-KG in Section 4), and effective use of figures to illustrate results. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) and computational details (Section 5) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.  
*Originality*: While IPA and knowledge gradient have been used separately, the novel combination for *batch* optimization—especially the decision-theoretic derivation of q-KG—provides a meaningful advance over iterative batch methods (e.g., parallel EI via constant liar). The comparison to prior work (Section 2) adequately situates the contribution.  
*Significance*: The method directly addresses a practical bottleneck in hyperparameter tuning (parallel evaluation), with demonstrated gains in real-world ML applications. The focus on noisy evaluations is particularly relevant, as most real-world optimization tasks involve measurement noise.

**Weaknesses**  
*Quality*: The experimental evaluation lacks critical comparisons to wall-clock time and scalability. The authors report function evaluations but do not quantify how much time q-KG saves versus sequential Bayesian optimization or random search—especially given the overhead of gradient computation. For instance, how does the 4x parallel speedup of q-KG (with q=4) compare to the time cost of computing the batch?  
*Originality*: The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its method from Wang et al. (2015), which also uses IPA for batch selection (though for expected improvement). If q-KG is an extension of Wang et al.’s approach, this should be explicitly clarified (e.g., "We extend Wang et al.’s IPA framework to knowledge gradient"). The reference to "parallel EI in MOE" should be labeled as *q-EI* to avoid confusion with Spearmint’s heuristic parallel EI.  
*Significance*: The choice of q=4 across all experiments is unexplained. Given modern multi-core systems (e.g., 800 cores in ICML’15, 25 in LION’12), the paper should address scalability: *How does q-KG perform with q>4, and what is the diminishing return of larger batches?*  
*Clarity*: The x-axis in Figures 3–4 is ambiguous ("iterations" could mean batches or evaluations). The initial design is included in the x-axis, but methods start at different points (e.g., Branin2 in Figure 3a). This should be clarified.

**Key Questions for Authors**  
1. Wang et al. (2015) introduced IPA for batch Bayesian optimization (specifically for expected improvement). How does your q-KG method differ from theirs, and why is it not covered in your related work? If q-KG builds on Wang et al., please clarify the extension in Section 2.  
2. The paper uses q=4 uniformly, but modern hardware supports larger parallelism (e.g., 8–1000 cores). What scalability insights do you have for q>4? How does the *speedup* (vs. sequential BO) change with q?  
3. Your method assumes synchronous evaluation. Could it be adapted to asynchronous settings (like Spearmint), and how might this affect performance?  
4. Wall-clock time is critical for real-world adoption. How does q-KG compare to sequential BO or random search in terms of *total runtime* (including batch computation overhead)?  
5. In Section 6.1.2, the x-axis label "iterations" is unclear. Does it count batches or function evaluations? If batches, does the initial design count toward the total?

**Minor Corrections**  
- "achieve" → "achieves" (Section 6.1.2: "has been carefully tuned... and achieves").  
- Cite Chevalier et al. (2014) as the IPA reference, not Wang et al. (2015).  
- Equation (4.1) requires a missing \( z^{(1:n)} \) (see "Is there a \( z^{(1:n)} \) missing in Eq (4.1)?").  

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper makes a strong technical contribution to batch Bayesian optimization with a well-motivated method and convincing results in noisy settings. However, the lack of wall-clock time comparisons, unexplained q=4 choice, and insufficient differentiation from Wang et al. (2015) prevent full confidence in its practical impact. Addressing the questions above would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend *acceptance* with minor revisions.
