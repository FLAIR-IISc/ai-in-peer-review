This paper introduces a batch-sequential version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) criterion, providing a novel approach to Bayesian optimization. The authors present a thorough background on related work and Gaussian processes, followed by the definition of the parallel (q-KG) criterion. The computation of q-KG is detailed, mirroring the standard KG criterion, and numerical experiments demonstrate its superior performance compared to state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization algorithms.

This is a well-written and impactful paper, possessing qualities that justify its publication in NIPS. The potential benefits of parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms are significant, and this work makes a valuable contribution to the field. However, I have a few minor criticisms and suggestions for improvement.

Firstly, the paper does not investigate the speed-ups achieved by transitioning from sequential to batch-sequential optimization, which would provide additional insight into the efficiency of the proposed method. Furthermore, some statements in the literature review require clarification. For instance, the idea of integrating Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was previously explored in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" and "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design." Additionally, Chevalier et al. introduced the CL-mix method in [2].

I also have several remarks and questions regarding the paper:

1. Is the function f assumed to be compact and continuous over the set A?
2. Why is the set A restricted to be a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) at the initial stage?
3. In Algorithm 1, is hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating performed?
4. In Section 5.2, is the definition of g a maximization or minimization problem?
5. The smoothness of g is assumed, but this may not be immediately apparent. Similarly, the derivative of the Cholesky factor may not be obvious.
6. The Matérn kernel is typically denoted as 5/2, not 2/5.
7. Regarding Figure 1 and the corresponding experiments, was it possible to fit the same Gaussian processes using different software packages?

Addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and provide clarity for the readers.
