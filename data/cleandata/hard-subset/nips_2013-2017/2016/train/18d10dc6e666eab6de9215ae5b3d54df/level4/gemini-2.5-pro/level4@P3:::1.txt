This paper addresses the problem of batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), where multiple function evaluations can be performed in parallel at each iteration. The authors introduce a novel acquisition function, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which extends the sequential Knowledge Gradient criterion to the batch setting. The q-KG criterion is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, selecting the batch of points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality after one more step. To overcome the computational challenge of maximizing this acquisition function, the paper proposes an efficient gradient estimation technique based on Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), enabling the use of gradient-based optimizers. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through a comprehensive set of experiments on synthetic benchmarks and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, showing that q-KG often outperforms state-of-the-art batch BO algorithms, particularly in noisy environments.

### Strengths

*   **Significance:** The problem of parallelizing BO is of paramount importance for the machine learning community, as it directly enables more efficient hyperparameter tuning and experimental design in modern parallel computing environments. This work provides a principled and effective solution to this problem, which is likely to be impactful.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The paper's main contribution, the q-KG acquisition function, is a novel and well-motivated generalization of a strong sequential BO criterion. The decision-theoretic foundation is elegant and provides a clear justification for the method. The use of IPA to derive an unbiased gradient estimator for the Monte Carlo-based acquisition function is a key technical contribution that makes the approach computationally feasible.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare q-KG against several relevant and strong baselines (including different implementations of parallel EI and parallel UCB) across a variety of synthetic and real-world problems. The consistent and sometimes significant performance gains, especially in the noisy setting, strongly support the paper's claims and demonstrate a clear advance over the state-of-the-art. The inclusion of code is a commendable step towards reproducibility.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a very strong paper, there are several areas where clarity could be improved and minor technical details could be addressed.

1.  **Clarity on Technical Assumptions:** The paper would benefit from explicitly stating the assumptions on the domain `A` (e.g., compactness) and the objective function `f` (e.g., continuity) required for the theoretical development. Furthermore, the use of IPA to interchange the derivative and expectation in Equation (5.3) relies on the smoothness of the function `g`. The differentiability of `g` with respect to `z`, particularly due to the `argmin` operation and the Cholesky factor `D(n)`, is not self-evident. A brief justification or citation supporting these steps, perhaps in the supplementary material, would strengthen the paper's technical rigor.

2.  **Relation to Prior Work:** The literature review is generally good, but could be slightly more precise in a few places.
    *   The idea of maximizing Expected Improvement (EI) by integrating over the posterior distribution of previously selected points in the batch was introduced in Ginsbourger et al. ("Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization", 2010), which also proposed the "Constant Liar" heuristic.
    *   The gradient-based optimization of a multi-point EI acquisition function has also been explored, for instance, in Marmin et al. ("Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design", 2015). Acknowledging these related efforts would provide a more complete context for the paper's contributions.

3.  **Details for Reproducibility:**
    *   **Algorithm 1:** The algorithm overview mentions re-training the GP hyperparameters at each iteration (line 5). Is this done after every batch evaluation? Clarifying the frequency and method (e.g., maximizing the marginal log-likelihood) would be helpful.
    *   **Experimental Comparison:** The experiments use open-source implementations for benchmark methods. For a fair comparison, it is crucial that the underlying GP models (e.g., hyperparameter optimization, choice of priors) are configured as similarly as possible across all methods. A brief comment confirming this would strengthen the validity of the experimental results.

4.  **Minor Clarifications and Typos:**
    *   In Section 5.2, the definition of `g` involves `min x∈A ...` and `min x∈A ...`. The text should likely read `g = min x∈A µ(n)(x) - min x∈A ( µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z(1:q))Zq )`, implying a subtraction, not a maximization. Please clarify.
    *   In the description of the experimental setup (Section 6), the kernel is referred to as "ARD Matérn 2/5", which should almost certainly be "ARD Matérn 5/2".
    *   The initial design stage uses a Latin Hypercube Design. While this is a standard choice, a brief justification for why this is preferred over, for example, a random design, could be useful.

### Summary

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, principled, and highly effective method for batch Bayesian Optimization. The strengths in originality, significance, and empirical validation are substantial. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to areas where additional clarification would improve the paper's technical precision and reproducibility. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly relevant and important problem in machine learning.
*   Proposes a novel and theoretically sound acquisition function (q-KG).
*   Provides a key technical contribution for optimizing the acquisition function efficiently.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance through extensive and convincing experiments.
*   The work is well-presented and provides code for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Minor lack of precision in the discussion of related work.
*   Some technical assumptions underlying the gradient computation could be more formally justified.
