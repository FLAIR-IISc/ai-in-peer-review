The paper proposes a new variant of a stochastic gradient algorithm combined with Bayesian modeling to optimize a multivariate objective over a finite domain. The novel aspect is that, at each iteration, it identifies multiple points at which to evaluate the objective, enabling parallel computation. The central challenge is to select these sampling points in an optimal manner. To address this, the authors adopt the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, which estimates the expected improvement from adding one more sampling point and, based on that, decides whether to sample and which point to select. They generalize this approach to a batch setting, making decisions over multiple sampling points simultaneously. Because this optimization is computationally intractable, they develop an algorithm that uses Monte Carlo and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to estimate the relevant gradients and thereby maximize information gain over candidate sampling sets.

The algorithm is tested on several problems and is shown to either outperform or be strongly competitive with other approaches in the literature, particularly those based on confidence bounds. The paper is well written and appears useful for practical applications.

That said, I am not fully convinced about the level of novelty: the work primarily extends a previously studied approach (KG) to the batch setting, which mainly entails a more sophisticated optimization of the acquisition criterion over multiple points. In my view, the main novelty lies in estimating the q-KG gradient via an IPA approach, which is also fairly standard.

One specific comment/suggestion: In equation (3.1), since x^(1:n) is a finite sequence of vectors, it is unclear how μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n), x(1:n)) are defined, given that μ and K are functions of one (or two) vectors, not sequences.
