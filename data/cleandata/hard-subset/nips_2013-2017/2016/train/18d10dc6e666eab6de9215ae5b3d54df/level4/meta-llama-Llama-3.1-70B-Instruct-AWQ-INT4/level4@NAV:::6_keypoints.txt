- Proposes a parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization method using the knowledge gradient (q-KG) to select Bayes-optimal batches.  
- Uses infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients and maximize the q-KG acquisition function.  
- Empirical results show q-KG is competitive on noise-free problems and outperforms existing methods on noisy observations across three experiments.  
- Overall experimental evidence is not entirely convincing and some aspects of the method remain unclear.  
- Experiments use standard synthetic benchmarks and real tasks (CNN/logistic regression on CIFAR-10/MNIST) but lack wall-clock time comparisons and analysis versus random search or sequential optimization.  
- The constant choice of batch size q=4 across experiments needs justification.  
- Novelty lies in applying q-KG in this context; IPA and KG are not new, and prior work (e.g., "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions") used IPA with expected improvement.  
- Parallel hyperparameter tuning is impactful and relevant to many real-world domains.  
- Technical/background sections are well-described and experiments are sufficiently explained.  
- Question: Have you compared to Wang et al. (2015) and, if so, can differences be clearly stated given their publicly available code?  
- Question: How does the method scale to more than 4 parallel runs and what gains are expected for larger q?  
- Question: Can the method be extended to asynchronous evaluations similar to Spearmint?  
- Question: Have you measured wall-clock time and overhead for computing batches compared to random search or sequential BO?  
- Question: Clarify the x-label "iterations"—does it count function evaluations or batches, and does it include the initial design (and why do methods not start at the same value)?  
- Minor: Possible missing z^(1:n) in Eq. (4.1); grammar fix ("achieve" → "achieves"); avoid parenthetical citation phrasing; correct attribution that Wang et al. (2015) applied IPA rather than introduced it.
