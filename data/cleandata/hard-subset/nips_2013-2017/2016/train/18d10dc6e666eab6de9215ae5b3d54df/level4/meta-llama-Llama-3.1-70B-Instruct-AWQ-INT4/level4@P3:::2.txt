The paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method, which computes Bayes-optimal batches by generalizing the concept of the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms existing Bayesian optimization approaches, particularly in problems with noisy evaluations. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making a strong theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.

The parallelization of Bayesian optimization is an important subject for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed approach is interesting and more elegant than most existing approaches. The fact that a Bayes-optimal batch is determined is very promising. However, it is worth noting that the authors assume independent normally distributed errors, which is common in most Bayesian optimization methods based on Gaussian processes. This assumption may be problematic in hyperparameter optimization, as measurement errors represent the difference between generalization performance and empirical estimates, and structural bias is common in certain regions of hyperparameter-space, especially given small sample sizes.

The theoretical aspect of the paper is strong, but the experiments are somewhat limited. The use of test set error as an optimization criterion is problematic, as it is well known that such score-functions are suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning. Threshold-based metrics like test set error introduce a number of problems for model selection and hyperparameter optimization, and it would be better to use metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss.

Furthermore, the authors used the MNIST and CIFAR10 data sets as a basis of their experiments, which are indeed commonly used in benchmarks. However, it would have been more convincing to see benchmarks in HPOlib, which is a widely-used library specifically designed to benchmark hyperparameter optimization algorithms. Additionally, the amount of data sets used in the benchmark is very low, and the entire benchmark is based on a single machine learning task (classification) with only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). It would have been more comprehensive to see more variety in the benchmark, as it is known that some optimizers perform well for some types of learning algorithms but poorly for others.

Overall, I like the paper and the theoretical contribution is definitely valuable. However, the current benchmark is too limited to be truly convincing. I would recommend the authors to consider addressing the limitations mentioned above to strengthen the experimental evaluation of their method.

Strengths:

* Strong theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization
* Novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches
* Promising results in problems with noisy evaluations

Weaknesses:

* Limited experimental evaluation
* Problematic assumption of independent normally distributed errors
* Limited benchmark with only two data sets and two learning algorithms

Suggestions for improvement:

* Consider using more robust metrics for hyperparameter tuning, such as area under the ROC curve or log loss
* Benchmark the method in HPOlib to provide a more comprehensive evaluation
* Increase the variety of data sets and learning algorithms used in the benchmark
* Address the limitations of the assumption of independent normally distributed errors

Acceptance:

* I would recommend accepting the paper, but with revisions to address the limitations mentioned above.
* The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, but the experimental evaluation needs to be strengthened to support the theoretical claims.
