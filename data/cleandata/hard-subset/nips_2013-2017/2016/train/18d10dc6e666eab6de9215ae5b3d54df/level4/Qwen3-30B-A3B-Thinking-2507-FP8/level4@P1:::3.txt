

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), extending the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting. The authors address the problem of selecting a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously, a common scenario in hyperparameter tuning for machine learning (e.g., training multiple neural networks in parallel). The q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective, aiming to maximize the expected improvement in the minimum of the posterior predictor after adding a batch of q new samples. Crucially, the authors provide an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients of the otherwise intractable q-KG objective, enabling scalable optimization. The method is evaluated on synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical machine learning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN tuning on CIFAR10), demonstrating consistent superiority over benchmarks like parallel EI, batch UCB, and GP-UCB-PE—particularly in noisy settings.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a clear technical contribution by generalizing KG to parallel batch selection with a computationally feasible solution. The IPA-based gradient estimation (Section 5.2) is a significant practical advancement over prior work that relies on iterative greedy selection (e.g., [3, 5, 10, 18, 25]), which fails to optimize the batch jointly. The experimental validation is thorough: q-KG achieves 11% test error on CIFAR10 CNN tuning (vs. 14% with expert-tuned hyperparameters) and substantially outperforms baselines on noisy synthetic functions (e.g., 40% faster convergence on noisy Rosenbrock3). The open-source implementation (GitHub) and detailed experimental setup (including hyperparameter choices and noise handling) enhance reproducibility. The connection to prior work (e.g., KG [7, 17], parallel EI [2, 10, 25]) is well-motivated, and the paper avoids overclaiming novelty by clarifying how q-KG differs from prior batch methods.

**Weaknesses:**  
The novelty is incremental rather than transformative: extending KG to batch selection is conceptually straightforward, and the core innovation lies in the IPA-based computational approach. While the IPA technique itself is not entirely novel (e.g., [14, 25]), its application to q-KG’s gradient estimation is well-executed and addresses a genuine bottleneck in batch BO. The paper would benefit from a clearer justification of why IPA was chosen over alternatives (e.g., stochastic gradient methods [2, 15]). Additionally, the notation in Equation (3.1) requires clarification: μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n)) are defined for a sequence of points, but μ and K are functions of vectors, not sequences. The authors should specify whether these denote vectorized operations (e.g., μ(x^(1:n)) = [μ(x^(1)), ..., μ(x^(n))]^T). The claim that "q-KG reduces to parallel EI when noise-free and final solution is restricted to sampled points" (Section 4) is slightly misleading; parallel EI typically uses a different formulation (e.g., [10]), and q-KG’s reduction is not explicitly verified in the experiments.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper presents a high-quality, well-motivated contribution to batch Bayesian optimization. The technical execution is sound, the experiments are comprehensive, and the method offers clear practical advantages—especially in noisy settings where parallel EI and UCB-based methods underperform. While the conceptual novelty is modest (extending KG to batch), the computational innovation (IPA for gradient estimation) and empirical results justify its significance. The paper adheres to NIPS standards: it is technically rigorous, clearly written (with minor notational caveats), and addresses a meaningful problem in machine learning. The authors carefully contextualize their work against prior art (e.g., [2, 3, 5, 10, 18, 25]), and the comparison with state-of-the-art baselines is fair and thorough.

**Recommendation:**  
**Strong Accept.** The paper’s practical impact on hyperparameter tuning and its efficient solution to a non-trivial computational problem make it a valuable contribution. The weaknesses noted are minor and do not undermine the core technical merit. The authors have demonstrated that q-KG is not merely a theoretical extension but a practically superior method, particularly for noisy, real-world ML tasks. The suggested clarification on notation would strengthen the manuscript further, but this is not a barrier to acceptance.
