This paper introduces a batch-sequential approach to Bayesian Optimization based on the Knowledge Gradient criterion. Specifically, the authors define a parallelized Knowledge Gradient criterion (q-KG) and detail its computation, following a similar derivation to the standard KG approach. The paper presents numerical experiments demonstrating that q-KG outperforms several state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization algorithms. Overall, this is a strong paper with qualities that warrant publication in NIPS; the potential societal impact of parallelizing Bayesian optimization is significant. 

A minor criticism concerns the lack of analysis regarding speed-ups achieved through batch-sequential processing. Furthermore, several statements in the literature review could be more precise. For example, the integration of Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was previously explored in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization” (along with Constant Liar), while Chevalier et al. introduced CL-mix in [2]. Additionally, the maximization of q-EI using natural gradients is studied in “Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design”.

I have the following specific remarks and questions: Does the set A assume compactness, and is the function f continuous? Why is A restricted to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) during the initial stage? Regarding Algorithm 1, is hyperparameter re-estimation (or Bayesian updating) considered? In Section 5.2, is the definition of g a maximization or a minimization?  Also in Section 5.2, the smoothness of g is not immediately apparent, and the differentiability of the Cholesky factor may not be trivial. The Matérn parameter appears to be 5/2, not 2/5. Finally, concerning Figure 1 and the related experiments, was it feasible to fit the same Gaussian Processes using different software packages?
