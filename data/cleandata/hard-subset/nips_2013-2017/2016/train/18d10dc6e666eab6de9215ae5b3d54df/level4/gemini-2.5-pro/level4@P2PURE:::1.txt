This paper addresses batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization by introducing a parallel version of the Knowledge Gradient criterion, termed q-KG. Following a review of Gaussian Processes and related work, the paper defines the q-KG criterion and details its computation, which follows a similar approach to the standard KG criterion. The authors present numerical experiments demonstrating that q-KG appears to outperform several state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization algorithms.

This is a very good paper presenting a valuable contribution that merits publication at NIPS. The parallelization of Bayesian Optimization is an important problem with significant potential impact, and the proposed method is well-motivated and promising.

My primary criticisms are minor. First, the paper does not analyze the computational speed-ups achieved by the batch-sequential approach over a purely sequential one. Second, a few statements in the literature review could be more precise. For instance:
*   The idea of integrating Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was already presented in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization," which also introduced the Constant Liar heuristic.
*   The CL-mix heuristic was notably presented by Chevalier et al. in [2].
*   The maximization of q-EI using the natural gradient was studied in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design."

Finally, I have the following remarks and questions for the authors:
1.  Are there implicit assumptions that the domain A is compact and the function f is continuous? These should be stated explicitly.
2.  What is the motivation for restricting the initial design points to be a Latin Hypercube Sample?
3.  In Algorithm 1, is there a step for re-estimating or performing a Bayesian update of the Gaussian Process hyperparameters?
4.  In Section 5.2, should the definition of g involve a maximization or a minimization?
5.  Also concerning Section 5.2, the smoothness of g is assumed but is not self-evident. Could the authors provide a brief justification?
6.  Similarly, the derivative of the Cholesky factor is used in the computation, which can be non-trivial. A reference or brief explanation would be helpful.
7.  There may be a typo regarding the Matérn kernel; should it be 5/2 instead of 2/5?
8.  Regarding the experiments in Figure 1: to ensure a fair comparison, were the Gaussian Process models for all competing methods fitted using the same software framework?
