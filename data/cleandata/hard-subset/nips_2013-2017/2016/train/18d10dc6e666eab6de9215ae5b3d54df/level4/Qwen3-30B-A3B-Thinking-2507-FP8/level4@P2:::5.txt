

This paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization method called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which extends the Knowledge Gradient (KG) approach to the batch setting. The authors derive a batch acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the expected minimum of the Gaussian process predictor when only one batch of evaluations remains. To address the computational challenge of optimizing this acquisition function, they develop an efficient gradient estimation method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The paper demonstrates that q-KG outperforms existing batch Bayesian optimization methods on synthetic test functions and machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly when evaluations are noisy.

The work builds on foundational Bayesian optimization literature, including the Knowledge Gradient method [17] and prior parallel Bayesian optimization approaches [3, 5, 18, 25]. While some prior work has explored batch Bayesian optimization (e.g., [2, 18, 25]), this paper's key contribution is providing a decision-theoretic derivation of a batch acquisition function that considers points collectively rather than iteratively, along with an efficient computational method for optimizing it.

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality**: The theoretical derivation of q-KG is sound and well-motivated. The IPA-based gradient estimation method represents a significant computational advance over naive approaches. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing on multiple synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show q-KG outperforming baselines, with particularly strong gains in noisy settings where the method demonstrates clear advantages.

2. **Significance**: The method has clear practical value for speeding up hyperparameter tuning in machine learning, where parallel evaluation is common. The demonstration of improved performance in noisy settings is especially valuable, as many real-world optimization problems involve noisy evaluations. The code is publicly available, enhancing reproducibility and practical utility.

3. **Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written with clear mathematical formulations. The experimental setup is detailed and reproducible. The connection between theoretical development and practical applications is well-established.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Originality**: The paper does not sufficiently clarify how its contribution differs from previous batch KG work. The draft review correctly identifies that batch KG is not entirely new, citing Wang et al. [1] and Peng's thesis [2] as prior work. The authors should explicitly compare their method with these prior approaches and clarify the specific novelty of their gradient estimation method.

2. **Experimental Fairness**: The comparison with Expected Improvement (EI) in noisy settings is problematic, as standard EI assumes noise-free evaluations. The paper should either use a noisy variant of EI or explicitly discuss why standard EI is used in noisy settings and how this affects the comparison.

3. **Technical Detail**: Section 5.2, which contains the key technical contribution (the gradient estimation method), could be expanded with more mathematical detail and intuition. The current description is somewhat brief for such a critical component.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper presents a theoretically grounded extension of Bayesian optimization to the batch setting with a well-motivated acquisition function.
- The IPA-based computational method is novel and addresses a significant bottleneck in batch Bayesian optimization.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and compelling, particularly in noisy settings where the method demonstrates clear advantages.
- The code is publicly available, enhancing reproducibility and practical utility.

*Con:*
- The paper does not sufficiently clarify its novelty relative to previous batch KG work, which weakens the contribution claim.
- The comparison with standard EI in noisy settings is potentially misleading and requires better justification.
- The key technical contribution (Section 5.2) is not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to fully assess the method's computational advantages.

**Recommendation:** Accept

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with a well-motivated method that addresses an important practical challenge (batch optimization with noisy evaluations). While the novelty relative to previous work needs clearer articulation and the noisy comparison requires better justification, the method's effectiveness is well-demonstrated through comprehensive experiments. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of related work, particularly regarding batch KG methods, and more detailed explanation of the IPA-based gradient estimation in Section 5.2. Nevertheless, the contribution is significant and the paper is of high quality overall. The authors have provided a solid theoretical foundation, an efficient computational method, and compelling empirical results that advance the state of the art in batch Bayesian optimization.
