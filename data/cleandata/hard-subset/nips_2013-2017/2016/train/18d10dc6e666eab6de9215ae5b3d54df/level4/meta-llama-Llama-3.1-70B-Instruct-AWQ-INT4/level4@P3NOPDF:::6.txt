This paper proposes a method for parallel batch Bayesian optimization by computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next. The proposed method measures the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations using the knowledge gradient (q-KG). To efficiently maximize the acquisition function, the authors present a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the gradient of q-KG. In three experiments, they demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on problems without observation noise and outperforms these methods on problems with observation noise.

While I appreciate the idea of performing parallel hyperparameter optimization to efficiently utilize multi-core environments, some aspects of the paper remain unclear, and I am not entirely convinced by the experimental results.

**Technical Quality**

The paper uses common artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. Additionally, the paper presents results for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression on Cifar10/MNIST. To further strengthen the experimental section, I would like to see an experiment showing wall-clock time or an analysis of the potential gains from using this parallel method compared to simple random search or sequential optimization. Furthermore, it is unclear why q is set to 4 for all experiments. A justification for this choice would be beneficial.

**Novelty/Originality**

The paper proposes using the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize q-KG. Although both methods themselves are not new, the paper does not provide sufficient clarity on how it differs from existing work, such as "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions," which already demonstrates how to use IPA to select a batch of configurations using expected improvement.

**Impact/Usefulness**

Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for real-world applications across various disciplines, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

**Clarity/Presentation**

The technical details and background section are well-described, and the experiment section contains all necessary information to comprehend the performed comparisons.

**Questions to the Authors**

1. As Wang et al. (2015) seems to be very relevant to this work, and code is available online, have you compared your method to this approach? If so, it would be beneficial to clearly state the differences in the paper to avoid confusion. If not, please clarify why this comparison was not made.
2. Nowadays, multi-core systems allow for many more than 4 parallel runs. For example, "Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks" (ICML'15) used up to 800 parallel runs of DNGO, and "Parallel Algorithm Configuration" (LION'12) used 25 SMAC runs in parallel. Do you have any insights into the scalability of your method and the potential gains from more parallel runs?
3. Currently, your method is designed to run synchronously. Would it be possible to extend it to run asynchronously, similar to Spearmint?
4. Wall-clock time is a critical factor in optimization methods. Have you evaluated how your method compares to random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization with respect to wall-clock time? What is the overhead for computing a new batch?
5. In the plots, what does "iterations" on the x-axis represent? Is it the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If yes, why do not all methods start from the same value?

**Minor Comments**

* In equation (4.1), is there a missing z^(1:n)?
* In the text, "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" should be "achieves."
* Parenthetical references should be rephrased, e.g., "[16] suggests parallelizing" should be "Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing."
* The reference "Wang et al. (2015)" is not the correct citation for IPA, as they do not introduce the method but rather apply it.
