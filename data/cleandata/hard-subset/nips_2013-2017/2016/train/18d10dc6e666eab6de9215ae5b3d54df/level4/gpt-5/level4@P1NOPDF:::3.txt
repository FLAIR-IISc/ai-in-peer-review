The paper proposes a stochastic gradient algorithm integrated with Bayesian modeling to optimize a multivariate objective over a finite domain. The key feature is that, at each iteration, the method selects multiple points at which to evaluate the objective, allowing parallel computation. The central challenge is to choose these sampling points in an optimal sense.

To address this, the authors adopt the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, which estimates the expected improvement in the objective from an additional evaluation and uses this to guide sampling decisions. They generalize KG to a batch setting, selecting multiple points simultaneously (q-KG). Because exact optimization of the resulting acquisition function is computationally intractable, they develop a Monte Carlo method combined with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients of the q-KG objective, with the goal of maximizing information gain over candidate batches. The algorithm is evaluated on several test problems and shown to either outperform or be strongly competitive with approaches in the literature, particularly those based on confidence bounds. The paper is clearly written and appears potentially useful for practical applications.

That said, I am not fully convinced about the level of novelty. The work primarily extends a previously studied approach (KG) to the batch setting, which mainly necessitates a more sophisticated optimization of the acquisition criterion over multiple points. In my view, the principal contribution lies in the IPA-based estimation of the q-KG gradient, which, while appropriate, is also fairly standard.

I have one specific comment/suggestion: In equation (3.1), since x^(1:n) is a finite sequence of vectors, it is unclear how μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n)) are defined, given that μ and K are functions of one or two vectors, not sequences. Please clarify the intended meaning of this notation.
