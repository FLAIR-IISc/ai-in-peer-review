The paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient algorithm combined with Bayesian modeling for optimizing a multivariate objective function over a finite domain. The key innovation of the algorithm lies in its ability to identify multiple points for objective function computation in each iteration, allowing for parallelization. The challenge is to optimally select these sampling points, which the authors address by adopting the Knowledge Gradient framework. This approach estimates the expected improvement in the objective value when an additional sampling point is used, informing the decision on whether and which sampling point to utilize. The authors generalize this method to enable batch decisions on multiple sampling points. However, due to the computational intractability of this problem, they develop an algorithm leveraging Monte Carlo and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis to estimate the relevant gradients, thereby maximizing information gain over the set of candidate sampling sets.

The algorithm is evaluated on several test problems, demonstrating competitive performance with other approaches in the literature, primarily those based on Confidence Bounds. The paper is well-written and potentially useful for practical applications. Nevertheless, I remain unconvinced about the level of novelty, as the authors essentially extend a previously studied approach (Knowledge Gradient) to a batch setting. This extension necessitates a more sophisticated method for optimizing the criterion over multiple points. In my opinion, the primary novelty of the paper lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient using an Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis approach, which, although a valuable contribution, is a relatively standard technique.

I have a specific comment regarding equation (3.1): Since x^(1:n) represents a finite sequence of vectors, it is unclear how mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n),x^(1:n)) are defined, as mu and K are functions of one or two vectors, not sequences.
