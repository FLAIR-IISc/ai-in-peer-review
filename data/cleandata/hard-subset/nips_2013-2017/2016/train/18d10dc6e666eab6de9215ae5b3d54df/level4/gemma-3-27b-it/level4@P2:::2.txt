This paper addresses the important problem of parallel Bayesian optimization, a crucial area for efficient hyperparameter tuning in modern machine learning. The authors propose a novel algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), which aims to compute Bayes-optimal batches of points for evaluation. The method is theoretically grounded and offers an elegant approach compared to existing parallel BO techniques. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core ideas. 

The key contribution lies in extending the knowledge gradient concept to the parallel setting, allowing for simultaneous evaluation of multiple points. This approach has the potential to significantly accelerate optimization, particularly when function evaluations are expensive. The authors also provide an efficient strategy for computing these Bayes-optimal batches via infinitesimal perturbation analysis, addressing a key practical challenge.

However, the experimental evaluation, while demonstrating improvements, feels limited. A significant concern is the use of test set error as the optimization criterion. As highlighted in the literature (e.g., Provost & Fawcett, 1998), metrics like test set error can be suboptimal for hyperparameter optimization due to inherent statistical biases. Employing metrics like area under the ROC curve or log-loss would provide a more robust evaluation.

Furthermore, the benchmark suite could be expanded. While MNIST and CIFAR-10 are common datasets, relying solely on these limits the generalizability of the findings. Incorporating benchmarks from HPOlib (Eggensperger et al., 2013), a dedicated library for hyperparameter optimization benchmarking, would strengthen the evaluation. The current benchmark also focuses on classification tasks with only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). A more diverse set of tasks and algorithms is needed to comprehensively assess the algorithm’s performance across different optimization landscapes, as some optimizers exhibit algorithm-specific performance.

The assumption of independent normally distributed errors in the Gaussian process is also a point to consider. While common, this assumption may not hold in practical hyperparameter optimization scenarios where measurement errors represent the difference between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., from cross-validation). Structural biases in hyperparameter space, especially with limited data, can violate this assumption. Acknowledging this limitation would strengthen the discussion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The q-KG method offers a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian optimization.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the q-KG algorithm and the efficient computation strategy are significant contributions.
*   **Potential Impact:**  Parallelizing Bayesian optimization is crucial for practical applications, and this method shows promise for accelerating hyperparameter tuning.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The benchmark is somewhat narrow in scope and relies on a potentially flawed optimization criterion.
*   **Simplifying Assumption:** The assumption of independent normally distributed errors may not hold in realistic hyperparameter optimization scenarios.
*   **Lack of Broader Comparison:** The evaluation lacks comparisons on a wider range of datasets, tasks, and algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical framework is strong, and the proposed method is promising. However, the limited experimental evaluation weakens the claims. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the concerns raised regarding the benchmark and the discussion of the error assumptions. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including metrics beyond test set error and a wider range of datasets and algorithms, would significantly strengthen the paper.
