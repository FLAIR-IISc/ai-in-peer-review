

The paper introduces a novel parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which computes Bayes-optimal batches of points to evaluate simultaneously. This work extends the sequential knowledge gradient method [17] to the parallel setting, providing a decision-theoretic framework for batch acquisition that maximizes expected solution quality improvement. The approach is grounded in Gaussian process modeling with efficient gradient computation via infinitesimal perturbation analysis, addressing a key computational challenge in parallel BO. The authors demonstrate superior performance over existing batch methods (parallel EI, batch UCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly under noisy evaluations—a significant contribution given the practical need for parallelization in machine learning pipelines.

Strengths:  
- **Originality**: The q-KG acquisition function is a theoretically sound generalization of knowledge gradient to parallel settings, distinct from iterative greedy approaches (e.g., [2, 5, 18]). It explicitly optimizes the batch jointly rather than sequentially, offering a principled alternative to prior work.  
- **Quality**: The theoretical derivation is rigorous, and the computational strategy (IPA for gradient estimation) is well-motivated and validated. The method correctly reduces to parallel EI under noise-free conditions, as shown in Section 4.  
- **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear mathematical exposition and logical flow. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) and experimental setup are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.  
- **Significance**: Parallel BO is critical for scalable hyperparameter optimization, and q-KG’s empirical gains—especially in noisy settings (Section 6.2)—address a practical gap. The code availability further enhances reproducibility.

Weaknesses:  
- **Experimental scope**: The benchmarking is limited to two datasets (MNIST, CIFAR10) and two learning tasks (logistic regression, CNN), which may not capture the full generality of hyperparameter optimization. While standard, broader validation on HPOlib [2] or diverse tasks (e.g., regression, clustering) would strengthen significance claims.  
- **Evaluation metrics**: Using test set error as the optimization criterion is suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning, as it introduces threshold-based bias [1]. Metrics like log loss or AUC would better align with standard HPO practices.  
- **Noise assumption**: The independence assumption for measurement errors is standard in GP-BO but may not fully reflect hyperparameter optimization, where errors often arise from cross-validation variance (e.g., small test sets inducing correlated noise). The authors should discuss this limitation in context.  

Pro/Con List:  
- **Pro**: Theoretically grounded parallel acquisition function; efficient computation via IPA; consistent empirical superiority in noisy settings; clear writing and reproducibility.  
- **Con**: Limited benchmark scope (only 2 datasets, 2 model types); test set error as evaluation metric; insufficient discussion of noise assumptions in hyperparameter contexts.  

The paper makes a valuable contribution to parallel Bayesian optimization, with strong theoretical and empirical foundations. While the current experimental evaluation is compelling for the tested tasks, expanding the benchmark scope would better establish the method’s broader significance. The technical content is sound, and the approach aligns with NIPS’ focus on rigorous, impactful contributions. Accept.
