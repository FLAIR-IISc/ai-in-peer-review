This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), designed for scenarios where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The core idea builds upon the Knowledge Gradient framework, which aims to maximize the expected improvement in the objective function by strategically selecting the next point(s) to sample. This work extends KG to a batch setting, enabling parallel evaluation of multiple candidate points. A key challenge is the computational intractability of optimizing the batch selection, which the authors address by developing an efficient algorithm leveraging Monte Carlo sampling and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) for gradient estimation. Through experiments on synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems (logistic regression and CNNs), the authors demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms or remains competitive with existing methods, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

The paper is well-written and presents a clear and potentially valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The approach of extending the Knowledge Gradient to a parallel batch setting is a logical and promising direction. While the core concept of extending KG isn't entirely novel – representing an incremental advancement rather than a revolutionary shift – the efficient implementation via IPA for gradient estimation represents a significant technical contribution. This allows for practical application of the method, especially in high-dimensional spaces. The experimental results are compelling, showcasing q-KG's performance gains, particularly when dealing with noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Sound Technical Foundation:** The method is grounded in a well-established decision-theoretic framework (Knowledge Gradient).
*   **Practical Efficiency:** The authors effectively address the computational challenges of batch optimization through the use of Monte Carlo and IPA, making the method applicable to real-world problems.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** Results demonstrate consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and applied tasks, notably in noisy environments.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup.
*   **Availability of Code:** Providing code fosters reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** The primary novelty lies in the adaptation of an existing technique (KG) to a batch setting and the specific IPA-based gradient estimation. While valuable, it’s an extension rather than a fundamentally new approach.
*   **Dependence on Discretization:** The practical implementation relies on discretizing the search space (Section 5.3), which introduces an approximation and might require careful tuning of the discretization parameters (M). The sensitivity of performance to this parameter could be further explored.
*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a justification for the method based on Bayes optimality, a more detailed theoretical analysis of convergence or regret bounds might strengthen the contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and well-implemented algorithm that addresses a practical problem in Bayesian optimization.
*   The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate a clear performance advantage over existing methods, especially in the presence of noise.
*   The inclusion of code promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.
*   The method offers a valuable tool for practitioners dealing with expensive, parallelizable function evaluations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The level of novelty is somewhat incremental, building upon an existing framework.
*   The reliance on discretization introduces an approximation that could affect performance.
*   The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive theoretical analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. While the novelty isn’t groundbreaking, the practical implementation and empirical results are compelling. The efficient gradient estimation technique is particularly noteworthy. I recommend **acceptance**, with a minor request for clarification regarding the definition of vector/sequence operations in equation (3.1). Specifically, it should be clarified how µ and K, which operate on single vectors, are applied to the sequence x^(1:n).
