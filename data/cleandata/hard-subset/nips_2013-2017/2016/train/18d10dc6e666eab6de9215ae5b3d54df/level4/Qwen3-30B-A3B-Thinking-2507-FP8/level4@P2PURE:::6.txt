

This work proposes a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization by computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations for evaluation. The approach utilizes the knowledge gradient (q-KG) as the utility measure for batch evaluation and introduces an efficient gradient estimation technique for maximizing the acquisition function based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). In three experiments, the authors demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noise-free problems and outperforms them on problems with observation noise. While I appreciate the motivation for parallel hyperparameter optimization to leverage multi-core environments, several aspects remain unclear, and the experimental results do not fully convince me. Below is my detailed feedback:

Technical Quality  
The paper employs common artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the proposed method's superiority. Additionally, it presents results for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression on Cifar10/MNIST. However, the experimental section lacks wall-clock time comparisons or an analysis of the practical gains from using this parallel method versus simple random search or sequential optimization. Furthermore, the choice of setting q=4 for all experiments is not justified.

Novelty/Originality  
The paper proposes using the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function and IPA to maximize q-KG. Both components are not novel; the paper references "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions," which already demonstrates IPA for batch selection using expected improvement.

Impact/Usefulness  
Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for real-world applications across machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

Clarity/Presentation  
The technical details and background are well-described, and the experiment section provides sufficient information to understand the comparisons.

Questions to the Authors:  
1. Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant to this work, with code available online. Have you compared to this method? If this is "parallel EI in MOE," it should be explicitly called q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint's parallel method. The paper should clearly state the differences.  
2. Modern multi-core systems support far more than 4 parallel runs (e.g., ICML'15 used up to 800 parallel DNGO runs; LION'12 used 25 SMAC runs; NIPS'12 used up to 10 parallel Spearmint runs). Do you have insights on the scalability of your method and the gains from increased parallelism?  
3. Your method currently runs synchronously. Could it be extended to support asynchronous execution, as Spearmint does?  
4. Wall-clock time is critical. How does your method compare to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead for computing a new batch?  
5. What does "iterations" on the x-axis of your plots represent? Is it the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do not all methods start from the same value?

Minor Comments:  
- Equation (4.1) appears to be missing a $z^{(1:n)}$.  
- "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" → "achieves a test error".  
- Parenthetical references should not be nouns: "[16] suggests" → "Snoek et al. [16] suggest".  
- The reference "Wang et al. (2015)" is incorrect for IPA, as they do not introduce the method but apply it.
