

This paper proposes a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm that extends the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework to select multiple sampling points simultaneously, enabling parallel evaluation of the objective function. The core contribution lies in adapting KG for batch acquisition—where the optimal set of points must be chosen in a single step—by developing a computationally efficient gradient estimation method based on Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) and Monte Carlo sampling. The authors address the intractability of direct optimization over candidate sets by leveraging IPA to approximate the gradient of the q-KG criterion, which quantifies the expected information gain from adding a batch of points. The approach is evaluated on several benchmark problems, demonstrating competitive or superior performance relative to state-of-the-art methods like Confidence Bound-based techniques.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong emphasis on practical applicability. The technical exposition is accessible, and the experimental results provide convincing evidence of the method’s effectiveness. However, the novelty claim requires careful consideration. While extending KG to batch mode represents a meaningful practical advancement, the core innovation—using IPA to estimate the q-KG gradient—is not entirely new; IPA has been applied in similar contexts for gradient estimation in Bayesian optimization. Thus, the primary contribution is the *application* of a well-established technique to solve a specific batch-optimization challenge, rather than introducing a fundamentally novel algorithmic paradigm. The incremental nature of this work merits acknowledgment, as the extension to batch settings is non-trivial but builds directly on existing KG theory and IPA methodology.

A significant technical concern arises in Equation (3.1), where the notation $\mu(\mathbf{x}^{(1:n)})$ and $K(\mathbf{x}^{(1:n)}, \mathbf{x}^{(1:n)})$ appears ambiguous. The superscript $(1:n)$ denotes a finite sequence of vectors (i.e., $\mathbf{x}^{(1)}, \dots, \mathbf{x}^{(n)}$), yet $\mu$ and $K$ are defined as functions of individual points or pairs of points, not sequences. This inconsistency requires clarification: the authors should explicitly define whether $\mu(\mathbf{x}^{(1:n)})$ refers to a vector of means (e.g., $\mu(\mathbf{x}^{(1)}), \dots, \mu(\mathbf{x}^{(n)})$) or a scalar summary (e.g., an average), and similarly for the kernel matrix $K$. Without this, the mathematical formulation lacks precision, potentially confusing readers attempting to implement or extend the method. A minor revision to the notation—such as specifying $\mu(\mathbf{x}^{(1:n)}) = [\mu(\mathbf{x}^{(1)}), \dots, \mu(\mathbf{x}^{(n)})]^\top$—would resolve this issue.

Overall, the paper presents a useful and well-executed extension to Bayesian optimization for parallelizable settings. The method is practical, the experiments are thorough, and the writing is clear. While the novelty is modest—primarily in the application of IPA to batch KG—the contribution fills a valuable gap in the literature for high-dimensional or expensive-to-evaluate objectives where parallelization is critical. The authors should explicitly acknowledge the incremental nature of the contribution in the discussion, contrasting it with foundational KG work and emphasizing the practical benefits of the batch approach. Addressing the notation issue in Equation (3.1) would strengthen the technical rigor significantly. Given its clarity, relevance, and empirical validation, this work is suitable for publication at NIPS, though it would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of novelty in the revised version.
