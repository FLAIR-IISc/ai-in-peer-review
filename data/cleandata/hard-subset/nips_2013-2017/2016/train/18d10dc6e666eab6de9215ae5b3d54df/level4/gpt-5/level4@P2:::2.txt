Summary
The paper introduces a batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which selects a set of q points by maximizing the expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean after one additional batch. This generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient to the parallel setting and, by construction, is Bayes-optimal when only one batch remains. Because direct optimization of q-KG is expensive, the authors propose an efficient Monte Carlo approach together with an infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) estimator for gradients, enabling gradient-based optimization. They also describe a practical discretization strategy for continuous domains. Experiments on synthetic functions and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR-10) show that q-KG is competitive with or better than parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, especially under noisy evaluations. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Gaussian process (GP) modeling and acquisition functions such as PI, EI, UCB, entropy search (ES), and the knowledge gradient (KG). In the parallel setting, prior work includes parallel EI (exact and approximate/fantasized), GP-BUCB and GP-UCB-PE, and parallel ES. The contribution here is a principled extension of KG to batch selection over continuous domains, optimized via an unbiased IPA gradient estimator. This differs from earlier discrete-space nested-batch KG and from methods that greedily construct batches or restrict the final solution to sampled points.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A decision-theoretic, Bayes-optimal batch acquisition for the one-step setting that evaluates the batch jointly rather than greedily.
- Computational method: An efficient IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG, with a clear Monte Carlo scheme and a practical discretization of the candidate set.
- Empirical performance: Consistent improvements over strong baselines in synthetic benchmarks and notably in noisy regimes, where the method is expected to shine.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is generally well written, provides algorithmic details, and releases code.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Noise model realism: The assumption of independent Gaussian observation noise is standard, but in hyperparameter optimization the “noise” often reflects generalization error estimates (e.g., from cross-validation), which can be biased, heteroscedastic, and correlated across configurations. This can systematically affect parts of the hyperparameter space. A discussion and, if possible, experiments with more realistic noise (e.g., heteroscedastic GP, correlated folds, or heavy-tailed noise) would strengthen the paper.
- Evaluation metrics for HPO: Using test set error as the objective is problematic for hyperparameter selection; accuracy-type metrics can be misleading compared to proper scoring rules (log loss) or threshold-independent metrics (ROC AUC) [1]. Consider using cross-entropy or AUC and employing nested cross-validation or a validation/hold-out protocol to avoid test-set leakage.
- Experimental scope: The HPO experiments cover only two datasets and two algorithms (both classification). This limits the generality of the claims, as optimizers can behave differently across model families. It would be beneficial to evaluate on a broader suite (e.g., SVMs, gradient boosting, random forests, regression tasks) and additional datasets. Leveraging HPOlib/BOB benchmark suites [2] would enable standardized, diverse comparisons.
- Baselines and ablations: Including additional batch BO baselines such as q-EI with analytic gradients, local penalization, q-Thompson sampling, and parallel ES (if feasible) would clarify where gains come from. An ablation on q, the discretization size M, number of Monte Carlo samples, and the impact of GP hyperparameter re-estimation would help assess robustness.
- Scalability and runtime: The method incurs Monte Carlo and gradient computation costs. Reporting wall-clock time, GPU/CPU usage, and scaling with q and dimension would make the practical benefits clearer, especially relative to fast heuristics (e.g., BUCB, local penalization).
- Clarity/notation: Section 5 uses two meanings of A (domain vs. discretized candidate set); this is noted but still confusing. Consider distinct symbols. Minor typos (e.g., “Main Stange”). Provide more detail on constructing AM_n (number of samples, sampling method) and sensitivity to this choice.

Evaluation against review criteria
- Quality: The derivation is sound for the one-step Bayes-optimal batch objective, and the IPA gradient estimator is appropriate. However, theoretical guarantees beyond the one-step optimality (e.g., multi-step behavior, regret or asymptotic consistency under discretization) are not discussed.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. A few places would benefit from clarifying notation and discretization details.
- Originality: Extends KG to batch selection in continuous domains with an efficient stochastic gradient method; this is a meaningful contribution beyond existing parallel EI/UCB/ES approaches.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for BO in noisy, parallel settings. The current experimental scope constrains the strength of empirical claims; expanding benchmarks would substantially increase significance.

Pros
- Principled, Bayes-optimal batch acquisition derived from first principles.
- Efficient gradient estimation via IPA enabling practical optimization.
- Strong performance in noisy evaluations; code released; clear exposition.

Cons
- Limited HPO evaluation (two datasets, two algorithms, accuracy objective).
- Noise assumptions may be unrealistic for HPO; no analysis of heteroscedastic or correlated noise.
- Missing runtime/scalability analysis and broader baselines.

Overall assessment
A strong and elegant contribution to batch BO with promising empirical results, particularly under noise. I recommend acceptance if the authors can broaden and improve the HPO evaluation (more tasks/datasets, appropriate metrics/protocols, additional baselines) and add a brief discussion of noise realism and computational scaling. Otherwise, it is a borderline accept on the strength of the methodological contribution. References: [1] Provost et al., ICML 1998. [2] Eggensperger et al., NIPS 2013 BO workshop.
