Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which selects a Bayes-optimal batch of points to evaluate next when one batch remains. The method extends the knowledge gradient from the sequential setting to parallel evaluations, valuing a batch collectively rather than via greedy selection. Because exact evaluation and optimization of q-KG are costly, the authors develop an estimator for its gradient using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling efficient gradient-based maximization. Empirically, across three groups of experiments, q-KG is competitive on noise-free problems and outperforms existing batch BO methods under observation noise.

This work sits alongside prior batch/parallel BO approaches, including q-EI and heuristic “constant liar” strategies, GP-BUCB and GP-UCB-PE for UCB-based batch selection, and parallel entropy search. It is closely related to the sequential KG literature, and to the gradient-based optimization of q-EI (e.g., Wang et al., “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions,” MOE), which also uses gradients to optimize a batch acquisition function. The paper’s contribution is to carry the KG principle into the batch setting and to address the resulting computational challenges.

Strengths
- Principled acquisition: q-KG is derived from a decision-theoretic objective that is Bayes-optimal for one remaining batch, without restricting the final recommendation to previously sampled points.
- Efficient computation: the IPA-based gradient estimator is well motivated for optimizing a Monte Carlo–defined acquisition.
- Empirical behavior under noise: the method consistently performs best in the noisy setting, which is practically important.
- Clear technical exposition: the background and derivations are generally well organized, and code availability is valuable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited experimental scope for parallelism: all experiments fix the batch size to q = 4. Modern systems routinely support much larger degrees of parallelism, and it is unclear how q-KG scales (statistically and computationally) as q increases.
- Missing wall-clock analysis: there is no comparison in time-to-good-solution versus random search or sequential BO, nor a report of optimization overhead to select each batch. In practice, time-to-result is critical.
- Baselines and naming clarity: it is unclear whether the comparison to “parallel EI in MOE” corresponds to q-EI as in Wang et al. (2015). The paper should clearly distinguish MOE’s q-EI from Spearmint’s heuristic parallelization and ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Synchrony assumption: the method is synchronous; many applications require asynchronous BO. Discussion or experiments on asynchronous variants would strengthen practical relevance.
- Plot interpretation: the x-axis is labeled “iterations,” but it is unclear whether this counts batches or function evaluations, and whether initial designs are included. The fact that methods do not start at the same value suggests a lack of alignment.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The derivation of q-KG and the use of IPA for gradient estimation are technically sound, and the algorithmic components are described in sufficient detail. However, the empirical validation does not fully support claims about practical efficiency due to missing wall-clock measurements and limited exploration of larger batch sizes.
- Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized. A few notational issues and minor language errors remain (see minor comments).
- Originality: Extending KG to batch selection and optimizing it via IPA is a natural but nontrivial step. IPA itself and the idea of jointly optimizing a batch acquisition function are not new, but applying them to q-KG is original relative to prior q-EI work.
- Significance: Parallel hyperparameter optimization is widely relevant. If the observed gains under noise persist and the method scales to larger q and asynchronous settings, the impact could be substantial. As is, the significance is promising but not fully established experimentally.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled batch acquisition with a clear decision-theoretic interpretation.
- Strong performance under noisy observations.
- Solid technical development with practical gradient-based optimization and released code.

Cons for acceptance
- No time-to-solution analysis or overhead measurements; unclear practical efficiency.
- Batch size fixed at q = 4; no evidence on scalability to larger parallelism.
- Incomplete comparison and naming clarity regarding q-EI (MOE) vs. Spearmint’s parallel method.
- Synchronous-only evaluation; no discussion of asynchronous operation.

Questions for the authors
1) Comparison to Wang et al. (2015): Did you compare directly against q-EI as implemented in MOE? If so, please label it explicitly as q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s heuristic parallelization and clarify the differences in the paper.
2) Scalability in parallelism: Many recent applications use >10, up to hundreds of parallel workers. How does q-KG’s performance and computational cost scale with q? Do the gains persist, and what are the bottlenecks?
3) Asynchronous extension: Can q-KG be run asynchronously (e.g., filling in incomplete batches as workers finish), similar to Spearmint? What changes would be required?
4) Wall-clock comparisons: How does q-KG compare to random search and sequential BO in time-to-target performance? What is the overhead for computing each batch (as a function of q, M, and dimensionality)?
5) Plot axes: Does “iterations” denote number of batches or total evaluations? Does it include the initial design? If so, why don’t all methods start from the same value?

Minor comments
- Possible missing notation in Eq. (4.1): Should there be a z(1:q) or z(1:n) in the conditional to make the dependence explicit?
- Grammar: “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” → “achieves.”
- Citations: Parenthetical references are not nouns; use “Snoek et al. [16] suggest …” rather than “[16] suggests …”
- IPA citation: Wang et al. (2015) apply IPA; they do not introduce the method. Please cite an appropriate source (e.g., L’Ecuyer) for IPA foundations.
