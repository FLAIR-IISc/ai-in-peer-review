

The paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, which extends the knowledge gradient framework [7, 17] to the parallel setting where multiple points can be evaluated simultaneously. This work builds directly on prior Bayesian optimization research, particularly the sequential knowledge gradient approach [7, 17], and relates to recent parallel Bayesian optimization methods [3, 5, 18, 25], though it distinguishes itself by optimizing the batch of points collectively rather than iteratively constructing the batch. The paper also connects to related work on parallel expected improvement [2, 10, 24] and parallel upper confidence bound [3, 5].

Strengths:
- The theoretical foundation is sound, with the q-KG method being Bayes-optimal for minimizing the minimum of the predictor when only one batch of evaluations remains.
- The computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate the q-KG gradient is well-motivated and effectively addresses the key computational challenge of optimizing the batch acquisition function.
- The empirical evaluation demonstrates consistent performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods across both synthetic test functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), with particularly notable gains in noisy settings.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the code is publicly available.

Weaknesses:
- The novelty is somewhat incremental as the core approach extends a well-established sequential method to a batch setting. The primary contribution lies in the computational technique for batch optimization rather than a fundamentally new conceptual framework.
- The paper could more clearly differentiate its approach from closely related work, such as [2, 15, 25], which also consider jointly optimizing parallel acquisition functions.
- A technical issue exists in equation (3.1) regarding the definition of μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) when x^(1:n) is a sequence rather than a vector.

Quality: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with sound theoretical analysis and comprehensive experimental validation. The authors provide appropriate evaluations of both strengths and limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and logically structured, with clear explanations of the methodology and thorough experimental design. The presentation of results is effective and enhances understanding.

Originality: While the extension to batch settings is meaningful, the novelty is incremental rather than groundbreaking. The work represents a significant technical contribution to the implementation of batch Bayesian optimization but does not introduce a fundamentally new conceptual approach.

Significance: The method provides practical value for applications requiring parallel function evaluations, particularly in machine learning hyperparameter tuning where noise is common. The demonstrated performance improvements, especially in noisy settings, make this a valuable addition to the Bayesian optimization toolbox.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper provides a well-executed extension to batch Bayesian optimization with clear empirical advantages, particularly in noisy settings; the computational approach using IPA is efficient and well-justified; the paper is well-written and reproducible.

Arguments against acceptance: The novelty is incremental rather than transformative; the main technical contribution (IPA-based gradient estimation) is not substantially novel compared to recent parallel Bayesian optimization work.

Overall, the paper makes a solid contribution to Bayesian optimization for parallel settings with strong technical execution and practical significance. While the novelty is not revolutionary, the method is well-developed and provides clear advantages over existing approaches. I recommend acceptance.
