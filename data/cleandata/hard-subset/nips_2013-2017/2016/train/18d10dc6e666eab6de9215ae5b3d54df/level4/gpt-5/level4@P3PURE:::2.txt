Summary
This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, addressing the inherent sequential nature of most existing BO methods. The approach is clearly presented and, based on the reported results, outperforms several existing BO techniques. Parallelization of BO is highly relevant for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed method is conceptually appealing and more elegant than many alternatives I am aware of. The idea of determining a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising.

Assumptions
The method assumes independent, normally distributed errors, which is standard in GP-based BO. However, for hyperparameter optimization this assumption can be problematic: measurement error reflects the gap between true generalization performance and its empirical estimate (e.g., via cross-validation). In practice, structural bias can arise in certain regions of hyperparameter space, especially with small sample sizes, and independence is not always a realistic assumption. It would be worth explicitly acknowledging these limitations.

Theory vs. Experiments
The theoretical contribution is strong, but the empirical evaluation is less convincing for three main reasons:

1) Optimization metric. Using test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic, and such threshold-based metrics are known to be suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). They can lead to unstable or misleading model comparisons. Metrics such as area under the ROC curve or log loss would be more appropriate.

2) Benchmark selection. While MNIST and CIFAR-10 are common benchmarks, I would have preferred evaluations using HPOlib [2], a widely used library specifically designed to benchmark hyperparameter optimization algorithms. This would facilitate fairer and more comprehensive comparisons.

3) Breadth of tasks and models. The benchmark spans only two datasets (both classification) and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). Greater diversity in datasets, tasks, and learning algorithms would strengthen the empirical case, as it is well known that different optimizers can excel on some classes of problems and underperform on others.

Overall Assessment
I like the paper and find the theoretical contribution valuable. The proposed Bayes-optimal batching for parallel BO is both interesting and practically relevant. However, the current empirical evaluation is too limited to be fully convincing. Broadening the benchmarks (e.g., via HPOlib), adopting more appropriate optimization metrics, and including a wider variety of datasets and learning algorithms would substantially improve the paper.
