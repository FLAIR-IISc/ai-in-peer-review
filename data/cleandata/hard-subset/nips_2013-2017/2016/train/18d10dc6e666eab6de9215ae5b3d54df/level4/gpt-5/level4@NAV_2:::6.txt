This paper introduces a batch Bayesian optimization approach that selects a Bayes-optimal set of points to evaluate in parallel. The method defines a batch acquisition function, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which quantifies the value of evaluating a set of q points. To optimize q-KG efficiently, the authors employ infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to obtain stochastic gradients. Across synthetic test functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10), the method is reported to be competitive with existing batch BO baselines in the deterministic setting and to outperform them under observation noise. The motivation for parallel hyperparameter search is strong, but several aspects are unclear, and the empirical section does not fully convince me. Detailed comments follow.

Technical quality
- The evaluation uses standard synthetic benchmarks and two applied tuning tasks (CNN and logistic regression for CIFAR-10/MNIST), which is appropriate. However, I miss an experiment or analysis that reports wall-clock time, or quantifies practical speedups relative to random search or sequential BO when parallel resources are available. This would be crucial for assessing the benefit of batching in real systems.
- The choice to fix q = 4 throughout is not justified. Please explain this setting and, ideally, analyze sensitivity to q.

Novelty/Originality
- The contribution is to use the knowledge gradient as a batch acquisition function and to optimize it via IPA-derived gradient estimates. While the combination is useful, both components (KG and IPA) are established. Moreover, “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions” already demonstrates IPA for optimizing a batch EI objective; the relationship and differences should be made explicit.

Impact/Usefulness
- Parallel hyperparameter optimization is highly relevant across domains (ML, vision, biology, robotics). A robust and scalable batch KG method could have substantial practical impact if it demonstrates clear gains in end-to-end time to good solutions and scales to larger degrees of parallelism.

Clarity/Presentation
- The background and technical sections are clearly written, and the experimental setup is generally understandable. The comparisons appear reproducible given the implementation notes.

Questions to the authors:
1) Wang et al. (2015) seems closely related and code is public. Did you compare against that method directly? If this corresponds to “parallel EI in MOE,” please name it q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s heuristic parallelization and clearly delineate the differences to avoid confusion.
2) Modern hardware supports far more than 4 parallel workers. For example:
   - “Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks” (ICML’15) used up to 800 DNGO evaluations in parallel;
   - “Parallel Algorithm Configuration” (LION’12) ran 25 SMAC instances in parallel;
   - “Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms” (NIPS’12) used up to 10 parallel Spearmint runs.
   What is the scaling behavior of q-KG with increasing q, both computationally (acquisition optimization overhead) and in terms of optimization performance? How much additional benefit do larger batches yield?
3) The current algorithm is synchronous. Can it be adapted to an asynchronous setting (as in Spearmint), and what changes would be required?
4) Ultimately, wall-clock time is what matters. Have you compared against random search or standard sequential BO in terms of time-to-target? What is the overhead for computing each new batch under your implementation?
5) In the plots, what does “iterations” on the x-axis denote—number of batches or total function evaluations? Does the x-axis include the initial design points? If so, why do methods not start at the same value?

Minor comments:
- Is a z(1:q) term missing in Eq. (4.1)?
- “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” → “achieves”
- Parenthetical citations should not act as nouns; e.g., “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing,” not “[16] suggests parallelizing.”
- Wang et al. (2015) is not the canonical reference for IPA; they apply it rather than introduce it.
