

This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, extending the sequential knowledge gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretically optimal batch acquisition function that selects q points simultaneously to maximize expected information gain, addressing a key limitation in prior parallel BO work (e.g., Contal et al., 2014; Desautels et al., 2011). The method leverages infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently compute gradients for optimization, enabling scalable implementation. Empirical results demonstrate consistent superiority over parallel EI (Chevalier et al., 2013), batch UCB (Kandasamy et al., 2015), and GP-UCB-PE (Contal et al., 2014) across synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), with particularly pronounced gains in noisy evaluation scenarios.  

**Strengths:**  
- *Quality:* Theoretical foundation is rigorous (Bayes-optimal batch selection), with thorough computational analysis using IPA to resolve scalability challenges. Experiments are comprehensive, covering both noise-free and noisy settings with statistical significance (100/20/5 runs for synthetic/practical tasks).  
- *Originality:* Directly addresses the gap in collective batch optimization (vs. iterative greedy approaches), with clear differentiation from related work (e.g., parallel EI via closed-form vs. joint optimization; see Shah & Ghahramani, 2015).  
- *Significance:* Provides a practical, scalable solution for parallelizable hyperparameter tuning—critical for modern ML pipelines where sequential BO is prohibitively slow. The noise-robustness is especially valuable for real-world applications.  
- *Clarity:* Well-structured, with clear algorithmic description (Algorithm 1) and effective use of figures (Figures 3–4) to illustrate convergence.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- *Quality:* The Gaussian process assumption may require justification for high-dimensional neural architectures (e.g., input dimensionality in CNN tuning exceeds typical GP scalability limits). Theoretical error bounds for q-KG (e.g., regret guarantees) are not provided, though the empirical results are strong.  
- *Clarity:* Notation could be improved (e.g., ambiguous use of "A" for both domain and discretized set in Sections 4.1 and 5.3; line 95 defines A but omits its domain specification). The parallel EI comparison (line 128) could be briefly elaborated for context.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**  
*Pro:* Strong technical contribution with clear theoretical and empirical advances over prior parallel BO methods; highly relevant to NIPS community’s focus on scalable optimization; well-executed experiments addressing a critical practical bottleneck.  
*Con:* Limited discussion of GP limitations in high dimensions (though this is a common challenge in BO, not specific to this work); no theoretical error analysis.  

The paper makes a significant, well-executed contribution to Bayesian optimization. With minor clarifications to notation (e.g., explicitly defining A’s domain) and a brief discussion of GP scalability concerns, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The empirical evidence robustly supports the claims, and the code availability enhances reproducibility. **Recommendation: Accept.**
