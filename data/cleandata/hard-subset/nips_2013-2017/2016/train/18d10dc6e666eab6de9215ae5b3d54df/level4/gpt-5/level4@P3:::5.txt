Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a batch (parallel) extension of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization, called q-KG. Under a GP prior and a batch size q, q-KG selects the set of points that maximizes the expected reduction in the posterior mean’s minimum after one batch, which is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining iteration. The authors show that, with noiseless observations and when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Because the q-KG objective involves expectations over future noisy observations, the paper develops an efficient gradient estimator based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and Monte Carlo “fantasizing,” enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch as a whole. Experiments on synthetic test functions and hyperparameter tuning (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10) indicate that q-KG is competitive with or better than parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, especially with noisy evaluations.

The paper positions itself relative to sequential KG and prior parallel acquisition functions (parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel ES). However, there is prior work on batch/nested-batch KG (e.g., Wang et al., SIAM J. Sci. Comput., 2015; Peng thesis 2010), typically on discrete domains. The authors briefly mention this line, claiming their method is more efficient in continuous domains since gradients exist, but a clearer comparison is needed.

Strengths:
- A principled decision-theoretic acquisition function for batch BO that jointly optimizes the entire batch, rather than greedily filling it.
- An efficient IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG that makes joint batch optimization tractable.
- Strong empirical performance in both noiseless and noisy settings, with particularly notable gains under noise.
- Clear connection to existing methods: reduction to parallel EI under standard noiseless/selection-restricted conditions.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
1) Novelty vs. prior batch KG: Please clarify the precise contribution compared to prior batch/nested-batch KG methods (e.g., Wang et al. 2015; Peng 2010). My reading is that your main novelty is an efficient and differentiable q-KG objective with an unbiased IPA gradient estimator on continuous domains plus a practical discretization scheme; if so, this distinction should be made explicit. Ideally, add an empirical comparison on a discrete domain (where prior batch KG operates) or provide a complexity and accuracy comparison demonstrating your computational advantages.

2) Gradient estimator details (Section 5.2): This is the core technical contribution and needs elaboration. Please:
- State assumptions under which exchanging gradient and expectation is valid (differentiability of µ and K, uniqueness of the minimizer almost surely, no ties, dominated convergence).
- Clarify that ∂µ(n)(x)/∂z = 0 so the first two derivative terms in the displayed expression should vanish; as written they are misleading.
- Describe how you handle the non-differentiability at argmin ties and whether you use subgradients or measure-zero tie-breaking.
- Provide computational complexity per MC sample (e.g., O(q^3) for Cholesky, dependence on |A_n|), number of MC samples used, variance reduction, and convergence behavior of the stochastic optimizer.
- Comment on differentiating through the Cholesky factor (stability and implementation).

3) Discretization of A (Section 5.3): The description “drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior” is unclear. If you mean sampling from the posterior distribution over the minimizer (as in PES/PPES), please describe the procedure precisely and cite appropriately. Report sensitivity to M and discuss the implications of including z(1:q) in A_n for bias/variance of the q-KG estimate and for gradient stability.

4) Noise-aware baselines: EI in its basic form assumes noiseless observations, so comparisons in Section 6.2 can be unfair to EI. Please either:
- Use a noise-aware EI baseline (e.g., noisy EI via fantasizing/repeated sampling or established variants), or
- Explicitly discuss and justify the limitations of EI under noise and interpret the results accordingly.
Ensure all baselines use the same GP priors/kernels, hyperparameter fitting, and comparable optimization budgets.

5) Empirical reporting:
- Include wall-clock time or acquisition optimization time per iteration vs. baselines to quantify computational overhead.
- For the CNN and MNIST tuning, report budgets (epochs, wall-clock, number of evaluations), and address fairness when hyperparameters affect training time.
- Provide ablations on q, M, and number of MC samples. For noisy settings, report the learned noise variance and its effect on performance.

6) Exposition and editorial issues:
- Expand Section 5.2 with a self-contained derivation/algorithmic summary (even if details are in the supplement).
- Fix typos and numbering (e.g., “Main Stange,” “.1” subsections, “gaussian,” “th Conference” header artifact).
- Avoid redefining IPA at line 162 and ensure consistent notation for A (suggest renaming the discretized set to Ã_n).
- Strengthen the proof/argument for the reduction to parallel EI (or provide a formal citation/appendix derivation).

Assessment by review criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically sound in conception. The stochastic gradient estimator is plausible but needs clearer assumptions, corrected derivative expressions, and more computational detail. Experiments are solid but need noise-aware baselines and runtime reporting for a complete assessment.
- Clarity: Generally readable, but Section 5.2 is too terse and some notation is confusing. Several editorial fixes are needed.
- Originality: Extends KG to batch with an efficient differentiable formulation in continuous domains; novelty is moderate given prior nested/batch KG work, but the computational advance appears meaningful.
- Significance: Likely to be useful in practice, especially under noisy parallel evaluations; empirical gains suggest impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Principled acquisition function with demonstrated gains, especially in noisy settings.
- Practical algorithm enabling joint batch optimization with gradients.
- Clear relationship to and improvement over widely used baselines.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient comparison and positioning against prior batch KG work.
- Key technical section lacks detail and has a likely derivative error.
- No noise-aware EI baseline and limited runtime analysis.

Recommendation: Borderline accept. With clarifications to Section 5.2, a stronger discussion/experiment vs. prior batch KG, and noise-aware baselines or a careful discussion thereof, this would be a clear accept.
