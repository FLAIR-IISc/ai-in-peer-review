This paper proposes a method for parallel batch Bayesian optimization that selects a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next. The acquisition function is the batch knowledge gradient (q-KG). To efficiently maximize q-KG, the authors use infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate its gradient. Across three experiments, q-KG is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noise-free problems and outperforms them when observations are noisy. I appreciate the goal of enabling efficient use of multi-core environments via parallel hyperparameter optimization, but several aspects remain unclear, and I am not fully convinced by the experimental results. Below I provide detailed feedback and questions.

Technical quality
- The paper evaluates on common artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the proposed method’s performance. In addition to these cheap-to-evaluate functions, the paper includes results for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR-10/MNIST.
- I would like to see an experiment reporting wall-clock time or an analysis of the practical speedups from parallelization, compared with simple random search or sequential Bayesian optimization. Since parallelism is the main motivation, end-to-end time is critical.
- I also do not see why q was fixed to 4 in all experiments. Please justify this choice or provide sensitivity analyses for different batch sizes.

Novelty/Originality
- The paper proposes using the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as the acquisition function and IPA to maximize q-KG. Both ingredients are established. The paper “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions” already shows how to use IPA to select a batch using expected improvement. Please clarify the methodological contribution relative to that prior work.

Impact/Usefulness
- Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant to real-world applications across machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics. A robust, noise-aware batch method would be valuable in these domains.

Clarity/Presentation
- The technical background and details are presented clearly, and the experiments include sufficient information to understand the comparisons. However, additional clarity on experimental protocol (see questions below) and practical runtime would improve the paper.

Questions to the authors
1) Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant and code seems to be available. Have you compared against this method? If your baseline is “parallel EI in MOE,” please refer to it as q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel method. To avoid confusion, clearly state the differences among these approaches.
2) Modern multi-core systems support far more than four parallel runs. For example:
   - “Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks,” ICML’15, used up to 800 parallel DNGO runs.
   - “Parallel Algorithm Configuration,” LION’12, used 25 SMAC runs in parallel.
   - “Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms,” NIPS’12, used up to 10 parallel Spearmint runs.
   Do you have insights into how your method scales with batch size and the practical gains from larger q? Are there computational or statistical limits to increasing q?
3) Your method currently runs synchronously. Can it be extended to an asynchronous setting, as in Spearmint? If so, what changes would be required for q-KG and its IPA-based gradient estimation?
4) Ultimately, wall-clock time matters. How does your method compare with random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization in wall-clock time, including the overhead of computing each new batch? Please quantify the batch-selection time and discuss its impact as q grows.
5) In your plots, what does the x-axis label “iterations” denote: the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If yes, why do not all methods start from the same initial value?

Minor comments
- Is z^(1:n) missing in Eq. (4.1)?
- “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” → “achieves.”
- Parenthetical references are not nouns. For example, “[16] suggests parallelizing” should be “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing.”
- “Wang et al. (2015)” is likely not the correct primary reference for IPA; they apply it rather than introduce it. Please cite foundational IPA sources accordingly.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to parallel Bayesian optimization with encouraging results under observation noise. Strengthening the experimental evaluation—particularly with wall-clock comparisons, justification of q, and scaling analyses—and clarifying distinctions from closely related work would substantially improve the paper.
