The paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method, for optimizing a multivariate objective function over a finite domain. The algorithm identifies a set of points to evaluate in parallel, making it suitable for applications where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously.

The authors provide a clear and concise background on Bayesian optimization, Gaussian processes, and the knowledge gradient method, which is essential for understanding the proposed algorithm. They also discuss related work, highlighting the differences between their approach and existing methods.

The parallel knowledge gradient method is derived using a decision-theoretic analysis, which is a novel contribution of the paper. The authors also develop an efficient strategy for computing the Bayes-optimal batch of points, using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of the acquisition function.

The paper presents an extensive experimental evaluation of the proposed algorithm, comparing its performance to several state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization algorithms on synthetic and real-world problems. The results show that the parallel knowledge gradient method consistently outperforms or is competitive with existing methods, especially in problems with noisy function evaluations.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and detailed description of the proposed algorithm and its implementation. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner.

In terms of novelty, the paper builds upon existing work on the knowledge gradient method, extending it to the batch setting. While the authors' contribution is significant, it is not entirely new, as they are expanding a previously studied approach to a more complex setting. However, the estimation of the q-KG gradient using an IPA approach is a notable contribution, as it enables the efficient optimization of the acquisition function over a set of multiple points.

One minor comment is that in equation (3.1), the notation for mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) could be clarified, as these are functions of sequences of vectors, rather than individual vectors.

Strengths:

* Novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm
* Efficient strategy for computing the Bayes-optimal batch of points
* Thorough experimental evaluation
* Clear and concise writing style

Weaknesses:

* Limited novelty, as the paper builds upon existing work
* Minor notation issue in equation (3.1)

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient batch Bayesian optimization algorithm
* The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the algorithm's performance
* The paper is well-written and provides a clear description of the proposed algorithm and its implementation

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's novelty is limited, as it builds upon existing work
* The notation issue in equation (3.1) could be clarified

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed algorithm is a significant contribution to the field of batch Bayesian optimization. While the novelty is limited, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and it is recommended for acceptance.
