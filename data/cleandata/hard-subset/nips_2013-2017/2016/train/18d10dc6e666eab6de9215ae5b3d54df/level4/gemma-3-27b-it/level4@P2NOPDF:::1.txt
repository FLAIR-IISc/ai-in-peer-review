This paper introduces a batch-sequential approach to Bayesian Optimization based on a parallelized Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) criterion. The authors provide a clear definition of q-KG, detailing its computation in a manner consistent with the standard Knowledge Gradient framework. Empirical evaluation demonstrates that q-KG performs favorably when compared to existing state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization algorithms. Overall, this is a strong contribution with the potential to significantly impact the field, given the broad applicability and importance of efficient Bayesian Optimization. The ability to parallelize these algorithms has considerable practical implications.

While the paper is compelling, a few minor points could be addressed. The experiments do not explicitly investigate the speed-ups achieved through batch-sequential optimization compared to sequential methods. Additionally, there are a few imprecisions within the related work section. The integration of Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was previously explored in the work by Lizotte et al. (“Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization,” including the Constant Liar approach), and Chevalier et al. presented the CL-mix strategy in [2]. Furthermore, differentiating the multi-point Expected Improvement for optimal batch design was studied by, for example, [cite relevant paper].

I have several specific remarks and questions for the authors:

1.  Regarding the theoretical foundation: Is the set *A* assumed to be compact, and is the objective function *f* assumed to be continuous? Clarifying these assumptions would strengthen the theoretical grounding.
2.  Regarding the initial experimental setup: Why was a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) specifically chosen for the initial design *A*? What alternatives were considered, and what motivated this choice?
3.  Regarding Algorithm 1: Does the algorithm include a mechanism for hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating of the Gaussian process model? If so, please detail its implementation. If not, a discussion of the implications of using fixed hyperparameters would be beneficial.
4.  Regarding Section 5.2: In the definition of *g*, is it a maximization or a minimization problem? This requires clarification.
5.  Regarding Section 5.2 (continued): The smoothness of *g* is stated, but this does not appear self-evident. Could the authors provide further justification or a proof of smoothness? 
6.  Regarding Section 5.2 (continued): Similarly, the differentiability of the Cholesky factor is not immediately obvious. Providing details regarding the derivative calculation would be valuable.
7.  Regarding a typographical error: The Matérn kernel parameter is typically denoted as ν = 5/2, not 2/5 as stated in the paper.
8.  Regarding Figure 1 and the experimental results: Was it feasible to fit the same Gaussian Processes using different software packages (e.g., GPy, scikit-learn) to assess the robustness and reproducibility of the results? This would provide confidence in the implementation and prevent potential software-specific biases.
