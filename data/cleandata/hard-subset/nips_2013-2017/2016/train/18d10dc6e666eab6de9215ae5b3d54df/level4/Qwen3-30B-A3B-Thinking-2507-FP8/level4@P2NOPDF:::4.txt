

This paper introduces a parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, addressing the challenge of time-consuming function evaluations. The proposed method efficiently outputs multiple points for simultaneous evaluation, significantly accelerating optimization compared to sequential approaches. The authors provide a scalable algorithm for evaluating q-KG and empirically validate its effectiveness, demonstrating strong alignment with theoretical claims. The problem is highly relevant, as distributed evaluation is crucial for scaling data-driven applications, and the q-KG approach offers a compelling solution to this bottleneck. The method is well-motivated, the experimental design is sound, and the paper is clearly written overall.

However, several concerns warrant attention. The assumption of Gaussian processes (GPs) as the underlying model may be questionable, particularly for high-dimensional neural network architectures where input dimensionality is large. The authors should address whether GP approximations remain effective in such settings, or provide justification for their applicability. Additionally, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees on error bounds and scalability with respect to input dimension. Explicit analysis of computational complexity (e.g., in terms of dimensionality *d* and batch size *q*) would strengthen the theoretical foundation and help assess practical viability for large-scale problems.

The paper also contains minor presentation issues requiring clarification. Line 36 ("the set of points to evaluate next that is") contains redundant phrasing; this should be streamlined. Line 95 references "A" without definition—this symbol must be explicitly introduced. Line 104 asks about the input to *μⁿ*; the authors should specify its domain (e.g., "input points *x*"). Line 128 suggests briefly comparing with parallel expected improvement (EI), which would contextualize q-KG’s advantages. Line 140 notes inconsistent notation for *A*; using distinct symbols for different concepts would improve readability. A few typos also appear (e.g., "fasten" should be "accelerate" in the abstract).

Overall, this is a solid contribution to Bayesian optimization. The q-KG method is conceptually novel, practically useful, and supported by rigorous experiments. While the theoretical gaps and notation inconsistencies are notable, they do not undermine the core technical merit. Addressing these points would elevate the paper to a strong NIPS submission. The work is methodologically sound, the experiments are thorough, and the writing is generally clear—these minor issues are easily resolvable in revision. The proposed approach offers a valuable step toward efficient parallel optimization, making it a worthy addition to the field.
