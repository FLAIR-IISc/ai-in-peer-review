This paper proposes a method for parallel batch Bayesian optimization that computes a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next. The utility of a batch is measured using the knowledge gradient (q-KG), and the authors introduce an approach based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate gradients of q-KG for acquisition maximization. Across three experiments, the paper reports that q-KG is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noiseless problems and outperforms them when observations are noisy. I appreciate the goal of enabling parallel hyperparameter optimization to better utilize multi-core environments, but several aspects remain unclear, and I am not fully convinced by the experimental evaluation. Feedback on the main points and questions follow.

Technical quality
- The paper evaluates on common synthetic benchmark functions to demonstrate superiority of the proposed method. In addition to these inexpensive-to-evaluate problems, it also presents results on optimizing a CNN and logistic regression for CIFAR-10/MNIST.
- In the experimental section, I would like to see a study of wall-clock performance or an analysis quantifying the practical gains of parallelization compared to simple random search or sequential Bayesian optimization. As it stands, it is hard to assess end-to-end efficiency.
- It is also unclear why q is fixed to 4 in all experiments.

Novelty/Originality
- The paper uses the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as the acquisition function and applies IPA to maximize q-KG. Neither ingredient is new, and the paper cites “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions,” which already demonstrates how to use IPA to select a batch via expected improvement.

Impact/Usefulness
- Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for real-world applications across machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

Clarity/Presentation
- The technical background and methodological details are well presented, and the experimental section contains sufficient information to follow the comparisons.

Questions to the authors
1) Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant and code seems to be available online. Have you compared against this method? If this corresponds to “parallel EI in MOE,” please refer to it as q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel method. To avoid confusion, it would help to clearly state these distinctions in the paper.
2) Modern multi-core systems often support far more than 4 parallel evaluations. For example:
   - “Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks” (ICML’15) used up to 800 parallel DNGO runs
   - “Parallel Algorithm Configuration” (LION’12) used 25 SMAC runs in parallel
   - “Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms” (NIPS’12) used up to 10 parallel Spearmint runs
   Do you have insights into how well your method scales with increasing parallelism, and what performance gains one can expect as q grows?
3) Your method is currently synchronous. Can it be extended to run asynchronously, as Spearmint does?
4) Ultimately, wall-clock time is what matters. Have you compared your method to random search or standard sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead for constructing each new batch?
5) In your plots, what does “iterations” on the x-axis denote—the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do not all methods start from the same value?

Minor comments
- Is there a z^(1:n) missing in Eq. (4.1)?
- “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” → “achieves”
- Parenthetical references are not nouns; e.g., “[16] suggests parallelizing” should be “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing.”
- I do not think “Wang et al. (2015)” is the correct citation for IPA; they apply the method but do not introduce it.
