

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, addressing an important problem in scenarios where multiple function evaluations can be performed in parallel, such as when optimizing hyperparameters across multiple machines. The authors develop a novel acquisition function that provides the Bayes-optimal batch of q points to evaluate, derived from a decision-theoretical perspective. They demonstrate that this method significantly outperforms existing batch Bayesian optimization approaches, particularly in noisy settings, on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks.

The work is well-positioned within the Bayesian optimization literature. The authors correctly note that while sequential Bayesian optimization has been extensively studied, parallel evaluation settings have received less attention. They build upon the knowledge gradient (KG) framework, which is Bayes-optimal for sequential settings, and extend it to the batch setting. The paper provides a clear comparison with related work, including parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration, acknowledging the iterative greedy approaches of previous methods while highlighting how q-KG considers the batch collectively rather than sequentially.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The technical contribution is significant: the authors successfully generalize the knowledge gradient to the parallel setting and provide an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), which addresses the computational challenges of optimizing the acquisition function. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, testing on multiple synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show that q-KG outperforms benchmarks, with particularly strong gains in noisy evaluation scenarios. The code is publicly available, enhancing reproducibility. The writing is generally clear and well-structured, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical development to empirical validation.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that warrant attention. First, the assumption of Gaussian process priors may be questionable in high-dimensional settings common in neural network hyperparameter tuning. While the authors acknowledge the dimensionality challenge, they do not provide theoretical justification for why Gaussian processes remain appropriate in such scenarios or discuss alternative modeling approaches. This is particularly relevant given the increasing use of Bayesian optimization for deep learning hyperparameter tuning, where high-dimensional input spaces are the norm.

Second, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees for the proposed method. While the authors demonstrate empirically that q-KG outperforms alternatives, they do not provide error bounds or complexity analysis with respect to input dimensionality. A theoretical analysis of how the method scales with dimension would strengthen the contribution, especially for readers interested in applying the method to high-dimensional problems.

Third, the paper could improve its discussion of related work. The authors briefly mention parallel EI but could more thoroughly explain how their approach differs from existing methods that also optimize batches collectively (e.g., [2, 15, 25]). A more detailed comparison would clarify the novelty of the q-KG approach.

The paper is generally well-written, but contains a few technical inconsistencies that should be addressed. For instance, the definition of the feasible domain A is not explicitly stated in the main text (l.95), and the input to the posterior mean function μ^(n) is not clearly specified (l.104). The notation for the set of candidate points (z^(1:q)) is also somewhat confusing, and using different notation for the feasible domain and the discretized set would improve clarity (l.140).

Regarding the four review criteria:

Quality: The paper presents a technically sound method with rigorous theoretical development and comprehensive empirical validation. The computational approach using IPA is well-justified and efficient. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding the Gaussian process assumption.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, but some notational inconsistencies and missing definitions (as noted above) reduce clarity. The explanation of the q-KG acquisition function could be slightly more intuitive for readers unfamiliar with knowledge gradient.

Originality: The extension of knowledge gradient to the parallel setting is novel, particularly in its collective batch optimization approach versus the iterative greedy methods of prior work. The method represents a meaningful contribution to the literature, distinct from previous batch Bayesian optimization approaches.

Significance: The method addresses an important practical problem in distributed optimization, with clear benefits for applications where parallel evaluation is possible. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages, particularly in noisy settings, suggesting the method could be widely adopted in machine learning hyperparameter tuning.

In summary, this is a strong contribution to Bayesian optimization with clear practical benefits. The main weaknesses concern the theoretical justification for Gaussian processes in high dimensions and the lack of theoretical guarantees. Addressing these would significantly strengthen the paper. The empirical results are compelling and well-presented, and the method appears to be a valuable addition to the Bayesian optimization toolbox.

Pro: Novel batch acquisition function with decision-theoretic foundation; Efficient computational approach using IPA; Strong empirical results, particularly in noisy settings; Comprehensive evaluation on synthetic and real-world problems.

Con: Limited theoretical analysis of Gaussian process assumptions in high dimensions; Missing error bounds and complexity analysis; Some notational inconsistencies that reduce clarity.
