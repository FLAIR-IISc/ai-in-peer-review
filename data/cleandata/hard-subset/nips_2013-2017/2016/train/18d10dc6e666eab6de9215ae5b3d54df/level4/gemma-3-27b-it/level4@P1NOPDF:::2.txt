Bayesian optimization has gained significant popularity for machine learning tasks such as hyperparameter optimization, but most existing approaches are inherently sequential. This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes batches in a Bayes-optimal manner. The authors demonstrate that their proposed method outperforms existing Bayesian optimization techniques. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. Parallelizing Bayesian optimization is an important challenge for practical hyperparameter tuning, and this approach is both interesting and more elegant than many others I have encountered. The determination of a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising. 

The authors assume independent, normally distributed errors, a common assumption in Gaussian process-based Bayesian optimization methods. However, this assumption can be problematic in the context of hyperparameter optimization, where measurement errors typically reflect the discrepancy between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., obtained through cross-validation). Structural bias is often present in certain regions of the hyperparameter space, particularly with small sample sizes. It’s worth noting that assuming independence isn’t always realistic.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are strong, but the experimental evaluation is somewhat disappointing for three main reasons. First, using test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic; it is well-established that such threshold-based metrics are suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Test set error introduces several issues for model selection and hyperparameter optimization, and metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss would be more appropriate. Second, the authors used MNIST and CIFAR-10 as their experimental datasets, which are common benchmarks. However, I would have preferred to see benchmarks using HPOlib [2], a widely-used library specifically designed for benchmarking hyperparameter optimization algorithms. Third, the benchmark relies on a limited number of datasets (two) and focuses on a single machine learning task (classification) with only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). Greater variety in the benchmark would be desirable, as some optimizers perform well with certain learning algorithms but poorly with others. 

Overall, I find this paper to be strong, and the theoretical contribution is definitely valuable. However, the current benchmark is too limited to be fully convincing.

[1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998. 

[2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
