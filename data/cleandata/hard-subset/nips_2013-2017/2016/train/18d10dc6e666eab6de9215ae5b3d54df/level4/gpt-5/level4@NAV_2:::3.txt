The paper presents a variant of a stochastic, gradient-based procedure coupled with Bayesian modeling for optimizing a multivariate objective over a finite domain. The distinguishing feature is that, at each iteration, the method selects a batch of points at which to evaluate the objective, enabling parallel execution. The central challenge is to determine these sampling locations in an optimal manner. To address this, the authors adopt the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, which evaluates the expected gain from an additional measurement and uses this to decide whether and where to sample; here, this idea is extended to select multiple points jointly in a batch. Because direct optimization of the batched criterion is computationally prohibitive, the authors develop an approach that combines Monte Carlo estimation with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to obtain gradients and thus maximize the information gain over candidate batches. Empirically, the algorithm is tested on several benchmarks and practical tasks and is shown to outperform or remain strongly competitive with existing methods, particularly those based on upper confidence bounds. The paper is clearly written and appears useful for practice. However, the conceptual novelty seems moderate: it extends a known approach (KG) to a batch setting, which chiefly necessitates a more sophisticated optimization of the multi-point criterion. In my view, the primary innovation lies in the IPA-based estimation of the q-KG gradient, which is itself a fairly standard technique.

Clarifying this notation (e.g., by explicitly indicating vectorization of µ and the construction of the Gram matrix for K) would improve readability.
