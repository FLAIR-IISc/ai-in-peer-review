This paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient optimization algorithm leveraging Bayesian modeling to efficiently optimize multivariate objective functions over finite domains. The core contribution lies in a parallelizable strategy for selecting points at which to evaluate the objective. Specifically, the algorithm addresses the challenge of optimally choosing a batch of sampling points in each iteration. To achieve this, the authors extend the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework, traditionally used for single-point acquisition, to a batch setting. KG estimates the expected improvement in the objective value gained by adding another sample, guiding the decision of whether and which point to sample. 

The extension to batch sampling introduces significant computational challenges. The paper addresses this intractability by developing an algorithm that employs Monte Carlo estimation and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to approximate the gradients needed for maximizing information gain across candidate sampling sets. Through experiments on several benchmark problems, the proposed method demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to existing approaches, particularly those based on Confidence Bounds.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a potentially valuable tool for practical applications. However, I have some reservations regarding the degree of novelty. While the extension of KG to a batch setting is a logical and useful step, it builds directly upon a previously established framework. The increased complexity stems primarily from optimizing the acquisition criterion over multiple points simultaneously. Furthermore, the proposed q-KG gradient estimation, while effective, relies on IPA, a relatively standard technique. The key innovative aspect, in my view, resides in the combination of these elements – the batch KG extension *and* the IPA-based gradient approximation – rather than a single groundbreaking contribution.

I have one specific technical comment: In equation (3.1), the definitions of  μ(x<sup>(1:n)</sup>) and K(x<sup>(1:n)</sup>, x<sup>(1:n)</sup>) are unclear. Given that x<sup>(1:n)</sup> represents a finite sequence of vectors, it’s not immediately obvious how these functions, which are typically defined for single vectors or pairs of vectors, are extended to operate on a sequence. Clarification of this point would strengthen the presentation.
