

This paper introduces a batch version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization. The authors propose a Monte-Carlo-based gradient estimation method to determine an optimal batch of design points, demonstrating superior performance over existing batch BO methods in experiments. However, the contribution requires clearer articulation and several critical points need addressing:

1.  **Contribution Clarification:** The batch KG framework is not novel; Wang et al. (2015) and Peng (2010) present foundational batch KG approaches. The paper must explicitly state how its contribution differs from these works. The core novelty appears to be the gradient estimation method for batch selection. If this is the case, the authors must rigorously compare the *efficiency and effectiveness* of their gradient estimation against Wang et al. (2015) and Peng (2010) through either experiments or mathematical analysis. Simply claiming "outperforms other batch BO methods" without this specific comparison is insufficient.

2.  **Section 5.2 Elaboration:** Section 5.2, which details the gradient estimation method, is the paper's central technical contribution. This section requires significant expansion. The current description lacks sufficient mathematical rigor and clarity on *how* the Monte-Carlo estimation is derived, implemented, and why it is suitable for batch KG. Providing concrete algorithmic steps, justification for the estimation approach, and potentially a small-scale illustrative example would greatly strengthen this key section.

3.  **Fairness of Noisy Setting Comparison:** The comparison in Section 6.2 uses standard Expected Improvement (EI), which assumes noiseless observations. This is problematic when evaluating performance in a *noisy* setting, as standard EI is not designed for noise and will generally underperform compared to noise-aware methods. The authors must clarify: (a) Whether a noise-aware variant of EI was used, and (b) If standard EI was used, they must explicitly discuss the resulting unfairness in the comparison and justify why this comparison remains meaningful. The current results require this critical justification.

4.  **Abbreviation Handling:** The abbreviation "IPA" is redefined in line 162. While this is acceptable, ensure "IPA (Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis)" is defined upon first use in the main text for clarity, rather than solely in the citation.
