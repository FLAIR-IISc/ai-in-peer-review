This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, addressing the inherently sequential nature of most existing Bayesian optimization (BO) approaches. The authors demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing techniques.

The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The parallelization of BO is an important problem for practical applications like hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed approach is interesting and more elegant than many existing methods. The ability to determine a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising, and the paper's theoretical contributions are strong.

A minor theoretical concern is the assumption of independent, normally distributed errors. While common in Gaussian process-based BO methods, this assumption can be problematic in hyperparameter optimization. In this setting, measurement error represents the difference between true generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., from cross-validation), where structural bias is common in certain regions of the hyperparameter space, especially with small sample sizes. It would be worth acknowledging that the independence assumption is not always realistic.

While the theoretical aspect of the paper is strong, the experiments are somewhat disappointing for three main reasons:

1.  The choice of test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic. It is well-established that such threshold-based score functions are suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning and model selection [1]. Metrics such as the area under the ROC curve or log loss would have been more appropriate and would have led to a more robust evaluation.

2.  The experiments are based on the MNIST and CIFAR-10 datasets. While these are common benchmarks, the evaluation would have been significantly more compelling had the authors used a standardized library specifically designed for benchmarking hyperparameter optimization algorithms, such as HPOlib [2].

3.  The experimental scope is very limited. The evaluation is based on only two datasets, a single machine learning task (classification), and just two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). Given that the performance of optimizers can vary significantly across different types of learning algorithms and problem domains, this lack of variety makes it difficult to assess the general applicability of the proposed method.

In summary, the paper presents a valuable and elegant theoretical contribution to an important problem. However, the current experimental benchmark is too limited to be fully convincing. The paper would be much stronger if the empirical evaluation were broadened to address the concerns raised above.

[1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998.
[2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
