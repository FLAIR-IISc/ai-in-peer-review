This paper presents a batch-sequential version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) criterion for Bayesian Optimization. The authors introduce a parallel variant, denoted as q-KG, and provide a detailed computation of the criterion, following the standard KG route. The paper is well-structured, starting with a thorough background on related work and Gaussian processes, followed by the definition of the q-KG criterion and its numerical experiments. The results show that q-KG outperforms some state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization algorithms.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that possesses important qualities justifying its publication in NIPS. The potential impact of parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms on society is significant, and the authors' contribution to this field is substantial. However, there are a few minor criticisms and remarks that can be addressed to further improve the paper.

Firstly, the authors do not study the speed-ups achieved by transitioning from sequential to batch-sequential optimization. This would be a valuable addition to the paper, as it would provide insight into the practical benefits of the proposed approach. Furthermore, some statements in the literature review are slightly imprecise. For instance, the idea of integrating Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was already explored in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (along with Constant Liar), and Chevalier et al. presented CL-mix in [2]. Additionally, q-EI maximization using natural gradient is studied in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design".

Regarding the technical aspects of the paper, several questions and remarks arise. It is unclear whether the set A is compact and the function f is continuous. The authors restrict A to be a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) at the initial stage, but the reasoning behind this choice is not explicitly stated. In Algorithm 1, the process of hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating is not discussed. In Section 5.2, the definition of g seems to involve a maximization problem, but it is not explicitly stated whether it is a max or a min problem. The smoothness of g is also assumed, but this might not be immediately obvious. Similarly, the derivative of the Cholesky factor might require further clarification. Lastly, it appears that the Matérn parameter is incorrectly stated as 2/5 instead of 5/2.

Regarding the experiments presented in Figure 1, it would be interesting to know whether it was possible to fit the same Gaussian processes using different software packages. This would help to ensure that the results are not dependent on the specific implementation used.

In conclusion, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization. While some minor criticisms and remarks have been raised, the overall quality of the paper is high, and it is well-suited for publication in NIPS.
