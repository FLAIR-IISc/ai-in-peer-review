

This paper introduces a batch-sequential variant of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) criterion for Bayesian optimization. After establishing background on related work and Gaussian processes, the authors define the parallel (q-KG) criterion, detailing its computation along the lines of the standard KG approach. Numerical experiments demonstrate that q-KG outperforms several state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization algorithms. This is a strong contribution with significant potential impact on the field, as parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms holds substantial societal relevance. A minor limitation is the lack of analysis of speed-ups achieved by transitioning from sequential to batch-sequential optimization. Additionally, the literature review contains a few imprecise statements: specifically, the integration of Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was already presented in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (along with the Constant Liar method), and Chevalier et al. notably introduced CL-mix in [2]. Furthermore, q-EI maximization using natural gradient is addressed in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design." The following specific remarks and questions are provided:  
1. Is the domain \( \mathcal{A} \) compact and the objective \( f \) continuous?  
2. Why restrict \( \mathcal{A} \) to a Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) design at initialization?  
3. In Algorithm 1, how are hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating handled?  
4. In Section 5.2, is the definition of \( g \) a maximization or minimization?  
5. In Section 5.2, the smoothness of \( g \) requires justification and does not follow immediately.  
6. The derivative of the Cholesky factor may not be obvious to readers.  
7. The Matérn kernel is specified as 5/2 (not 2/5).  
8. For Figure 1 and its experiments, was it feasible to fit the same Gaussian processes using alternative software packages?
