Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization method, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), that selects a batch of q inputs by maximizing the expected one-step improvement in the GP posterior’s predicted minimum. The method generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient to the batch setting and shows that, in the noise-free case with the final recommendation restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to q-EI. Because the q-KG objective lacks a closed form in the batch/noisy setting, the authors develop a Monte Carlo estimator and an efficient stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based optimization of the batch.

The paper situates q-KG among batch BO approaches based on EI (e.g., q-EI with exact or approximate gradients), UCB-based batch policies (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), and entropy-search variants (parallel PES). Compared with greedy batch construction used by many prior methods, q-KG treats the batch jointly and is derived from a decision-theoretic Bayes-optimality argument for the one-batch-to-go setting. The paper also acknowledges related sequential KG work and recent nested-batch KG for discrete domains.

Empirically, the method is evaluated on synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10. Results indicate competitive or improved performance in the noise-free setting and notably larger gains under observation noise. An implementation is released.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The definition of q-KG as the expected reduction in the posterior minimum is standard KG reasoning extended to batches. The Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG and the IPA-based gradient estimator are appropriate; the use of the Cholesky factor for efficient sampling and gradient computation is sound. The discretization of A for evaluating the inner minima is standard in BO (e.g., ES). However, the paper would benefit from: (i) a clearer complexity analysis per batch optimization step (in q, the number of Monte Carlo samples, and the discretization size M), (ii) a discussion of variance reduction for the stochastic gradients, and (iii) a more explicit statement of conditions ensuring unbiasedness and differentiability (currently deferred to supplement).
- Experimental methodology: The benchmarks are standard and the inclusion of noisy evaluations is valuable. That said, several practical aspects that determine real-world utility are missing: wall-clock time versus random search and sequential BO; scaling with larger batch sizes; and a discussion of asynchronous execution. The choice q = 4 throughout lacks justification, and the impact of the discretization size M = 1000 is not explored.
- Claims: The Bayes-optimality claim is correct for one remaining batch under the GP posterior and for the discretized domain used in evaluation. It would be good to make explicit that the practical algorithm optimizes an approximation due to discretization and Monte Carlo estimation.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a solid background section and a readable derivation. The algorithmic steps and the definition of q-KG are transparent. Some notation and reference issues should be cleaned up (see Minor comments), and more clarity on the x-axes of figures and the interpretation of “iterations” is needed.

Originality
Extending the KG principle to batch selection and providing an efficient stochastic gradient estimator via IPA in the continuous-domain setting is a meaningful contribution. While IPA and batch EI gradients have been used (e.g., q-EI in MOE and related work), applying IPA to a KG-based batch objective, together with the decision-theoretic justification and noisy-setting focus, provides incremental novelty. The paper clearly differentiates from UCB- and ES-based batch methods.

Significance
Batch BO is practically important in ML and beyond. The empirical results suggest q-KG can particularly help under noise, which is common in hyperparameter tuning. With further evidence on scalability (larger q), computational overhead, and asynchronous settings, q-KG could be a strong candidate for deployment. As presented, significance is promising but somewhat limited by the scope of the experiments.

Pros for acceptance
- Well-motivated decision-theoretic formulation; reduces to q-EI in the appropriate limit.
- Efficient stochastic gradient estimator enabling joint batch optimization.
- Consistent improvements under noisy evaluations across synthetic and practical tasks.
- Clear exposition and released code.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited exploration of scalability: only q = 4; no results for larger batches common in practice.
- No wall-clock comparison against random search and sequential BO; overhead of computing batches not quantified.
- Synchronous-only setting; no discussion or experiments on asynchronous execution.
- Sensitivity to discretization size M and effect on performance not analyzed.
- Some ambiguity in baselines and nomenclature (e.g., distinguishing MOE’s q-EI from Spearmint’s heuristic batching).

Questions and requests for clarification
1) Baselines and naming: Please clarify whether “parallel EI in MOE” corresponds to exact/approximate q-EI (Wang et al., 2015). If so, adopt the “q-EI” nomenclature to avoid confusion with Spearmint’s heuristic batching. If not, please include q-EI as a baseline (code is publicly available).
2) Scaling in batch size: Modern settings often use much larger q (10–100+). What is the computational complexity per q-KG evaluation/gradient step in terms of q and M? Can you report results (or at least ablations) for larger q, and discuss practical limits (e.g., O(q^3) Cholesky, Monte Carlo budget)?
3) Asynchronous execution: Can q-KG be adapted to asynchronously return a new point as soon as one worker finishes (“fantasizing” pending outcomes), analogous to Spearmint and other systems? A brief discussion or preliminary results would strengthen the case for real-world use.
4) Wall-clock time: How does q-KG compare to random search and sequential BO in elapsed time to reach a given regret on your noisy synthetic and ML tasks? Please report the overhead of batch selection (seconds/minutes per batch) and its fraction of total runtime.
5) Figure axes: What does “iterations” denote—batches or total function evaluations? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do methods not start from the same value?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include an ablation on discretization size M and its impact on performance and runtime.
- Report the number of Monte Carlo samples used for objective and gradient estimation, and any variance-reduction techniques.
- Provide sensitivity analyses to GP hyperparameters and noise variance estimation.
- Add at least one higher-dimensional synthetic benchmark and a larger-q setting to probe scalability.

Minor comments
- Equation (4.1): Please check whether conditioning terms are complete; notation around y(z(1:q)) and the role of z(1:q) in A versus in the discretized An could be clarified. There may also be a missing argument in the expectation notation.
- Grammar: “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” → “achieves.”
- References: Use author-citation style in text (e.g., “Snoek et al. [19] suggest …”). Also, IPA was not introduced by Wang et al. (2015); please cite foundational IPA work (e.g., L’Ecuyer, 1990) appropriately.
- Please standardize capitalization and naming (e.g., “q-KG,” “q-EI,” “GP-UCB-PE”) and ensure all URLs are complete.

Overall evaluation
A solid and well-presented contribution that extends KG to batch BO with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator and shows strong performance under noise. I am positive about the idea and its potential impact, but I recommend strengthening the empirical evaluation with wall-clock analyses, larger batch sizes, and clearer baseline positioning before acceptance.
