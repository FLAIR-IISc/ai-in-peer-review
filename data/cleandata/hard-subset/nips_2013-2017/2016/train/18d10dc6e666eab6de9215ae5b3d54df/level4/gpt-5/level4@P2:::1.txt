Summary
The paper introduces a batch (parallel) version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization, termed q-KG. The authors motivate the need for parallel evaluations in expensive black-box optimization and derive q-KG as the Bayes-optimal one-step policy for selecting a batch of q points, when the final recommendation is not restricted to lie among previously sampled points. They show how to compute q-KG via Monte Carlo and efficiently optimize it using an infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA)–based stochastic gradient estimator. Because exact computation is infeasible in continuous domains, they discretize the set over which the minimum of the GP posterior mean is taken. Experiments on standard synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and CNNs (CIFAR-10) show that q-KG outperforms or matches strong batch BO baselines, with especially pronounced gains under observation noise. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on the sequential KG literature (e.g., Frazier et al.) and on parallel BO methods including parallel EI/CL and extensions (e.g., Ginsbourger et al., Chevalier & Ginsbourger), gradient-based optimization of q-EI (e.g., Marmin et al.), parallel UCB-based methods (GP-BUCB; GP-UCB-PE), and parallel entropy search (PPES). The paper’s main novelty is a principled extension of KG to batch selection with an efficient stochastic gradient computation for continuous domains. Note that integrating EI over the posterior of pending outcomes and the Constant Liar family already appear in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization,” with additional variants such as CL-mix discussed in Chevalier & Ginsbourger. Also, differentiating and optimizing multipoint EI (including natural/stochastic gradient methods) has been studied (e.g., Marmin et al.).

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic foundation: q-KG is Bayes-optimal for one remaining batch when the final decision is not restricted to sampled points, and reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless, incumbent-restricted case.
- Computational contribution: an IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator enables practical joint optimization over q points; the discussion of discretization for continuous A is sensible and aligns with prior BO practice.
- Empirical performance: strong and consistent results on both synthetic and realistic ML tuning tasks, with notable advantages under noise.
- Reproducibility: public code and a reasonably detailed algorithmic description.

Weaknesses and questions
- Speedups vs. sequential BO: The paper does not quantify wall-clock or sample-efficiency speedups enabled by batching (e.g., time-to-target with varying q, computational overhead of acquisition optimization vs. evaluation time). A brief analysis would strengthen the practical case.
- Assumptions and regularity: The derivations in Section 5.2 rely on differentiability and interchange of expectation and differentiation. Please clarify assumptions (e.g., compactness of A, continuity of f/µ/K, uniqueness of argmins with probability one) under which the stochastic gradient is unbiased and g is almost surely differentiable.
- Definition of g (Sec. 5.2): There appears to be potential confusion between min and max in the expression for g. Please double-check signs and the optimization direction to avoid ambiguity.
- Cholesky derivatives: The gradient expressions involve derivatives of the Cholesky factor. Please cite or briefly state the formula used (e.g., standard results for differentiating Cholesky) and discuss numerical stability.
- Initial design: Why constrain the initial stage to a Latin hypercube sample? Would Sobol sequences or adaptive initial designs affect performance? Please comment on sensitivity.
- Hyperparameter learning: Algorithm 1 mentions retraining GP hyperparameters by MLE each iteration. Have you considered Bayesian treatments (e.g., marginalization or MAP with priors), or stabilizing strategies in the noisy regime (e.g., noise floor, priors on length-scales)? How sensitive is q-KG to hyperparameter misspecification?
- Discretization of A for computing q-KG: You propose sampling candidate points from “global optima of the posterior.” Please clarify this sampling scheme (e.g., local minima of µ(n), Thompson sampling over functions, or sampling from high-utility regions as in ES/PPES) and its effect on bias/variance of the q-KG estimate.
- Comparative fairness: Different implementations are used across baselines (MOE, Spearmint, Gpoptimization). Could differences in GP fitting, kernels, or optimization routines affect the outcomes? Were the same GP priors, kernels (ARD Matérn 5/2), and hyperparameter learning procedures enforced across methods?
- Minor issues:
  - Matérn kernel: it is Matérn 5/2 (not 2/5).
  - Please clarify whether A is assumed compact and whether f is continuous; this matters for the existence of minima.
  - In the related work, please acknowledge earlier treatments of integrating EI over pending outcomes (Ginsbourger et al.), CL variants (Chevalier & Ginsbourger), and gradient-based q-EI optimization (Marmin et al.).

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical grounding with a principled extension of KG to parallel settings.
- Practical and novel gradient estimator enabling joint batch selection in continuous spaces.
- Strong empirical results, especially under noise, on both synthetic and real tasks.
- High potential impact for large-scale hyperparameter tuning and other parallel evaluation settings.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited analysis of computational cost, scalability, and practical speedups with increasing q.
- Some attribution and clarity issues in the related work and technical derivations (g definition, differentiability, Cholesky derivatives).
- Reliance on discretization of A without a detailed sensitivity analysis.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution to batch Bayesian optimization, with a clear decision-theoretic basis, a thoughtful computational strategy, and convincing empirical performance. Addressing the clarity and attribution points, tightening technical assumptions, and adding a brief analysis of computational overhead and speedups would further strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
