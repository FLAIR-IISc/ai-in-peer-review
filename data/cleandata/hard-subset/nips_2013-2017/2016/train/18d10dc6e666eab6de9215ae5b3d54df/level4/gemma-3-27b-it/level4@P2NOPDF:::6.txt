This work introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization (BBO) that selects the next batch of configurations to evaluate based on the Bayes-optimal criterion, utilizing the knowledge gradient (q-KG) as its acquisition function. To efficiently maximize this acquisition function, the authors propose an approach leveraging infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of q-KG. The method is evaluated on three experimental tasks, demonstrating competitive performance against existing parallel BBO techniques on noise-free problems and superior performance on problems with observation noise. While the core idea of accelerating hyperparameter optimization through parallelization is appealing and well-motivated, several aspects of the work require further clarification and the presented experimental validation is not entirely convincing.

**Technical Quality:**

The paper employs standard benchmark functions to demonstrate the proposed method's advantages. Importantly, the evaluation extends beyond these easily evaluated functions to include optimization of a CNN and logistic regression models for the CIFAR10 and MNIST datasets. However, the experimental section would benefit significantly from the inclusion of wall-clock time measurements, or a more direct analysis of the gains achieved by this parallel method relative to simpler baselines like random search or sequential Bayesian optimization. Currently, it's unclear how substantial the practical benefits of parallelization are. Furthermore, the choice of *q* = 4 across all experiments is not justified. The rationale behind this specific value, and a sensitivity analysis demonstrating its robustness, should be provided.

**Novelty/Originality:**

The work leverages the knowledge gradient (a generalization of Expected Improvement) as an acquisition function in a parallel BBO setting and applies IPA for maximizing q-KG. While these individual components are not novel, their combination is presented as a contribution. However, the paper acknowledges prior work ("Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions") that already demonstrates the use of IPA in conjunction with Expected Improvement for batch selection. The novelty of this work, therefore, appears incremental. 

**Impact/Usefulness:**

Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant to a wide range of disciplines, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics, and have the potential to significantly accelerate research and development. The proposed method offers a potentially valuable tool in these areas.

**Clarity/Presentation:**

The paper is generally well-written, with clear technical details and a comprehensive background section. The experiment section provides sufficient information to understand the performed comparisons. However, there are a few minor stylistic issues (see “Minor Comments” below).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.) Wang et al. (2015) appears to be highly relevant to this work, and their code is publicly available. Have you compared your method to theirs? If the work referenced is “parallel EI in MOE”, clarifying this and explicitly distinguishing it from Spearmint’s parallel approach (perhaps by adopting the notation q-EI) would reduce potential confusion. A clear statement of the differences between your method and existing approaches is crucial.

2.) Modern multi-core systems commonly support significantly more than 4 parallel runs. Previous work, such as Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks (ICML’15) with 800 parallel DNGO runs, Parallel Algorithm Configuration (LION’12) with 25 SMAC runs, and Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms (NIPS’12) with 10 Spearmint runs, demonstrates this. What insights can you offer regarding the scalability of your method, and the potential gains from increasing the number of parallel runs?

3.) Currently, your method is implemented synchronously. Would it be feasible to extend it to an asynchronous implementation, similar to Spearmint?

4.) Ultimately, wall-clock time is a critical performance metric. Have you evaluated how your method compares to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead associated with determining each new batch?

5.) The x-axis in your plots is labeled “iterations.” Does this represent the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do the different methods not begin from the same value?

**Minor Comments:**

*   There appears to be a missing z^(1:n) in equation (4.1).
*   “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” should read “achieves a test error.”
*   Parenthetical references should not be used as nouns. For example, "[16] suggests parallelizing" should be “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing.”
*   The reference to Wang et al. (2015) regarding IPA may be inaccurate, as they apply the method rather than introduce it.
