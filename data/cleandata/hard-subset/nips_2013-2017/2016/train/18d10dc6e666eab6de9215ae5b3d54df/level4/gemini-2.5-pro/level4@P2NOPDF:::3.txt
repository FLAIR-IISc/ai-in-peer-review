This paper presents a Bayesian optimization algorithm for maximizing a multivariate objective function over a finite domain. The key contribution is a method for selecting a batch of points to evaluate in parallel at each iteration. This problem is framed as identifying a set of sampling points that is optimal in some sense.

To solve this, the authors extend the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework. The standard KG policy estimates the expected improvement in the objective value from sampling one additional point and selects the point that maximizes this value. This paper generalizes the approach to the batch setting, proposing a "q-KG" criterion that seeks to select a batch of `q` points that jointly maximize the expected improvement.

As the direct optimization of the q-KG criterion is computationally intractable, the authors develop a stochastic gradient-based algorithm to find a good batch of points. The main technical novelty lies in the use of Monte Carlo simulation combined with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function. This allows the algorithm to search for the optimal set of candidate points. The proposed algorithm is evaluated on several test problems, where it is shown to either outperform or be highly competitive with existing approaches, particularly those based on Confidence Bounds.

The paper is well-written, and the proposed method addresses a practical need for parallelization in Bayesian optimization, making it potentially useful for real-world applications. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm.

My main reservation concerns the level of conceptual novelty. The core idea is an extension of the previously studied Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to a batch setting. While this extension is non-trivial and requires a more sophisticated optimization method, the foundational concept remains the same. The primary technical contribution appears to be the application of a stochastic gradient method using IPA to estimate the q-KG gradient. While effective, this is a relatively standard technique in the context of simulation-based optimization. Therefore, the work feels more like a strong and valuable combination of existing ideas rather than a fundamental breakthrough.

I have one specific comment regarding notation:

In equation (3.1), the notation for the mean and covariance of the Gaussian Process posterior is slightly ambiguous. The terms `μ(x^(1:n))` and `K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n))` are used, where `x^(1:n)` is a finite sequence of `n` vectors. However, the prior mean function `μ` and covariance kernel `K` are typically defined on individual points or pairs of points, not on sequences. I presume the authors mean that `μ(x^(1:n))` is the vector `[μ(x_1), ..., μ(x_n)]^T` and `K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n))` is the `n x n` covariance matrix whose `(i,j)`-th entry is `K(x_i, x_j)`. Clarifying this notation would improve the paper's precision.
