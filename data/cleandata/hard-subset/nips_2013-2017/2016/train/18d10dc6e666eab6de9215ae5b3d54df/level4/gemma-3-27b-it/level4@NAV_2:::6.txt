This work provides a method to perform parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization by computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next. The proposed method measures the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations using the knowledge gradient (q-KG). To efficiently maximize the acquisition function they present a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the gradient of q-KG. In three experiments, they demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on problems without observation noise, and outperforms these methods on problems with observation noise. I appreciate the focus on parallel hyperparameter optimization to enable efficient use of multi-core environments; however, some aspects remain unclear and I am not entirely convinced by the experimental results presented.

**Technical Quality:** The paper leverages established artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. Supplementing these computationally inexpensive problems, the authors present results for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR10/MNIST. I believe the experimental section would benefit from including a comparison based on wall-clock time, or an analysis detailing the gains achievable from using this parallel method compared to simpler approaches like random search or sequential optimization. Additionally, the justification for setting the batch size *q* to 4 consistently across all experiments is unclear.

**Novelty/Originality:** The paper proposes utilizing the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function and employs infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for maximizing q-KG. While these techniques are not novel in themselves, the work effectively combines them in the context of parallel Bayesian optimization. The authors should clarify the relationship to prior work, specifically "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions" [Wang et al., 2015], which also demonstrates the application of IPA to batch selection using expected improvement.

**Impact/Usefulness:** Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant to applications across diverse fields including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics. The potential for accelerating optimization processes in these areas is significant.

**Clarity/Presentation:** The technical details and background information are well-described, and the experimental section provides sufficient context for understanding the performed comparisons.

**Questions to the Authors:**

1.  Given the relevance of Wang et al. (2015) and the potential availability of their code, have you compared your method against it? (If the "parallel EI in MOE" refers to this work, please clarify this and distinguish it from the parallel EI implementation in Spearmint). A clear statement of the differences between these approaches would be beneficial.
2.  Modern multi-core systems readily support significantly more than four parallel runs (e.g., Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks [ICML'15] used 800 parallel runs, Parallel Algorithm Configuration [LION'12] used 25, and Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms [NIPS'12] used 10). Do you have any insights into the scalability of your method and the potential gains achievable with a larger number of parallel runs?
3.  Currently, your method appears to operate synchronously. Would it be possible to extend it to an asynchronous implementation, similar to Spearmint?
4.  Ultimately, wall-clock time is crucial. Have you evaluated how your method compares to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead associated with computing each new batch?
5.  What does "iterations" represent on the x-axis of your plots? Does it refer to the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Also, why do not all methods begin from the same value on the x-axis if the x-axis represents iterations?

**Minor Comments:**

*   There appears to be a missing z^(1:n) in equation (4.1).
*   "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" should be "achieves a test error."
*   Parenthetical citations should not be nouns (e.g., "[16] suggests parallelizing" should be "Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing").
*   I question whether Wang et al. (2015) is the appropriate reference for introducing IPA, as they apply the method rather than introducing it.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to parallel Bayesian optimization. The technical contributions appear sound, and the results are promising, particularly in noisy settings. However, the lack of comparison to related work (especially Wang et al., 2015) and the absence of wall-clock time analysis weaken the overall impact. Addressing the questions and comments raised above would significantly strengthen the paper.
