This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), which provides the one-step Bayes optimal batch of points to sample in the parallel setting. The authors derive this method using a decision-theoretic analysis and develop an efficient strategy for computing the Bayes-optimal batch of points.

The paper relates to previous work in Bayesian optimization, particularly in the parallel setting, and highlights the differences between their approach and existing methods. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work, discussing the strengths and weaknesses of various approaches.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of an efficient method for estimating the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function, which enables the optimization of this function using gradient-based methods. This is a significant improvement over previous batch KG methods, which often rely on Monte Carlo sampling or other computationally expensive methods.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its underlying assumptions. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the q-KG method in both noise-free and noisy settings, outperforming other batch Bayesian optimization algorithms.

However, there are a few areas that require clarification or further discussion. Firstly, the authors should provide a more detailed comparison with previous batch KG papers, highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of their approach. This would help to better understand the contribution of this paper and its position within the existing literature.

Secondly, Section 5.2, which describes the gradient estimation method, could be elaborated further. This section is crucial to understanding the efficiency and effectiveness of the proposed method, and additional details would be beneficial.

Thirdly, the comparison with EI in the noisy setting may be unfair, as EI assumes noiseless observations. The authors should discuss this limitation and consider using a variant of EI that accounts for noise.

Lastly, the abbreviation "IPA" is redefined in line 162, which may cause confusion. It would be better to use a consistent definition throughout the paper.

In conclusion, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, introducing an efficient and effective method for batch optimization in the parallel setting. With some minor revisions to address the above points, this paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
