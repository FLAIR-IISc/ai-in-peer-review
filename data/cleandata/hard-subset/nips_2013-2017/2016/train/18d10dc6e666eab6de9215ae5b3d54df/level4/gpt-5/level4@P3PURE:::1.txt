Summary
This paper introduces a batch-sequential (parallel) version of the Knowledge Gradient acquisition function for Bayesian optimization, denoted q-KG. After a clear overview of related work and Gaussian process modeling, the paper defines the q-KG criterion, derives an efficient computation that parallels the standard (sequential) KG derivation, and presents numerical experiments in which q-KG appears to outperform several state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization methods. The paper is well written, technically solid, and, in my view, meets the bar for NIPS. The potential impact on society of parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms is paramount.

Strengths
- A principled extension of the Knowledge Gradient to the batch setting (q-KG), with a computation strategy closely aligned with the classical KG framework.
- Well-executed empirical study showing strong performance relative to established batch BO baselines.
- Clear potential impact, given the importance of parallel experimentation in modern applications.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Speed-ups from sequential to batch-sequential are not studied. Wall-clock performance and scaling with batch size are essential to fully assess the practical benefits of q-KG.
- The literature review contains a few imprecisions or omissions (details below), which should be corrected to accurately position the contribution.

Clarifications on related work
- The idea of integrating Expected Improvement with respect to posterior distributions was already present in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization” (which also discusses Constant Liar).
- Chevalier et al. presented, notably, CL-mix in [2].
- Maximization of q-EI using the natural gradient is studied in “Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design.”
These points should be acknowledged to more precisely situate the contribution relative to prior multipoint acquisition design and optimization methods.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Assumptions on the domain and objective: Is the decision space A compact, and is f assumed continuous? Please state these assumptions explicitly, as they inform existence/regularity arguments and the behavior of the optimizer.
- Initialization: Why is the initial design restricted to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS)? Could other space-filling or adaptive schemes be used, and does this choice materially affect performance in your experiments?
- Algorithm 1: How are GP hyperparameters handled during the optimization loop? Is there hyperparameter re-estimation (e.g., via MLE/MAP) or Bayesian updating across iterations, and at what frequency? This can significantly affect both accuracy and computational cost.
- Section 5.2: In the definition of g, should it be a maximum or a minimum? Please double-check the sign/convention and correct if necessary.
- Section 5.2 (smoothness): The smoothness of g does not seem to be immediate. Please provide conditions under which g is differentiable (almost surely or in expectation), or a reference/argument ensuring the validity of gradient-based optimization for q-KG.
- Differentiating the Cholesky factor: The derivative of the Cholesky factorization is not obvious. Please clarify how you compute these derivatives (e.g., via an explicit formula, reverse-mode differentiation through Cholesky, or citing a matrix calculus result/software implementation).
- Figure 1 and experiments: Were the same GP models and hyperparameter learning procedures used across all methods, and—if different software packages were involved—was it possible to fit the same GPs consistently? Please clarify to rule out implementation confounds.

Minor comments and typos
- Matérn is 5/2, not 2/5.
- Please ensure consistent notation for max/min in the definition of g in Section 5.2.

Overall assessment
This is a very good paper with a principled contribution and strong empirical results. Addressing the clarity issues in the related work, explicitly stating assumptions and differentiability conditions, detailing hyperparameter treatment, and reporting wall-clock speed-ups versus sequential baselines would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance after these revisions.
