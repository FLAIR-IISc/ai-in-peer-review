Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), a batch Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for the one-step lookahead setting (i.e., when only one batch remains). The method generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) for Gaussian process (GP) priors to choose a set of q points jointly by maximizing the expected reduction in the GP posterior’s predictive minimum. Because exact evaluation is intractable for q > 1, the authors develop an efficient stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch.

The work is well connected to BO literature. It extends KG (Frazier et al., 2008) to the batch setting, relates to parallel EI and its differentiated/closed-form variants (Ginsbourger; Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.), UCB-based batch policies (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), and parallel entropy-search variants (PPES, PES). Compared to greedy batch construction (qEI via sequential fantasies, UCB-PE), q-KG optimizes the batch jointly via a principled decision-theoretic objective. Compared to PPES, it targets expected immediate improvement in solution quality rather than information-theoretic criteria. The paper also contrasts with nested batch KG work on discrete spaces that lacks differentiability and efficient gradient computation.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic foundation: q-KG is Bayes-optimal in the one-batch-remaining setting and reduces to parallel EI in the noise-free, “recommend-from-sampled-points” regime.
- Solid computational contribution: an unbiased IPA gradient estimator for the batch objective and a practical continuous optimization procedure with adaptive discretization of the A used inside the acquisition.
- Good empirical performance on synthetic functions and two practical hyperparameter tuning tasks, with especially strong gains under noise.
- Thorough exposition with code release, facilitating reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Experimental design and scope:
  • The real-world evaluation is limited to two classification datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10) and two algorithms (logistic regression, a CNN). BO behavior can vary across tasks; broader coverage (e.g., regression, structured prediction, different model classes) would strengthen claims of generality.
  • Consider benchmarking on standardized HPO suites such as HPOlib to ease comparison and ensure diverse, representative tasks.
  • Important batch BO baselines appear missing in some comparisons (e.g., parallel/predictive entropy search, differentiated qEI with joint optimization). Including PPES and state-of-the-art qEI optimizers would provide a stronger baseline set.

- Evaluation protocol and metrics:
  • The paper appears to optimize directly against test-set error. This risks optimistic bias and is not standard practice. A train/validation/test split or nested CV should be used for selection, with final reporting on a held-out test set.
  • Accuracy/error is threshold-dependent and can be insensitive to probabilistic calibration. For classification, consider proper scoring rules such as log loss; for imbalanced settings, AUC/PR-AUC can be more informative. At a minimum, justify metric choice and report multiple metrics when relevant.

- Noise modeling and assumptions:
  • The GP observation model assumes independent Gaussian noise with constant variance in the noisy experiments. In HPO, noise is often heteroscedastic (e.g., due to stochastic training, early stopping) and can exhibit correlations across hyperparameter regions (e.g., due to data subsampling or cross-validation folds). Discuss limitations of the i.i.d. Gaussian assumption, and consider heteroscedastic or replication-aware models, or modeling CV folds explicitly.
  • Clarify how the noise variance is estimated and whether replication is used to reduce variance or assess model mis-specification.

- Computational considerations:
  • q-KG is more computationally intensive than greedy batch heuristics. Please report wall-clock time and time-to-target metrics alongside evaluations-vs-regret, including number of Monte Carlo samples, gradient steps, multi-starts, and the dependence on q and dimension.
  • Provide sensitivity analyses for the discretization size M and the adaptive candidate set construction; show how performance and runtime change with M.
  • Discuss stability/variance of the IPA gradient estimator and any variance-reduction techniques used.

- Theoretical clarity:
  • The differentiability assumptions required for the unbiased gradient (e.g., measure-zero ties in argmins, smoothness of kernel/mean) should be stated prominently in the main text with a brief intuition for why they hold in practice.
  • The paper focuses on one-step Bayes optimality. A short discussion of multi-batch horizons and myopic performance (and whether any performance guarantees or regret bounds are known/possible) would contextualize the decision-theoretic contribution.

Quality
Technically sound and well motivated. The reduction to parallel EI under special conditions is correct and illuminating. The IPA-based gradient estimator is a useful contribution. Experimental methodology could be strengthened in evaluation protocol, baselines, and breadth, and runtime reporting is needed to assess practical trade-offs.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The distinction between the continuous feasible domain A over which z(1:q) is optimized and the discretized set used to evaluate the inner minimum is helpful; consider emphasizing this early in Section 5. Minor typos: “Main Stange” → “Main Stage”; standardize capitalization (e.g., “Gaussian”). Consider a brief algorithmic complexity summary.

Originality
Extending KG to batch selection with an efficient differentiable estimator for joint optimization is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution beyond prior batch EI/UCB/ES methods and earlier discrete-space nested KG.

Significance
Batch BO is practically important. Given the empirical gains, especially under noise, q-KG is likely to be adopted in hyperparameter tuning and other expensive evaluation settings. Strengthening the experimental suite and computational analysis would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, Bayes-optimal one-step batch objective with a novel, efficient stochastic gradient estimator.
- Strong results on synthetic and noisy settings; public code.
- Clear exposition and relation to prior work.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited real-world evaluation breadth; missing widely used HPO benchmarks and some strong batch BO baselines.
- Evaluation protocol (optimizing test-set error) is problematic; lack of runtime comparisons.
- Noise assumptions may be restrictive for HPO without discussion or ablation.

Overall
A strong and elegant contribution on the acquisition side with promising empirical results, particularly under noise. I recommend acceptance if the authors (i) adopt a proper validation/testing protocol and report wall-clock overheads, and (ii) broaden baselines and tasks (or add ablations/sensitivity analyses) to bolster empirical evidence.
