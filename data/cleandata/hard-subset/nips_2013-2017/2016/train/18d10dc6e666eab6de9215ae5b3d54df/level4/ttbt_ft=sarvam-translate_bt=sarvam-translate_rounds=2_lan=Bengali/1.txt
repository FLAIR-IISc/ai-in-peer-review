This paper is about batch-sequential Bayesian optimization. It presents a batch-sequential version of the Knowledge gradient criterion. After some background on related work and Gaussian processes, the parallel (q-KG) criterion is defined. Its calculation is discussed in detail, following the standard KG criterion, and several numerical tests are presented where the q-KG seems to outperform some state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization algorithms. It's a very good paper, and I believe it has some important qualities that justify its publication in NIPS. The impact of Bayesian optimization algorithms on society is very significant. One minor criticism is that speed-up (from sequential to batch-sequential) research has not been done, and some statements in the literature review are slightly ambiguous; for example, the idea of combining EI against a background distribution was already present in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (with Constant Lauwer), where Chevalier and others presented CL-mixture [2]. Also, research has been done on q-EI maximization using natural gradients in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design". Finally, I have some comments and questions: Is A compact, is f continuous? Why is A primarily limited to an LHS? In Algorithm 1: What is said about re-estimating hyperparameters (or Bayesian updates)? In 5.2: Is it the maximum or minimum of g? Again, regarding 5.2: The smoothness of g goes without saying. Similarly, the derivative of the Cholesky factor may be ambiguous. Matérn is 5/2, not 2/5. Regarding Figure 1 and related tests: Was it possible to fit the same GP using different software?
