This work proposes a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization by computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next. The proposed method utilizes the knowledge gradient (q-KG) to measure the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations. To efficiently maximize the acquisition function, the authors present a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the gradient of q-KG. In three experiments, they demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on problems without observation noise and outperforms these methods on problems with observation noise.

While I appreciate the idea of performing parallel hyperparameter optimization to efficiently utilize multi-core environments, some aspects of the paper remain unclear, and I am not entirely convinced by the experimental results. Below, I provide feedback on each of the points above and pose some questions to the authors.

**Technical Quality**

The paper uses common artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. Additionally, the paper presents results for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression on Cifar10/MNIST. However, I would like to see an experiment showing wall-clock time or an analysis of the potential gains from using this parallel method compared to simple random search or sequential optimization. Furthermore, the choice of q=4 for all experiments is not justified, and I would appreciate an explanation for this decision.

**Novelty/Originality**

The paper proposes using the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize q-KG. Although both methods are not new, the paper refers to "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions," which already shows how to use IPA to select a batch of configurations using expected improvement. It would be beneficial to clearly state the differences between the proposed method and existing approaches.

**Impact/Usefulness**

Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for real-world applications across various disciplines, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

**Clarity/Presentation**

The technical details and background section are well-described, and the experiment section contains all necessary information to comprehend the performed comparisons.

**Questions to the Authors**

1. As Wang et al. (2015) seems to be very relevant to this work, and code is available online, have you compared your method to this approach? If so, please clarify the differences between your method and "parallel EI in MOE" to avoid confusion.
2. Modern multi-core systems allow for many more than 4 parallel runs. What insights do you have on the scalability of your method, and how much can be gained from more parallel runs?
3. Is it possible to extend your method to run asynchronously, similar to Spearmint?
4. Have you evaluated how your method compares to random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the overhead for computing a new batch?
5. In the plots, what does "iterations" on the x-label represent? Is it the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do not all methods start from the same value?

**Minor Comments**

* Equation (4.1) appears to be missing a z^(1:n) term.
* In the text, "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" should be "achieves."
* Parenthetical references should be rewritten as noun phrases, e.g., "[16] suggests parallelizing" should be "Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing."
* I believe "Wang et al. (2015)" is not the correct reference for IPA, as they do not introduce the method but rather apply it.
