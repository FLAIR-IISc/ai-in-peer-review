

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), extending the knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for selecting a batch of q points to evaluate, and propose an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize this acquisition function. The method is evaluated on both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, demonstrating consistent improvements over existing batch Bayesian optimization methods, particularly in noisy settings.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient method to the batch setting, providing a theoretically grounded acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the expected minimum of the posterior predictor when only one batch remains. Second, the authors develop an efficient computational strategy using IPA to estimate the gradient of the acquisition function, which is critical for practical implementation. Third, the experimental results demonstrate that q-KG outperforms existing batch BO methods, including parallel expected improvement (EI), batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a thorough review of related work that contextualizes the contribution within the broader Bayesian optimization literature. The background on Gaussian processes is concise yet sufficient for understanding the method. The algorithm description is precise, and the computational details in Section 5 are logically presented.

Regarding originality, the paper builds on the knowledge gradient framework but extends it to the batch setting in a theoretically sound manner. However, the novelty claim could be strengthened by more clearly differentiating from prior batch KG approaches. The draft review rightly points out that batch KG has been previously considered in Wang et al. (2015) [1] and Peng's thesis [2], and the authors should explicitly clarify how their work differs from these earlier contributions. Specifically, the paper should address whether their IPA-based optimization approach is more efficient than the methods proposed in these prior works, and provide either experimental comparisons or theoretical analysis to substantiate their computational advantage.

The paper's treatment of noisy function evaluations requires more careful consideration. As the draft review correctly notes, standard expected improvement (EI) assumes noiseless observations, making comparisons with EI in noisy settings potentially misleading. The authors should clarify whether they are using a noise-aware variant of EI or if they acknowledge this limitation in their comparison. If they are using standard EI without noise handling, they should discuss this limitation and its implications for the experimental results.

Section 5.2, which details the gradient estimation technique, is indeed the technical core of the paper. While the authors provide the theoretical foundation, more elaboration on the practical implementation challenges and computational complexity would strengthen this section. Specifically, they could include a brief discussion of how the IPA approach compares to alternative gradient estimation methods in terms of computational cost and accuracy.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, covering both synthetic functions and practical machine learning applications. The results consistently show that q-KG outperforms the benchmarks, with particularly significant improvements in noisy settings. The implementation details and comparison with open-source baselines (including parallel EI from the Metrics Optimization Engine and GP-UCB variants) are thorough and transparent.

The paper's significance is evident from its potential to accelerate hyperparameter tuning in machine learning, where parallel evaluations are common. The method's ability to handle noisy evaluations effectively addresses an important practical challenge in real-world applications.

In summary, this is a strong contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature with clear theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. The main areas for improvement are:

Strengths:
- Theoretically grounded batch acquisition function
- Efficient computational method using IPA
- Comprehensive experimental validation on synthetic and real-world problems
- Strong performance, especially in noisy settings

Weaknesses:
- Need for clearer differentiation from prior batch KG work (Wang et al., Peng's thesis)
- Insufficient discussion of EI's noise assumption in noisy settings
- Section 5.2 could benefit from more detailed explanation of the gradient estimation method
- IPA is redefined in line 162 without sufficient context

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. With the suggested clarifications regarding novelty and the noise assumption, it would be an excellent contribution to the field. The authors should address these points in the revision to strengthen their claims and provide a more complete picture of their method's advantages.
