This paper addresses the problem of batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization by introducing a parallel version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) criterion, termed q-KG. Following a review of Gaussian processes and related work, the paper defines the parallel q-KG acquisition function and details the computational procedure for its optimization, drawing parallels to the standard sequential KG criterion. The authors present a series of numerical experiments in which the proposed q-KG algorithm demonstrates superior performance compared to several state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization methods.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions that merit publication at NIPS. The development of effective methods for parallelizing Bayesian optimization is of considerable practical importance, and this work represents a valuable step in that direction.

The paper could be further improved by addressing a few minor points. One weakness is the absence of a study on the computational speed-ups achieved by the batch-sequential approach relative to its sequential counterpart. Additionally, the literature review contains a few minor imprecisions:
*   The concept of integrating Expected Improvement (EI) over posterior distributions, along with the Constant Liar heuristic, was previously introduced in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization."
*   The discussion of Chevalier et al. [2] should perhaps note that the authors specifically presented the CL-mix heuristic.
*   The optimization of q-EI using natural gradients was explored in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design."

Finally, I have the following questions and remarks for the authors:
1.  Are there implicit assumptions that the domain A is compact and the objective function f is continuous? Clarifying this would be helpful.
2.  The paper initializes the design using Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS). Is there a specific reason for this restriction, or could other space-filling designs be used?
3.  Regarding Algorithm 1, how are the Gaussian Process hyperparameters handled? Are they re-estimated at each iteration (e.g., via marginal likelihood maximization) or updated in a fully Bayesian manner?
4.  In Section 5.2, could the authors clarify whether the definition of the function `g` involves a maximization or a minimization?
5.  Also in Section 5.2, the smoothness of `g` is relied upon for optimization. A brief justification for this property would strengthen the argument.
6.  Similarly, the paper mentions computing the derivative of the Cholesky factor. As this is not a trivial operation, a reference or brief explanation would be beneficial for reproducibility.
7.  There appears to be a minor typo: the Matérn kernel should likely be 5/2, not 2/5.
8.  For the experiments presented in Figure 1, were the Gaussian Process models for all compared methods fitted using the same software and configuration? This would ensure that performance differences are attributable to the acquisition functions rather than implementation details.
