This paper addresses the critical challenge of parallelizing Bayesian optimization (BO), a widely adopted technique for tasks like hyperparameter tuning that is traditionally sequential. The authors introduce a novel and elegant algorithm for computing Bayes-optimal batches of points to evaluate in parallel. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, and the proposed method is shown to outperform existing parallel BO strategies in the provided experiments.

The parallelization of BO is a highly relevant problem for the machine learning community, particularly for accelerating practical hyperparameter optimization. The proposed approach is notable for its principled foundation, moving beyond common heuristics. The formulation of a Bayes-optimal batch is a significant theoretical contribution and a notable strength of this work.

A key assumption, common to many Gaussian process-based BO methods, is that of independent, normally distributed observation noise. While standard, this assumption can be a limitation in the context of hyperparameter optimization. The measurement error—often the difference between true generalization performance and an empirical estimate (e.g., via cross-validation)—can exhibit structural biases, particularly in certain regions of the hyperparameter space or when evaluated on small datasets. It would strengthen the paper to acknowledge that this assumption may not always hold in practice and briefly discuss its potential implications.

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the empirical evaluation could be significantly strengthened. The current experimental design has three main limitations:

1.  **Choice of Optimization Criterion:** The use of test set error as the optimization objective is a potential weakness. As argued by Provost et al. (1998), threshold-based metrics like accuracy or error rate can be suboptimal for model selection and hyperparameter tuning due to their sensitivity to class imbalance and decision thresholds. The evaluation would be more robust if it employed alternative metrics such as the area under the ROC curve (AUC) or log loss.

2.  **Benchmark Environment:** While MNIST and CIFAR-10 are standard datasets, the experiments would be more impactful and easily comparable if conducted on a dedicated hyperparameter optimization benchmark suite, such as HPOlib (Eggensperger et al., 2013). Using such a framework would contextualize the proposed method's performance against a wider and more standardized set of established baselines and problems.

3.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experimental scope is narrow. The evaluation is limited to two datasets, a single machine learning task (classification), and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and a CNN). The performance of optimization algorithms can vary significantly across different problem types and model architectures. A more diverse benchmark, including different tasks (e.g., regression) and a wider range of machine learning models, would be necessary to establish the general applicability and robustness of the proposed algorithm.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and elegant theoretical contribution to the important field of parallel Bayesian optimization. The core idea of computing Bayes-optimal batches is compelling. However, the empirical validation in its current form is not sufficiently comprehensive to be fully convincing of the method's practical superiority. The limitations in the choice of performance metrics, the lack of standardized benchmarks, and the narrow scope of the tested problems detract from the paper's overall impact. Despite these concerns regarding the experimental section, the theoretical novelty makes this a strong paper worth consideration.
