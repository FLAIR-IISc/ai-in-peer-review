Bayesian optimization is widely used for machine learning tasks such as hyperparameter tuning, yet most existing methods are fundamentally sequential. This paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that selects Bayes-optimal batches. The authors demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms current Bayesian optimization approaches. The manuscript is clearly written and easy to follow. Parallelizing BO is important for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed approach is both interesting and, in my view, more elegant than most alternatives I know. The ability to determine a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising.

The method assumes independent Gaussian noise, which is standard in GP-based BO. However, for hyperparameter optimization this assumption can be problematic, because measurement error reflects the gap between true generalization and empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation). Structural bias often arises in parts of the hyperparameter space, especially with limited data. It is worth noting that independence may not always be realistic.

The theoretical side of the paper is strong, but the experiments are somewhat underwhelming for three main reasons:
1. The reliance on test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic and is known to be suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Thresholded metrics like test error introduce several issues for model selection and HPO; metrics such as area under the ROC curve or log loss would be preferable.
2. The experiments use the MNIST and CIFAR-10 datasets, which are indeed common benchmarks. I would have preferred evaluations using HPOlib [2], a widely used library specifically designed for benchmarking hyperparameter optimization algorithms.
3. The number of datasets (2) is very small, and the entire benchmark focuses on a single task (classification) with only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and a CNN). More variety would strengthen the evaluation, since some optimizers perform well for certain types of learners but poorly for others.

Overall, I like the paper and the theoretical contribution is clearly valuable. However, the current benchmark is too limited to be fully convincing.

[1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998.
[2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
