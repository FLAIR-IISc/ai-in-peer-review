

The paper introduces a novel batch optimization algorithm combining stochastic gradient methods with Bayesian modeling for multivariate objective functions over finite domains. The key innovation lies in its parallelizable selection of multiple sampling points per iteration, extending the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework to a batch setting. Specifically, the authors generalize KG to select a set of points simultaneously, rather than sequentially, by estimating the expected improvement in the objective value from adding multiple points. To address the computational intractability of optimizing the batch KG criterion, the authors propose an efficient method leveraging Monte Carlo sampling and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients for maximizing information gain over candidate sets. Empirical results on benchmark problems demonstrate that the proposed method either outperforms or competes strongly with existing approaches, particularly those based on Confidence Bounds. The paper is well-structured and presents a method with clear practical relevance.  

However, the novelty appears limited, as the core contribution is an extension of the established KG framework to batch selection—a natural generalization that requires addressing computational complexity. While the development of the IPA-based gradient estimator for the q-KG criterion is technically sound, this technique itself is not novel and aligns with standard practices in Bayesian optimization. The primary technical contribution lies in the adaptation of IPA to this specific batch setting, but this alone does not constitute a substantial advance over prior work.  

A specific technical concern arises in Equation (3.1): since $x^{(1:n)}$ denotes a finite sequence of vectors, it is unclear how $\mu(x^{(1:n)})$ and $K(x^{(1:n)}, x^{(1:n)})$ are defined, as $\mu$ and $K$ are functions of individual vectors (or pairs thereof), not sequences. The authors should clarify the domain of these functions or adjust the notation to avoid ambiguity.
