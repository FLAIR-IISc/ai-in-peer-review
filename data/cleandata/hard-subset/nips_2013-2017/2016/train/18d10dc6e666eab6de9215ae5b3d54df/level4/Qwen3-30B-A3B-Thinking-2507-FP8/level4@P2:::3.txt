

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) that extends the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) approach to the parallel setting. The authors address the common scenario where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously, such as when tuning hyperparameters across multiple machines. Building on the decision-theoretical foundation of KG, they develop a batch acquisition function that jointly optimizes q points to maximize expected information gain. The key technical contribution is an efficient gradient estimation method using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), which overcomes the computational intractability of directly maximizing the q-KG acquisition function.

The paper relates well to prior NIPS work, particularly the sequential KG method by Frazier et al. (2009) and recent parallel Bayesian optimization approaches like parallel EI (Snoek et al., 2012) and GP-UCB (Desautels et al., 2012). Unlike previous parallel methods that construct batches iteratively by optimizing single-point acquisition functions (e.g., [10, 18, 25]), the q-KG method considers the entire batch collectively, which the authors demonstrate leads to more efficient exploration.

The paper excels in Quality. The technical development is sound, with clear theoretical justification for the q-KG acquisition function and rigorous computational methods for gradient estimation. The experimental validation is comprehensive, testing on both synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical machine learning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show q-KG outperforms or matches state-of-the-art benchmarks, particularly in noisy settings where it demonstrates significantly larger gains. The implementation details are thorough, and the authors provide code on GitHub, enhancing reproducibility.

Clarity is strong throughout. The paper is well-organized with clear section headings, and the mathematical notation is carefully defined. The authors effectively explain the intuition behind their approach before presenting the technical details. The writing is accessible to Bayesian optimization researchers while maintaining necessary mathematical rigor. The experimental setup is well-motivated and the results are presented with appropriate statistical measures (mean and standard deviation across multiple runs).

Regarding Originality, the paper extends a well-established sequential method (KG) to the parallel setting, which is a meaningful contribution. While the core concept of batch Bayesian optimization isn't entirely novel (see [3, 5, 18, 25]), the authors make a significant methodological advance by developing an efficient computational approach for the batch setting. The IPA-based gradient estimation technique is particularly noteworthy as it addresses a key computational bottleneck. The paper distinguishes itself from prior work by emphasizing the decision-theoretic optimality of their approach (Bayes-optimal for minimizing simple regret) rather than focusing on regret minimization, which is more relevant to BO applications.

The Significance of this work is substantial. Efficient batch Bayesian optimization has practical value in hyperparameter tuning for deep learning, where parallel evaluation across multiple machines is common. The demonstrated performance gains, especially in noisy settings (where the authors achieve a 14% to 11% test error improvement on CIFAR10), make this method immediately applicable to real-world machine learning pipelines. The approach could accelerate hyperparameter tuning for complex models like CNNs, potentially reducing the time and computational resources required.

The main weakness is that the novelty might be perceived as incremental by some reviewers, as the paper builds directly on established KG theory. However, the authors convincingly demonstrate that the computational challenges of the batch setting require non-trivial extensions beyond straightforward adaptation of sequential methods. The IPA-based gradient estimation technique is sophisticated and represents a meaningful contribution to the computational toolkit for batch Bayesian optimization.

I have one specific technical concern regarding equation (3.1), where the notation μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n),x^(1:n)) is ambiguous. Since x^(1:n) represents a sequence of points rather than a single vector, the authors should clarify how these functions of sequences are defined (e.g., as vectors of function values or matrices of pairwise kernel evaluations).

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to Bayesian optimization. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrated to be effective in both synthetic and practical settings. The authors have carefully addressed the computational challenges of batch optimization through a novel IPA-based approach, and their experimental results provide convincing evidence of the method's superiority over existing approaches. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and would be a valuable addition to the proceedings. I recommend acceptance.
