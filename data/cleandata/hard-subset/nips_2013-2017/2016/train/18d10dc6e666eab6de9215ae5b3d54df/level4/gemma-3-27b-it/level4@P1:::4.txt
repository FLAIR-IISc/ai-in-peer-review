This paper considers the parallel knowledge gradient method for batch Bayesian Optimization. The authors address the important problem of efficiently optimizing expensive black-box functions, particularly in scenarios where function evaluations can be performed in parallel – for example, when evaluating different neural network architectures. They propose a novel acquisition function, termed q-KG, designed to select a batch of `q` points for simultaneous evaluation. Furthermore, they provide an efficient computational strategy for optimizing this acquisition function. Empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning problems.

The core idea of considering multiple points for parallel evaluation is well-motivated and a valuable contribution. Leveraging parallelization can significantly accelerate Bayesian optimization, and the proposed q-KG method offers a principled approach to batch selection. The derivation of the acquisition function from a decision-theoretic perspective is sound, and the development of an efficient gradient-based optimization technique for q-KG is crucial for its practical applicability. The experiments demonstrate competitive performance, especially in noisy evaluation settings.

However, it would be valuable to discuss the limitations of relying on a Gaussian process (GP) model, especially when dealing with high-dimensional input spaces often encountered in modern machine learning applications like neural network architecture search. While GPs are flexible, their computational complexity can become prohibitive in high dimensions, and their representational capacity might be insufficient to accurately model complex functions. Exploring alternatives or modifications to the GP model could strengthen the work.  Additionally, providing theoretical results regarding error bounds for the method, or a quantitative analysis of its computational complexity as a function of input dimension, would further solidify its theoretical foundations.

The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, a few minor clarifications would improve readability. Specifically:

*   Line 36: “the set of points to evaluate next that is…” could be streamlined to “the next set of points to evaluate which…”
*   Line 95: The definition of the set A should be explicitly stated earlier in the paper for clarity.
*   Line 104: Clarifying the input of µ<sup>n</sup>(x) would be helpful.
*   Line 128: A brief discussion of the parallel EI algorithm (mentioned in the comparison) would provide valuable context for readers unfamiliar with the method.
*   Line 140:  Using distinct notations for the two instances of 'A' (the feasible domain and the discretization set) could prevent confusion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to parallel Bayesian optimization.
*   Sound theoretical foundation based on decision theory.
*   Efficient computational method for optimizing the q-KG acquisition function.
*   Strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world problems, particularly in noisy settings.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of discussion regarding the limitations of using a Gaussian process model in high-dimensional spaces.
*   Absence of theoretical results (error bounds, complexity analysis).
*   Minor clarity issues with notation and explanations (as noted above).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. While addressing the limitations regarding the GP model and adding theoretical analysis would further strengthen the paper, the novel approach, efficient implementation, and strong empirical results warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to address the clarity points mentioned above.
