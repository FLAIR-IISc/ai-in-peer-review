

This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization method using a knowledge gradient (q-KG) acquisition function, optimized via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The approach computes a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations for parallel evaluation, with experiments showing competitive performance on noise-free benchmarks and superior results on noisy problems. While the core idea of parallel hyperparameter optimization is valuable for multi-core environments, the paper’s technical claims and experimental validation require significant strengthening.

**Technical Quality**  
The experimental section relies heavily on artificial benchmarks and limited real-world tasks (CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR-10/MNIST). Crucially, the paper lacks wall-clock time comparisons against random search or sequential Bayesian optimization (e.g., Spearmint), which is essential for assessing practical utility. Without this, the claimed efficiency gains remain unverified. Additionally, the choice of \(q = 4\) for all experiments is unjustified—no analysis explores how batch size impacts performance or scalability. The paper should demonstrate how the method scales beyond \(q = 4\) (e.g., to 10+ parallel runs, as in prior work [12,16]), especially given modern multi-core systems routinely support far more parallel evaluations.

**Novelty/Originality**  
The paper presents q-KG with IPA as novel, but both components are already established in Wang et al. (2015) for expected improvement (q-EI). The work does not clarify how q-KG differs from Wang’s approach (e.g., is IPA applied to a new acquisition function, or is it a reimplementation?). The reference to "Parallel Bayesian global optimization" (Wang et al.) should explicitly distinguish this work from prior q-EI methods. If the contribution is limited to applying IPA to q-KG (rather than q-EI), this must be clearly stated to avoid confusion with existing literature. The novelty claim is therefore overstated without a precise technical differentiator.

**Impact/Usefulness**  
Parallel BO is indeed relevant for real-world applications. However, the paper’s failure to address wall-clock time—especially the overhead of batch computation versus sequential evaluation—undermines its practical impact. For instance, if computing a batch takes 5× longer than a single evaluation, parallelism offers no benefit for small \(q\). The authors should quantify this trade-off and compare against standard baselines (e.g., random search, sequential BO) in terms of *time-to-solution*.

**Clarity and Presentation**  
The technical exposition is generally clear, but critical ambiguities persist:  
- The x-axis in Figure 3 (labeled "iterations") is undefined—does it denote function evaluations or batches? If batches, why do methods start at different points (e.g., Spearmint vs. q-KG)?  
- Equation (4.1) likely omits \(z^{(1:n)}\), requiring correction.  
- Minor grammatical errors exist (e.g., "achieve" → "achieves," "[16] suggests" → "Snoek et al. [16] suggest").

**Key Questions for Authors**  
1. **Comparison to Wang et al. (2015):** The paper cites Wang et al. but does not compare to their q-EI method (which uses IPA). Why? If this is the same approach applied to KG, it must be explicitly acknowledged and contrasted. If not, clarify the technical distinction.  
2. **Scalability beyond \(q = 4\):** Prior work (e.g., ICML’15, LION’12, NIPS’12) uses \(q \geq 10\). What is the computational cost of larger \(q\)? Does performance plateau or degrade?  
3. **Asynchronous support:** Spearmint operates asynchronously. Can the proposed method be adapted for asynchronous execution (e.g., handling delayed evaluations)?  
4. **Wall-clock time analysis:** How does the method’s *total runtime* compare to random search or sequential BO? What is the overhead for batch computation?  
5. **Experimental metrics:** Clarify the meaning of "iterations" in figures and whether initial designs are included in the x-axis.

**Minor Comments**  
- Correct "achieve" to "achieves" in Section 3.  
- Fix citation style: "Wang et al. (2015)" → "Wang et al. [16]" (per conference conventions).  
- The claim that "Wang et al. (2015) is the correct reference for IPA" is incorrect; Wang *applies* IPA but does not introduce it. Cite the original IPA work (e.g., Glasserman & Yao [1992]) if relevant.

**Conclusion**  
The paper addresses a practical problem but lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims. The experimental design omits critical metrics (wall-clock time, scalability analysis), and the novelty is overstated due to insufficient differentiation from Wang et al. (2015). To strengthen the work, the authors must: (1) include wall-clock time comparisons against baselines, (2) justify \(q = 4\) or analyze scaling, (3) clarify the relationship to prior q-EI work, and (4) resolve ambiguities in the experiments. Without these revisions, the contribution’s significance remains unclear.
