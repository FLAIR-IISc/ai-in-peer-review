Bayesian optimization is a widely adopted technique for tasks such as hyperparameter optimization; however, many existing methods are inherently sequential. This paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm designed to efficiently compute batches approximating Bayes-optimality. The authors demonstrate that their proposed method achieves superior performance compared to current Bayesian optimization approaches. The paper is clearly written and accessible. Parallelization of Bayesian optimization is a crucial area for practical hyperparameter optimization, and this approach is both interesting and, in my assessment, more refined than many existing solutions. The determination of a Bayes-optimal batch is a particularly promising aspect.

The authors appropriately leverage the common assumption of independent, normally distributed errors, typical in Gaussian process-based Bayesian optimization. Nevertheless, this assumption warrants discussion in the context of hyperparameter optimization, where measurement errors often reflect the discrepancy between true generalization performance and empirical estimates obtained through methods like cross-validation. Structural bias is frequently observed in hyperparameter spaces, particularly with limited sample sizes, and relaxing the independence assumption could be beneficial. The theoretical foundations of the paper are strong; however, the experimental evaluation presents some limitations. 

Specifically, I have three primary concerns. First, the use of test set error as the optimization criterion is questionable, as it is known to be a suboptimal choice for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss would offer a more robust and informative evaluation, avoiding the issues inherent in threshold-based metrics for model selection and optimization. Second, while the use of MNIST and CIFAR10 is standard, I would have preferred to see results presented using the HPOlib benchmark suite [2], a widely-used resource specifically designed for evaluating hyperparameter optimization algorithms. Third, the scope of the benchmark is limited, utilizing only two datasets and focusing solely on classification with logistic regression and CNNs. A more comprehensive benchmark, incorporating a wider variety of datasets and machine learning algorithms, would be more representative, given the known sensitivity of optimizer performance to the underlying task and model. 

In summary, I find this paper to be valuable, with a definite and significant theoretical contribution. However, the current experimental results, due to the limited scope of the benchmark, are not entirely convincing. [1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998. [2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
