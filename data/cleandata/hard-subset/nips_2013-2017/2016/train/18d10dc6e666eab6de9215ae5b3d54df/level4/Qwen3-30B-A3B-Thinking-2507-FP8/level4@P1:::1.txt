

This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, extending the knowledge gradient (KG) criterion to the parallel setting. The authors propose a decision-theoretic acquisition function that selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously, which is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the expected minimum of the Gaussian process predictor when only one batch remains. The paper provides an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis to optimize the q-KG acquisition function, demonstrating superior performance over existing batch Bayesian optimization methods on both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly when function evaluations are noisy.

The paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian optimization by providing a theoretically grounded approach to batch selection. While prior work has considered parallel Bayesian optimization (e.g., parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), the q-KG method differs fundamentally in that it optimizes the batch collectively rather than iteratively. The authors correctly cite related work, noting that while Chevalier et al. (2013) and Ginsbourger et al. (2010) introduced parallel EI variants like "Constant Liar," and Marmin et al. (2015) studied differentiating multipoint expected improvement, their approach is distinct in its decision-theoretic foundation and collective optimization.

Key strengths include the method's theoretical soundness, computational efficiency through infinitesimal perturbation analysis, and compelling empirical validation. The q-KG approach consistently outperforms state-of-the-art batch methods on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN tuning on CIFAR10), with particularly pronounced advantages in noisy settings. The paper's experimental design is thorough, with appropriate baselines, multiple test functions, and real-world applications demonstrating practical relevance.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from theoretical development to empirical validation. The authors provide sufficient detail about the computational approach (Section 5) while maintaining readability. The implementation is made available, enhancing reproducibility.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The paper does not explicitly analyze the speed-up achieved by parallelization (e.g., wall-clock time improvements compared to sequential methods).
2. The initial Latin hypercube sampling strategy is used without thorough justification compared to alternative initial sampling approaches.
3. Some technical details in Section 5 (particularly regarding the smoothness assumptions required for gradient estimation) could be clarified for readers unfamiliar with infinitesimal perturbation analysis.
4. The Matérn 5/2 kernel is mentioned, but the draft review's reference to "Matérn is 5/2 and not 2/5" appears to be a typo that should be corrected.

The paper's significance is substantial. Batch Bayesian optimization is increasingly important in modern machine learning, where parallel evaluations across multiple GPUs or computing nodes are common. The q-KG method provides a theoretically grounded approach that consistently outperforms existing methods, offering practitioners a more efficient tool for hyperparameter tuning and other optimization tasks where function evaluations are expensive.

The paper makes a clear contribution distinct from prior work. While previous approaches like "Constant Liar" and GP-BUCB optimize batches iteratively, q-KG jointly optimizes the entire batch, resulting in better performance, particularly in noisy settings. The authors correctly acknowledge related work, including Chevalier et al.'s parallel EI and Marmin et al.'s gradient-based approach.

The paper would be strengthened by:
- Briefly discussing theoretical guarantees (e.g., regret bounds)
- Clarifying the relationship between q-KG and expected improvement when the final solution is restricted to evaluated points
- Providing more explicit comparison of computational complexity with baselines

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to Bayesian optimization. The theoretical foundation is sound, the computational approach is efficient, and the empirical results are compelling. The method addresses an important practical problem with clear potential impact on machine learning applications. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Pro for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution, clear empirical validation demonstrating consistent advantages over baselines, particularly in noisy settings, significant practical relevance to modern machine learning applications.

Con for acceptance: Minor gaps in theoretical analysis (regret bounds), limited discussion of computational complexity, initial sampling strategy could be better justified.

Given the paper's strong theoretical foundation, clear empirical validation, and significant practical impact, I recommend acceptance. The work represents an important advancement in batch Bayesian optimization that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners in the field.
