- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization method maximizing a parallel knowledge gradient acquisition for joint selection of q points.
- Decision-theoretic motivation: q-KG is Bayes-optimal when only one batch remains, mirroring sequential KG.
- Uses discretization of the search space for acquisition evaluation and IPA-based stochastic gradients to jointly optimize batches.
- Empirical results: competitive in noise-free cases and notably better in noisy settings versus parallel EI (MOE, Spearmint), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic and hyperparameter tasks.
- Builds on sequential KG, parallel EI/PES, and UCB-based batch policies; key novelty is extending KG to parallel decisions with efficient gradient estimation.
- Theoretical foundation is sound; reduction to parallel EI in the noiseless sampled-points case is correct.
- Monte Carlo evaluation with IPA gradients is plausible and consistent with prior q-EI optimization approaches.
- Empirical evaluation omits wall-clock runtimes and computational overhead analysis, which is critical for batch BO.
- Experiments fix q=4 and do not study scalability or performance for larger batch sizes.
- Discretization strategy may introduce bias; no sensitivity analysis w.r.t. discretization size M or sampling scheme is provided.
- Paper is generally well written with clear background, methods, and reproducible empirical setup, though minor notational/style issues remain.
- Novelty is incremental: applying KG to batches is natural and IPA-based optimization has prior use for q-EI.
- Demonstrated robustness to noise is practically significant, particularly for hyperparameter tuning in parallel environments.
- Lack of wall-clock and scalability experiments reduces practical impact and leaves tradeoffs unclear.
- Strength: principled Bayes-optimal acquisition for single-step batch decisions.
- Strength: solid handling of noisy evaluations with clear empirical benefits.
- Strength: joint non-greedy batch selection with efficient stochastic gradient estimator and comparisons to multiple baselines; code released.
- Weakness: no wall-clock or acquisition overhead analysis, so sample-efficiency vs time tradeoffs are unknown.
- Weakness: scalability to large q not explored; all experiments use q=4.
- Weakness: limited discussion of asynchronous execution and integration into real parallel systems.
- Weakness: incremental computational novelty relative to IPA-optimized q-EI; relationship to related methods needs clarification.
- Weakness: reliance on discretization without sensitivity analysis may bias acquisition evaluation.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled extension of KG to batch settings, strong noisy-performance, clear methodology with adoption potential.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing wall-clock and scalability studies, moderate computational novelty, ambiguous comparisons to prior q-EI implementations.
- Key questions for authors: clarify comparisons to IPA-optimized q-EI (MOE), report scalability and runtimes for larger q, describe extensions to asynchronous settings, provide wall-clock comparisons versus random/sequential BO, and clarify plotting axes/initialization.
- Minor comments: check conditioning in Eq.(4.1), fix grammatical errors, use named citations in prose, cite foundational IPA references, and clarify/discuss discretization scheme and sensitivity.
- Overall: solid, well-presented contribution with clear advantages in noisy settings but weakened by limited scalability, wall-clock, and asynchronous evaluations; additional experiments/clarifications would strengthen the paper.
