This paper studies the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization in settings where function evaluations are expensive. The authors introduce an acquisition function that selects multiple points per iteration for parallel evaluation and outline an efficient procedure to compute and optimize this criterion. Empirical studies on synthetic benchmarks and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks demonstrate strong performance, particularly under observation noise, and comparisons against established batch BO methods (e.g., parallel EI and UCB variants) indicate faster progress toward the global optimum.

The problem addressed is important: enabling concurrent evaluations can substantially accelerate data-driven workflows. By proposing a q-point knowledge gradient that recommends a batch of locations each round, the approach can improve efficiency over strictly sequential designs. The idea is interesting and timely for parallel computing environments.

There are, however, some concerns and potential improvements. It is not clear that a Gaussian process prior is always appropriate in high-dimensional settings such as neural network hyperparameter spaces. The paper would benefit from theoretical analysis quantifying error bounds and providing complexity guarantees with respect to dimensionality. Such results would clarify when the approach is expected to perform well and how it scales.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to Bayesian optimization. The methodology appears sound, and the experimental evaluation is careful and convincing. The paper is clearly written.
