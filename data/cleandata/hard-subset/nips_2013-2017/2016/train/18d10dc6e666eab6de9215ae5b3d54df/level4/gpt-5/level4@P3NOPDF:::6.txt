Summary:
The paper proposes a method for parallel batch Bayesian optimization that selects a Bayes-optimal batch of points via the q-knowledge gradient (q-KG) acquisition function. The authors develop an estimator for the gradient of q-KG using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling efficient stochastic optimization of the acquisition function. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and two ML tasks (CNN on CIFAR-10 and logistic regression on MNIST) show that q-KG is competitive on noise-free problems and outperforms existing batch methods when observations are noisy.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important practical setting: parallel Bayesian optimization under observation noise.
- The use of knowledge gradient provides a principled measure of the value of information for a batch.
- The IPA-based gradient estimator is technically sound and allows scalable optimization of an otherwise challenging acquisition function.
- The paper is generally well written and the technical background is clear.

Concerns and areas for improvement:
- Evaluation lacks wall-clock comparisons and an analysis of computational overhead. For practitioners, the end-to-end time to good solutions relative to simpler baselines (random search, sequential BO, q-EI) is critical.
- Batch size is fixed to q=4 in all experiments. Modern compute environments often support much larger parallelism. The paper does not study how performance scales with q or provide guidance on choosing q.
- Novelty relative to prior work is not fully established. Knowledge gradient and IPA are not new; the relation to prior batch methods, especially those using IPA for expected improvement (e.g., Wang et al., 2015), should be clarified both conceptually and empirically.
- The method appears to be synchronous only. Many real-world scenarios require asynchronous parallelization due to variable evaluation times.
- Some experimental details and figure axes are unclear (e.g., definition of “iterations” and whether initial designs are included).

Technical quality:
The methodology is sound, and the acquisition optimization via IPA is well motivated. The experimental suite includes standard synthetic functions and two real ML tasks, which is commendable. However:
- There is no analysis of computational cost per batch (e.g., time to optimize q-KG vs. time to evaluate the objective), number of Monte Carlo samples used, or variance of the gradient estimator. Without this, it is difficult to assess the practical efficiency of the approach.
- The absence of wall-clock time comparisons makes it hard to judge the real benefits over simpler baselines.
- The choice of q=4 is not justified; no sensitivity analysis is provided.
- It is unclear whether comparisons include the most relevant baselines (e.g., q-EI as implemented in MOE, Spearmint’s parallel heuristic).

Novelty and originality:
Using KG for batch selection and applying IPA to optimize q-KG are logical extensions of known ideas. Prior work has already employed IPA to optimize batch EI. The main novelty appears to be formulating and optimizing the batch knowledge gradient specifically for noisy settings. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what is new (the acquisition, the gradient estimator, theoretical properties) and from direct empirical comparisons to closely related methods (q-EI with IPA).

Impact and usefulness:
Parallel hyperparameter optimization is important across ML, vision, robotics, and beyond. A robust method that handles noise well is valuable. The potential impact is significant if the method proves competitive in wall-clock terms and scales to larger batch sizes. As presented, the practical impact is promising but not fully demonstrated.

Clarity and presentation:
The paper is generally clear and self-contained, with a solid background section and sufficient experimental detail to understand the setup. Figures and certain experimental choices could be clearer (see questions below), and there are minor writing issues noted in the minor comments.

Questions to the authors:
1) Baselines and naming: Did you compare against Wang et al. (2015) and q-EI as implemented in MOE? If so, please report results explicitly and use consistent terminology (e.g., “q-EI”) to avoid confusion with Spearmint’s parallel heuristic. Please clarify the methodological differences among these approaches.
2) Scalability with batch size: Many systems support far more than 4 concurrent evaluations (e.g., DNGO with up to 800, SMAC with 25, Spearmint with up to 10). How does your method scale with q in terms of (a) optimization difficulty of q-KG, (b) computational overhead, and (c) optimization performance? Please provide results or a discussion, and guidance on choosing q.
3) Asynchrony: Can the approach be extended to an asynchronous setting where completed evaluations are incorporated immediately and the remaining batch is updated? If so, how would q-KG and its gradient estimation change?
4) Wall-clock time: How does your method compare to random search and sequential BO in total wall-clock time to reach a given objective value? What is the overhead of computing each batch (including Monte Carlo and optimization), and how does it scale with dimension, q, and the number of pending points?
5) Plot axes and initial design: In your figures, what does “iterations” denote—number of function evaluations or number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design points? If yes, why do different methods start from different initial values?

Minor comments:
- Equation (4.1): It appears a z^(1:n) term may be missing; please check the notation.
- “This architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” → “...and achieves a test error...”.
- Parenthetical references should not be used as nouns. For example, “[16] suggests parallelizing” should be “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing.”
- IPA attribution: “Wang et al. (2015)” should not be cited as introducing IPA; they apply it. Please cite foundational IPA references.
- Please justify the choice of q=4 or provide a sensitivity analysis.
- Include details on the number of Monte Carlo samples, optimization tolerances, number of restarts, and any variance-reduction techniques used for the gradient estimator.
- Consider adding a small ablation comparing q-KG to q-EI and to sequential KG under varying noise levels to isolate the contribution of the acquisition choice.

Overall assessment:
A well-motivated and technically solid approach with promising results under noise, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by clearer positioning against prior work, wall-clock and scalability evaluations, and clarification of experimental details.
