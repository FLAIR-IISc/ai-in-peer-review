This paper derives a batch version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) method for Bayesian optimization. The core idea is to formulate a parallel acquisition function that selects the batch of points to evaluate next based on a decision-theoretic analysis, aiming for Bayes-optimality in the one-step-ahead sense. To address the computational challenges of maximizing this acquisition function, the authors propose an efficient gradient estimation strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) combined with Monte Carlo simulation. Empirical results on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning demonstrate that the proposed method, termed the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), outperforms several existing batch Bayesian optimization algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

The paper builds upon the existing literature in Bayesian optimization, particularly extending the sequential knowledge gradient method to the parallel batch setting. While batch BO has seen increased attention, efficiently maximizing acquisition functions for batch selection remains a challenge. This work addresses this by providing a practical gradient-based approach for optimizing the proposed q-KG acquisition function. The connection to previous work, including extensions of Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) to the parallel setting, is clearly outlined in Section 2.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretically sound derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and the development of an efficient optimization strategy. The use of IPA to estimate the gradient is a significant contribution, enabling scalability to higher-dimensional problems and larger batch sizes. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of q-KG, particularly its robustness to noisy function evaluations. The code availability further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

However, there are some areas for improvement. The novelty of the batch KG approach requires further clarification. Specifically, the authors should more explicitly acknowledge and differentiate their work from prior efforts in batch KG, such as Wang et al. (2015) and the work referenced in the Princeton thesis linked in the original review, detailing the specific contributions of their gradient estimation approach in comparison to previous methods. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed IPA-based gradient estimation would also be beneficial. Expanding Section 5.2 to provide greater intuition and detail regarding the IPA derivation would strengthen the paper. The comparison with EI in noisy settings warrants further discussion; acknowledging that standard EI doesn't inherently handle noise and potentially comparing against a noise-aware variant of EI would provide a fairer assessment.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to batch Bayesian optimization.
*   The proposed q-KG method demonstrates strong empirical performance, particularly in noisy settings.
*   The development of an efficient gradient estimation strategy using IPA is a significant contribution.
*   The code is publicly available, promoting reproducibility and impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the batch KG approach could be more clearly articulated with respect to prior work.
*   The explanation of the IPA-based gradient estimation could be more detailed.
*   The comparison with EI in noisy settings could be strengthened by considering noise-aware alternatives.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. Addressing the minor concerns outlined above would further enhance the quality and impact of the work. I recommend acceptance.
