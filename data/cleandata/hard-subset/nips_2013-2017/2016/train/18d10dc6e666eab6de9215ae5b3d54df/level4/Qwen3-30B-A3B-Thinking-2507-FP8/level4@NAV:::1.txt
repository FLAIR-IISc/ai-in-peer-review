

This paper presents a batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization approach centered on a novel batch-sequential Knowledge Gradient criterion. After establishing foundational background on related work and Gaussian processes, the authors formally define the parallel (q-KG) criterion, detailing its computation via an extension of the standard KG framework. Numerical experiments demonstrate that q-KG achieves superior performance compared to state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization methods. This constitutes a strong contribution with significant potential impact, particularly regarding the societal benefits of parallelizing Bayesian optimization. The paper merits publication at NIPS due to its technical rigor and relevance. A minor critique concerns the absence of speed-up analysis when transitioning from sequential to batch-sequential execution, alongside slight imprecisions in the literature review: specifically, the integration of Expected Improvement (EI) with posterior distributions was previously addressed in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (alongside Constant Liar), while Chevalier et al. notably introduced CL-mix in [2]; additionally, q-EI maximization using natural gradient was explored in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design." The following remarks and questions are provided:  
1. Is the domain $A$ compact and $f$ continuous?  
2. Why is $A$ restricted to a Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) at initialization?  
3. Algorithm 1 omits hyperparameter re-estimation (or Bayesian updating) – how is this handled?  
4. In Section 5.2, is the definition of $g$ a maximization or minimization?  
5. The smoothness of $g$ requires justification – it is not self-evident.  
6. The derivative of the Cholesky factor is non-trivial; is this elaborated?  
7. Matérn is specified as 5/2, not 2/5.  
8. For Figure 1 experiments: Could equivalent Gaussian Processes be fitted using alternative software?
