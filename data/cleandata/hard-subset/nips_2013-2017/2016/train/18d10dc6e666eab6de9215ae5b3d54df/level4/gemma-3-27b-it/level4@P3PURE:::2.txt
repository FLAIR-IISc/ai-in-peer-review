Bayesian optimization has gained significant popularity for tasks such as hyperparameter optimization, but most existing approaches are inherently sequential. This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes batches in a Bayes-optimal manner. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms existing Bayesian optimization approaches. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. Parallelization of Bayesian optimization is a crucial topic for practical hyperparameter optimization, and this approach is both interesting and more elegant than many I have encountered. The determination of a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising. The authors adopt the common assumption of independent, normally distributed errors, often used in Gaussian process-based Bayesian optimization methods. However, this assumption can be problematic in the context of hyperparameter optimization, where measurement errors reflect the difference between true generalization performance and empirical estimates obtained through techniques like cross-validation. Structural bias is frequently present in hyperparameter space, especially with limited sample sizes, and it is worth noting that the assumption of independence may not always be realistic. 

The theoretical foundations of this work are strong, but the experimental evaluation is somewhat disappointing for three key reasons. First, using test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic. It is well-established that such threshold-based scoring functions are suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Metrics like test set error introduce complexities for model selection and hyperparameter optimization; using metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss would be preferable. Second, the authors utilize MNIST and CIFAR-10 as experimental datasets, which are standard benchmarks. However, I would have preferred to see results reported using HPOlib [2], a widely-used library specifically designed for benchmarking hyperparameter optimization algorithms. Third, the benchmark’s limited scope – only two datasets and a single machine learning task (classification) with two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN) – is concerning. The field recognizes that the performance of optimizers can vary significantly depending on the learning algorithm and task, and a more diverse benchmark would provide a more robust evaluation.

Overall, this is a valuable paper with a significant theoretical contribution. However, the current benchmark is insufficiently comprehensive to be fully convincing. 

[1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998.
[2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
