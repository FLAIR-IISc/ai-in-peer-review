This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), which generalizes the knowledge gradient criterion to the parallel setting. The authors provide a thorough background on related work and Gaussian processes, define the q-KG criterion, and detail its computation. The paper also presents several numerical experiments where q-KG outperforms state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization algorithms.

The paper is well-written, and the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the subject matter. The potential impact of parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms on society is significant, and this paper makes an important contribution to this field.

However, there are a few minor criticisms and questions that need to be addressed. Firstly, the authors do not study the speed-ups obtained by parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms, which is an important aspect of this research. Additionally, some statements in the literature review are slightly imprecise. For instance, the idea of integrating expected improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was already present in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (along with Constant Liar), and Chevalier et al. presented notably CL-mix in [2]. Furthermore, q-EI maximization using natural gradient is studied in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design".

There are also some technical questions and remarks. Is the feasible domain A compact, and is the function f continuous? Why do the authors restrict A to be a Latin hypercube design (LHS) at the initial stage? In Algorithm 1, what about hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating? In Section 5.2, is it a maximum or a minimum in the definition of g? The smoothness of g does not seem to be obvious, and the derivative of the Cholesky factor might be non-trivial. Additionally, the kernel used in the experiments is the Matérn 5/2 kernel, not the 2/5 kernel.

Finally, regarding Figure 1 and the corresponding experiments, was it possible to fit the same Gaussian processes using different software? This would help to verify the results and ensure that they are not dependent on a specific implementation.

In conclusion, this is a very good paper that makes an important contribution to the field of batch-sequential Bayesian optimization. With some minor revisions to address the criticisms and questions mentioned above, this paper has the potential to be published in NIPS.
