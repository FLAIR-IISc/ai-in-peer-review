### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a new method for batch Bayesian optimization, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). This method extends the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to a parallel setting, allowing for the simultaneous selection of a batch of `q` points for evaluation. The core principle is to choose the batch of points that provides the greatest one-step expected improvement in the final solution quality, as measured by the posterior mean of the Gaussian Process model.

A key technical contribution is an efficient method for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function. As direct optimization is computationally intensive, the authors propose a gradient-based approach. They leverage Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased Monte Carlo estimator for the gradient of the q-KG factor, enabling the use of standard gradient-based optimizers to find the optimal batch.

The authors conduct experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression and convolutional neural networks. The results demonstrate that the proposed q-KG method is competitive with or outperforms other state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization algorithms, such as parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and parallel Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) variants. The performance advantage of q-KG is shown to be particularly pronounced in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function is a logical extension of the sequential KG framework, and the decision-theoretic motivation is clear. The use of IPA to estimate the gradient is a clever approach to make the optimization of the acquisition function tractable. The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering both synthetic and real-world problems in noise-free and noisy settings. The provision of open-source code is a significant strength that facilitates reproducibility.

However, a potential weakness lies in the fairness of the experimental comparisons in the noisy setting. The standard Expected Improvement (EI) acquisition function is derived under the assumption of noise-free observations. While q-KG is naturally equipped to handle noise by modeling the posterior mean, comparing it against a standard, noise-agnostic EI baseline may not be entirely fair. The authors should clarify which version of parallel EI was used in the experiments. If a standard version was used, the observed performance gap could be partially attributed to an ill-suited baseline. A more rigorous comparison would involve a noise-aware variant of EI or, at a minimum, a discussion acknowledging this limitation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the problem is clear, and the background on Gaussian processes is presented succinctly. The core idea behind q-KG is explained effectively in Section 4.

However, the clarity could be improved in Section 5.2, which details the gradient estimation via IPA. Given that this is arguably the central methodological contribution of the paper, this section feels overly condensed. A more detailed exposition of the derivation of the gradient estimator would strengthen the paper, improve reader understanding, and aid in the reproduction of the work.

**Originality:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of batch Bayesian optimization. However, the primary concern is the positioning of the work with respect to prior art on batch Knowledge Gradient methods. The paper presents q-KG as a novel method, but similar concepts have been explored previously, including in reference [26] of the paper's own bibliography (Wang et al., 2015). The authors briefly distinguish their work from [26] by highlighting the focus on continuous domains and the use of gradient-based optimization. This distinction needs to be more thoroughly elaborated. The central novelty appears to be the application of IPA for efficient gradient estimation in this context, rather than the concept of a parallel KG itself. This should be made more explicit throughout the paper.

Critically, the paper lacks an experimental or analytical comparison to these prior batch KG methods. To substantiate claims of a more efficient or effective implementation, a direct comparison with the method from Wang et al. (2015) would be necessary, even if it requires adapting their method from a discrete to a continuous domain.

**Significance:** The work is significant as it provides a principled and effective method for batch Bayesian optimization, a problem of high practical relevance in machine learning and other fields where parallel computation is available. The strong empirical performance, especially in noisy environments, suggests that this method could be widely adopted by practitioners. The development of an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy for a complex, non-myopic acquisition function is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of batch Bayesian optimization.
*   The q-KG method is well-motivated from a decision-theoretic perspective and is a natural extension of the established KG policy.
*   The gradient-based optimization strategy using IPA is a significant technical contribution that makes the method practical.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear advantages over several state-of-the-art methods, particularly in noisy settings.
*   The authors provide code, which is a major asset for reproducibility and adoption.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The novelty of the core "parallel Knowledge Gradient" concept is not clearly distinguished from prior work (e.g., Wang et al., 2015). The paper needs to better contextualize its specific contributions.
*   The lack of a direct comparison to other batch KG methods is a critical omission that weakens the paper's claims of superiority.
*   The experimental comparison against Expected Improvement in the noisy setting may be unfair, potentially overstating the performance gains of the proposed method.
*   The key technical details regarding the gradient estimation in Section 5.2 are presented too concisely, hindering clarity and reproducibility.

**Additional Comments:**
*   Minor point: The abbreviation "IPA" is defined in the introduction and then defined again in Section 5.2 (line 162). This is redundant.
