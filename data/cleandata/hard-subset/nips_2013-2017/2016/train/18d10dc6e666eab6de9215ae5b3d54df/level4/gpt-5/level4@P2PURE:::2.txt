Bayesian optimization has become a popular tool for machine learning tasks such as hyperparameter optimization, yet most existing methods are inherently sequential. This paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches and shows empirically that it outperforms existing approaches. The paper is well written and easy to follow. Parallelizing BO is important for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed method is both interesting and, in my view, more elegant than most existing approaches. The fact that it computes a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising.

The authors assume independent, normally distributed errors, which is standard in Gaussian process–based BO. However, in hyperparameter optimization this assumption can be problematic: measurement error reflects the gap between true generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation). Structural bias can arise in specific regions of hyperparameter space, especially with small sample sizes. It is worth noting explicitly that the independence assumption is not always realistic in this setting.

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the experimental evaluation is somewhat underwhelming for three main reasons:
1. Using test set error as the optimization objective is problematic and is known to be suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Threshold-based metrics like test error create issues for model selection and hyperparameter optimization. Metrics such as area under the ROC curve or log loss would be more appropriate.
2. The experiments rely on MNIST and CIFAR-10, which are common benchmarks, but I would have preferred inclusion of HPOlib [2], a widely used library specifically designed for benchmarking hyperparameter optimization algorithms.
3. The benchmark includes only two datasets and focuses solely on a single task (classification) with two learning algorithms (logistic regression and a CNN). Greater diversity would strengthen the claims, as some optimizers excel for certain algorithm families but perform poorly for others.

Overall, I like the paper, and the theoretical contribution is valuable. However, the current experimental benchmark is too limited to be fully convincing.

[1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. “The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms.” ICML. Vol. 98. 1998.  
[2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. “Towards an empirical foundation for assessing Bayesian optimization of hyperparameters.” NIPS Workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
