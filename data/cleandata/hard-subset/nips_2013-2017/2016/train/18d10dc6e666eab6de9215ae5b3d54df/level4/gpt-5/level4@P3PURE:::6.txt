Summary
This paper proposes a method for parallel batch Bayesian optimization that selects a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next via the knowledge gradient (q-KG). The acquisition function is maximized using a gradient estimated by infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). Across three experiments, the authors show that q-KG is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noise-free problems and outperforms them when observations are noisy. I like the goal of enabling efficient parallel hyperparameter optimization to leverage multi-core environments. However, several aspects remain unclear, and I am not fully convinced by the experimental results.

Technical quality
- The paper evaluates on standard artificial benchmark functions and on more realistic tasks: optimizing a CNN and logistic regression for CIFAR-10/MNIST. This mix is appropriate, but the experimental analysis is incomplete in key ways.
- I would like to see wall-clock results or a clear analysis of the practical gains from using the proposed parallel method compared to simple random search and to sequential Bayesian optimization. Since engineering overhead and batch selection time can dominate in practice, this is crucial for assessing real-world usefulness.
- It is unclear why the batch size q is fixed to 4 throughout. Please justify this choice (e.g., due to typical hardware availability, algorithmic stability, or acquisition optimization difficulty) or include results exploring performance as a function of q.

Novelty/Originality
- The paper uses the knowledge gradient (presented here as a generalization of expected improvement) as the acquisition function and employs IPA to optimize q-KG. Neither component is new.
- The paper “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions” already demonstrates how to use IPA to select a batch via expected improvement. Please clarify the incremental contribution beyond adopting KG in place of EI and using IPA for gradient estimation, and position the work relative to prior methods that already optimize q-point acquisition functions.

Impact/Usefulness
- Parallel hyperparameter optimization methods are highly relevant to real-world applications in machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics. A robust, noise-aware parallel method could be valuable if it translates to practical wall-clock gains and scales with available parallelism.

Clarity/Presentation
- The background and technical development are clearly presented, and the experimental section provides sufficient procedural detail to understand the comparisons. Strengthening the experimental analysis (see above) and clarifying naming/positioning relative to closely related methods would improve the paper.

Questions to the authors
1) Comparison to related work and naming:
   - Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant, and code seems to be publicly available. Have you compared against this method?
   - If your baseline or reference is “parallel EI in MOE,” please call it q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel method and avoid confusion. It would be helpful to clearly enumerate the differences between your approach, q-EI (as implemented in MOE), and Spearmint’s parallelization strategy.

2) Scaling with parallelism:
   - Modern systems often permit far more than four parallel evaluations. For example:
     • “Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks” (ICML’15) used up to 800 parallel runs of DNGO.
     • “Parallel Algorithm Configuration” (LION’12) used 25 SMAC runs in parallel.
     • “Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms” (NIPS’12) used up to 10 parallel runs of Spearmint.
   - Do you have insights into how your method scales with q and how much one can gain from increasing the number of parallel evaluations?

3) Asynchronous execution:
   - Your method is currently synchronous. Can it be extended to run asynchronously, as in Spearmint? If so, how would you adapt q-KG and its gradient estimator to pending evaluations?

4) Wall-clock performance:
   - Ultimately, wall-clock time is what matters. How does your method compare to random search and to simple sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of total time to reach a given performance level? What is the computational overhead for computing each new batch?

5) Plot interpretation:
   - In your figures, what does “iterations” on the x-axis denote: number of function evaluations or number of batches? Does it include the initial design? If so, why do different methods not start at the same value?

Minor comments
- Equation (4.1): Is there a missing z^(1:n) term?
- Grammar: “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” → “achieves a test error.”
- Citations: Parenthetical references are not nouns. For example, “[16] suggests parallelizing” should be “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing.”
- IPA reference: “Wang et al. (2015)” is likely not the correct source to cite for introducing IPA; they apply the method rather than introduce it. Please adjust the citation accordingly.
