This paper presents a novel parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm designed to compute batches of points in a Bayes-optimal manner. Given the increasing popularity of BO for tasks like hyperparameter optimization, particularly where scalability is crucial, the development of efficient parallel strategies is highly valuable. The proposed approach is well-written, clearly explained, and represents a conceptually elegant advancement over many existing parallel BO methods. The determination of a Bayes-optimal batch is a particularly promising aspect of this work.

The authors appropriately leverage the common assumption of independent, normally distributed errors within the Gaussian process framework typically employed in BO. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of this assumption in the context of hyperparameter optimization. In practice, “errors” in this domain often reflect the discrepancy between true generalization performance and empirical estimates derived from methods like cross-validation. This introduces inherent dependencies and structural biases, particularly when sample sizes are limited, which can significantly impact the validity of the independence assumption. Acknowledging and discussing these potential issues would strengthen the theoretical foundation of the work.

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and raises concerns about the robustness of the reported performance gains. There are three primary reasons for this. First, the use of test set error as the sole optimization criterion is questionable. It is well-established that optimizing directly for test set error can be suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning due to its threshold-based nature and susceptibility to overfitting [1]. Utilizing metrics less prone to these issues, such as area under the ROC curve or log loss, would provide a more reliable assessment of the algorithm’s efficacy.

Second, while MNIST and CIFAR-10 are commonly used benchmark datasets, the experiments would be significantly strengthened by including results on the HPOlib benchmark suite [2]. HPOlib is specifically designed for benchmarking hyperparameter optimization algorithms and offers a standardized and widely-recognized platform for comparison. 

Finally, the limited diversity of the experimental setup is a significant drawback. The benchmark relies on only two datasets and focuses solely on classification tasks with logistic regression and CNNs. The performance of different BO algorithms can vary considerably depending on the underlying machine learning task and algorithm characteristics. A more comprehensive evaluation, including regression problems and a wider range of learning algorithms (e.g., SVMs, random forests), is crucial to demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method. The current limited scope makes it difficult to confidently assess its performance across a broader spectrum of hyperparameter optimization problems.

Despite these concerns regarding the experimental evaluation, the paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed parallel batch acquisition strategy is innovative and has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of BO in practical applications. However, a more robust and comprehensive benchmark, addressing the points outlined above, is necessary to fully validate the claims made in the paper and establish its significance within the research community.
