

This paper introduces a batch-sequential Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) criterion for Bayesian optimization, extending the sequential KG framework to handle parallel evaluations. The authors provide a rigorous derivation of the q-KG acquisition function, leveraging Gaussian process (GP) models and computational techniques analogous to the standard KG criterion. Empirical results demonstrate consistent superiority of q-KG over state-of-the-art batch-sequential BO methods across multiple benchmark problems, establishing its practical value for parallel optimization. The work makes a significant contribution to the field, with clear potential to accelerate high-dimensional optimization tasks in scientific and engineering applications—making it highly relevant for NIPS audiences.  

The paper is well-structured and technically sound, though several minor points warrant clarification to enhance precision and reproducibility:  

1. **Literature Review Accuracy**: The claim that "integrating EI with respect to posterior distributions was first introduced in [Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization]" requires correction. The integration approach was explicitly used in *Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization* (with Constant Liar), while Chevalier et al. introduced CL-mix in [2]. Additionally, the natural gradient method for q-EI maximization is covered in *Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design*, which should be cited to contextualize the novelty of q-KG.  

2. **Technical Clarifications**:  
   - **Assumptions**: The compactness of the search space $\mathcal{A}$ and continuity of $f$ are assumed but not explicitly justified. These should be stated or referenced.  
   - **Initial Design**: Why restrict the initial design to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS)? A broader justification (e.g., theoretical guarantees for convergence) would strengthen the methodological choice.  
   - **Algorithm 1**: Hyperparameter re-estimation (e.g., via Bayesian updating of GP hyperparameters after each batch) is omitted. Clarify whether this is intentional (e.g., for computational efficiency) or an oversight.  
   - **Equation 5.2**: The definition of $g$ uses "max" but appears to require "min" (as $g$ is defined as a minimization objective). This inconsistency needs correction.  
   - **Smoothness of $g$**: The assertion that $g$ is smooth requires justification. The derivative of the Cholesky factor (used in the gradient computation) is non-trivial; a brief note on its tractability would be helpful.  
   - **Matérn Kernel**: The text states "Matérn is 5/2" (likely a typo for "Matérn 5/2"), which should be corrected for standard notation.  

3. **Experimental Gaps**:  
   - The paper demonstrates performance gains but does not quantify computational speed-ups from parallelization (e.g., wall-clock time reduction relative to sequential KG). Including this would better contextualize the practical impact of the proposed method.  
   - For Figure 1 experiments, it is unclear whether identical GP models were fitted across different software platforms (e.g., GPflow vs. GPy). Reproducibility would be improved by specifying implementation details or confirming consistency across tools.  

The core contribution—q-KG—is well-motivated and empirically validated. The minor issues noted above are largely editorial or require minor clarifications in the presentation, not fundamental flaws. The paper’s impact is substantial: parallelizing BO is critical for modern applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, experimental design), and q-KG offers a theoretically grounded, high-performance solution. The authors’ attention to computational details (e.g., efficient q-KG evaluation) further elevates the work.  

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper is technically strong, well-written, and addresses a timely problem with significant practical relevance. Addressing the clarifications above would enhance its clarity and reproducibility, but these are not essential for publication. The work merits inclusion in NIPS as a valuable addition to the BO literature.
