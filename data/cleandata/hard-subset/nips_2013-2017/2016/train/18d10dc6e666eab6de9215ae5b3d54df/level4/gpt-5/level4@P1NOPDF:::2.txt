Bayesian optimization (BO) has become very popular for tasks such as hyperparameter optimization, but most existing approaches are inherently sequential. This paper proposes a parallel BO algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches and shows that it outperforms existing BO methods. The paper is well written and easy to follow. Parallelizing BO is important for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed approach is both interesting and more elegant than most existing methods I am aware of. The fact that the method determines a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising.

The authors assume independent, normally distributed errors, which is common in GP-based BO. However, in hyperparameter optimization this assumption can be problematic, since measurement error reflects the gap between true generalization performance and its empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation). Structural bias can arise in certain regions of hyperparameter space, especially with small sample sizes. It is worth noting that the independence assumption is not always realistic in this setting.

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the experimental evaluation is somewhat disappointing for three main reasons:

1) The use of test set error as the optimization criterion is problematic. It is well known that such threshold-based metrics are suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning [1]. Metrics like test set error introduce several issues for model selection and BO, and it would be preferable to use measures such as area under the ROC curve (AUC) or log loss.

2) The experiments are based on MNIST and CIFAR-10, which are indeed common benchmarks. However, I would have preferred to see results on HPOlib [2], a widely used library specifically designed to benchmark hyperparameter optimization algorithms.

3) The benchmark includes only two datasets and focuses solely on classification, with just two learning algorithms (logistic regression and a CNN). I would have liked to see more variety in the benchmark, since it is known that some optimizers perform well for certain types of learning algorithms but poorly for others.

Overall, I like the paper and find the theoretical contribution valuable. However, the current benchmark is too limited to be truly convincing.

[1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. “The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms.” ICML, 1998.

[2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. “Towards an empirical foundation for assessing Bayesian optimization of hyperparameters.” NIPS Workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice, 2013.
