This paper introduces a batch-sequential version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization, termed q-KG. After a concise review of related work and Gaussian process (GP) modeling, the authors define the parallel criterion, detail its computation by extending the standard KG machinery, and present numerical experiments showing that q-KG often outperforms strong batch Bayesian optimization baselines. Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution with solid theoretical underpinnings and promising empirical results.

Strengths
- Novelty and significance: Extending KG to batch settings is nontrivial and valuable. The proposed q-KG addresses a well-motivated gap and has the potential to improve parallel experimental design and large-scale hyperparameter tuning workflows.
- Technical clarity: The derivation and computational strategy largely follow the KG framework, making the method interpretable and systematically connected to prior work on KG.
- Empirical performance: The experiments indicate that q-KG compares favorably to strong batch BO methods on a variety of test problems. The results are consistently compelling.
- Practical relevance: Parallelizing BO is important in practice. A principled batch KG can have substantial impact in real-world scenarios where synchronous resources are available.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Wall-clock speedups: While the batch setting is motivated, the paper does not quantify end-to-end speedups from sequential to batch-sequential optimization (e.g., in terms of wall-clock time given realistic parallelization overheads). Including such results would strengthen the case for practical impact.
- Literature positioning: Some statements in the related work could be tightened. For example:
  - Integration of EI with respect to posterior distributions, and strategies for parallelization (including Constant Liar), were already discussed in work such as “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization.”
  - Chevalier et al. introduced CL-mix in [2].
  - q-EI maximization using natural gradient methods has been studied in “Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design.”
  Clarifying these points and citing appropriately will better situate the contribution.
- Assumptions and smoothness: Several differentiability assumptions used in the derivations (e.g., in Section 5.2) would benefit from clearer statements, conditions, or citations to ensure the gradients are well-defined and that interchanges of limits/expectations are justified.

Requests for clarification
- Domain and regularity: Is the action set A assumed compact and the objective f continuous (or more regular) over A? The theoretical guarantees and some optimization steps may implicitly rely on such assumptions; please state them explicitly.
- Initialization: Why restrict the initial design to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS)? Would other space-filling or adaptive initial designs be acceptable, and do they materially affect performance for q-KG?
- Hyperparameter learning: In Algorithm 1, how are GP hyperparameters handled? Do you re-estimate them after each batch (maximum likelihood) or perform Bayesian updating (e.g., marginalizing via MCMC or variational methods)? Please specify the schedule and its computational impact, since acquisition values and gradients can be sensitive to hyperparameter drift.
- Section 5.2 objective: In the definition of g, is the objective a maximization or minimization? Please ensure the sign convention is consistent with the rest of the paper.
- Smoothness of g: The smoothness of g used to justify gradient-based optimization is not self-evident. Could you provide a brief argument or reference establishing differentiability (almost everywhere) and the conditions under which gradients exist and are unbiased?
- Cholesky derivatives: Differentiating through the Cholesky factorization is delicate. Please cite a standard result or provide a short derivation to justify the gradient expressions, and clarify any assumptions (e.g., positive definiteness, numerical stabilization).
- Figure 1 comparability: For the experiments underlying Figure 1, were all methods fit with the same GP kernels and hyperparameter learning procedures, ideally within the same software stack? If different toolchains were used, how did you ensure comparability (e.g., identical priors, optimization tolerances, and numerical settings)?

Minor issues and typos
- Matérn kernel: The paper mentions Matérn 2/5; presumably this should be Matérn 5/2.
- Notation consistency: Please verify the notation for maxima/minima in Section 5.2 and across the acquisition definitions.
- Terminology: Ensure consistent use of “batch-sequential,” “parallel,” and “q-” prefixes to avoid confusion.

Additional suggestions
- Complexity and scalability: A brief discussion of the computational complexity of q-KG with respect to batch size q, dataset size n, and dimensionality d would be useful. In particular, how does the method scale with larger q, and what are the memory/runtime bottlenecks?
- Optimization details: Please report key optimization settings (e.g., number of random restarts, sample counts for Monte Carlo expectations, gradient tolerances) for acquisition maximization, and whether common settings were used across baselines.
- Robustness: If space permits, include sensitivity analyses (e.g., to kernel choice, hyperparameter learning frequency, batch size q) or point to an appendix.

Overall evaluation
This is a very good paper that advances batch Bayesian optimization by proposing a principled q-KG criterion, deriving practical estimators, and demonstrating strong empirical performance. With clearer positioning relative to prior work, explicit assumptions, and some attention to practical considerations (hyperparameter learning, derivative justifications, and wall-clock speedups), the paper would be even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.
