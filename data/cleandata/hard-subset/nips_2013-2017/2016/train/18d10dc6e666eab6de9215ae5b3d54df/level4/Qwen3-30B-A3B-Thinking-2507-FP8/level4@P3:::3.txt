

The paper introduces a novel parallel Bayesian optimization method, the "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG), which extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) approach to the batch setting. The authors address a significant practical challenge in Bayesian optimization: when multiple function evaluations can be performed in parallel, how to optimally select a batch of points to evaluate simultaneously. The q-KG method is derived from decision-theoretic principles, choosing the batch of q points that maximizes the expected improvement in the solution quality with one additional batch of evaluations. This generalizes the sequential KG method to the batch setting, where the acquisition function must be optimized over a set of points rather than a single point.

The paper relates its work to previous NIPS contributions on Bayesian optimization, particularly noting that while several parallel BO methods have been proposed (e.g., parallel EI [2, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and parallel UCB with exploration [3]), these typically construct batches iteratively by optimizing single-point acquisition functions. The authors correctly identify that their approach differs by optimizing the acquisition function jointly over the entire batch. The paper also properly situates itself relative to the knowledge gradient method [7, 17], noting that while sequential KG can be evaluated exactly, the parallel case requires Monte Carlo methods, which the authors address through their IPA-based gradient estimation.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for the q-KG acquisition function and develop an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing q-KG against relevant baselines on both synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, and Hartmann) and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show that q-KG outperforms or matches existing methods, with particularly strong gains in noisy settings. The paper includes appropriate statistical analysis with multiple runs and standard deviations, and the code is publicly available.

The clarity of the paper is good. The writing is generally accessible, and the technical content is well-organized. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear, and the experimental setup is well-documented. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the discretization of the domain (Section 5.3) affects the quality of the solution, but this is a minor point.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by extending the KG method to the batch setting, which is a non-trivial generalization. While the core idea of extending sequential BO to batch settings has been explored in prior work [3, 5, 18, 25], the authors' decision-theoretic approach to batch selection and their IPA-based computational method represent significant improvements over existing parallel BO methods. The paper clearly articulates how their approach differs from previous work, particularly noting that other methods optimize iteratively over single points, while q-KG optimizes jointly over the entire batch. The novelty is not merely in the batch extension but in the specific computational method for optimizing the batch acquisition function.

The significance of the work is substantial. As machine learning applications increasingly rely on hyperparameter tuning for complex models (e.g., deep neural networks), the ability to parallelize this process can lead to significant time savings. The paper demonstrates clear practical value, showing that q-KG achieves better optimization results with fewer evaluations compared to existing methods, especially in noisy settings where parallelization is particularly valuable.

The main weakness of the paper is the limited discussion of computational complexity. While the authors claim efficiency through IPA, they don't provide detailed complexity analysis or runtime comparisons with other methods. Additionally, the paper could better clarify the relationship between the feasible domain A and the discretized set An used in the q-KG computation (Section 5.3).

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Include a more detailed analysis of computational complexity, particularly the scaling with batch size q.
2. Clarify the discretization process in Section 5.3, explaining how the choice of M (number of samples for discretization) affects the quality of the solution.
3. Add a brief discussion of limitations, such as how the method scales to very high dimensions.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical foundation for batch Bayesian optimization
- Efficient computational method using IPA
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on both synthetic and practical problems
- Strong empirical results, especially in noisy settings
- Practical relevance to machine learning hyperparameter tuning

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited discussion of computational complexity
- Some ambiguity in the discretization process description
- Minor grammatical issues (e.g., in equation 3.1, as noted in the draft review)

Overall, this is a strong contribution to Bayesian optimization that addresses an important practical problem. While the novelty is not revolutionary, the paper makes a clear, well-executed contribution that advances the state of the art in batch Bayesian optimization. The method has clear practical value for machine learning practitioners, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its advantages. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the computational complexity and discretization clarity concerns.
