This paper introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization, focusing on the selection of a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations for evaluation. The core of the approach lies in utilizing the knowledge gradient (q-KG) to quantify the utility of evaluating a batch, and an infinitesimal perturbation analysis-based technique to efficiently maximize this acquisition function. The authors demonstrate through three experiments that q-KG achieves competitive performance compared to existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods in noise-free settings, and surpasses them when observation noise is present. The concept of leveraging parallelization for efficient hyperparameter optimization in multi-core environments is appealing, however, certain aspects require further clarification and the presented experimental validation is not entirely convincing.

**Technical Quality:** The paper employs standard benchmark functions to demonstrate the proposed method’s advantages. Beyond these readily evaluated problems, the authors also present results for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression on the Cifar10/MNIST datasets. To strengthen the analysis, the inclusion of wall-clock time measurements or a comparative assessment against simple random search or sequential optimization – quantifying the benefits of this parallel approach – would be valuable. Furthermore, the rationale for consistently setting *q* to 4 across all experiments is unclear.

**Novelty/Originality:** The work proposes the application of the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function, coupled with infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for maximizing q-KG. While both techniques are established, the paper references “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions,” which already demonstrates the use of IPA with expected improvement for batch selection. 

**Impact/Usefulness:** Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods hold significant relevance for a wide range of applications across diverse fields, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

**Clarity/Presentation:** The technical details are well-described, the background information is comprehensive, and the experiment section provides sufficient information for understanding the comparisons performed.

**Questions to the Authors:**

1.) Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant to this work, and their code is publicly available. Have you conducted a comparison with this method? (If the referenced work is “parallel EI in MOE,” clarifying this and adopting the notation q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel method would be beneficial.) A clearer articulation of the differences between the approaches would reduce potential confusion.

2.) Modern multi-core systems readily support far more than 4 parallel runs. For instance, “Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks” (ICML’15) utilized up to 800 parallel runs of DNGO, “Parallel Algorithm Configuration” (LION’12) employed 25 SMAC runs in parallel, and “Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms” (NIPS’12) used up to 10 parallel runs of Spearmint. Do you have any insights into the scalability of your method and the potential gains achievable with a larger number of parallel runs?

3.) Currently, your method operates synchronously. Would it be feasible to extend it to support asynchronous execution, similar to Spearmint?

4.) Ultimately, wall-clock time is a critical metric. Have you evaluated the performance of your method compared to random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead associated with determining a new batch?

5.) The x-axis label “iterations” in your plots is ambiguous. Does it represent the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do not all methods begin from the same value?

**Minor Comments:**

*   A z^(1:n) term appears to be missing in equation (4.1).
*   “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” should be “achieves.”
*   Parenthetical references should not function as nouns; for example, “[16] suggests parallelizing” should be “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing.”
*   The reference to “Wang et al. (2015)” may be inaccurate regarding the introduction of IPA, as they apply rather than introduce the method.
