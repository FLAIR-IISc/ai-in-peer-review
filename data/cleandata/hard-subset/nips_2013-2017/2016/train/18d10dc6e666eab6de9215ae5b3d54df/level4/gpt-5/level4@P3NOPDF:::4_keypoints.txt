- Proposes q-KG: a batch knowledge-gradient acquisition function for parallel Bayesian optimization.
- Addresses expensive function evaluations by selecting q points per iteration.
- Provides an efficient procedure to compute or approximate the q-KG objective.
- Empirical results indicate q-KG outperforms or improves over sequential methods.
- Relies on Gaussian process modeling, which may struggle in high-dimensional problems; discuss mitigations like additive structure or embeddings.
- Lacks formal theoretical guarantees (regret bounds, consistency, or approximation error) for the q-KG policy or estimator.
- Missing quantified computational complexity as a function of q, n, and d; needs explicit cost drivers and scaling behavior.
- Insufficient comparison and discussion relative to existing batch methods (q-EI, local penalization, constant liar, Thompson sampling).
- Notation and certain definitions need clarification for readability.
- Recommend including an algorithm box summarizing the q-KG selection and gradient computation.
- Unclear whether evaluations are noiseless or noisy and how noise is incorporated into q-KG and the GP posterior.
- Ask how sensitive q-KG is to kernel choice and hyperparameter re-estimation during optimization.
- Ask whether the evaluation method uses reparameterization or common random numbers and how many Monte Carlo samples are needed.
- Question whether q-KG can handle constraints or multi-fidelity extensions.
- Suggest providing a concise explicit definition of the q-KG objective and its one-step lookahead formulation.
- Suggest adding complexity/scalability experiments (wall-clock vs q and n) and ablation studies for efficiency components.
- Suggest expanding related work to position q-KG against parallel EI and other batch strategies.
- Suggest experiments in moderately high-dimensional settings or with dimensionality reduction to demonstrate scalability.
- If full regret bounds are out of scope, suggest at least an error analysis of the q-KG estimator (Monte Carlo bias/variance, gradient convergence).
- Minor wording and notation fixes: incomplete phrase l.36; define set A at first use l.95; clarify μ^n at l.104; discuss parallel EI at l.128; avoid overloaded symbol A at l.140.
- Overall: solid and timely contribution with practical evaluation scheme, but would be stronger with clearer notation, comparative analysis, complexity quantification, and partial theoretical or estimator-error results.
