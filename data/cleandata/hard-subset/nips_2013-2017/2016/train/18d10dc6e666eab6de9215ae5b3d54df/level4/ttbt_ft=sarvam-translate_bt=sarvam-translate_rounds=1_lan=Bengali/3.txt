This paper proposes a new version of a stochastic gradient algorithm combined with Bayesian modeling for optimizing a multivariate objective function over a finite domain. The new part of the algorithm is that, in each iteration, it identifies a set of points based on which the objective will be calculated, and this calculation can be done in parallel. The problem is identifying the sampling points in an optimal way. To this end, the authors adopt the structure of a knowledge gradient, which estimates the expected improvement in the objective value based on the use of an additional sampling point and makes decisions about which sampling point to use based on this. This method is generalized here so that decisions can be made based on multiple sampling points in a batch. Since this problem is computationally difficult, the authors have created an algorithm based on Monte Carlo and infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the relevant gradients and maximize the gain of information on potential sampling sets. The algorithm has been applied to several experimental problems and has been shown to perform better than or compete seriously with other methods in the literature, mainly those based on confidence bounds. The paper is well-written and potentially useful for practical applications. I am not sure if the level of novelty is very high, as the authors have extended a previously studied method (KG) to the batch setting. This requires more sophisticated methods for optimizing criteria over multiple points. In my opinion, the main novelty of the paper is the estimation of q-KG gradients based on the IPA method, which is quite standard. I have a specific comment/suggestion: In equation (3.1), since x^(1:n) is a finite sequence of vectors, it is not clear how mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) are defined, as mu and K are functions of (one or two) vectors, not sequences.
