This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel approach for batch Bayesian Optimization. The core idea is to select a batch of points that is Bayes-optimal under a one-step lookahead assumption, maximizing the expected improvement in the final solution quality. To make the optimization of the q-KG acquisition function tractable, the authors propose an efficient gradient estimation technique based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method is evaluated on synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, where it is shown to be competitive with existing methods in noise-free settings and superior in settings with noisy function evaluations.

While I appreciate the principled approach and the importance of the problem, I have some concerns regarding the experimental evaluation and the practical scalability of the method.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of parallelizing Bayesian Optimization, which is highly relevant for leveraging modern multi-core computing environments for expensive optimization tasks like hyperparameter tuning.
*   The proposed q-KG acquisition function is well-motivated from a decision-theoretic perspective, providing a principled one-step Bayes-optimal policy for selecting the evaluation batch. This is a more rigorous foundation than many heuristic approaches to batch selection.
*   The experimental results, particularly in the noisy settings, are promising and suggest a clear advantage for q-KG over other batch methods. This is a significant finding, as many real-world optimization problems involve noisy observations.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clear, with the technical details of the method and the background on Gaussian Processes being well-explained.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

**1. Experimental Evaluation and Practical Utility:**
While the reported results are promising, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened to better demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.
*   **Wall-Clock Time:** A key missing component is an analysis of wall-clock time. Bayesian Optimization methods trade off a higher computational overhead per iteration for fewer function evaluations. It would be highly beneficial to see a comparison against simpler baselines like parallel random search and even sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of total time to reach a certain performance level. This would also clarify the computational overhead of optimizing the q-KG acquisition function. How large is this overhead compared to the function evaluation time?
*   **Choice of Batch Size:** The choice of batch size `q=4` across all experiments seems arbitrary. An ablation study on the effect of `q` would provide valuable insight into the method's scalability and its performance as the degree of parallelism increases.
*   **Plot Clarity:** In the experimental plots, it is unclear what "iterations" on the x-axis represents. Is it the number of batches or the total number of function evaluations? Also, the x-axis should probably include the initial design points. If it does, why do the different methods not start from the same performance value on average after the initial design?

**2. Relation to Prior Work and Originality:**
The paper’s relationship with Wang et al. (2015), "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions," which also uses IPA for batch selection with Expected Improvement, could be clarified. The benchmark "parallel EI in MOE" appears to be this method. If so, for clarity, it might be better to refer to it as q-EI (as in the original work) to distinguish it from heuristic parallel EI methods like the one in Spearmint. Given that both KG and EI are well-known acquisition functions and the use of IPA for batch selection has been previously demonstrated, a more direct discussion of the differences and contributions relative to this highly relevant work would strengthen the paper.

**3. Practical Considerations and Scalability:**
*   **Degree of Parallelism:** The experiments are limited to a small degree of parallelism (`q=4`). Many modern computing systems offer much higher parallelism (e.g., Snoek et al., 2012 used up to 10 parallel runs; other work has used dozens or hundreds). It is crucial to understand how q-KG's performance and computational overhead scale with larger `q`. Does the benefit saturate? Does the optimization of the acquisition function become intractable?
*   **Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Execution:** The proposed method is synchronous, requiring all `q` evaluations to finish before selecting the next batch. In many real-world settings, function evaluations have heterogeneous runtimes, making asynchronous methods significantly more efficient. A discussion on the potential for extending q-KG to an asynchronous setting, as has been done for other BO methods, would be a valuable addition.

**Minor Comments:**
*   In Equation (4.1), the notation `En[...|y(z(1:q))]` is slightly confusing. I believe the intent is that the expectation is over the distribution of `y(z(1:q))` given the data collected so far. Clarifying this would be helpful.
*   In Section 6.1.2: "...and achieve a test error" should be "...and achieves a test error".
*   Please correct the citation style. Parenthetical references should not be used as nouns. For example, "[10, 19] suggests parallelizing EI" should be "Ginsbourger et al. [10] and Snoek et al. [19] suggest parallelizing EI".
*   The reference for Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) seems incorrect. While Wang et al. (2015) apply the method, they did not introduce it. A more appropriate citation would be to foundational work on IPA, such as L'Ecuyer (1990).
