Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization. Building on the knowledge gradient (KG) framework, the authors generalize KG from the sequential to the parallel setting by defining an acquisition function that jointly evaluates the expected improvement in the GP predictor after a batch of q evaluations. The method is Bayes-optimal for the one-step decision problem: it selects the batch that maximizes the expected decrease in the posterior mean minimum. Under noise-free evaluations and when the final recommendation is restricted to previously sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel expected improvement (q-EI). Because exact evaluation is intractable in the batch setting, the authors estimate q-KG via Monte Carlo and develop an efficient unbiased gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch. They also describe a practical discretization of the search space and present empirical results on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and CNNs (CIFAR-10). The method is compared to parallel EI (including a widely used heuristic version), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, showing especially strong performance under noisy evaluations.

This work relates closely to prior BO methods based on EI, UCB, entropy search (ES), and KG. Unlike greedy batch construction (e.g., sequential UCB within a batch or “constant liar” heuristics), q-KG optimizes the batch jointly using a principled decision-theoretic objective. Compared to prior work on batch EI and batch ES, it offers a clean derivation of Bayes-optimality for the one-step batch setting and an efficient stochastic gradient computation.

Strengths:
- A principled acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for one-step batch decisions, extending the KG framework beyond the sequential setting.
- A clear and careful derivation, with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator via IPA that makes joint batch optimization computationally feasible.
- Good empirical performance on both synthetic and practical problems, with notable gains in noisy settings.
- The paper is well written and easy to follow, and the approach is more elegant than many existing batch BO heuristics.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Noise model assumptions: The method assumes independent, normally distributed observation noise, which is standard in GP-based BO. However, in hyperparameter optimization this assumption can be problematic. Measurement error stems from differences between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation), and these errors can exhibit structural bias and dependence in regions of hyperparameter space, especially with small sample sizes. It would be valuable to acknowledge that independence may be unrealistic in HPO and discuss robustness or possible extensions (e.g., heteroscedastic or correlated noise).
- Evaluation metric choice: Using test set error as the optimization objective is problematic and has long been recognized as suboptimal for model selection and hyperparameter optimization (e.g., Provost et al. [1]). Threshold-based metrics like accuracy can be misleading; metrics such as area under the ROC curve or log loss are generally more informative and better behaved for optimization.
- Benchmarking scope: The experimental benchmark is narrow: two datasets (MNIST and CIFAR-10), a single task type (classification), and two algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). Given that optimizers can behave differently across tasks and models, broader evaluation would strengthen the empirical case. In particular, using HPOlib [2] would provide a more standardized and diverse benchmark suite.

Quality:
- The technical development is sound. The derivation of q-KG as a Bayes-optimal one-step batch policy is clear, and the IPA-based gradient estimator appears correct and well motivated.
- The computational strategy (Monte Carlo estimation plus stochastic gradient ascent with discretization) is reasonable and practical.
- The empirical methodology is standard for BO papers, though the reliance on test error and the limited variety of problems reduce the strength of the experimental evidence.

Clarity:
- The paper is well organized and readable. The connection to prior work (KG, EI, UCB, ES) is explained, and the reduction to q-EI under noise-free, restricted recommendations is helpful. Implementation details and code availability are appreciated.

Originality:
- Extending KG to the batch setting with a tractable stochastic gradient optimization procedure is a novel and useful contribution. The decision-theoretic perspective provides a coherent alternative to existing batch heuristics.

Significance:
- Parallel BO is important in practice, especially for hyperparameter tuning with parallel resources. The proposed method is promising and appears to advance the state of the art, particularly under noisy evaluations. However, the limited empirical scope makes it harder to assess general applicability across diverse tasks.

Pros:
- Principled, Bayes-optimal batch acquisition function.
- Efficient gradient estimation enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong performance on synthetic and noisy settings.
- Clear exposition and publicly available code.

Cons:
- Independence/normality noise assumptions are questionable in HPO with structural bias and dependence.
- Use of test error as the optimization metric; more suitable metrics exist.
- Narrow empirical benchmark (few datasets, only classification, two algorithms); lack of standardized HPO benchmarks such as HPOlib.

Overall assessment:
I like the paper and find the theoretical contribution valuable. The algorithm is elegant and well motivated, and the empirical results are encouraging, especially in noisy regimes. However, the current experimental evaluation is too limited to be fully convincing. I recommend strengthening the empirical section (broader tasks, datasets, and metrics, ideally via HPOlib) and discussing the implications of the noise model in HPO.
