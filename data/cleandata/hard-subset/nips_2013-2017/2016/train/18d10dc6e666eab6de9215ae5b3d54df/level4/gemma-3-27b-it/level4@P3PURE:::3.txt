The paper proposes a novel stochastic gradient algorithm, integrating Bayesian modeling for optimizing multivariate objective functions over finite domains. The key innovation lies in its parallelizable computation of the objective function – in each iteration, the algorithm identifies a set of points for evaluation. The central challenge is to optimally select these sampling points, which the authors address using the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework. KG estimates the expected improvement in the objective value from an additional sampling point, guiding the decision of whether and which point to evaluate. This work generalizes KG to a batch setting, deciding on multiple sampling points simultaneously. Recognizing the computational intractability of this generalized problem, the authors develop an algorithm based on Monte Carlo methods and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to efficiently estimate the gradients needed to maximize information gain over candidate sampling sets. Empirical evaluation on several test problems demonstrates that the proposed algorithm either outperforms or is highly competitive with existing methods, particularly those relying on Confidence Bounds.

The paper is well-written and presents a potentially useful approach for practical applications. However, the level of novelty appears incremental. The authors extend a previously studied framework (KG) to a batch setting, which naturally necessitates a more sophisticated optimization method for the selection criterion. In my opinion, the primary novelty resides in the IPA-based estimation of the q-KG gradient, although this technique itself is relatively standard. 

I have one specific comment: In equation (3.1), the notation x^(1:n) represents a finite sequence of vectors. Consequently, the definitions of μ(x^(1:n)) and K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n)) are unclear, as μ and K are defined as functions of one or two vectors, not sequences.
