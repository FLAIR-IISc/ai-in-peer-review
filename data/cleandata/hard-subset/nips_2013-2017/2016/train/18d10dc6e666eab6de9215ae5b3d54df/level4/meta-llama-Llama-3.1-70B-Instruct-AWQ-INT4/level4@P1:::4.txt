This paper considers the parallel knowledge gradient method for batch Bayesian Optimization. The authors address the case when evaluating the function value is time-consuming. They propose a new acquisition function called q-KG that can output several new points for parallel evaluation. They also provide an efficient approach to evaluate the q-KG function. Empirical results are presented to verify their theoretical results.

The problem considered in this paper is important. Being able to split the work in a distributed way is useful for improving the performance of data-driven applications. Instead of evaluating one point at a time, the algorithm considers q knowledge gradients that propose q points to evaluate at the next iteration, which significantly accelerates the efficiency of the algorithm. This is an interesting idea worth exploring.

However, it is unclear whether the Gaussian process assumption is suitable, especially in neural network architectures where the dimension of the input is very large. It would be helpful if the authors could provide some theoretical results on the error bound of their method and a quantitative analysis of the complexity with regard to dimension.

Overall, this is a solid work in the field of Bayesian Optimization. The method is sound, and the experiment is well-designed. The paper is well-written, but there are some minor issues that need to be addressed. For example, on line 36, "the set of points to evaluate next that is" could be rephrased for better clarity. Additionally, the definition of A (line 95) and the input of mu^n (line 104) could be explicitly stated. It would also be beneficial to briefly discuss the parallel EI algorithm (line 128) and consider using different notations for A (line 140).

Strengths:

* The paper addresses an important problem in the field of Bayesian Optimization.
* The proposed q-KG acquisition function is novel and efficient.
* The empirical results are sound and well-presented.

Weaknesses:

* The Gaussian process assumption may not be suitable for high-dimensional input spaces.
* Theoretical results on error bounds and complexity analysis are lacking.
* Minor issues with notation and clarity.

Suggestions for improvement:

* Provide theoretical results on error bounds and complexity analysis.
* Consider alternative assumptions to the Gaussian process.
* Address minor issues with notation and clarity.

Acceptance:

Based on the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, I would suggest accepting it for publication. However, I would like to see the authors address the minor issues and provide additional theoretical results to strengthen their work.
