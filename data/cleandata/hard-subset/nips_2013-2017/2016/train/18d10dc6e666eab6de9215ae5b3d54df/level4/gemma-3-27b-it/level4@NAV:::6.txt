This paper introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization, focusing on the selection of a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations for evaluation. The core of the approach lies in utilizing the knowledge gradient (q-KG) to quantify the utility of evaluating a batch, and employing infinitesimal perturbation analysis to efficiently estimate the gradient of this acquisition function for maximization. The authors demonstrate, through three experiments, that q-KG achieves competitive performance compared to existing parallel Bayesian optimization techniques in noise-free settings, and surpasses them in the presence of observation noise. The motivation of enabling efficient multi-core utilization for hyperparameter optimization is appealing, however, several aspects require further clarification and the presented experimental evidence is not entirely convincing.

Regarding the technical quality, the paper validates the proposed method using standard benchmark functions, alongside applications to CNN and logistic regression optimization on the CIFAR10 and MNIST datasets. To strengthen the analysis, I recommend including an experiment that reports wall-clock time, or a comparative analysis evaluating the gains achieved by this parallel method against simpler approaches like random search or sequential optimization. Furthermore, the justification for consistently setting *q* to 4 across all experiments is unclear.

The novelty of the work resides in applying the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function, combined with infinitesimal perturbation analysis for maximizing q-KG. While both techniques are established, the paper references prior work ("Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions") that already demonstrates the application of IPA with expected improvement for batch selection. 

The potential impact of parallel hyperparameter tuning methods is significant across numerous fields, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics. The paper is well-written and provides clear technical details, with a comprehensive experimental section enabling thorough comprehension of the comparisons performed. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.) Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant to this work, and its code is publicly available. Have you performed a comparison against this method? If the referenced work is "parallel EI in MOE", it would be beneficial to distinguish it as q-EI to avoid confusion with Spearmint’s parallel method and clearly articulate the differences within the paper.

2.) Modern multi-core systems readily support far more than 4 parallel runs (e.g., DNGO in "Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks", ICML’15, utilized up to 800 parallel runs; SMAC in "Parallel Algorithm Configuration", LION'12, employed 25 parallel runs; Spearmint in "Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms", NIPS’12, used up to 10 parallel runs). What insights can you offer regarding the scalability of your method and the benefits of increasing the number of parallel runs?

3.) Currently, your method is implemented synchronously. Would it be feasible to extend it to support asynchronous execution, similar to approaches like Spearmint?

4.) Ultimately, wall-clock time is a crucial factor. Have you evaluated the performance of your method relative to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead associated with determining a new batch?

5.) The x-axis label "iterations" in your plots is ambiguous. Does it represent the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do the different methods not all begin at the same value?
