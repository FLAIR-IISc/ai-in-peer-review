This paper introduces a method for parallel batch Bayesian Optimization that selects a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations by maximizing the knowledge gradient (q-KG). To make this acquisition function tractable, the authors propose using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute its gradient. The experiments demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noiseless problems and outperforms them on noisy problems. While the goal of enabling efficient parallel hyperparameter optimization is highly relevant, several aspects of the paper, particularly the experimental evaluation, require further clarification.

**Technical Quality**
The empirical evaluation relies on common artificial benchmark functions and two machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10). While these experiments are a good starting point, the evaluation could be significantly strengthened in a few ways:

1.  A crucial metric for parallel optimization is wall-clock time. The current experiments, which plot performance against the number of evaluations, do not capture the overhead of the optimization method itself. An experiment comparing the wall-clock time of the proposed method against baselines like sequential Bayesian optimization and parallel random search would provide a much clearer picture of its practical benefits.
2.  The batch size is fixed at q=4 for all experiments without justification. It is unclear why this value was chosen and how sensitive the method's performance is to this hyperparameter.

**Novelty and Originality**
The paper proposes a combination of the knowledge gradient acquisition function with infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for gradient-based maximization. Neither of these components is new. In particular, the use of IPA for selecting a batch of points in Bayesian optimization has been previously explored by Wang et al. (2015) in the context of Expected Improvement. The primary novelty of this work lies in the application of this combination to the knowledge gradient, but its relationship and distinction from prior work should be clarified.

**Impact and Usefulness**
Methods for parallel hyperparameter tuning are highly relevant to real-world applications across many disciplines, including machine learning, robotics, and biology, where computational resources are often available to run multiple experiments simultaneously. This work has the potential for significant impact if its practical advantages and scalability can be more clearly established.

**Clarity and Presentation**
The paper is generally well-written. The technical details and background are well-described, and the experimental setup is explained with sufficient detail to understand the comparisons.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  The work of Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant, particularly their use of IPA for parallel Expected Improvement. Have the authors considered a direct comparison against this method? If the "parallel EI in MOE" baseline is this method, it would be clearer to label it "q-EI" to distinguish it from other parallel EI variants (e.g., Spearmint's). A more detailed discussion of the differences between the proposed approach and that of Wang et al. would also strengthen the paper.

2.  The experiments are limited to q=4 parallel evaluations. However, modern systems often support much larger degrees of parallelism (e.g., up to 10 in Snoek et al., NIPS'12; 25 in Hutter et al., LION'12; 800 in Snoek et al., ICML'15). What are the scaling properties of q-KG with respect to the batch size q? How does the performance gain change as the number of parallel workers increases?

3.  The proposed method operates in a synchronous batch setting. Could it be extended to an asynchronous setting to handle workers that finish at different times, as is common in real-world distributed systems?

4.  Ultimately, wall-clock time is a critical performance metric. What is the computational overhead of selecting a new batch using q-KG, and how does this affect the overall runtime compared to simpler methods like sequential BO or parallel random search?

5.  The x-axis in the plots is labeled "iterations." Does this refer to the number of batches evaluated or the total number of function evaluations? Furthermore, does the x-axis include the initial design points? If so, it is unclear why the performance curves for different methods do not originate from the same value after the initial design.

**Minor Comments**

*   Equation (4.1): Is there a z^(1:n) missing in the conditioning set of the posterior?
*   The reference to Wang et al. (2015) as the source for IPA seems incorrect. They apply the method but did not introduce it. The paper should cite the original source for IPA.
*   Typo: "...and achieve a test error" should be "...and achieves a test error".
*   Grammar: Parenthetical citations should not be used as nouns. For example, "[16] suggests..." should be rephrased as "Snoek et al. [16] suggest...".
