

This paper proposes a batch variant of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function for Bayesian optimization, introducing a Monte-Carlo-based gradient estimation approach to identify optimal batches of design points. The experimental results demonstrate superior performance compared to existing batch BO methods. While the core idea shows promise, the paper requires significant revisions to clarify its contribution, address methodological gaps, and strengthen its technical presentation.  

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Contribution Clarification:** The paper claims novelty for the batch KG formulation, but this is misleading. As noted in [1] (Wang et al., 2015) and [2] (Peng, 2010), batch KG has been previously studied for sequential multi-stage testing and stochastic optimization. The *true contribution* appears to be the proposed gradient estimation method for batch selection (Section 5.2). To justify this, the authors must explicitly contrast their approach with prior batch KG works, specifying *how* their gradient estimation improves upon existing methods (e.g., computational efficiency, convergence guarantees). The current comparison in Section 6 only benchmarks against generic batch BO methods (e.g., MES, LCB), not against batch KG variants. The authors should either:  
   - Add experiments comparing their method directly with [1] and [2] (e.g., on the same test functions), or  
   - Provide mathematical analysis (e.g., asymptotic complexity bounds) demonstrating the superiority of their gradient estimation.  
   Without this, the claimed novelty is unsubstantiated.  

2. **Insufficient Detail in Section 5.2:** This section is pivotal to the paper’s technical core but remains underdeveloped. The Monte-Carlo gradient estimator’s derivation lacks clarity—key steps (e.g., how the estimator handles batch dependencies, its convergence properties) are omitted. The authors should:  
   - Include a concise theoretical justification for the estimator (e.g., unbiasedness, variance analysis).  
   - Compare computational costs against alternative gradient estimation methods (e.g., finite differences) in a small-scale experiment.  
   - Clarify how the estimator scales with batch size *m* and input dimension *d*.  
   Without these details, the method’s practical viability remains questionable.  

3. **Inconsistent Treatment of Noise in Experiments:** Section 6.2 evaluates methods in a noisy setting but uses standard Expected Improvement (EI) without noise modeling (which assumes noiseless observations). This creates a methodological inconsistency: standard EI is not designed for noisy objectives, making comparisons unfair. The authors should:  
   - Explicitly state whether they used a noise-aware EI variant (e.g., EI with noise variance [3]) or the standard form.  
   - If standard EI was used, provide a discussion explaining why it remains a reasonable baseline despite its noise-handling limitations (e.g., "EI’s simplicity makes it a common baseline, though noise-aware variants would be preferable").  
   - Consider adding results for a noise-aware EI variant to ensure a fair comparison.  
   [3] is referenced in the draft but not included in the text; this should be resolved.  

4. **Minor Technical Issues:**  
   - Line 162: The abbreviation "IPA" (Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis) is redefined. It should be defined at first use (e.g., "Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA)"), not redefined.  
   - Reference [2] is a thesis URL (http://castlelab.princeton.edu/theses/Peng%20-%20Senior%20Thesis%20-%20May032010.pdf). For NIPS, it is preferable to cite a peer-reviewed publication if available (e.g., Peng’s later conference paper). If no such publication exists, the thesis should be cited as a technical report with a stable identifier.  
   - Section 5.2’s claim that "the gradient is computed in closed form" is inaccurate; the method relies on Monte-Carlo sampling, so it is *approximate*, not closed-form. This must be corrected.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper addresses a relevant problem in batch Bayesian optimization, and the proposed gradient estimation approach has potential. However, the current presentation fails to establish its novelty relative to existing batch KG work, omits critical technical details, and contains methodological inconsistencies in the experiments. The core contribution (gradient estimation for batch KG) is not sufficiently distinguished from prior art, and the experimental validation is incomplete.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Reject** (with a strong recommendation for resubmission after major revisions). The paper requires substantial work to:  
- Clarify the contribution relative to [1] and [2] via new experiments or analysis.  
- Expand Section 5.2 with theoretical and empirical justification for the gradient estimator.  
- Address the noise-handling inconsistency in Section 6.2.  
- Correct minor technical errors (IPA definition, reference formatting).  
Without these, the paper’s claims lack sufficient grounding for publication at NIPS. The current version risks misleading readers about the novelty of the batch KG framework.
