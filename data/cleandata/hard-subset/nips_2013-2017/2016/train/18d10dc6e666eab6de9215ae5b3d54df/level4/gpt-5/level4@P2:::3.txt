Summary
This paper introduces a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), for optimizing expensive black-box functions when multiple evaluations can be run in parallel. Building on the decision-theoretic knowledge gradient (KG) idea, q-KG selects a set of q points that maximizes the expected reduction in the GP posterior predictor’s minimum after one additional batch. The authors show that q-KG is one-step Bayes-optimal for this objective (when only one batch remains), and it reduces to parallel EI in the noise-free case when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points. Because exact evaluation and optimization of q-KG are intractable, they estimate the acquisition via Monte Carlo and optimize it with stochastic gradients computed using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization set adaptively via posterior sampling around likely optima. Experiments on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10) show that q-KG typically outperforms or matches parallel EI and UCB-based batch methods, with especially strong gains under noisy evaluations.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned relative to sequential acquisitions (PI, EI, UCB, ES, KG) and prior batch methods: heuristic and exact variants of parallel EI, GP-BUCB/GP-UCB-PE, and parallel ES. It extends the KG framework from sequential to batch selection and contrasts with nested/batch KG on discrete spaces by providing a differentiable, gradient-based optimizer over continuous domains. The use of IPA to obtain unbiased stochastic gradients for a batch acquisition builds on ideas used for batch EI, but here is tailored to q-KG’s structure.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic formulation: q-KG directly targets the expected improvement in the posterior predictor’s minimum after one batch, yielding a principled selection rule.
- Handles noisy evaluations naturally and shows pronounced empirical advantages in the noisy setting.
- Practical optimization strategy: a reparameterized Monte Carlo estimator with IPA-based stochastic gradients enables gradient ascent over batches without greedy construction.
- Solid empirical evaluation across synthetic and real ML tuning tasks; comparisons include strong baselines; code availability increases impact and reproducibility.
- Useful connections: demonstrates reduction to parallel EI in the appropriate limit, clarifying relationships between methods.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: the main conceptual step is extending KG to batches; the primary technical contribution is an IPA-based gradient estimator, which, while effective, follows a now-standard reparameterization/IPA pattern seen in recent batch acquisitions.
- Dependence on discretizing the inner minimization set A may introduce approximation bias; the paper could better quantify sensitivity to the discretization strategy and size M, especially in higher dimensions.
- Computational cost and scalability are not fully characterized (e.g., scaling with q, number of Monte Carlo samples, dimension, and Cholesky factorizations per evaluation). Wall-clock runtime comparisons would strengthen the empirical case.
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to one-step Bayes optimality; no regret or convergence analysis is provided for multi-batch optimization.
- Some implementation details (number of Monte Carlo samples, gradient variance control, step sizes/restarts) are not fully specified, which may hinder reproducibility and tuning.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The derivation of the q-KG estimator via reparameterization is standard and appropriate; the IPA argument for unbiased gradients is reasonable under smoothness assumptions. The discretization-based approximation is practical, though its error is not analyzed. Experiments are well executed and support the claims, particularly for noisy problems.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The algorithmic description is clear, and the relation to prior work is appropriate. One notational issue merits correction: in equation (3.1), x(1:n) is a finite sequence, so please define µ(x(1:n)) as the vector [µ(x(1)), …, µ(x(n))] and K(x(1:n), x(1:n)) as the n×n Gram matrix with entries K(x(i), x(j)). Similarly, clarify the role of diag{σ2(x(1)), …, σ2(x(n))}. Minor typos (e.g., “Main Stange”) should be fixed.

Originality
Moderate. Extending KG to batch selection in continuous domains and optimizing it via IPA-based stochastic gradients is a useful but evolutionary step relative to prior batch EI/ES and discrete batch KG work.

Significance
High practical relevance for parallel hyperparameter tuning and other settings with noisy, expensive evaluations. The method’s consistent empirical edge under noise, along with released code, suggests it could be adopted by practitioners.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide complexity and runtime analyses (as functions of q, n, dimension d, and Monte Carlo samples), and include wall-clock benchmarks.
- Report sensitivity/ablation studies: number of Monte Carlo samples, batch size q, discretization size M, and gradient variance reduction (e.g., common random numbers).
- Discuss numerical stability and implementation details for differentiating through the Cholesky factorization and for hyperparameter refitting.
- Clarify how unknown noise variance is estimated during optimization and whether misspecification affects performance.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled batch acquisition with strong performance, especially under noise.
- Practical optimization method with available code.
- Clear relation to and improvement over strong baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty and limited theory beyond one-step optimality.
- Approximation via discretization with limited sensitivity analysis.
- Missing runtime comparisons and some reproducibility details.

Overall, this is a solid and practically useful contribution to batch Bayesian optimization with clear empirical benefits in noisy settings, though the novelty is more methodological than conceptual.
