This work introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization, focusing on computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations for the next evaluation. The proposed approach utilizes the knowledge gradient (q-KG) to measure the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations. To efficiently maximize this acquisition function, the authors present a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the gradient of q-KG. Through three experiments, they demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on problems without observation noise, and outperforms them on problems *with* observation noise. I appreciate the idea of enabling parallel hyperparameter optimization to leverage multi-core environments effectively, but several aspects remain unclear, and I am not fully convinced by the experimental results. Below is detailed feedback and a set of questions for the authors.

**Technical Quality**

The paper employs common artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. In addition to these computationally inexpensive problems, results are also presented for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR10/MNIST. I would like to see an experiment showing wall-clock time, or an analysis quantifying the gains achievable by using this parallel method compared to simpler alternatives like random search or sequential optimization. Furthermore, the choice of setting *q* to 4 across all experiments is not justified. 

**Novelty/Originality**

The paper proposes using the knowledge gradient (a generalization of Expected Improvement) as an acquisition function, and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize q-KG. While the individual methods are not novel, the paper references “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions,” which already demonstrates how to use IPA to select a batch of configurations using Expected Improvement.

**Impact/Usefulness**

Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for real-world applications across diverse fields such as machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

**Clarity/Presentation**

The technical details and background information are well-described, and the experiment section provides sufficient information to understand the performed comparisons.

**Questions to the Authors:**

1.) Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant to this work, and code seems to be publicly available. Have you compared your method to theirs? (If this refers to “parallel EI in MOE”, clarifying this by renaming it q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel method would be beneficial). To avoid confusion, a clearer statement of the differences between your approach and existing work would be helpful.

2.) Modern multi-core systems often support far more than 4 parallel runs. For example, “Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks” (ICML’15) used up to 800 parallel runs of DNGO, “Parallel Algorithm Configuration” (LION’12) used 25 SMAC runs in parallel, and “Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms” (NIPS’12) used up to 10 parallel runs of Spearmint. Do you have any insights into the scalability of your method, and how much further performance improvement could be gained with a larger number of parallel runs?

3.) Currently, your method appears to operate synchronously. Would it be possible to extend it to asynchronous execution, similar to Spearmint?

4.) Ultimately, wall-clock time is critical. Have you evaluated how your method compares to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead associated with computing each new batch?

5.) The x-label "iterations" in your plots is ambiguous. Does it represent the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do not all methods start from the same value?

**Minor Comments:**

* Is there a missing  z^(1:n) in equation (4.1)?
* “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” should read “achieves a test error.”
* Parenthetical references should not function as nouns; for example, “[16] suggests parallelizing” should be “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing.”
* I do not believe “Wang et al. (2015)” is the appropriate reference for IPA, as they apply the method rather than introduce it.
