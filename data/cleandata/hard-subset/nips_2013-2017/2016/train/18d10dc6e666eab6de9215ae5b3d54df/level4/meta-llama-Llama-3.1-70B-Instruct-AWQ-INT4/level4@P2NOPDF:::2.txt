The paper proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, a significant contribution to the field of machine learning, particularly in hyperparameter optimization. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by comparing it to existing Bayesian optimization methods, showcasing its superior performance. The writing is clear and concise, making the paper easy to follow. 

Parallelizing Bayesian optimization is a crucial area of research for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed method is both interesting and more elegant than most existing approaches. One of the most promising aspects of this work is the determination of a Bayes-optimal batch. The authors assume independent normally distributed errors, a common assumption in most Gaussian process-based Bayesian optimization methods. However, this assumption may be problematic in hyperparameter optimization, as measurement errors often represent the difference between generalization performance and empirical estimates. For instance, structural bias can be present in certain regions of hyperparameter space, particularly with small sample sizes. It is worth noting that assuming independence may not always be realistic.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are strong, providing a solid foundation for the proposed algorithm. However, the experimental evaluation falls short in several areas. Firstly, using test set error as the optimization criterion is suboptimal for hyperparameter tuning, as it is a threshold-based metric that can introduce problems for model selection and hyperparameter optimization. Metrics such as the area under the ROC curve or log loss would be more suitable. Secondly, while the MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets are commonly used in benchmarks, it would be more convincing to see experiments conducted using the HPOlib library, a widely-used benchmarking library specifically designed for hyperparameter optimization algorithms. Finally, the experimental evaluation is limited to only two datasets and a single machine learning task (classification) with two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). It would be beneficial to include more variety in the benchmark, as some optimizers perform well for certain types of learning algorithms but poorly for others.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. However, the experimental evaluation is too limited to be truly convincing, and addressing the aforementioned concerns would strengthen the paper's claims.
