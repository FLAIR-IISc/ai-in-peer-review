This paper presents a batch version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) algorithm for Bayesian optimization. The core idea is to estimate the gradient of the expected improvement with respect to the batch of design points using Monte Carlo simulation, allowing for the selection of an optimal batch. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves superior performance compared to other batch Bayesian optimization techniques.

However, several aspects of the paper require clarification and further elaboration. First, the claim of novelty regarding the batch KG approach needs to be addressed. As I understand it, batch KG methods have been previously explored, for example in the work of Wang et al. [1] and the thesis by Peng [2]. The paper should clearly articulate its specific contribution relative to these prior works. While the gradient estimation method for batch selection appears to be the primary novel component, a more explicit statement highlighting this contribution is necessary. Furthermore, a comparative analysis – either experimental or theoretical – against existing batch KG approaches would significantly strengthen the paper’s claims.

Second, Section 5.2, which details the proposed gradient estimation technique, is crucial to the paper’s core contribution and should be expanded upon. A more detailed explanation of the method, including perhaps a more formal presentation of the estimation process, would be beneficial for understanding and reproducibility.

Third, the experimental setup in Section 6.2, comparing against Expected Improvement (EI) in a noisy setting, raises a concern. EI is fundamentally based on the assumption of noiseless observations. Therefore, a direct comparison in a noisy environment may be misleading. If the standard, noiseless EI was employed, the results should be discussed with a clear acknowledgment of this limitation. Alternatively, if a noise-aware variant of EI was used, the specific implementation should be described.

Finally, a minor point: the abbreviation “IPA” is redefined on line 162, which is potentially confusing. Ensuring consistent use of abbreviations throughout the manuscript is recommended.

In summary, while the proposed gradient estimation method for batch KG shows promise, the paper requires further clarification regarding its novelty and a more thorough comparison with related work. A more detailed explanation of the core gradient estimation technique and a discussion of the fairness of the comparison against EI in the noisy setting would also significantly improve the quality of this submission.
