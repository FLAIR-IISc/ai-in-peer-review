This paper proposes a novel stochastic optimization algorithm that incorporates Bayesian modeling to optimize a multivariate objective function over a finite domain. A key feature of the algorithm is its ability to select, at each iteration, a batch of points for parallel evaluation of the objective function. The central problem addressed is how to identify this batch of sampling points in an optimal manner.

To this end, the authors adopt and extend the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework. A standard KG policy estimates the expected improvement in the objective value gained from a single additional function evaluation and uses this to guide selection. This paper generalizes the KG approach to a batch setting, enabling the simultaneous selection of multiple points for evaluation. As directly maximizing the batch KG criterion is computationally intractable, the authors develop an efficient algorithm based on Monte Carlo simulation and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of this criterion. This enables the use of gradient-based methods to find the optimal batch of candidate points.

The proposed algorithm is evaluated on several test problems, where it is shown to outperform or be highly competitive with existing approaches, particularly those based on Upper Confidence Bounds. The paper is well-written, and the proposed method appears promising for practical applications.

My main reservation, however, concerns the degree of novelty. The core contribution is the extension of a well-established method (the Knowledge Gradient) to the batch setting. While this extension is non-trivial and requires a more sophisticated optimization procedure, the primary technical contribution—the use of Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis to estimate the gradient of the batch KG criterion—also leverages a relatively standard technique.

I have one specific suggestion for improving clarity:
In Equation (3.1), the notation `mu(x^(1:n))` and `K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n))` is ambiguous. Since `x^(1:n)` represents a finite sequence of vectors, it is not immediately clear how these terms are defined. The mean function `mu` is typically a function of a single vector, and the covariance kernel `K` is a function of two vectors, not sequences. I presume this is shorthand for the posterior mean vector and covariance matrix evaluated at the points `(x_1, ..., x_n)`, but the authors should define this notation explicitly for the reader.
