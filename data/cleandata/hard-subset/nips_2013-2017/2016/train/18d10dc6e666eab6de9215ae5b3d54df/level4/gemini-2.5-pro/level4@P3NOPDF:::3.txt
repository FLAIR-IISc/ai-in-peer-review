This paper addresses the problem of optimizing a multivariate objective function over a finite domain, a common scenario in Bayesian optimization. The authors propose a new algorithm that combines stochastic gradient methods with Bayesian modeling, specifically designed for parallelized or batch evaluations. The key idea is to extend the Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to a batch setting, allowing for the simultaneous selection of multiple points (`q` > 1) for evaluation in each iteration.

The core challenge lies in selecting this batch of `q` points to maximize the expected improvement, a problem that is computationally intractable. To overcome this, the authors develop a stochastic gradient-based optimization method. They employ Monte Carlo simulation and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of the batch KG criterion (referred to as q-KG), enabling them to efficiently search for an optimal set of candidate points.

The proposed algorithm is empirically evaluated on several test problems. The results demonstrate that it is highly competitive with, and in some cases superior to, existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly those based on Upper Confidence Bounds (UCB).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Practical Relevance:** The work addresses an important and practical problem. As parallel computing resources become more accessible, developing Bayesian optimization methods that can effectively leverage this parallelism is crucial. The proposed q-KG algorithm provides a principled and effective approach for this setting.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the problem is clearly formulated. The motivation for extending the KG framework and the challenges involved are well-explained.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing, showing that the algorithm either outperforms or is a strong competitor to other relevant approaches in the literature.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

My main concern relates to the level of conceptual novelty. The core idea is an extension of the previously studied Knowledge Gradient policy to a batch setting. While this is a valuable and non-trivial engineering contribution, the conceptual leap from single-point KG to batch q-KG feels incremental.

The primary technical innovation appears to be the application of a stochastic gradient method using an IPA-based gradient estimator to optimize the q-KG acquisition function. While this is executed well, the use of IPA for estimating gradients in Monte Carlo simulations is a relatively standard technique in the stochastic optimization and simulation literature. Therefore, the contribution feels more like a skillful and effective combination of existing ideas rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new paradigm.

**Specific Comments and Suggestions:**

I have one specific point that could benefit from clarification. In Equation (3.1), the notation `μ(x^(1:n))` and `K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n))` is used. However, the prior mean function `μ` and covariance kernel `K` are typically defined on single points (e.g., `μ(x)`) or pairs of points (e.g., `K(x, x')`). It is not immediately clear how these functions are extended to operate on a sequence of vectors `x^(1:n)`. I assume that `μ(x^(1:n))` refers to the vector `[μ(x_1), ..., μ(x_n)]^T` and `K(x^(1:n), x^(1:n))` refers to the `n x n` covariance matrix where the `(i,j)`-th entry is `K(x_i, x_j)`. Explicitly clarifying this notational convention would improve the paper's readability and precision.
