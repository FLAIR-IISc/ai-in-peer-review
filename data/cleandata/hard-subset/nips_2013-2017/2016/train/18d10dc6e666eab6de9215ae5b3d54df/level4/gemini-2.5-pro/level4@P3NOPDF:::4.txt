### **Summary**

This paper introduces a principled method for batch Bayesian Optimization by extending the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting. The proposed acquisition function, termed the q-Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), is designed to select a batch of `q` points for simultaneous evaluation, which is particularly valuable in scenarios where function evaluations are time-consuming and can be distributed. The authors also present a practical and efficient stochastic gradient-based algorithm for optimizing the q-KG function. The effectiveness of the approach is demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on synthetic and real-world optimization problems, showing favorable performance against other batch Bayesian optimization methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the highly relevant and practical problem of accelerating Bayesian optimization through parallelization. The ability to intelligently select batches of points for evaluation is critical for leveraging modern distributed computing resources and making Bayesian optimization applicable to more complex, real-world problems.

2.  **Principled Method:** The proposed q-KG acquisition function is a well-motivated and natural extension of the single-point Knowledge Gradient. By directly quantifying the expected value of information from evaluating a *set* of points, it offers a more fundamentally sound approach than many heuristic or myopic methods for constructing batches.

3.  **Computational Feasibility:** A key contribution is the development of a practical algorithm to optimize the non-trivial q-KG function. The use of stochastic gradients to handle the expectation over the posterior distribution makes the method computationally feasible and scalable.

4.  **Sound Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing q-KG against relevant baselines on a variety of benchmark functions. The results provide convincing evidence of the method's strong practical performance.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Scalability and GP Assumptions:** The method's reliance on a standard Gaussian Process (GP) model may limit its applicability in very high-dimensional spaces, a common scenario in applications like neural architecture search. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss the potential limitations of their approach in this context and comment on how the complexity scales with the input dimension.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper would be significantly strengthened by the inclusion of theoretical analysis. Providing results on the convergence properties, error bounds, or a formal analysis of the computational complexity with respect to the batch size `q` and dimension `d` would offer deeper insight into the algorithm's behavior.

### **Clarity and Minor Comments**

The paper is generally well-written and clear. The following minor points could further improve readability:

*   **l.36:** The phrase "the set of points to evaluate next that is" appears to be an incomplete sentence. Please revise.
*   **l.95:** The set `A` is used without being formally defined. Please provide a definition upon its first use.
*   **l.104:** The input argument for the posterior mean function `μ^n(·)` should be explicitly stated for clarity (e.g., `μ^n(x)`).
*   **l.128:** A brief discussion comparing q-KG to the parallel Expected Improvement (EI) algorithm would provide valuable context and help better position the proposed method within the literature.
*   **l.140:** The notation `A` appears to be used in multiple contexts. It might be helpful to use a different notation for one of the concepts to avoid potential ambiguity for the reader.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The paper introduces a methodologically sound approach to the important problem of batch selection, supported by a practical optimization algorithm and strong experimental results. While the work could be improved with a more thorough discussion of its high-dimensional limitations and the inclusion of theoretical guarantees, it stands as an impactful and well-executed piece of research.
