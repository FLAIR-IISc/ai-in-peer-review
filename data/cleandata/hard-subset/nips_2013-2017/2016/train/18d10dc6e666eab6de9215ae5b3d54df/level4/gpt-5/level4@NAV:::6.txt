This paper introduces a method for parallel batch Bayesian optimization that selects a Bayes-optimal set of configurations for the next evaluations. The approach quantifies the utility of a batch via the knowledge gradient (q-KG). To efficiently optimize the acquisition, the authors use infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the gradient of q-KG. Across three experiments, q-KG is reported to be competitive with existing parallel BO methods on noise-free problems and to outperform them when observations are noisy. I appreciate the focus on parallel hyperparameter optimization to better utilize multi-core resources, but several aspects remain unclear and I am not fully persuaded by the empirical results. Below I provide comments on each criterion and questions at the end:

Technical quality
The paper evaluates on standard synthetic benchmark functions to demonstrate the method’s advantages. In addition to these inexpensive test cases, it also reports results for tuning a CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR-10/MNIST. In the experimental section, I would expect either wall-clock time measurements or an analysis quantifying the practical gains of the parallel approach relative to simple random search or sequential optimization. Moreover, it is not clear why q is fixed to 4 across all experiments.

Novelty/Originality
The contribution is to use the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as the acquisition function and to apply infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize q-KG. While this combination is interesting, neither component is new, and the paper cites “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions,” which has already shown how to use IPA to choose a batch under expected improvement.

Impact/Usefulness
Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant in practical settings across many domains, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

Clarity/Presentation
The technical background and derivations are clearly presented, and the experimental section contains sufficient detail to understand the comparisons performed.

Questions to the authors:
1) Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant, and code seems to be publicly available. Have you compared against this method? (If this corresponds to parallel EI in MOE, please refer to it as q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel approach.) To avoid confusion, it would help to explicitly lay out the differences in the paper.
2) Modern multi-core systems allow far more than 4 simultaneous evaluations. For example:
   - “Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks,” ICML’15, used up to 800 parallel DNGO runs
   - “Parallel Algorithm Configuration,” LION’12, used 25 SMAC runs in parallel
   - “Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms,” NIPS’12, used up to 10 parallel Spearmint runs
   Do you have insights into how your method scales with q and the practical gains from increasing the degree of parallelism?
3) Your approach is currently synchronous. Could it be extended to an asynchronous setting, as in Spearmint?
4) Ultimately, wall-clock time is what matters. Have you evaluated performance relative to random search or simple sequential BO in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead of constructing a new batch?
5) In your plots, what does “iterations” on the x-axis denote—number of function evaluations or number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do the methods not all start from the same value?
