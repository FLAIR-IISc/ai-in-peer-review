This paper explores batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization, introducing a batch-sequential variant of the Knowledge Gradient criterion. Following a comprehensive background on related work and Gaussian processes, the parallel (q-KG) criterion is formally defined. Its computational details are provided, drawing parallels with the standard KG criterion, and supported by a series of numerical experiments that demonstrate q-KG's superior performance over state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization algorithms. This paper is of high quality and possesses significant merits that make it suitable for publication in NIPS. The potential societal impact of parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms is substantial. A minor critique is that the paper does not investigate speed-ups from sequential to batch-sequential optimization and contains a few minor inaccuracies in the literature review. For example, the concept of integrating EI with respect to posterior distributions was previously explored in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (alongside Constant Liar), and Chevalier et al. introduced CL-mix in [2]. Additionally, q-EI maximization using natural gradient was studied in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design". I have several remarks and questions: Is the set A compact and the function f continuous? What is the rationale behind restricting A to be a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) at the initial stage? In Algorithm 1, what is the approach to hyperparameter re-estimation (or Bayesian updating)? In Section 5.2, is the optimization problem defined as a maximization or minimization of g? Furthermore, the smoothness of g is not explicitly justified, and the derivative of the Cholesky factor may not be straightforward. It appears that the Matérn parameter is 5/2, not 2/5. Regarding Figure 1 and the corresponding experiments, was it feasible to fit the same Gaussian processes using different software packages?
