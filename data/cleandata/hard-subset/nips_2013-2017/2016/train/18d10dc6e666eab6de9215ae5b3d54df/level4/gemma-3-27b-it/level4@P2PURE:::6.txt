This work introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization, aiming to efficiently select the next batch of configurations for evaluation using a Bayes-optimal approach. The proposed method utilizes the knowledge gradient (q-KG) to measure the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations. To enable efficient maximization of this acquisition function, the authors present a technique based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis to estimate the gradient of q-KG. Through three experiments, they demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods in scenarios without observation noise and outperforms them in the presence of noise. While the idea of leveraging parallel hyperparameter optimization for efficient utilization of multi-core environments is appealing, certain aspects remain unclear, and the presented experimental results are not entirely convincing. Below is detailed feedback and several questions for the authors.

**Technical quality:** The paper employs standard benchmark functions to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. In addition to these computationally inexpensive problems, results are also presented for optimizing a CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR10/MNIST. The experimental section would benefit from including an experiment measuring wall-clock time or an analysis quantifying the gains achieved through this parallel method compared to simple random search or sequential optimization. Furthermore, the rationale for fixing *q* to 4 across all experiments is unclear.

**Novelty/Originality:** The paper proposes applying the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) as an acquisition function and utilizing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize q-KG. While these methods individually are not novel, the combination is presented as a contribution. However, the paper references “Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions,” which already demonstrates the use of IPA for batch selection using expected improvement. 

**Impact/Usefulness:** Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for applications across numerous fields, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

**Clarity/Presentation:** The technical details and background material are well-described, and the experiment section provides sufficient information to understand the conducted comparisons.

**Questions to the authors:**

1.) Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant to this work, and code is publicly available. Have you compared against this method? (If this is “parallel EI in MOE”, it should be explicitly denoted as q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s parallel implementation.) Clearly stating these differences in the paper would mitigate potential confusion.

2.) Modern multi-core systems readily support significantly more than 4 parallel runs. For example, “Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks” (ICML’15) employed up to 800 parallel runs of DNGO, “Parallel Algorithm Configuration” (LION’12) used 25 SMAC runs in parallel, and “Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms” (NIPS’12) utilized up to 10 parallel runs of Spearmint. Do you have any insights regarding the scalability of your method and the potential benefits of increasing the number of parallel runs?

3.) Currently, your method appears to operate synchronously. Would it be feasible to extend it to support asynchronous execution, similar to Spearmint?

4.) Ultimately, wall-clock time is a critical consideration. Have you evaluated how your method compares to random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead associated with determining a new batch?

5.) The x-axis label “iterations” in your plots is ambiguous. Does this represent the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do not all methods begin from the same value?

**Minor comments:**

*   There appears to be a missing z^(1:n) in equation (4.1).
*   “this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error” should read “achieves a test error.”
*   Parenthetical references should not be treated as nouns; for example, “[16] suggests parallelizing” should be “Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing.”
*   I question whether “Wang et al. (2015)” is the correct reference for IPA, as they apply the method rather than introducing it.
