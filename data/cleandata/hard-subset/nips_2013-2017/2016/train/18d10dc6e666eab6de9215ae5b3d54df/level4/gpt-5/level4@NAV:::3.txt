The paper presents a variant of a stochastic gradient algorithm integrated with Bayesian modeling to optimize a multivariate objective over a finite domain. The distinctive feature of the method is that at each iteration it selects multiple points at which to evaluate the objective, allowing parallel computation. The core challenge is to choose the sampling locations in an optimal manner. To address this, the authors adopt the Knowledge Gradient framework, which estimates the expected improvement in the objective from adding one more sampling point and uses this estimate to decide whether to sample and which point to select. This idea is extended here to a batch setting, where decisions are made for multiple sampling points simultaneously. Because the resulting optimization is computationally intractable, the authors develop a Monte Carlo algorithm with Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis to estimate the necessary gradients so as to maximize information gain over candidate sampling sets. The approach is evaluated on several test problems and is shown to either outperform or be strongly competitive with methods in the literature, especially those based on Confidence Bounds. The paper is clearly written and appears useful for practical applications. That said, I am not fully convinced the level of novelty is very high, as the work primarily extends a previously studied approach (KG) to a batch context. This extension does require a more sophisticated procedure to optimize the criterion over sets of multiple points. In my view, the main novelty lies in the IPA-based estimation of the q-KG gradient, which is also fairly standard. I have one specific comment/suggestion: In equation (3.1), since x^(1:n) is a finite sequence of vectors, it is unclear how mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n), x(1:n)) are defined, given that mu and K are functions of single (or pairs of) vectors rather than sequences.
