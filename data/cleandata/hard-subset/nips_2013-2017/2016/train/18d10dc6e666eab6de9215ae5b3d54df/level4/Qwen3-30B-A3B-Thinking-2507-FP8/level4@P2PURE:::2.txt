

Bayesian optimization has gained significant traction for machine learning tasks such as hyperparameter optimization, though most existing methods are inherently sequential. This paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing approaches. The paper is well-written and accessible. Parallelization of Bayesian optimization is a critical challenge for practical hyperparameter optimization, and the proposed method is both interesting and more elegant than most alternatives. The derivation of a Bayes-optimal batch is particularly promising. The authors assume independent, normally distributed errors—a standard assumption in Gaussian process-based Bayesian optimization. However, this assumption is problematic in hyperparameter optimization, where measurement errors reflect the discrepancy between generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation). Structural bias frequently arises in hyperparameter regions, especially with small sample sizes, and the independence assumption is not universally realistic. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the experimental evaluation has notable limitations:  
1. The use of test set error as the optimization criterion is suboptimal, as established in prior work [1]. Threshold-based metrics like test set error introduce well-known issues for model selection and hyperparameter tuning; metrics such as area under the ROC curve or log loss would be more appropriate.  
2. The experiments rely on MNIST and CIFAR10, common benchmark datasets, but would benefit from comparison against HPOlib [2], a widely adopted library specifically designed for hyperparameter optimization benchmarking.  
3. The benchmark uses only two datasets and focuses on a single learning task (classification) with two algorithms (logistic regression and CNN), lacking sufficient diversity. Given that optimizer performance varies across algorithm types, a broader benchmark is essential for meaningful validation.  
Overall, the theoretical contribution is valuable, but the current experimental validation is too limited to convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages. [1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998. [2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
