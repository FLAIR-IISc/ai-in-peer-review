

This paper proposes a batch extension of Knowledge Gradient (KG) for Bayesian optimization, introducing a Monte-Carlo-based gradient estimation method to identify optimal design points in batches. Experimental results indicate superior performance over existing batch BO methods. However, the contribution requires clearer articulation relative to prior work, and key sections warrant deeper analysis.  

The primary contribution appears to be the gradient estimation technique for batch KG, rather than the batch KG framework itself, which has been previously explored (e.g., Wang et al. [1], Peng [2]). The authors must explicitly clarify how this work advances beyond [1] and [2], particularly regarding the gradient estimation method. If this is the core novelty, the paper must demonstrate its efficiency and effectiveness through direct comparisons with these prior batch KG approaches—either via experiments or mathematical analysis—rather than solely contrasting with non-KG batch BO methods. Without this, the claimed novelty remains ambiguous.  

Section 5.2, which details the gradient estimation method, is pivotal to the paper’s technical contribution but lacks sufficient rigor. The derivation of the Monte-Carlo estimator should be expanded to include: (i) justification for the sampling strategy, (ii) convergence guarantees, and (iii) a discussion of computational complexity relative to alternative gradient estimation techniques. As currently written, this section reads as a procedural description rather than a substantive technical contribution.  

A critical methodological flaw concerns the comparison with Expected Improvement (EI) in noisy settings (Section 6.2). EI assumes noiseless observations by design, making its use in noisy experiments inherently unfair. The authors must either: (i) employ a noise-aware variant of EI (e.g., EI with heteroscedastic noise modeling), or (ii) explicitly discuss why plain EI is still a valid baseline despite its noise assumptions—such as by acknowledging its limitations in the noisy context and contextualizing the results accordingly. The current comparison risks misleading readers about the relative robustness of the proposed method.  

Minor but necessary corrections: The abbreviation "IPA" is redefined in line 162 (presumably referring to "Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis"), but this should be introduced upon first use to avoid confusion. Additionally, the reference [2] is a thesis; the citation should be formatted consistently with academic standards (e.g., including the year and institution).  

In summary, the paper’s core technical contribution—gradient estimation for batch KG—holds promise but requires stronger justification against existing batch KG work. The experimental comparisons must address the noise-handling limitations of EI, and Section 5.2 must provide deeper theoretical and algorithmic justification. With these revisions, the paper would better establish its novelty and technical merit.
