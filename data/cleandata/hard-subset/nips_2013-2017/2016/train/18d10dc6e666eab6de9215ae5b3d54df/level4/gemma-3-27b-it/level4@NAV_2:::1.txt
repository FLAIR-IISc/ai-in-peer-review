This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, extending the knowledge gradient criterion to the parallel setting. The authors derive this method from a decision-theoretic perspective and present an efficient computational approach for its implementation. They demonstrate q-KG’s superior performance compared to existing batch Bayesian optimization algorithms on both synthetic test functions and in tuning practical machine learning algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction to the problem and outlining the contributions. It provides necessary background on Gaussian processes and related work, setting the stage for the proposed method. The derivation of q-KG and the subsequent discussion of its efficient computation via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) are detailed and technically sound. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering both noise-free and noisy settings, and includes comparisons with several relevant baseline algorithms. 

A key strength of this work lies in its theoretical grounding. The method is explicitly derived from a Bayes-optimal perspective, which is clearly articulated. The IPA-based computational approach is a significant contribution, enabling efficient optimization of the q-KG acquisition function. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, especially when dealing with noisy function evaluations. The availability of the code further enhances the reproducibility and impact of this work.

However, there are a few areas for potential improvement. While the paper demonstrates performance gains, it does not explicitly analyze the speed-ups achieved through parallelization. Investigating and reporting these speed-ups would strengthen the practical relevance of the work. Additionally, the literature review, while extensive, could be more precise. For example, the iterative construction of batches by averaging single-evaluation Expected Improvement (EI) over the posterior was presented in Ginsbourger et al. (2010), and the CL-mix approach is also found in that work. Similarly, gradient-based maximization of parallel EI is explored in Marmin et al. (2016). 

I have the following specific questions and remarks: 1) Is the feasible domain A assumed to be compact, and is the objective function f continuous? 2) Why is a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) used for the initial set of points? 3) In Algorithm 1, clarification is needed regarding hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating of the GP. 4) In Section 5.2, is 'g' minimized or maximized in its definition? 5) The smoothness assumptions regarding 'g' in Section 5.2 warrant further justification, as do the derivatives involved. 6) The kernel is specified as Matérn 5/2, not 2/5. 7) Regarding Figure 3 and the experiments: was it possible to verify the consistency of GP fitting across different software implementations?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation based on decision theory.
*   Novel and efficient computational approach using IPA.
*   Empirically demonstrated superior performance, particularly in noisy settings.
*   Clear presentation and well-structured paper.
*   Code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of analysis of parallel speed-ups.
*   Minor imprecisions in the related work section.
*   Some technical details require further clarification or justification.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. Addressing the minor points raised above would further strengthen the paper and solidify its position as a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
