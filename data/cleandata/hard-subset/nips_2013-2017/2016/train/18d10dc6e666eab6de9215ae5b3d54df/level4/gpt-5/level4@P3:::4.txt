Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization algorithm based on the knowledge gradient (KG). The authors define a new acquisition function, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which selects a set of q points jointly by maximizing the expected reduction in the GP posterior mean minimum after one batch of evaluations. By construction, q-KG is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch when the goal is to minimize the minimum posterior mean (and it reduces to parallel EI under noise-free evaluations when the returned solution is restricted to sampled points). Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG requires Monte Carlo sampling, the paper develops an efficient stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and optimizes q-KG via stochastic gradient ascent. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization over A with a dynamically constructed set from posterior samples of likely minima plus previously sampled points and the candidate batch.

The work builds on sequential KG for correlated normal beliefs (Frazier et al.) and connects to batch acquisition functions such as multi-point EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Wang et al.), GP-BUCB and GP-UCB-PE (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.), and batch entropy search (Shah & Ghahramani). Empirically, q-KG outperforms or matches parallel EI variants and UCB-based batch policies on standard test functions and in hyperparameter tuning, with particularly strong gains under noise. Code is released.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic grounding: q-KG is Bayes-optimal for the one-batch remaining setting, and the reduction to parallel EI in the noise-free constrained-return case is well-explained.
- Methodological contribution: an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG via IPA that enables efficient joint optimization of batches, avoiding greedy selection.
- Practical relevance: addresses the common setting of parallel resources; experiments show consistent gains, especially for noisy evaluations where EI and UCB can struggle.
- Sound empirical work: comparisons to multiple baselines (MOE parallel EI, Spearmint heuristic EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic functions and realistic ML hyperparameter tuning; open-source implementation.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality (technical soundness)
- The computational complexity is not fully characterized. A clear cost analysis per q-KG evaluation/gradient step (including Monte Carlo sample size, O(q^3) Cholesky factorization, and dependence on discretization size M) would help assess scalability with q, M, and dimensionality d.
- The discretization An used inside q-KG depends on z(1:q). Please discuss potential bias/variance and convergence implications of this adaptive inner discretization, and sensitivity to M.
- The “Bayes-optimal” claim is for one remaining batch; the paper uses q-KG myopically across multiple iterations. It would be useful to clarify any theoretical properties of this repeated one-step policy (e.g., asymptotic consistency under mild assumptions), or to cite known results.
- The gradient estimator relies on differentiability and unique minima in the inner argmins. The supplementary material should more prominently summarize the regularity conditions and how degeneracies (ties) are handled in practice.
- The noisy setting assumes homoscedastic noise estimated by MLE. Please comment on extensions to heteroscedastic noise and robustness of performance when the noise model is misspecified.

Clarity
- The notation A is overloaded: A denotes both the continuous feasible set and the discrete set used inside q-KG to approximate the inner minimum. The paper later clarifies this, but early sections would benefit from distinct symbols (e.g., A for the feasible set and Ān for the discretization used in (4.1)).
- Algorithm 1: clarify that the inner minimizations for q-KG are over the discretized set, and specify the number of Monte Carlo samples and gradient steps used.
- The relationship to parallel ES is mentioned but not evaluated empirically; if that baseline is omitted due to computational burden, please state so.

Originality
- Extending KG to the batch setting with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution beyond sequential KG and prior batch EI/UCB approaches. While related nested batch-KG work exists for discrete sets, this paper’s continuous-domain, differentiable formulation and IPA-based optimization appear novel.

Significance
- The empirical improvements in the noisy regime are compelling and practically important. The method likely will be adopted in applications with parallel resources. That said, demonstrations remain in modest dimensions (up to 6 on test functions and 8 for CNN hyperparameters). Discussion of performance in higher dimensions (or with embedding/ARD priors mitigating high-d issues) would strengthen claims of broad applicability.

Suggestions for improvement and minor issues
- Provide runtime comparisons per iteration versus baselines, and scaling with q (e.g., q=4, 8, 16). Include ablations on M (discretization size) and number of Monte Carlo samples.
- Report optimization details for q-KG: step sizes, number of multi-starts, convergence criteria, and the fraction of total BO wall-time spent optimizing the acquisition.
- Sensitivity: include results for different kernels (e.g., SE vs Matérn) and noise levels; report learned noise variance trajectories in the noisy experiments.
- Consider adding a parallel ES or Thompson sampling (batch) baseline if feasible, or discuss limitations that prevent inclusion.
- Clarify the precise meaning of “Bayes-optimal” and the constraint under which parallel EI is recovered; a short formal statement would aid readers.

- l.140: Use distinct symbols for the continuous domain and the discretized set inside q-KG (see clarity point).

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, principled extension of KG to batch setting with a practical and efficient optimization scheme.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise, with open-source code.
- Addresses an important practical need (parallel evaluations) with clear advantages over common baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond one-step optimality and limited analysis of computational scaling and high-dimensional behavior.
- Dependence on inner discretization introduces an approximation whose impact is not fully quantified.
- Missing some baselines (e.g., batch ES) or broader dimensionality evaluations.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-executed and timely contribution to batch Bayesian optimization. The decision-theoretic grounding, efficient IPA-based optimization, and strong empirical results (notably in noisy settings) make a compelling case for acceptance. Strengthening the discussion of computational complexity, approximation effects from discretization, and practical optimization details would further improve the paper.
