- Introduces q-KG: a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that jointly selects q points by maximizing expected reduction in the GP posterior minimum, with one-step Bayes-optimality for the posterior mean minimum.
- Uses Monte Carlo estimation and an IPA-based stochastic gradient to enable practical joint optimization of the batch, plus Cholesky-based GP updates.
- Builds on prior KG work and contrasts with batch EI, UCB, and entropy-based batch methods; contributes a continuous-domain batch KG with unbiased gradient estimator and adaptive discretization.
- Empirically outperforms or matches state-of-the-art batch BO methods on synthetic benchmarks and ML tuning (MNIST, CIFAR-10), especially under noisy evaluations.
- Conceptual strength: clear decision-theoretic formulation that retains one-step Bayes-optimality (for the discretized inner problem).
- Methodological strength: efficient IPA-based stochastic gradient and careful Cholesky parameterization enabling joint batch optimization.
- Practical relevance: consistent empirical gains in noisy regimes relevant to ML hyperparameter tuning.
- Reproducibility: public code, detailed baseline implementations, and sensible hyperparameter/kernel choices.
- Novelty is moderate: main advance is extending KG to batch with IPA gradient; many components (MC, IPA, discretization) are standard.
- Lacks theoretical guarantees beyond one-step Bayes-optimality; missing convergence/regret analysis and analysis of discretization bias.
- Needs explicit statement of assumptions justifying interchange of expectation and differentiation, and discussion of non-differentiability from the min operator and non-unique minimizers.
- Scalability/runtime not addressed: no wall-clock comparisons or complexity analysis in n, q, and discretization size M despite repeated Cholesky factorizations and MC sampling.
- Sensitivity to discretization and MC sampling (M and number of samples) is not analyzed; variance and variance-reduction techniques are not discussed.
- Adaptive discretization procedure is underspecified—unclear how “samples from the global optima of the posterior” are obtained and how including z(1:q) in An affects bias/variance.
- Experimental scope limited to low-to-moderate dimensional problems (d ≤ 6); no study on higher-dimensional hyperparameter tuning or ARD/dimensionality-reduction approaches.
- Minor notational ambiguities (e.g., vector/matrix definitions in Eq. (3.1) and dimensions of D(n)(z(1:q))) need clarification.
- Algorithmic development is sound and implementable, but would benefit from regularity conditions, estimator variance analysis, and possible variance-reduction methods.
- Empirical evaluations are thorough on chosen benchmarks but should include runtime plots or per-iteration cost comparisons to assess practical utility.
- Paper is generally well written and organized but should explicitly define vector/matrix quantities, state conditions for IPA validity, detail discretization strategy, and fix minor typos.
- Originality: a nontrivial engineering contribution to batch KG optimization but closely related to existing batch EI/ES methods and prior KG literature—novelty rated moderate.
- Significance: valuable for parallel noisy hyperparameter tuning and likely to be adopted if runtime and scalability concerns are addressed.
- Pros for acceptance: principled batch KG formulation, strong empirical results in noisy settings, and public code with careful baselines.
- Cons against acceptance: moderate novelty, missing theoretical analysis and runtime/scalability evidence, limited dimensionality experiments, and discretization/notation ambiguities.
- Key questions for authors: (1) What assumptions ensure unbiased IPA gradients in Eq. (5.3) given nondifferentiability and non-unique minimizers? (2) How do runtime and memory scale with n, q, and M; can wall-clock times for larger q be reported? (3) How sensitive are results to M and MC sample count; any variance reduction used? (4) Clarify the procedure for constructing An and its practical impact.
- Overall assessment: solid and practically relevant contribution with compelling empirical gains under noise; leans toward acceptance pending clarifications on notation/discretization and additional runtime/scalability evidence.
