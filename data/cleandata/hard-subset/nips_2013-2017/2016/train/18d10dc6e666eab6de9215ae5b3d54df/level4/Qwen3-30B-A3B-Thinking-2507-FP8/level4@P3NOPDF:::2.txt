

The paper presents a parallel Bayesian optimization (BO) algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, addressing the inherently sequential nature of most BO methods. The theoretical contribution is strong, with a clear derivation of the optimal batch selection criterion. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms existing sequential and parallel BO methods, and the exposition is well-structured and accessible. The focus on parallelization is timely and practically significant for hyperparameter optimization (HPO), where computational efficiency is critical. The elegance of the Bayes-optimal batch formulation is particularly promising, offering a principled alternative to heuristic batch selection strategies.

However, the experimental evaluation presents significant limitations that undermine the paper’s claims of superiority. Three key issues require clarification and improvement:

First, the use of test set error as the optimization criterion is fundamentally problematic. As established in the literature (e.g., Provost et al., 1998), test set error is a suboptimal metric for HPO due to its discontinuous nature and susceptibility to overfitting during optimization. Metrics like AUC, log loss, or balanced accuracy—which better reflect model utility and are less prone to overfitting—are standard in rigorous HPO studies. Relying solely on test error risks optimizing for a noisy, high-variance proxy rather than the true objective, potentially misleading readers about the method’s practical value.

Second, the experimental benchmarks lack breadth and relevance. The authors evaluate only two datasets (MNIST and CIFAR10) and two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN), both confined to classification tasks. This narrow scope fails to capture the diversity of real-world HPO scenarios. Crucially, the benchmark does not leverage HPOlib [2], a widely adopted, standardized library specifically designed for fair HPO algorithm comparison. HPOlib provides diverse datasets, algorithms, and metrics, enabling robust, reproducible evaluation. Its omission weakens the paper’s credibility and limits the generalizability of its conclusions.

Third, the experimental design suffers from insufficient scale. With only two datasets and a single task type (classification), the results cannot reliably generalize across algorithm families or problem domains. It is well-documented that HPO performance varies significantly across different learning algorithms (e.g., tree-based vs. neural networks) and problem characteristics (e.g., small vs. large datasets). A convincing evaluation should include multiple tasks (e.g., regression, clustering), diverse algorithms (e.g., SVMs, random forests, GNNs), and larger benchmark suites like those in HPOlib or AutoML Challenges. The current setup risks overfitting conclusions to a narrow slice of HPO.

A related theoretical concern involves the assumption of independent, normally distributed errors in the Gaussian process model. While common in BO literature, this assumption is particularly questionable in HPO. Here, "errors" represent the discrepancy between empirical cross-validation estimates and true generalization performance—a source of structural bias, not random noise. Small sample sizes in HPO exacerbate this bias, especially in complex regions of hyperparameter space (e.g., near optimal configurations). The authors should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and discuss how their method might be adapted to account for structured bias (e.g., via heteroscedastic models or bias-aware acquisition functions).

Despite these shortcomings, the theoretical contribution remains valuable. The Bayes-optimal batch formulation is novel and well-motivated, offering a solid foundation for future work. To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) replace test set error with a more robust metric (e.g., AUC or log loss) in experiments; (2) expand the benchmark to include HPOlib or equivalent standardized resources with diverse tasks and algorithms; and (3) address the error assumption’s relevance to HPO contexts. These revisions would significantly bolster the paper’s impact and convince reviewers of the method’s practical utility.

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to parallel BO, but the experimental validation is underwhelming due to metric limitations, insufficient benchmark diversity, and lack of standardization. With the suggested improvements, this work could become a strong candidate for publication. As presented, however, the evidence does not fully support the claim of broad superiority over existing methods. The theoretical novelty is commendable, but the empirical foundation requires substantial strengthening.
