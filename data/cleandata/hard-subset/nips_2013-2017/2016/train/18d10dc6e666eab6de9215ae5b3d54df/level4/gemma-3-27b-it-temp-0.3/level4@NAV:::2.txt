Bayesian optimization is a widely adopted technique for machine learning tasks, particularly hyperparameter optimization, yet many existing methods are fundamentally sequential. This paper introduces a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm designed to efficiently compute batches in a Bayes-optimal manner. The authors demonstrate that their proposed method achieves superior performance compared to current Bayesian optimization approaches. The paper is clearly written and readily understandable. Parallelizing Bayesian optimization is a crucial area of research for practical hyperparameter tuning, and this approach is both interesting and, in my assessment, more refined than many alternatives. The determination of a Bayes-optimal batch is a particularly promising aspect. 

The authors appropriately leverage the common assumption of independent, normally distributed errors, typical in Gaussian process-based Bayesian optimization. However, it’s important to acknowledge that this assumption can be problematic in the context of hyperparameter optimization, where measurement errors often reflect the discrepancy between generalization performance and empirical estimates obtained through methods like cross-validation. Structural biases are frequently present in hyperparameter spaces, especially with limited sample sizes, and the potential impact of the independence assumption deserves further consideration. The paper’s theoretical foundations are strong, but the experimental evaluation presents some concerns. 

Specifically, three points limit the strength of the empirical results: First, the use of test set error as the optimization criterion is questionable, as it is known to be a suboptimal choice for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., [1]). Metrics like area under the ROC curve or log loss would be more appropriate, avoiding the issues inherent in threshold-based evaluation. Second, while MNIST and CIFAR10 are common benchmark datasets, I would have preferred to see results reported using the HPOlib benchmark suite [2], which is specifically designed for evaluating hyperparameter optimization algorithms. Third, the benchmark’s scope is limited, utilizing only two datasets and focusing on a single machine learning task (classification) with only two learning algorithms (logistic regression and CNN). A more diverse benchmark, acknowledging the varying performance of different optimizers across different learning algorithms, would be more compelling.

Overall, this is a valuable paper with a significant theoretical contribution. However, the current experimental results are not sufficiently robust to fully validate the proposed method due to the limitations of the benchmark. [1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998. [2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
