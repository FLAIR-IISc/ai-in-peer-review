This paper introduces the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel algorithm for batch Bayesian optimization. The method is derived from a decision-theoretic framework to select a batch of points that is one-step Bayes-optimal. The authors propose an efficient computational strategy to maximize the q-KG acquisition function by estimating its gradient using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). The paper's structure logically presents the background, the proposed method, the computational approach, and a series of numerical experiments. The empirical results demonstrate that q-KG outperforms or is competitive with several state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization algorithms, showing particularly strong performance in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The problem of parallelizing Bayesian optimization is of paramount importance for practical applications, such as large-scale hyperparameter tuning, where simultaneous evaluations are feasible. This work makes a valuable contribution to this critical area.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The proposed q-KG method is well-motivated and theoretically sound, extending the established Knowledge Gradient criterion to the batch setting in a principled way. The use of IPA to develop a tractable gradient-based optimization procedure for the acquisition function is a key technical strength that makes the approach practical.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental comparison against multiple state-of-the-art benchmarks on both synthetic and real-world machine learning tasks. The results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of q-KG, especially its robustness to noise, which is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
The paper is strong, but could be improved by addressing the following points:
*   **Positioning within Related Work:** The literature review is generally good, but a few statements could be more precise. For instance, the idea of averaging Expected Improvement over the posterior distribution of other points in the batch was introduced in Ginsbourger et al. ("Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization"). Additionally, related work on gradient-based optimization of batch acquisition functions, such as Marmin et al. ("Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design"), could be more explicitly acknowledged.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments effectively compare q-KG against other batch methods in terms of sample efficiency. However, a study of the wall-clock speed-up of the batch-sequential approach compared to a standard sequential approach would further underscore the practical impact of the method.

I also have a few technical questions and suggestions for clarification:
*   What are the formal assumptions on the domain A (e.g., compactness) and the objective function f (e.g., continuity) required for the analysis?
*   In Algorithm 1, what is the justification for selecting an initial Latin Hypercube Design over other space-filling designs?
*   The update step in Algorithm 1 involves re-training the GP hyperparameters. Could you confirm if this is performed at every iteration and comment on the computational overhead this adds?
*   In Section 5.2, the derivation of the gradient in equation (5.3) relies on interchanging the gradient and expectation operators. This requires some smoothness conditions on the function `g`, which may not be trivial given the presence of the `argmin` operator. A brief justification or a reference would strengthen this claim.
*   Similarly, the computation requires the derivative of a Cholesky factor. While this is a known result, adding a reference would improve the paper's self-containedness for readers less familiar with the technique.
*   The experiments utilize several different open-source packages for the benchmarks. Were efforts made to ensure that the underlying GP models and hyperparameter optimization routines were configured as similarly as possible across these different software frameworks to ensure a fair comparison?
