

The paper introduces a novel stochastic gradient algorithm integrated with Bayesian modeling for optimizing multivariate objective functions over finite domains. The key innovation lies in its iterative identification of multiple points for objective evaluation, enabling parallel computation. The authors address the challenge of selecting these points optimally by extending the Knowledge Gradient (KG) framework to batch settings. Specifically, they generalize KG to determine optimal batches of points, where the KG criterion estimates the expected objective improvement from adding a single point. To handle the computational intractability of optimizing over multiple points, the authors propose an algorithm leveraging Monte Carlo methods and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to approximate gradients of the q-KG criterion, thereby maximizing information gain across candidate point sets. The method is evaluated on multiple test problems, demonstrating competitive or superior performance against existing approaches, particularly those based on Confidence Bounds. The paper is well-written and presents practical utility. However, the novelty is limited: the primary contribution is the extension of KG to batch settings, which requires a more sophisticated optimization method for the batch criterion. While the IPA-based gradient estimation for q-KG is technically sound, this technique itself is standard in the field. A specific concern arises in Equation (3.1): since $x^{(1:n)}$ denotes a finite set of points (not a sequence), the definitions of $\mu(x^{(1:n)})$ and $K(x^{(1:n)}, x^{(1:n)})$ are unclear, as $\mu$ and $K$ are defined for vectors, not sequences.
