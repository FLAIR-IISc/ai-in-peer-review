

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, which selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously by maximizing a decision-theoretic acquisition function. The authors derive this method by generalizing the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting, where the goal is to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected improvement in the solution quality. To address computational challenges in maximizing q-KG, they propose an efficient gradient estimation technique using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), building upon prior work by Wang et al. (2015) who applied IPA to expected improvement (EI) acquisition functions. The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression and CNNs, showing particularly strong performance in noisy evaluation scenarios.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with a clear theoretical foundation in Bayesian optimization and decision theory. The experimental design covers both synthetic benchmarks (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical machine learning applications (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR10 CNN tuning). However, the paper lacks wall-clock time comparisons between q-KG and baseline methods, which is critical for assessing practical utility. The choice of batch size q=4 for all experiments is not justified, especially given that modern parallel computing environments often support more than four simultaneous evaluations. The authors should provide evidence on how the method scales with larger batch sizes and clarify whether the computational overhead of q-KG is justified by the performance gains.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough background section on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization. The technical details of the q-KG acquisition function and its gradient estimation are presented with sufficient mathematical rigor. The experimental section is comprehensive, providing mean and standard deviation results across multiple runs. However, the x-axis labeling in Figure 3 and Figure 4 (whether representing iterations or function evaluations) is ambiguous, and the initial design points are not consistently accounted for across all methods.

Originality: The core contribution is the extension of the knowledge gradient acquisition function to the parallel setting. While the knowledge gradient itself is not new (it was introduced in Frazier et al., 2009), the adaptation to parallel batch selection with efficient gradient computation via IPA represents a meaningful advancement. The paper adequately references related work, including the parallel EI implementation in Wang et al. (2015) (which should be referred to as q-EI to distinguish from Spearmint's parallel EI). However, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from this prior work, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and performance benefits.

Significance: The method addresses an important practical challenge in hyperparameter tuning for machine learning, where parallel evaluations can significantly reduce optimization time. The paper demonstrates clear benefits in noisy settings, which is particularly relevant for real-world applications where function evaluations are often noisy. The performance gains over existing methods, especially in noisy environments, suggest the method could be valuable for practitioners. However, the significance would be strengthened by showing concrete time savings compared to sequential optimization and random search, which are common baselines in hyperparameter tuning.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian optimization
- Demonstrates consistent performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods in noisy settings
- Offers a computationally efficient method for batch selection using IPA
- Addresses a practical need in machine learning hyperparameter tuning

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks wall-clock time comparisons to assess practical efficiency
- Batch size q=4 is not justified for all experiments
- Insufficient comparison to Wang et al. (2015) despite their relevance
- Ambiguous experimental design details (e.g., x-axis labeling)

The authors should address the following questions to strengthen their contribution: 1) Why is batch size q=4 used consistently across all experiments when modern parallel systems support larger batch sizes? 2) How does the method scale with increasing batch size, and what are the computational trade-offs? 3) Can the method be adapted for asynchronous execution like Spearmint, which would be important for practical deployment? 4) What is the actual wall-clock time advantage of q-KG compared to sequential optimization and random search? 5) Please clarify the meaning of "iterations" on the x-axis of your experimental plots and whether the initial design points are included. The paper should also correct minor issues including the missing z^(1:n) in equation (4.1) and the grammatical error in "achieve" (should be "achieves").
