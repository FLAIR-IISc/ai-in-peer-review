This paper introduces a batch-sequential version of the Knowledge Gradient criterion, termed q-KG, for efficient Bayesian Optimization. The authors provide background on related work and Gaussian processes before formally defining the parallel criterion and detailing its computation, which closely follows the established methodology of the standard KG criterion. A comprehensive set of numerical experiments demonstrates that q-KG performs favorably compared to several state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization algorithms. Overall, this is a strong paper with significant merit and is well-suited for publication in NIPS. The potential impact of parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms is considerable.

A minor criticism is the lack of analysis regarding the speed-ups achieved by transitioning from sequential to batch-sequential optimization. Additionally, some statements within the literature review could benefit from increased precision; for example, the integration of Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was previously explored in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization” (along with Constant Liar), while Chevalier et al. notably presented CL-mix in [2]. Furthermore, the work “Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design” investigates q-EI maximization using the natural gradient.

I have the following specific remarks and questions for the authors: 

1. Is the set A assumed to be compact, and is the function f continuous on A?
2. What motivated the restriction to Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) for the initial design of A?
3. In Algorithm 1, is there a provision for hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating of the Gaussian process?
4. In Section 5.2, is the definition of g a maximization or a minimization problem?
5. Concerning Section 5.2 again, the smoothness of g is not immediately obvious and requires further justification. Similarly, the differentiability of the Cholesky factor may not be trivial.
6. The Matérn kernel mentioned in the text appears to have a parameter of 5/2, rather than 2/5.
7. Regarding Figure 1 and the corresponding experiments: was it possible to replicate the results by fitting the same Gaussian processes using different software implementations?
