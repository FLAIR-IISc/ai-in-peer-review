This paper proposes a novel method for batch Bayesian optimization called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective to select a batch of points that is one-step Bayes-optimal. To make the maximization of the q-KG acquisition function tractable, the authors leverage infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an efficient gradient estimator. The empirical evaluation on synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks shows that q-KG is competitive with other state-of-the-art parallel Bayesian optimization methods in the noise-free setting and demonstrates superior performance when function evaluations are noisy. This work builds upon the sequential knowledge gradient method and extends it to the batch setting, using gradient-based techniques for acquisition function optimization similar to recent work on parallel expected improvement.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The problem of parallelizing Bayesian optimization is highly relevant for leveraging modern multi-core computing environments, with direct applications in fields that rely on expensive black-box optimization, such as automated machine learning.
*   **Quality:** The proposed q-KG acquisition function is theoretically well-founded, providing a principled, decision-theoretic approach to batch selection that generalizes the established knowledge gradient policy. The empirical results, particularly the strong performance gains in noisy settings, are compelling and represent a significant contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The background on Gaussian processes and the derivation of the q-KG method are clearly explained, providing the necessary details for an expert to understand the work.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Originality:** The novelty of the technical contribution could be clarified with respect to prior work. While q-KG is a novel acquisition function, the use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for gradient-based optimization of a batch acquisition function has been previously explored by Wang et al. (2015) for parallel expected improvement. The paper would be stronger if it more explicitly differentiated its contribution from this work.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating good performance, has some limitations.
    *   The experiments are conducted with a fixed batch size of q=4. This makes it difficult to assess how the method scales, both in terms of optimization performance and computational overhead, as the number of parallel workers increases.
    *   The evaluation lacks a comparison of wall-clock time. The optimization of the q-KG acquisition function appears computationally intensive. A comparison against baselines like parallel random search or even sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time would be crucial for understanding the practical utility and overhead of the proposed method.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The proposed algorithm operates in a synchronous fashion, requiring all `q` function evaluations from a batch to complete before the next batch is selected. A discussion on the feasibility of extending the method to an asynchronous setting, which is often more practical, would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  The work by Wang et al. (2015), "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions," seems highly relevant. Could you clarify if the "parallel EI in MOE" baseline is an implementation of their method? If so, it would be clearer to label it as q-EI to distinguish it from heuristic methods like Spearmint's. Could you also elaborate further on the differences between your approach and theirs?
2.  Your experiments are limited to q=4 parallel evaluations. Prior work has explored much larger batch sizes (e.g., up to 10 in Snoek et al., 2012, or even hundreds in other contexts). Do you have any insights into the scalability of q-KG? How does the computational cost of optimizing the acquisition function and the regret of the method behave as `q` grows?
3.  The current method is synchronous. Could it be extended to an asynchronous setting where new points are chosen as individual workers become free, as is done in Spearmint? What would be the challenges in doing so?
4.  Ultimately, wall-clock time is a key metric for parallel optimization. How does the overhead for computing a new batch with q-KG compare to other methods? Could you provide an analysis of how your method compares to sequential Bayesian optimization or parallel random search in terms of wall-clock time?
5.  In the experimental plots, what does the x-axis label "iterations" represent—the number of batches or the total number of function evaluations? Also, does the x-axis include the initial design points? If so, why do the performance curves for the different methods not start from the same value after the initial design?

**Minor Comments**

*   In Section 6.1.2: "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" -> "... and achieves a test error".
*   As a general point of style, parenthetical references should not be used as nouns. For example, in Section 2, "[10, 19] suggests parallelizing EI" should be rephrased as "Ginsbourger et al. [10] and Snoek et al. [19] suggest parallelizing EI".
*   The reference for infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) seems to be L’Ecuyer (1990). Citing Wang et al. (2015) in this context might be misleading, as they applied the method rather than introducing it.
