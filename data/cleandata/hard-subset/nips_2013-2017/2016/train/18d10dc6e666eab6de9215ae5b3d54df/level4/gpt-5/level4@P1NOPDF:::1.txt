This paper studies batch-sequential Bayesian optimization and introduces a batch version of the Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) criterion. After reviewing related work and Gaussian processes, the authors define the parallel q-KG acquisition function and detail its computation by closely following the derivation of the standard (sequential) KG. They present several numerical experiments in which q-KG appears to outperform a number of state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization methods.

Overall, this is a strong paper with clear technical contributions and convincing empirical results. I believe it has the necessary qualities for publication at NIPS. The ability to parallelize Bayesian optimization is important in practice, and the potential societal impact of more efficient global optimization under limited evaluations is substantial.

That said, I have a few concerns and suggestions:

- Missing evaluation of speedups: While the paper focuses on statistical efficiency (objective values vs. number of evaluations), it does not study practical speedups when moving from sequential to batch-sequential settings. Reporting wall-clock performance, computational overhead of q-KG optimization, and end-to-end gains would strengthen the case for parallelization in realistic scenarios.

- Literature review precision: A few statements in the related work could be more precise.
  - The idea of integrating Expected Improvement with respect to posterior distributions (and the use of Constant Liar) was already discussed in “Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization” (along with Constant Liar).
  - Chevalier et al. introduced, among others, the CL-mix strategy in [2].
  - Maximization of q-EI using natural gradients is studied in “Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design.”
Clarifying these points and positioning q-KG relative to these contributions would improve the scholarly context.

Questions and technical remarks:

- Assumptions on the domain and objective: Is the design space A compact, and is the objective f continuous? Stating these assumptions explicitly would help interpret the theoretical properties and the optimization guarantees of q-KG.

- Initial design: Why restrict A to a Latin hypercube (LHS) at the initial stage? Is this a constraint on the candidate set, or simply the initialization strategy for the GP? A short justification (and whether other space-filling designs are acceptable) would be useful.

- Hyperparameter learning: In Algorithm 1, how are GP hyperparameters handled over time? Is there re-estimation or Bayesian updating after each batch, and is the uncertainty in hyperparameters accounted for in q-KG (e.g., via marginalization or plug-in estimates)? This can materially affect performance and gradients.

- Definition and smoothness of g (Section 5.2): 
  - Is the definition of g based on a maximization or a minimization? Please check the sign.
  - The smoothness of g does not seem immediate; some justification or a reference would help, especially because gradient-based optimization of q-KG relies on differentiability.
  - Relatedly, differentiating through the Cholesky factor is non-trivial. A brief derivation or a citation for the derivative of the Cholesky decomposition (and its stability) would be valuable for reproducibility.

- Kernel specification: I believe the Matérn kernel used is ν = 5/2, not 2/5. Please correct if that is a typo.

- Experimental setup (Figure 1 and related): Was it possible to fit the same Gaussian process models using different software packages to ensure the comparisons are not confounded by implementation details? If not, a short discussion of implementation parity (e.g., optimization tolerances, jitter, numerical linear algebra backends) would increase confidence in the results.

Strengths:
- Clear formulation of q-KG and its relation to the standard KG.
- Solid empirical performance across several benchmarks, with q-KG often outperforming competitive batch methods.
- Practical relevance of batch-sequential BO for parallel experimentation.

Weaknesses / opportunities to improve:
- Lack of wall-clock or computational efficiency analysis for batch vs. sequential.
- Minor inaccuracies and omissions in the literature review.
- Some technical details (smoothness, Cholesky derivatives, hyperparameter handling) would benefit from clearer statements or references.

In summary, I recommend acceptance, conditional on addressing the points above—particularly clarifying the related work, correcting minor technical details, and, if possible, adding a brief discussion or experiment on practical speedups and hyperparameter treatment.
