This paper introduces a method for parallel batch Bayesian Optimization that selects a Bayes-optimal set of configurations for simultaneous evaluation. The core of the proposed method is an acquisition function based on the knowledge gradient for batch queries (q-KG), which quantifies the utility of evaluating a batch of points. To maximize this acquisition function efficiently, the authors employ infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute its gradient. The experimental results suggest that q-KG is competitive with existing parallel BO methods on noiseless problems and outperforms them on problems with observation noise.

The motivation for this work is strong, as efficient parallel hyperparameter optimization is a significant practical challenge. However, I have some reservations regarding the experimental validation and the novelty of the contribution. My feedback is detailed below.

### Technical Quality
The paper is evaluated on a combination of standard artificial benchmark functions and two machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (CNN for CIFAR-10 and Logistic Regression for MNIST). This provides a solid initial validation of the proposed method. However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened in several key areas.

A crucial missing component is an analysis of wall-clock time. While parallelization aims to reduce overall optimization time, the paper does not demonstrate this benefit explicitly. A comparison against simpler baselines like parallel random search or even sequential Bayesian optimization, in terms of wall-clock time, would provide a much clearer picture of the practical gains. Furthermore, the overhead of computing the q-KG batch itself is not discussed, which is a critical factor in determining the method's real-world utility.

Finally, the choice of batch size `q=4` is used consistently across all experiments without justification. It is unclear how the method's performance or computational overhead changes with different values of `q`.

### Novelty and Originality
The primary contributions appear to be the application of the knowledge gradient to the batch BO setting (q-KG) and the use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for its optimization. While the combination is interesting, both components have been established in prior literature. In particular, the use of IPA for optimizing a batch acquisition function was explored by Wang et al. in "Parallel Bayesian Global Optimization of Expensive Functions" for Expected Improvement. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could more clearly delineate their novel contributions in light of this highly related work. What are the specific advantages of using q-KG over the q-EI approach that warrant its development?

### Impact and Usefulness
The problem of parallel hyperparameter optimization is of high practical importance across numerous fields, including machine learning, robotics, and computational biology. Methods that can effectively leverage multi-core architectures to speed up this process are highly valuable, and this work addresses a very relevant problem.

### Clarity and Presentation
The paper is generally well-written. The background material and technical details of the proposed method are described clearly, and the experimental setup is detailed enough to understand the comparisons being made.

### Questions for the Authors
1.  The work of Wang et al. on parallel Expected Improvement using IPA seems highly relevant. Have you considered a direct comparison to this method? If the "parallel EI in MOE" baseline is indeed this method, I suggest renaming it to q-EI for consistency and explicitly discussing the differences between your q-KG approach and their q-EI to avoid confusion.
2.  The experiments are limited to `q=4`. Modern computational environments often allow for much larger-scale parallelism (e.g., dozens or even hundreds of parallel workers, as seen in other BO literature). What are the theoretical or practical limitations of q-KG as `q` increases? How does the computational cost of finding the optimal batch scale with `q`, and what gains in optimization performance can be expected from larger batches?
3.  The proposed method operates in a synchronous batch mode, where the system must wait for all `q` evaluations to finish. Have you considered extending the approach to an asynchronous setting, where new jobs are dispatched as soon as workers become free? This is often a more practical model for real-world distributed systems.
4.  As mentioned above, wall-clock time is the ultimate metric for parallel optimization. Could you provide an analysis of how q-KG compares to sequential BO and parallel random search in terms of actual time? What is the computational overhead (in seconds) for finding a batch of `q=4` points?
5.  In the experimental plots, what does the x-axis label "iterations" represent—the number of individual function evaluations or the number of batches? Additionally, does this count include the initial design points? If so, why do the performance curves for different methods not all start from the same initial value after the initial design phase?

### Minor Comments
*   Equation (4.1): Is a term z^(1:n) missing from the conditioning set?
*   Grammar: In the abstract or introduction, "...this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" should be "...and achieves a test error."
*   Citation Style: Please avoid using parenthetical references as nouns (e.g., "[16] suggests..."). The preferred format is "Snoek et al. [16] suggest...".
*   Citation: The reference for Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) seems incorrect. As far as I know, Wang et al. apply the method but did not introduce it. Please cite the original work that introduced IPA.
