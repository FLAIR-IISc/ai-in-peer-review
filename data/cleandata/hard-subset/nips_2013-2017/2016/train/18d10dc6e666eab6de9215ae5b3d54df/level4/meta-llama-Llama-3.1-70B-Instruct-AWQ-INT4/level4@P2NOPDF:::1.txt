This paper presents a batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization approach, introducing a batch-sequential version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) criterion. After providing background on related work and Gaussian processes, the parallel (q-KG) criterion is defined, and its computation is detailed. The authors also present numerical experiments where q-KG appears to outperform state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization algorithms. 

Overall, this is a well-written paper that possesses important qualities that would justify its publication in NIPS. The potential impact of parallelizing Bayesian optimization algorithms on society is significant. However, there are a few minor criticisms. Firstly, the paper does not study the speed-ups achieved by transitioning from sequential to batch-sequential optimization. Secondly, some statements in the literature review are slightly imprecise. For instance, the idea of integrating Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was already explored in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" and "Constant Liar." Additionally, Chevalier et al. presented the CL-mix method in [2]. Furthermore, q-EI maximization using natural gradient is studied in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design."

I also have several remarks and questions regarding the paper. Firstly, is the set A compact, and is the function f continuous? Secondly, why is the set A restricted to be a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) at the initial stage? In Algorithm 1, what about hyperparameter re-estimation or Bayesian updating? In Section 5.2, is it a maximization or minimization problem in the definition of g? Additionally, the smoothness of g is not explicitly stated, and the derivative of the Cholesky factor might not be obvious. It is also worth noting that the Matérn parameter is 5/2, not 2/5. 

Regarding the experiments presented in Figure 1, was it possible to fit the same Gaussian processes using different software packages? This would help to verify the results and increase confidence in the conclusions drawn.
