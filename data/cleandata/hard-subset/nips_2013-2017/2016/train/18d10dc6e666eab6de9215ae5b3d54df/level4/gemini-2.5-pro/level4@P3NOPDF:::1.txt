This paper introduces the q-Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), a novel criterion for batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization. The authors successfully extend the established Knowledge Gradient (KG) principle to the parallel setting, enabling the simultaneous selection of a batch of `q` points for evaluation. The paper begins with a solid background on Gaussian Processes and related work in parallel Bayesian Optimization. It then provides a detailed derivation of the q-KG criterion and, crucially, its gradient, which is essential for its practical optimization. The effectiveness of the proposed method is demonstrated through a series of numerical experiments on synthetic and benchmark functions, where q-KG shows superior performance compared to several state-of-the-art batch-sequential algorithms.

**Strengths**

This is a high-quality paper with several significant strengths that warrant its publication at NIPS.

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The extension of the Knowledge Gradient to the batch setting is a well-motivated and important contribution. The proposed q-KG criterion is an elegant and principled approach to balancing exploration and exploitation across multiple simultaneous queries.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and its gradient is thorough and technically sound. The explanation of the computational strategy, which involves Monte Carlo estimation, is clear and provides a practical path to implementation.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. The comparison against established methods, including q-EI and variants of the Constant Liar heuristic, effectively demonstrates the practical advantages of the q-KG criterion in terms of sample efficiency.
4.  **Potential Impact:** The problem of parallelizing Bayesian Optimization is of paramount importance for its application to real-world problems where function evaluations can be run concurrently (e.g., large-scale hyperparameter tuning, automated scientific experiments, and industrial design). This work represents a significant step forward in making Bayesian Optimization more practical and scalable.

**Areas for Improvement**

While the paper is strong overall, a few points could be addressed to further enhance its clarity and completeness.

1.  **Analysis of Computational Cost and Speed-up:** The paper focuses exclusively on sample efficiency (i.e., function value vs. number of evaluations). While this is the primary metric in many BO applications, a discussion of the computational trade-offs would be valuable. An analysis of the wall-clock time improvement from moving from a sequential to a batch-sequential approach, even if brief, would provide a more complete picture of the method's practical utility.
2.  **Precision in Literature Review:** The literature review is generally good, but a few citations could be more precise to accurately reflect prior contributions.
    *   The idea of integrating Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to the posterior distribution over the incumbent's location was also a key component of the q-EI method proposed in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization," which also discussed the Constant Liar heuristic.
    *   The work by Chevalier et al. in [2] is more specifically credited with introducing the CL-mix variant, which is an important extension of the basic Constant Liar principle.
    *   The authors might also find it relevant to cite "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design," which studies the maximization of q-EI using natural gradient-based methods.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

I have a few specific questions and remarks for the authors:

*   **Assumptions:** Could the authors explicitly state the theoretical assumptions on the search domain `A` (e.g., compactness) and the objective function `f` (e.g., continuity) that are required for the method?
*   **Initial Design:** The initial design points are selected using a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS). Is there a particular reason for this choice over other space-filling designs, or is this simply a common and effective practical choice?
*   **Hyperparameter Management:** Algorithm 1 does not specify how the Gaussian Process hyperparameters are managed after the initial fit. Are they fixed throughout the optimization, or are they re-estimated (e.g., via maximizing the marginal likelihood) after each batch is evaluated? Clarification here would be helpful for reproducibility.
*   **Definition of g in Section 5.2:** In the definition of the function `g`, is the operation intended to be a `max` or a `min`? Please double-check this formulation.
*   **Smoothness of g:** The paper relies on the differentiability of `g` for gradient-based optimization. Given that `g` involves a `max` operation over posterior samples, its smoothness is not immediately obvious. Could the authors add a brief comment on the differentiability of this function?
*   **Cholesky Derivative:** The computation of the gradient involves the derivative of a Cholesky factor. As this is a non-trivial step, a brief reference or note on its computation would be beneficial for readers seeking to implement the algorithm.
*   **Typo:** There appears to be a minor typo in the description of the kernel. The Matérn kernel is likely the 5/2 variant, not 2/5.
*   **Reproducibility of GP models:** Regarding the experiments shown in Figure 1, different GP software packages can sometimes yield slightly different results. Were the results cross-validated or compared against models fit with other standard GP libraries to ensure that the performance gains are attributable to the acquisition function rather than implementation details of the underlying model?

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant and practical contribution to the Bayesian Optimization literature. My comments are intended to help the authors further strengthen an already impressive manuscript. I recommend acceptance.
