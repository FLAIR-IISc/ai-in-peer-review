This paper introduces a parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization, targeting scenarios where function evaluations are expensive and can be performed in parallel. The core contribution is a novel acquisition function, q-KG, designed to select a batch of `q` points for simultaneous evaluation at each iteration. The authors also present an efficient, stochastic gradient-based algorithm for optimizing this acquisition function, which is a non-trivial problem. The effectiveness of the proposed method is demonstrated through empirical results on several benchmark problems.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of accelerating Bayesian Optimization through parallelization. The ability to select batches of points for distributed evaluation is highly valuable for many data-driven applications where wall-clock time is a critical bottleneck.
*   The formulation of the q-Knowledge Gradient is an elegant and principled extension of the sequential KG policy. It provides a well-motivated approach for selecting a diverse set of points that jointly maximize the expected improvement in the optimum.
*   The method is technically sound, and the experimental evaluation is thorough. The results convincingly show that q-KG outperforms other batch Bayesian Optimization strategies, supporting the paper's claims. The paper is generally well-written and the core ideas are communicated clearly.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The reliance on Gaussian Processes (GPs) may limit the method's applicability in very high-dimensional settings, such as tuning large neural network architectures. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on the scalability of the GP model and the q-KG method with respect to input dimensionality.
*   While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a formal theoretical analysis. The inclusion of theoretical results, such as convergence guarantees or an error bound for the q-KG algorithm, would be a significant addition. Furthermore, a quantitative analysis of the computational complexity, especially with regard to the batch size `q` and the input dimension, would provide valuable insights for practitioners.

**Minor Comments and Typos:**

*   **l.36:** The phrase "the set of points to evaluate next that is" appears to be incomplete. Please revise for clarity.
*   **l.95:** The set `A` is used without a prior definition. Please clarify what this notation represents.
*   **l.104:** The input argument for the posterior mean µⁿ(·) is not specified. Please clarify.
*   **l.128:** It would be helpful to provide a brief discussion of alternative parallel acquisition functions, such as those based on Expected Improvement, to better contextualize the contribution of q-KG.
*   **l.140:** The notation `A` seems to be used for multiple concepts. Using distinct notations would help prevent ambiguity.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The proposed q-KG method is novel, well-motivated, and shown to be empirically effective. Despite the suggestions for improvement regarding theoretical analysis and high-dimensional scalability, the work is a valuable step forward for parallel optimization.
