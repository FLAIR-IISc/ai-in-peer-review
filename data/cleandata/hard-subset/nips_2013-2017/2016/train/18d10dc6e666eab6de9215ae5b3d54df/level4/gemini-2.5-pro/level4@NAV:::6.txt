This work introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization that identifies a Bayes-optimal set of configurations for simultaneous evaluation. The utility of a given batch is quantified using the knowledge gradient (q-KG) as the acquisition function. To maximize this function efficiently, the authors employ infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of q-KG. The experimental evaluation demonstrates that the proposed q-KG approach is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian optimization techniques on noiseless problems and surpasses them when observation noise is present. While the goal of enabling efficient parallel hyperparameter optimization on multi-core architectures is commendable, several aspects of the work require clarification, and the experimental evidence is not fully persuasive.

**Technical Quality**
The paper's evaluation relies on standard artificial benchmark functions, supplemented by experiments optimizing a CNN for CIFAR-10 and logistic regression for MNIST, to demonstrate the method's effectiveness. A key omission in the experimental section is an analysis of wall-clock time. Such an experiment would be crucial to quantify the practical gains of this parallel approach over alternatives like standard random search or sequential Bayesian optimization. Furthermore, the rationale for fixing the batch size `q` to 4 across all experiments is not provided.

**Novelty/Originality**
The core components of the proposed approach, namely the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for its optimization, are established techniques. Specifically, the paper acknowledges "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions," a prior work that has already demonstrated the use of IPA for batch selection with the expected improvement acquisition function. This raises questions about the incremental contribution of the current work.

**Impact/Usefulness**
Methods for parallel hyperparameter tuning are of significant practical importance, with direct applications in numerous fields, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

**Clarity/Presentation**
The manuscript is well-written; the technical background and methodological details are explained clearly, and the experimental setup provides sufficient information to understand the comparisons performed.

**Questions to the Authors:**
1.  Given its relevance, was a comparison performed against the method from Wang et al. (2015)? If this corresponds to the "parallel EI in MOE" baseline, we suggest renaming it to q-EI for consistency and to differentiate it from Spearmint's parallel method. A clearer statement of the differences between your work and theirs would strengthen the paper.
2.  Modern multi-core systems support parallelism far exceeding `q=4`. For instance, "Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks" (ICML'15) used up to 800 parallel evaluations, and "Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms" (NIPS'12) used up to 10. Could you provide insights into your method's scalability and the expected performance gains with a larger number of parallel workers?
3.  The proposed method operates synchronously. Have you considered an extension to an asynchronous setting, similar to the approach used by Spearmint?
4.  Ultimately, wall-clock time is a critical performance metric. How does your method compare against random search or a simple sequential Bayesian optimization baseline in terms of total time? What is the computational overhead required to select each new batch of configurations?
5.  Could you clarify the meaning of "iterations" on the x-axis of your plots? Does this refer to the number of batches evaluated or the total number of function evaluations? Additionally, does the x-axis include the initial design points? If it does, why do the performance curves for different methods not originate from the same value?

**Minor Comments:**
*   In Equation (4.1), should there be a z^(1:n) term?
*   In the phrase "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error," the verb should be "achieves."
*   Citations should not be used as nouns. For example, "\[16] suggests parallelizing" should be rephrased to "Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing."
*   The citation for infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) might be incorrect. Wang et al. (2015) apply the method but did not introduce it.
