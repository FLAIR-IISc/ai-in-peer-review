This paper studies batch Bayesian optimization under expensive function evaluations and introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) acquisition function to select multiple points for simultaneous evaluation. The authors also propose an efficient method to compute q-KG and present empirical results that support its effectiveness and align with the theoretical development.

Strengths:
- The problem is important: enabling parallel, distributed evaluations can substantially reduce wall-clock time in data-driven applications where each evaluation is costly.
- Extending the knowledge gradient to a batch setting is a natural and interesting idea. Selecting q points per iteration can significantly improve overall efficiency compared with sequential policies.
- The methodology appears solid, and the experiments are convincing and well executed.
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow.

Concerns and suggestions:
- The Gaussian process assumption may be restrictive in high-dimensional settings or when the underlying model is a neural network. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the practical implications of this assumption, and to provide theoretical guarantees (e.g., error bounds or regret rates) under explicit conditions.
- Please include a quantitative analysis of computational complexity, describing how the cost of evaluating and optimizing q-KG scales with the batch size q, the number of observations, and the input dimension. Discussion of memory requirements would also be helpful.
- Positioning relative to existing batch acquisition functions (e.g., parallel Expected Improvement) could be expanded. A brief comparison of theoretical properties and practical trade-offs would clarify the contribution.

Minor comments and typos:
- l.36: Consider rephrasing “the set of points to evaluate next that is” for clarity.
- l.95: Please define A explicitly when it first appears.
- l.104: Clarify the argument(s) of μ^n and its dependence on observed data.
- l.128: A short discussion of the parallel EI algorithm would improve context.
- l.140: Consider using different notation to avoid overloading A.
