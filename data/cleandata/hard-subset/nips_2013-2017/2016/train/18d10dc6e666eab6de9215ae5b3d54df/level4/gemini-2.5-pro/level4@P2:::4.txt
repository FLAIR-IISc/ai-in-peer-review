This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, a novel algorithm for batch Bayesian optimization. The core problem addressed is the efficient global optimization of expensive-to-evaluate black-box functions when multiple function evaluations can be performed in parallel. The authors propose a new acquisition function, the q-KG factor, which is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective to select a batch of `q` points that is one-step Bayes-optimal. A key contribution is the development of an efficient computational method to maximize this acquisition function using a gradient-based optimizer, for which an unbiased gradient estimator is derived via Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). The method is evaluated on synthetic benchmark functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression and convolutional neural networks, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing batch Bayesian optimization methods, particularly in noisy settings.

The work builds upon the established knowledge gradient (KG) framework for sequential Bayesian optimization and extends it to the batch setting. It distinguishes itself from other parallel methods, such as those based on Expected Improvement (EI) or Upper Confidence Bounds (UCB), by directly optimizing the expected value of the posterior mean optimum after the batch evaluation. This provides a more principled basis for selecting the batch of points collectively, rather than constructing it greedily.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Novel Approach:** The generalization of the knowledge gradient to the batch setting is a natural and powerful idea. The method is well-motivated from a decision-theoretic perspective, aiming to find the one-step Bayes-optimal batch of points. This provides a solid theoretical foundation compared to more heuristic approaches for parallelizing acquisition functions.

2.  **Significant Technical Contribution:** A major challenge in this setting is the optimization of the acquisition function over a `q*d` dimensional space. The paper's primary technical contribution is an effective solution to this problem. By deriving an unbiased stochastic gradient of the q-KG factor using IPA, the authors enable the use of efficient gradient-based optimization methods, making the proposed approach computationally feasible.

3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough empirical study on a variety of synthetic and real-world problems. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms several state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization algorithms. The performance improvement is particularly pronounced in the noisy evaluation setting, which is a common and important scenario in practice (e.g., when evaluating models on a subset of data). The experiments on tuning machine learning models (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10) highlight the practical significance of the proposed method.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, and the proposed method is clearly described. The authors provide open-source code, which is a significant plus for reproducibility and for the broader community.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** While the method is empirically strong and well-motivated, the paper would be further strengthened by a theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm. For instance, an analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties or regret bounds, similar to those established for UCB-based methods, would provide deeper insight and formal guarantees. Additionally, a more formal analysis of the computational complexity with respect to the batch size `q` and the input dimension `d` would be valuable.

2.  **Limitations of the Gaussian Process Model:** The method inherits the standard limitations of GP-based Bayesian optimization. The GP assumption can be restrictive, and its computational cost scales cubically with the number of observations, which can be a bottleneck. More critically, GP performance can degrade in very high-dimensional search spaces, such as those encountered when tuning large neural network architectures. While the CNN experiment is a good showcase, a brief discussion of these scalability challenges would be welcome.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

The paper is generally clear, but the following points could be improved for the final version:

*   **l.36:** The phrase "the set of points to evaluate next that is" is slightly awkward. Consider rephrasing to "chooses the set of points for the next evaluation that is optimal...".
*   **l.95:** The feasible domain `A` is introduced in the Introduction, but it might be helpful to restate its definition at the beginning of Section 4 for clarity.
*   **l.104:** What is the input for `µ^(n)` in the expression `min_{x∈A} µ^(n)(x)`? It appears to be `x`, but the notation could be made more explicit, e.g., `min_{x'∈A} µ^(n)(x')`.
*   **l.128:** When comparing q-KG to parallel EI, a brief sentence reminding the reader how parallel EI is typically implemented (e.g., via greedy selection or heuristics like "constant liar") would provide helpful context for readers less familiar with that specific literature.
*   **l.140 (and Section 5.3):** The paper uses `A` to denote both the continuous feasible domain for the optimization problem (in eq. 4.2) and the discrete set over which the posterior minimum is computed (in eq. 4.1 and 5.4). This is a potential source of confusion. Using a different notation, such as `A_d` or `A_disc`, for the discretized set in Section 5 would improve clarity.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of batch Bayesian optimization.
    *   It proposes a novel and principled acquisition function (q-KG) based on a sound decision-theoretic framework.
    *   It provides a significant technical contribution by developing an efficient method to optimize the acquisition function via stochastic gradients (IPA).
    *   The empirical results are comprehensive and demonstrate a clear performance advantage over state-of-the-art methods, especially in noisy environments.
    *   The paper is well-written and includes publicly available code.

*   **Arguments against Acceptance:**
    *   The work lacks a theoretical analysis of convergence or regret bounds.
    *   It relies on a standard GP model, inheriting its known limitations in high-dimensional settings without proposing specific remedies.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The strengths, particularly the novel formulation and the strong empirical results, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The paper presents a complete and valuable piece of research that is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.
