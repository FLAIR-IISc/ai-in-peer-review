

This work proposes a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization that computes a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations via the knowledge gradient (q-KG). The authors employ infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to efficiently maximize the q-KG acquisition function. Experiments demonstrate competitive performance against existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noiseless benchmarks and superior results on noisy problems. While the idea of parallel hyperparameter optimization for multi-core systems is compelling, several aspects remain unclear, and I am not fully convinced by the experimental evaluation. Below are my detailed comments:

**Technical Quality**  
The paper uses standard artificial benchmarks and applies the method to CNN and logistic regression optimization on CIFAR-10/MNIST. However, the experimental section lacks a wall-clock time analysis or comparison to random search and sequential Bayesian optimization. It is crucial to demonstrate the practical efficiency gains of parallelization. Additionally, the choice of q=4 for all experiments is not justified—why not test larger batch sizes? The paper should clarify this decision.

**Novelty/Originality**  
The use of q-KG as an acquisition function and IPA for its maximization is not novel. The paper references "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions" (which uses IPA with expected improvement), yet fails to clearly distinguish their q-KG approach from prior work. If the method builds directly on this prior work, it should be explicitly stated and contrasted (e.g., "Unlike q-EI in [prior work], we extend to q-KG"). The paper should clarify this to avoid confusion.

**Impact/Usefulness**  
Parallel hyperparameter tuning is highly relevant for real-world applications across machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics. The method’s potential impact is promising, but this depends on clear evidence of scalability and efficiency gains.

**Clarity/Presentation**  
The technical background and methodology are well-explained. The experimental setup is sufficient for understanding the comparisons, but the lack of wall-clock time metrics weakens the practical contribution.

**Questions for Authors**  
1.  Wang et al. (2015) introduced a parallel EI method (q-EI) using IPA, which is directly comparable to your q-KG approach. Why was this method not included in comparisons? If your approach is equivalent to their q-EI (but generalized to q-KG), you should explicitly state this and clarify the distinction (e.g., "We extend [Wang et al.] to q-KG").  
2.  Modern multi-core systems support far more parallel runs than q=4 (e.g., ICML'15 used 800 parallel DNGO runs, LION'12 used 25 SMAC instances, NIPS'12 used up to 10 Spearmint runs). What is the scalability ceiling of your method? How do gains diminish as q increases beyond 4?  
3.  The method assumes synchronous execution. Could it be adapted for asynchronous evaluation (like Spearmint), especially for heterogeneous or unreliable compute environments?  
4.  Wall-clock time is critical for real-world adoption. How does your method compare to random search and sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of time-to-solution? What is the computational overhead of generating a new batch?  
5.  The x-axis labels in your plots ("iterations") are ambiguous. Do they represent function evaluations or batches? Does the count include the initial design? If so, why do methods start from different initial points?

**Minor Comments**  
-  Equation (4.1) appears to miss a term: is \( z^{(1:n)} \) omitted?  
-  "achieve a test error" → "achieves a test error" (subject-verb agreement).  
-  Avoid parenthetical references as nouns: e.g., "[16] suggests" → "Snoek et al. [16] suggest".  
-  Wang et al. (2015) does not introduce IPA; they apply it. The reference should be corrected to the original IPA source (e.g., [Glynn & Whitt, 1989]).  

**Summary**  
The core idea of using q-KG with IPA for parallel Bayesian optimization is reasonable, but the paper lacks critical comparisons (e.g., to Wang et al. 2015), scalability analysis for larger q, and wall-clock time evidence. Without these, the practical significance and novelty remain unclear. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the contribution.
