

# Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which addresses the important practical problem of parallelizing Bayesian optimization to accelerate hyperparameter tuning in machine learning. The authors extend the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting, providing a decision-theoretically sound approach that selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously. The q-KG criterion is defined as the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor after evaluating a batch of points, and the authors develop an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize this acquisition function. The paper demonstrates that q-KG consistently outperforms existing batch BO methods, particularly in noisy evaluation settings, on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks.

The paper builds upon a solid foundation of previous work in Bayesian optimization. The sequential KG method was introduced by Frazier et al. (2009) and has been used in several NIPS papers (e.g., Snoek et al., 2012; Scott et al., 2011). The parallelization of Bayesian optimization has been explored in several recent works, including parallel EI (Ginsbourger et al., 2010; Chevalier et al., 2014), parallel UCB (Contal et al., 2014; Desautels et al., 2014), and parallel entropy search (Shah and Ghahramani, 2015). The authors properly contextualize their contribution relative to these works, noting that previous parallel methods typically optimize batches in an iterative greedy fashion, whereas q-KG jointly optimizes the entire batch.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide a rigorous decision-theoretic derivation of the q-KG criterion and a detailed computational approach using IPA. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including both synthetic test functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR10). The comparison against multiple state-of-the-art baselines (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) is thorough, and the results consistently demonstrate q-KG's superiority, particularly in noisy settings. The paper carefully acknowledges limitations and provides a clear computational strategy for handling the intractable nature of q-KG evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with clear notation and logical flow. The background on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization is accessible to readers familiar with the field. The description of the q-KG criterion and its computational approach is detailed and precise. However, some sections could benefit from additional clarification: the distinction between the feasible domain A in the acquisition function and the discretized set used for optimization (Section 5.3) could be made clearer, and the relationship between q-KG and expected improvement (Section 4.2) could be more explicitly stated.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the knowledge gradient to the batch setting with a theoretically sound approach. While previous work has explored parallel Bayesian optimization (e.g., parallel EI, parallel UCB), the joint optimization of the batch using a decision-theoretic criterion represents a novel approach that differs from the iterative greedy methods of prior work. The authors properly cite related work, including the parallel EI methods of Ginsbourger et al. (2010) and Chevalier et al. (2014), and clarify how their approach differs from these. The paper does not simply rehash previous work but provides a new acquisition function with theoretical justification and efficient computational implementation.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high, as batch Bayesian optimization has substantial practical importance for accelerating hyperparameter tuning in machine learning, especially when parallel computing resources are available. The paper demonstrates clear practical benefits, with q-KG consistently finding better solutions faster than existing methods, particularly in noisy settings where the gains are most pronounced. The code availability on GitHub further enhances the paper's impact by enabling reproducibility and adoption by the research community.

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical foundation for batch Bayesian optimization is strong and well-justified
- Efficient computational approach using IPA enables practical implementation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse synthetic and real-world problems
- Clear demonstration of superiority over existing methods, especially in noisy settings
- Code availability enhances reproducibility and practical impact

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could more explicitly discuss the computational complexity of q-KG compared to existing methods
- The initial Latin hypercube design could be better motivated (why LHS specifically, and why not more adaptive initial sampling?)
- The relationship between q-KG and expected improvement needs more precise clarification (Section 4.2)
- The paper could more thoroughly address the trade-off between exploration and exploitation in the batch setting

**Pro and Con Acceptance List:**

*Pro:*
- Novel theoretical contribution to batch Bayesian optimization
- Strong experimental validation across diverse problem domains
- Practical significance for accelerating hyperparameter tuning
- Efficient computational implementation
- Clear presentation of methodology and results

*Con:*
- Limited discussion of computational complexity trade-offs
- Some aspects of the theoretical development could be clarified
- Initial sampling strategy could be better justified
- The paper does not explicitly compare to some recent batch BO methods (e.g., parallel predictive entropy search)

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The technical quality is high, the experimental results are convincing, and the practical significance is clear. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution. The work is well-suited for publication at NIPS, as it represents a substantial advance in a topic of significant interest to the machine learning community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
