This work introduces a batch-sequential formulation of the Knowledge Gradient criterion for Bayesian Optimization. Following a review of relevant literature and Gaussian processes, the paper defines the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The authors detail a computational approach for this criterion that closely mirrors the methodology used for the standard KG. The paper concludes with a series of numerical experiments demonstrating that the proposed q-KG method shows superior performance compared to several state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian Optimization algorithms.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution. The work presents significant qualities that make it a strong candidate for publication at this conference. The societal impact of developing effective parallel Bayesian optimization methods is substantial, further strengthening the paper's relevance.

Minor weaknesses include the absence of an analysis on the computational speed-ups achieved by the batch-sequential approach over its sequential counterpart. Additionally, the literature review contains a few minor imprecisions. For example, the concept of integrating Expected Improvement over posterior distributions, along with the Constant Liar heuristic, was previously discussed in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization." Similarly, the CL-mix method was a notable contribution from Chevalier et al. in [2]. Furthermore, the maximization of q-EI via natural gradients was explored in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design."

I also have the following specific questions and remarks:

*   Are there assumptions that the domain `A` is compact and the function `f` is continuous?
*   What is the motivation for restricting the initial design of experiments `A` to a Latin Hypercube Sample?
*   Regarding Algorithm 1, how is hyperparameter re-estimation (or Bayesian updating) handled within the optimization loop?
*   In Section 5.2, should the definition of the function `g` involve a maximization or a minimization?
*   Also concerning Section 5.2, the assumed smoothness of `g` is not immediately apparent and may warrant further justification.
*   In a similar vein, the derivation of the Cholesky factor's derivative could be non-trivial and might benefit from additional explanation.
*   A minor typo: should the Matérn kernel parameter be 5/2 instead of 2/5?
*   For the experiments presented in Figure 1, were the Gaussian Process models cross-validated or compared against implementations in different software packages to ensure consistency?
