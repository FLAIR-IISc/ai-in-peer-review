The paper introduces an innovative stochastic gradient algorithm integrated with Bayesian modeling for optimizing a multivariate objective function within a finite domain. The distinctive aspect of this algorithm lies in its ability to identify multiple points for objective function computation in each iteration, allowing for parallelization. The challenge is to optimally determine these sampling points. To address this, the authors utilize the Knowledge Gradient framework, which estimates the expected improvement in the objective value upon adding a sampling point, and extend this approach to make decisions on multiple sampling points in batches. Due to the computational intractability of this problem, the authors develop an algorithm leveraging Monte Carlo and Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis to estimate relevant gradients, thereby maximizing information gain across candidate sampling sets. The algorithm is applied to various test problems, demonstrating competitive performance with existing approaches, primarily those based on Confidence Bounds. The paper is well-written and holds potential for practical applications. However, the novelty of the work seems somewhat limited, as it builds upon a previously explored approach (Knowledge Gradient) by extending it to a batch setting, which necessitates a more sophisticated optimization method for multiple points. The primary innovation, in my opinion, lies in the estimation of the q-KG gradient using an IPA approach, which, while valuable, is a relatively standard technique. One specific comment I have is regarding equation (3.1): given that x^(1:n) represents a finite sequence of vectors, the definitions of mu(x^(1:n)) and K(x(1:n),x(1:n)) are unclear, as mu and K are functions of single or paired vectors, not sequences.
