This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, which is derived from a decision-theoretical perspective. The q-KG method measures the utility of evaluating a batch of configurations using the knowledge gradient, a generalization of expected improvement. To efficiently maximize the acquisition function, the authors present a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of q-KG.

The paper provides a clear and concise background section, describing the necessary technical details and related work in Bayesian optimization. The experiment section is well-organized and contains all necessary information to comprehend the performed comparisons. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the q-KG method in three experiments, showing that it performs competitively with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on problems without observation noise and outperforms these methods on problems with observation noise.

The q-KG method is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of its properties and behavior. The use of IPA to estimate the gradient of q-KG is a key contribution of the paper, as it allows for efficient maximization of the acquisition function. The authors also provide a clear and concise description of the q-KG algorithm and its implementation.

The paper has several strengths, including its novelty, originality, and impact. The q-KG method is a new and innovative approach to batch Bayesian optimization, and its ability to handle noisy observations is a significant contribution to the field. The paper is also well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a wide range of readers.

However, there are some minor issues with the paper. For example, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of why they chose to set q to 4 for all experiments. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the q-KG method and its scalability to larger batch sizes.

Overall, I believe that this paper is a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, and I recommend its acceptance. However, I do have some questions and minor comments that I would like the authors to address.

Questions:

1. Have you compared the q-KG method to other parallel Bayesian optimization methods, such as the one proposed in "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions"?
2. How does the q-KG method scale to larger batch sizes, and what are the limitations of the method in terms of computational complexity?
3. Is it possible to extend the q-KG method to run asynchronously, such as for example Spearmint does?
4. Have you evaluated the wall-clock time of the q-KG method compared to random search or simple sequential Bayesian optimization?
