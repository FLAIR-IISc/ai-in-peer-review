This paper addresses the sequential nature of most Bayesian optimization methods, which are widely used for tasks such as hyperparameter optimization. The authors introduce a novel parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm designed to identify Bayes-optimal batches of points for simultaneous evaluation. The work demonstrates superior performance of the proposed method when compared against established Bayesian optimization techniques. Overall, the manuscript is clear, well-structured, and accessible.

The central contribution is timely and significant, as the parallelization of Bayesian optimization is a critical challenge for its practical application in hyperparameter tuning. The proposed method is conceptually appealing and presents a more elegant formulation than many existing alternatives. The formal determination of a Bayes-optimal batch is a particularly compelling aspect of this work.

A minor concern lies with the assumption of independent, normally distributed errors. While this is a standard assumption for many Gaussian Process-based BO methods, its validity can be questionable in the context of hyperparameter optimization. In this domain, measurement errors often stem from the discrepancy between true generalization performance and empirical estimates (e.g., via cross-validation), which can exhibit structural biases in certain regions of the search space, particularly with limited data. A brief discussion acknowledging this potential limitation would strengthen the paper.

While the theoretical foundations of the paper are robust, the experimental evaluation is unfortunately not as convincing and could be substantially improved in three key areas:

1.  The choice of test set error as the optimization objective is a significant weakness. It is widely recognized that such threshold-based metrics are not ideal for hyperparameter tuning due to several known issues (e.g., [1]). The evaluation would be more meaningful if based on more suitable metrics like AUC or log loss.

2.  The experiments are conducted on the MNIST and CIFAR10 datasets. Although these are standard image classification datasets, the evaluation would benefit from the use of a dedicated and standardized benchmark suite for hyperparameter optimization, such as HPOlib [2], which is designed for this specific purpose.

3.  The empirical validation is limited in scope, employing only two datasets for a single machine learning task (classification) and two model architectures (logistic regression and a CNN). Given that the performance of optimization algorithms can vary considerably across different problem types and models, a more diverse and comprehensive benchmark would be necessary to substantiate the claims of the method's general effectiveness.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and elegant theoretical contribution to the field of parallel Bayesian optimization. However, the current experimental evidence is insufficient to fully support the proposed method's superiority due to the limited scope and design of the benchmarks. I believe the paper has strong potential, but its claims would be significantly bolstered by a more thorough empirical evaluation.

[1] Provost, Foster J., Tom Fawcett, and Ron Kohavi. "The case against accuracy estimation for comparing induction algorithms." ICML. Vol. 98. 1998.
[2] Eggensperger, Katharina, et al. "Towards an empirical foundation for assessing bayesian optimization of hyperparameters." NIPS workshop on Bayesian Optimization in Theory and Practice. 2013.
