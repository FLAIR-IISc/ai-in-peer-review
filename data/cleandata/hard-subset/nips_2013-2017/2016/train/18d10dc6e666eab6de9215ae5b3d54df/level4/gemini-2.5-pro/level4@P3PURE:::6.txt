This paper proposes a method for parallel batch Bayesian Optimization that selects a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations for simultaneous evaluation. The core of the proposed method is a new acquisition function, the q-Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), which quantifies the utility of evaluating a batch of `q` points. To efficiently maximize this acquisition function, the authors employ infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate its gradient. The experimental results, conducted across three benchmarks, suggest that q-KG is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noiseless problems and outperforms them on problems with observation noise.

The goal of developing effective parallel methods for hyperparameter optimization is highly relevant for leveraging modern multi-core computing environments. However, while the proposed approach is interesting, several aspects of the paper remain unclear, and the experimental evaluation is not fully convincing.

**Technical Quality**
The authors demonstrate the proposed method's performance on common artificial benchmark functions as well as on hyperparameter optimization for logistic regression (MNIST) and a convolutional neural network (CIFAR-10). However, the experimental section is missing a crucial element: a comparison based on wall-clock time. Without this, it is difficult to assess the practical gains of this parallel method over simpler baselines like parallel random search or even standard sequential Bayesian optimization. An analysis of the computational overhead incurred by optimizing the q-KG acquisition function is needed. Furthermore, the rationale for setting the batch size `q` to 4 in all experiments is not provided, making it unclear how this choice was made or how sensitive the method is to this parameter.

**Novelty/Originality**
The primary contributions appear to be the application of the knowledge gradient to the batch setting (q-KG) and the use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for its optimization. Neither of these components is novel in itself. As the authors note, the knowledge gradient is a generalization of expected improvement. More importantly, the use of IPA to select a batch of points for parallel evaluation has been previously explored in the context of expected improvement (as in "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions" by Wang et al.). The paper should more clearly articulate its novel contributions in light of this highly relevant prior work.

**Impact/Usefulness**
Methods for parallel hyperparameter tuning are of high practical importance and have the potential for significant impact across numerous disciplines that rely on machine learning, including computer vision, robotics, and computational biology.

**Clarity/Presentation**
The paper is generally well-written. The background material and technical details of the proposed method are described clearly, and the experiments are presented with sufficient detail for comprehension.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  The work by Wang et al. (2015), "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions," appears highly relevant, and code seems to be available. Was a comparison performed against this method? If this is the method referred to as "parallel EI in MOE," it may be beneficial to adopt the "q-EI" naming convention to better distinguish it from other parallel EI methods (e.g., Spearmint's). To avoid confusion, the paper should clearly state the differences between the proposed method and this prior work.

2.  Modern multi-core systems often allow for a much higher degree of parallelism than `q=4`. For example, Snoek et al. (NIPS 2012) used up to 10 parallel runs of Spearmint, Bergstra et al. (LION 2012) used 25 parallel runs of SMAC, and Snoek et al. (ICML 2015) used up to 800 parallel runs. Do the authors have any insights into how the proposed method scales with `q`? What are the expected gains and computational challenges when moving to a much larger number of parallel evaluations?

3.  The proposed method appears to operate synchronously, waiting for all evaluations in a batch to complete before selecting the next one. Would it be possible to extend the framework to an asynchronous setting, as has been done for other Bayesian optimization methods like Spearmint?

4.  Ultimately, wall-clock time is the most critical metric for performance. How does the proposed method compare against parallel random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead required to compute a new batch of `q` points?

5.  Regarding the plots, what does the x-axis label "iterations" represent? Does it refer to the number of batches evaluated or the total number of individual function evaluations? Additionally, does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do the performance curves for different methods not all originate from the same value?

**Minor Comments**

*   In Equation (4.1), is a z^(1:n) term missing in the conditioning of the posterior distribution?
*   Grammar: "...this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" should be "...achieves a test error."
*   Citation style: Parenthetical references should not be used as nouns. For example, "[16] suggests parallelizing..." should be rephrased as "Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing..."
*   The reference to Wang et al. (2015) for Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) may be inappropriate, as they apply the method rather than introduce it. A more foundational citation for IPA should be considered.
