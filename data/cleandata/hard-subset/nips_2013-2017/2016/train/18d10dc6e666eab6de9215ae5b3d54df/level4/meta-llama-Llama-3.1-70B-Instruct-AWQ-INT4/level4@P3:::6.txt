This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method, to efficiently optimize black-box functions in parallel environments. The authors derive the q-KG method using a decision-theoretic analysis, which chooses the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior when there is only one batch of points remaining. The method is an extension of the knowledge gradient policy to the parallel setting, and it measures the information gain of evaluating q points via a new acquisition function.

The authors provide an efficient strategy for computing the q-KG factor and its gradient using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). They also propose a method to discretize the domain to approximate q-KG when the domain is infinite. The experimental results show that the q-KG method outperforms or is competitive with state-of-the-art benchmark algorithms on several synthetic functions and in tuning practical machine learning algorithms, especially in the presence of noise.

Technical quality:
The paper is well-written, and the technical details are clearly explained. The authors provide a thorough background on Gaussian processes and the knowledge gradient policy, making it easy to follow their derivation of the q-KG method. The experimental results are also well-presented, with clear plots and tables that effectively compare the performance of the q-KG method with other benchmark algorithms.

However, there are a few aspects that could be improved. Firstly, the authors could provide more details on the computational complexity of the q-KG method, especially when compared to other benchmark algorithms. Secondly, it would be beneficial to include more experiments that demonstrate the scalability of the q-KG method, especially when the number of parallel runs is large. Finally, the authors could provide more insights on how to choose the optimal batch size q, as it seems to be set arbitrarily to 4 in all experiments.

Novelty/Originality:
The paper proposes a novel extension of the knowledge gradient policy to the parallel setting, which is a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The use of IPA to efficiently compute the q-KG factor and its gradient is also a notable contribution. However, it is worth noting that the idea of using IPA to select a batch of configurations using expected improvement has been explored before in the literature.

Impact/Usefulness:
The q-KG method has the potential to significantly impact the field of machine learning and other disciplines that rely heavily on hyperparameter tuning. By efficiently optimizing black-box functions in parallel environments, the q-KG method can reduce the computational time and resources required for hyperparameter tuning, making it more accessible to practitioners.
