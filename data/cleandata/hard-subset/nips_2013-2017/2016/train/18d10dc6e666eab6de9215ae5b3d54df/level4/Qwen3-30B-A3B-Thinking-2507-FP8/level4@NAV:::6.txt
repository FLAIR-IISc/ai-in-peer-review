

This work introduces a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization through the computation of a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations for evaluation. The approach employs the knowledge gradient (q-KG) to measure the utility of evaluating a batch, and proposes an efficient gradient estimation technique for maximizing the acquisition function via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). In three experiments, q-KG demonstrates competitive performance against existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noise-free problems and outperforms them on noisy problems. While I appreciate the focus on parallel hyperparameter optimization for efficient multi-core utilization, certain aspects remain unclear, and I am not convinced by the experimental results. Below are my specific comments and questions:  

**Technical Quality**  
The paper employs standard artificial benchmark functions to demonstrate superiority but lacks wall-clock time analysis or comparisons to random search/sequential optimization for real-world efficiency gains. Additionally, the choice of q=4 for all experiments is unjustified.  

**Novelty/Originality**  
The use of q-KG as an acquisition function and IPA for gradient estimation are not novel; the paper references "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions" (which already applies IPA for batch selection using expected improvement). The distinction between q-KG and prior methods should be explicitly clarified.  

**Impact/Usefulness**  
Parallel hyperparameter tuning is highly relevant across machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.  

**Clarity/Presentation**  
The technical details and background are well-explained, and the experiment section provides sufficient context for the comparisons.  

**Questions to the Authors:**  
1. Wang et al. (2015) is highly relevant (with available code) and addresses parallel EI via IPA. Have you compared against this method? If this is the "parallel EI in MOE" approach, it should be termed q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint’s method. Clarify the differences in the paper.  
2. Modern multi-core systems support far more than 4 parallel runs (e.g., ICML’15 used 800 parallel DNGO runs, LION’12 used 25 SMAC runs, NIPS’12 used 10 parallel Spearmint runs). What is the scalability of your method, and how does performance change with higher parallelism?  
3. The method currently runs synchronously. Could it be extended to asynchronous execution (like Spearmint)?  
4. Wall-clock time is critical. How does your method compare to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead for batch computation?  
5. What does "iterations" (x-axis label) represent? Is it the number of function evaluations or batches? Does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, why do methods not start from the same baseline?  

**Minor Comments:**  
- Is the term \( z^{(1:n)} \) missing in Eq. (4.1)?  
- "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" → "achieves" (typo).  
- Parenthetical references should not function as nouns: e.g., "[16] suggests" → "Snoek et al. [16] suggest".
