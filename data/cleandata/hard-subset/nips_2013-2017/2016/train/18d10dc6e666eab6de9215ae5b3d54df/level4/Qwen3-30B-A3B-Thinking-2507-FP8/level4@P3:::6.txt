

This paper introduces a novel parallel Bayesian optimization method called the "parallel knowledge gradient" (q-KG) that selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function for batch selection and propose an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to maximize this acquisition function. The paper builds upon previous work in Bayesian optimization, particularly extending the knowledge gradient (KG) method [17] to the parallel setting, where previous work has typically used iterative greedy approaches to construct batches (e.g., parallel EI [2, 19, 25] or batch UCB [5]).

The theoretical foundation of the paper is sound, with a clear derivation of the q-KG acquisition function and its Bayes-optimality for the parallel setting. The authors present a well-structured approach to efficiently computing the acquisition function using IPA, which addresses the computational challenges of batch selection. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results demonstrate consistent improvements over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings, which is significant for practical applications where function evaluations are often noisy.

The paper is well-written with clear mathematical notation and good organization. The background on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization is appropriate for the target audience, and the experimental section contains sufficient detail to understand the comparisons made. The authors correctly acknowledge related work, though they should clarify their relationship with Wang et al. (2015), which also introduced parallel Bayesian optimization using IPA for expected improvement.

Several improvements would strengthen the paper:

1. The paper should clarify the distinction between their q-KG method and the parallel EI implementation in the Metrics Optimization Engine (MOE), which they reference as "parallel EI." The authors should use "q-EI" to distinguish it from Spearmint's parallel implementation, as suggested by Wang et al. (2015).

2. The experimental section lacks wall-clock time comparisons, which are crucial for understanding the practical utility of the method. It would be valuable to see how q-KG compares to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of actual time-to-solution, including the overhead for computing new batches.

3. The choice of q=4 across all experiments is not justified. The authors should explore how the method scales with larger batch sizes (e.g., q=10, 20, 40) and discuss the practical limits of parallelism for their approach.

4. The paper does not address asynchronous operation, which is a key practical consideration for real-world implementations (e.g., Spearmint's asynchronous approach [12]). This would make the method more applicable to distributed computing environments.

5. The x-axis labels in Figures 3 and 4 are ambiguous regarding whether they represent "number of batches" or "number of function evaluations." Clarifying this would improve the interpretability of the results.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to parallel Bayesian optimization, particularly for noisy function evaluations. The method has clear practical relevance for hyperparameter tuning in machine learning, where parallel computation is increasingly common. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the experimental results support the claims made. The minor issues identified above are easily addressable and would further strengthen the paper's contribution.

Overall, this is a strong paper with clear technical contributions to the field. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrates practical improvements over existing approaches. The authors have made a significant contribution to parallel Bayesian optimization that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners in machine learning.

Recommendation: Accept
