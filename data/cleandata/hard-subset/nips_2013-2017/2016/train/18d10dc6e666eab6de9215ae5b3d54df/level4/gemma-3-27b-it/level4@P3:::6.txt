This work proposes a novel method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization, computing a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate next using a knowledge gradient-based acquisition function (q-KG). To enable efficient maximization of this function, the authors leverage infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate its gradient. Experimental results on synthetic functions and machine learning hyperparameter tuning demonstrate that q-KG performs competitively with existing methods in noise-free settings and outperforms them in noisy scenarios. The idea of efficiently utilizing multi-core environments for hyperparameter optimization is valuable, though some aspects of the approach and the presentation of results require clarification.

**Technical Quality:**

The paper employs standard benchmark functions to assess the superiority of the proposed method, and extends this evaluation to practical problems involving CNN and logistic regression optimization for CIFAR10/MNIST. However, the current experiments lack a crucial component: an analysis of wall-clock time or a direct comparison with simpler baselines like random search or sequential optimization. Understanding the practical speedup offered by this parallel method is essential. Furthermore, the choice of a fixed batch size (q=4) across all experiments is not adequately justified. A sensitivity analysis exploring different batch sizes would strengthen the conclusions. The technical details and derivations are generally sound, but could benefit from more concise notation and clearer explanations of certain steps.

**Novelty/Originality:**

The work builds upon existing concepts – the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) – but integrates them in a novel way for parallel Bayesian optimization. The authors correctly acknowledge related work, including "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions" (Wang et al., 2015), which also employs IPA for batch selection with expected improvement. However, the paper doesn’t fully articulate the specific advantages of using the knowledge gradient over expected improvement in the parallel setting, leaving a gap in understanding the contribution's uniqueness. 

**Impact/Usefulness:**

Parallel hyperparameter tuning methods are highly relevant for diverse applications, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics. The potential to accelerate the optimization process, particularly for expensive-to-evaluate functions, is significant. However, the paper needs to provide stronger evidence of real-world applicability and scalability to fully demonstrate its impact.

**Clarity/Presentation:**

The technical background and experimental setup are described clearly and comprehensively. The paper provides sufficient information to understand the performed comparisons. However, some areas could be improved: The x-axis labels in the plots ("iterations") require clarification – do they represent function evaluations or batches? The initial design’s inclusion on the x-axis is unclear, as it leads to inconsistent starting points across methods. Minor grammatical errors (e.g., "achieve a test error" should be "achieves") and stylistic issues (e.g., parenthetical references) are present but do not significantly detract from the overall clarity. Equation (4.1) appears to be missing a z^(1:n) term.

**Questions to the Authors:**

1. Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant, and its code is publicly available. Have you compared your method against it directly? Clarifying the differences between q-KG and the parallel EI implementation in Wang et al. (2015) (or explicitly identifying which "parallel EI in MOE" refers to) is crucial to avoid confusion.
2. Modern multi-core systems offer far more than 4 parallel evaluations. Prior work (e.g., Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks, ICML'15; Parallel Algorithm Configuration, LION'12; Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms, NIPS'12) demonstrates scaling to hundreds of parallel runs. What insights can you offer regarding the scalability of your method and the potential gains from increasing the number of parallel runs beyond q=4?
3. Currently, your method seems to operate synchronously. Is asynchronous execution, similar to Spearmint, a feasible extension?
4. Ultimately, wall-clock time is critical. Have you evaluated your method's performance compared to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of wall-clock time? What is the computational overhead of calculating a new batch?
5. Clarify what "iterations" represents on the x-axis of your plots. Is it the number of function evaluations or the number of batches? If it includes the initial design, explain why the different methods do not start from the same value.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of the knowledge gradient to parallel Bayesian optimization.
*   Efficient gradient-based optimization of the acquisition function using IPA.
*   Demonstrated performance gains over existing methods in noisy settings.
*   Well-written and clearly presented paper.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of wall-clock time analysis and comparison with simpler baselines (random search, sequential BO).
*   Unjustified choice of a fixed batch size (q=4).
*   Insufficiently articulated advantage of q-KG over parallel EI.
*   Limited discussion of scalability and asynchronous execution.
*   Minor technical and stylistic issues.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the technical contributions are solid and the paper is well-written, the lack of practical performance analysis (wall-clock time) and a clearer justification for key design choices (batch size) weaken its impact. Addressing the questions raised above and providing a more comprehensive experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the submission.
