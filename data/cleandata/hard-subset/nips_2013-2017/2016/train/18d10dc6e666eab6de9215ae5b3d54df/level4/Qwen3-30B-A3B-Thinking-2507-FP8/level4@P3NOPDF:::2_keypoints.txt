- Proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, addressing BO’s sequential nature.  
- Strong theoretical contribution with a clear derivation of the optimal batch selection criterion.  
- Empirical claims that the approach outperforms existing sequential and parallel BO methods.  
- Exposition is well-structured, accessible, and the Bayes-optimal batch formulation is elegant and promising.  
- Focus on parallelization is timely and practically significant for hyperparameter optimization (HPO).  
- Major experimental flaw: using test set error as the optimization criterion is inappropriate and prone to overfitting; metrics like AUC, log loss, or balanced accuracy are preferable.  
- Benchmark scope is too narrow: only two datasets (MNIST, CIFAR10) and two algorithms (logistic regression, CNN), all classification tasks.  
- Omission of HPOlib (or other standardized HPO benchmark suites) weakens evaluation reproducibility and fairness.  
- Experimental scale is insufficient to generalize claims—needs diverse task types (regression, clustering), more algorithms (SVMs, random forests, GNNs), and larger benchmark suites.  
- The GP assumption of independent, normally distributed errors is questionable in HPO due to structured bias between CV estimates and true performance; this should be acknowledged.  
- Recommend addressing structured bias via heteroscedastic models or bias-aware acquisition functions.  
- Overall: novel and solid theoretical work, but empirical validation is inadequate; stronger, standardized, and broader experiments are needed to support claims of superiority.
