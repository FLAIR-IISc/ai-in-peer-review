This work presents a method for parallel Batch Bayesian Optimization that selects a Bayes-optimal batch of configurations to evaluate at each step. The proposed approach uses the knowledge gradient (q-KG) to measure the utility of a batch. To maximize this acquisition function efficiently, the authors employ a method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate its gradient. The experiments demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods on noiseless problems and outperforms them on problems with observation noise.

While the idea of parallelizing hyperparameter optimization for modern multi-core systems is appealing, several aspects of the work remain unclear, and the experimental results are not fully convincing. Please find my detailed feedback below.

**Technical Quality**
The authors demonstrate their method on common artificial benchmark functions and on hyperparameter optimization for a CNN and logistic regression on CIFAR-10 and MNIST. However, the experimental section would be significantly strengthened by an analysis of wall-clock time. Such a comparison against simpler baselines like random search or sequential optimization is crucial for quantifying the practical gains of this parallel method. Furthermore, the paper does not provide a justification for setting the batch size q=4 across all experiments.

**Novelty/Originality**
The core components of the proposed method, the knowledge gradient (a generalization of expected improvement) and infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), are not new in themselves. The paper should more clearly distinguish its contribution from prior work, particularly "Parallel Bayesian global optimization of expensive functions," which also used IPA to select a batch of configurations via expected improvement.

**Impact/Usefulness**
Methods for parallel hyperparameter tuning are highly relevant for real-world applications in numerous disciplines, including machine learning, computer vision, biology, and robotics.

**Clarity/Presentation**
The technical details and background are well-described, and the experiment section contains the necessary information to understand the comparisons.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The work of Wang et al. (2015) appears highly relevant. Have you compared your method to theirs? If the "parallel EI in MOE" baseline is indeed this method, we suggest renaming it to q-EI to distinguish it from Spearmint's parallel approach and clarifying the differences between your work and theirs in the paper.

2.  Modern multi-core systems support much greater parallelism than the q=4 used in the experiments. For instance, prior work has demonstrated scaling up to 10 ("Practical Bayesian Optimization of Machine Learning Algorithms", NIPS'12), 25 ("Parallel Algorithm Configuration", LION'12), or even 800 ("Scalable Bayesian Optimization Using Deep Neural Networks", ICML'15) parallel evaluations. Could you comment on the scalability of your method and the expected performance gains when increasing the number of parallel runs?

3.  The proposed method operates synchronously. Is it possible to extend it to an asynchronous setting, similar to Spearmint, to handle workers that finish at different times?

4.  Ultimately, wall-clock time is a critical metric for practical performance. How does your method compare to random search or sequential Bayesian optimization in terms of total time? Could you quantify the computational overhead required to select each new batch?

5.  The x-axis label "iterations" in the plots is ambiguous. Does it represent the number of individual function evaluations or the number of batches? Also, does the x-axis include the initial design? If so, it is unclear why the different methods do not start from the same performance value.

**Minor Comments:**
*   In Eq. (4.1), is z^(1:n) missing?
*   Grammar: "this architecture has been carefully tuned by the human expert, and achieve a test error" should be "...and achieves a test error".
*   Grammar: Parenthetical citations should not be used as nouns. For example, "[16] suggests parallelizing" should be rephrased as "Snoek et al. [16] suggest parallelizing".
*   The citation for infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) seems inappropriate. Wang et al. (2015) apply the method but did not introduce it.
