- Introduces q-KG, a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm extending the sequential knowledge gradient to select a batch of q points jointly in a decision-theoretic way.  
- Defines q-KG as the expected improvement in the minimum of the GP predictor after evaluating a batch, and uses infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for efficient optimization.  
- Empirically outperforms existing batch BO methods, especially in noisy settings, on synthetic functions and real hyperparameter tuning tasks (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR10 CNN).  
- Builds on prior BO work and differs from prior parallel methods by jointly optimizing the entire batch rather than using iterative greedy heuristics.  
- Technically sound with rigorous derivation and a detailed computational approach enabling practical implementation.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive and comparisons to multiple state-of-the-art baselines are thorough.  
- Paper is generally well-written with clear notation and logical flow, accessible to readers familiar with GPs and BO.  
- Clarity issues: distinction between feasible domain A and discretized optimization set (Sec. 5.3) could be clearer; relation between q-KG and expected improvement (Sec. 4.2) needs more explicit explanation.  
- Original contribution: first decision-theoretic joint-batch KG method with theoretical justification and efficient computation.  
- Significance: high practical impact for accelerating hyperparameter tuning with parallel resources; code availability improves reproducibility and adoption.  
- Strengths: strong theoretical foundation, IPA-based computational approach, comprehensive experiments, clear superiority in noisy settings, public code.  
- Weaknesses: insufficient discussion of computational complexity trade-offs relative to other methods; initial Latin hypercube sampling choice not well motivated.  
- Additional weaknesses: relationship to expected improvement requires clarification; exploration–exploitation trade-offs in batch setting not fully addressed; missing explicit comparison to some recent methods (e.g., parallel predictive entropy search).  
- Overall judgement: excellent, significant contribution with minor clarifications needed; strong recommendation for acceptance.
