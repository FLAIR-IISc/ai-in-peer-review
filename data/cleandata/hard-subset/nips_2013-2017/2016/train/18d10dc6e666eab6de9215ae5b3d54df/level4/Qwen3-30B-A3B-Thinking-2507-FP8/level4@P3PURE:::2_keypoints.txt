- Proposes a parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm that computes Bayes-optimal batches, addressing a limitation of sequential BO.  
- Strong theoretical contribution and an elegant approach compared to alternatives.  
- Empirically outperforms state-of-the-art sequential Bayesian optimization methods in presented experiments.  
- Paper is well-written and accessible, making a compelling case for parallelization in practical hyperparameter optimization.  
- The assumption of independent normally distributed errors requires critical discussion and may be questionable in HPO contexts.  
- Measurement errors in HPO reflect generalization vs empirical estimates (e.g., cross-validation), so independence and normality may not hold.  
- Structural bias in hyperparameter space, especially with small samples, undermines the independence error assumption.  
- Experimental evaluation supports theory but is insufficient to convincingly validate practical advantages.  
- Optimization criterion is suboptimal: using test set error is problematic for HPO; metrics like AUC or log loss are more appropriate.  
- Benchmark datasets are limited (MNIST, CIFAR10); the study should leverage HPOlib, the standard HPO benchmark library.  
- Benchmark scope is narrow: only two datasets, one task (classification), and two algorithms (logistic regression, CNN), failing to capture problem diversity.  
- Conclusion: theoretical value is clear, but broader experimental benchmarks (diverse metrics, datasets, and algorithms) are needed to establish general superiority.
