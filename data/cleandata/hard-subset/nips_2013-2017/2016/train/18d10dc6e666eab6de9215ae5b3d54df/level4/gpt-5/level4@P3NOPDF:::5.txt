Summary
The paper proposes a batch version of the Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function and introduces a Monte Carlo–based gradient estimator to optimize the selection of a batch of design points. Empirical results suggest that the proposed approach outperforms competing batch Bayesian optimization (BO) methods on several benchmarks.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for batch decision-making in BO and for optimizing the batch acquisition via gradient methods.
- The Monte Carlo gradient estimator appears practical and scalable, enabling direct optimization of the batch-KG objective rather than relying on heuristics.
- Empirical results are promising, showing competitive or superior performance to existing batch BO baselines.

Main concerns and requests for clarification
1) Novelty and positioning relative to prior batch KG work
- Batch KG has been studied previously (e.g., [1], [2]). The paper should explicitly delineate what is new. My reading is that the contribution lies in the construction and application of a differentiable Monte Carlo estimator for the gradient of the batch-KG objective, enabling efficient gradient-based optimization over the joint batch.
- Please add a dedicated related-work subsection that:
  - Clearly compares your formulation to [1] and [2] in terms of modeling assumptions (noise treatment, correlated priors), objective definitions, and optimization strategies.
  - Highlights what aspects of your estimator (e.g., unbiasedness, variance control, reparameterization vs. likelihood-ratio vs. IPA) are novel.
  - Discusses computational complexity per iteration and wall-clock implications.

2) Empirical comparison to prior batch KG methods
- If the contribution is primarily the gradient-based optimization of batch KG, please compare directly to prior batch KG implementations (from [1], [2] or faithful re-implementations) on:
  - Final objective quality vs. total evaluations.
  - Wall-clock time to reach a given performance.
  - Sensitivity to batch size and noise level.
- If such implementations are not publicly available, consider a controlled comparison using the same KG objective optimized with common alternatives (e.g., random restarts with local search, heuristic construction, greedy selection) to isolate the benefit of your gradient estimator.

3) Section 5.2 needs elaboration
- Section 5.2 appears central to the paper; please expand and make it self-contained. Specifically:
  - Provide a precise statement of the gradient estimator, its assumptions, and conditions under which the interchange of differentiation and expectation is valid.
  - Clarify whether the estimator is unbiased or asymptotically unbiased, and characterize its variance.
  - Describe variance-reduction techniques (e.g., common random numbers, antithetics, control variates) if used, and their impact.
  - Detail the optimization procedure: step-size schedule, stopping criteria, handling of constraints (e.g., domain bounds), and initialization for the batch.
  - Discuss theoretical properties: convergence guarantees of the stochastic optimization, smoothness of the objective, and any regularity conditions.
  - Provide computational complexity per iteration as a function of batch size, number of Monte Carlo samples, and dimension.

4) Fairness of comparisons in noisy settings
- Expected Improvement (EI) in its classical form assumes noiseless observations. In noisy environments, plain EI can be disadvantaged. Please clarify:
  - Did you use a noise-aware variant (e.g., fantasization/conditional simulations, plug-in of posterior means for the incumbent, or other standard modifications)? If yes, specify exactly how.
  - If plain EI was used in noisy experiments, please add a discussion stating that EI does not model observation noise and interpret the results accordingly. Ideally, include a noise-aware EI baseline or ablate the impact of noise on both methods.

5) Terminology and notation
- On line 162, “IPA” is redefined. Please define the abbreviation once (e.g., Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis) and use it consistently. Ensure all acronyms are introduced at first use and remain unambiguous throughout the text.
- Unify notation across sections so that gradients, expectations, and sampling distributions are clearly and consistently denoted.

Additional suggestions
- Ablations: Show performance as a function of the number of Monte Carlo samples for the gradient, batch size, and dimension. Include sensitivity to kernel hyperparameters and noise variance.
- Robustness: Report variability over seeds and provide confidence intervals for performance metrics.
- Practical guidance: Provide heuristics for setting the number of samples, step sizes, and initialization. Discuss failure modes (e.g., high-dimensional settings or highly multimodal objectives).
- Comparators: In addition to EI-based batch methods, consider including UCB-based batch methods and recent q-EI implementations where applicable. If these are excluded, explain why.
- Clarity: A schematic or pseudo-code for the batch-KG optimization loop would help readers reproduce the method.

Minor comments
- Check all references for completeness and accessibility. For [1], include the full citation as you have; for [2], provide a stable URL, title, and author (the thesis link may change).
- Proofreading: there are a few grammatical inconsistencies and places where sentences can be tightened; a careful pass will improve readability.
- If you rely on common-random-number coupling between candidate batches when computing gradients, state this explicitly.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important problem in BO and proposes a practically useful Monte Carlo gradient estimator for optimizing batch KG. The empirical results are encouraging. However, the paper’s contribution relative to prior batch KG work needs to be made more explicit, and Section 5.2 should be expanded to present the estimator and its properties with enough detail for reproduction and critical assessment. Addressing the fairness of comparisons in noisy settings is also necessary. With these clarifications and additions, the paper would make a solid and impactful contribution.
