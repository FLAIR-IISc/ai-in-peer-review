

This paper introduces a batch-sequential Bayesian optimization approach centered on a parallel variant of the Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) criterion. After establishing foundational context on related work and Gaussian processes, the authors formally define the q-KG criterion and detail its computation, following the structure of the standard KG framework. Extensive numerical experiments demonstrate that q-KG outperforms state-of-the-art batch-sequential Bayesian optimization methods. This work makes a strong contribution to the field, with significant potential societal impact through enabling efficient parallelization of Bayesian optimization. 

Minor concerns warrant attention: (1) The paper does not analyze computational speedups from sequential to batch-sequential execution; (2) The literature review contains slight imprecisions: the integration of Expected Improvement (EI) with respect to posterior distributions was already presented in "Kriging is well-suited to parallelize optimization" (alongside Constant Liar), and Chevalier et al. introduced CL-mix in [2]; additionally, q-EI maximization via natural gradient is studied in "Differentiating the multipoint Expected Improvement for optimal batch design." 

The following specific remarks and questions require clarification:  
1. Is the domain $A$ compact and $f$ continuous?  
2. Why restrict $A$ to a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) at initialization?  
3. Does Algorithm 1 include hyperparameter re-estimation (or Bayesian updating)?  
4. In Section 5.2, is the definition of $g$ a maximization or minimization?  
5. The smoothness of $g$ requires justification; it does not follow immediately.  
6. The derivative of the Cholesky factor is non-trivial and merits discussion.  
7. The Matérn kernel is specified as 5/2, not 2/5.  
8. For Figure 1 experiments: Could the same Gaussian processes have been fitted using alternative software?  

Overall, this is a high-quality paper deserving publication at NIPS.
