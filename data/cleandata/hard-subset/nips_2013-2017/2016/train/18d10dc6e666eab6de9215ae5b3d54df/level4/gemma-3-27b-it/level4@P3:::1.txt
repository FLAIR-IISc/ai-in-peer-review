This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method, the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) approach, extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient criterion to parallel evaluations. The authors provide a theoretically grounded derivation of q-KG based on decision theory, addressing the challenge of efficiently computing the acquisition function using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). Comprehensive experiments on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms existing batch Bayesian optimization algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings. This is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field by enabling faster and more efficient optimization of expensive black-box functions.

The paper begins with a clear introduction to Bayesian optimization and the motivation for parallelization. The core contribution – the q-KG acquisition function – is well-defined, and the computational strategy leveraging IPA is a significant technical achievement. The experimental validation is thorough, covering both synthetic benchmarks and real-world applications like tuning logistic regression and CNNs. The inclusion of noisy evaluation scenarios is particularly valuable, as this is a common challenge in practice. The code availability further strengthens the paper’s reproducibility and potential impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The q-KG method is derived from a solid decision-theoretic foundation, providing a strong justification for its approach.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The use of IPA enables efficient gradient-based optimization of the q-KG acquisition function, addressing a key practical challenge.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The extensive experimental results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of q-KG over existing methods, especially in noisy settings.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application of q-KG to hyperparameter tuning for machine learning algorithms highlights its practical utility.
*   **Reproducibility:** The availability of code facilitates reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Speedup Analysis:** The paper does not explicitly analyze the speedups achieved by parallelizing the optimization process, which would further strengthen the contribution. While performance is demonstrated to be *better* with parallelization, quantifying the speedup would be valuable.
*   **Imprecision in Literature Review:** The literature review contains minor inaccuracies. For example, the iterative construction of EI batches and the "Constant Liar" method were both introduced in Ginsbourger et al. (2010), not just mentioned. Marmin et al. (2017) provides further work on optimizing parallel EI using natural gradients.
*   **Assumptions and Clarifications:** The paper could benefit from explicitly stating assumptions regarding the domain *A* (e.g., compactness, continuity of *f*) and justifying the initial use of a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS).
*   **Technical Details:** Several technical aspects could be clarified, such as the precise definition of *g* in Section 5.2 (is it a maximization or minimization?), a discussion on the smoothness of *g*, and a justification for the differentiability of the Cholesky factor.
*   **Minor Typo:** The kernel used is Matérn 5/2, not 2/5.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to batch Bayesian optimization.
*   The proposed method demonstrably outperforms existing algorithms in a variety of settings.
*   The computational efficiency of q-KG makes it practical for real-world applications.
*   The code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.
*   The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of speedup analysis is a minor drawback.
*   The minor inaccuracies in the literature review need to be addressed.
*   The lack of clarity in certain technical details requires further elaboration.

**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016. The contributions are significant, the presentation is clear, and the experimental results are compelling. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. The potential impact of this work on the field of Bayesian optimization, particularly in the context of parallel computing, is substantial.



**Specific Questions/Requests for the Authors:**

*   Could you please provide an analysis of the speedups achieved by using the parallel q-KG method compared to sequential Bayesian optimization?
*   Please revise the literature review to accurately reflect the contributions of Ginsbourger et al. (2010) and Marmin et al. (2017).
*   Please explicitly state the assumptions made regarding the domain *A* and the function *f*.
*   Could you provide a justification for using a Latin Hypercube Sample (LHS) for the initial samples?
*   Please clarify whether *g* in Section 5.2 is a maximization or minimization problem.
*   Please provide a more detailed justification for the smoothness of *g* and the differentiability of the Cholesky factor.
*   Correct the typo regarding the Matérn kernel (5/2, not 2/5).
*   Was it possible to fit the same GPs using different software packages to verify consistency?
