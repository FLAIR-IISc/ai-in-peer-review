

This paper proposes q-KG, a parallel knowledge gradient acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, addressing the practical challenge of time-consuming function evaluations. The authors introduce an efficient method to compute q-KG, enabling the simultaneous selection of *q* points for evaluation in each iteration. This approach significantly accelerates optimization by leveraging parallelism, a critical advantage for data-driven applications requiring distributed computation. The empirical results demonstrate strong performance against baselines, validating the theoretical claims and underscoring the method’s practical utility. The problem addressed is highly relevant, and the contribution represents a meaningful advancement in batch Bayesian optimization.  

The core strength lies in the elegant adaptation of knowledge gradient principles to the batch setting, with the proposed algorithm delivering tangible efficiency gains over sequential methods. The computational approach for evaluating q-KG is well-motivated and implemented effectively, avoiding prohibitive complexity. The paper is clearly written, with logical flow and sufficient technical detail for reproducibility.  

However, two critical concerns warrant attention. First, the reliance on Gaussian processes (GPs) as the underlying surrogate model appears questionable for high-dimensional settings, such as neural network hyperparameter tuning, where GPs often struggle with scalability and representational limitations. The authors should explicitly discuss the suitability of GPs in such contexts or provide empirical evidence of their effectiveness in high-dimensional regimes. Second, while the empirical validation is sound, the theoretical foundations require deeper scrutiny. The authors should establish:  
1) **Error bounds** for the q-KG acquisition function relative to the optimal solution, and  
2) **Quantitative analysis** of computational complexity with respect to input dimension *d*, particularly how the proposed method scales as *d* increases.  
Without these, the theoretical justification for the method’s robustness in real-world scenarios remains incomplete.  

Minor issues also require clarification:  
- **Line 36**: "the set of points to evaluate next that is" → grammatical error; should read "the set of points to evaluate next is."  
- **Line 95**: The definition of *A* (likely the action set) is ambiguous; clarify its role in the acquisition function.  
- **Line 104**: The input to *μ<sup>n</sup>* (posterior mean) is unspecified; define its domain (e.g., input space *X*).  
- **Line 128**: A brief comparison with parallel Expected Improvement (EI) would strengthen the motivation for q-KG over existing batch methods.  
- **Line 140**: Notation for *A* should be consistently distinguished from other uses (e.g., use *A<sub>q</sub>* for the set of *q* points).  

Overall, this is a strong contribution to Bayesian optimization with clear practical value. The method is technically sound, and the experiments are well-executed. Addressing the theoretical gaps and notational ambiguities would significantly elevate the paper’s impact. The concerns raised are not deal-breakers but are essential for a rigorous submission to NIPS. With these revisions, the paper would make a compelling addition to the field.  

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions). The work is novel, well-executed, and addresses an important problem in optimization. The suggested improvements are straightforward to implement and would substantially enhance the paper’s theoretical depth and clarity.
