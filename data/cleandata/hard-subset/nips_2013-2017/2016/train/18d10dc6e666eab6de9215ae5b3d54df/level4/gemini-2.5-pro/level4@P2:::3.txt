### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method for batch Bayesian optimization called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The goal is to efficiently find the global optimum of an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function by selecting a batch of `q` points to evaluate in parallel at each iteration. The proposed q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic framework, aiming to select the batch of points that provides the greatest expected improvement in the final solution quality after the batch is evaluated. This makes the policy one-step Bayes-optimal.

Recognizing that the direct optimization of the q-KG acquisition function is computationally challenging, the authors present a practical and efficient optimization strategy. This involves using Monte Carlo sampling to estimate the acquisition function and, crucially, employing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic estimator for its gradient. This allows the use of standard gradient-based methods to find the optimal batch of points. The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation on synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems (Logistic Regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10), demonstrating that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization methods, with particularly strong performance in noisy environments.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled Derivation:** The primary strength of the q-KG method is its principled, decision-theoretic foundation. Unlike some heuristic-based batch methods, q-KG is derived as the one-step Bayes-optimal policy for improving the final recommended solution. This provides a strong theoretical motivation for the approach. It correctly generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient, which is known to perform well, to the parallel setting.

2.  **Efficient Optimization Strategy:** A significant technical contribution is the development of an efficient method to optimize the q-KG acquisition function over continuous domains. The use of IPA to obtain an unbiased gradient estimator is key to making this principled approach computationally feasible. This allows the joint optimization over the entire batch of `q` points, in contrast to greedy or sequential construction methods.

3.  **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare q-KG against several relevant and strong baselines (multiple parallel EI variants, parallel UCB methods) across a variety of synthetic and practical machine learning tasks. The consistent and sometimes significant outperformance of q-KG, especially in the noisy setting, effectively demonstrates the practical value and robustness of the proposed method. The improvement on the carefully tuned CNN architecture for CIFAR-10 is a particularly notable result.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the method's derivation and implementation. The problem is well-motivated, and the contributions are clearly stated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The proposed q-KG method is a direct and well-motivated generalization of the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to the batch setting. While this is a valuable extension, the conceptual leap from sequential KG to batch KG could be seen as incremental. However, the main technical novelty and contribution lies in making this generalization computationally tractable for continuous domains through the use of IPA for gradient estimation, which distinguishes it from prior work on batch KG in discrete settings. The paper is clear about this context, but the framing of originality rests heavily on the computational method rather than the acquisition function's core concept.

2.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of optimizing the q-KG acquisition function. While described as "efficient," the optimization still involves Monte Carlo sampling and gradient ascent in a `q*d` dimensional space. How does the cost of this optimization step scale with the batch size `q`, the problem dimension `d`, and the number of Monte Carlo samples required for a stable gradient estimate? Providing some analysis or empirical timing results would strengthen the paper's claims of efficiency and help practitioners understand the trade-offs.

3.  **Dependence on Discretization:** The method relies on discretizing the optimization domain `A` to compute the expectation in the q-KG factor. While the proposed dynamic discretization scheme (sampling from the posterior optima) is reasonable and follows related work, the paper would be strengthened by an analysis of the method's sensitivity to the size `M` of this discrete set. A poor or small discretization could potentially limit the performance of the algorithm.

4.  **Minor Suggestion for Clarity:** A minor point on notation: in Equation (3.1), it could be made slightly more explicit that µ(x(1:n)) and K(x(1:n), x2) are shorthand for the vector of means and the matrix of covariances evaluated at the specified points, respectively. While this is standard notation in the GP literature, a brief clarification could improve readability for a broader audience.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. It presents a theoretically sound batch acquisition function and, more importantly, a practical computational method to optimize it. The strong empirical results, particularly in noisy settings, highlight its significance and utility for real-world problems like hyperparameter tuning. While the conceptual novelty might be viewed as an extension of existing ideas, the technical contribution required to make it work effectively is significant. The paper meets the criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance for NIPS.
