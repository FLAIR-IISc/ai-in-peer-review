This paper proposes a batch Bayesian optimization algorithm based on the Knowledge Gradient (KG) criterion, which they term the parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG). The core idea is to select a batch of `q` points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality, looking one step ahead. The main technical contribution is a computationally efficient method for optimizing this q-KG acquisition function. The authors propose a Monte Carlo-based estimator for the q-KG factor and derive an unbiased gradient estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), which allows for the use of gradient-based optimization methods. The experimental evaluation on synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems shows that the proposed q-KG method performs favorably against other batch Bayesian optimization algorithms, particularly in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper is well-motivated, addressing the important practical problem of batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization.
*   The proposed q-KG acquisition function is derived from a clear decision-theoretic principle, aiming to be Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch.
*   The development of an efficient gradient-based method to optimize the q-KG acquisition function is a significant contribution, as naive optimization would be computationally prohibitive.
*   The empirical results are comprehensive, covering both synthetic benchmarks and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression and CNN tuning). The method shows strong performance, especially in the noisy setting, which is a common and challenging scenario in practice.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Clarification of Originality:** The concept of a batch Knowledge Gradient has been explored in prior work (e.g., [1, 2]). The authors cite one such work ([26] in the paper's bibliography, which corresponds to [1] below) and argue that their method is distinct because it handles continuous domains via gradient-based optimization, whereas the previous work was limited to discrete sets. This is a valid and important distinction. However, to make the contribution clearer and more impactful, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a direct comparison—either theoretical or empirical—to the optimization approach in [1]. This would help to quantify the claimed efficiency gains of the proposed gradient-based method.

2.  **Clarity of the Gradient Estimation Section:** Section 5.2, which describes the gradient estimation for q-KG, is central to the paper's contribution but is presented in a very condensed manner. Key details of the derivation are deferred to the supplementary material. Given the importance of this gradient estimator to the entire method, expanding this section with more intuition and detail in the main paper would improve the paper's clarity and self-sufficiency.

3.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison in Noisy Settings:** The paper demonstrates that q-KG has a particularly strong advantage in noisy environments. However, the standard Expected Improvement (EI) acquisition function is designed for noise-free observations. It is unclear if the parallel EI benchmarks used in the experiments are based on a noise-aware variant of EI. If a standard, noise-free version of EI was used, the comparison in Section 6.2 may not be entirely fair, as the benchmark would be operating outside of its modeling assumptions. The authors should clarify which variant of parallel EI was implemented and discuss whether its formulation is appropriate for noisy optimization. If not, the results should be contextualized with this limitation in mind.

4.  **Minor Issue:** The abbreviation "IPA" is defined in the introduction (line 51) and then defined again in the heading of Section 5.2.

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a well-founded method for batch Bayesian optimization and provides a novel computational technique to make it practical. The experimental results are promising. My main concerns relate to clarifying the novelty with respect to prior work through direct comparison and ensuring the fairness of the experimental comparisons in the noisy setting. Addressing these points would strengthen the paper's contribution.
