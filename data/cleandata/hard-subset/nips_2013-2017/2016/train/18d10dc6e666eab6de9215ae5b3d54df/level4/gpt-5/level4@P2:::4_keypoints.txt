- Introduces q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that jointly selects q points via a one-step Bayes-optimal criterion maximizing expected reduction in the GP posterior minimum.

- Derives an acquisition function and proposes an efficient gradient-based optimizer using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) with Monte Carlo estimation and a discretization strategy for continuous domains.

- Empirically compares q-KG to parallel EI (MOE and Spearmint), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR-10 CNNs), showing strong performance, especially under noisy evaluations.

- Open-source code is released for reproducibility.

- Extends sequential KG to the batch setting; novelty lies in a principled collective batch selection rule and a tractable gradient estimator for joint optimization.

- Shows that in the noiseless case with final recommendation restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI, situating it among existing methods.

- Decision-theoretic derivation and IPA-based gradient estimator are sound given smoothness assumptions; discretization and MC estimation are standard BO practices.

- Experiments are thorough and include both noise-free and noisy settings, but theoretical guarantees (regret bounds or convergence under discretization/MC error) are not provided.

- Paper is generally well written and organized, though some notational ambiguities and minor typos remain; a brief self-contained discussion of competing batch EI variants would improve clarity.

- Novel and timely extension of KG to batch selection with an insightful connection to parallel EI.

- Practically significant for settings with parallel resources and noisy evaluations, with empirical gains over strong baselines.

- Strengths: principled one-step Bayes-optimal derivation, efficient IPA gradient estimation, strong empirical robustness to noise, comparisons to common baselines, and open-source implementation.

- Weaknesses: scalability and performance in higher-dimensional hyperparameter spaces not fully addressed; no experiments or methods (embeddings/warping/sparse kernels) for high-d settings.

- Weaknesses: lacks theoretical analysis—provide bounds on IPA gradient bias/variance, stochastic gradient convergence, and discretization approximation error; discuss how q and dimension d affect complexity.

- Weaknesses: impact of discretization parameters (M) and sampling scheme on solution quality and runtime should be analyzed or ablated.

- Weaknesses: GP hyperparameter learning by MLE can be unstable under noise; discuss marginalization/prior/regularization and sensitivity to kernel/noise estimation.

- Weaknesses: include computational complexity estimates per q-KG eval/gradient (as functions of n, q, M) and wall-clock comparisons to baselines.

- Weaknesses: consider adding comparisons to parallel entropy search (PES/PPES) and experiments with larger batch sizes.

- Minor comments/typos: several line-specific issues (e.g., l.36 grammar, define A at l.95, notation for µ(n)(·) at l.104, clarify parallel EI variants at l.128, distinct notation for two uses of A at l.140, Algorithm 1 typo, capitalization consistency).

- Pros for acceptance: novel principled batch acquisition with efficient optimization, strong empirical results under noise, clear presentation, and released code.

- Cons for acceptance: limited theoretical analysis, scalability/high-dimensional performance not fully addressed, and some clarity/editorial issues.

- Overall recommendation: solid and timely contribution warranting acceptance after addressing theoretical guarantees, scalability concerns, and minor clarity issues.
