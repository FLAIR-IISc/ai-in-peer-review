

This paper introduces a novel parallel Bayesian optimization algorithm called the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch function evaluation, addressing an important practical challenge in black-box optimization where multiple evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors generalize the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) acquisition function to the batch setting, providing a theoretically grounded approach to selecting q points for parallel evaluation. This work builds upon recent NIPS contributions in Bayesian optimization, particularly the sequential KG method by Frazier et al. (2009) and parallel EI approaches (Snoek et al., 2012; Contal et al., 2014), while offering a decision-theoretic alternative that jointly optimizes the entire batch rather than iteratively constructing it.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it develops a theoretically sound acquisition function q-KG that is Bayes-optimal for minimizing the expected simple regret when only one batch of evaluations remains. Second, the authors provide an efficient computational method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize q-KG, addressing the high computational cost of directly maximizing the acquisition function. Third, extensive experiments on synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms state-of-the-art parallel Bayesian optimization methods, particularly in noisy evaluation settings.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with thorough background on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization. The experimental design is strong, comparing against multiple relevant baselines (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning problems (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results are compelling, showing significant improvements in noisy settings where other methods struggle.

Strengths:
1. Quality: The technical approach is sound with proper theoretical grounding. The IPA-based optimization method effectively addresses computational challenges. The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, with appropriate baselines and statistical significance testing.
2. Clarity: The paper is well-structured with clear definitions and intuitive explanations. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is particularly helpful for implementation. The experimental results are presented clearly with informative figures.
3. Originality: The paper significantly extends previous work by providing a decision-theoretic parallel acquisition function that jointly optimizes the batch rather than sequentially constructing it. This differs fundamentally from prior approaches like iterative parallel EI or batch UCB.
4. Significance: The method addresses a critical practical limitation of Bayesian optimization (sequential evaluation) that hinders its application to modern distributed computing environments. The performance gains, especially in noisy settings, make this highly valuable for hyperparameter tuning of machine learning algorithms.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could strengthen its theoretical foundation by providing error bounds or convergence guarantees for the q-KG method, particularly regarding the discretization approach in Section 5.3.
2. While the authors acknowledge the Gaussian process assumption, a more thorough discussion of its limitations for high-dimensional problems (e.g., neural network hyperparameter spaces) would be valuable. The current experiments focus on moderate-dimensional problems (d=2-6), which may not reflect the most challenging applications.
3. Some minor notational inconsistencies exist (e.g., the use of A in both the feasible domain and the discretized set in Section 5.3), which could confuse readers.
4. The discussion of related work could more explicitly contrast with the parallel knowledge gradient method proposed by Wang et al. (2016), which appears to be a closely related but distinct approach.

Specific suggestions:
- Clarify the definition of A in Section 1 (l.95) - is this the feasible domain or the discretized set?
- Provide a brief explanation of the input to μ^n in Section 3 (l.104) to improve clarity.
- Consider using different notation for the feasible domain (e.g., Ω) and the discretized set (e.g., A) to avoid confusion (l.140).
- Add a sentence explaining why the GP assumption might be reasonable for hyperparameter tuning despite high dimensionality.
- Address the relationship with Wang et al.'s "nested batch knowledge gradient" more explicitly in the related work section.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature with clear theoretical foundations, practical computational advances, and compelling empirical results. The method offers a significant improvement over existing parallel Bayesian optimization approaches, particularly for noisy function evaluations, which is a common challenge in hyperparameter tuning of machine learning algorithms.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel decision-theoretic approach to batch Bayesian optimization
- Theoretical grounding with practical computational solution
- Strong empirical results demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines
- Clear, well-organized presentation
- Practical relevance to distributed machine learning applications

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited theoretical analysis of error bounds and computational complexity
- Need for deeper discussion of GP limitations in high dimensions
- Minor notational inconsistencies

This work makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with clear practical implications for distributed hyperparameter tuning. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated, making it suitable for publication at NIPS.
