- Proposal: Introduces q-KG, a batch (parallel) extension of the Knowledge Gradient acquisition that measures expected reduction in the posterior minimum after a batch of q observations.  
- Optimality: q-KG is Bayes-optimal when only one batch remains; reduces to parallel EI for noiseless observations when final recommendation is restricted to sampled points.  
- Gradient estimator: Derives an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for q-KG using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and enables gradient-based optimization.  
- Discretization: Inner minimization on continuous domains is approximated by discretizing candidate sets using samples from posterior draws (details sparse).  
- Experiments: Empirical results on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR-10 CNN) show consistent improvements over q-EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, especially under observation noise.  
- Strength — motivation: Strong decision-theoretic grounding, directly targeting one-step Bayes-optimal batch selection rather than greedy approximations.  
- Strength — computation: Practical computational contribution via IPA-based unbiased gradient and GP posterior update decomposition for Monte Carlo feasibility.  
- Strength — experiments & reproducibility: Solid empirical evaluation across regimes, with notable gains under noise and public code release.  
- Strength — positioning: Clear connections to prior work and correct reduction to parallel EI under special cases.  
- Weakness — theory: Limited theoretical guarantees beyond one-batch optimality; no convergence analysis as batches accumulate or for discretization consistency.  
- Weakness — discretization details: Insufficient description of how posterior function draws are optimized to produce candidate minima, sensitivity to M, and computational overhead.  
- Weakness — runtime/scaling: No wall-clock timing or scaling analysis with q and M; q×q Cholesky costs and Monte Carlo overhead not compared to baselines.  
- Weakness — batch size: Experiments only use q=4; lack of results for larger batch sizes (e.g., q=8,16) to assess joint vs. greedy benefits.  
- Weakness — fairness of comparisons: Unclear whether GP priors, hyperparameter re-fitting, and optimization restarts were standardized across toolkits/implementations.  
- Weakness — missing PPES: No empirical comparison to parallel entropy search (PPES), a natural batch objective competitor.  
- Weakness — noise model: Assumes homoscedastic Gaussian noise estimated by MLE; lacks discussion of robustness to heteroscedasticity or heavy-tailed noise.  
- Quality: Technical development appears sound and differentiability conditions are addressed, but experimental omissions (runtime, sensitivity) weaken practical assessment.  
- Clarity: Well written overall; discretization procedure and optimizer choices for posterior samples need clearer, more explicit description.  
- Originality: Novel extension of KG to batch with an unbiased IPA gradient estimator; nontrivial application of IPA beyond prior q-EI work.  
- Significance: Practical importance for batch BO, with meaningful empirical gains in noisy evaluation settings if computational overhead is moderate.  
- Suggested improvements: Report wall-clock times and scaling with q/M, ablation studies (M, gradient steps/restarts, IPA variance), experiments for larger q, include/justify PPES omission, and expand discretization and noise-robustness discussion.  
- Acceptance arguments: Principled Bayes-optimal batch acquisition, practical unbiased gradient estimator, consistent empirical improvements, and released code.  
- Rejection arguments: Limited theoretical analysis beyond one-batch optimality, missing runtime/scaling and large-q experiments, and no PPES comparison.
