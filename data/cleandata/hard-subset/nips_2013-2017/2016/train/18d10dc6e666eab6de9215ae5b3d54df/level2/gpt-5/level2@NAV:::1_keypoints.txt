- Paper proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that generalizes the knowledge gradient to select q points jointly, claiming one-step Bayes-optimality for minimizing the GP posterior mean, an efficient MC/IPA gradient estimator, and empirical gains over prior batch BO methods (especially with noise).

- Decision-theoretic derivation is sound and the Monte Carlo formulation with IPA yields well-motivated unbiased gradients.

- Extending KG to the batch setting with a differentiable stochastic objective and IPA gradients is novel and nontrivial versus prior greedy or closed-form multi-point EI methods.

- Parallel evaluations are common; a one-step optimal criterion robust to noise is practically valuable and shows consistent gains on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning tasks.

- Presentation is generally clear with explicit algorithm, derivations, and available code.

- “Bayes-optimal” claim is limited to the single remaining batch and depends on discretization of the domain; need clarification on continuous vs. discretized A and discretization-induced bias.

- Computational characterization is lacking: provide complexity and wall-clock comparisons as q, d, M, and MC sample count vary, plus gradient variance analysis and sensitivity to M/number of fantasies.

- Variance reduction techniques (quasi-Monte Carlo, common random numbers) could reduce estimator variance and should be discussed/experimented with.

- Baselines are incomplete; include parallel PES/PPES and parallel Thompson sampling (fantasization) for a more complete empirical comparison.

- Experimental reporting needs improvement: specify number of runs/seeds, provide confidence intervals, and note that CNN results with only five runs are weak evidence for large improvements.

- Include ablations (e.g., learned vs. fixed noise variance, discretization strategies) to better understand performance drivers.

- A brief theoretical discussion on consistency or asymptotic behavior as M and evaluations increase would strengthen the paper.

- Pros for acceptance: principled novel acquisition, strong empirical performance under noise, practical relevance, and open-source implementation.

- Cons for acceptance: limited baselines and ablations, optimality qualified by discretization and single-batch assumption, some results based on few runs, and sparse runtime analysis.
