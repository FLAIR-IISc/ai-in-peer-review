

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which aims to select multiple points for simultaneous evaluation in a decision-theoretic framework. The authors derive a Bayes-optimal acquisition function for batch selection that maximizes the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor. They address computational challenges by developing an efficient gradient estimation method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling practical implementation of q-KG.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance of q-KG across synthetic test functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR10), showing consistent improvements over existing batch BO methods like parallel EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, especially in noisy evaluation settings.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical foundation is sound, with a clear derivation of the q-KG acquisition function as a Bayes-optimal batch selection criterion for the one-batch remaining setting. The connection to the sequential knowledge gradient method is well-established.
2. The computational approach using IPA is clever and well-justified, addressing a key practical limitation of batch Bayesian optimization.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic functions and practical machine learning applications with appropriate baselines. The noisy evaluation experiments effectively demonstrate the method's advantages in realistic settings.
4. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper claims q-KG is "Bayes-optimal" for batch selection, but the optimality only holds for the specific case of one batch remaining. The paper should clarify that this is not a globally optimal batch selection method for multiple iterations, which could mislead readers about the scope of the theoretical contribution.
2. The computational complexity analysis is insufficient. While IPA makes q-KG more efficient than naive approaches, the paper doesn't provide complexity bounds for the gradient estimation or optimization process, making it difficult to assess scalability for large q or high-dimensional problems.
3. The explanation of why q-KG performs particularly well in noisy settings is lacking. The paper states this as an empirical finding without theoretical justification or deeper analysis of how noise affects the acquisition function.
4. The comparison to related work could be strengthened. The paper cites [25] (which appears to be the authors' own prior work) as proposing "gradient-based methods to jointly optimize a parallel EI criterion," but doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from this prior work. The paper should more explicitly address how their IPA-based gradient estimation differs from and improves upon existing methods.
5. The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the discretization approach (Section 5.3) for infinite domains. The choice of M=1000 is arbitrary, and the paper doesn't analyze how this choice affects performance or computational cost.

Significance:
The paper addresses an important practical problem in Bayesian optimization (parallel evaluations) with a theoretically grounded approach that shows clear empirical benefits. The method has potential impact in hyperparameter tuning for machine learning, where parallel evaluations are common and expensive function evaluations are typical. The demonstration of improved performance in noisy settings is particularly valuable for real-world applications.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with clear theoretical and empirical merits. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the clarification of the theoretical optimality claim, better computational complexity analysis, and more thorough discussion of the noisy evaluation results.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to batch Bayesian optimization with a novel acquisition function and efficient computational approach. The empirical results are compelling, and the method shows clear practical advantages over existing approaches, especially in noisy settings. The weaknesses identified are fixable and don't undermine the core contribution.
