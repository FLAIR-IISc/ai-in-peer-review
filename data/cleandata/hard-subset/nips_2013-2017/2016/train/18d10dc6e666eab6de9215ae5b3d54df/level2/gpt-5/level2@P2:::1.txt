Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select a set of q points jointly. q-KG is defined as the expected reduction in the minimum posterior mean after collecting a batch of q observations, making it Bayes-optimal in the one-batch-to-go setting when the final recommendation is the minimizer of the posterior mean. The authors derive a Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG and, crucially, an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) that enables efficient gradient-based joint optimization of the batch. The acquisition is approximated using a dynamically updated discrete set of candidate locations, constructed by sampling posterior minimizers (as in PES-like methods) and augmenting with previously sampled and candidate batch points. Experiments on standard synthetic functions and on hyperparameter tuning (logistic regression on MNIST and CNN on CIFAR-10) show q-KG outperforming batch EI (MOE and Spearmint variants), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with particularly strong gains under observation noise.

The work builds on sequential KG (Frazier et al.; Scott et al.) by extending to batch selection and on prior batch acquisitions including q-EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.) and parallel PES (Shah & Ghahramani), as well as UCB-based batch methods (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.). The paper’s decision-theoretic perspective contrasts with EI/UCB, and is closest in spirit to expected value of information approaches (KG, ES/PES).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The definition of q-KG is well-motivated, and the one-step Bayes-optimality (for minimizing min_x μ) follows directly from the decision-theoretic argument. The Monte Carlo estimator is standard. The IPA-based gradient for the discrete-A case is the main technical contribution; the derivation seems plausible under smoothness and non-degeneracy assumptions, though important regularity details are deferred to the supplement. The use of Cholesky factor derivatives is appropriate; complexity scales with O(q^3) per gradient sample.
- Approximations: The need to discretize A is common. The proposed evolving discrete set informed by posterior minimizer samples is sensible, but implementation details are sparse and the dependence of the discrete set on z(1:q) (An includes z(1:q)) raises subtlety: as z moves, the set elements move, so the min over a moving set introduces non-differentiabilities. The authors treat the finite set case in the gradient derivation; a discussion of how this carries over when elements move with z would strengthen the argument.
- Empirical evaluation: Results are favorable across noise-free and noisy settings. However, the paper reports evaluation efficiency in terms of function calls but not wall-clock time. Given q-KG requires Monte Carlo and gradient computations, timing comparisons versus q-EI (closed-form or low-variance estimators) and UCB baselines would be valuable. Sensitivity to the number of MC samples, M (discretization size), and q is not reported.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a precise statement of q-KG and the computational approach. Some sections (discretization via “drawing M samples from the global optima of the posterior”) could be clarified—presumably this is sampling from p(x* | D) as in PES. The gradient derivation conditions and assumptions could be summarized in the main text. Minor typos and some incomplete references should be fixed.

Originality:
Extending KG to batch selection with an efficiently optimizable acquisition is novel and non-trivial. While batch EI and PES have been extensively studied, a practical, differentiable q-KG with an IPA-based gradient appears new. The connection to expected improvement in the noise-free, “recommend from sampled points” special case is insightful.

Significance:
The method is likely to be adopted by practitioners who need parallel BO under noise. Decision-theoretic acquisitions that explicitly value information for the final recommendation are particularly relevant when noise is present. The empirical gains in the noisy setting are noteworthy. If computational overhead is acceptable, q-KG advances the state of the art in batch BO.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear, principled formulation with a compelling decision-theoretic justification and Bayes-optimality for one batch.
- Novel and useful stochastic gradient estimator enabling joint optimization over batches; addresses a known challenge in q-acquisitions.
- Strong empirical results, especially under noise, on both synthetic and practical hyperparameter tuning tasks; open-source implementation.
- Good coverage of related work; positions contribution relative to q-EI/UCB/PES and sequential KG.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Insufficient discussion of computational costs and wall-clock time; no timing comparisons to baselines.
- Gradient derivation relies on a finite, fixed discretization; practical implementation uses a moving discrete set that depends on z(1:q). Theoretical justification for unbiasedness/consistency under this approximation is not fully addressed.
- Experimental details are sometimes sparse (e.g., number of MC samples for q-KG and gradients, number of restarts, optimizer settings), and statistical significance is not consistently reported.
- Missing comparisons to some contemporary batch BO methods (e.g., local penalization or batch TS) could leave gaps in empirical positioning.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a computational complexity analysis and wall-clock time comparisons per BO iteration and per function evaluation saved, across q and dimension. Include ablations on the number of MC samples and effect on performance/variance.
- Clarify the discretization procedure: how are samples from p(x*) generated; how large is M; how sensitive are results to M; do you fix AMn during the inner optimization of z(1:q)?
- Discuss the impact of re-estimating GP hyperparameters on the Bayes-optimality interpretation. If possible, add a brief theoretical note on conditions under which the IPA gradient remains unbiased with moving discrete sets, or adopt a scheme that fixes the candidate set during acquisition optimization.
- Report additional baselines if feasible (e.g., parallel PES, local penalization, batch Thompson sampling), or justify their omission.
- Include variance/error bars for all plots, and perform statistical tests where appropriate. Add results for varying batch sizes q and noise levels.
- Minor: fix typos (e.g., “Main Stange”), standardize capitalization (Gaussian), and complete several references (years, venues, URLs).

Overall evaluation:
A solid and timely contribution to batch BO with a principled acquisition and a practical optimizer. With strengthened clarity about computational costs and the discretization/gradient interplay, the paper would be a strong addition. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the computational and clarity concerns.
