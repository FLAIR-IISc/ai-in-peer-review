Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization method based on the Knowledge Gradient (KG), termed q-KG. KG is known to be Bayes-optimal in the single-step sequential setting for minimizing posterior mean without restricting the final recommendation to previously sampled points. This paper extends KG to batch selection by defining the expected reduction in the posterior minimum after obtaining a batch of q observations. The acquisition is Bayes-optimal when there is only one batch left. The authors show that for noiseless observations and when the final recommendation is restricted to the set of sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Because evaluating and optimizing q-KG requires Monte Carlo, they derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and then apply gradient-based optimization. For continuous domains, they approximate the inner minimization by discretization using samples from posterior draws (as in predictive entropy search and related methods). Experiments on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR-10) show consistent improvements over batch EI (MOE and Spearmint versions), GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE, with particularly strong gains under observation noise.

This work builds on and differentiates itself from: (i) the original KG and correlated KG for sequential settings; (ii) parallel EI and its gradient-based optimization (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.); (iii) parallel UCB variants (GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE); and (iv) parallel entropy search (PPES). The contribution is to define a batch KG objective with a tractable, unbiased gradient estimator and to show empirical benefits, especially in noisy evaluations.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic motivation: q-KG directly targets the one-step Bayes-optimal batch for minimizing the posterior minimum, avoiding greedy approximations common in batch BO.
- Computational contribution: IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator for the batch KG objective, enabling practical gradient-based optimization, and a careful decomposition of the GP posterior update to make Monte Carlo feasible.
- Solid empirical evaluation: multiple synthetic functions across dimensions, noise-free and noisy regimes, and two practical hyperparameter tuning tasks. Gains under noise are substantial, which is a regime where EI and UCB often struggle.
- Connections to existing methods are made explicit, including the reduction to parallel EI under special conditions.
- Public code availability enhances reproducibility.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical guarantees beyond the one-batch Bayes optimality are limited. There is no analysis of convergence as the number of batches grows, nor of consistency of the discretized inner minimization. A discussion or theorem providing conditions under which the discretization error vanishes (e.g., with increasing M) would strengthen the work.
- Discretization strategy for the inner minimization (sampling candidate sets from posterior draws) is only briefly described. Details matter for correctness and performance: how are posterior function draws optimized to produce candidate minima? What is the computational overhead, and how sensitive are results to M and to the sampling method?
- Computational scaling with q and M is not reported in wall-clock terms. Since q-KG uses Monte Carlo and Cholesky decompositions of q×q kernels, reporting per-iteration overhead versus baselines (q-EI, PPES, UCB variants) would clarify practical trade-offs, especially for larger q.
- Experiments use q = 4; it would be useful to see performance and scaling at larger batch sizes (e.g., q ∈ {8, 16}) to assess the value of joint optimization over greedy batch construction.
- Fairness of comparisons: implementations derive from different toolkits (MOE, Spearmint, gpoptimization). While understandable, it would be helpful to confirm consistent GP priors, hyperparameter training schedules, and restarts across methods. For example, do all methods re-fit kernel hyperparameters after each batch via MLE?
- No comparison with PPES (parallel entropy search), which is a natural competitor optimizing a collective batch objective. Even a small-scale comparison, or a discussion of why it was omitted (e.g., computational burden), would be informative.
- Assumption of homoscedastic Gaussian noise estimated by MLE may be violated in hyperparameter tuning; discussing robustness or alternatives (e.g., Student-t likelihood, heteroscedastic models) would be valuable.

Quality
The technical development is sound. The IPA gradient derivation and its regularity conditions are plausible; the paper notes differentiability assumptions and provides details in the supplement. The reduction to parallel EI under special cases is correct and clarifies positioning. The experimental methodology is generally careful, with multiple runs and reporting mean/std. However, the absence of runtime comparisons and limited analysis of sensitivity to discretization and batch size are notable omissions.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized. The distinction between the continuous domain A and the discretized set An in the inner minimization is explained (though adding a small figure might aid intuition). The description of the discretization procedure could use more explicit steps (how AMn is constructed from posterior samples) and parameter settings (e.g., optimizer used for each posterior sample).

Originality
Extending KG to the batch setting with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator is a meaningful and novel contribution relative to prior batch BO work focused on EI, UCB, or entropy search. While IPA has been used for q-EI, applying it to q-KG and establishing unbiasedness is nontrivial and original.

Significance
Batch BO is practically important. The demonstrated gains in noisy settings are significant for applications like hyperparameter tuning with subsampled evaluation. If computational overhead remains moderate and scales to larger q, practitioners could adopt q-KG as a strong default for parallel BO.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide wall-clock time per batch and scaling with q and M, and report the number of Monte Carlo samples used for acquisition and gradient estimation.
- Add ablations: effect of M, number of gradient steps/restarts, and the variance of the IPA gradient.
- Include results for larger q. Compare to a greedy “fantasized” sequential KG within-batch to quantify the benefit of joint optimization.
- If feasible, include PPES or discuss its omission explicitly.
- Expand the description of the discretization procedure, including the optimizer for posterior draws and how many candidates are retained.
- Briefly discuss robustness to noise-model misspecification and potential extensions (e.g., heteroscedastic noise, common in ML evaluations).

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, principled acquisition function with a practical, unbiased gradient estimator.
- Consistent empirical improvements, especially under noise.
- Clear positioning relative to EI and UCB-based batch methods; code released.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond one-batch optimality and no convergence analysis under discretization.
- Missing runtime/scaling analysis and experiments for larger batch sizes.
- No comparison to PPES.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution to batch Bayesian optimization with a clear decision-theoretic foundation and promising empirical results. Addressing the noted experimental and theoretical gaps would further strengthen the paper.
