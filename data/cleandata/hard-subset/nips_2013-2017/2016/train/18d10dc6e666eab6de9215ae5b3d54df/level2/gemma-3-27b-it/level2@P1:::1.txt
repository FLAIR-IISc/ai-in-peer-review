This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO). The core idea is to extend the knowledge gradient approach to the parallel setting, aiming to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected incremental value of a single additional iteration. The authors derive an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy for computing this batch, and demonstrate its superior performance on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy environments.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It effectively contextualizes the work within the broader landscape of Bayesian Optimization, particularly highlighting the recent surge in interest in parallel BO. The connection to the sequential Knowledge Gradient method is clearly explained, and the rationale for extending it to the parallel setting is compelling. The mathematical derivation of q-KG appears sound, and the proposed infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for efficient gradient computation is a significant contribution. The empirical results are comprehensive, showcasing the method's effectiveness on both synthetic benchmarks and practical machine learning tasks (logistic regression and CNN tuning). The comparison against several state-of-the-art parallel BO algorithms (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) is thorough and demonstrates consistent performance gains, especially in noisy scenarios. Providing a code release is excellent and promotes reproducibility.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be strengthened. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The q-KG method represents a novel approach to parallel BO, moving beyond iterative greedy strategies towards a more holistic batch selection process.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation based on decision-theoretic principles and the connection to the sequential KG method provide a strong theoretical grounding.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The IPA-based gradient estimation is crucial for making the method practical and scalable.
*   **Empirical Performance:**  The consistent outperformance of q-KG over existing methods, especially in noisy settings, is a significant result.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms.
*   **Reproducibility:** Code availability is a major plus.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Discretization of A:** While the approach to discretizing the search space A using samples from the posterior distribution seems reasonable, the sensitivity of the performance to the number of samples (M = 1000) is not thoroughly investigated. A more detailed analysis of the trade-off between discretization accuracy and computational cost would be valuable.  Is 1000 a generally appropriate value, or does it heavily depend on the problem dimension and function characteristics?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments primarily focus on demonstrating the superiority of q-KG, but limited analysis is presented regarding the sensitivity of the method to its own hyperparameters (e.g., the parameters of the ARD Matern 5/2 kernel).
*   **Computational Cost Comparison:** While the authors claim computational efficiency through IPA, a more direct comparison of the computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time) of q-KG with the other methods would be beneficial.  Especially given the gradient-based optimization step.
*   **Limited Exploration of Noisy Scenarios:** The noisy experiments use a fixed noise level (σ = 0.5). Investigating the performance of q-KG across a wider range of noise levels would provide more robust insights.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations of q-KG. For instance, the computational complexity may still be prohibitive for very high-dimensional problems or extremely large batch sizes.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization.
*   The proposed method demonstrates superior empirical performance on a variety of benchmarks, including practical hyperparameter tuning tasks.
*   The efficient gradient computation strategy makes the method practical for real-world applications.
*   The clarity of the presentation and the availability of code enhance the paper's value to the community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The sensitivity analysis of the discretization parameter (M) is lacking.
*   A more detailed comparison of the computational cost with other methods is needed.
*   The discussion of limitations could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization.  The proposed q-KG method is a well-motivated and effective approach for parallel BO, and the authors have provided compelling evidence of its performance. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, particularly regarding the sensitivity analysis of the discretization parameter and a more thorough discussion of the method's limitations.
