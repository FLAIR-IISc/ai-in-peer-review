This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel algorithm for batch Bayesian optimization. The core contribution is a new acquisition function that generalizes the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) policy to the parallel setting. The q-KG acquisition function quantifies the expected value of simultaneously evaluating a batch of `q` points, based on the expected improvement in the final recommended solution (the minimum of the posterior mean). This provides a one-step Bayes-optimal selection criterion for the batch. Recognizing that naively maximizing this acquisition function is computationally prohibitive, the authors derive an efficient optimization strategy based on an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA). The paper provides empirical evidence on synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems, demonstrating that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms other state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization methods, with particularly strong performance in noisy environments.

This work builds upon a well-established line of research in Bayesian optimization. It directly extends the sequential KG method [7, 17] to the important and practical batch setting. The paper does an excellent job of positioning its contribution relative to other parallel Bayesian optimization approaches, such as those based on Expected Improvement (e.g., [2, 10, 25]) and Upper Confidence Bounds (e.g., [3, 5]). The key distinction is that q-KG jointly optimizes the batch of points based on a decision-theoretic principle for minimizing simple regret, whereas many competing methods either construct the batch greedily or are designed to minimize cumulative regret.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function from a decision-theoretic standpoint is sound and principled. The main technical contribution, the application of IPA to derive a stochastic gradient for optimizing q-KG, is non-trivial and essential for making the method practical. This allows the use of standard gradient-based optimizers. The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering four synthetic benchmarks and two real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10). The experiments are conducted in both noise-free and noisy settings, and the claims of the paper, particularly the superior performance in the noisy case, appear well-supported by the results described. The authors are also careful to compare against multiple relevant and well-implemented baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the background on Gaussian Processes is concise, and the derivation of the q-KG factor in Section 4 is easy to follow. The computational strategy in Section 5 is explained logically, moving from the estimation of the factor itself to its gradient, and finally to the handling of continuous domains via discretization. The experimental setup and results are also clearly described. The provision of code is a welcome addition that enhances reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel and significant extension of the knowledge gradient method to the batch setting. While other batch BO methods exist, the q-KG formulation, which is one-step Bayes-optimal for the final solution, is a novel approach in this context. The use of IPA to create a scalable gradient-based optimizer for this specific type of acquisition function is also an original technical contribution. This work is clearly distinct from prior art and represents a solid step forward in the field of Bayesian optimization.

**Significance:** The problem of batch (or parallel) optimization is of great practical importance, as parallel computing resources become increasingly available. An algorithm that can make more effective use of these resources to find optima of expensive black-box functions faster is highly valuable. The application to hyperparameter tuning is particularly compelling and a major driver of recent interest in BO. The demonstrated strength of q-KG in noisy settings is a key advantage, as many real-world problems (e.g., evaluating a model on a subset of a test set) involve noisy evaluations. Researchers and practitioners working on expensive optimization problems are likely to find this method useful and build upon these ideas.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Principled Approach:** The q-KG acquisition function is derived from a solid decision-theoretic foundation, making it one-step Bayes-optimal. This is a more principled approach than many ad-hoc or greedy heuristics for batch selection.
2.  **Efficient Optimization:** The development of an efficient stochastic gradient-based method for maximizing the acquisition function is a key strength that makes the approach computationally feasible.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method is shown to outperform or be competitive with several state-of-the-art methods on a variety of benchmarks. The advantage is particularly pronounced in the presence of noise, which is a common and challenging scenario in practice.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Computational Cost Analysis:** While the proposed optimization method is efficient relative to a naive approach, it is still computationally intensive, requiring Monte Carlo sampling and stochastic gradient ascent. A discussion of the wall-clock time per iteration compared to the baselines would be very valuable. For a practitioner, the trade-off between improved sample efficiency and increased computational overhead per batch is a critical consideration. How does this overhead scale with the batch size `q` and the dimensionality `d`?
2.  **Sensitivity to Discretization:** The method relies on a discretization `An` of the continuous domain `A` to compute the minimum of the posterior mean. The quality of this approximation depends on the number of points `M` and the sampling strategy. The paper uses M=1000, but some analysis of the method's sensitivity to the size and composition of this discrete set would strengthen the paper. This could be a significant issue in higher-dimensional problems.
3.  **Clarity on Regret Metric:** The y-axis in the experimental plots is described as the "base 10 logarithm of the immediate regret". It should be explicitly defined in the text or figure captions what this quantity is, e.g., `log10(f(x_n*) - f*)`, where `x_n*` is the best point found after `n` evaluations and `f*` is the true global minimum. Clarity on whether `x_n*` is the point with the best observed value or the point `argmin µ(n)(x)` is also important.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and empirically strong method for batch Bayesian optimization. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be addressed with additional discussion. The paper is a significant contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS.
