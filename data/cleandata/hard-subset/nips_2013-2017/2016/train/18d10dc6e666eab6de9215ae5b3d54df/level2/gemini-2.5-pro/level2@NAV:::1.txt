This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization. The core idea is to extend the decision-theoretic foundation of the sequential knowledge gradient to the parallel setting. Instead of greedily building a batch, q-KG selects the set of `q` points that jointly maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality after the batch evaluation. This work builds upon prior research in both sequential knowledge gradient methods and parallel extensions of other acquisition functions like EI and UCB. The main technical contribution is a computationally efficient method for optimizing the q-KG function using a gradient estimator based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA).

The strengths of this paper are significant. First, the proposed method is theoretically well-motivated, providing a one-step Bayes-optimal policy for selecting a batch of points. Second, the use of IPA to enable efficient gradient-based optimization of the acquisition function is a clever and crucial contribution that makes the approach practical. Third, the empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating strong performance against state-of-the-art benchmarks on both synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks for machine learning models. The method's particularly strong performance in the noisy evaluation setting is a key finding and highlights its practical significance, as many real-world optimization problems involve stochasticity. The paper is also clearly written and provides access to the code, enhancing reproducibility.

The paper could be improved by addressing a few points. The computational complexity of optimizing the q-KG acquisition function is a potential drawback. While the gradient estimation is efficient, the overall optimization can still be costly. A brief discussion on how the wall-clock time scales with the batch size `q` and problem dimensionality `d` compared to the benchmarks would be valuable. The discretization strategy for approximating the posterior minimum (Section 5.3) is a key heuristic; a more detailed justification for this specific choice over alternatives would strengthen the paper. Finally, the experiments are limited to a batch size of q=4. An analysis of the method's performance as `q` varies would provide useful insights into its behavior and limitations.

Overall, this is a high-quality submission presenting a novel, well-founded, and empirically validated method for an important problem. The strengths substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses.
