- Novel contribution: extends the knowledge gradient (KG) to the parallel setting with a decision-theoretic foundation that optimizes batches collectively rather than greedily.
- Strong empirical validation: comprehensive experiments on synthetic and real tasks (e.g., logistic regression, CNN tuning) showing faster convergence, especially in noisy settings.
- Technical efficiency: uses infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for gradient computation and a discretization strategy to handle continuous domains.
- Clarity and reproducibility: well-organized exposition and available code enhance reproducibility.
- Computational cost: q-KG is more expensive than simpler methods (parallel EI/UCB), raising concerns for high-dimensional problems or large batches.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity not explored: no detailed analysis of sensitivity to discretization size, initial samples, or other method hyperparameters.
- Comparison scope limited: lacks comparisons to recent BO advances (e.g., deep kernel learning, scalable high-dimensional approximations).
- Theoretical gaps: does not discuss potential suboptimality in multi-step decision-making despite one-step Bayes-optimality.
- For acceptance: addresses a relevant challenge and provides a clear, novel contribution to batch Bayesian optimization.
- For acceptance: empirical results are convincing across noise-free and noisy scenarios.
- For acceptance: grounded in rigorous decision-theoretic framework with practical computational solutions.
- Against acceptance: computational overhead may limit applicability to large-scale or high-dimensional problems.
- Against acceptance: missing comparisons to recent state-of-the-art methods leave open questions about ultimate performance.
- Recommendation: accept, with a suggestion that authors address computational complexity and broaden comparisons in future work.
