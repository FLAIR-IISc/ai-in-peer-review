This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization. The core idea is to leverage a decision-theoretic analysis to select a batch of points that are optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior, assuming only one batch remains. The paper provides a computationally efficient strategy based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize the q-KG acquisition function and demonstrates superior performance on both synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy settings.

The work builds upon existing literature in Bayesian optimization, specifically addressing the parallel evaluation setting which has seen increased attention recently. It correctly identifies the limitations of greedy iterative approaches used in prior work (e.g., parallel EI, UCB) and proposes a method that considers the batch as a whole. The connection to the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method is clearly articulated, and the authors explain how q-KG generalizes it to the parallel case. The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the mathematical formulation and algorithmic details. The inclusion of both synthetic and practical experiments strengthens the evaluation. 

A significant strength of the paper lies in the derivation and efficient computation of the q-KG gradient. The use of IPA to enable gradient-based optimization is a valuable contribution, addressing a key computational bottleneck. The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over existing methods, particularly when dealing with noisy function evaluations, which is a common scenario in real-world applications like hyperparameter tuning. The code availability is also a positive aspect.

However, some areas could be improved. While the paper mentions discretizing the search space, the specific details of how this discretization (M=1000 samples from the posterior) is implemented and its impact on performance are not sufficiently discussed. A sensitivity analysis on the value of M, or a comparison with other discretization strategies, would be beneficial. The explanation of how the GP hyperparameters are handled in the noisy setting (MLE is mentioned) could be expanded; specifying which hyperparameters are estimated and how it impacts computation time would be useful. 

Additionally, while the paper provides a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, exploring the computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time) of q-KG versus the benchmarks would provide a more complete picture. For example, it’s not immediately clear whether the improved performance comes at a significantly higher computational cost. Finally, the discussion of related work, while thorough, could be more directly connected to the specific contributions of this paper. For example, the authors could explicitly state how their approach differs from the nested batch knowledge gradient policy [26] beyond the computational efficiency and setting differences already mentioned.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization based on decision theory.
*   Efficient gradient computation via IPA enabling scalability.
*   Strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world problems, especially in noisy settings.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the method and experiments.
*   Code availability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the discretization strategy and its impact.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of computational cost compared to benchmarks.
*   The connection to some related works could be strengthened.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The proposed q-KG method offers a principled and effective approach to batch optimization, and the efficient gradient computation makes it practical for real-world applications. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. This reviewer recommends acceptance, with a request for the authors to address the points raised regarding discretization, computational cost, and connections to related work in a revised version.
