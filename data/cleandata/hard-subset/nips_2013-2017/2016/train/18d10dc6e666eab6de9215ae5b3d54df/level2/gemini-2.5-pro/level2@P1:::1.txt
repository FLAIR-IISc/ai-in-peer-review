This paper addresses the problem of batch Bayesian optimization (BO), where one aims to find the global optimum of an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function by selecting `q` points to evaluate in parallel at each iteration. The authors propose a novel acquisition function called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). This function is derived from a decision-theoretic framework and quantifies the expected improvement in the final recommended solution after observing the outcomes of a batch of `q` evaluations. By construction, q-KG is the one-step Bayes-optimal policy for selecting a batch.

A key challenge is that naively optimizing the q-KG acquisition function is computationally intractable. The main technical contribution of this paper is an efficient method for performing this optimization. The authors formulate the q-KG value as an expectation over a standard normal random vector, which can be estimated via Monte Carlo sampling. Crucially, they use Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic estimator for the gradient of the q-KG function. This enables the use of efficient gradient-based methods to find the optimal batch of points to evaluate. The authors also propose a dynamic discretization scheme to handle continuous optimization domains. The effectiveness of the q-KG method is demonstrated through extensive experiments on synthetic benchmark functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression and convolutional neural networks, showing superior performance compared to several state-of-the-art parallel BO algorithms, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and principled acquisition function for the highly relevant problem of batch Bayesian optimization. The generalization of the knowledge gradient criterion from the sequential to the batch setting is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. The decision-theoretic motivation provides a strong foundation for the method, distinguishing it from more heuristic approaches to batch construction.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The use of IPA to obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient of the Monte Carlo-estimated acquisition function is a sophisticated and powerful technique. This is a key contribution that makes an otherwise intractable optimization problem feasible. The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering both synthetic and real-world problems in noise-free and noisy settings, and includes comparisons to a strong set of recent baselines.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the proposed q-KG acquisition function is formally defined, and the computational procedure for optimizing it is explained in sufficient detail for an expert to understand the approach. The connection to and distinction from prior work is also clearly articulated.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** The results, particularly the strong performance in noisy environments, are highly significant. In many practical applications, such as tuning machine learning models by evaluating on a subset of the validation data, the function evaluations are inherently noisy. The demonstrated advantage of q-KG in this setting makes it a very promising method for real-world use.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Cost:** A potential weakness of the proposed method is its computational overhead. Optimizing the q-KG acquisition function requires repeated Monte Carlo sampling to estimate both the function value and its gradient at each step of the gradient ascent procedure. This is likely to be significantly more computationally expensive than the baseline methods. While the paper focuses on sample efficiency (number of function evaluations), a discussion on the trade-off with wall-clock time would be very beneficial. For problems where function evaluations are only moderately expensive, the time spent selecting the next batch could become a bottleneck. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion or experiment comparing the wall-clock time per iteration of q-KG against the baselines.
2.  **Details on Discretization and MC Sampling:** The discretization of the domain `A` into `An` to compute the q-KG factor is a critical component. The paper suggests a reasonable strategy based on sampling from the posterior optima, but the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to the size of this discrete set (`M`) is not analyzed. A brief discussion on the choice of `M` would be helpful. Furthermore, the number of Monte Carlo samples used to estimate the q-KG and its gradient is a key hyperparameter that balances accuracy and computational cost. Mentioning the number of samples used in the experiments would improve the paper's reproducibility.
3.  **Intuition on Noisy Performance:** The paper empirically shows that q-KG has a larger performance advantage in the noisy setting. This is an important result. The paper would be even stronger if it included a brief discussion providing intuition for *why* this is the case. One might hypothesize that because KG directly models the expected value of the final posterior minimum, it is inherently more robust to observation noise than methods based on expected improvement, which can be more sensitive to single optimistic but noisy observations. Explicitly stating this or a similar argument would enhance the reader's understanding.

**Summary of evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by strong empirical evidence.
*   **Clarity:** High. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** High. The paper proposes a novel, well-motivated batch acquisition function and a sophisticated method to optimize it.
*   **Significance:** High. The work addresses an important practical problem and the proposed method demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, especially in noisy settings.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The proposed q-KG method is novel, principled, and empirically effective. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to areas where additional details or discussion could further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
