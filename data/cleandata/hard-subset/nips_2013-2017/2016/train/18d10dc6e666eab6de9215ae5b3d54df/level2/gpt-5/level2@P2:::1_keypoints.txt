- Proposes q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that generalizes Knowledge Gradient to select q points jointly by expected reduction in minimum posterior mean.  
- Derives a Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG and an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based joint batch optimization.  
- Approximates the acquisition using a dynamically updated discrete candidate set sampled from posterior minimizers and augmented with prior candidates.  
- Empirical results show q-KG outperforms batch EI, GP-BUCB, and GP-UCB-PE on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning, with strong gains under observation noise.  
- Builds on sequential KG and relates to q-EI, parallel PES, and UCB-based batch methods, offering a decision-theoretic alternative to EI/UCB.  
- Definition and one-step Bayes-optimality are well-motivated; IPA-based gradient derivation is the main technical contribution and appears plausible under assumed regularity.  
- Gradient computation uses Cholesky derivatives and scales as O(q^3) per gradient sample.  
- Discretization of the domain is standard, but the moving discrete set (dependent on z(1:q)) introduces potential non-differentiabilities not fully addressed.  
- Supplement defers important regularity details; main-text summary of gradient derivation conditions would improve clarity.  
- Experiments are favorable but lack wall-clock time comparisons; computational cost of Monte Carlo and gradients versus closed-form q-EI or UCB is not reported.  
- Sensitivity analyses (number of MC samples, discretization size M, effect of q) and timing/variance studies are missing.  
- Paper is generally clear and organized, but some procedural descriptions (sampling p(x*), discretization) and minor typos/references need fixing.  
- Extending KG to batch selection with an IPA-based differentiable optimizer is novel and non-trivial.  
- Method is significant for practitioners needing parallel BO under noise and advances decision-theoretic acquisitions for noisy settings.  
- Pros: principled Bayes-optimal formulation, novel unbiased gradient enabling joint batch optimization, strong empirical gains, and open-source code.  
- Cons: insufficient discussion of computational/wall-clock costs, theoretical gap regarding moving discretization and unbiasedness, sparse experimental detail (MC counts, optimizer settings), and missing comparisons to some batch methods (e.g., local penalization, batch TS).  
- Suggested improvements: add wall-clock/time complexity analysis, ablations on MC samples/M, clarify and justify discretization procedure, provide theoretical note or fix candidate set during optimization, include more baselines, and add error bars/statistical tests.  
- Overall: solid, timely contribution likely to be accepted if computational and discretization/gradient clarity concerns are addressed; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
