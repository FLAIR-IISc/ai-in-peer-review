- Introduces q-KG: a batch Bayesian optimization method selecting q points jointly to maximize expected improvement in the GP predictor minimum.  
- Extends sequential KG to the batch setting with a decision-theoretic objective.  
- Main technical innovation: uses infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate gradients of q-KG for efficient acquisition optimization.  
- Theoretical foundation: derives q-KG from first principles and establishes Bayes-optimality for the one-step batch problem.  
- Provides rigorous justification for the IPA-based optimization strategy.  
- Empirical results: consistent improvements over parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE on synthetic functions and real hyperparameter tuning tasks.  
- Particularly strong gains in noisy settings (e.g., 3–4% lower test error in CNN tuning vs. parallel EI).  
- Experiments are comprehensive (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann; MNIST logistic regression; CIFAR10 CNN) and contrast noisy vs. noise-free cases.  
- Implementation: open-source code enhances reproducibility; Algorithm 1 offers a clear implementation roadmap.  
- Clarity: generally high and mathematically precise, though gradient-estimator derivation could be more intuitive for non-specialists.  
- Missing explicit computational-complexity statement (suggestion: state O(Mq) per gradient step).  
- Originality: distinguishes from prior batch methods by jointly optimizing batches rather than fixing other points; improves on nested batch KG ([26]) which is discrete-domain and less efficient.  
- Significance: high practical relevance for distributed hyperparameter tuning; demonstrated tangible improvements (e.g., CNN test error reduction).  
- Weaknesses: inadequate runtime benchmarking against baselines, lack of sensitivity analysis for discretization parameter M, and no numerical comparison to nested KG results.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to add runtime comparisons and discussion/analysis of M's impact (authors suggest M=500 vs M=1000 tradeoff).
