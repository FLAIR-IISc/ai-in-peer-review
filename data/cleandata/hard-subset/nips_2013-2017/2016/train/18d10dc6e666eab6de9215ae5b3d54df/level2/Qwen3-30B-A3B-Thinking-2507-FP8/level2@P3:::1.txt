

The paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), designed for scenarios where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. It extends the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method to the batch setting by deriving a decision-theoretic acquisition function that selects a batch of q points to maximize the expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor. The key innovation lies in efficiently computing the q-KG acquisition function and its gradient using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), avoiding the prohibitive cost of naive Monte Carlo optimization. The method is rigorously compared against established batch BO approaches (parallel EI, batch UCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression and CNNs.

The work demonstrates strong theoretical grounding: q-KG is proven Bayes-optimal for the final batch under the posterior, and the IPA-based gradient estimator is unbiased and computationally efficient. Empirically, the method consistently outperforms benchmarks, particularly in noisy settings where the advantage is most pronounced (e.g., CIFAR10 CNN tuning achieving 11% test error versus 14% for expert-tuned models). The experimental design is thorough, covering diverse synthetic functions (Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann), noise-free and noisy evaluations, and real-world ML applications with appropriate baselines.

Strengths include the clear theoretical contribution to batch BO, the practical computational solution via IPA, and compelling empirical results that validate the method's superiority in both synthetic and real-world scenarios. The paper effectively situates itself within prior work (e.g., distinguishing from iterative greedy batch methods [3, 5, 10] and discrete-set approaches [25]), with a well-structured comparison in Section 2. The implementation details and open-source code (GitHub) enhance reproducibility.

A primary weakness is the limited scalability analysis for larger batch sizes (q > 4). While the paper tests q=4, the computational complexity of the IPA gradient estimation (dependent on the discretization size M=1000) and the evolving candidate set (Section 5.3) may not scale efficiently for q=10+ or high-dimensional domains. The claim of "efficient optimization" lacks quantitative analysis of runtime versus q or dimensionality. Additionally, the assumption of constant noise variance (σ²) in Section 6 is restrictive for some applications; the paper does not explore adaptive noise modeling beyond MLE.

The significance is high, as batch BO is critical for parallel hyperparameter tuning in ML, where the proposed method reduces evaluation time while improving solution quality—especially valuable for noisy, expensive evaluations. The CIFAR10 result (11% vs. 14% error) provides tangible evidence of practical impact. The work advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally feasible batch method that outperforms iterative heuristics.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) include experiments with q=8–16 to assess scalability, reporting runtime and solution quality trade-offs; (2) justify the discretization size M (e.g., sensitivity analysis on M=500 vs. 1000) and explore adaptive discretization strategies; (3) briefly discuss extensions for non-constant noise (e.g., learning σ² per point). These are reasonable requests that address the scalability concern without requiring major revisions.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to batch Bayesian optimization with strong theoretical and empirical validation. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches, particularly in noisy settings. The minor weaknesses regarding scalability and noise assumptions are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution. The work is highly relevant to NIPS' focus on optimization and machine learning, advancing both theory and practice.
