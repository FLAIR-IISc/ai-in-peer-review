

This paper proposes the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, extending the sequential knowledge gradient approach to the parallel setting. The authors claim that q-KG provides the one-step Bayes-optimal batch of points to sample, and that their efficient IPA-based computational strategy enables practical implementation. The paper demonstrates that q-KG consistently outperforms state-of-the-art batch BO algorithms (parallel EI, batch UCB, GP-UCB-PE) on both synthetic functions and real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, with particularly strong performance in noisy evaluation settings.

The paper's strengths include a clear theoretical foundation for q-KG, rigorous experimental validation across multiple problem domains (including 4 synthetic functions and two practical ML applications), and a well-organized presentation of the computational approach. The comparison against relevant baselines is thorough, and the results convincingly demonstrate q-KG's advantages, especially in noisy scenarios. The authors provide sufficient implementation details and code availability, enhancing reproducibility.

However, the paper could strengthen its novelty claim by more explicitly differentiating from [25] and [18], which also address parallel knowledge gradient. While the IPA approach is well-explained, the computational complexity analysis of q-KG versus other methods would be valuable for practitioners. The paper also lacks discussion about the method's limitations when q becomes very large or in high-dimensional settings, which would help readers understand the approach's practical boundaries.

The work makes a significant contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature, providing a theoretically sound and practically effective solution for parallel function evaluation. The quality of the experimental results is strong, with statistically significant improvements demonstrated across multiple benchmarks. The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with well-structured sections that guide readers through the theoretical development to experimental validation.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in batch Bayesian optimization. The paper would be strengthened by slightly more detailed discussion of computational complexity and limitations, but these are minor concerns for a strong technical contribution. I recommend acceptance.
