

This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian optimization, addressing the important practical problem of evaluating multiple points simultaneously in expensive black-box optimization. The authors extend the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the batch setting by formulating a decision-theoretic objective that selects q points jointly to maximize the expected improvement in the minimum of the GP predictor. The key innovation lies in their efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of q-KG, enabling practical optimization of the acquisition function. The paper demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over existing batch BO methods (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on both synthetic functions and real hyperparameter tuning tasks, with especially pronounced gains in noisy settings.

The paper excels in technical quality and significance. The theoretical foundation is sound: the authors clearly derive the q-KG acquisition function from first principles, establish its Bayes-optimality for the one-step batch problem, and provide rigorous justification for their IPA-based optimization strategy. The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, covering multiple synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and two practical ML applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The noise-free and noisy settings are appropriately contrasted, and the results consistently show q-KG outperforming baselines, particularly in the noisy regime where it achieves 3-4% lower test error in CNN tuning compared to parallel EI. The open-source implementation further enhances reproducibility.

The clarity is generally high, with well-structured exposition. The mathematical derivations (Section 4-5) are precise, and Algorithm 1 provides a clear implementation roadmap. Minor clarity improvements could be made in Section 5.2 (the gradient estimator derivation could be more intuitive for non-specialists) and by explicitly stating the computational complexity of q-KG optimization (e.g., O(Mq) per gradient step where M is discretization size).

Originality is strong: the paper correctly identifies that existing batch methods (e.g., [2, 3, 5, 18, 25]) optimize batches iteratively by fixing other points, whereas q-KG considers the batch collectively. The comparison to [26] (nested batch KG) is appropriate, noting their discrete-domain restriction and less efficient computation. The work builds meaningfully on sequential KG [7, 17] while addressing a critical gap in parallel BO.

Significance is high. Batch BO is increasingly important as hyperparameter tuning scales across distributed systems. The paper demonstrates tangible benefits for real ML applications (e.g., improving CNN test error from 14% to 11% on CIFAR10), and the method's robustness to noise makes it particularly valuable for noisy real-world evaluations. The results suggest q-KG could become a standard baseline for parallel BO.

A few minor weaknesses warrant mention. First, the computational cost of q-KG (Section 5.3) is not thoroughly benchmarked against alternatives; while the paper states "efficient," it would strengthen the claim to provide runtime comparisons with parallel EI or GP-BUCB, especially as q increases. Second, the discretization strategy (Section 5.3) relies on sampling from the GP's posterior optima, but the paper does not discuss how the choice of M=1000 affects convergence or sensitivity to this parameter. Third, while [26] is cited as related work, a brief comparison of results (e.g., "q-KG achieves X% better than nested KG on Y task") would better contextualize the contribution.

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS: it advances the state of the art in Bayesian optimization with a theoretically grounded, practically effective method. The writing is clear, the experiments are thorough, and the significance to both theory and practice is evident. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the computational cost discussion and discretization sensitivity. The authors should add a short paragraph comparing q-KG's runtime to baselines and briefly discussing M's impact on results (e.g., "We observed that M=500 yields comparable results to M=1000 with 30% faster optimization"). This would strengthen the empirical claims without requiring new experiments. The paper represents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization that would be valuable to both researchers and practitioners.
