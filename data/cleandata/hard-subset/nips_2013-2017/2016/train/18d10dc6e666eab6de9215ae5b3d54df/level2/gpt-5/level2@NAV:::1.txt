Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. The key claims are: (1) q-KG is Bayes-optimal in the one-step setting for minimizing the GP posterior mean; (2) an efficient Monte Carlo/IPA-based gradient estimator enables practical optimization of the acquisition; (3) q-KG outperforms prior batch BO methods (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), especially under noisy evaluations. The work builds on KG for sequential BO and compares to established batch methods, including multi-point EI and UCB variants, and discusses links to entropy-search–style discretization.

Strengths:
- Quality: The decision-theoretic derivation is sound, and the Monte Carlo formulation with IPA for unbiased gradients is well motivated. The algorithm integrates standard GP modeling and re-estimation of hyperparameters.
- Originality: Extending KG to the batch setting with a differentiable stochastic objective and IPA gradients is novel and nontrivial relative to prior greedy or closed-form multi-point EI approaches.
- Significance: Parallel evaluations are common; a one-step optimal criterion that is robust to noise is practically valuable. Results on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning (MNIST logistic regression, CIFAR-10 CNN) suggest consistent gains, notably with noise.
- Clarity: The presentation is generally clear, with an explicit algorithm, derivations, and code availability.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Scope of “Bayes-optimal”: The optimality statement holds for a single remaining batch and, in practice, after discretization of A. Please clarify the conditions (continuous A vs. discretized An) and the effect of discretization on optimality and bias.
- Computational characterization: Provide complexity and wall-clock comparisons (as q, d, M, and number of MC samples vary), gradient variance analysis, and sensitivity to M and the number of fantasies. Quasi-Monte Carlo and common random numbers could further reduce variance.
- Baselines: Consider including parallel PES/PPES and parallel Thompson sampling (“fantasization”) for a more complete empirical picture.
- Experimental completeness: Report the number of runs, seeds, and confidence intervals for all plots; the CNN result with only 5 runs is weak evidence for a large claimed improvement. Include ablations (noise variance learned vs. fixed; discretization strategy).
- Theory: While regret guarantees are not required, a brief discussion of consistency or asymptotic behavior under increasing M and evaluations would strengthen the work.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled acquisition with solid derivation
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise
- Practical relevance and open-source implementation

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited baselines and ablation; sparse runtime analysis
- Optimality qualified by discretization and single-batch assumption
- Some results based on few runs

Overall, a strong and useful contribution; with the suggested clarifications and empirical additions, it would be even more compelling.
