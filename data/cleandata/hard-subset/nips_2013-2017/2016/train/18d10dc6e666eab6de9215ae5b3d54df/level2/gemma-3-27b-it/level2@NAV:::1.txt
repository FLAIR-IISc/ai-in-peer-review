## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) algorithm designed for parallel function evaluations. The core idea is to select a batch of points that are Bayes-optimal in the average-case for a single remaining batch, extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient approach to the parallel setting. The authors also present an efficient method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function, enabling its optimization.

The paper clearly identifies the problem of efficiently utilizing parallel evaluations in BO, a common scenario in applications like hyperparameter tuning. The q-KG method represents a theoretically sound approach, directly stemming from decision-theoretic analysis. The authors rightly point out that a naive maximization of the proposed acquisition function would be computationally prohibitive and address this with the IPA-based gradient estimation. Empirical results on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning tasks (logistic regression and CNN tuning) demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings. The inclusion of a code repository is also a valuable contribution.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical motivation is strong, the presentation could be more accessible. The jump between the theoretical derivation and the practical implementation details (particularly the discretization strategy in Section 5.3) is somewhat abrupt. While the authors mention the computational complexity of q-KG, a more detailed analysis comparing it to other methods would be helpful. Moreover, the experimental section, while comprehensive, could benefit from a more rigorous statistical analysis. Reporting only mean and standard deviation without significance tests limits the strength of the conclusions. The comparisons to other methods are somewhat uneven, relying on different implementations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded approach to parallel BO.
*   Demonstrated superior performance over existing methods on multiple benchmarks.
*   Practical contribution with code release.
*   Addresses a relevant and increasingly important problem in machine learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Presentation can be dense and lacks some clarity, especially regarding implementation details.
*   Limited statistical rigor in the experimental evaluation.
*   Uneven comparison to other methods due to reliance on different implementations.



Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. Despite some areas for improvement, the novelty of the approach, strong empirical results, and practical relevance warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and strengthen the experimental analysis. Specifically, the authors should provide more detailed explanations of the discretization strategy, add statistical significance tests to the results, and ideally, compare against a unified implementation of all benchmark methods.
