Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization algorithm based on the knowledge gradient (KG), termed q-KG. The acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective: for a given batch of q points, it measures the expected reduction in the posterior mean’s minimum after one additional parallel evaluation, and selects the batch that maximizes this reduction. This generalizes the sequential KG (Frazier et al., INFORMS J. Computing, 2008) to the parallel setting. The work connects to and contrasts with prior batch BO methods that either greedily construct batches using single-point heuristics (e.g., EI with “fantasizing”/constant liar; GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) or jointly optimize multi-point EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.) and PPES (Hernandez-Lobato et al.). A key computational contribution is an efficient stochastic gradient estimator of q-KG via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch acquisition. The paper also clarifies that, in the noiseless case and if the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points, q-KG recovers parallel EI. Empirically, q-KG is evaluated on standard synthetic benchmarks (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and on hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression (MNIST) and CNNs (CIFAR-10), with and without observation noise, showing consistent gains, particularly under noise.

Strengths:
- Principled objective: q-KG is Bayes-optimal for the one-step lookahead objective of minimizing the posterior mean’s minimum, providing a clean decision-theoretic justification for batch selection.
- Sound computational strategy: the use of IPA to obtain unbiased stochastic gradients of the acquisition under mild conditions is well-motivated and practically important; the derivations appear careful.
- Solid empirical performance: consistent improvements over strong baselines (MOE parallel EI, Spearmint heuristic EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE), with especially notable gains under noise, where EI-based methods are known to struggle.
- Clear connection to existing methods: reduction to parallel EI in a particular setting aids interpretability and positions the contribution within the BO landscape.
- Availability of code and use of established GP modeling (ARD Matérn-5/2 with MLE) support reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Discretization of A in acquisition evaluation: q-KG requires discretizing the inner minimization domain, which weakens the “Bayes-optimal” claim in practice and may affect performance in higher dimensions. Suggest reporting sensitivity to M (e.g., M=250, 500, 1000, 2000), and discussing the trade-off between accuracy and runtime. Clarify the procedure for drawing AM_n (“samples from the global optima of the posterior”) with a precise citation and algorithmic detail; as written, this step is under-specified.
- Scalability and runtime: batch BO is attractive when wall-clock savings from parallelism outweigh acquisition optimization overheads. The paper lacks wall-clock or complexity comparisons. Suggest reporting per-iteration runtimes and scaling with q and d, including cost components (MC samples for Z_q, Cholesky and its derivatives, number of gradient steps). Evaluations with larger q (e.g., 8–16) would strengthen the case.
- Baselines: while several strong baselines are included, joint acquisition baselines such as PPES (and, where applicable, closed-form multi-point EI [Chevalier & Ginsbourger] in the noiseless case) are missing from the empirical comparisons. Including PPES, or discussing why it is impractical under the evaluated settings, would improve completeness.
- Assumptions on noise: experiments estimate a constant σ^2 for noisy settings; practical ML hyperparameter tuning often exhibits heteroscedastic noise. A brief discussion or an ablation with repeated evaluations or simple heteroscedastic modeling would be valuable.
- Theoretical aspects: the IPA argument relies on differentiability and uniqueness of minimizers; while ties occur with probability zero under smooth kernels, a short statement outlining conditions and referencing the supplementary proofs would help readers assess generality. Any convergence guarantees or regret properties (even in simplified settings) would further strengthen the contribution.

Quality:
- The methodological development is technically sound, with appropriate use of GP posteriors, Monte Carlo estimation, and IPA for stochastic gradients. The reduction to parallel EI under specific conditions is correctly argued. The main caveat is the discretization approximation, whose accuracy and impact are not quantified. Experimental design is standard and fair; hyperparameters are re-estimated by MLE each iteration.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized. A few places merit clarification: the construction of AM_n; computational complexity per acquisition evaluation; details of the gradient through the Cholesky factor; and experimental budgets (number of iterations, total function evaluations) and wall-clock times. Minor typos (e.g., “Main Stange”) should be corrected.

Originality:
- Extending KG to the batch setting with an efficient gradient estimator via IPA is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution. While related in spirit to multi-point EI/PPES, the decision-theoretic objective and gradient estimator distinguish the approach.

Significance:
- Batch BO is an important practical setting; gains under noise are especially impactful for ML hyperparameter tuning. With code provided and results showing consistent improvements, the work is likely to influence practice and spur further research on principled batch acquisition optimization.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Principled and well-motivated acquisition with a solid computational solution.
- Strong empirical gains, particularly in noisy scenarios.
- Clear relationship to and improvement over widely used baselines.
- Open-source implementation.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical Bayes-optimality depends on discretization; sensitivity and scaling are not quantified.
- Missing runtime analyses and some relevant baselines (e.g., PPES).
- Limited discussion of scalability to higher dimensions and larger batch sizes.

Constructive suggestions (prioritized):
- Essential: add runtime/scaling results versus baselines; clarify and evaluate sensitivity to discretization M and the AM_n construction.
- Important: include or justify omission of PPES and multi-point EI (closed-form, where applicable) in comparisons.
- Optional: examine larger q; provide brief theoretical conditions for IPA unbiasedness; discuss handling of heteroscedastic noise or repeated evaluations.
- Minor: fix typos; expand implementation details for gradient computation and optimization settings.
