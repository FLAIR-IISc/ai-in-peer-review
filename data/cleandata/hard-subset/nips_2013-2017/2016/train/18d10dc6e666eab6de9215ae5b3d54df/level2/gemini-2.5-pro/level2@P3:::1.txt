**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization, termed the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method extends the sequential knowledge gradient policy to the parallel setting, where a batch of `q` points can be evaluated simultaneously. The q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic framework, aiming to select the batch of points that provides the maximal one-step expected improvement in the final solution quality, defined as the minimum of the Gaussian process posterior mean.

A key contribution of the work is a computationally efficient method for maximizing this new acquisition function. As a naive optimization is intractable, the paper proposes a gradient-based approach. It leverages infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased estimator of the q-KG factor's gradient, allowing the joint optimization of all `q` points in the batch. The work is positioned as a more principled alternative to existing parallel Bayesian optimization methods, such as greedy or heuristic extensions of Expected Improvement (EI) and Upper Confidence Bound (UCB). The effectiveness of the proposed q-KG method is demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on synthetic functions and two real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, showing superior or competitive performance against state-of-the-art baselines, with particularly notable gains in noisy environments.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The derivation of the q-KG factor is principled and well-founded in decision theory. The application of IPA to create a tractable, gradient-based optimization procedure for a complex, stochastic acquisition function is a strong technical contribution. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple relevant baselines on a variety of problems, and the claims of improved performance, especially in the noisy setting, are well-supported by the results. The provision of open-source code is a commendable step toward reproducibility.

For potential improvement, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity would be beneficial. The proposed method relies on Monte Carlo estimation and multi-start stochastic gradient ascent, which can be computationally intensive. A comparison of the wall-clock time required to select a batch versus the baseline methods would provide valuable practical insight for potential users. Additionally, the reliance on discretizing the domain to compute the minimum of the posterior mean is a critical component. While the proposed dynamic discretization scheme is sensible, a brief analysis of its sensitivity to the number of discretization points (M) could further strengthen the work by clarifying the trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational cost.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation for the problem is clear, the background is presented succinctly, and the proposed method is developed in a step-by-step manner. The distinction from prior work is well-articulated. To enhance clarity and reproducibility, it would be helpful to provide more detail on the hyperparameters of the q-KG optimization procedure itself. For instance, information regarding the number of Monte Carlo samples used to estimate the expectation and its gradient, the number of random starts, and the parameters of the gradient-based optimizer would allow other researchers to replicate the setup more easily.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on the established knowledge gradient framework, its generalization to the parallel batch setting is non-trivial and represents a significant conceptual advance. Unlike many prior parallel BO methods that construct batches greedily, this work proposes a method to jointly optimize all points in the batch under a single, coherent objective. The computational technique developed to achieve this, using IPA for gradient estimation, is also a novel contribution in this context. The paper adequately situates its contributions within the existing literature and clearly distinguishes its approach from previous parallel EI and UCB methods.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of high practical and research importance. As parallel computing resources become more accessible, methods that can effectively leverage them for expensive black-box optimization are increasingly valuable. The q-KG method offers a powerful new tool for this purpose. The strong empirical performance, particularly the demonstrated robustness and efficiency in noisy settings, is a key result. Many real-world optimization problems, such as hyperparameter tuning using cross-validation or on data subsets, inherently involve noisy evaluations, making this contribution especially relevant. The work is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers in the Bayesian optimization community. A potential avenue to further demonstrate significance would be to explore the method's scalability with respect to the batch size `q`, as the experiments are limited to `q=4`.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and fully Bayesian acquisition function for the important problem of batch Bayesian optimization.
*   Introduces a sophisticated and effective computational technique (IPA-based gradient estimation) to make the optimization of the acquisition function tractable.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance against several state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world problems.
*   The method shows particularly significant advantages in noisy environments, a common and challenging practical scenario.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational cost of the batch selection procedure is not explicitly analyzed or compared to baselines, which is an important practical consideration.
*   The performance and computational scaling of the method for larger batch sizes (`q > 4`) are not explored.

**Overall Recommendation**
This is a strong paper presenting a novel and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The theoretical motivation is sound, the technical execution is impressive, and the empirical results are convincing. The identified weaknesses are minor and represent opportunities for future work or additions that would further strengthen an already excellent paper. The work is a clear candidate for acceptance.
