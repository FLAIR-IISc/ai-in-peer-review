- Proposes q-KG: a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that selects q points jointly by the expected reduction in the posterior mean minimum (one-step Bayes-optimal for simple regret with one batch left).

- Under noise-free settings with final recommendation restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to q-EI.

- q-KG evaluation/optimization requires Monte Carlo over batch outcomes; authors derive an IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator for efficient gradient-based optimization.

- For continuous domains, inner optimization is discretized using sampled candidate optima from the GP posterior (ES/PES-style) augmented with previous samples and the candidate batch.

- Empirical evaluation on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning (MNIST, CIFAR-10) compares q-KG to q-EI variants, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE in noisy and noise-free settings.

- q-KG is consistently competitive and often superior to baselines, with especially large gains under noisy evaluations.

- The work connects to sequential KG, correlated KG, multipoint EI, parallel UCB variants, and parallel entropy methods, and extends KG into the parallel setting with a practical optimizer.

- Decision-theoretic formulation and equivalence to q-EI in the stated special case are sound and clarifying.

- The IPA-based gradient estimator is technically solid under differentiable kernels and almost-sure unique minimizers, addressing a core computational challenge in batch selection.

- Important technical details are deferred to the supplement: conditions for exchanging expectation and differentiation, handling non-unique argmins, and the effect of discretization on unbiasedness.

- The discretization strategy is reasonable (based on ES/PES) but its approximation error and sensitivity to the number of candidates (M) are not quantified.

- Experiments are fairly extensive and support robustness claims, but lack wall-clock runtimes vs. q and dimension and may suffer from non-uniform GP modeling across baselines.

- Presentation is generally clear, but notation overload (A used for two meanings) is confusing and should be fixed.

- More algorithmic details are needed for reproducibility: numbers of Monte Carlo samples, optimizer/step sizes, stopping criteria, and batch optimizer initialization.

- The description of how posterior minimizer samples are drawn (ES/PES reference) should be expanded to be self-contained.

- Extending KG to batch with an IPA gradient method is novel and a meaningful contribution despite borrowing discretization and fantasy sampling ideas from prior work.

- The method is practically significant for batch BO and noisy/stochastic objectives like hyperparameter tuning, though lack of convergence/regret guarantees and runtime analysis temper impact.

- Specific recommended additions: complexity analysis per gradient step (function of q, M, n) and empirical runtimes vs q and d.

- Address non-differentiability from ties in the inner min (e.g., soft-min smoothing) and report frequency of ties in practice.

- Add ablations on the number of discretization points M and the discretization strategy to quantify performance vs cost trade-offs.

- Ensure consistent GP priors and hyperparameter learning across baselines or provide a control experiment with a unified GP implementation.

- Include comparisons to other batch BO heuristics (e.g., local penalization, batch Thompson sampling) and report results for larger batch sizes (q = 8, 16).

- Pros: principled one-step Bayes-optimal batch acquisition, efficient IPA-based gradient, strong empirical performance under noise, clarifying relation to q-EI, and open-source implementation.

- Cons: missing runtime/scalability analysis, incomplete articulation of discretization/unbiasedness conditions, potential confounds from differing GP implementations, no theoretical convergence/regret guarantees beyond one-step optimality, limited exploration of larger q and sensitivity to M.

- Overall recommendation: strong contribution; recommend acceptance after addressing clarity and experimental rigor issues (runtime reporting, ablations, and clarifying technical assumptions).
