- Paper contribution: Introduces Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG), extending the knowledge gradient acquisition to batch Bayesian Optimization with a gradient-based optimization strategy.
- Methodology: Derives q-KG acquisition function and uses infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for efficient gradient computation.
- Empirical results: Shows superior performance over existing parallel BO methods on synthetic and hyperparameter-tuning tasks, especially under noise.
- Writing and organization: Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow.
- Reproducibility: Code repository is provided, facilitating reproduction and follow-up work.
- Strength — Novelty: q-KG is a principled, decision-theoretic extension to batch BO that selects batches jointly rather than greedily.
- Strength — Theoretical soundness: Derivation is principled and IPA addresses a key computational challenge.
- Strength — Empirical performance: Consistent outperformance of baselines, with practical demonstrations (logistic regression, CNNs).
- Strength — Clarity: Explanations and presentation are clear and logically organized.
- Weakness — Computational complexity: Optimization of q-KG remains computationally demanding for high dimensions or large batch sizes despite IPA.
- Weakness — Discretization approximation: Use of discrete candidate set (A) and 1000 samples may introduce approximation errors; sensitivity analysis is lacking.
- Weakness — Comparison depth: More analysis is needed on why q-KG outperforms baselines (e.g., its exploration–exploitation behavior).
- Weakness — Hyperparameter sensitivity: Limited study of q-KG’s own hyperparameters (e.g., IPA optimization settings).
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound, strong empirical results, well-written, and reproducible work addressing a relevant problem.
- Arguments against acceptance: Remaining concerns about computational cost and discretization approximations that need further investigation.
- Overall assessment: Strong paper recommending acceptance with minor revisions to address computational complexity, discretization sensitivity, and deeper comparative analysis.
