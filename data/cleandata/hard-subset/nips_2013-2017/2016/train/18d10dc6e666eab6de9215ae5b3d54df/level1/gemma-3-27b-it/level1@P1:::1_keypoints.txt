- Paper proposes q-KG, a Parallel Knowledge Gradient method for batch Bayesian optimization extending KG to parallel settings.
- Core idea: optimize a Bayes-optimal batch selection for one remaining batch using a KG framework.
- Uses infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute gradients for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function.
- Empirical validation provided on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks.
- Well-motivated problem: addresses efficient parallel evaluation in Bayesian optimization.
- Theoretical foundation: grounded in decision-theoretic, Bayes-optimal principles under specific conditions.
- Novelty: valuable extension of Knowledge Gradient to batch/parallel evaluation.
- Computational efficiency: IPA-based gradient estimation offers a practical way to mitigate optimization complexity.
- Empirical performance: shows consistent improvements over parallel EI, batch UCB, etc., especially in noisy settings.
- Clarity and presentation: paper is well-written, algorithm and derivations are clearly presented.
- Code availability: authors provide code, aiding reproducibility.
- Discretization weakness: relies on discretizing search space A with limited justification for the chosen sampling strategy.
- Sensitivity to discretization: insufficient exploration of sensitivity to the number of samples M for discretization.
- Computational cost concern: q-KG evaluation and gradient remain expensive for high dimensions or large batches; needs detailed complexity analysis.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: limited analysis of how kernel parameters and other hyperparameters affect performance.
- Limited noise exploration: experiments mainly use a single noise level (σ = 0.5), reducing conclusions about robustness across noise regimes.
- Comparison issues: dismissal of nested batch KG/[26] may be premature; a more nuanced or fair comparison is needed.
- Recommendation: overall strong contribution with empirical support; reviewer recommends acceptance with minor revisions.
- Suggested revisions: justify discretization method and study M sensitivity, provide complexity analysis, test wider noise ranges, discuss hyperparameter tuning guidance, and offer a more balanced comparison to related approaches like [26].
