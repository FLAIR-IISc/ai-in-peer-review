**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The goal of batch BO is to select a set of `q` points to evaluate in parallel at each iteration to find the global minimum of an expensive-to-evaluate black-box function. The proposed q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic framework. It quantifies the expected improvement in the final recommended solution (the minimum of the posterior mean) after observing the outcomes of evaluating a batch of `q` points, assuming this is the final batch. This makes q-KG a one-step Bayes-optimal policy for this objective.

This work generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) policy [7, 17] to the parallel setting. A major contribution is the development of a computationally feasible method to optimize the q-KG acquisition function. Since the function lacks a closed form, the authors propose a Monte Carlo-based estimator. Crucially, they derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA), enabling the use of efficient gradient-based methods to jointly optimize the locations of all `q` points in the batch. To handle continuous domains, the method relies on a dynamic discretization scheme.

The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation, comparing q-KG to state-of-the-art parallel BO methods like parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and parallel Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) variants. The experiments cover synthetic benchmark functions and two real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10). The results demonstrate that q-KG is highly competitive and often outperforms existing methods, with a particularly significant advantage in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function as a one-step Bayes-optimal policy is principled and sound. The main technical challenge—the optimization of this acquisition function—is addressed with a sophisticated and appropriate tool, namely the IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator. The authors correctly point out that this is necessary because naive optimization would be computationally intractable. The mathematical details behind the gradient calculation are outlined, and the final algorithm is clearly presented.

The experimental methodology is solid. The authors compare against a good selection of relevant and strong baselines from recent literature, using open-source implementations where available. The choice of both synthetic and real-world problems demonstrates the practical applicability of the method. The investigation of both noise-free and noisy settings is particularly valuable, and the claim that q-KG excels in the noisy case is well-supported by the evidence provided. The authors are honest about the results, noting for instance that on the Branin2 function, their method is slightly outperformed by a baseline. The provision of code is a major strength that speaks to the completeness and reproducibility of the work.

One minor point for improvement would be a brief discussion on the regularity conditions required for the interchange of expectation and differentiation used in the IPA gradient estimator (Eq. 5.3). While likely standard and detailed in the supplement, mentioning the key assumptions (e.g., differentiability of the kernel) in the main text would further bolster the stated soundness.

**Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem of batch BO and situates the contribution within the field. The related work section is comprehensive and effectively distinguishes q-KG from prior approaches, particularly greedy batch construction methods versus their joint optimization approach. Section 4 clearly defines the q-KG factor, and Section 5 systematically breaks down the computational strategy for optimizing it. The figures are clear and effectively communicate the empirical results. The overall structure makes the paper easy to follow for a reader familiar with Bayesian Optimization.

**Originality**

The work is highly original. While it builds upon the established Knowledge Gradient framework, its generalization to the parallel setting is non-trivial and novel. The key original contribution is not just the formulation of the q-KG acquisition function, but the method for making its optimization practical. Using IPA to derive a stochastic gradient for a KG-style acquisition function in the context of continuous-domain BO is a significant and novel technical idea. This approach for joint optimization of the batch stands in contrast to many existing heuristics that build batches sequentially or use approximations. The paper correctly cites and differentiates itself from related work, including other joint-optimization methods like parallel EI [2, 15, 25] and parallel ES [18], highlighting its unique decision-theoretic foundation.

**Significance**

The paper's contributions are significant and likely to have a substantial impact on the field. Batch BO is a problem of great practical importance, as parallel computing resources become increasingly ubiquitous for tasks like hyperparameter tuning. The proposed q-KG method advances the state of the art, providing a new, high-performing algorithm for practitioners.

The demonstrated strength in noisy environments is a particularly significant result. Many real-world optimization problems, such as tuning machine learning models based on cross-validation accuracy, inherently involve noisy evaluations. An algorithm that robustly handles this noise is of immense practical value. Given its strong theoretical grounding and impressive empirical performance, q-KG is likely to be widely adopted, and the underlying ideas (especially the use of IPA for optimizing complex acquisition functions) could inspire further research in BO and related fields.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Principled Approach:** Proposes a well-motivated, decision-theoretic acquisition function (q-KG) that generalizes the successful KG method to the important batch BO setting.
*   **Novel Technical Contribution:** Develops a clever and efficient optimization strategy based on Monte Carlo sampling and an IPA-based stochastic gradient estimator, making the principled approach practical.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance against multiple strong baselines on a variety of synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems.
*   **High Impact in Noisy Settings:** Shows a particularly strong advantage in the presence of noise, a common and challenging scenario in practice.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors provide code, facilitating adoption and verification by the community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper could benefit from a discussion of the computational overhead of the q-KG acquisition function optimization compared to baselines. The procedure appears more complex than some alternatives, and an analysis of its scalability with respect to batch size `q` and dimension `d` would be valuable.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** The performance of the method relies on a discretization heuristic (sampling `M` points from the posterior). A brief discussion on the sensitivity to the size of this set (`M`) would strengthen the paper.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to Bayesian Optimization. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core strengths of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
