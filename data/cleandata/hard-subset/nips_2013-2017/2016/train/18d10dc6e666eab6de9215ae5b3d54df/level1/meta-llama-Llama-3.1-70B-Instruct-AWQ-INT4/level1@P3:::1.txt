This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG), for efficiently optimizing expensive-to-evaluate black-box functions. The authors derive a new acquisition function that measures the information gain of evaluating q points and demonstrate its effectiveness in both synthetic and real-world problems, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their methodology. The introduction effectively sets the context for the work, and the related work section provides a comprehensive overview of existing batch Bayesian optimization algorithms.

The proposed q-KG algorithm is based on a decision-theoretic analysis that chooses the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior when there is only one batch of points remaining. The authors provide an efficient strategy for computing this Bayes-optimal batch of points using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to evaluate the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the q-KG algorithm in both noise-free and noisy settings. The authors compare their algorithm with several state-of-the-art batch Bayesian optimization algorithms, including parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with pure exploration. The results show that q-KG consistently outperforms or is competitive with these algorithms, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for their algorithm, which is based on a decision-theoretic analysis. Secondly, the experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm in a variety of settings. Finally, the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their methodology, which makes the paper accessible to a wide range of readers.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, the authors could provide more details on the computational complexity of their algorithm, especially in comparison to other batch Bayesian optimization algorithms. Secondly, the authors could provide more insights into the behavior of the q-KG algorithm in different settings, such as the effect of the batch size on the performance of the algorithm. Finally, the authors could consider providing more comparisons with other optimization algorithms, such as gradient-based methods or evolutionary algorithms.
