

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient method to the parallel setting. The authors derive q-KG using decision-theoretic analysis to select a batch of q points that maximizes expected improvement in the minimum of the Gaussian process predictor, providing a one-step Bayes-optimal batch for the case of a single remaining iteration. The paper develops an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis to optimize the acquisition function, addressing the high computational cost of naively maximizing q-KG.

The work builds upon Frazier et al.'s knowledge gradient (NIPS 2008, 2009) and extends recent parallel Bayesian optimization methods like parallel EI (Snoek et al., NIPS 2012) and GP-UCB-PE (Contal et al., ECML 2014). The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by formulating a batch acquisition function that considers points collectively rather than iteratively.

Strengths include strong theoretical foundations, thorough experimental validation across both synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), and clear demonstration of superiority over state-of-the-art methods, especially in noisy settings. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Weaknesses include limited discussion of computational complexity for large batch sizes (q) and insufficient comparison with very recent batch BO methods like "Parallel predictive entropy search" (Shah & Ghahramani, NIPS 2015). The discretization strategy could also be more thoroughly explained.

The paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian optimization, addressing an important practical problem (parallel evaluation) with a theoretically sound and empirically superior method. The results advance the state of the art in batch Bayesian optimization, particularly for noisy function evaluations.

Pro: Novel theoretical contribution, strong empirical results, clear presentation, significant practical impact for hyperparameter tuning. Con: Limited discussion of computational scalability for large q, insufficient comparison with some very recent methods. Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field and should be accepted.
