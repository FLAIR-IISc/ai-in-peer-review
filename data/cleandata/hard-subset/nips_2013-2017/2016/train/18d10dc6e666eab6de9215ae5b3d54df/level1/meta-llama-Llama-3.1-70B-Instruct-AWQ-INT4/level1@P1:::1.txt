**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG). The q-KG method is designed to optimize the evaluation of multiple points simultaneously in a parallel computing environment. The authors derive the q-KG method using a decision-theoretic analysis, which chooses the set of points to evaluate next that is optimal in the average-case with respect to the posterior when there is only one batch of points remaining. The authors also provide an efficient strategy for computing the q-KG method using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and demonstrate its performance on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning algorithms.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The q-KG method is related to previous work on Bayesian optimization, particularly the knowledge gradient method for sequential Bayesian optimization. The authors extend this method to the parallel setting, where multiple points are evaluated simultaneously. The q-KG method is also related to other parallel Bayesian optimization methods, such as parallel expected improvement (EI) and parallel upper confidence bound (UCB). However, the q-KG method is distinct in that it considers the batch of points collectively and chooses the batch to jointly optimize the acquisition function.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Novel contribution**: The q-KG method is a novel contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization, and the authors provide a thorough derivation and analysis of the method.
2. **Efficient computation**: The authors provide an efficient strategy for computing the q-KG method using IPA, which makes the method practical for large-scale optimization problems.
3. **Strong empirical performance**: The authors demonstrate the strong empirical performance of the q-KG method on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning algorithms, particularly in the presence of noisy function evaluations.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited theoretical analysis**: While the authors provide a thorough derivation of the q-KG method, they do not provide a detailed theoretical analysis of the method's convergence properties or regret bounds.
2. **Computational complexity**: While the authors provide an efficient strategy for computing the q-KG method, the method still requires significant computational resources, particularly for large-scale optimization problems.
3. **Limited comparison to other methods**: While the authors compare the q-KG method to other parallel Bayesian optimization methods, they do not provide a comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art methods.
