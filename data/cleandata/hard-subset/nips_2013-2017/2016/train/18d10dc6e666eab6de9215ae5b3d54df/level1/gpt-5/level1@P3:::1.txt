Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization (BO) acquisition function that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to select q points jointly. q-KG is defined as the expected reduction in the minimum of the posterior mean after a batch of evaluations, i.e., the one-step Bayes-optimal policy for minimizing the posterior mean’s minimum when only one batch remains. The authors derive a Monte Carlo estimator of q-KG and an efficient unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization over the q-point batch. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner minimization over a set built from samples of posterior optima, previously sampled points, and the candidate batch. They show that in the noiseless case with “recommend-from-sampled,” q-KG reduces to parallel EI. Experiments on standard synthetic benchmarks and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression and CNNs (MNIST/CIFAR10) show that q-KG outperforms or matches parallel EI variants and GP-UCB-based batch methods, with larger gains under noise.

The work fits squarely within the BO literature at NIPS/ICML/AIStats: it extends sequential KG [Frazier et al. 2009] to batch; relates to parallel EI (constant liar, fantasization and exact q-EI) [Ginsbourger et al. 2010; Chevalier & Ginsbourger 2013; Wang et al. 2016], parallel UCB [Desautels et al. 2014; Contal et al. 2013], and information-theoretic methods such as PES/PPES [Hernández-Lobato et al. 2014; Shah & Ghahramani 2015]. Compared with prior batch methods that use greedy construction or myopic metrics (EI, UCB), q-KG jointly optimizes the batch under a decision-theoretic criterion and handles noise naturally.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound formulation: q-KG is a principled one-step Bayes-optimal criterion (for fixed GP hyperparameters) and the stated reduction to q-EI under specific constraints is correct.
- Computational contribution: The IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient for q-KG is nontrivial and practically important, enabling scalable joint optimization over z(1:q).
- Empirics: Consistent improvements over strong baselines on several synthetic test functions and realistic hyperparameter tuning tasks; the noisy setting results are particularly compelling.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Discretization of the inner minimization: The Bayes-optimality statement holds on the continuous domain, but the implementation relies on a discretized An that depends on z(1:q). This raises two issues: (i) the gradient estimator’s unbiasedness when An changes with z during optimization; and (ii) sensitivity of performance to M and to the sampling scheme for AMn. Empirical sensitivity analyses (M, sampling strategy) and a discussion of the bias induced by a z-dependent discretization would strengthen the paper.
- Hyperparameter uncertainty: q-KG is derived under fixed GP hyperparameters, yet the implementation re-estimates hyperparameters each iteration via MLE. As in prior work, this can degrade decision-theoretic optimality. A discussion of integrating out hyperparameters (e.g., via marginalization or sampling) and its computational trade-offs would be valuable.
- Computational complexity and wall-clock: The paper lacks a clear complexity/timing analysis versus q, dimension d, grid size |An|, and Monte Carlo sample size. Since the method is motivated by parallel evaluation, wall-clock comparisons and scaling with q (beyond q=4) are important to assess practical usefulness.
- Comparisons: The set of baselines is solid, but comparisons to PPES [Shah & Ghahramani 2015] and to batch local penalization [González et al., AISTATS 2016] would better position the contribution among batch methods that jointly evaluate batches. Also, an ablation showing the effect of noise on q-EI vs q-KG would isolate the mechanism behind the reported gains.
- Statistical rigor: Reported averages and standard deviations are useful, but formal statistical tests (e.g., confidence intervals on final regret) and clearer stopping criteria/budgets for all methods would increase confidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The notation around A vs An is potentially confusing (the authors note this), and Section 5 would benefit from explicit pseudocode for the stochastic gradient ascent, including Monte Carlo sample sizes, step sizes, restarts, and stopping conditions. Some minor typos (e.g., “Main Stange”) can be fixed.

Originality
Extending KG to the batch setting with a tractable IPA-based gradient estimator is novel and distinct from previously proposed batch EI/UCB/entropy-based approaches. The reduction to q-EI in the noiseless restricted setting nicely situates the contribution. The discretization strategy is adapted from ES/PES, but the combination with q-KG and IPA is new.

Significance
Batch BO is important in practice. A method that is principled, handles noise naturally, and empirically improves over widely used baselines is valuable. If computational overhead is moderate relative to training time of models being tuned, q-KG is likely to see adoption. The significance would be further enhanced by stronger scaling evidence and broader baselines.

Specific suggestions/questions
- Provide a complexity analysis and wall-clock timing versus q and d; include experiments with larger q (e.g., 8–16).
- Detail the IPA assumptions (uniqueness of minimizers on An, differentiability) and discuss robustness when ties occur; consider subgradient handling.
- Clarify whether AMn is fixed during the inner optimization over z(1:q). If AMn changes with z, explain how gradients are computed consistently.
- Study sensitivity to M, the number of Monte Carlo samples for Zq, and the number of gradient steps/restarts.
- Compare against PPES and local penalization; include a Thompson sampling–based batch method if feasible.
- Consider marginalizing GP hyperparameters, or at least show an experiment comparing MLE vs marginalization on a smaller problem.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled extension of KG to batch with an efficient stochastic gradient estimator.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise, on both synthetic and real tasks.
- Clear positioning within BO literature; code available.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient analysis of computational cost and scaling with q; limited wall-clock evidence.
- Dependence on discretization with limited justification/sensitivity analysis; potential issues with gradient unbiasedness when An varies with z.
- Missing comparisons to some contemporary batch BO methods and limited statistical testing.
