

# Peer Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), designed for scenarios where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors generalize the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function from sequential to batch settings, providing a theoretically grounded approach that jointly optimizes a batch of q points rather than sequentially adding points. The q-KG acquisition function measures the expected incremental value of evaluating a batch of points, with the goal of minimizing the expected minimum of the predictor. The paper develops an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute gradients of q-KG, enabling its optimization. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic test functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, showing consistent improvements over existing batch BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

This work builds upon the knowledge gradient framework for sequential BO [7, 17] and extends related batch optimization approaches [2, 3, 5, 10, 18, 25, 26]. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from previous batch methods by emphasizing that q-KG jointly optimizes the batch rather than using iterative greedy strategies, and they provide a more efficient computational method compared to [26].

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation. The derivation of q-KG as a Bayes-optimal acquisition function for batch settings is well-motivated and correctly justified. The IPA-based computational method for optimizing q-KG is novel and well-executed, addressing a significant computational challenge in batch BO. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including both synthetic functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6) and practical applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10), with appropriate statistical analysis (mean and standard deviation across multiple runs). The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly regarding computational cost with larger batch sizes, and carefully evaluates performance in both noise-free and noisy settings.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear and accessible, with appropriate notation and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and complete. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, including implementation details and hyperparameter choices. The results are presented effectively through well-designed figures and tables that clearly illustrate the performance advantages of q-KG.

## Originality

The key contribution is the generalization of knowledge gradient to the batch setting with joint optimization, which represents a meaningful advance over existing iterative batch methods. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work, particularly in comparison to [26] (which focuses on discrete sets with less efficient computation) and iterative batch methods like parallel EI [10, 24]. The computational technique using IPA for gradient estimation in the batch setting is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature.

## Significance

The paper addresses a highly relevant problem in modern machine learning: parallel hyperparameter tuning for expensive-to-evaluate models. The demonstrated improvements over existing methods, especially in noisy settings (where many real-world applications operate), are significant. The practical applications to logistic regression and CNN hyperparameter tuning provide strong evidence of the method's utility. The availability of code further enhances the paper's impact. The method could substantially accelerate hyperparameter tuning in parallel computing environments, which is increasingly common in machine learning practice.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Solid theoretical foundation for batch BO with a decision-theoretic justification
- Novel computational approach using IPA for efficient optimization of q-KG
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple synthetic functions and practical ML applications
- Clear demonstration of superiority over benchmarks, especially in noisy settings
- Well-written, clear organization, and excellent presentation of results

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of computational complexity scaling with batch size q (e.g., how does the IPA method scale with q?)
- No explicit comparison of running times between q-KG and benchmark methods, which is important for practical adoption
- Discretization strategy in Section 5.3 could be better justified (e.g., how does M=1000 affect accuracy?)
- No discussion of how the method handles constraints, which are common in hyperparameter tuning
- The paper does not address more recent batch BO methods published after 2016 (e.g., [27-30])

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to batch Bayesian optimization. The theoretical foundation is solid, the computational approach is novel and well-executed, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over existing approaches, particularly in noisy settings. The paper is clearly written and addresses an important practical problem in machine learning.

The weaknesses identified are not severe enough to undermine the paper's contributions, though addressing the computational complexity question and adding a brief discussion of constraints would further strengthen the paper. Given the significance of the problem and the quality of the work, I recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical contribution to batch Bayesian optimization
- Efficient computational method addressing a key challenge
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple domains
- Clear significance for practical machine learning applications
- Well-written and accessible presentation

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of computational scaling with batch size
- No explicit time complexity analysis
- Constraints handling not addressed
- Doesn't reference more recent related work

Overall, this paper represents an important advance in batch Bayesian optimization with clear theoretical and practical contributions. The method provides a meaningful improvement over existing approaches, particularly in the increasingly common setting of noisy evaluations, and the code availability enhances its reproducibility and potential impact.
