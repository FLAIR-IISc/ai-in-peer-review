This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), aiming to select a batch of points for simultaneous evaluation in a principled, decision-theoretic manner. The core idea is to maximize the expected incremental value of a single batch of evaluations, extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient to the parallel setting. The authors also provide an efficient gradient-based approach to optimize this acquisition function, leveraging infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The work is well-motivated, addressing the increasingly common scenario of parallel function evaluations, and is clearly presented.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the foundation of Bayesian Optimization, particularly the Knowledge Gradient (KG) method.  While sequential KG seeks the single best point to evaluate, q-KG extends this to a batch, explicitly considering the joint benefit of evaluating multiple points simultaneously. This contrasts with earlier parallel BO approaches like parallel Expected Improvement (EI) or Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) which often rely on iterative or greedy selection of points, optimizing single-point acquisition functions sequentially. The authors correctly position their work relative to these existing methods, highlighting the advantages of a holistic batch optimization approach.  The connection to [25] is particularly well noted, as that work also explores gradient-based optimization of parallel acquisition functions, but utilizes a different (and less efficient, according to the authors) approach.  The paper also acknowledges the related work of [26] on nested batch knowledge gradients, but correctly points out the differences in computational efficiency and problem setting.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The method is grounded in solid decision-theoretic principles, deriving the acquisition function from a Bayes-optimal perspective. The justification for maximizing the expected incremental value of a single remaining batch is clear and compelling.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The use of IPA to compute gradients of the q-KG acquisition function is a significant contribution. Efficient gradient computation is crucial for scaling to higher dimensions and larger batch sizes.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over existing parallel BO algorithms, particularly in noisy evaluation settings. The tests on both synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems strengthen the validity of the findings. The comparison to multiple baseline algorithms (parallel EI, UCB variants) is thorough.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is reasonably clear, and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner. The connection between the q-KG and the parallel EI acquisition function is clearly explained.
* **Code Availability:** Providing the code fosters reproducibility and facilitates further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization of A:** The discretization approach for continuous search spaces, relying on sampling from the posterior distribution, could be a bottleneck, especially in high-dimensional problems. The choice of M=1000 is somewhat arbitrary and may not scale well. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the impact of this discretization on performance and potential alternative approaches (e.g., quasi-Monte Carlo sampling).
* **Scalability of Gradient Computation:** While IPA improves gradient computation, the complexity likely still grows with the batch size (q) and the number of candidate points used in the discretization. Further analysis of the computational cost and potential bottlenecks would be valuable.
* **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t extensively investigate the sensitivity of q-KG’s performance to its own hyperparameters (e.g., the parameters of the GP kernel).
* **Lack of Theoretical Regret Bounds:**  While the method is motivated by a decision-theoretic optimality criterion, the paper doesn’t provide any theoretical regret bounds to quantify its performance guarantees.
* **Minor: Redundancy in Explanation:** The explanations surrounding the relation between q-KG and parallel EI are slightly repetitive across sections 4 and 2.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The method is theoretically sound and offers a principled approach to parallel Bayesian optimization.
* The computational efficiency achieved through IPA is a significant practical contribution.
* The empirical results demonstrate clear performance gains over existing methods.
* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in the field of Bayesian optimization.
* The code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The discretization approach for continuous search spaces may limit scalability.
* The lack of theoretical regret bounds is a weakness.
* Limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The q-KG method is well-motivated, computationally efficient, and empirically effective.  While there are some minor weaknesses related to scalability and theoretical analysis, the strengths of the work clearly outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should expand the discussion on the discretization strategy, explore its computational complexity more thoroughly, and consider including a brief discussion of potential future work on regret bounds.
