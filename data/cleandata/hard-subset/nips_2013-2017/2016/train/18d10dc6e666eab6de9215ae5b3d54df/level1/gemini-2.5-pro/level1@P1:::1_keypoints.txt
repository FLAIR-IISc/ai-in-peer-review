- Introduces q-KG: a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition that is one-step Bayes-optimal for selecting q parallel points by maximizing expected improvement in the final posterior-mean minimizer.
- Main technical challenge is optimizing the complex q-KG acquisition function, which is computationally intensive.
- Authors derive an unbiased stochastic gradient via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to enable gradient-based optimization of q-KG.
- q-KG generalizes sequential KG to the batch setting and is contrasted with parallel EI and UCB methods.
- Empirical results on synthetic benchmarks and real-world hyperparameter tuning (Logistic Regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR-10) show q-KG is competitive or superior, especially under noisy evaluations.
- Strength: principled decision-theoretic formulation that outperforms common heuristics (e.g., greedy or constant-lie EI) in rationale and performance.
- Strength: novel, practical computational method (IPA-based unbiased gradients) that makes q-KG feasible.
- Strength: comprehensive experiments covering noise-free and noisy settings, demonstrating strong gains in realistic noisy scenarios.
- Strength: paper is clearly written and well organized.
- Weakness: likely significant computational overhead due to Monte Carlo sampling and stochastic gradient ascent at each BO iteration, with no wall-clock/time comparisons to baselines.
- Weakness: method depends on discretizing domain A (parameter M), but sensitivity to M and sufficiency of M=1000 are not explored.
- Weakness: ambiguity in definition of An = AMn ∪ x(1:n) ∪ z(1:q) — unclear whether An changes during optimization of z and how that affects gradient validity.
- Question: provide computational-complexity and wall-clock time comparisons versus baselines to clarify practical trade-offs.
- Question: analyze sensitivity to discretization size M and behavior in higher-dimensional problems.
- Question: clarify whether An is fixed during inner-loop optimization of z(1:q) and how z is incorporated without invalidating gradients.
- Question: provide hyperparameter search space details (ranges/scales) for the CIFAR-10 CNN experiment for reproducibility.
- Summary pro-accept: novel, principled, and practically effective method with significant empirical gains, notably in noisy settings.
- Summary con-accept: main concern is missing discussion of acquisition optimization cost and minor discretization-clarity issues.
- Final assessment: high-quality paper with important contribution and recommendation for acceptance.
