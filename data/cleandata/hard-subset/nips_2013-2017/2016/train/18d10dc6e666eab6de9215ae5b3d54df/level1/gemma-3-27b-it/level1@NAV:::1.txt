## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method, a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm. The core idea is to extend the knowledge gradient approach – which selects the Bayes-optimal point in the sequential setting – to the parallel case, aiming for a Bayes-optimal *batch* of points. The authors address the computational challenges of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function through an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy utilizing infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method is empirically evaluated on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning tasks for logistic regression and CNNs, demonstrating improved performance, especially in noisy evaluation settings, compared to several baseline algorithms.

This work builds upon a significant body of research in Bayesian Optimization (BO), particularly extending recent efforts in parallel BO [3, 5, 18, 25]. The paper clearly positions itself within this landscape, accurately referencing relevant literature and highlighting the limitations of existing iterative, single-evaluation-based approaches. The connection to the sequential Knowledge Gradient [7, 17] is also well articulated. The proposed q-KG method stands out by considering the batch collectively rather than greedily constructing it, aligning with more recent approaches like [2, 15]. 

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of q-KG from a decision-theoretic perspective is well-motivated and grounded in the principles of optimal experimentation.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The IPA-based gradient estimation offers a practical solution to the computational burden of maximizing the q-KG function, making the algorithm scalable.
* **Empirical Performance:** The results consistently demonstrate q-KG’s superior performance compared to existing methods, particularly in noisy settings, which is a crucial advantage for many real-world applications.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the method and its implementation details.
* **Code Availability:**  Providing code fosters reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization of A:** While the method for approximating q-KG with a discrete A is described, the choice of M = 1000 feels somewhat arbitrary. More discussion on sensitivity to this parameter and potential adaptive strategies would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of other hyperparameters beyond M isn’t extensively analyzed. A sensitivity analysis might reveal potential robustness issues.
* **Limited Scope of Benchmarks:** While the chosen benchmarks are standard, expanding the evaluation to a broader range of problems, especially those with different characteristics (e.g., high dimensionality, multi-modality), would strengthen the conclusions.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to parallel Bayesian optimization.
* The proposed method demonstrably outperforms existing techniques on a variety of problems.
* The computational efficiency is addressed effectively, making the algorithm practical.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The discretization strategy could benefit from further justification and analysis.
* The lack of a comprehensive hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is a minor concern.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The q-KG method represents a significant advance in parallel BO and has the potential to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners. Despite minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work clearly outweigh them.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points above.
