

# Peer Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

This paper proposes a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously in a decision-theoretically optimal manner. The authors extend the knowledge gradient (KG) method from sequential to parallel settings, providing a Bayes-optimal acquisition function for batch selection. They develop an efficient computational approach using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to optimize this acquisition function, addressing the computational challenges of the parallel setting. The paper demonstrates the method's superiority over existing batch BO approaches on synthetic functions and practical machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy settings.

The work builds upon the knowledge gradient framework introduced by Frazier et al. (INFORMS Journal on Computing, 2009) and extends it to the parallel setting, differentiating from previous work on parallel Bayesian optimization (e.g., parallel EI [10, 19, 25], batch UCB [5], and parallel UCB with exploration [3]). While Shah and Ghahramani (NIPS 2015) also proposed a batch acquisition function (Parallel Predictive Entropy Search), their approach differs fundamentally from the decision-theoretic q-KG framework. The authors correctly acknowledge related work but could better distinguish their approach from the more recent work by Marmin et al. (2016) on differentiating multipoint expected improvement.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for the q-KG acquisition function as the Bayes-optimal batch selection criterion for the one-iteration remaining problem. The extension from sequential to parallel KG is logically sound and well-motivated.

2. **Computational Innovation:** The IPA-based approach to efficiently estimate the gradient of q-KG is a significant technical contribution that addresses the main computational bottleneck of the parallel setting. This enables practical implementation of the theoretically optimal acquisition function.

3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering both synthetic test functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10). The results consistently show q-KG outperforming benchmarks, with particularly strong advantages in noisy settings.

4. **Practical Relevance:** The paper demonstrates clear value for real-world applications where parallel function evaluations are common (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines), making it highly relevant to the NIPS community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Limitations:** The claim that q-KG is "Bayes-optimal" is qualified as being for the one-iteration remaining problem. The paper lacks a formal proof of this optimality and doesn't sufficiently discuss the limitations of this optimality guarantee beyond the single-iteration context.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the IPA approach, particularly how it scales with batch size q and dimensionality d. This is crucial for understanding the practical limitations of the method.

3. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper doesn't adequately compare with the most recent batch BO methods, particularly the work by Shah and Ghahramani (NIPS 2015) and Marmin et al. (2016), which also address batch selection. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution claim.

4. **Discretization Justification:** The discretization approach in Section 5.3 (using M=1000 samples from the posterior) lacks sufficient justification. The authors should discuss how this discretization affects the quality of the solution and provide sensitivity analysis for different M values.

5. **Computational Cost:** While the paper demonstrates superior performance, it doesn't clearly quantify the computational cost of q-KG compared to alternatives. Given that batch BO is often used when function evaluations are expensive, the computational overhead of the acquisition function optimization is an important consideration.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to batch Bayesian optimization with a theoretically sound and practically effective method. The q-KG approach addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by providing a decision-theoretically optimal batch selection method that outperforms existing approaches, particularly in noisy settings. The IPA-based computational technique is a significant innovation that enables practical implementation.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear technical contributions and comprehensive experimental validation. The main weakness is the insufficient comparison with the most recent batch BO methods and the lack of detailed computational complexity analysis. However, these do not undermine the paper's core contribution.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to Bayesian optimization with a novel, theoretically grounded, and practically effective batch method. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. The minor weaknesses identified do not significantly detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution to the field. The work is highly relevant to the NIPS community, especially given the growing interest in Bayesian optimization for machine learning hyperparameter tuning. The availability of code further enhances the paper's impact.
