

The paper "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization" proposes a novel approach to batch Bayesian optimization (BO) that extends the knowledge gradient (KG) acquisition function to the parallel setting. The authors develop a decision-theoretic acquisition function, q-KG, which chooses a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously by maximizing the expected incremental value of the solution quality. Unlike previous batch BO methods that use iterative greedy approaches, q-KG optimizes the batch collectively rather than sequentially. The authors provide an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to compute the gradient of q-KG, enabling its optimization. Extensive experiments on synthetic functions and practical machine learning applications demonstrate that q-KG consistently outperforms existing batch BO methods, particularly in noisy settings.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a strong theoretical foundation. The derivation of q-KG as the Bayes-optimal batch acquisition function for the parallel setting is rigorous and well-justified. The authors provide a clear explanation of the computational approach using IPA, which is theoretically sound and efficiently implemented. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including multiple synthetic test functions (Branin2, Rosenbrock3, Ackley5, Hartmann6), noise-free and noisy settings, and practical applications (tuning logistic regression on MNIST and CNNs on CIFAR10). The paper carefully compares with relevant baselines (parallel EI, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) and provides statistical analysis (mean and standard deviation over multiple runs), demonstrating that q-KG consistently achieves better results, especially in noisy environments.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and motivation for parallel BO. The background on Gaussian processes and Bayesian optimization is concise and accessible. The mathematical presentation of q-KG and its computational approach is precise and well-structured. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is complete and would enable reproduction of the results. The experimental section is thorough, with appropriate figures (Figures 3 and 4) and clear statistical analysis. The paper effectively distinguishes their approach from related work, particularly noting that [26] (nested batch knowledge gradient) focuses on discrete feasible sets and is less efficient than their method.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by extending sequential KG to the parallel setting in a principled way. Previous batch BO methods (e.g., [2, 3, 5, 18, 25]) typically use iterative greedy approaches that optimize single-point acquisition functions sequentially. The q-KG approach is fundamentally different, as it optimizes the batch collectively. The authors clearly articulate how their method differs from previous work, especially in their comparison with [25] and [26]. The computational technique using IPA for gradient estimation is novel in the context of batch BO and addresses a key limitation of previous approaches that required expensive Monte Carlo sampling.

**Significance:** Batch Bayesian optimization is highly relevant to real-world applications where parallel evaluations are common (e.g., hyperparameter tuning across multiple machines). The paper demonstrates significant practical improvements over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings where the performance gains are more pronounced. The application to tuning machine learning algorithms (logistic regression, CNNs) shows clear relevance to current machine learning practice. The authors provide compelling evidence that q-KG can substantially reduce the number of evaluations needed to find good hyperparameters, which is valuable given the computational cost of training ML models. The code availability (https://github.com/wujian16/qKG) further enhances the significance and reproducibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:** The computational complexity of q-KG increases with batch size q, and while the authors provide an efficient gradient-based approach, the paper doesn't thoroughly address how the method scales to very large batch sizes (e.g., q > 20). The description of the dynamic discretization strategy (Section 5.3) could be slightly more detailed, particularly regarding how the discrete set An is constructed from the posterior distribution. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates improvements over existing methods, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of scenarios where q-KG might not be the optimal choice (e.g., for extremely high-dimensional problems).

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization with a novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective approach to batch BO. The quality of the work is high, the presentation is clear, the originality is significant, and the significance of the results is well-demonstrated for both academic and practical applications. The minor weaknesses noted above don't detract from the overall strength of the paper.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical foundation, clear and well-executed experiments, significant practical improvements over state-of-the-art, relevant to current machine learning practice, and the authors provide code for reproducibility.

Con arguments against acceptance: Limited analysis of scalability to very large batch sizes (q > 20), and the discretization strategy could be slightly more detailed.

Overall, this paper meets all NIPS criteria and represents an important contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2016.
