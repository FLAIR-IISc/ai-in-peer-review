

# Review: The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization method called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), which generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method to the parallel setting. The authors derive a decision-theoretic acquisition function that selects a batch of q points to evaluate simultaneously by maximizing the expected incremental value of the batch, providing a one-step Bayes-optimal batch under the assumption of only one remaining batch of evaluations. The paper develops an efficient computational strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to estimate the gradient of the q-KG acquisition function, enabling its optimization. The authors compare their method against several parallel Bayesian optimization approaches including parallel EI, batch UCB, and parallel UCB with exploration on synthetic functions and machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature on Bayesian optimization. It builds on the knowledge gradient work of Frazier et al. (2009) and extends the parallel BO work of Wang et al. (2016), Contal et al. (2014), and Desautels et al. (2014). The authors clearly distinguish their approach from prior work by noting that previous methods construct batches iteratively (maximizing single-point acquisition functions while holding other points fixed), whereas their method considers the batch collectively.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical foundation of the method is sound and well-motivated. The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function as the one-step Bayes optimal batch is clear and technically rigorous.
2. The computational approach using IPA to efficiently estimate the gradient of q-KG is novel and well-executed, addressing a significant practical challenge in parallel BO.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic functions (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and practical machine learning applications (logistic regression on MNIST, CNN on CIFAR10).
4. The results demonstrate consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods, with particularly notable gains in noisy settings (where q-KG outperforms other methods by a larger margin).
5. The paper is well-written with clear pseudocode, appropriate mathematical notation, and sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. While the paper demonstrates superior performance in terms of function evaluations, it does not provide detailed comparison of computational runtime between q-KG and baselines, which is important for practical adoption.
2. The paper could better explain why q-KG particularly excels in noisy settings, beyond simply reporting the empirical results. A more detailed theoretical justification would strengthen the contribution.
3. The discussion of related work could more thoroughly contextualize the relationship between the proposed method and recent parallel BO approaches, particularly the nested batch knowledge gradient in Wang et al. (2016).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a technically sound and novel extension of Bayesian optimization to the parallel setting with a decision-theoretic foundation.
- The computational approach to efficiently optimize the acquisition function is a significant contribution.
- The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, especially in practical noisy settings.
- The method has direct applicability to hyperparameter tuning in machine learning, a major application area for Bayesian optimization.
- The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and includes open-source code.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The computational complexity comparison with baselines is limited to function evaluations rather than runtime, which is a practical concern.
- The theoretical explanation for the method's superior performance in noisy settings is not sufficiently developed.
- While the paper cites relevant work, it could more explicitly connect to recent NIPS papers on parallel BO (e.g., Shah and Ghahramani, 2015; Marmin et al., 2015).

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient method for batch optimization. The technical soundness is excellent, the novelty is clear, and the significance is demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world problems. The paper addresses an important practical challenge in Bayesian optimization where parallel evaluations are possible, with clear advantages in noisy settings that are particularly relevant to real-world machine learning applications.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, novel in its approach to batch Bayesian optimization, and significant for its practical impact on hyperparameter tuning in machine learning. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the substantial contribution of the work.

I recommend acceptance.
