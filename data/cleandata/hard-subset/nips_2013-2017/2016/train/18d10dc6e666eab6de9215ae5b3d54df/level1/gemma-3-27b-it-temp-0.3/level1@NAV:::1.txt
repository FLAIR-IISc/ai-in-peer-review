## Review of "The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization"

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO), extending the sequential Knowledge Gradient approach to parallel evaluations. The core idea is to select a batch of points that maximizes the expected incremental value of information gain, effectively performing a one-step Bayes optimal batch selection. The authors address the computational challenges of maximizing this acquisition function by proposing an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). The method is evaluated on synthetic test functions and hyperparameter tuning of logistic regression and CNNs, demonstrating superior performance, particularly in noisy evaluation settings, compared to existing parallel BO algorithms like parallel EI and UCB.

This work builds upon a rich history of BO research, particularly the sequential Knowledge Gradient (KG) method [Frazier et al., 2008] and recent efforts to extend BO to parallel settings [Chevalier et al., 2014, Contal et al., 2014]. The q-KG method distinguishes itself by explicitly considering the batch of points collectively, rather than iteratively adding points based on single-point acquisition functions. The use of IPA for efficient gradient computation is a significant contribution, enabling scalability to larger batch sizes. The paper clearly positions itself within the existing literature and provides adequate references.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is grounded in a solid decision-theoretic framework, aiming for Bayes optimality.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The IPA-based gradient estimation is a clever solution to the computational complexity of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The results demonstrate consistent improvements over existing parallel BO algorithms, especially in noisy environments.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the method relatively easy to understand.
*   **Code Availability:** Providing the code is a significant plus for reproducibility and adoption.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Discretization of A:** The discretization approach for continuous domains, while practical, introduces an approximation that could impact performance. The sensitivity to the number of samples (M) used for discretization isn’t thoroughly explored.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method might be sensitive to the choice of GP hyperparameters and the optimization algorithm used to maximize the q-KG function. This isn’t extensively discussed.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are well-executed, the range of practical problems is somewhat limited. Expanding to more complex and diverse applications would strengthen the claims.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization.
*   The proposed method demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing algorithms.
*   The computational challenges are addressed effectively with a practical gradient estimation technique.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The discretization of the search space introduces an approximation that could limit performance.
*   The sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters isn’t fully investigated.



**Overall:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The q-KG method represents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization, offering a theoretically grounded and empirically effective approach to parallel batch optimization. While some limitations exist, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper is likely to be of interest to a broad audience within the NIPS community.
