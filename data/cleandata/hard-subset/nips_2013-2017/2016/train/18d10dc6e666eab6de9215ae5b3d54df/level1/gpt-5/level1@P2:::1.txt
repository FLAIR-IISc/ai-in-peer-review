Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization under Gaussian process (GP) priors with homoscedastic or known heteroscedastic Gaussian noise. q-KG is defined as the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after one additional batch of q evaluations, making it Bayes-optimal for one remaining batch in the average-case sense when the final recommendation is unrestricted (standard KG assumption). The authors provide a reparameterization of the posterior update via a standard normal vector Zq and derive an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator of q-KG using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling joint optimization of the entire batch by stochastic gradient ascent. Because exact computation is intractable for continuous domains, the acquisition value is approximated by discretizing the candidate set using samples from regions of high posterior optimality probability plus previously sampled and candidate points. Experiments on standard test functions and hyperparameter tuning for MNIST logistic regression and CIFAR-10 CNNs demonstrate consistent improvements over parallel EI (closed-form and heuristic variants) and UCB-based batch policies, especially under noisy evaluations.

This work connects to and extends: (i) sequential KG (Frazier et al.) by moving to the batch setting; (ii) multi-point EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Wang et al.) by proposing a different, decision-theoretically justified objective; and (iii) batch entropy-based methods (Hernández-Lobato et al.; Shah & Ghahramani) by offering a computationally competitive alternative with an efficient gradient estimator. It also complements BUCB and GP-UCB-PE, which target cumulative rather than simple regret.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The decision-theoretic derivation of q-KG is standard and correct, and the reparameterization using Zq is appropriate. The IPA-based gradient estimator is plausible and well-motivated; the assumptions (continuous differentiability of mean and kernel, finite discretization) are reasonable. The discrete-approximation of A is clearly stated; the method is Bayes-optimal only with respect to the discretized set, which is an important limitation but common in KG/EI practice.
- Computational aspects: The paper outlines an implementable pipeline: sampling Zq, evaluating the min over a finite An, and computing gradients via backprop through the Cholesky factor. Using multi-start stochastic gradient ascent is appropriate. However, the paper would benefit from explicit complexity analysis (dependence on n, q, |An|, and number of Monte Carlo samples) and wall-clock comparisons to baselines, as the nested Monte Carlo plus inner minimization can be costly for larger q or |An|.
- Empirics: The benchmarks are sensible and include both clean/noisy settings. The gains under noise are compelling and align with KG’s information-focused nature. That said, most tests use q=4; it would strengthen the case to show scaling with q (e.g., q=8, 16) and to include runtime. Sensitivity to M (discretization size), Monte Carlo sample counts, and kernel/hyperparameter learning would also be informative. Comparison to batch entropy search (PES/PPES) is mentioned but not shown; including it would position the contribution more clearly versus other non-myopic batch methods.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The distinction between the two uses of A (domain of z(1:q) vs. discretized set for evaluating the inner min) is acknowledged but still potentially confusing; consistently using different symbols (e.g., A for domain, An for discretization) earlier would help. More details in the main text on the unbiasedness conditions for the IPA estimator and a brief complexity discussion would improve readability. Minor typographical issues (e.g., spacing, superscripts) can be corrected easily.

Originality:
Extending KG to the batch setting with a tractable, unbiased stochastic gradient estimator is a novel and meaningful contribution. While batch selection via EI or entropy is known, a principled batch KG with continuous domains and differentiable optimization was not previously available in this form. The connection showing q-KG reduces to parallel EI under noise-free evaluations and restricted final recommendations is insightful.

Significance:
Batch BO is practically important, and the method addresses a real need: choosing batches jointly with a decision-theoretic objective and handling noise effectively. The empirical results suggest nontrivial improvements in noisy environments common in ML tuning, and releasing code increases impact. With broader comparisons and runtime analysis, this could become a go-to batch method in practice.

Pros:
- Principled, decision-theoretic batch acquisition that is Bayes-optimal for one remaining batch.
- Efficient, unbiased stochastic gradient via IPA enabling joint batch optimization.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise; code available.
- Clear linkage to prior work and reduction to q-EI in the noiseless, restricted-recommendation setting.

Cons:
- Inner optimization approximates continuous A via discretization without error analysis; Bayes-optimality holds only for An.
- Missing runtime/complexity analysis and scalability results for larger q and |An|.
- Limited ablations (sensitivity to M, Monte Carlo samples, hyperparameter learning).
- Missing comparisons to batch entropy search methods in experiments.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add wall-clock and asymptotic complexity analysis versus q-EI and BUCB/GP-UCB-PE; report scalability with q and n.
- Include PPES (or related batch entropy) baselines and/or Thompson sampling with fantasizing for completeness.
- Provide sensitivity studies for M and the number of Monte Carlo samples; show performance versus different discretization schemes.
- Report results for larger batch sizes and higher-dimensional problems.
- Clarify the conditions for IPA unbiasedness and the effect of re-estimating GP hyperparameters each iteration on theoretical guarantees.

Overall assessment and recommendation:
This is a solid and timely contribution that advances batch Bayesian optimization with a principled and implementable acquisition function, and demonstrates clear empirical benefits in noisy settings. Despite missing runtime analyses and some experimental baselines/ablations, the paper meets the NIPS criteria well.

Recommendation: Accept.
