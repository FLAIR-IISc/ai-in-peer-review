This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG). The method selects a batch of `q` points to evaluate simultaneously by maximizing a new acquisition function. This function is derived from a decision-theoretic perspective and represents the expected improvement in the final solution quality after observing the outcomes of the `q` parallel evaluations, where the final solution is taken to be the minimum of the posterior mean function. This formulation provides a one-step Bayes-optimal batch selection policy. The main technical challenge is the optimization of the q-KG acquisition function, which is computationally intensive. The authors address this by developing an efficient method based on infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to obtain unbiased stochastic gradients, enabling the use of gradient-based optimizers. This approach generalizes the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) to the batch setting and is contrasted with other parallel methods like those based on expected improvement (EI) or upper confidence bounds (UCB). The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning problems that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms state-of-the-art methods, showing particularly strong performance in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths:**
*   **Principled Formulation:** The q-KG acquisition function is well-grounded in decision theory, aiming to maximize the expected value of information for the final recommendation. This is a more principled approach than many common heuristics for parallelizing acquisition functions (e.g., greedy selection or "constant liar" heuristics for EI). The distinction from parallel EI, which is optimal only when the final recommendation is restricted to be one of the evaluated points, is important and well-argued.
*   **Novel Computational Method:** A primary contribution of the paper is a practical method for optimizing the complex q-KG function. The use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased estimator for the stochastic gradient is a significant technical contribution. This makes the theoretically appealing but computationally challenging q-KG policy feasible in practice.
*   **Strong and Comprehensive Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength. The authors compare q-KG against several relevant and state-of-the-art baselines across a variety of benchmarks. These include four standard synthetic functions and two real-world machine learning hyperparameter tuning tasks (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10). The inclusion of both noise-free and noisy settings is crucial, and the results convincingly show that q-KG offers substantial benefits, especially when evaluations are noisy. This setting is particularly relevant for hyperparameter tuning, where training processes are often stochastic. The reported improvement on the CNN tuning task is compelling.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. It logically progresses from the theoretical motivation of q-KG to the computational challenges and the proposed solution, and finally to the empirical validation.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Computational Overhead:** While the proposed optimization method is "efficient" relative to naive approaches, it is likely to be significantly more computationally expensive than the baselines. The procedure involves Monte Carlo sampling for both the acquisition function and its gradient at each step of a stochastic gradient ascent routine. This entire optimization must be performed at each iteration of the BO loop. The paper lacks a discussion or empirical results (e.g., wall-clock time) comparing the cost of optimizing q-KG to that of the baselines. This trade-off between acquisition cost and sample efficiency is a critical aspect for practitioners.
*   **Dependence on Discretization:** The method relies on discretizing the continuous domain `A` to compute the `min` over the posterior mean. The quality of this discretization, controlled by the parameter `M`, is critical to the performance of the algorithm, but this dependency is not explored. It would be valuable to understand how sensitive the results are to the choice of `M` and whether `M=1000` is sufficient for problems with higher dimensionality than those tested.
*   **Clarity on Discretization Scheme:** There is a potential ambiguity in the definition of the discrete set `An` in Section 5.3. It is defined as `An = AMn ∪ x(1:n) ∪ z(1:q)`. Since `z(1:q)` is the variable being optimized, this suggests the optimization landscape (the acquisition function) changes as the optimizer explores different values of `z`, which would complicate the gradient-based optimization. A clarification is needed on whether `An` is fixed before the optimization of `z` begins for a given BO iteration.

**Questions for Authors:**
*   Could you please provide a discussion on the computational complexity and wall-clock time of optimizing the q-KG acquisition function compared to the baselines used in your experiments? This would help readers better understand the practical trade-offs.
*   The performance of the method seems dependent on the discretization of the domain `A`. Could you comment on the sensitivity of q-KG to the size of the discrete set, `M`?
*   Regarding the definition `An = AMn ∪ x(1:n) ∪ z(1:q)` in Section 5.3: does the set `An` change during the inner loop of optimizing `z(1:q)`? If so, how does this affect the validity of the gradient computation? If not, please clarify how `z(1:q)` is incorporated into `An`.
*   Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter search space (ranges and scales) for the CNN experiment on CIFAR-10 to improve reproducibility?

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel, theoretically principled, and practically effective method for batch Bayesian optimization. The technical contribution for optimizing the acquisition function is significant, and the strong empirical results, especially in the important noisy setting, demonstrate a clear advance over the state of the art.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The primary concern is the lack of discussion on the computational cost, which may be a significant barrier to adoption. There are also minor clarity issues regarding the implementation of the discretization scheme that should be addressed.

**Final Assessment:**
This is a high-quality paper with a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization. The strengths—a principled new method, a novel computational technique to make it practical, and compelling empirical results—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are mainly related to a lack of discussion on computational cost and minor points of clarity. The paper addresses a significant and practical problem and is likely to be influential. I recommend acceptance.
