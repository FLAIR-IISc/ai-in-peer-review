## Review of “The Parallel Knowledge Gradient Method for Batch Bayesian Optimization”

This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization, aiming to select a batch of points that is Bayes-optimal in the average-case for a single remaining batch. The core idea builds on the Knowledge Gradient framework, extending it to parallel evaluation settings. The authors address the computational challenges of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function through infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) and provide empirical validation on synthetic functions and hyperparameter tuning problems.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a relevant problem in Bayesian Optimization – efficient parallel evaluation. The extension of the Knowledge Gradient to a batch setting is a natural and potentially powerful approach, especially given its theoretical optimality under specific conditions.  The comparison to existing parallel BO algorithms (parallel EI, batch UCB, etc.) provides a good benchmark, and the demonstration of improvements, particularly in noisy settings, is promising. This work connects to a significant body of research in BO, including the foundational work of Frazier et al. [17] on the sequential KG method and more recent advancements in parallel BO by authors like Ginsbourger et al. [10] and Wang et al. [25]. The authors clearly position their work within this landscape, citing relevant papers and highlighting their contributions.  The provision of code is also a strong positive.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The method is grounded in a solid decision-theoretic framework, aiming for a Bayes-optimal batch selection.
* **Novelty:** Extending the Knowledge Gradient to the parallel batch setting is a valuable contribution.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The IPA-based gradient estimation provides a practical way to address the computational complexity of maximizing q-KG.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over existing methods, particularly in noisy scenarios. The results on both synthetic and real-world (hyperparameter tuning) problems lend credibility.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The algorithm is clearly defined and the mathematical derivations are reasonably presented.
* **Code Availability:** Providing code allows for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization of A:** The method relies on discretizing the search space *A* to enable gradient-based optimization. The discussion of this discretization in Section 5.3 feels somewhat brief and lacks detailed justification for the chosen method (sampling from the posterior optima). The sensitivity of the results to the number of samples *M* is not thoroughly explored.
* **Computational Cost:** While IPA improves efficiency, the computational cost of evaluating q-KG and its gradient still appears significant, especially for high-dimensional problems or large batch sizes. A more detailed complexity analysis would be helpful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithm likely depends on several hyperparameters (e.g., kernel parameters, the number of samples for discretization). The paper doesn’t provide extensive analysis of how these parameters affect performance.
* **Limited Exploration of Noise Levels:** While the results show an advantage in noisy settings, the experiments primarily use a single noise level (σ = 0.5). It would be valuable to explore a wider range of noise levels to assess the robustness of the method.
* **Comparison to Nested Batch KG:** The paper mentions [26] but dismisses it due to computational inefficiency and a different application.  However, a fairer comparison might consider that the "finite feasible set" approach in [26] is motivated by a different problem setting and may not be directly comparable to the continuous optimization attempted here.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to batch Bayesian optimization.
* The experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings.
* The authors address the computational challenges of the method with a practical solution.
* The code availability enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on discretization of the search space introduces potential approximations and requires careful tuning.
* The computational complexity remains a concern for large-scale problems.
* The analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and robustness is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The proposed q-KG method is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While there are some weaknesses, particularly concerning the discretization strategy and computational complexity, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

*  Provide a more detailed justification for the discretization method and investigate the sensitivity of the results to the number of samples *M*.
*  Include a more detailed complexity analysis of the q-KG algorithm.
*  Conduct experiments with a wider range of noise levels.
*  Expand the discussion of hyperparameter tuning and provide guidance on selecting appropriate values for key parameters.
*  Acknowledge the potential benefits and limitations of approaches like [26] with a more nuanced comparison.
