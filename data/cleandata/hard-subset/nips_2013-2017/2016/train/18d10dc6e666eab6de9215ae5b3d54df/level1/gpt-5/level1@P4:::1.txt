Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), a batch acquisition function for Bayesian optimization (BO) that directly maximizes the expected one-step reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after evaluating a batch of q points. This generalizes the (sequential) knowledge gradient (KG) framework of Frazier et al. to the batch setting while allowing the final recommendation to be any point in the domain, not only among evaluated points (unlike EI). The authors show that in the noise-free case, and if the final recommendation is restricted to previously sampled points, q-KG reduces to parallel EI. They present a Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG and an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) applied to a reparameterization of the GP update via Cholesky factors. For continuous domains, they approximate the inner minimizations over A by a dynamically updated discrete set constructed from samples of posterior minimizers supplemented with past samples and current candidates. Empirically, they compare against batch EI variants, GP-BUCB and GP-UCB-PE, and parallel predictive entropy search (as cited), reporting improved simple regret, especially under observation noise, on standard test functions and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and a small CNN (CIFAR-10). Code is provided.

The work builds on: (i) KG for sequential BO (Frazier et al.; Scott et al. for correlated KG), (ii) parallel EI and its closed-form/gradient-based optimization (Chevalier & Ginsbourger; Marmin et al.; MOE), (iii) batch UCB methods (Desautels et al.; Contal et al.), and (iv) parallel entropy search (Shah & Ghahramani). A related nested batch KG for discrete domains (Wang et al.) is cited; this work targets continuous domains and uses IPA for efficient optimization.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic derivation: q-KG is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch with respect to minimizing the posterior mean minimum, mirroring the key property of sequential KG.
- Technically sound Monte Carlo formulation and pathwise (IPA) gradient estimator, enabling practical gradient-based optimization of a nontrivial batch acquisition function.
- Empirically strong performance, particularly with noisy observations, a regime where information-based methods often excel; comparisons span synthetic benchmarks and two real hyperparameter tuning tasks.
- Sensible connection to existing methods: reduction to parallel EI under noise-free/restricted recommendation clarifies relationships and expectations.
- Public implementation and reuse of established GP infrastructure, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Discretization of the inner minimization over A is central but under-analyzed. There is no quantitative study of approximation error, sensitivity to M, or convergence behavior as M increases.
- Computational aspects are insufficiently reported: number of Monte Carlo samples for q-KG and its gradient, wall-clock runtimes, scaling with q, d, and M, and variance of the stochastic gradients are not detailed. Fair runtime comparisons to baselines are missing.
- The IPA gradient through the min operator and Cholesky factorization is relegated to the supplement. Conditions ensuring unbiasedness (e.g., uniqueness of argmin with probability 1, differentiability) and practical handling of nondifferentiable points are not discussed in the main text.
- Experimental setup exhibits potential confounds: different libraries are used across methods (MOE vs. Spearmint vs. Gpoptimization), which may entail different GP priors/hyperparameter learning and optimization tolerances. Equating kernels, noise models, and training procedures across baselines is not fully documented.
- Real-task experiments use modest repetitions (5 runs for CIFAR-10), and do not report confidence intervals for final recommendations. There is no ablation on batch size q or noise misspecification, and no comparison to other competitive batch BO heuristics such as local penalization (González et al., 2016).

Quality
- The methodological core is sound: the q-KG objective is correctly defined; the reparameterization µ(n+q)(x) = µ(n)(x) + σ̃n(x, z(1:q))Z enables unbiased Monte Carlo estimation; the pathwise derivative argument is standard under stated smoothness.
- However, the reliance on discretization without error analysis and the limited reporting of computational details restricts a full assessment of robustness and scalability.
- Experimental evidence is consistent with the claimed advantages, particularly under noise, but would benefit from stronger statistical rigor and runtime reporting.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, with explicit notation and a clear algorithmic description. The distinction between the two appearances of A (optimization over z(1:q) versus discretization within q-KG) is noted, though still a source of potential confusion.
- Important technical details (IPA conditions, derivative of Cholesky, Monte Carlo/sample sizes, hyperparameter training protocols) are missing from the main text. Adding a concise subsection on computational complexity and practical settings would improve clarity.

Originality
- Extending KG to the batch setting for continuous domains with an efficient, differentiable estimator is a meaningful and novel contribution within BO. While pathwise gradient ideas are known, their application to q-KG and the specific estimator structure appear original relative to prior batch EI and batch ES work.
- The reduction to EI under specific conditions is a useful theoretical connection.

Significance
- Batch BO is widely used in parallel computing environments; an information-theoretic batch method with strong performance in noisy settings is of practical and scientific interest.
- If computational overhead is acceptable, practitioners could adopt q-KG in scenarios where noise undermines improvement-based heuristics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a complexity and runtime analysis (per-iteration cost versus baselines), report the number of Monte Carlo and gradient samples used, and include wall-clock comparisons.
- Analyze sensitivity to discretization (vary M), batch size q, and noise variance mis-specification; discuss convergence as M→∞.
- Clarify and summarize in the main text the conditions under which the IPA gradient is unbiased; describe handling of nondifferentiability and numerical stability of Cholesky differentiation.
- Ensure and document consistent GP priors and hyperparameter learning across baselines; increase the number of independent runs on real tasks and report uncertainty on final recommendations.
- Consider adding comparisons to local penalization batch BO and, if relevant, discussing asynchronous batch selection.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean extension of KG to batch setting with a practical optimizer.
- Empirical gains, especially under noise, on both synthetic and real tasks.
- Relevance and potential impact to practitioners needing parallel BO.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient analysis of discretization error and computational scalability; lack of runtime and sensitivity studies.
- Limited statistical rigor in real-task experiments and potential baseline comparability issues due to different implementations.
- Key technical conditions and stability considerations are outside the main text.
