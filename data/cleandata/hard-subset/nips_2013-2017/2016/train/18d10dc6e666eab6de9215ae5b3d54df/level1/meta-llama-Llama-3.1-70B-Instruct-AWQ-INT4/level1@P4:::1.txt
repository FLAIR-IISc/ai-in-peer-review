**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient method (q-KG). The algorithm is designed for the parallel setting, where multiple function evaluations can be performed simultaneously. The authors derive a new acquisition function, q-KG, which measures the information gain of evaluating q points. They also develop an efficient strategy for computing q-KG's gradient using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), allowing for efficient optimization. The paper demonstrates that q-KG outperforms other parallel Bayesian optimization algorithms on both synthetic test functions and real-world problems, especially when function evaluations are noisy.

**Related Work**

The paper is related to previous work on Bayesian optimization, particularly in the parallel setting. The authors discuss how their method differs from existing approaches, such as parallel expected improvement (EI) and parallel upper confidence bound (UCB) algorithms. They also highlight the connection to the knowledge gradient (KG) method for sequential Bayesian optimization.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and concise derivation of the q-KG algorithm. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm, and the authors provide a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods. However, some of the mathematical derivations could be more detailed, and the authors could provide more insight into the computational complexity of the algorithm.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the q-KG algorithm and its components. The notation is consistent, and the figures and tables are helpful in illustrating the experimental results. However, some of the sections could be better organized, and the authors could provide more transitions between sections.

**Originality**

The paper introduces a novel batch Bayesian optimization algorithm, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors also develop an efficient strategy for computing q-KG's gradient, which is an important innovation. However, the paper builds on existing work in Bayesian optimization, and some of the ideas may not be entirely new.

**Significance**

The paper has significant implications for the field of Bayesian optimization, particularly in the parallel setting. The q-KG algorithm has the potential to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of Bayesian optimization in a wide range of applications, from hyperparameter tuning to materials science. The authors demonstrate the algorithm's performance on several real-world problems, which highlights its practical significance.
