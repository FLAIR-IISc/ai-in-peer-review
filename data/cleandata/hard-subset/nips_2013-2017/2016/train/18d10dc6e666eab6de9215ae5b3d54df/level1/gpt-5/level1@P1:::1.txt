Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes q-KG, a batch (parallel) Bayesian optimization algorithm that generalizes the knowledge gradient (KG) to selecting q points jointly. Under a GP prior with Gaussian observation noise, q-KG defines the value of a batch z(1:q) as the expected reduction in the minimum of the GP posterior mean after taking that batch, i.e., the one-step Bayes-optimal decision wrt simple regret when a single batch remains. In the noise-free case and when the final recommendation is restricted to sampled points, q-KG reduces to q-EI. Evaluating and optimizing q-KG requires Monte Carlo over the posterior predictive of batch outcomes. The authors derive an efficient stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA), enabling gradient-based optimization of the batch. For continuous domains, they discretize the inner optimization over A using a set constructed by sampling candidate optima of the GP posterior (as in ES/PES), augmented with previously sampled points and the candidate batch.

Empirically, q-KG is compared to several batch BO baselines (q-EI implementations, GP-BUCB, GP-UCB-PE) on synthetic benchmarks (Branin, Rosenbrock, Ackley, Hartmann) and on hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression (MNIST) and a CNN (CIFAR-10), in both noise-free and noisy settings. q-KG is consistently competitive and often superior, with especially large gains under noisy evaluations.

This work connects to: sequential KG (Frazier et al. 2008/2009), correlated KG for continuous domains (Scott et al. 2011), multipoint EI (Chevalier & Ginsbourger 2013; Marmin et al. 2015), parallel UCB variants (Desautels et al. 2014; Contal et al. 2013), and parallel entropy-based methods (Hernández-Lobato et al. 2014; Shah & Ghahramani 2015). It advances KG into the parallel setting with a practical optimizer.

Quality
- The decision-theoretic formulation is sound; the equivalence to q-EI in the specified special case is correct and clarifying.
- The IPA-based unbiased gradient estimator for q-KG (under differentiable kernels and almost-sure uniqueness of the minimizer) is technically solid and addresses a core computational challenge in batch selection. However, important details are delegated to the supplement: conditions for exchanging expectation and differentiation, handling non-unique argmins, and the effect of discretization on unbiasedness.
- The discretization strategy (sampling candidate optima) is reasonable and used in ES/PES, but its approximation error and sensitivity to M are not quantified.
- Experiments are fairly extensive and use multiple baselines. Results convincingly support the claim that q-KG is robust and particularly strong under noise.
- The work would benefit from reporting wall-clock runtimes vs. q and dimension, and from ensuring uniformity of GP modeling and hyperparameter learning across baselines to rule out confounding.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. The dual use of A (inner minimization vs. feasible set for z) is confusing; the text warns about this, but a notational distinction (e.g., A for the domain, Ãn for the discretization used inside q-KG) would help.
- More algorithmic detail would improve reproducibility: number of Monte Carlo samples for q-KG and its gradient, step sizes or optimizer used, stopping criteria, and initialization for the batch optimizer.
- The description of how samples from the posterior minimizer distribution are drawn (referencing ES/PES) could be expanded briefly to be self-contained.

Originality
- Extending KG from sequential to batch with a principled one-step Bayes-optimal criterion is novel to my knowledge, especially with an efficient IPA-based gradient method for continuous domains.
- The discretization and fantasy sampling ideas draw on prior ES/PES work, and the use of Monte Carlo for parallel acquisition optimization is in line with PPES and q-EI gradient methods, but the combination with KG’s decision-theoretic objective is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
- Batch BO is practically important; a method that is robust to noise and provides strong performance with parallel resources is valuable.
- The decision-theoretic grounding is appealing, and the empirical improvements—especially under noise—suggest impact for hyperparameter tuning and other expensive stochastic objectives.
- Lack of theoretical convergence or regret guarantees beyond the one-step optimality and absence of runtime/scalability analysis temper the impact somewhat, but the contribution is still significant.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Provide a brief complexity analysis per gradient step as a function of q, M, and n (e.g., O(q^3) Cholesky + O(Mq) per MC sample), and include empirical runtimes vs. q and d.
- Discuss and, if possible, mitigate non-differentiability due to ties in the inner min (e.g., soft-min smoothing) and report how often it occurs in practice.
- Add ablations on M and the discretization strategy; quantify the effect on performance and cost.
- Ensure consistent GP priors/hyperparameter learning across baselines (e.g., Spearmint vs. MOE differences) or provide a control experiment with a unified GP stack.
- Consider including comparisons to more recent batch BO heuristics popular at the time, e.g., local penalization (González et al., ICML 2016), and/or batch Thompson sampling, to broaden the empirical context.
- Report results for larger batch sizes (e.g., q=8,16) to test the claimed efficiency at scale.

Pros
- Principled, Bayes-optimal (one-step) batch acquisition extending KG.
- Efficient and elegant IPA-based stochastic gradient for batch optimization.
- Strong empirical performance, especially under noise; relevant real-world tasks.
- Clear connection to and generalization of q-EI in the noise-free restricted setting.
- Open-source implementation leveraging established libraries.

Cons
- No runtime/scalability analysis; computational efficiency claims are not quantified.
- Discretization and gradient unbiasedness conditions are not fully articulated in the main text.
- Potential confounds from differing GP implementations across baselines.
- No theoretical convergence/regret guarantees beyond the one-step decision optimality.
- Limited exploration of larger q and sensitivity to M/discretization.

Overall recommendation
A strong contribution to batch BO with a principled objective and practical optimizer, supported by convincing experiments. I recommend acceptance after addressing clarity and experimental rigor issues (runtime reporting, ablations, and clarifying technical assumptions).
