**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a new batch Bayesian Optimization (BO) algorithm called the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) method. The goal is to select a batch of `q` points to evaluate in parallel that maximizes the expected improvement in the quality of the final solution. The paper defines the solution quality as the minimum of the Gaussian Process (GP) posterior mean, `min_x µ(x)`. The proposed q-KG acquisition function is derived from a decision-theoretic framework and represents the one-step Bayes-optimal choice of points, assuming only one batch evaluation remains.

A key challenge is the computational complexity of maximizing the q-KG function, which involves an expectation over the outcomes of the `q` parallel evaluations. The authors' main technical contribution is a computationally efficient method for this maximization. They use infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to derive an unbiased stochastic gradient of the q-KG factor. This enables the use of gradient-based optimizers to find the optimal batch of points. For continuous domains, the method relies on a dynamic discretization of the domain to approximate the `min` operator within the acquisition function.

The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation on synthetic benchmark functions and two real-world hyperparameter tuning problems (Logistic Regression on MNIST and a CNN on CIFAR-10). The results demonstrate that q-KG is competitive with or outperforms several state-of-the-art parallel BO methods, including parallel Expected Improvement (EI) and parallel Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) variants. The performance advantage of q-KG is shown to be particularly pronounced in settings with noisy function evaluations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The derivation of the q-KG acquisition function from a one-step Bayes-optimality principle is a major strength. Unlike many heuristic approaches for parallel BO, q-KG has a clear and compelling decision-theoretic motivation. The distinction from parallel EI—that KG optimizes for the best posterior mean over the entire domain rather than just among evaluated points—is crucial and well-articulated, providing a strong rationale for its superior performance in noisy settings.

2.  **Novel and Significant Technical Contribution:** The development of an efficient, gradient-based optimization scheme for the q-KG acquisition function is a significant contribution. The use of infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to obtain an unbiased stochastic gradient is clever and effectively addresses the primary computational barrier to using such a sophisticated, non-myopic acquisition function. This technique is of broader interest for developing other advanced acquisition functions.

3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are thorough and well-presented. The authors compare against a strong set of relevant baselines on both synthetic and practical machine learning tasks. The consistent outperformance of q-KG, especially the substantial gains observed in the noisy function evaluation settings (Sec 6.2), provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness and validates its theoretical design. The improvement shown on the carefully-tuned CNN architecture for CIFAR-10 is particularly impressive.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, with clear explanations of the motivation, the method itself, and its relation to prior work. The provision of open-source code is highly commendable and greatly enhances the paper's potential impact and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Overhead Analysis:** While the proposed gradient estimation method is efficient relative to naive approaches, the overall process of maximizing the q-KG acquisition function appears computationally intensive. It involves Monte Carlo sampling combined with stochastic gradient ascent, where each gradient evaluation requires operations on the discretized domain. The paper lacks a discussion or empirical analysis of the wall-clock time required to select a batch compared to the baselines. This is a critical practical consideration, as the overhead of BO should not overwhelm the cost of the function evaluations it aims to save. A discussion of the trade-offs would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Sensitivity to Discretization:** The method's performance relies on the quality of the domain discretization `An`. The paper follows previous work (e.g., Entropy Search) by sampling from the posterior optima, which is a reasonable heuristic. However, the sensitivity of q-KG to the size of this discretization (`M`) is not explored. It is possible that for some functions, this sampling strategy might fail to capture important regions, leading to a poor approximation of the true KG value. An ablation study on the effect of `M` would be valuable.

3.  **Scalability with Batch Size `q`:** The experiments are conducted with a fixed batch size of `q=4`. The computational complexity of the method involves a Cholesky decomposition of a `q x q` matrix in each Monte Carlo sample, which scales as O(q³). Furthermore, the optimization landscape of `z(1:q)` is `q*d`-dimensional and may become significantly more difficult to optimize for larger `q`. It is unclear how the method's computational cost and optimization stability scale to larger batch sizes (e.g., q=16 or 32), which are common in modern distributed computing environments. A discussion of these scalability aspects would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Proposes a novel, principled, and Bayes-optimal (one-step) acquisition function for batch BO.
*   Provides a significant technical contribution by making the optimization of this complex acquisition function tractable via IPA-based stochastic gradients.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art empirical performance, with a particularly strong and well-motivated advantage in noisy environments.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and provides code for reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical computational cost and its scaling with problem dimension and batch size are not sufficiently addressed.
*   The analysis of the approximation quality due to domain discretization is limited.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the Bayesian optimization literature. The proposed q-KG method is theoretically sound, technically novel, and empirically powerful. The weaknesses identified are primarily areas for future work and do not detract from the core contributions of the paper. This work is of high quality and is very likely to be of significant interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
