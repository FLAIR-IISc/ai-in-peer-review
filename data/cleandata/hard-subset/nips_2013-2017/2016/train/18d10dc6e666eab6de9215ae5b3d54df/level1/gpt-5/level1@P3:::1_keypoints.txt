- Proposal: Introduces q-KG, a batch Bayesian optimization acquisition function generalizing knowledge gradient to jointly select q points as the one-step Bayes-optimal criterion for minimizing the posterior mean’s minimum.
- Method: Derives a Monte Carlo estimator for q-KG and an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator via infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) for gradient-based joint optimization of batches.
- Implementation detail: For continuous domains, inner minimization is discretized using samples of posterior optima, previous points, and candidates; q-KG reduces to parallel EI in the noiseless “recommend-from-sampled” setting.
- Empirics: Shows consistent improvements or parity with parallel EI variants and GP-UCB-based batch methods on synthetic benchmarks and hyperparameter tuning for logistic regression and CNNs (MNIST/CIFAR10), especially under noise.
- Relation to prior work: Extends sequential KG and relates to parallel EI, parallel UCB, and information-theoretic methods (PES/PPES), positioning q-KG as a jointly optimized, decision-theoretic batch method.
- Strength — principled formulation: q-KG is a sound one-step Bayes-optimal criterion for fixed GP hyperparameters, with a correct reduction to q-EI under stated constraints.
- Strength — computational contribution: The IPA-based unbiased stochastic gradient estimator is nontrivial and enables scalable joint optimization over z(1:q).
- Strength — empirical results: Strong empirical performance, particularly in noisy settings, supports practical value.
- Weakness — discretization dependence: Implementation relies on a z-dependent discretized An, raising concerns about gradient unbiasedness when An changes and sensitivity to M and sampling scheme.
- Weakness — hyperparameter handling: Derived for fixed GP hyperparameters but implemented with per-iteration MLE updates; lacks discussion or experiments on marginalizing hyperparameters.
- Weakness — computational complexity: No clear complexity or wall-clock analysis with respect to q, dimension d, |An|, and Monte Carlo sample size; limited scaling evidence beyond q=4.
- Weakness — missing comparisons: Lacks comparisons to PPES and local penalization (and possibly Thompson-sampling batch methods) to better position among joint batch approaches.
- Weakness — statistical reporting: Presentation would benefit from formal statistical tests (confidence intervals) and clearer stopping criteria/budgets across methods.
- Clarity: Paper is generally readable but notation around A vs An is confusing; Section 5 needs pseudocode for stochastic gradient ascent (MC sizes, steps, restarts, stopping).
- Originality: Novel extension of KG to batch setting combined with IPA-based gradient estimation; discretization strategy borrows from ES/PES but the combination is new.
- Significance: Batch BO is practically important; a principled, noise-capable method with moderate overhead could be widely adopted, but significance depends on demonstrated scaling and runtimes.
- Suggested experiments/analyses: Provide complexity/wall-clock scaling vs q and d, experiments for larger q (8–16), sensitivity studies for M and Monte Carlo samples, and comparison of MLE vs hyperparameter marginalization.
- Technical clarifications requested: Detail IPA assumptions (uniqueness/differentiability), handling of ties/subgradients, whether AMn is fixed during inner optimization, and bias introduced by z-dependent discretization.
- Acceptance arguments: Principled batch KG extension, efficient unbiased gradient estimator, strong empirical gains under noise, and clear literature positioning with available code.
- Rejection arguments: Insufficient analysis of computational cost and scaling, questionable discretization impacts and gradient unbiasedness, missing key baselines and limited statistical rigor.
