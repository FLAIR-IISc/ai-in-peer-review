This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG), a novel acquisition function for batch Bayesian optimization. Extending the decision-theoretic foundation of the sequential knowledge gradient (KG) method, previously seen in operations research literature and more recently at NIPS, q-KG selects a batch of points that maximizes the expected improvement in the final solution quality after one parallel evaluation step. This contrasts with prior work like parallel Expected Improvement, which is often constructed greedily, and parallel UCB methods, which are typically designed to minimize cumulative regret. The authors make this computationally challenging acquisition function practical by developing an efficient gradient-based optimization strategy using Infinitesimal Perturbation Analysis (IPA).

The paper’s strengths lie in its originality, technical quality, and significance. The formulation of q-KG as the one-step Bayes-optimal batch policy provides a strong theoretical motivation that is novel in this context. The technical contribution is significant; deriving an efficient stochastic gradient estimator via IPA for this complex objective is non-trivial and essential for the method's viability. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating superior performance against several state-of-the-art baselines on both synthetic and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks. The method's particularly strong performance in noisy environments is a key finding, as this setting is highly relevant for many practical applications, such as evaluating models on subsets of data.

Despite these strengths, the paper could be improved in a few areas. The method's performance relies on a discretization of the optimization domain to compute the minimum of the posterior mean. The paper does not analyze the sensitivity of the results to the number of discretization points (M), nor does it discuss the associated computational overhead. Furthermore, a quantitative comparison of the wall-clock time per iteration against computationally cheaper baselines like GP-BUCB would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs involved for practitioners.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   A novel and principled acquisition function for batch BO with strong theoretical motivation.
*   Technically sound, featuring an efficient gradient estimation method for a complex objective.
*   Comprehensive experiments showing significant performance gains, especially in the practical noisy setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Lack of analysis on the sensitivity to the discretization parameter M.
*   The computational cost relative to baselines is not explicitly discussed.

Overall, the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. This paper presents a high-quality, original, and impactful contribution to the field of Bayesian optimization.
