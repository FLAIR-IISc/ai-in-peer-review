This paper introduces the parallel knowledge gradient (q-KG) for batch Bayesian optimization. Building on the knowledge gradient for sequential decisions [Frazier et al., 2009] and GP-based BO [Snoek et al., 2012], the authors define an acquisition function that is Bayes-optimal for a single remaining batch under a GP posterior, without restricting the final recommendation to lie among sampled points. They derive an efficient stochastic gradient estimator using infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA) to jointly optimize the batch, and approximate the inner minimization over the domain by a dynamically updated discretization inspired by sampling near posterior optima (akin to ES/PES ideas [Hernández-Lobato et al., 2014; Shah & Ghahramani, 2015]). Empirically, q-KG is compared against batch EI (both heuristic and exact multi-point EI [Chevalier & Ginsbourger, 2013; Marmin et al., 2015]) and batch UCB variants [Desautels et al., 2014; Contal et al., 2013], showing consistent improvements, especially with noisy evaluations and in hyperparameter tuning tasks.

Quality: The decision-theoretic formulation and the Monte Carlo/IPA machinery are technically sound, with appropriate references. The Bayes-optimality statement is correct for the one-batch setting. The gradient estimator is well motivated; however, the required regularity (differentiability, unique argmin) and the impact of hyperparameter re-estimation on optimality are only discussed briefly. The experiments are solid and include both synthetic and practical tasks; runtime and scalability analyses (as q, d grow) are limited.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized. The dual use of A (feasible set vs. discretized set in the inner objective) is acknowledged but still somewhat confusing; a cleaner notation would help. Implementation details and baselines are described, and code availability is a plus.

Originality: Extending KG to parallel batch selection and developing an efficient IPA-based optimizer is novel relative to prior batch EI and PPES work. The connection to EI in the noiseless, recommend-from-samples setting is insightful.

Significance: The method advances batch BO by offering a principled, non-greedy, information-directed criterion that performs particularly well under noise—an important regime for ML hyperparameter tuning. Likely to be used and extended.

Pros:
- Decision-theoretic batch acquisition with clear relation to KG and EI
- Efficient stochastic gradient optimization via IPA
- Strong empirical gains, especially with noise; open-source code

Cons:
- No theoretical regret or convergence guarantees; optimality limited to one-batch horizon
- Dependence on discretization; sensitivity and MC budget not fully analyzed
- Missing comparisons to batch Thompson sampling and asynchronous settings; limited wall-clock/runtime scaling study

Recommendation: Accept. Constructive suggestions: clarify assumptions for IPA unbiasedness, report computational cost vs. q and d, analyze discretization sensitivity, and consider asynchronous and batch Thompson baselines.
