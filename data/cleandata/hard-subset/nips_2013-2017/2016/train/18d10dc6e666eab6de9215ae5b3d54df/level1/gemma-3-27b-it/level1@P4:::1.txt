This paper introduces the Parallel Knowledge Gradient (q-KG) method for batch Bayesian Optimization (BO). The core idea is to extend the knowledge gradient concept to the parallel setting, aiming to select a batch of points that are Bayes-optimal in terms of expected incremental value. The authors also develop an efficient gradient-based approach for maximizing the q-KG acquisition function, leveraging infinitesimal perturbation analysis (IPA). They demonstrate the effectiveness of q-KG on both synthetic functions and real-world hyperparameter tuning tasks, particularly in noisy evaluation scenarios.

The paper builds upon prior work in sequential BO, such as the Knowledge Gradient (KG) method [Frazier et al., 2008], and extends it to the parallel setting, addressing the computational challenges that arise. Related work in parallel BO, including parallel EI [Chevalier et al., 2013], batch UCB [Desautels et al., 2014], and parallel UCB with exploration [Contal et al., 2016], are appropriately discussed. The paper positions q-KG as considering the batch collectively, unlike some iterative greedy approaches.  The connection to parallel EI is clearly stated, noting the equivalence under specific conditions (noise-free and restricted solution space).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending the knowledge gradient to a parallel batch setting is a significant contribution. The q-KG acquisition function represents a theoretically sound approach to batch selection, aiming for Bayes optimality.
* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of q-KG and the use of IPA for efficient gradient computation appear well-justified and mathematically rigorous. The decomposition of the gradient calculation using IPA is a clever approach to address the computational complexity.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides comprehensive experimental results on both synthetic and real-world problems. The consistent outperformance of q-KG compared to existing methods, especially in noisy settings, is convincing. The experiments cover a good range of problem types and complexities.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a reasonably accessible manner.
* **Implementation Availability:** Providing the code (https://github.com/wujian16/qKG) is a strong positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Discretization of A:** While Section 5.3 briefly describes discretization, the choice of discretization method and its potential impact on performance aren't thoroughly discussed.  The number of samples (M=1000) seems fixed and might be sensitive to the dimensionality of the problem. More analysis on this aspect would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:**  Although IPA is used to improve efficiency, the paper doesn’t provide detailed computational cost analysis of q-KG compared to the baselines.  Understanding the scalability of q-KG to higher-dimensional problems and larger batch sizes would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The experiments don't explicitly investigate the sensitivity of q-KG's performance to its own hyperparameters.  How robust is the method to choices in the GP kernel or other settings?
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** While q-KG performs well overall, the paper doesn't delve into scenarios where it might struggle. Identifying such limitations would strengthen the analysis.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to parallel Bayesian Optimization.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in noisy settings.
* The efficient gradient-based optimization technique makes q-KG practical for real-world applications.
* The code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The discretization method could be a potential bottleneck and deserves further scrutiny.
* A more detailed computational cost analysis and robustness analysis are needed.
* The paper could benefit from a discussion of potential failure cases and limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian Optimization. The proposed q-KG method is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated.  While some aspects could be further strengthened (discretization, computational cost, robustness), the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance.  I recommend **accepting** the paper.
