This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, which utilizes a Cholesky factorization instead of the square root of the covariance matrix. The primary motivation is to reduce the computational complexity associated with covariance matrix updates, which can dominate runtime, particularly in high-dimensional problems. The authors provide a theoretical justification for their approach, arguing that it maintains the algorithm's performance, and demonstrate empirical improvements in runtime on benchmark functions. This work builds upon prior attempts to accelerate CMA-ES updates, such as those employing Cholesky factors, and distinguishes itself by theoretically justifying the approach’s convergence properties and presenting a comprehensive empirical evaluation.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a solid theoretical argument for why replacing the matrix square root with a Cholesky factor should not deteriorate performance. The lemma regarding the convergence of orthogonal transformation matrices is particularly insightful and supports their claim.
* **Complexity Analysis:** The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck in CMA-ES and clearly articulate the potential for improvement through Cholesky factorization. The stated reduction in complexity to O(µd²) is significant.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the speedup achieved by the Cholesky-CMA-ES, especially as dimensionality increases. The inclusion of results comparing against CMA-ES-Ref and other variants strengthens the claim.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and understand the proposed method.  The discussion of related work is comprehensive.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The proposed approach has the potential to make CMA-ES more practical for high-dimensional optimization problems commonly encountered in machine learning applications like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning.
* **Open-Source Implementation:** Mentioning the addition of the algorithm to the Shark library is beneficial for reproducibility and broader adoption.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Error Introduced:** While the authors acknowledge the introduction of error due to the approximation in the step-size adaptation, the discussion remains relatively high-level. A more in-depth analysis of the magnitude of this error and its impact on convergence in different scenarios would be valuable.
* **Experimental Setup Detail:** The experimental setup could benefit from more detail regarding hyperparameter tuning, specific benchmark function settings, and hardware specifications. While the authors mention running 100 trials, information about statistical significance testing would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Scalability beyond d=256:** The authors note performance degradation at d=256 due to cache limitations. Exploring techniques to mitigate this, or discussing the practical limitations of the method for even larger dimensionalities, would be helpful.
* **Comparison to Other Modern Optimization Algorithms:** While comparisons to different CMA-ES variants are included, a comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art derivative-free optimization methods (e.g., those based on Bayesian optimization or other evolutionary algorithms) could better contextualize the performance gains.
* **Connection to Polar Decomposition:** The discussion regarding the polar decomposition and its cubic complexity feels somewhat abrupt.  A clearer explanation of why avoiding this step is crucial for maintaining the quadratic complexity would enhance understanding.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound modification to a widely used optimization algorithm.
* The empirical results demonstrate a significant and consistent speedup, particularly in higher dimensions.
* The writing is clear, and the organization is logical, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.
* The work has the potential to impact research areas that rely heavily on CMA-ES, such as reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The analysis of the error introduced by the approximation could be more thorough.
* The experimental setup lacks some detail, making it difficult to fully assess the reproducibility and robustness of the results.
* The comparison to other modern optimization algorithms is missing, limiting the ability to contextualize the contributions.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The theoretical justification, combined with the compelling empirical results, makes a strong case for the Cholesky-CMA-ES as a practical improvement over the original algorithm. While there are areas for improvement, particularly regarding a more detailed error analysis and a more comprehensive experimental comparison, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper with the request that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically by:

1.  Providing a more detailed analysis of the error introduced by the approximation in the step-size adaptation.
2.  Expanding the experimental section to include more details about the experimental setup (hyperparameter tuning, statistical significance testing).
3.  Adding a comparison to other modern derivative-free optimization algorithms.
