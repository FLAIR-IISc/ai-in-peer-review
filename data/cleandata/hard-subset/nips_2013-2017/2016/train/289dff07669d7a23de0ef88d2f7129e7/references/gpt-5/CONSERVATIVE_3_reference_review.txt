Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T instead of recomputing the matrix square root/eigendecomposition. The key engineering contribution is an O(µ d^2) per-iteration update (µ effective selection size) that applies rank-one updates to A for the rank-1 and rank-µ covariance terms, plus a scaling step. The step-size path pσ typically requires C^{-1/2}; the authors approximate it by using A^{-1}, arguing via an RQ/polar decomposition that the induced rotation error vanishes as C stabilizes. They claim optimal asymptotic time and storage complexity among full-covariance strategies, and show on standard noiseless benchmarks that function-evaluation counts match a reference CMA-ES while wall-clock time is substantially reduced, especially in higher dimensions. Comparisons include a reference implementation with delayed decompositions, a variant with d-step delays, and a prior non-triangular factor update (Suttorp et al., 2009).

Strengths
- Addresses a real bottleneck in full-covariance CMA-ES with a practical, implementable method that preserves much of the behavior of the original algorithm.
- Complexity is clearly improved for the full covariance case: O(µ d^2) update and O(d^2) storage are appropriate and practically important.
- Integration into a standard library (Shark) and use of LAPACK indicate care in implementation.
- Empirical results on several standard benchmarks suggest unchanged evaluation counts and substantial wall-clock gains that increase with d.

Weaknesses
- The central approximation (replacing C^{-1/2} by A^{-1} in CSA) lacks rigorous performance guarantees. Lemma 1 assumes convergence of the normalized covariance; this is not established for the general CMA-ES in the settings considered. There is no quantitative bound on the induced error in step-size adaptation or its effect on algorithm behavior.
- The claimed property “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix” is incorrect in general. For C = L L^T, diag(L) contains square roots of pivots/Schur complements, not eigenvalues. This misstatement undermines one of the touted advantages (“eigenvalues for free”).
- The experimental comparison to a “reference” CMA-ES seems unfavorable to the baseline: the paper states the decomposition delay equals 1 for the tested dimensions, effectively recomputing each iteration. State-of-the-art implementations often use larger delays or amortization strategies; results should include tuned delays to present a fair wall-clock comparison.
- Evaluation is relatively narrow: noiseless functions only, up to d = 256, and exclusion of non-convergent Rosenbrock runs. No tests on noisy/objective disturbances, constraints, or representative ML tasks (policy search, hyperparameter tuning) where step-size adaptation robustness matters. No COCO/BBOB suite results.
- The effect on invariance is not assessed in the main paper. The CSA approximation breaks the precise rotational correction; the claim that the error is small is plausible but unverified beyond limited benchmarks. Supplementary materials are referenced but not summarized.
- Numerical stability of the presented rank-one update (Algorithm 2) is not discussed relative to well-known stable Cholesky up-/downdate routines (e.g., LINPACK/BLAS xCHUD). Edge cases (very ill-conditioned C, small 1−c1−cµ) and accumulation of round-off errors are not addressed.
- Related work discussion is somewhat thin on alternative acceleration strategies and amortized EVD updates; reference formatting has issues, and there are several typos.

Quality
Technically competent in terms of algorithmic engineering, but the theoretical justification for the CSA approximation is weak. Lemma 1 is trivial under its assumptions and does not translate into guarantees for CMA-ES in non-asymptotic, changing-C regimes. A serious factual error about eigenvalues needs correction. The empirical evidence supports speed-ups but does not convincingly establish that the approximation is safe in broader settings or under challenging dynamics.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and the main idea is clear. However, there are inaccuracies (eigenvalues claim), reference formatting issues, and insufficient detail on key aspects (baseline settings, stability of Algorithm 2, supplemental analyses). Reproducibility would benefit from more implementation specifics and parameter settings for all baselines.

Originality
Incremental. Prior work has updated non-triangular factors (Suttorp et al., 2009) and provided rank-one Cholesky updates for certain CMA-ES variants (Krause & Igel, 2015). The contribution is to extend triangular Cholesky updates to full CMA-ES with rank-µ and to approximate CSA efficiently. This is a reasonable engineering step but not a fundamentally new algorithmic idea.

Significance
Practically relevant for users who need full covariance CMA-ES at moderate-to-high d and care about wall-clock time. That said, without stronger theoretical backing or broader, more challenging experiments, the impact is more of an implementation optimization than a research advance appropriate for NeurIPS.

Suggestions for improvement
- Remove or correct the claim about eigenvalues from Cholesky diagonals.
- Provide a quantitative analysis of the CSA approximation: bound the discrepancy between true and approximated pσ norms as a function of ∥Et E_{t−1}^T − I∥ and rate of change of C; ideally relate it to parameter choices cσ, dσ and provide non-asymptotic guarantees or at least controlled conditions.
- Include experiments with: (i) aggressive and tuned decomposition delays for the baseline CMA-ES; (ii) COCO/BBOB testbed across multiple dimensions; (iii) noisy objectives; (iv) constrained variants; and (v) realistic ML tasks (policy search, hyperparameter tuning).
- Report invariance diagnostics in the main text: statistics of Et rotations, distribution of pσ lengths, and any observed drift due to the approximation.
- Discuss numerical stability, citing standard up-/downdate routines, and include conditioning diagnostics and safeguards (e.g., jitter, variance floor enforcement).
- Consider an ablation that occasionally corrects the CSA path using an exact polar decomposition to quantify the approximation error in practice.

Pros
- Addresses a real performance bottleneck with a practical method.
- Clear complexity benefits and substantial empirical speed-ups.
- Integrates cleanly with standard CMA-ES and maintains evaluation counts on tested problems.

Cons
- Key theoretical justification for the CSA approximation is weak; no guarantees.
- Incorrect claim regarding eigenvalues of C from Cholesky diagonals.
- Experimental baselines and coverage are insufficient to establish robustness and fairness.
- Limited novelty relative to prior factor-update methods.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is useful and likely to be adopted in practice, but the paper overclaims (eigenvalues), lacks rigorous support for its central approximation, and provides a limited and somewhat unfair experimental comparison. With corrected claims, stronger theory or careful empirical validation across broader settings and fair baselines, it could be a solid contribution.
