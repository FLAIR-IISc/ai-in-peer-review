## Review of CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper proposes a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aiming to reduce runtime and memory footprint by updating the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix instead of the matrix itself. The authors provide a theoretical justification for the approach, arguing that it doesn't deteriorate performance, and demonstrate empirical improvements in runtime, particularly in higher dimensions, while maintaining comparable function evaluation counts.

The motivation for this work is well-established. The computational cost of covariance matrix adaptation in CMA-ES, specifically the matrix square root, is a known bottleneck, especially in high-dimensional spaces. Prior attempts to address this, such as delayed updates or restricting model complexity, have their own drawbacks. The proposal to utilize the Cholesky factorization directly, avoiding the square root computation, is a natural and potentially significant optimization.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the computational bottleneck in CMA-ES and frames the Cholesky-CMA-ES as a direct solution.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors attempt to provide a theoretical argument, through Lemma 1, regarding the convergence of the proposed method and the impact of replacing the covariance matrix inverse with its Cholesky factorization inverse. While not a complete proof of equivalence, the argument provides a reasonable basis for expecting comparable performance.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate a consistent and substantial reduction in runtime without a significant increase in the number of function evaluations. The scaling with dimensionality is particularly encouraging.
* **Practical Relevance:** The potential for faster optimization, especially in high-dimensional problems, is relevant to a wide range of applications where CMA-ES is employed.
* **Well-integrated into existing library:** Adaptation to Shark simplifies reproducibility and allows for direct comparison to established methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Limitations:** The theoretical justification feels incomplete. While Lemma 1 addresses the asymptotic behavior of the coordinate system rotation, it doesn't fully address the impact of the approximation introduced in Equation (5) on the step-size adaptation.  The statement that the error is "small" lacks rigorous quantification. A more thorough analysis, potentially including error bounds, would strengthen the argument.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  The experiments, while demonstrating a runtime improvement, primarily focus on standard benchmark functions. The inclusion of more challenging, real-world problems or problems specifically designed to expose potential weaknesses in the Cholesky update would bolster the claims. The limited scope of CMA-ES-Ref comparison (up to d=128) is also a concern, given the stated benefits in higher dimensions.
* **Comparison to Suttorp-CMA-ES:** The comparison to Suttorp et al.'s approach feels cursory. A more detailed comparison, exploring the trade-offs between the two methods, would be valuable. The authors briefly mention the advantages of a triangular matrix (eigenvalue access, efficient inversion) but don’t provide a detailed performance breakdown to justify the additional complexity.
* **Lack of discussion on numerical stability:** While claiming numerical stability, there's no discussion of potential issues arising from Cholesky factorizations, particularly concerning conditioning and potential breakdown. This is a crucial aspect when dealing with ill-conditioned covariance matrices, which CMA-ES is often used for.
* **Modest originality:** While the presented work is a valid and useful optimization, it builds heavily on existing CMA-ES variations (Suttorp et al.) and doesn’t present a fundamentally new algorithmic principle.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practical bottleneck in a widely-used optimization algorithm.
* The empirical results demonstrate a significant and consistent runtime improvement.
* The theoretical justification, while not complete, provides a reasonable basis for the observed performance.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical justification lacks rigor and quantifiable error bounds.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
* The originality of the contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking.
* The discussion of potential numerical stability issues is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

I assess this paper as a **weak accept**. While the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a valuable optimization that demonstrably improves the runtime of CMA-ES, the theoretical justification is not entirely convincing, and the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive. The paper feels more like an engineering optimization than a fundamentally new algorithmic contribution. It offers a practical improvement to an established method, which is valuable, but falls short of the level of novelty typically expected at NIPS. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing a more rigorous theoretical analysis and expanding the experimental evaluation to include more challenging problems.  A discussion of numerical stability concerns would also be beneficial.
