Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that replaces the explicit covariance matrix update and subsequent eigenvalue decomposition with direct rank-one updates to the triangular Cholesky factor At of the covariance Ct = At At^T. The main algorithmic change is to carry out the rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates via a quadratic-time Cholesky rank-one update routine, yielding an O(µ d^2) per-iteration update cost and optimal O(d^2/2) storage. A key technical issue is step-size adaptation (CSA), which in standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2}. The authors argue that replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} introduces only a rotation error that vanishes asymptotically as the shape of Ct stabilizes. They prove a lemma showing that the orthogonal factor of the RQ decomposition of C^{1/2} converges under convergence of the normalized covariance sequence, supporting the approximation.

Empirically, on standard black-box benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) with random rotations and dimensions up to 256, the proposed method matches the number of function evaluations of a reference CMA-ES (with decomposition every iteration in the tested regime) while significantly reducing wall-clock time; gains increase with dimension (≈20× at d=64). Comparisons also include a delayed-decomposition CMA-ES and a non-triangular Cholesky-factor approach (Suttorp et al., 2009). The method relates to prior efforts to reduce CMA-ES cost via delayed updates (Hansen and Ostermeier), restricted models (diagonal/limited-memory variants), and factor updates (Suttorp et al.; Krause and Igel for (1+1)-CMA-ES). The novelty here is a full CMA-ES with triangular Cholesky updates and a theoretically reasoned approximation for CSA.

Strengths
- Addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in full covariance CMA-ES with a principled, implementable solution.
- Clear derivation of the O(µ d^2) update by decomposing the rank-1 and rank-µ covariance terms into Cholesky rank-one updates; storage is optimal for full covariance.
- Useful theoretical insight: the CSA approximation error is shown to vanish asymptotically under mild convergence assumptions (Lemma 1 and associated discussion).
- Empirical results are consistent with claims: nearly identical evaluation counts and substantially lower runtime; experimental setup includes random rotations to probe invariance.
- Practical advantages: triangular form eases inversion in O(d^2) and integrates naturally with sampling and monitoring.

Weaknesses
- A key claim is incorrect: “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” In general, diag(At) are not equal to sqrt eigenvalues of Ct = At At^T; eigenvalues of Ct are squares of singular values of At, which differ from its diagonal entries. Hence, “eigenvalues for free” is not justified; only coarse conditioning proxies can be inferred from the Cholesky factors without additional computation.
- The CSA approximation analysis is asymptotic and qualitative; there is no finite-time bound on the norm error in pσ. The argument relies on convergence (in expectation) and slow variation of Ct, which may not hold during early adaptation phases, on multimodal functions, or under noise.
- Invariance: Step-size adaptation already breaks affine invariance in standard CMA-ES; replacing C^{-1/2} by A^{-1} introduces additional rotation dependence. While the authors monitor rotation changes (Et) in the supplement, quantitative invariance results are not shown in the main paper.
- Numerical stability of repeated rank-one updates (including rank-µ compositions) is not analyzed. Stability, drift, and the effect of finite precision over long runs (especially with downdating components if β < 0 arises effectively) merit discussion or diagnostics.
- Experimental scope is limited to synthetic benchmarks; no results on noisy functions, constraints, or representative ML tasks (e.g., policy search), where CSA behavior and robustness are critical. Comparisons to large-scale alternatives (e.g., LM-CMA) are missing for higher d where full covariance becomes prohibitive.
- Presentation issues: several typos and reference formatting problems; some notational slips (e.g., Σ = HH should be Σ = H H^T). These detract from clarity.

Quality
- Technical core (triangular rank-one updates to realize the rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates) is sound and aligns with known Cholesky update methods. The CSA approximation is justified by a plausible asymptotic argument. However, the erroneous eigenvalue claim should be corrected, and numerical stability considerations should be addressed. Empirical methodology (100 trials, random rotations, varying d, reporting evals and wall-clock) is appropriate.

Clarity
- Overall readable, with a clean high-level description and pseudocode. Some derivations are succinct but adequate for the target audience. Clarity is reduced by typos/formatting in equations and references, and by relegating key invariance diagnostics to the supplement.

Originality
- Extends prior Cholesky-update ideas from restricted CMA-ES variants to the standard rank-µ CMA-ES, and contributes a new CSA approximation rationale specific to using A^{-1}. This is a nontrivial and useful combination; originality is moderate to good within the CMA-ES literature.

Significance
- For practitioners needing full covariance adaptation on moderate-to-large d, the reported speedups without evaluation overhead are valuable. The method offers an improved baseline implementation for CMA-ES and may see adoption in libraries. Impact is practical rather than conceptual; broader significance would be strengthened by results on noisy/realistic tasks and a sharper invariance/stability analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the claim about eigenvalues and clarify what diagnostic quantities can be obtained cheaply from At (e.g., determinant from product of diag(At), rough condition proxies), or add a lightweight routine to estimate eigenvalues if needed.
- Provide finite-time empirical bounds or diagnostics on the CSA approximation error (e.g., measure ‖pσ‖ discrepancy vs. exact C^{-1/2} on smaller d) and show how it decays over iterations.
- Include invariance experiments in the main text: quantify sensitivity to rotations and affine transforms for both evaluation counts and runtime.
- Discuss numerical stability of Algorithm 2 under long runs and provide safeguards (e.g., periodic re-Cholesky from Ct reconstructed, or re-orthogonalization checks), with empirical evidence.
- Add experiments on noisy functions and at least one ML-relevant task (policy search or hyperparameter tuning) to demonstrate robustness.
- Expand comparisons to limited-memory variants (LM-CMA) at higher dimensions to clarify the regime where full covariance Cholesky-CMA-ES is preferable.
- Fix typographical and reference formatting issues; ensure all matrices are transposed appropriately in formulas; unify notation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, implementable method that materially reduces runtime and memory while preserving optimization performance in evaluation counts.
- Theoretical reasoning for the CSA approximation is credible and aligns with observed outcomes.
- Extends and consolidates prior work into a standard CMA-ES variant with optimal asymptotic update/storage complexity.

Arguments against acceptance
- Contains a substantive incorrect claim about eigenvalues from the Cholesky factor; needs correction.
- Lacks finite-time analysis and detailed invariance/stability evaluation in the main text.
- Experimental scope omits noisy/realistic ML settings and large-scale alternatives, limiting demonstrated generality.
