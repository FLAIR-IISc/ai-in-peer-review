## Review of CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper presents a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aiming to reduce runtime and memory complexity by operating directly on the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix instead of the matrix square root. The core idea is to leverage existing fast Cholesky update methods, specifically those introduced by Suttorp et al. [2009] and Krause and Igel [2015], extending them to the full CMA-ES framework. The authors provide a theoretical justification for the approximation introduced by avoiding the correction factor in the step-size adaptation and present empirical results demonstrating significant speedups, particularly in higher dimensions.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a practical bottleneck:** The computational cost of covariance matrix adaptation is a known limitation of CMA-ES, particularly for high-dimensional problems. This work directly tackles this issue.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The authors attempt to provide a theoretical justification for the approximation introduced, addressing concerns about convergence and performance degradation. Lemma 1, while reliant on assumptions, is a positive step in this direction.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments demonstrate a clear speedup of the Cholesky-CMA-ES compared to the standard CMA-ES and other variants, especially with increasing dimensionality. The inclusion of runtime comparisons is valuable.
* **Optimal Complexity:** The algorithm achieves the stated optimal asymptotic complexity for covariance update and storage.
* **Practical benefits:** The algorithm provides a numerically stable computation of the inverse of the Cholesky factor and provides the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix without additional cost.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Approximation justification is weak:** The theoretical justification based on the convergence of rotation matrices (Lemma 1) feels somewhat hand-wavy. The reliance on the assumption that Ct converges and the statement that this justifies neglecting the correction factor is not fully convincing. A more rigorous analysis of the error introduced by omitting the correction term is needed. The argument that "only the last few coordinate systems matter" lacks quantitative support.
* **Limited novelty:** While the application of fast Cholesky updates to the *full* CMA-ES is a valid contribution, the core idea builds heavily on prior work (Suttorp et al. [2009], Krause and Igel [2015]). The paper doesn't sufficiently highlight the specific challenges overcome in extending these methods to the standard CMA-ES beyond the initial statement.
* **Experimental setup lacks detail:** The experimental section is somewhat superficial. While the results are presented clearly, crucial details are missing. For example, the parameter settings for each algorithm variant beyond the default CMA-ES parameters are not specified. The statement that the delay strategy in CMA-ES-Ref equals one for the considered dimensions feels incomplete without providing the exact formula used for calculating the delay.
* **Benchmarking concerns:** The exclusion of trials where CMA-ES-Ref failed to converge on the Rosenbrock function is problematic. Reporting only successful runs can bias the comparison. What percentage of trials were excluded, and why?
* **Minor notation inconsistencies and typos:** Equation (5) is referred to in the text but isn’t explicitly labelled as such in the paper. There are numerous minor typographical errors (e.g., "ω(d2)" in the introduction should be O(d^2)).
* **Overly strong claims:** Assertions like "The Cholesky-CMA-ES was significantly faster" and "The speed-up increases with the search space dimensionality" could be tempered with more nuanced language and statistical significance tests. The claim of achieving “optimal” storage and runtime complexity feels overstated without acknowledging the complexity of matrix multiplication.
* **Missing Discussion of Numerical Stability:** While mentioned as a benefit, a dedicated section analyzing the numerical stability of the proposed approach, comparing it against standard CMA-ES under different conditions (e.g., ill-conditioning), is missing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and practical problem in CMA-ES.
* Provides a functional and demonstrably faster variant of the algorithm.
* Includes a theoretical attempt at justifying the approximation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical justification is not rigorous enough to fully support the claims.
* The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail and contains potential biases.
* The level of novelty is incremental, building heavily on existing work.
* Several minor issues (typos, notation inconsistencies) detract from the presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization, but its execution falls short of NIPS standards. While the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a clear performance improvement, the lack of a robust theoretical justification and the shortcomings in the experimental evaluation raise concerns. The incremental nature of the contribution, combined with the numerous minor flaws, leads me to recommend **rejection**. 

The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by providing a more rigorous theoretical analysis, a more detailed and unbiased experimental setup, and a more careful presentation of the results. A stronger emphasis on the specific challenges of adapting existing Cholesky update methods to the full CMA-ES would also enhance the paper’s novelty.
