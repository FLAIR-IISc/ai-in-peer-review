Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = AA^T via O(µ d^2) rank-one updates, rather than updating C and recomputing its square root or eigen-decomposition. The main challenge is the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), which in standard CMA-ES requires C^{-1/2}; the authors replace C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}, argue the discrepancy is a time-varying orthogonal rotation E_t, and provide a lemma stating that, if normalized covariances converge, E_t stabilizes so the error in the CSA norm vanishes. Empirically, on standard deterministic, rotated benchmarks up to d=256, Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves essentially identical evaluation counts to a reference CMA-ES and significantly lower wall-clock time; the advantage grows with d.

Relation to prior work
The work extends earlier rank-one Cholesky-update approaches used in special CMA-ES variants ((1+1)-CMA-ES, MO-CMA-ES) and in Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015). It argues for the benefits of maintaining a triangular factor (unique mapping, O(d^2) triangular solves) and contrasts with delayed eigen-decomposition and restricted models (diagonal/separable, LM-CMA). The paper situates the method within the CMA-ES literature, though some comparisons are missing (see below).

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic contribution: a practical Cholesky-factor update for standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 that avoids cubic-time decompositions while keeping full covariance modeling.
- Sensible engineering: triangular solves and updates are O(d^2); no need to maintain both A and A^{-1}; rank-one updates are standard and likely stable for positive updates.
- Empirical results show substantial wall-clock speedups while retaining evaluation efficiency on standard smooth benchmarks, including rotated instances and ill-conditioned functions.
- The paper discusses invariance considerations and the CSA approximation’s effect, rather than ignoring it.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical support for the CSA approximation is weak. The lemma presumes convergence of normalized covariances and uses continuity of the RQ decomposition to conclude that the inter-iteration rotations ET_{t-1} E_t → I, hence the CSA norm error decays. In CMA-ES, convergence of C̄_t is not generally guaranteed; the argument remains heuristic. No quantitative bound on the bias/variance introduced in σ is provided, nor conditions when the approximation may fail (e.g., early transients, highly nonstationary C updates, multimodal landscapes, noise).
- Invariance properties: standard CSA already lacks a formal affine invariance guarantee; replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} introduces an additional, iteration-dependent rotation. The paper claims the error is small due to slow changes, but an explicit test of affine invariance (beyond rotations) or an empirical ablation comparing exact CSA vs A^{-1}-CSA (where feasible) is missing.
- Baselines and fairness: the primary wall-clock baseline is a CMA-ES reference implementation that recomputes the matrix square root every iteration in the tested regimes. Many established implementations amortize decompositions aggressively (e.g., update every O(d) iterations) to achieve O(d^2) per iteration on average. While CMA-ES/d is included, the delay choice is not tuned or swept, and there is no comparison to active CMA-ES (negative updates), sep-CMA-ES with occasional full updates, or LM-CMA (Loshchilov) for higher d. The claim “optimal O(µ d^2)” is less compelling without demonstrating parity or superiority against carefully tuned delayed decompositions and strong full-covariance baselines.
- Empirical scope: only deterministic benchmarks are shown; CMA-ES is widely used in noisy settings and constrained optimization. The CSA approximation might behave differently under noise; no results are provided. Evaluations on the COCO/BBOB testbed with ERT statistics would strengthen claims of unchanged evaluation complexity.
- A technical inaccuracy: the paper states “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is incorrect in general; the eigenvalues of C equal the squares of the singular values of A, not the squares of the diagonal entries of A unless C is diagonal in the basis of A. This needs correction; it also affects the claim of “eigenvalues for free.”
- Presentation issues: Algorithm 2 resembles a rank-one Cholesky update, but no numerical stability discussion or references to standard cholupdate/downdate routines are provided; handling negative β (not used here) is unclear. The references contain formatting errors and some incomplete entries. Reproducibility details (code availability, seeds, BLAS/LAPACK settings) could be more explicit.

Quality
Technically competent on the Cholesky update side; the CSA approximation lacks rigorous analysis and quantified guarantees. Empirical evidence is positive but limited in scope and baselines. There is at least one factual error (eigenvalues claim).

Clarity
Overall clearly written and well-organized. The CSA approximation and its implications are explained, though the statement about eigenvalues is misleading. Reference formatting needs fixing.

Originality
Extends known ideas (rank-one Cholesky updates) to standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 and proposes a specific CSA approximation with a convergence-heuristic argument. Incremental but useful.

Significance
Potentially valuable for practitioners who need full covariance CMA-ES at moderate to high d where decompositions dominate time. However, without stronger theoretical guarantees and broader empirical comparisons (including noisy settings and established amortized-decomposition baselines), impact is uncertain.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide a quantitative bound (even under simplifying assumptions) on the CSA norm error induced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}? For example, a bound in terms of ∥ET_{t-1}E_t − I∥ and the CSA horizon?
- Please correct the statement about eigenvalues of C from the Cholesky factor and clarify what spectral information is actually available “for free.” If you rely on diagonal entries of A as variance proxies, justify this or use inexpensive eigenspectrum estimates if needed.
- Include experiments with noise and constraints, and report standard COCO/BBOB metrics (ERT). Also compare against tuned delayed-decomposition CMA-ES, active CMA-ES, sep-CMA-ES with periodic full updates, and LM-CMA for larger d.
- Add an ablation that computes exact CSA (using C^{-1/2}) every k iterations to quantify the σ-update approximation error vs k and its effect on evaluation counts.
- Discuss numerical stability of Algorithm 2 and reference standard cholupdate algorithms; report any regularization used.

Pros
- Practical algorithm with strong speedups in wall-clock time.
- Maintains full covariance modeling and preserves evaluation counts on tested problems.
- Clean O(µ d^2) updates and memory footprint; triangular solves are efficient.

Cons
- Weak theoretical guarantee for the CSA approximation; no bounds.
- Limited baselines and empirical scope; no noisy/constrained tests.
- Incorrect claim about eigenvalues from the Cholesky factor.
- Fairness of baseline choice (decomposition frequency) not fully established.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is sensible and empirically promising, but the theoretical justification for the σ-update approximation is too thin, a key claim about eigenvalues is incorrect, and the experimental comparisons are not comprehensive enough against established, amortized-decomposition CMA-ES variants and noisy settings. With corrected claims, stronger theory (or tighter empirical ablations), and broader baselines, this would be a strong submission.
