The paper proposes a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aiming to reduce runtime and memory complexity by utilizing the Cholesky factorization instead of the square root of the covariance matrix. The core idea is to update the Cholesky factor directly, avoiding repeated matrix square root computations. The authors provide a theoretical justification, based on Lemma 1 concerning the convergence of rotation matrices, to support the claim that this approximation doesn't significantly deteriorate performance. Empirical results on benchmark functions demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance in terms of function evaluations but exhibits a substantial speedup in wall-clock time, particularly in higher dimensions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a relevant bottleneck:** The computational cost of covariance matrix manipulation is a known limitation of CMA-ES, especially for high-dimensional problems.
*   **Theoretical justification:** The authors attempt to provide a theoretical basis for their approach, addressing concerns about potential performance degradation. Lemma 1 is a noteworthy effort to formalize the impact of coordinate system rotations.
*   **Empirical validation:** The experiments demonstrate a clear speedup in runtime without a loss in the number of function evaluations, supporting the practical benefit of the proposed method. The experimental setup including varying dimensionality and rotation invariance tests is reasonable.
*   **Clear presentation of the algorithm:** Algorithm 1 and 2 provide a relatively easy-to-understand description of the proposed method and the rank-one update.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lemma 1's assumptions are strong:** The convergence argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that *Ct* converges to a certain shape. While the authors acknowledge the randomized nature of CMA-ES and that convergence isn’t guaranteed, the assumption feels insufficiently justified in the context of a practical optimization algorithm. The discussion of convergence being proven only for simplified algorithms feels like a brush-off.
*   **The “approximation” feels glossed over:** The correction factor removed in equation (5) is presented as a minor detail, with the error assumed to be small. However, the impact of omitting this correction isn't thoroughly investigated or quantified. The statement that "we can expect this error to be small" is not supported with sufficient evidence. A more detailed error analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Limited comparison to other state-of-the-art methods:** While comparison to CMA-ES-Ref and CMA-ES/d is provided, the comparison to the Suttorp-CMA-ES is too limited. The authors state the algorithm exceeded the L2 cache at d=256, but the results don't show a clear comparison for any dimension where *all* algorithms can run efficiently. A more comprehensive benchmark would enhance the validity of the claims.
*   **The discussion of quadratic complexity is misleading:** The statement about optimal asymptotic complexity is potentially misleading. While the Cholesky update itself is O(µd<sup>2</sup>), the overall complexity also includes the other steps in the CMA-ES iteration. The authors acknowledge a naive matrix multiplication assumption, but don't fully acknowledge that *other* parts of the algorithm might become dominant at very large scales.
*   **Minor formatting/writing issues:** Throughout the paper, there are small inconsistencies in notation (e.g., subscripting of variables). The paper could benefit from a more thorough proofread. For example, the sentence "This introduces some error, but we will show in the following that we can expect this error to be small and to decrease over time as the algorithm converges to the optimum." appears redundant and awkwardly worded.
*   **Reference formatting:** The references are not uniformly formatted.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a genuine practical challenge in CMA-ES.
*   The proposed method demonstrably improves runtime without sacrificing performance.
*   The theoretical justification, while not entirely conclusive, is a reasonable attempt to understand the algorithm's behavior.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification relies on strong assumptions that may not hold in all practical scenarios.
*   The error analysis related to removing the correction factor is insufficient.
*   The empirical comparison lacks sufficient depth, especially against closely related methods like Suttorp-CMA-ES.
*   The paper contains minor formatting and writing issues.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be a mixed bag. It presents a potentially valuable improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm, backed by some theoretical reasoning and empirical evidence. However, the theoretical justification feels incomplete, and the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive. The minor flaws in writing and formatting, while not major, contribute to the impression of a somewhat rushed or insufficiently polished submission. While the core idea is strong and the observed speedup is significant, I am not fully convinced that the authors have adequately addressed all the potential issues with their approach.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

I recommend rejection due to the insufficiently rigorous theoretical justification and the limited empirical validation. While the proposed method shows promise, further investigation and more thorough evaluation are needed to establish its robustness and general applicability. The authors should address the concerns regarding the error analysis and provide a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods before resubmitting.
