Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = A A^T instead of computing a matrix square root or eigendecomposition each iteration. The main technical challenge is the step-size adaptation term, which in standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2}; the authors replace it by A^{-1} and provide a justification based on the convergence of the orthogonal factor in the RQ decomposition (Lemma 1), arguing that the induced rotation error vanishes as the algorithm stabilizes. They extend prior quadratic-time rank-one Cholesky update (previously shown for µ=1) to the standard CMA-ES with rank-1 and rank-µ updates (overall O(µ d^2) per iteration), achieving optimal asymptotic time and storage complexity for full-covariance adaptation. Experiments on standard test functions (with random rotations) show comparable numbers of function evaluations but substantially reduced wall-clock time compared to a state-of-the-art CMA-ES with delayed decompositions and to a non-triangular Cholesky approach.

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015), who update decompositions instead of C; the novelty here is applying triangular Cholesky updates to the full CMA-ES (µ>1) and addressing the CSA step-size term by an approximation with theoretical justification.
- Distinct from diagonal/limited-memory variants (Ros & Hansen 2008; Loshchilov 2014/2015; Sun et al. 2013), as this work preserves full covariance modeling and its invariance properties to a large extent.
- Empirical scope similar to standard CMA-ES benchmarking; practical details (Shark, BLAS/LAPACK) align with prior literature.

Strengths
- Clear practical contribution: O(µ d^2) per-iteration update with optimal storage while keeping full covariance adaptation. For typical µ = O(log d), reported speedups (e.g., 20x at d=64) are compelling for practitioners.
- Sensible and simple modification to CSA with a plausible convergence argument (Lemma 1) explaining why the rotation-induced error diminishes as Ct stabilizes.
- Careful comparison versus both a reference CMA-ES with delayed updates and a non-triangular factor approach; demonstrates same evaluation efficiency and significantly lower wall-clock time.
- Triangular factor permits efficient A^{-1} computation in O(d^2), which can be important in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Important technical error: the paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. Diagonals of A are not eigenvalues of C. You cannot obtain the eigenvalues of C “for free” from a Cholesky factor. This should be corrected; while not central to the method, it undermines one advertised advantage.
- Theoretical justification for the CSA approximation is asymptotic and contingent on shape convergence of C̄t; there are no finite-time bounds or guarantees under non-stationarity/noise, and CMA-ES itself lacks a general convergence proof. The argument is intuitive but not rigorous enough to preclude corner cases where rotations oscillate.
- Experimental scope is limited to noiseless unconstrained benchmarks (albeit with random rotations). No tests on noisy functions, constraints, or real ML applications (e.g., RL policy search), where CSA behavior is critical. The method’s robustness in those regimes remains unclear.
- Fairness/representativeness: the “reference” CMA-ES recomputes the decomposition every iteration for the chosen dimensions (as per the stated delay rule yielding 1), which may disadvantage it; many implementations amortize this cost more aggressively. The CMA-ES/d variant helps, but a stronger baseline configuration and profiling breakdown would bolster claims.
- Clarity and presentation: several typos/formatting issues, missing or malformed references, orphaned footnote markers, and some derivations that could be more self-contained. Algorithm 2 could use more explanation on numerical stability and conditioning safeguards.

Quality
- The algorithmic contribution is sound and practically valuable, with a reasonable (if asymptotic) theoretical rationale for the CSA approximation. The incorrect eigenvalue claim is a notable flaw that should be fixed. Empirical evidence is positive but could be broader.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, with a good high-level narrative, but marred by reference and typographic issues. Suggestions:
  - Remove/repair the eigenvalue claim; instead, state what can be obtained cheaply from A (e.g., determinant via product of diagonals; diagonal of C via rowwise norms of A).
  - Clarify the derivation of Algorithm 2 and note any numerical stabilization (e.g., jitter, rescaling).
  - Organize assumptions around Lemma 1 and make explicit the conditions under which the CSA approximation might fail.

Originality
- Moderate but meaningful. Extending rank-one Cholesky updates to the full CMA-ES and tackling CSA with a justified approximation is a nontrivial and useful advance over prior decomposition-update methods.

Significance
- High practical impact for the CMA-ES community: if adopted, this can substantially reduce runtime without harming evaluation efficiency on full-covariance problems. The idea is likely to be used and extended (e.g., hybrid with limited-memory schemes, noisy settings).

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide ablations quantifying the discrepancy between true CSA with C^{-1/2} and the proposed A^{-1} (e.g., cosine between pσ sequences, step-size trajectories), and relate it to the measured Et rotations?
- How does the method behave under noise or in RL tasks where CSA is notoriously sensitive?
- Can you compare against a CMA-ES baseline that recomputes the decomposition less frequently (e.g., every O(d) or adaptive scheduling) with tuned BLAS to ensure fairness?
- Please correct the eigenvalue claim and, if you need spectrum information, discuss the cost of obtaining it or alternatives (e.g., periodic lightweight eigenspectrum estimation).
- Any numerical stability tricks (renormalization of A, enforcing min variances) used in practice?

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept). Despite some presentation issues and the incorrect eigenvalue claim, the paper offers a practically important, computationally efficient variant of CMA-ES with a clear, novel idea (CSA via A^{-1}) and supportive evidence. With minor fixes and broader empirical validation, it will be a valuable contribution to the community.
