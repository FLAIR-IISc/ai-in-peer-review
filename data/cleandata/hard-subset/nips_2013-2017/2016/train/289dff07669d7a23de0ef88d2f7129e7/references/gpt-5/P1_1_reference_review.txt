Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = A A^T instead of recomputing C and its matrix square root (or eigendecomposition). The key challenge is the step-size adaptation (CSA), which in standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2} to normalize the evolution path. The authors replace C^{-1/2} by A^{-1}, noting that C^{1/2} = A E for some orthogonal E from an RQ decomposition, and argue that ignoring the rotation E introduces only a small, diminishing error as the algorithm converges. They provide an O(µ d^2) rank-one update scheme for A (applied µ+1 times per iteration), optimal storage O(d^2), and show empirically that their method preserves the number of function evaluations while significantly reducing wall-clock time vs. a reference CMA-ES with delayed decompositions and vs. a non-triangular Cholesky update variant. Benchmarks include rotated Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers for d up to 256; speedups >20x are reported at d=64 with similar evaluation counts.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Suttorp et al. (2009), who proposed maintaining a Cholesky-like factor, and on Krause & Igel (2015), who derived quadratic-time rank-one updates for the (1+1)-CMA-ES. The present paper extends these ideas to the standard multi-offspring CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and addresses the CSA normalization by an approximation. It contrasts with restricted covariance models (diagonal, low-rank, limited memory) such as Ros & Hansen (2008), Sun et al. (2013), and Loshchilov (2014, 2015), by maintaining a full covariance while achieving reduced time and storage complexity for the update. The information geometry perspective (Akimoto et al., 2012) is cited to motivate standard CMA-ES updates.

Strengths
- Practical contribution: an implementable, drop-in variant of CMA-ES that yields large wall-clock speedups without sacrificing evaluation efficiency on standard non-separable benchmarks.
- Complexity: O(µ d^2) update per iteration with optimal O(d^2) storage; clear algorithmic description including a rank-one Cholesky update routine.
- Sensible argument for CSA approximation via a convergence lemma on the rotation E_t (Lemma 1), and an empirical check of rotation effects (supplement).
- Thorough experimental sweep over dimensions and rotated functions; results align with claims.

Weaknesses
- A serious factual error: “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general; diag(A)^2 are not eigenvalues of C unless C is diagonal in the chosen basis. This undermines one of the touted “additional advantages” (eigenvalues for free, conditioning monitoring on principal components).
- Theoretical support for the CSA approximation is heuristic/asymptotic. Lemma 1 shows E_t^T E_{t-1} → I if C̄_t converges in shape, but no quantitative bound is provided on the induced error in ‖p_σ‖, nor on its effect on invariance or performance away from convergence.
- Experimental methodology: excluding unsuccessful Rosenbrock runs biases evaluation counts and time; success rates should be reported. The baseline “CMA-ES-Ref” recomputes the square root every iteration for the reported dimensions, which may not reflect the most efficient commonly used CMA-ES settings (lazy eigendecomposition at longer intervals). More discussion of baseline tuning and fairness would strengthen the case.
- Some clarity/rigor issues: uniqueness conditions in the RQ decomposition are not fully specified; numerical stability of repeated rank-one updates over long runs is not analyzed (e.g., need for occasional re-factorization); platform details and timing breakdowns are sparse; reference formatting is uneven.

Quality
Technically sound overall regarding the Cholesky update machinery and algorithm design; the rank-one update is standard and the complexity claims are credible. The CSA approximation is plausible and borne out empirically, but the theoretical treatment is limited to an asymptotic argument without finite-time guarantees. The incorrect statement about eigenvalues should be corrected. Experiments support the claim of equal evaluation counts and large speedups, but could be strengthened by reporting success rates, sensitivity to decomposition delays in the baseline, and additional comparisons (e.g., to LM-CMA for very high d, acknowledging different modeling power).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The background on CMA-ES and CSA is helpful. Algorithms are given explicitly. However, a few places need correction/clarification: the eigenvalue claim, conditions for uniqueness in RQ/Cholesky (e.g., positive diagonal), and a brief discussion of numerical stability and possible periodic re-cholesky. Figures are referenced but not shown here; ensure they are legible with error bars and report medians plus spread. References require cleanup.

Originality
Extends known ideas (Cholesky updates, (1+1)-CMA-ES) to the full CMA-ES with rank-µ and addresses the key blocker (CSA normalization) via a novel approximation. While incremental, it is a meaningful contribution for practitioners who require full covariance adaptation.

Significance
High practical significance for derivative-free optimization with full covariance models. Reported speedups can materially impact applications in RL and hyperparameter tuning when evaluation cost is moderate and d is large. The approach is likely to be adopted in libraries. The lack of stronger theory is acceptable for a performance-focused contribution, but the paper should correct the factual error and bolster experimental fairness.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please correct the claim about eigenvalues of C being given by diag(A)^2; propose instead to monitor conditioning via eigendecomposition at low frequency or Gershgorin bounds, or clarify what statistic you actually use.
- Report success rates (not only conditional medians) on Rosenbrock and others; include ECDFs over trials.
- Compare against a tuned reference CMA-ES with a realistic decomposition delay schedule; provide a CPU time breakdown (sampling, ranking, update, decomposition) and platform details.
- Provide a finite-horizon bound or at least an empirical study of the CSA path length bias induced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}, possibly on ill-conditioned and rapidly changing covariance regimes.
- Discuss numerical stability and whether you occasionally rebuild A from C or enforce positivity on the diagonal to avoid drift.
- Consider an ablation where you occasionally compute E_t (via polar decomposition) to “correct” the path or to validate the approximation online.

Pros
- Simple, implementable algorithm with clear speedups and no loss in evaluations.
- Optimal asymptotic update/storage complexity for full covariance CMA-ES.
- Empirical validation across dimensions and rotated non-separable functions.

Cons
- Incorrect claim about eigenvalues; needs correction.
- Theory for CSA approximation is asymptotic and qualitative; lacks bounds.
- Experimental baselines and reporting (success rates, fairness) can be improved.

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on correcting the eigenvalue statement and improving experimental reporting. The contribution is practical and useful, and the empirical gains are compelling, even if the theoretical guarantees are limited.

Confidence
Moderate.
