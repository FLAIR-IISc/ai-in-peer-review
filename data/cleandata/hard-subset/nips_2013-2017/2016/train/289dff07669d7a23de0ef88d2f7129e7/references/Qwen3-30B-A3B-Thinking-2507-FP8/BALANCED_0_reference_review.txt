

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the computationally expensive matrix square root operation with a Cholesky factorization. The standard CMA-ES has a runtime complexity of O(d^3) per iteration due to the need to compute the matrix square root, which becomes a bottleneck for high-dimensional problems. The authors argue that using the Cholesky factor instead of the matrix square root (while adjusting the step-size adaptation formula) can reduce the complexity to O(d^2) with minimal impact on performance. This builds on previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), but extends it to the full CMA-ES with theoretical justification.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES. The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 is particularly strong, showing that the error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} (where A is the Cholesky factor) diminishes as the algorithm converges. The empirical results are convincing, showing that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES while achieving significant runtime improvements (up to 20x faster for d=64). The paper is clearly written with well-structured algorithms and a thorough experimental setup. The authors also highlight important practical benefits, such as optimal storage complexity, free eigenvalue computation, and numerically stable matrix inversion.

**Weaknesses:**
The theoretical analysis, while strong for the step-size adaptation, doesn't fully address potential impacts on the covariance matrix adaptation itself. The paper assumes that the error in the step-size adaptation doesn't affect the overall optimization performance, but this could be more rigorously demonstrated. The numerical stability of the proposed method, particularly with respect to the approximation in the step-size adaptation, requires more detailed analysis. The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with other high-dimensional CMA-ES variants like Loshchilov (2015), which also addresses the computational bottleneck through dimensionality reduction. While the empirical results on benchmark functions are good, the paper would benefit from more challenging real-world optimization problems to demonstrate practical utility.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical justification is solid, and the experimental results are thorough. However, the paper could strengthen its quality by addressing potential numerical stability issues more explicitly and providing a more complete comparison with other high-dimensional optimization approaches.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with CMA-ES. The algorithms are clearly described, though the rankOneUpdate function (Algorithm 2) might benefit from a brief explanation of the underlying numerical method. The experimental setup is detailed and reproducible.

**Originality:** The core idea of using Cholesky factorization instead of matrix square root is not entirely novel (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015), but the authors provide a novel theoretical justification for why this approximation works for the standard CMA-ES. The extension to the full algorithm with proper convergence guarantees represents a meaningful contribution that advances the state of the art.

**Significance:** The work has significant practical importance for scaling CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems where maintaining a full covariance matrix is still computationally feasible. The runtime improvements could enable CMA-ES to be applied to more complex machine learning problems, such as training larger neural networks in direct policy search. However, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this approach compares to other dimensionality-reduction techniques for high-dimensional optimization, which limits the perceived significance for very large-scale problems.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. This is a solid, well-executed piece of work that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The theoretical justification is strong, the empirical results are convincing, and the practical benefits are clear. The paper addresses a real computational bottleneck that has been recognized in previous literature.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Expand the numerical stability analysis to include a more detailed discussion of potential error propagation.
2. Include a more comprehensive comparison with Loshchilov (2015) and other high-dimensional CMA-ES variants, particularly regarding the trade-off between runtime and optimization quality.
3. Consider adding a small section discussing the practical implementation challenges, such as handling numerical precision issues in the Cholesky decomposition.
4. Provide more insight into how the error in the step-size adaptation (beyond the asymptotic analysis) might affect optimization performance in non-asymptotic regimes.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable and well-executed improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm. The theoretical justification and empirical results are strong, and the practical benefits are significant for the machine learning community.
