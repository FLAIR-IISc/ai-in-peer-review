

# Review: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors provide a theoretical justification for why this approximation does not significantly deteriorate the algorithm's performance, particularly noting that the error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} (where A is the Cholesky factor) vanishes as the algorithm converges. The approach achieves optimal asymptotic runtime complexity O(µd^2) and storage complexity compared to the standard CMA-ES's O(d^3) and O(d^2) respectively. The authors empirically validate their approach on standard benchmark functions across multiple dimensions, demonstrating significant runtime improvements without increasing the number of function evaluations.

## Strengths

The paper makes a strong contribution to a well-established optimization algorithm with clear practical benefits. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 is well-constructed and provides valuable insight into why the approximation works, which is more substantial than previous attempts to reduce CMA-ES complexity. The authors properly acknowledge previous work by Suttorp et al. and Krause & Igel, while clearly differentiating their contribution through the theoretical justification.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar/Discus/Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers), various dimensions (d=4 to 256), and multiple variants of CMA-ES for comparison (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES). The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves similar optimization performance to the standard CMA-ES while providing substantial runtime improvements, especially as dimensionality increases (20x faster at d=64). The paper also correctly identifies the key advantages of the Cholesky factorization approach: optimal storage complexity, free eigenvalue computation, and numerically stable inverse computation.

## Weaknesses

While the theoretical justification is strong, it relies on the assumption that the covariance matrix converges to a certain shape as the algorithm approaches the optimum. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this assumption holds in practice, particularly for non-convex or ill-conditioned functions where convergence might be slow or non-existent. The authors mention that "the CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm, we cannot assume convergence of Ct" but don't elaborate on how their approximation behaves in such cases.

The empirical evaluation is limited to standard benchmark functions. For a NIPS paper, it would be valuable to see results on machine learning problems where CMA-ES is commonly applied (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, reinforcement learning policy search), as this would better demonstrate the practical significance of the approach. The paper mentions "training neural networks in direct policy search" in the conclusion but doesn't provide any evidence of this application.

The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the numerical stability of the Cholesky update process. While the authors mention "numerically stable computation of the inverse," they don't address potential issues with ill-conditioned matrices during the optimization process, which could impact the reliability of the approach.

The comparison to other low-complexity approaches (like diagonal approximations or limited-memory variants) is somewhat limited. While the paper acknowledges that "if the dimensionality gets so large that maintaining a full covariance matrix becomes computationally infeasible, one has to resort to low-dimensional approximations," it doesn't compare the trade-offs between their approach and these alternatives.

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid technical contribution that addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm. The paper demonstrates clear theoretical and empirical advantages over the standard CMA-ES implementation. While the theoretical justification is strong, the lack of evaluation on real-world machine learning tasks and insufficient discussion of numerical stability are notable weaknesses for a NIPS submission.

The paper's significance is clear for practitioners working with derivative-free optimization in high dimensions, but the impact on the broader machine learning research community might be limited without demonstration on ML problems. The approach is elegant and well-motivated, but the paper would benefit from more concrete evidence of its value beyond standard benchmarks.

## Recommendation

**Acceptance** - This is a high-quality paper with a clear theoretical contribution and strong empirical validation. The authors have made a meaningful improvement to a well-established algorithm with practical significance. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened with real-world ML applications, the paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the numerical stability discussion and to better contextualize the significance for the NIPS audience.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**
1. Add a brief section on numerical stability considerations for the Cholesky update process
2. Include at least one experiment demonstrating the approach on a machine learning task (e.g., hyperparameter optimization for a neural network)
3. Expand the discussion of the convergence assumption in Lemma 1 to address non-convergent cases
4. Add a more detailed comparison with other low-complexity CMA-ES variants (like the diagonal approach) to better contextualize the trade-offs

The paper's contribution is significant for the optimization community, and with these minor improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
