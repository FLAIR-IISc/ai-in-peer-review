### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a highly effective derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard algorithm's per-iteration complexity is dominated by the need to compute the square root of the covariance matrix, which scales poorly with the problem dimension `d`. The authors propose a new variant, the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which avoids this costly operation by directly updating a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This reduces the time complexity to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for a full-rank update.

The core challenge, and the paper's primary contribution, is in handling the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. Standard CSA requires the matrix `C^(-1/2)` to normalize steps, which is not directly available from a Cholesky factorization `C = AA^T`. Simply substituting `A^(-1)` introduces a rotational error. The authors' key insight is to provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) showing that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, this rotational error vanishes. This justifies their approximation, which replaces `C^(-1/2)` with `A^(-1)`. The paper provides strong empirical evidence on several benchmark functions, demonstrating that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations) as the standard CMA-ES but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, with the speed-up growing substantially with dimensionality.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high quality. The proposed algorithmic modification is clearly derived, and the central approximation is identified and justified. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using standard benchmark functions, appropriate baseline comparisons (including the state-of-the-art implementation with delayed updates), and clear metrics. The results robustly support the central claims of the paper: equivalent optimization power for a massive reduction in runtime. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 is not a full convergence proof, as it relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges. However, this is a very reasonable assumption in practice and provides a strong, principled motivation for why the approximation should work well, which is more than sufficient for a work of this nature.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining the relevant components of the standard CMA-ES, and precisely pinpointing the difficulty of using a Cholesky update for the step-size adaptation. The explanation of their solution and the accompanying theoretical argument is lucid. The algorithms are presented clearly, and the figures effectively convey the experimental results.

**Originality:** This is the paper's greatest strength. While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor to speed up ES variants has been explored before (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this work makes a crucial and novel contribution. It successfully adapts the technique to the modern, full-featured (µ,λ)-CMA-ES and, most importantly, provides the first compelling theoretical justification for why the necessary approximation in the step-size update does not harm performance. This moves the idea from a heuristic trick to a principled algorithmic modification. The insight that the rotational error introduced by the approximation diminishes as the algorithm converges is a significant conceptual leap that solves a long-standing practical issue.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. CMA-ES is a workhorse algorithm in many areas of machine learning, including reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization. Its primary limitation has always been its scaling with dimensionality. By providing a "drop-in" replacement that is demonstrably much faster without sacrificing performance, this paper has the potential for immediate and widespread impact. It makes CMA-ES a more viable tool for higher-dimensional problems where the function evaluation is relatively cheap—a common scenario. Researchers and practitioners who have been forced to use less powerful, restricted-covariance models or other heuristics can now use the full-power CMA-ES at a much lower computational cost. This is a genuinely useful contribution that advances the state of the art in a practical and demonstrable way.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty and a Bold Idea:** The paper presents an elegant solution to a well-known, difficult problem. The theoretical justification for the approximation, while not a complete proof, is a novel insight that makes the approach principled rather than purely heuristic.
2.  **Major Practical Impact:** The proposed algorithm provides a significant speed-up (e.g., 20x for d=64) for one of the most important black-box optimization algorithms, with no apparent downside in optimization performance. This is a result that the community can immediately adopt and benefit from.
3.  **Strong and Clear Empirical Validation:** The experiments are convincing and directly support the paper's claims across a range of standard benchmark problems.
4.  **Excellent Presentation:** The paper is clear, concise, and a pleasure to read.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Incomplete Theoretical Guarantee:** The justification for the core approximation relies on an assumption of convergence. A purist might argue that without a rigorous proof that the error term vanishes under all conditions, the method is not fully understood. However, I believe this viewpoint is overly conservative. The provided argument is strong, and the empirical results are overwhelming, making this a very minor weakness in the face of the paper's strengths.

### Recommendation

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. It is a perfect example of what a NIPS paper should be: it tackles an important problem, introduces a novel and clever idea, supports it with both theoretical reasoning and strong empirical evidence, and delivers a result of high practical significance. The authors have taken a known bottleneck in a cornerstone algorithm and essentially removed it, paving the way for its application to a wider range of problems. While the theoretical justification is based on a convergence assumption, this is a bold and well-reasoned approach, and the flawless empirical results provide all the validation one could reasonably ask for. This is excellent work that will be of great interest and utility to the community.
