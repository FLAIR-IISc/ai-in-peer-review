

# Review: "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary
This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root operation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors address a key bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES algorithm, where computing the matrix square root dominates runtime with O(d²) complexity per iteration. Their approach maintains the full covariance matrix adaptation while achieving optimal asymptotic runtime complexity of O(µd²) (with µ typically O(log d)) and optimal storage complexity. The paper provides theoretical justification for why the approximation (replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}) does not deteriorate performance, supported by empirical results showing significant speedups without increasing function evaluations.

This work builds upon previous attempts to reduce CMA-ES complexity (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015), but makes a crucial theoretical contribution by providing justification for why their approach doesn't harm performance, unlike previous attempts that didn't address this issue. It relates to the broader literature on efficient evolutionary algorithms (e.g., Ros and Hansen, 2008; Loshchilov, 2015) but makes a specific contribution to the computational efficiency of full covariance matrix adaptation.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) is well-motivated and provides a solid foundation for the proposed approximation. The authors carefully address the challenge of step-size adaptation (equation 3), which is the main obstacle to using Cholesky factorization directly. Their argument that the error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} diminishes as the algorithm converges is compelling, particularly given the exponential weighting in the cumulative step-size adaptation.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, using standard benchmark functions across multiple dimensions (d = 4 to 256) with 100 trials per function. The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES-Ref but achieves significantly faster wall-clock times (up to 20x faster for d=64). The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the cache effects observed at d=256, and discuss the trade-offs when dimensions become too large for full covariance matrix adaptation.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section clearly explains the CMA-ES algorithm and the computational bottleneck. The theoretical justification is presented with sufficient mathematical rigor without becoming overly dense. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and would allow for easy implementation. The experimental setup is clearly described, including the benchmark functions, dimensionality range, and evaluation metrics.

The figures effectively communicate the key results, particularly Figure 2 showing the speedup increasing with dimensionality. The supplementary material on rotation invariance is a nice touch that adds depth to the empirical evaluation.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by extending previous Cholesky-based approaches (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) to the standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification. Previous attempts either restricted to diagonal matrices (Ros and Hansen, 2008) or used non-triangular Cholesky factors (Suttorp et al., 2009), but the authors are the first to provide a theoretical foundation for why their approximation doesn't degrade performance.

The key innovation is recognizing that the error introduced by the approximation diminishes as the algorithm converges (Lemma 1), which is a crucial insight that previous work lacked. This theoretical justification elevates the work from a practical optimization to a principled contribution that advances our understanding of CMA-ES.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. CMA-ES is a widely used algorithm for real-valued optimization in machine learning, reinforcement learning, and hyperparameter tuning. The computational bottleneck of O(d²) per iteration has been a known limitation, and this paper provides a theoretically grounded solution that achieves optimal asymptotic complexity without compromising performance.

The empirical results demonstrate practical impact: for d=64, Cholesky-CMA-ES is 20x faster than standard CMA-ES-Ref, with the speedup increasing with dimensionality. This makes CMA-ES more applicable to large-scale optimization problems where maintaining the full covariance matrix is still feasible (e.g., training neural networks in direct policy search). The paper also provides additional benefits: free eigenvalue computation, numerically stable inversion, and optimal storage complexity.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical justification for the approximation
- Thorough empirical evaluation across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions
- Clear presentation of the algorithm and its properties
- Practical benefits beyond just speed (eigenvalue computation, numerical stability)
- Addresses a well-known bottleneck in a widely-used optimization algorithm

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical justification assumes Ct converges to a certain shape, though the authors acknowledge CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm. A bit more discussion on this assumption would strengthen the argument.
- Limited to standard benchmark functions; more complex, real-world optimization problems would further demonstrate practical impact.
- Doesn't compare with other state-of-the-art large-scale optimization methods (e.g., L-BFGS, other low-rank approximations).

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. It provides a theoretically grounded solution to a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES with clear practical benefits. The quality of the theoretical analysis, the thoroughness of the experimental evaluation, and the significance of the problem addressed all meet NIPS standards.

While the paper has minor limitations (theoretical assumptions, scope of experiments), these don't detract from the core contribution. The work is clearly differentiated from previous literature, and the authors have carefully addressed the limitations of their approach.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2023. This paper provides a valuable improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm that will be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning. The Cholesky-CMA-ES variant is likely to see immediate adoption in practice due to its simplicity and significant performance benefits.
