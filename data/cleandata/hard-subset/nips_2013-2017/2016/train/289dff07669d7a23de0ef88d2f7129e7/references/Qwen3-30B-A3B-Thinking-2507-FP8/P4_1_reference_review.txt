

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes an efficient variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) called Cholesky-CMA-ES. The authors address the computational bottleneck in CMA-ES where each iteration requires computing the matrix square root of the covariance matrix, which dominates runtime for large dimensions. While previous approaches have explored using Cholesky factorization (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015), these methods were limited to specific CMA-ES variants. The authors extend this to the standard CMA-ES, providing theoretical justification for why replacing the matrix square root with a Cholesky factorization does not significantly degrade performance, while achieving optimal asymptotic runtime complexity (O(μd²) vs O(d³)) and storage complexity.

The paper makes several key contributions: (1) a theoretical analysis showing the error introduced by the approximation diminishes as the algorithm converges (Lemma 1), (2) a complete algorithmic implementation (Algorithm 1) with a rank-one update procedure (Algorithm 2) for maintaining the Cholesky factor, and (3) empirical validation showing equivalent function evaluation counts with significantly reduced wall-clock time (up to 20x faster for d=64).

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical justification for the approximation (Lemma 1), demonstrating that the introduced error vanishes as the algorithm converges. This is a significant improvement over previous attempts that lacked such justification.
2. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical notation, detailed algorithm descriptions, and appropriate background on CMA-ES. The connection between the Cholesky factorization and the standard CMA-ES is logically explained.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental section uses appropriate benchmark functions across multiple dimensions (4-256), with 100 trials per function. The results clearly demonstrate equivalent performance in terms of function evaluations while showing substantial runtime improvements.
4. **Practical Impact:** The paper addresses a real computational bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm, providing a practically useful improvement that maintains the algorithm's performance characteristics.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Dimensionality Scope:** The experiments stop at d=256, but the paper claims the method is beneficial for "large optimization problems." It would be stronger to include results for larger dimensions where cache effects might become more pronounced.
2. **Theoretical Complexity:** While the paper claims O(μd²) complexity, the authors note this assumes naive matrix multiplication. For completeness, the paper should briefly discuss how faster matrix multiplication algorithms might affect the complexity.
3. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper references Loshchilov (2015) on low-dimensional approximations but doesn't explicitly compare Cholesky-CMA-ES with these methods. Given the paper's conclusion about "scaling up CMA-ES to large optimization problems," a direct comparison with recent low-rank methods would strengthen the significance argument.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a theoretically justified, practical improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm
- Demonstrates significant runtime improvements without sacrificing optimization performance
- Solves a clear computational bottleneck with optimal asymptotic complexity
- Well-written with clear presentation of algorithms and results
- Addresses a practical problem with meaningful impact on real-world applications

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The runtime improvement is limited to problems where full covariance matrix is feasible (quadratic scaling)
- The paper doesn't directly compare with the most recent low-rank CMA-ES variants
- The theoretical analysis, while sound, is limited to the convergence regime

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization by providing an efficient implementation of CMA-ES that maintains performance while significantly reducing computational requirements. The theoretical justification for the approximation is particularly compelling and addresses a key limitation in previous approaches. The experimental results robustly support the claims of improved efficiency without performance degradation.

The paper is clearly within NIPS scope, as it addresses a fundamental algorithmic challenge in optimization, which is relevant to machine learning applications including reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. The work advances the state of the art in optimization algorithms with demonstrable practical benefits.

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound, clearly written contribution that makes a meaningful improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm. The theoretical justification combined with empirical validation provides strong evidence for the approach's validity. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's significant contribution to the field.
