Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C=AA^T, rather than C itself or its symmetric square root. This enables O(µ d^2) time per iteration (with µ the effective selection size, typically O(log d)) and optimal O(d^2) storage while retaining full covariance adaptation. The key challenge is that standard CMA-ES step-size adaptation (CSA) uses C^{-1/2}; directly replacing it with a Cholesky factor is nontrivial. The authors approximate CSA by using A^{-1} instead of C^{-1/2} and justify this via a convergence argument: under mild assumptions on the (normalized) covariance sequence, the orthogonal factor E in the RQ decomposition of C^{1/2} converges, implying the rotational discrepancy between A^{-1} and C^{-1/2} vanishes asymptotically. They provide a rank-one update routine for A, derive an algorithm with O(µ d^2) updates, and empirically show large wall-clock speedups over a strong CMA-ES reference (with delayed decompositions) and over a non-triangular Cholesky-factor approach (Suttorp et al., 2009), while requiring essentially the same number of function evaluations on rotated benchmark functions up to d=256. They also discuss practical advantages: optimal storage, O(d^2) triangular inverses, and “eigenvalues for free”.

Relation to prior work
This builds on multiple lines: (i) speeding up CMA-ES via delayed eigen-decompositions (Hansen), low-complexity/separable approximations (Ros & Hansen; Loshchilov), or NES variants; (ii) updating Cholesky factors instead of C for special CMA-ES variants ((1+1)-CMA-ES, MO-CMA-ES; Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). The novelty here is extending a fast triangular Cholesky update to the standard CMA-ES with CSA, together with a theoretical argument explaining why using A^{-1} in CSA should not impair performance and an empirical demonstration that full-covariance performance is preserved while runtime is drastically reduced. The paper situates itself well in this landscape.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The covariance update via rank-one updates to A is well-motivated and grounded in prior work. The CSA approximation is justified by Lemma 1, which is mathematically reasonable under the stated convergence assumption on the normalized covariance shape; the authors are appropriately cautious that CMA-ES is stochastic and formal convergence of C is not guaranteed, but the heuristic argument (small, smooth rotations plus exponential forgetting) is plausible and consistent with experiments.
- Important correction: The paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is incorrect in general; Cholesky diagonals are not eigenvalue square roots except for diagonal/eigen bases. Please correct this; you can still obtain the spectrum via an eigendecomposition of C when needed, but not “for free” from diag(A).
- Invariance: Standard CSA is already not proved affine-invariant; replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} further perturbs rotational invariance, especially in transient phases when C changes quickly. You measure rotation changes (supplement only), but a more direct analysis or ablation would strengthen claims.
- Numerical stability: Repeated rank-one updates can accumulate roundoff. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of conditioning, whether occasional re-factorization is needed, and whether you explicitly invert A (discouraged) or use triangular solves for A^{-1}·v in CSA. Algorithm 1 suggests A^{-1} is used; please clarify that you avoid explicit inversion in the critical path.
- Experiments: The study is extensive on classical noiseless, rotated testbeds and convincingly shows same evaluations with large wall-clock gains (e.g., ~20× at d=64). However, results exclude non-converged Rosenbrock runs (introducing bias), and do not test noisy/constraints/multimodal landscapes or expensive objectives (where time savings may be less relevant). Statistical significance for “same evaluations” should be reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with helpful algorithms. Some issues:
- The incorrect Cholesky/eigenvalues statement should be fixed.
- Reference formatting is broken in several entries; please clean up.
- Include more details on implementation choices (triangular solve vs inversion, BLAS levels), reconditioning strategy, and parameter settings.
- Since figures/tables are central to the empirical claims, ensure they are self-contained and that Table 1 is present. Avoid excluding failures without reporting failure rates.

Originality
Extending fast triangular Cholesky updates to standard CMA-ES with CSA and providing a plausible theoretical justification for replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} appears novel. Prior work either used non-triangular factors (with extra degrees of freedom and no convergence control) or applied Cholesky updates only to special CMA-ES variants.

Significance
Practically significant: for cheap-to-evaluate or high-dimensional problems where decomposition dominates runtime, the reported speedups are compelling and likely to impact practitioners. Maintaining full covariance at optimal asymptotic time/storage is valuable when separable/low-rank surrogates underperform. Integration into an open-source library (Shark) increases potential adoption. The theoretical argument, while not a full proof, increases confidence that the approximation does not harm optimization performance.

Pros
- Clear, useful algorithmic contribution with optimal asymptotic time/storage for full-C covariance updates.
- Sensible theoretical justification for CSA approximation.
- Strong empirical evidence of large runtime savings with no increase in evaluations on standard rotated benchmarks.
- Good positioning relative to prior work; maintains full covariance unlike low-rank/diagonal variants.

Cons
- Incorrect claim about Cholesky diagonals and eigenvalues.
- Only heuristic/convergence-in-expectation justification for CSA; no quantitative bound on bias or invariance loss; limited empirical probing of worst-case transients.
- Exclusion of failed runs on Rosenbrock; missing tests on noisy/expensive/constraint/multimodal problems.
- Limited discussion of numerical stability and whether occasional re-factorization is needed.

Suggestions/Questions
- Correct the Cholesky/eigenvalue statement and clarify how you monitor conditioning (e.g., from A or C).
- Provide an ablation that stresses rapid rotations (e.g., synthetic time-varying rotations) to probe CSA accuracy early.
- Report failure rates, significance tests, and experiments with noisy functions. Include at least one expensive-objective setting to show overhead reduction in absolute terms.
- Clarify whether A^{-1} is formed explicitly or via triangular solves; discuss reconditioning frequency and stability.
- Compare more extensively to LM-CMA/low-rank methods at higher d to delineate regimes where full-C Cholesky-CMA-ES remains preferable.

Recommendation
Accept (clear practical impact, solid idea, convincing experiments), contingent on correcting the Cholesky/eigenvalue claim and clarifying numerical/implementation details. Confidence: high.
