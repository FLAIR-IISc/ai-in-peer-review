### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the high computational complexity of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES has a per-iteration complexity of at least Ω(d³) due to the need to compute the square root of the d x d covariance matrix for sampling and step-size adaptation. This cost can be prohibitive for problems with high dimensionality or inexpensive objective functions.

The authors propose a new variant, the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which reduces this complexity to O(µd²), where µ is the parent population size (typically O(log d)). This is achieved by maintaining and directly updating the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than the matrix itself. While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor has been explored previously for simpler evolution strategies, a key challenge for the standard CMA-ES has been the step-size adaptation mechanism (CSA), which requires the matrix inverse square root C⁻¹ᐟ². A Cholesky factor A (where C = AAᵀ) does not directly provide C⁻¹ᐟ².

The main contribution of this paper is to propose a well-justified approximation for the CSA update, replacing C⁻¹ᐟ² with A⁻¹. The authors provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) that the error introduced by this approximation vanishes as the shape of the covariance matrix converges during the optimization process. They argue that the uniqueness of the triangular Cholesky decomposition ensures that the sequence of rotation matrices relating C¹ᐟ² and A converges, making the approximation increasingly accurate.

The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on a set of standard benchmark functions. The results demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same convergence performance as the reference CMA-ES implementation in terms of the number of function evaluations. However, it is significantly faster in wall-clock time, with the speed-up becoming more pronounced as the problem dimension increases.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is clearly described, and the core approximation is supported by both a theoretical plausibility argument and strong empirical results. The experimental setup is sound, using standard benchmarks, rotation invariance tests, and relevant baselines, including the standard implementation and another Cholesky-based variant. The claims of achieving optimal asymptotic complexity (for a full-matrix approach) without sacrificing optimization performance are well-supported by the evidence provided.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work in the context of prior art. The background on CMA-ES is concise and sufficient. The core contribution in Section 3, including the description of the approximation and its justification via Lemma 1, is presented with remarkable clarity. The experimental results are displayed effectively, making the performance benefits of the proposed method immediately apparent.

**Originality:** While the idea of using Cholesky updates to accelerate evolution strategies is not new (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this work makes a novel and important contribution. It successfully adapts the technique to the *standard*, state-of-the-art CMA-ES, which previous attempts had not managed due to the difficulty of handling the step-size adaptation. The key novelty lies in the proposed approximation for the CSA mechanism and the theoretical argument that justifies it. This successfully resolves a known challenge and represents a significant advance over prior work.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. CMA-ES is a foundational algorithm in black-box optimization with numerous applications in machine learning, such as policy search in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization. Its cubic complexity has been a major limiting factor for its application to higher-dimensional problems. This paper provides a practical and effective solution that significantly extends the applicability of CMA-ES by reducing the complexity to quadratic. The Cholesky-CMA-ES is likely to be widely adopted and could become the new standard implementation for medium- to large-scale problems where a full covariance matrix is still feasible.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Solves a long-standing and practical computational bottleneck in a widely-used and important optimization algorithm.
2.  The proposed method achieves asymptotically optimal time and space complexity for a full-covariance update.
3.  Strong empirical evidence shows a significant wall-clock time speed-up without any degradation in sample efficiency (number of function evaluations).
4.  The core technical contribution is a novel and well-justified approximation that elegantly handles the difficult step-size adaptation component.
5.  The paper is very clearly written, making a technically detailed contribution accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) relies on the assumption that the shape of the covariance matrix converges, which is a strong assumption for a stochastic algorithm and is not formally proven to hold in the general case.
2.  The work is an incremental improvement on the general idea of using Cholesky updates for ES, although it is a very significant and impactful increment.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a complete, well-validated, and highly significant contribution to the field.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 is convincing but rests on the assumption that `C̄_t` converges. While the authors acknowledge this is not guaranteed, the argument could be further strengthened with empirical evidence.
*   **Suggestion:** Could you empirically validate the convergence of the rotation matrices? For example, a plot showing the Frobenius norm `||E_t E_{t-1}^T - I||_F` over iterations for a typical run would provide direct evidence supporting your central theoretical claim that the error from the approximation diminishes over time.

The paper effectively demonstrates the superiority of the proposed method over the reference implementation and a delayed-update strategy. The comparison with Suttorp-CMA-ES is also valuable.
*   **Suggestion:** For completeness, it would be helpful to briefly elaborate on the "non-triangular Cholesky factorization" used by Suttorp et al. (2009) and explain more explicitly why the non-uniqueness of that factorization is problematic (i.e., how it allows for non-converging sequences of `E_t` even for a constant `C_t`). This would further highlight the importance of using a unique triangular decomposition as you have done.

The conclusion accurately positions the work as an improvement for scaling CMA-ES to problems where a full covariance matrix is still storable and computationally manageable, distinguishing it from limited-memory approximations for even larger scales. This is a well-balanced assessment of the contribution's scope.

This is an excellent paper presenting a valuable and practical contribution. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES represents a significant step forward for the field of derivative-free optimization.
