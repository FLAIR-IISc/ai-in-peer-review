The paper proposes a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aimed at reducing runtime and memory complexity by utilizing the Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root during covariance matrix adaptation. The authors provide a theoretical justification for the approach, arguing it doesn’t deteriorate performance, and present empirical results demonstrating speedups, particularly in higher dimensions.

While the core idea of leveraging the Cholesky decomposition is sensible and aligns with existing work attempting to optimize CMA-ES computations (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), the presentation and justification are riddled with issues. The introduction provides a reasonable overview of CMA-ES and its computational bottlenecks, but the motivation for focusing *specifically* on the step-size adaptation and its interaction with the Cholesky factor is not sufficiently compelling. Many components of CMA-ES contribute to runtime, and focusing solely on this aspect feels somewhat arbitrary.

The theoretical justification is weak and relies heavily on Lemma 1, which, while technically correct, feels like a post-hoc rationalization rather than a fundamental driver of the method. The argument that the error introduced by replacing `st` with `ŝt` diminishes due to the rotation matrix converging isn't robustly demonstrated, and the caveat about requiring `Ct` to converge feels like a significant limitation given the exploratory nature of CMA-ES. The explanation lacks mathematical rigor; asserting that coordinate changes are “small” requires quantification, and the impact of these changes on the CSA update needs a more detailed analysis. The authors claim that “only the last few coordinate systems matter,” which feels intuitive but lacks formal proof. Furthermore, the statement that "the correction factor introduces a new random rotation matrix" is not entirely accurate; it introduces a transformation relative to the *current* factorization, not a completely independent rotation.

The experimental section is insufficient. While the claim of speedups is supported by the presented figures, the experimental setup lacks detail. The choice of benchmark functions is standard but doesn't adequately cover scenarios where the approximation might demonstrably fail. The comparison against CMA-ES-Ref and CMA-ES/d is reasonable, but the inclusion of Suttorp-CMA-ES feels tacked on and not fully integrated into the analysis. Crucially, the experiments appear to only focus on runtime and function evaluations. An analysis of the solution quality (e.g., final objective function value, convergence rate) is conspicuously absent. Showing equivalent performance in terms of *solution quality* is paramount given the claim of no performance deterioration. The statement that the matrix size exceeded the L2 cache at d=256 is concerning; this suggests that the implementation isn't optimized for large-scale problems and that the observed speedups might be heavily influenced by cache effects.  Moreover, the discussion of the additional benchmark functions is merely mentioned, without including any results - referencing supplementary material is not a substitute for including relevant findings in the main text.

The writing quality is uneven. While generally understandable, the paper suffers from imprecise language and a tendency towards hand-waving. Several statements are presented as facts without adequate justification. The notation is consistent but can be dense. 

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* **Weak Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical arguments are not sufficiently convincing and rely on assumptions that might not hold in practice.
* **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experimental setup is lacking in detail, and the analysis focuses solely on runtime and function evaluations, neglecting solution quality.
* **Imprecise Language and Lack of Rigor:** The paper contains imprecise statements and lacks mathematical rigor in several key areas.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The chosen benchmarks may not adequately represent the broad range of optimization problems for which CMA-ES is used.
* **Implementation Concerns:** The mention of cache limitations raises concerns about the scalability and practical relevance of the proposed method.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Weak):**

* **Novelty:** The approach of directly updating the Cholesky factor within the standard CMA-ES framework, while building on prior work, represents a novel combination of techniques.
* **Potential for Speedup:** The empirical results suggest that the Cholesky-CMA-ES can achieve significant speedups, particularly in higher dimensions.



**Overall Assessment:** Despite presenting a potentially useful optimization to the CMA-ES algorithm, the paper falls short of NIPS standards due to its weak theoretical foundation, incomplete experimental validation, and uneven writing quality. The authors have identified a practical problem but haven’t provided a sufficiently robust solution or compelling evidence of its benefits. I recommend **rejection**. Substantial revisions addressing the theoretical gaps and expanding the experimental analysis, with a particular focus on solution quality, would be necessary for reconsideration.
