

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

The paper proposes "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm that replaces matrix square root computations with Cholesky factor updates to reduce runtime complexity from O(d^3) to O(µd^2). The authors claim their approach maintains performance while achieving optimal storage complexity, with empirical results showing significant runtime improvements for larger dimensions.

## Strengths

The paper presents a theoretically motivated refinement to an established optimization algorithm. The theoretical argument (Lemma 1) that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with Cholesky factorization diminishes as the algorithm converges is sound and addresses a key concern about the approach. The algorithm is clearly presented with pseudocode (Algorithm 1), and the implementation details are well-specified. The authors have also added their implementation to the Shark library, which is a good practice for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

Despite the theoretical justification, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims and validation:

1. **Theoretical justification is insufficient**: The authors prove that the error vanishes asymptotically as the algorithm converges, but they fail to address how the error affects performance during the early iterations when the algorithm is most active. This is particularly problematic because the step-size adaptation (equation 3) is critical for convergence speed, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that the approximation error doesn't significantly impact the early optimization phases.

2. **Inadequate empirical validation**: The experiments are limited to standard benchmark functions up to dimension d=256. The paper doesn't test on real-world problems where CMA-ES is commonly applied (e.g., hyperparameter tuning in neural networks or reinforcement learning). The comparison against baselines is also limited - they compare against "CMA-ES-Ref" (a delayed update strategy) and "CMA-ES/d" (delaying updates for d steps), but fail to include a comparison against the standard CMA-ES implementation with the most common delayed update strategy (which is the default in Hansen's implementation).

3. **Overstated significance**: The paper claims "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity" but fails to acknowledge that the standard CMA-ES already uses a delayed update strategy (the "CMA-ES-Ref" variant they compare against) that achieves the same asymptotic complexity. The runtime improvement they demonstrate (20x faster at d=64) is impressive but doesn't address the practical question of whether this improvement is meaningful for problems where the covariance matrix is already too large to store (the very case they acknowledge where low-dimensional approximations would be needed).

4. **Lack of comprehensive comparison**: The paper compares against only three variants of CMA-ES, but the most relevant baseline is the standard implementation of CMA-ES with its default delayed update strategy. They claim their approach "reduces the runtime complexity of the algorithm with no significant change in the number of objective function evaluations," but they don't provide sufficient evidence that the number of function evaluations is truly identical across all problem types and dimensions.

5. **No analysis of numerical stability**: The paper claims "numerically stable computation" but provides no evidence of this. Cholesky factor updates can be numerically unstable if not implemented carefully, and the paper doesn't address this concern.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically interesting refinement to an established algorithm, but the claims of "optimal" complexity and performance are overstated. The theoretical justification is limited to the asymptotic behavior, and the empirical validation is insufficient to support the claims of maintaining performance while achieving significant runtime improvements. The paper would be significantly stronger with:
1) A more comprehensive comparison against the standard CMA-ES implementation with its default delayed update strategy
2) Experiments on real-world optimization problems where CMA-ES is commonly used
3) Analysis of the error introduced by the approximation during early iterations
4) Evidence of numerical stability

The paper makes a minor contribution to the optimization literature, but the claims of significant practical benefits are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. The approach is not fundamentally new (Suttorp et al. 2009 proposed similar ideas), and the incremental nature of the contribution doesn't justify the strong claims made in the paper.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper presents a theoretically motivated modification to an established algorithm, but the empirical validation is insufficient to support the claims of maintaining performance while achieving significant runtime improvements. The theoretical justification is limited to the asymptotic behavior, and the paper fails to address key concerns about the impact of the approximation error during the critical early phases of optimization. The contribution is incremental and not sufficiently validated against the most relevant baselines.
