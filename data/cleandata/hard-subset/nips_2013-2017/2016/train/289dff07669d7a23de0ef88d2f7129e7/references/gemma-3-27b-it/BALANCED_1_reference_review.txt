This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm, dubbed Cholesky-CMA-ES, which aims to reduce runtime and memory complexity by utilizing a Cholesky decomposition instead of the traditional square root of the covariance matrix. The authors provide a theoretical justification for why this approximation doesn't deteriorate performance and demonstrate empirical improvements, particularly in higher dimensions. This work builds upon prior attempts to avoid direct covariance matrix computation (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) and addresses limitations of these approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid theoretical argument for the validity of the Cholesky decomposition approach, specifically addressing potential issues related to step-size adaptation and coordinate system rotations (Lemma 1). This is a significant contribution, as prior work lacked such justification.
* **Complexity Analysis:** The paper clearly articulates the improved asymptotic complexity of the Cholesky-CMA-ES (O(µd<sup>2</sup>)), emphasizing its optimality for full covariance matrix adaptation.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate a substantial speedup, especially in higher dimensions, without sacrificing the number of function evaluations.  The comparison to CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, and Suttorp-CMA-ES provides a good benchmark.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper acknowledges potential limitations due to cache size at very high dimensions and discusses advantages like simplified inverse computation and eigenvalue access.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with a clear progression from introduction to experiments and conclusion.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in the Core Idea:** While the theoretical justification and complete implementation are novel, the fundamental concept of using Cholesky decomposition isn’t entirely new (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015).  The paper needs to more explicitly delineate *how* it meaningfully extends previous efforts beyond the theoretical grounding.
* **Experiment Setup Detail:** While the experimental setup is described, it lacks specific details on parameter tuning for each algorithm (beyond default CMA-ES parameters).  Were the parameters for the other algorithms carefully tuned to achieve their best possible performance? This could influence the reported speedup.
* **Focus on Runtime, Less on Robustness:** The emphasis is heavily on runtime speedup, with less discussion on the potential impact on robustness or convergence guarantees. Are there specific problem classes where the Cholesky-CMA-ES might be less reliable?
* **Lemma 1 Caveats:** The lemma's conclusion relies on the assumption of convergence of Ct. While acknowledged as not always guaranteed, a more in-depth discussion of the implications for non-convergent scenarios would be beneficial.
* **Reference Formatting:** A few references are missing complete information (e.g., year for Akimoto et al. citations).



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear theoretical foundation and supporting experimental results. The analysis appears careful and the authors acknowledge potential limitations. It's a complete piece of work, presenting a well-defined algorithm and thorough evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The authors do a good job of explaining the technical details of the Cholesky-CMA-ES and relating it to the standard CMA-ES.

**Originality:**  The originality lies primarily in the theoretical justification and complete implementation, rather than a completely novel algorithmic idea.  The authors should more strongly position their work relative to existing Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants.

**Significance:**  The potential impact of this work is significant. Reducing runtime complexity, especially in high-dimensional optimization problems commonly encountered in machine learning, is valuable. The algorithm could be readily adopted by practitioners and researchers using CMA-ES.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a rigorous theoretical justification for a practical optimization technique.
* Demonstrates significant speed improvements, particularly in higher dimensions.
* Addresses a key bottleneck (covariance matrix update) in CMA-ES.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Offers a potentially valuable tool for various applications, including reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The core idea builds on prior work, and the novelty primarily resides in the theoretical grounding.
* Experiment setup lacks detailed parameter tuning information for all algorithms.
* Limited discussion of potential robustness concerns.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The theoretical justification and empirical results are compelling. While the core idea isn't entirely novel, the authors have made a significant advancement by providing a strong theoretical foundation and demonstrating practical speedups. 

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance contingent on the following minor revisions:

1. **Strengthen Novelty Statement:**  More explicitly articulate how this work extends and improves upon prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES approaches.
2. **Experiment Details:** Provide more details on the parameter tuning process for each algorithm used in the experiments.
3. **Robustness Discussion:** Include a brief discussion of potential scenarios where the Cholesky-CMA-ES might exhibit reduced robustness, and consider including experiments to evaluate this.
4. **Reference Completion:**  Ensure all references are complete and accurately formatted.
