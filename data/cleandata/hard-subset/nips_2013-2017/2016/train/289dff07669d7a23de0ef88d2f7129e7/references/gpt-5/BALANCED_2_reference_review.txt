Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C (with C = A A^T) instead of computing the matrix square root or eigen-decomposition of C at each iteration. This aims to reduce the per-iteration computational cost from super-quadratic to O(µ d^2) while preserving the performance (in terms of number of function evaluations) of the standard CMA-ES. The main technical hurdle is the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), which in CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2}. The authors replace C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}, argue that the discrepancy amounts to a time-varying orthogonal rotation that vanishes asymptotically if the “shape” of C stabilizes, and support this with a lemma about the convergence of the RQ decomposition’s orthogonal factor. They provide a rank-one update routine for the triangular factor (Algorithm 2), claim asymptotically optimal time and storage complexities for full-covariance adaptation, and demonstrate substantial wall-clock speedups on standard test functions without a loss in evaluation efficiency. The approach builds on and extends prior work that updates factorizations rather than C directly (e.g., Suttorp et al. 2009; Krause & Igel 2015) and is positioned relative to low-rank/limited-memory CMA-ES (e.g., Ros & Hansen 2008; Loshchilov 2014/2015) which reduce model capacity.

Strengths:
- Practical impact: A clean modification yielding large wall-clock speedups while preserving evaluation counts on standard benchmarks.
- Conceptual contribution: Extends rank-one Cholesky updates from special CMA-ES variants to the mainstream CMA-ES, and provides a plausible theoretical rationale for the CSA approximation via orthogonal rotations.
- Complexity and implementation: The O(µ d^2) update and optimal storage for full covariance are attractive; maintaining A enables cheap triangular solves and avoids repeated decompositions.
- Empirics: Reasonable coverage of standard noiseless benchmarks; rotation tests are considered; consistent gains especially at higher d.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Incorrect claim about eigenvalues: The paper states that “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix… we get the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free.” This is false. The diagonal of the Cholesky factor are not related to the eigenvalues of C in general; eigenvalues of a triangular matrix equal its own diagonal, not those of C. This undermines a stated advantage and raises concerns about care in the presentation.
- Limited theory for CSA approximation: The lemma assumes convergence of the normalized covariance shape and gives only asymptotic reasoning; no finite-time error bounds on ||pσ|| are provided, nor conditions under which the approximation can degrade performance (e.g., rapidly changing covariance, multi-modal landscapes). The loss of affine invariance in CSA is not quantified.
- Experimental scope: Only noiseless, unconstrained functions up to d = 256; CMA-ES-Ref is not run at d = 256. Excluding non-converged Rosenbrock runs introduces selection bias. No BBOB-style comprehensive evaluation (including noise, constraints, restarts), no ablations (e.g., occasional exact C^{-1/2} updates), and no tests beyond 256D to characterize scaling limits.
- Comparisons: While Suttorp et al.’s non-triangular factor and delayed-decomposition CMA-ES are included, comparisons to limited-memory/full-covariance approximations (LM-CMA-ES, sep-CMA-ES) in settings where full covariance is beneficial but expensive would strengthen significance claims. Even if the goal is full covariance, practitioners will weigh speed vs. sample efficiency trade-offs.
- Numerical aspects: The paper does not analyze numerical stability of Algorithm 2 (e.g., accumulation of round-off, preservation of SPD under the scaled rank-one updates) or the conditioning of A and A^{-1} over long runs; details such as pivoting or re-factorization safeguards are not discussed.
- Reproducibility details: Wall-clock comparisons can be sensitive to BLAS/LAPACK, CPU cache, threading; the hardware and library configurations are not fully specified; code availability is mentioned indirectly (Shark), but exact experimental scripts are not clearly stated.

Quality:
- The algorithmic idea is sound and the empirical results are compelling. However, the theoretical justification is limited to asymptotic considerations under nontrivial assumptions, and there is at least one clear technical misstatement (eigenvalues claim). More rigorous analysis (e.g., bounds on the CSA norm error under bounded rotation drift) and careful numerical discussion would improve confidence.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and organized; the CSA approximation rationale is explained clearly. However, the erroneous eigenvalue claim should be corrected. Some reference formatting issues and missing implementation details detract from clarity.

Originality:
- Moderate to high. While Cholesky-based updates have been explored, adapting them to standard CMA-ES with a reasoned CSA approximation appears novel and practically useful.

Significance:
- High practical value for users of full-covariance CMA-ES, with substantial runtime savings without sacrificing evaluation efficiency on tested problems. Broader significance would be stronger with more comprehensive benchmarks and robustness analyses.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Correct the eigenvalue claim; clarify what is available “for free” (e.g., cheap triangular solves; determinant and log-determinant from diag(A); not eigenvalues).
- Provide finite-time analysis or empirical bounds for the CSA approximation error (e.g., measure ||E_t E_{t-1}^T − I|| over iterations and correlate with step-size adaptation drift).
- Include ablations: occasional exact C^{-1/2} updates to re-align; sensitivity to cσ, dσ; more challenging non-separable and rotated functions; noisy and constrained cases; higher dimensionalities.
- Add comparisons to LM-CMA-ES and diagonal/separable variants to contextualize when full covariance with Cholesky is preferable.
- Discuss numerical stability and SPD preservation; mention safeguards (e.g., periodic re-factorization, jitter, conditioning monitors).
- Improve reproducibility: specify hardware, BLAS/LAPACK versions, threading, compiler flags; release code and scripts.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear and useful engineering improvement with strong empirical speedups; preserves evaluation performance; straightforward to adopt.
- Novel extension of Cholesky updates to standard CMA-ES with a reasonable theoretical rationale.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incorrect technical claim about eigenvalues; limited theoretical guarantees for CSA approximation; experimental scope and comparisons could be broader; some methodological choices (excluding non-converged runs) may bias results.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is promising and likely valuable to the community, but the incorrect claim and the limited theoretical and experimental coverage need to be addressed. With these fixes, this work would be a strong candidate.
