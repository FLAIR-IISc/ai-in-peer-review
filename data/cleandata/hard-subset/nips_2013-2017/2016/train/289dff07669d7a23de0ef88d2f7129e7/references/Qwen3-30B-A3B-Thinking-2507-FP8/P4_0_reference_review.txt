

# Review: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

## Summary
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the standard matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization. The standard CMA-ES has O(d²) runtime complexity per iteration due to the matrix square root computation, which becomes problematic for high-dimensional optimization. The authors argue that by using the Cholesky factor A_t (where C_t = A_tA_t^T) instead of the matrix square root, they can reduce the runtime complexity to O(µd²) (with µ typically O(log d)), while maintaining similar performance. They provide theoretical justification (Lemma 1) for why replacing C_t^{-1/2} with A_t^{-1} in the step-size adaptation does not significantly deteriorate performance, especially as the algorithm converges. The paper includes experimental results demonstrating that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but achieves significant speedups in wall-clock time, with the speedup increasing as dimensionality increases.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical argument (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by replacing C_t^{-1/2} with A_t^{-1} vanishes as the algorithm converges. This addresses a key concern that was not adequately handled in previous work (e.g., Suttorp et al. [2009], Krause and Igel [2015]).

2. **Clear Experimental Design**: The experiments compare Cholesky-CMA-ES against multiple variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) across a range of benchmark functions and dimensions (d = 4 to 256). The results clearly demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same number of function evaluations as the reference implementation but with significantly reduced wall-clock time, particularly for higher dimensions.

3. **Practical Significance**: The paper addresses a genuine bottleneck in CMA-ES that becomes increasingly problematic as dimensionality increases. The speedup is substantial (20x faster at d=64) and scales with dimensionality, making it valuable for real-world applications like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning where CMA-ES is commonly used.

4. **Complete Presentation**: The paper provides all necessary algorithmic details (Algorithms 1 and 2), theoretical justification, and thorough experimental results. The implementation details (using Shark library with LAPACK) are sufficient for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scope of Experimental Validation**: While the experiments cover a range of standard benchmark functions, they don't include problems where covariance matrix adaptation is particularly critical (e.g., highly non-separable problems with strong correlations). The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't provide additional experiments to demonstrate performance on such problems.

2. **Numerical Stability Discussion**: The paper briefly mentions that the Cholesky approach provides "numerically stable computation" but doesn't provide empirical evidence or theoretical analysis of numerical stability compared to the standard method, which is important for practical implementation.

3. **Comparison with Recent Low-Rank Methods**: The paper mentions that for very high dimensions (d > 256), low-rank approximations might be necessary, but doesn't compare Cholesky-CMA-ES with recent low-rank variants like Loshchilov [2015] that also aim to reduce complexity. This limits the context of the contribution.

4. **Cache Effects at High Dimensions**: The paper notes that at d=256, the speedup diminishes due to cache effects, but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of this limitation or discuss how it might affect the practical applicability of the method.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:**
- Provides a theoretically justified approach to reducing the runtime complexity of CMA-ES from O(d²) to O(µd²) without sacrificing performance.
- Demonstrates significant practical speedups (20x at d=64) that scale with dimensionality.
- Addresses a genuine bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm.
- The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) is sound and fills an important gap in previous work.

**Con:**
- Limited experimental validation on problems where covariance adaptation is most critical.
- Insufficient discussion of numerical stability compared to the standard method.
- Does not compare with recent low-rank CMA-ES variants that also address complexity reduction.
- The diminishing speedup at very high dimensions (d=256) due to cache effects is not thoroughly analyzed.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization by providing an efficient variant of CMA-ES that addresses a practical bottleneck in the standard algorithm. The theoretical justification is rigorous, the experimental results are convincing, and the practical significance is clear. While there are some limitations in the scope of the experimental validation and the discussion of numerical stability, these do not undermine the core contribution.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims (Quality), clearly written with sufficient detail (Clarity), presents a novel approach with theoretical justification (Originality), and addresses a practical problem with significant real-world impact (Significance). The work is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS papers and related work, as the theoretical justification for the approximation is novel and fills an important gap.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a concrete, practical contribution to optimization algorithms that are widely used in machine learning applications. The method has the potential to be immediately useful for practitioners working with high-dimensional optimization problems.

**Recommendation: Accept**
