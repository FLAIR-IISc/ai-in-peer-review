Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C instead of maintaining C and recomputing C1/2 (via eigendecomposition). Sampling uses x = σAy + m, and the covariance update is decomposed into a sequence of O(d^2) rank-one updates to A (Algorithm 2). The cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), which in the standard CMA-ES uses C−1/2, is approximated by replacing C−1/2 with A−1, omitting the orthogonal factor E from the RQ decomposition of C1/2; a lemma informally justifies that the induced rotation error vanishes if the shape of C stabilizes. Experiments on standard test functions (with random rotations) suggest that the proposed method matches the function evaluation counts of CMA-ES and substantially reduces wall-clock time for moderate to high dimensions. Storage is reduced by a factor ~2 due to triangular factors.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on rank-one Cholesky updates for special CMA-ES variants (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), extending them to the standard CMA-ES with rank-μ. It compares against a baseline CMA-ES with delayed decompositions and a non-triangular “Suttorp-CMA-ES” adapted by the authors. Limited-memory and diagonal variants (Ros & Hansen 2008; Loshchilov 2014, 2015; Sun et al. 2013) are cited as orthogonal approaches.

Strengths
- Practical contribution: maintaining A via O(μd^2) rank-one updates avoids an O(d^3) decomposition, yielding clear runtime savings in the regime where full covariance adaptation is desirable.
- Conceptual simplicity: replaces expensive decompositions with standard cholupdate-like routines; sampling remains unchanged.
- Empirical evidence: consistent evaluation counts with large wall-clock speedups, increasing with d.
- Storage benefits: O(d^2) memory with a smaller constant factor (triangular).

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and theory
- Incorrect claim: “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. Cholesky diagonals are not eigenvalues unless C is diagonal in the chosen basis. This undermines claims about “eigenvalues for free” and “principal components” bounds.
- CSA approximation lacks quantitative guarantees. Lemma 1 assumes convergence of the normalized covariance shape C̄t and then concludes ETt−1Et → I. This is a very strong assumption (unjustified for the actual algorithm), and no error bound or rate is provided for ‖pσ‖ under non-stationary C. The argument is qualitative and does not constitute a convergence result for the modified CSA.
- Affine invariance is likely broken further by replacing C−1/2 with A−1. The paper acknowledges the standard CSA invariance is not proven; the proposed approximation removes the orthogonal factor entirely yet provides only anecdotal evidence of robustness.
- “Asymptotically optimal time complexity” is asserted without a formal lower bound. A plausible Ω(d^2) bound exists (at least to read/write C or A), but the paper does not prove it. The footnote about fast matrix multiplication contradicts the “optimal” wording.
- Numerical stability of Algorithm 2 is not analyzed. There is no discussion of conditioning, accumulation of round-off, or periodic re-triangularization/reorthogonalization. The update uses only positive β, which is fine here, but robustness in long runs is unaddressed.
- Using A−1 explicitly: the algorithm suggests computing A−1; one should avoid inversion and solve triangular systems for A−1v to improve stability and memory. The text notes that inverting a triangular matrix is O(d^2) but does not recommend the numerically preferable solve.

Methodological and experimental issues
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: The “CMA-ES-Ref” decomposition frequency formula seems garbled (“max{1, 110d(c1+cμ)}”), and the claim that it equals 1 for all tested d is doubtful and penalizes the baseline. Please clarify the exact schedule (as in the reference code) and use it faithfully.
- Success rates and censoring: For Rosenbrock, non-convergent trials are excluded rather than reported as failures; this biases the results. Success rates and time-to-target statistics with failures should be reported.
- Statistical rigor: No confidence intervals, statistical tests, or variance across seeds are reported for function evaluations or wall-clock time. Claims of “same evaluations” need quantitative support.
- Scope: The study is limited to a handful of functions; no comprehensive BBOB-style evaluation. The supplementary analysis of Et is referenced but not shown.
- Runtime reproducibility: Hardware, BLAS/LAPACK versions, compiler flags, and threading are not specified; cache effects are mentioned but not controlled. Provide full details and code.
- Complexity discussion ignores sampling cost O(λd^2) per iteration (matrix-vector products), which can dominate for larger λ. A breakdown of per-iteration costs would be helpful.

Presentation and clarity
- Numerous formatting/citation issues: broken references (e.g., “? \Q2012\E”), inconsistent citation years, stray macro artifacts, missing figures/tables here, and visible editorial placeholders (“th Conference ...”). These undermine readability.
- Notational inconsistencies: QR vs RQ decomposition switched; Σ = HH (missing transpose); inconsistent use of H/A/E; footnote text appears inline in odd places; indexing/order of At and At+1 in Algorithm 1 is confusing (pσ uses A−1t after At+1 was computed).
- Important constants not defined: expression for E{χ} not given; default parameter values (λ(d), μ, weights) not fully specified.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear practical speedups with full covariance CMA-ES while preserving evaluation efficiency.
- Simple, implementable idea likely useful for practitioners.
- Extends earlier Cholesky-update ideas to rank-μ.

Cons:
- One major technical error (eigenvalues from Cholesky diagonal) and several theoretical overclaims.
- Weak theoretical justification for CSA approximation; invariance impact not quantified.
- Experimental methodology has fairness and reporting issues; limited scope and missing details.
- Many presentation and formatting problems.

Questions for the authors
1) Please correct the eigenvalue claim and clarify what diagnostics you can obtain “for free” from A.
2) Can you provide a bound (even heuristic) on the CSA norm error when replacing C−1/2 with A−1 under bounded per-iteration changes of C?
3) What exact decomposition frequency did “CMA-ES-Ref” use? Can you rerun with the reference schedule and report success rates?
4) Did you compute A−1 explicitly, or perform triangular solves? If the former, what is the overhead and numerical impact?
5) Could you include a BBOB-style evaluation and report time-to-target with confidence intervals?

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea is practical and promising, but the paper contains a serious technical mistake (Cholesky diagonal ≠ eigenvalues), overstates theory (“optimal” complexity without proof; CSA justification under strong assumptions), and lacks rigor and clarity in experiments and presentation. With corrections, stronger theoretical support (or carefully circumscribed claims), fair baselines, and improved writing, this could be a valuable contribution.
