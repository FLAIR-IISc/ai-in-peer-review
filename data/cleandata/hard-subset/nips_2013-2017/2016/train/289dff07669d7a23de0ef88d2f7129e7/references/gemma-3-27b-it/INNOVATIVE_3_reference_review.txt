This paper presents a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky decomposition, aiming to reduce runtime and storage complexity. The core idea – utilizing the Cholesky factor directly within the CMA-ES adaptation steps – builds upon prior work exploring Cholesky factorizations for CMA-ES variants, but crucially provides a theoretical justification for its application to the *standard* CMA-ES and claims no significant performance degradation. The paper rigorously details the mathematical modifications and provides experimental results demonstrating significant speedups, particularly in higher dimensions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** This is a genuinely novel approach. While utilizing Cholesky decomposition within evolutionary strategies isn’t entirely new, applying it *directly* to the standard CMA-ES with a theoretical justification is a significant contribution. The authors directly address a known bottleneck in CMA-ES (matrix square root computation) and provide a plausible solution.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The paper isn’t merely an engineering hack; it provides a theoretical analysis (Lemma 1) attempting to explain why the approximation introduced by using the Cholesky factor instead of the matrix square root doesn't fundamentally break the algorithm’s convergence properties. This is a major plus.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, using a variety of benchmark functions and varying dimensionality. The reported speedups, particularly with increasing 'd', are compelling and support the claims made.  The comparison against CMA-ES-Ref and other Cholesky-based approaches adds further weight to the results.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the modifications to the algorithm. The presentation of the mathematical derivations is reasonably accessible.
* **Optimal Complexity:** Achieving optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity is a significant advantage, especially for high-dimensional problems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Approximation and Error Analysis:** The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) feels somewhat hand-wavy and relies on assumptions about convergence. A more rigorous error analysis quantifying the impact of replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor would be valuable. The authors acknowledge this error, but a deeper investigation into its practical effects would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experimental setup is good, the focus is primarily on standard benchmark functions.  Testing on real-world applications (e.g., reinforcement learning, hyperparameter optimization) would bolster the claims of practical significance. The limitations regarding CMA-ES-Ref only being tested up to d=128 should be acknowledged more strongly.
* **Cache Effects:** The authors acknowledge the influence of L2 cache size at d=256, but a more detailed discussion about the memory access patterns and potential optimizations to mitigate cache-related performance bottlenecks would be beneficial.
* **Algorithm 2 – Rank-One Update:** While presented, the complexity benefits of Algorithm 2 are not fully emphasized in the main text. A more direct comparison to the time complexity of the standard rank-one update would strengthen the argument.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **High Novelty:** The approach is genuinely novel and addresses a key limitation of a widely used algorithm.
* **Theoretical Justification:**  The authors provide a non-trivial attempt to theoretically justify their modifications, even if further refinement is desirable.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experimental results clearly demonstrate the performance benefits, particularly in higher dimensions.
* **Potential Impact:**  A significant speedup in CMA-ES could have a broad impact on various fields that rely on derivative-free optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Incomplete Error Analysis:** The lack of a more rigorous error analysis leaves some open questions about the algorithm’s robustness.
* **Limited Real-World Validation:** The absence of results on real-world applications reduces the immediate impact of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While the theoretical justification could be more rigorous and the empirical validation expanded, the demonstrated speedups and the thoughtful analysis outweigh these weaknesses. The paper presents a genuinely new idea, addresses a practical problem, and provides a solid foundation for future research. I strongly recommend acceptance, despite the minor flaws. The boldness of the approach and the clear potential benefits justify overlooking the lack of complete theoretical closure at this stage.
