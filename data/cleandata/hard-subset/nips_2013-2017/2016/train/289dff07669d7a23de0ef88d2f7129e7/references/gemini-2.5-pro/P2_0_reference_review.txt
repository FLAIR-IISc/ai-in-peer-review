### **Summary**

This paper addresses the well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), which stems from the O(d³) complexity of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix in each iteration. The authors propose a new variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, which directly updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This reduces the time complexity to O(µd²), which is asymptotically optimal for a full-rank update, and also reduces storage complexity to O(d²).

The key challenge in directly updating a Cholesky factor for the standard CMA-ES lies in the step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the matrix square root inverse (C⁻¹/²). Previous attempts to use Cholesky factors either applied to different CMA-ES variants or did not address this specific issue. The core contribution of this paper is to approximate C⁻¹/² with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor A⁻¹. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this approximation, arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this approximation diminishes. The paper supports its claims with strong empirical results on a suite of benchmark functions, demonstrating a significant reduction in wall-clock time compared to the standard CMA-ES, with no discernible loss in optimization performance (i.e., number of function evaluations).

### **Evaluation**

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is sound and well-motivated.

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The main technical contribution is the justification for approximating C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹. The authors correctly identify that the difference between the two is an orthogonal matrix E (from the RQ-decomposition C¹/² = AE). Their argument, formalized in Lemma 1, is that if the normalized covariance matrix converges, then the corresponding rotation matrices E_t will also converge. This implies that the rotational error between successive steps (E_t E_{t-1}ᵀ) approaches the identity, and due to the exponential weighting in the step-size adaptation path, the accumulated error in the norm of p_σ becomes negligible. This is a clever and convincing argument that provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approximation, which is the cornerstone of the paper.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their Cholesky-CMA-ES against appropriate baselines: the reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) which updates every step, a variant with delayed updates (CMA-ES/d), and an alternative Cholesky-based approach (Suttorp-CMA-ES). The choice of benchmark functions is standard and covers a range of properties (e.g., separable, non-separable, ill-conditioned), providing a comprehensive test of the algorithm's behavior. The results are presented clearly and strongly support the central claims: the number of function evaluations is nearly identical to the reference implementation, while the wall-clock time is dramatically reduced, especially in higher dimensions.
*   **Completeness:** The work is a complete and self-contained contribution. It identifies a clear problem, proposes a novel solution, provides theoretical justification, and validates it empirically. The authors are also careful to note the limitations (e.g., the method still scales quadratically and is not intended for extremely large `d` where low-rank approximations are necessary).

#### **Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

*   The introduction effectively sets up the problem and contextualizes the proposed solution within existing literature.
*   The background section on CMA-ES is concise but sufficient to understand the core mechanics and the specific challenge posed by the C⁻¹/² term in the CSA update.
*   Section 3, which presents the Cholesky-CMA-ES, is the highlight. It explains the problem with the direct substitution of A⁻¹ for C⁻¹/² and then builds the theoretical argument (Lemma 1) to justify why this approximation is valid in practice. This section is logically structured and easy to follow.
*   The algorithms are presented in pseudocode, which aids reproducibility. The experimental setup and results are also described with sufficient detail.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to explicitly state that Lemma 1's proof relies on the continuity of the RQ-decomposition, which is a key step in the argument.

#### **Originality**

The work is original and makes a significant new contribution to the field. While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix is not entirely new (as the authors correctly cite, e.g., Suttorp et al. 2009), this paper's contribution is novel in two key respects:

1.  It successfully applies this idea to the *standard*, state-of-the-art CMA-ES algorithm by providing a principled solution to the CSA step-size update problem, which had previously been a major obstacle.
2.  The theoretical justification via the convergence of the RQ-decomposition's orthogonal component is a novel insight that elegantly explains why the approximation works without degrading performance. This is a distinct and more satisfying approach than simply proposing an ad-hoc modification.

The paper does an excellent job of citing relevant prior work and clearly articulating how its approach differs and improves upon previous methods.

#### **Significance**

The significance of this work is high. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization with widespread applications in machine learning (e.g., reinforcement learning, hyperparameter tuning). Its primary drawback has always been its computational complexity in higher dimensions.

*   This paper provides a "drop-in" replacement for the core computational step of CMA-ES that makes it substantially faster. This significantly extends the range of problem dimensions where the full-covariance CMA-ES can be practically applied, especially in scenarios where function evaluations are relatively cheap.
*   Researchers and practitioners are highly likely to adopt this method. It offers a "free lunch": a massive speed-up with no apparent downside in terms of optimization performance. Given that the authors have implemented this in a public library (Shark), its adoption is further facilitated.
*   By making a powerful, general-purpose optimizer more efficient, this work has the potential to accelerate research and application in numerous areas that rely on black-box optimization. It is a clear and demonstrable advancement of the state of the art for this class of algorithms.

### **Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
*   Addresses a critical and long-standing performance bottleneck in a widely used and important optimization algorithm.
*   Proposes a computationally efficient solution (Cholesky-CMA-ES) with optimal asymptotic time and space complexity for a full-rank method.
*   Provides a novel and sound theoretical justification for the key approximation made in the step-size adaptation mechanism.
*   Demonstrates compelling empirical evidence of a significant speed-up (e.g., 20x at d=64) without sacrificing optimization performance.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly structured, and properly contextualized within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The theoretical argument hinges on the convergence of the covariance matrix shape, which is a reasonable assumption for the algorithm's behavior near an optimum but is not formally proven to hold in general. However, this is a very minor point, as proving such convergence for the full CMA-ES is an extremely difficult open problem. The provided justification is more than sufficient for a conference paper.

### **Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant practical and theoretical contribution. It solves a well-known problem with an elegant and well-justified approach, backed by strong empirical results. The work is of high quality, clarity, originality, and significance. It is a clear advancement for the field of derivative-free optimization and will be highly valuable to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
