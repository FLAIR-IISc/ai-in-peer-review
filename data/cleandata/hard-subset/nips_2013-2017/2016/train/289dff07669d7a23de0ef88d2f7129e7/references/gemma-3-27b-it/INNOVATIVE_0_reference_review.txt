This paper presents a Cholesky decomposition-based variant of the CMA-ES algorithm, aiming to reduce runtime and storage complexity, particularly for high-dimensional problems. The core idea – leveraging the Cholesky factor directly in the adaptation process instead of repeatedly computing the matrix square root – is a natural and potentially impactful extension of existing work (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). The theoretical justification, attempting to address the potential error introduced by abandoning the exact inverse square root correction in the step-size adaptation, is a key strength. The empirical validation demonstrates a significant speedup, especially as dimensionality increases, without sacrificing performance in terms of function evaluations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** While building on prior work using Cholesky decomposition with CMA-ES, this paper tackles the full-covariance adaptation scenario, a significant extension. The theoretical attempt to justify the approximation introduced by avoiding the inverse square root correction in step-size adaptation is particularly novel.
* **Significance:** The potential for substantial speedups in high-dimensional optimization is significant, particularly for applications where CMA-ES is a competitive choice, like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. Reducing both runtime *and* memory footprint is valuable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the modifications to the standard CMA-ES algorithm. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and 2 makes the implementation details accessible.
* **Soundness:** The authors provide a reasonable theoretical argument for the convergence of the modified algorithm, grounding the approximation in the asymptotic behavior of the covariance matrix adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Scope:** While the experiments demonstrate speedup, the benchmark functions are relatively standard.  More challenging, real-world problems (e.g., those arising in machine learning model training) would strengthen the validation. A more detailed analysis of where the speedup is most pronounced (problem structure, dimensionality) would also be valuable.
* **Theoretical Gap:** The Lemma 1 offers some justification, but the approximation made in equation (5) is a significant deviation from the original CMA-ES. The assertion that the error vanishes relies on the convergence of *Ct*, which isn’t guaranteed in all scenarios. A more rigorous analysis of the error bound, or at least a discussion of scenarios where the approximation might be less effective, would be beneficial.
* **Comparison Depth:** The comparison to Suttorp-CMA-ES is a bit superficial. A more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the different Cholesky-based approaches (this work vs. Suttorp et al.) would be insightful.
* **Cache Effects:** The acknowledgment of cache effects at d=256 feels somewhat dismissive. A more detailed investigation of the scaling behavior with respect to memory and cache size would be helpful, perhaps exploring techniques to mitigate these effects.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practical bottleneck in CMA-ES – the computational cost of covariance matrix adaptation – with a novel and theoretically motivated approach.
* The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating significant speedups without compromising performance.
* The work opens up new possibilities for applying CMA-ES to even higher-dimensional problems.
* The paper’s focus on algorithmic efficiency aligns well with the spirit of NIPS.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The theoretical justification, while reasonable, isn't entirely airtight and leaves room for concern about the approximation’s impact on convergence in certain scenarios.
* The empirical evaluation, while positive, lacks the breadth to fully convince the reader of the method’s robustness.
* The discussion of limitations (cache effects, error analysis) is somewhat cursory.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While the theoretical justification isn't perfect and the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the significant speedups achieved and the clear presentation of the ideas warrant acceptance. I value the boldness of the approach and the authors' willingness to tackle a challenging practical problem. The minor flaws can be addressed in future work. I lean towards **Accept**.
