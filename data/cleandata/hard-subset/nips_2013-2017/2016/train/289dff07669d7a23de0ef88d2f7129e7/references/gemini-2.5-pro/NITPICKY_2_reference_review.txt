**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aimed at reducing its computational complexity. The standard CMA-ES has a complexity that is at least quadratic in the problem dimension `d`, dominated by the need to decompose the covariance matrix `C` in every iteration to obtain its square root `C^(1/2)`. The authors propose to maintain and update the Cholesky factor `A` (where `C = AA^T`) directly, which can be done in O(µd^2) time, a significant improvement over the O(d^3) cost of a full decomposition. The primary technical challenge lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the term `C^(-1/2)`. The authors' key contribution is to approximate this term with `A^(-1)` and provide a theoretical justification for this approximation, arguing that the error introduced by this substitution diminishes as the shape of the covariance matrix converges. The paper presents empirical results on several benchmark functions, demonstrating that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a substantial speed-up in wall-clock time compared to standard implementations, without a significant increase in the number of function evaluations required for convergence.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper addresses a significant bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm. The empirical evaluation appears solid, with appropriate baselines and standard benchmark problems, and the results convincingly demonstrate a substantial practical speed-up.

However, the paper suffers from several severe technical and presentational flaws that undermine its quality.

1.  **Major Technical Error:** In Section 3, under "Properties of the update rule," the authors claim that "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix." This statement is fundamentally incorrect for a non-diagonal matrix. Consequently, the subsequent claim that "we get the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free" is also false. This error invalidates one of the key advertised advantages of the proposed method and suggests a misunderstanding of basic linear algebra.

2.  **Error in Pseudocode:** In Algorithm 1, the line `At+1 ← √ 1− c1 − cµAt` is mathematically ill-defined. The square root of a scalar cannot be applied to a matrix in this manner. Presumably, the authors intended scalar multiplication: `At+1 ← sqrt(1 - c1 - cµ) * At`. This is a critical error in the description of the core algorithm.

3.  **Weak Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix `C̄t` converges. While the authors acknowledge that this cannot be guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm, the justification for why the approximation is generally valid feels tenuous. The argument that the error diminishes is based on this convergence assumption, which may not hold in many practical scenarios, especially during the transient phases of optimization.

**Clarity:**
The overall structure of the paper is logical, but the writing is marred by a lack of precision and numerous errors.

1.  **Inconsistent Complexity Analysis:** The introduction states the runtime of CMA-ES is `ω(d^2)`, while Section 3 correctly notes that relevant operations are "cubic-time". The text should be precise and consistently use standard O-notation (e.g., O(d^3) for eigenvalue decomposition).

2.  **Typographical and Grammatical Errors:** The paper is littered with minor errors that detract from its readability. Examples include "it is it conjectured" (Section 2.1), missing words ("we refer Hansen" in Section 1), and inconsistent punctuation with respect to parentheses.

3.  **Unusable Reference Section:** The bibliography is completely corrupted, with formatting artifacts (`\.,? \Q2012\E,`) rendering every single entry unreadable and unverifiable. Furthermore, citations in the text (e.g., to Heidrich-Meisner and Igel [2009a,b]) cannot be disambiguated in the reference list. This demonstrates a profound lack of care in the manuscript's preparation and is unacceptable for a scientific publication.

**Originality:**
The idea of updating a matrix factorization directly is not new (e.g., Suttorp et al. [2009], Krause and Igel [2015]), as the authors acknowledge. The main novelty lies in adapting this approach to the standard (µ,λ)-CMA-ES while addressing the challenge of the CSA update. The approximation `C^(-1/2) ≈ A^(-1)` and the accompanying theoretical argument are the paper's central original contributions.

**Significance:**
The work is potentially significant. A faster CMA-ES would be a valuable tool for practitioners in machine learning and other fields, especially for hyperparameter tuning and direct policy search where `d` can be large. The demonstrated speed-up is impressive. However, the numerous flaws in the current manuscript severely diminish its contribution. A technically sound and clearly written version of this paper would be a valuable addition to the literature.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses an important practical limitation of CMA-ES.
*   The proposed method yields a significant and empirically verified improvement in wall-clock runtime.
*   The core idea of approximating the CSA update is novel and interesting.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Contains a major technical error regarding the relationship between Cholesky factors and eigenvalues.
*   The pseudocode for the proposed algorithm contains a critical error.
*   The theoretical justification for the core approximation is based on a strong, unproven assumption of convergence.
*   The manuscript is exceptionally sloppy, with a completely garbled reference section and numerous typos, indicating a lack of care unbecoming of a NIPS publication.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the research direction is promising and the empirical results are compelling, the manuscript in its current form falls far short of the standards for NIPS. The combination of a fundamental technical error in linear algebra, a critical error in the algorithm's pseudocode, and an abysmal level of presentation (most notably the unusable bibliography) makes it impossible to recommend acceptance. The authors have a potentially valuable contribution, but the paper requires a thorough revision to correct the technical inaccuracies and a complete re-editing to fix the extensive presentational flaws.
