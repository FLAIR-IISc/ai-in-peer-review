Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C instead of maintaining C and recomputing its square root. The covariance update is decomposed into a sequence of rank-1 updates that can be applied directly to A in O(d^2) per update, yielding overall O(µ d^2) time (with µ typically O(log d)) and optimal storage. The main difficulty is the step-size control (CSA), which in standard CMA-ES relies on C^{-1/2}. The authors replace C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}, yielding a systematic rotation error. They provide a lemma arguing that the rotation matrices arising from the RQ decomposition converge (under shape convergence of C), implying that the CSA error diminishes over time due to exponential forgetting. Experiments on standard noiseless benchmarks (including rotated versions) show essentially identical number of function evaluations to CMA-ES with delayed decompositions, and large wall-clock speedups (e.g., ~20x at d=64). The method is implemented in Shark with LAPACK.

Relation to prior work: The idea of updating a factorization rather than the covariance itself is known (e.g., Suttorp et al. 2009; Krause & Igel 2015). This paper extends these ideas to the standard (µ/µ_w, λ)-CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and provides a theoretically motivated approximation for CSA that avoids cubic-time decompositions, aiming to preserve the performance of full CMA-ES while achieving optimal O(µ d^2) time and storage.

Strengths
- Practical impact: A substantial speedup for full-covariance CMA-ES without sacrificing function evaluations; highly relevant when objective evaluations are cheap and d is moderate-to-large.
- Algorithmic novelty: Clean extension of Cholesky rank-1 updates to the standard CMA-ES covariance update with rank-µ, including scaling, and an intelligible modification of CSA with a convergence-based justification.
- Complexity and implementation: Asymptotically optimal storage and per-iteration time for full covariance; leverages triangular solves; easy to integrate in existing codebases.
- Empirical results: Across multiple classic test functions and randomized rotations, evaluation counts match the reference algorithm while wall-clock time improves markedly, and the advantage grows with d.
- Open-source implementation (Shark), aiding reproducibility and adoption.

Weaknesses
- Important technical misstatement: The paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. The diagonal of a triangular Cholesky factor are not related to the eigenvalues of C except in special cases (e.g., diagonal C). The eigenvalues of a triangular matrix equal its diagonal, but those are the eigenvalues of A, not of C; eigenvalues of C are squares of singular values of A, not its diagonal entries. This point should be corrected; the “eigenvalues for free” selling point does not hold.
- Theory limited to asymptotic convergence: The CSA approximation is justified by a lemma assuming convergence of the covariance shape; no non-asymptotic bounds or analysis of early-phase behavior when C changes rapidly. It remains unclear when, and how much, σ-adaptation can be biased in transient regimes.
- Evaluation scope: Only classic noiseless testbeds are reported. No results on noisy, constrained, or more realistic ML tasks (e.g., policy search), where CSA robustness matters. For Rosenbrock, excluding nonconverged trials may bias comparisons. Also, fairness of baselines could be strengthened by tuning the decomposition delay optimally per d and reporting corresponding evaluation/runtime trade-offs.
- Numerical stability: Long sequences of rank-1 updates to Cholesky factors can accumulate numerical error; the paper does not discuss periodic re-factorization, jitter, or safeguards (e.g., handling when c1+cµ approaches 1). Also, one should prefer triangular solves to explicit A^{-1} to avoid stability issues (the text suggests computing A^{-1} explicitly “when needed”).
- Clarity/formatting: Some references are malformed; the CSA invariance discussion could be tightened; and it would help to clearly distinguish the roles of RQ vs polar decompositions and what is or is not needed in the proposed algorithm.

Quality
- Technically sound core idea with a reasonable (asymptotic) justification for the CSA approximation; algorithms are standard Cholesky rank-1 updates. However, the eigenvalue claim is incorrect and should be fixed. Empirics are convincing on the chosen benchmarks but miss stress tests where CSA matters most.

Clarity
- Generally well written, with helpful algorithms. Some confusion around eigenvalues and factorization properties, and minor reference formatting issues. Reproducibility would benefit from releasing scripts and exact parameter settings; nevertheless defaults are standard.

Originality
- Moderate. Building on Suttorp et al. and Krause & Igel, the contribution is to generalize to standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and provide a CSA approximation with theoretical rationale. Not a radical new concept, but a solid and useful refinement.

Significance
- High practical significance for the CMA-ES community and black-box optimization practitioners who need full-covariance adaptation at moderate/large d. Likely to be adopted in libraries.

Questions for authors
- Please correct the eigenvalue claim and clarify what can be obtained “for free” from A (e.g., efficient triangular solves, log-det via sum of log diag(A)).
- Do you ever re-factorize C from A A^T to mitigate numerical drift? Any thresholds or heuristics?
- Can you quantify early-phase σ-bias vs standard CSA (e.g., on rapidly changing anisotropic functions)? Include rotation error statistics (Et) in the main paper.
- Report results with best-practice decomposition delays for the baseline to ensure fair comparison.
- Any experiments on noisy functions and reinforcement learning tasks?

Pros
- Strong practical speedups; full covariance preserved
- Clean, simple, and general algorithm; optimal O(µ d^2)
- Plausible theoretical rationale for CSA approximation
- Open-source implementation

Cons
- Incorrect eigenvalue claim
- Limited theory (asymptotic only) and evaluation scope
- Missing discussion of numerical stability and re-factorization
- Some baseline fairness concerns

Recommendation
- Accept (weak accept). Despite some issues (notably the eigenvalue claim and limited theory), the paper offers a meaningful, broadly useful advance that will speed up a widely used algorithm without compromising solution quality.
