

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called "Cholesky-CMA-ES" that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors claim this approach achieves asymptotically optimal runtime and storage complexity while maintaining similar optimization performance to the standard CMA-ES.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**
The paper builds upon the well-established CMA-ES algorithm (Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001), which has been widely adopted in derivative-free optimization. The authors identify the computational bottleneck of the matrix square root calculation (O(d^3)) and propose using a Cholesky factorization (O(d^2)) instead. They acknowledge prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) that explored similar ideas, but claim their approach has theoretical justification for not deteriorating performance. The paper extends this to the standard CMA-ES rather than the restricted (1+1)-CMA-ES case considered previously.

**Strengths**
1. The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for the approximation, with Lemma 1 establishing that the error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} vanishes as the algorithm converges.
2. The empirical results show consistent function evaluation counts across all benchmark functions, which is a positive sign for the theoretical claims.
3. The implementation details and complexity analysis are generally sound.

**Weaknesses and Concerns**

1. **Misleading Complexity Claims**: The authors claim "asymptotically optimal" complexity, but the standard CMA-ES with delay strategy (which updates the covariance matrix every τ ∈ Ω(d) steps) already achieves O(d^2) complexity. The Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves O(µd^2), and while µ is typically O(log d), this is not truly asymptotically optimal in the strictest sense. The paper fails to adequately compare against the standard delayed implementation, which is the practical baseline for large-scale applications.

2. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification**: Lemma 1 assumes Ct converges to a certain shape, but CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm where Ct does not necessarily converge. The paper acknowledges this limitation but provides no evidence that the error from the approximation remains negligible in practice. The claim that "the error caused by replacing st by ŝt is small and to decrease over time" is not sufficiently supported.

3. **Lack of Statistical Significance**: The paper states that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires "the same amount of function evaluations" as standard CMA-ES, but provides no statistical tests to demonstrate this claim. Small differences could be due to random variation rather than true equivalence.

4. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The paper compares against CMA-ES-Ref (with delay strategy) and CMA-ES/d (delaying for d steps), but doesn't provide enough detail on the implementation of these baselines. The claimed 20x speedup for d=64 seems significant, but without clear details on the hardware and implementation specifics, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance.

5. **Missing Discussion of Limitations**: The paper fails to address important limitations, such as whether the approach works well for extremely ill-conditioned problems or for very large dimensions where cache effects (as noted in Figure 3) might negate the theoretical speedup. The paper also doesn't discuss potential numerical stability issues with the Cholesky approach.

6. **Lack of Novelty**: The core idea of using Cholesky factorization in CMA-ES has been previously explored (Suttorp et al., 2009). While the authors extend it to standard CMA-ES, they don't sufficiently differentiate their approach from prior work or explain why previous attempts failed where theirs succeeds.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents a technically sound modification to an established algorithm, but the claims of "asymptotically optimal" complexity and negligible performance degradation are not sufficiently supported. The theoretical justification is incomplete, the empirical validation lacks statistical rigor, and the paper fails to adequately compare against the most relevant baseline (the standard delayed implementation). 

While the approach might offer practical speedups for certain problem dimensions, the paper doesn't demonstrate that this is a significant advancement over existing methods. The claims of "no significant change in the number of objective function evaluations" and "asymptotically optimal complexity" are overstated given the current evidence.

**Recommendation**: Reject. The paper presents a minor improvement to an established method without sufficient evidence that it constitutes a meaningful advancement over existing practice. The claims of theoretical and practical superiority are not adequately supported by the provided analysis and experiments. The paper would require substantially more rigorous validation and clearer differentiation from prior work to be considered for publication at NIPS.
