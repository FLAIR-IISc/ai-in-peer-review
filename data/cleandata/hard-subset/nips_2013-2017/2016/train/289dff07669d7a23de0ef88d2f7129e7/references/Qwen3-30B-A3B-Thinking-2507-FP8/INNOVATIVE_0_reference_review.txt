

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the computationally expensive matrix square root operation with a Cholesky factorization. The authors demonstrate theoretically and empirically that this approach achieves optimal asymptotic runtime complexity (O(μd²)) and storage complexity without significantly degrading optimization performance.

The paper builds upon existing work on CMA-ES variants that attempt to reduce computational complexity (e.g., Suttorp et al., Krause and Igel), but makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing justification for why the approximation (replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹) does not deteriorate algorithm performance. Their key insight is that the error introduced by this approximation diminishes as the algorithm converges, as formalized in Lemma 1. The authors show that the rotation matrix error vanishes asymptotically, which is crucial for the step-size adaptation mechanism.

The experimental section provides compelling evidence that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but achieves substantially faster wall-clock time (20x faster for d=64). The authors compare against three baselines: the reference implementation with delayed matrix square root (CMA-ES-Ref), a d-step delay variant (CMA-ES/d), and the non-triangular Cholesky approach (Suttorp-CMA-ES). The results consistently show Cholesky-CMA-ES outperforms alternatives in runtime while maintaining solution quality.

**Strengths:**
- **Novelty:** The theoretical justification for why the Cholesky factorization can replace the matrix square root in CMA-ES is a significant conceptual contribution. Previous attempts (Suttorp et al., Krause and Igel) did not provide this theoretical foundation.
- **Practical Impact:** The method addresses a real computational bottleneck in a widely used algorithm, with clear performance gains that increase with dimensionality.
- **Theoretical Rigor:** Lemma 1 provides a solid theoretical basis for the approach, which is rare in optimization method papers.
- **Experimental Validation:** The empirical results are thorough, with multiple benchmark functions, dimensionality tests, and careful comparison against relevant baselines.
- **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear pseudocode, logical flow, and proper mathematical presentation.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper acknowledges that for very large dimensions (d=256), cache effects reduce the speedup, but this is a practical limitation rather than a methodological flaw.
- The theoretical analysis focuses on the step-size adaptation, but it would be valuable to see more discussion about how the Cholesky factorization affects the covariance update itself (beyond the storage complexity claim).

**Significance and Contribution:**
This work makes a substantial contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. CMA-ES is widely used in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning, where computational efficiency is often a bottleneck. The authors have developed a variant that maintains the algorithm's performance while significantly reducing computational requirements, making it feasible to apply CMA-ES to larger-scale problems than previously possible.

The paper's significance is amplified by the fact that the authors provide not just a faster algorithm, but a theoretically justified one that preserves the key properties of CMA-ES. This distinguishes it from incremental improvements that simply tweak existing implementations.

**Comparison to Prior Work:**
The paper thoroughly contextualizes its contribution within the CMA-ES literature, clearly distinguishing their approach from previous attempts to reduce computational complexity. Their theoretical justification for why the approximation doesn't degrade performance is a key advancement over prior work.

**Recommendation:**
I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel approach that the NIPS community values. The authors have made a significant contribution to a well-established algorithm by providing a theoretically sound improvement with clear practical benefits. The paper's novelty, theoretical rigor, and empirical validation make it a strong candidate for publication at NIPS.

The paper's contribution is not incremental - it fundamentally rethinks a core component of CMA-ES with a theoretically justified approach that delivers tangible performance benefits. The authors have successfully balanced theoretical insight with practical implementation, which is exactly the kind of work that advances the field.

This work has clear potential for adoption in machine learning applications where CMA-ES is currently used, particularly in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization, where computational efficiency is critical. The authors have made a compelling case for why this variant should replace the standard implementation in practice.

The paper is of high quality, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. It meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS paper and should be accepted.
