This paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), one of the most powerful derivative-free optimization algorithms. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity of at least ω(d²) per iteration, dominated by the decomposition of the d x d covariance matrix C, which is typically an O(d³) eigenvalue decomposition to compute the matrix square root C¹/². The authors propose a variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that maintains and updates a Cholesky factor A (where C = AAᵀ) directly, reducing the update complexity to O(µd²), which is a significant improvement as µ is typically logarithmic in d.

The core challenge, which this paper tackles elegantly, is that the crucial step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism in CMA-ES requires the term C⁻¹/², which cannot be directly substituted with the inverse of an arbitrary Cholesky factor A⁻¹. The authors' key contribution is to propose using A⁻¹ as an approximation for C⁻¹/² (up to a rotation) and to provide a theoretical justification for why this approximation is sound. They argue via Lemma 1 that as the normalized covariance matrix converges, the implicit rotation matrix E in the RQ-decomposition C¹/² = AE also converges, meaning the rotational difference between consecutive steps vanishes (Eᵀt-₁Eₜ → I). Since the CSA path `pσ` is an exponentially decaying sum of past steps, this vanishing rotational error ensures that the norm of `pσ`, which governs the step-size update, is asymptotically correct. This provides a principled reason for the approximation, distinguishing this work from prior attempts at using Cholesky factor updates (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009) which either used non-triangular factors (introducing ambiguity and potential instability) or were applied to simpler CMA-ES variants without this specific CSA challenge.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a major, practical limitation of a cornerstone algorithm in black-box optimization. The promised speed-up makes CMA-ES more viable for higher-dimensional problems (d in the hundreds), which are common in areas like direct policy search for reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization of large models. The empirical results, showing a speed-up of over 20x at d=128, are compelling and demonstrate a substantial practical contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality and Originality:** The central idea of approximating C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ in the CSA path and justifying it via the convergence of the implicit rotation is novel and insightful. While Cholesky updates for covariance matrices have been explored before (e.g., for the rank-µ update by Krause & Igel, 2015), this paper is the first to integrate it successfully into the modern, full CMA-ES framework with a sound theoretical argument. The use of a *triangular* Cholesky factor is also a strong choice, as it guarantees uniqueness of the decomposition (up to sign), optimal storage complexity, and efficient inversion.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly explains the problem, the existing solutions and their drawbacks, and the proposed methodology. The theoretical argument in Section 3 is concise but sufficient to convey the main idea. The inclusion of pseudocode and a thorough experimental comparison against relevant baselines makes the work easy to understand and verify.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed. They compare against the standard reference implementation and another common speed-up heuristic (CMA-ES/d). The choice of benchmark functions is appropriate, covering a range of properties (separable, ill-conditioned, non-convex) that test the algorithm thoroughly. The key result—that the number of function evaluations is nearly identical to the original CMA-ES while wall-clock time is drastically reduced—is convincingly demonstrated.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Theoretical Assumptions:** The main theoretical argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix C̄t converges. The authors correctly note that for a stochastic algorithm, this convergence is not guaranteed. While they appeal to results on the convergence of the *expectation* of Ct in simplified models and the heuristic that Ct often changes slowly, the argument remains heuristic. It would be beneficial to discuss potential failure modes: for example, on highly multi-modal or dynamic problems where the covariance matrix might need to rotate rapidly, could this approximation lead to suboptimal step-size adaptation and degrade performance? The current experiments on standard benchmarks do not seem to trigger such a failure mode, but a discussion would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Minor Technical Inaccuracy:** In the "Properties of the update rule" section, the authors claim that "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix". This is incorrect. This property holds only if the matrix C is diagonal. In the general case, the diagonal elements of A are not directly related to the eigenvalues of C = AAᵀ. This statement should be corrected to avoid misleading readers. A more accurate, and still valid, point is that the diagonal entries of A provide information about the variance along the axes of the coordinate system defined by the Cholesky basis, which is still useful for monitoring purposes.

3.  **Scope of Experiments:** While the benchmark functions are standard, the paper's motivation strongly references machine learning applications. Demonstrating the wall-clock time advantage on a simple, well-known ML task (e.g., policy search for a classic control problem like Acrobot, or tuning hyperparameters for a small SVM/NN on a UCI dataset) would further cement the practical significance of the work for the NIPS audience.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a high-quality, significant, and practical contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. It solves a long-standing efficiency problem in CMA-ES with an elegant and well-justified approximation, resulting in an algorithm that is an order of magnitude faster in moderately high dimensions without sacrificing performance in terms of function evaluations. The work is technically sound, novel in its specific approach to the CSA update, and clearly presented. The minor weaknesses, primarily the heuristic nature of the convergence assumption and a small technical misstatement, do not detract from the overall strength and impact of the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Solves a major computational bottleneck in a widely used, state-of-the-art algorithm.
*   Provides dramatic, empirically verified speed-ups on standard benchmarks.
*   The core technical idea is novel, insightful, and supported by a plausible theoretical argument.
*   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers optimal time and space complexity for full covariance matrix adaptation.
*   The paper is very well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical justification relies on an assumption of convergence that is not strictly proven for the stochastic algorithm.
*   A minor technical claim about eigenvalues is incorrect and should be revised.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This is an excellent paper that will likely be of great interest and utility to researchers and practitioners using CMA-ES. I strongly recommend acceptance.
