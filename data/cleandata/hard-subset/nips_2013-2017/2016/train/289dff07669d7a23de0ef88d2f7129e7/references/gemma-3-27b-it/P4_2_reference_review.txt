The paper introduces the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that utilizes the Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root for covariance adaptation. The motivation stems from the computational cost of computing the matrix square root, which can dominate the runtime for high-dimensional problems. The authors argue that their approach maintains the algorithm's performance while reducing runtime and memory footprint, particularly for non-separable, ill-conditioned problems. They provide a theoretical justification based on the convergence of the Cholesky factor and empirical results demonstrating speedups compared to the standard CMA-ES and other variants.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical justification, specifically Lemma 1 and the surrounding discussion, provides a compelling argument for why the Cholesky factorization doesn't deteriorate performance. The complexity analysis is accurate, and the algorithmic description is clear. The experiments are well-designed, employing standard benchmark functions and varying dimensionality. However, the discussion of the error introduced by not correcting for rotations (using Et) feels a bit hand-wavy and relies heavily on the convergence of Ct. A more rigorous error bound or analysis would strengthen this aspect. The claim of optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity is also somewhat strong without more detailed comparison to other advanced techniques beyond the specific implementations tested. The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations like scaling issues with extremely high dimensionality due to cache limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background on CMA-ES and Cholesky decomposition is concise and sufficient. The presentation of the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm and its updates is clear, aided by the pseudocode. However, the theoretical sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations. While the math is correct, the reasoning behind some claims might not be immediately apparent to all readers. For example, the link between the convergence of Ct and the error introduced by omitting the rotation correction could be elaborated.

**Originality:** The idea of using Cholesky factorization within CMA-ES isn’t entirely new (as acknowledged through citations to Suttorp et al. and Krause and Igel), but the authors' contribution lies in the theoretical justification for its application in the *standard* CMA-ES and the resulting performance gains. They extend previous work by providing a detailed analysis of the error introduced and demonstrating that it doesn't significantly impact convergence. The focus on optimal asymptotic complexity and memory usage is also a notable addition. The thorough empirical validation further solidifies the originality of the work.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant. CMA-ES is a widely used optimization algorithm, and reducing its computational cost, especially in high-dimensional spaces, is valuable. The observed speedups, particularly as dimensionality increases, are substantial. This could enable the application of CMA-ES to larger and more complex problems, such as those encountered in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. The results are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on derivative-free optimization. The work provides a practical improvement to a well-established algorithm with a clear benefit.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical justification for the proposed approach.
*   Demonstrated performance gains (speedups) on benchmark functions, particularly with increasing dimensionality.
*   Clear and concise presentation of the algorithm and its advantages.
*   Addresses a known bottleneck in CMA-ES (matrix square root computation).
*   Well-written and organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The error analysis related to omitting the rotation correction (Et) could be more rigorous.
*   The claim of *optimal* complexity could be refined with comparison to more cutting-edge approaches.
*   The experimental setup, while comprehensive, could benefit from more diverse benchmark problems or real-world applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a practical and theoretically sound improvement to a widely used algorithm. While some aspects of the analysis could be further strengthened, the demonstrated performance gains and clear presentation make this a worthy addition to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
