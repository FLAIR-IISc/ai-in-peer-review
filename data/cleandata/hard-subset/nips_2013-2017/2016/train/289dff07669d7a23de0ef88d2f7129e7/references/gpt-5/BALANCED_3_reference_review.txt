Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C instead of computing the matrix square root C1/2 explicitly each time. This yields an O(µ d^2) per-iteration update (with typical µ = O(log d)) and optimal storage (triangular), while keeping evaluation complexity identical to standard CMA-ES on non-separable, ill-conditioned problems where full covariance adaptation is required. The key modification is in step-size adaptation (CSA): the exact update uses C−1/2, but the proposed algorithm replaces it with A−1, which introduces an unknown orthogonal rotation Et at each iteration. The authors argue (via an RQ-decomposition continuity lemma) that as C stabilizes in shape, Et varies slowly and the induced error on the CSA path norm decays because of exponential forgetting. They provide a rank-one Cholesky update routine, extend beyond µ = 1 (cf. Krause & Igel, 2015), and show empirically that the number of function evaluations is preserved while wall-clock time is substantially reduced over CMA-ES with delayed decompositions and over non-triangular Cholesky updates (Suttorp et al., 2009). Results on standard benchmarks (including rotated versions) up to d = 256 indicate up to ~20x wall-clock speedup at d = 64 with similar evaluation counts; cache effects at d = 256 are discussed.

The work situates itself among techniques that reduce CMA-ES overhead: delayed eigendecompositions (Hansen & Ostermeier), restricted models (diagonal/low-rank; Poland & Zell; Ros & Hansen; Loshchilov), and previous Cholesky-based updates (Suttorp et al.; Krause & Igel for µ = 1). Compared to these, the paper emphasizes (i) triangular factor with unique mapping to C, (ii) optimal storage and easy inversion in O(d^2), and (iii) an argument why the CSA approximation should not harm performance.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: the cubic-time matrix square root can dominate runtime in higher dimensions; avoiding it without sacrificing evaluations is valuable.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with a practical rank-one triangular update; uses standard BLAS/LAPACK.
- Solid empirical evidence on multiple standard test functions, including rotated versions, across a meaningful range of d; evaluation count parity with CMA-ES is key.
- Good discussion of storage and numerical aspects (triangular storage, eigenvalues available from diagonal, cheap inversion).
- Connection to prior work is mostly appropriate; extends Krause & Igel’s µ=1 rank-one update to the full rank-µ setting in standard CMA-ES.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical justification for replacing C−1/2 with A−1 in CSA is qualitative. The lemma (continuity of RQ decomposition) shows Et convergence when C’s shape converges; it does not provide finite-time bounds on the deviation in ‖pσ‖ or guarantees when C changes rapidly (e.g., during transients on ill-conditioned landscapes).
- Empirical evaluation, while broad on synthetic benchmarks, does not include noisy objectives, constraints, or real ML tasks (RL policy search, hyperparameter tuning) where CSA stability can be more challenging.
- Baseline fairness and implementation details could be clearer. The “reference” CMA-ES is said to recompute the square root every iteration for the considered d; however, standard implementations often delay eigendecomposition O(d) iterations. Strong speedup claims should include the best-practice delayed-eig baseline and highly optimized eigensolvers.
- No ablations on µ/λ or sensitivity analyses for CSA hyperparameters (cσ, dσ) under the approximation.
- Numerical stability of repeated rank-one Cholesky updates is not analyzed (e.g., error accumulation, need for periodic refactorization).
- Presentation issues: several reference entries appear malformed; some definitions/assumptions (e.g., typical µ = O(log d)) are stated without citation; a few notational slips.

Quality
- Algorithmic design is sound and consistent with known properties of triangular Cholesky updates.
- The theoretical argument is suggestive but incomplete; a finite-time error bound for the CSA path norm difference between C−1/2 and A−1 would strengthen the paper significantly.
- Experiments are adequate for noiseless synthetic benchmarks and support the main claims; more thorough baselines and ablations would increase confidence.

Clarity
- Overall readable; algorithms are explicit. However, fix reference formatting, clarify the decomposition delay policy in the reference baseline, and tighten the CSA approximation argument (assumptions, regimes when it may fail).
- It would help to summarize computational costs per operation (sampling, update, inversion) and report measured scaling vs d.

Originality
- The idea to maintain the triangular Cholesky factor with rank-µ updates in standard CMA-ES and to approximate CSA via A−1 is a practical and useful synthesis beyond prior µ=1 results. Not conceptually radical, but a meaningful contribution.

Significance
- For practitioners relying on full CMA-ES, a substantial reduction in wall-clock time with unchanged evaluation counts is impactful.
- The approach remains O(d^2); it does not replace large-scale (≈ linear-time) methods, but fills an important regime where full covariance is still feasible.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical algorithm with strong speedups and unchanged evaluation counts on standard benchmarks.
- Clean, minimal modification to CMA-ES with straightforward implementation.
- Extends and clarifies the status of Cholesky updates in standard CMA-ES.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical guarantees for the CSA approximation; lacks finite-time bounds.
- Limited evaluation breadth (noisy/constrained/real tasks) and potentially suboptimal baselines for runtime comparisons.
- Some clarity and presentation issues, including references.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-time bound on |‖pσ,t‖approx − ‖pσ,t‖exact| under a quantified bound on ‖ETt−1Et − I‖ and covariate drift; or provide an occasional, very infrequent polar correction and show it does not change wall-clock superiority.
- Add baselines: (i) CMA-ES with recommended delayed eigendecomposition frequencies and fast eigensolvers; (ii) a periodic re-factorization-from-C variant for numerical stability; (iii) LM-CMA / low-rank variants to contextualize the performance regime.
- Include sensitivity studies to µ, λ, and CSA hyperparameters; report numerical stability (e.g., need for jitter, periodic re-factorization).
- Evaluate on noisy functions and at least one RL or hyperparameter tuning task to corroborate CSA robustness in practice.
- Report memory footprint and measured scaling with d; include effect of cache sizes.
- Fix references and notational inconsistencies.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a practical, well-motivated improvement to a widely used optimizer, with convincing empirical speedups and minimal impact on evaluation counts. Strengthening the theoretical analysis of the CSA approximation and broadening/clarifying the empirical comparisons would make this a solid accept.
