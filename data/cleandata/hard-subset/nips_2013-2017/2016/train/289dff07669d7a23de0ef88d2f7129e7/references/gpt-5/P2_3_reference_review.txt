Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = A A^T directly, instead of updating C and recomputing its square root or eigendecomposition. The authors extend earlier Cholesky-update ideas that were limited to special CMA-ES variants (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES) and to rank-one settings, to the standard CMA-ES with rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates. The main technical hurdle is the CSA step-size update, which in standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2}; replacing it by A^{-1} introduces an unmodeled orthogonal rotation E from the RQ/polar factorization. The paper proposes to ignore this rotation and provides a lemma-based argument that the error in the CSA norm vanishes asymptotically when the normalized covariance converges (and practically remains small due to exponential forgetting). Empirically, the method matches the standard CMA-ES in number of function evaluations while substantially reducing wall-clock time, with asymptotic update complexity O(µ d^2) and optimal storage.

Relation to prior work is generally well covered: the paper situates itself between delayed decomposition and model-restricted CMA variants, and builds on Suttorp et al. (non-triangular factor update) and Krause & Igel (rank-one triangular Cholesky update), extending to the full rank-µ CMA-ES. It also contrasts to limited-memory or diagonal models that lose performance on non-separable problems.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The derivation of a quadratic-time Cholesky rank-one update for each term in the rank-1 and rank-µ covariance update is sound and consistent with prior work. The complexity claim O(µ d^2) is plausible and essentially optimal for full-covariance adaptation under naive multiplication.
- The step-size adaptation approximation is the main theoretical risk. The provided Lemma 1 (rotation convergence under convergence of normalized covariance) and the argument leveraging exponential forgetting in CSA justify that the rotation omission has vanishing asymptotic effect on the norm used in σ update, assuming slow covariance change. This is reasonable but not a full finite-time guarantee, and it relies on convergence properties that are not proven for the full algorithm (only for simplified models in expectation).
- Empirical evidence: The experiments on standard rotated benchmark functions up to d=256 show equal function-evaluation efficiency compared to the reference CMA-ES and substantial runtime gains (e.g., ~20x at d=64), and compare also to a “delayed” CMA-ES and a non-triangular Cholesky approach. This supports the claim that the approximation does not harm optimization efficiency while reducing computational cost.

Important issues to address:
- A factual error: The paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is incorrect in general. The diag(A) entries are related to the Cholesky pivots; eigenvalues of C are the squares of the singular values of A and are not simply the diagonal of A. The authors should correct this and carefully state what spectral information they get “for free.”
- Fairness and completeness of evaluation: (i) Excluding unsuccessful Rosenbrock trials biases results; standard practice (e.g., COCO/BBOB) is to report success rates and ERT. (ii) The “reference CMA-ES” recomputes the square root every iteration for the used d; a stronger baseline would use the recommended delayed eigendecomposition schedule for each d (or multiple settings). (iii) Provide ablations isolating the effect of the CSA approximation: e.g., compare σ dynamics with and without the rotation term on controlled problems, and quantify the rotation error over time (the supplement is mentioned but not summarized).
- Numerical stability: Algorithm 2’s stability and behavior for the full sequence of positive rank-one updates and scaling should be discussed (e.g., conditioning, accumulation of round-off), and whether reorthogonalization or periodic recomputation is ever needed.
- Scope of experiments: While synthetic, rotated, ill-conditioned functions are appropriate, adding results on noisy functions, constraints, and at least one practical ML task (policy search or hyperparameter tuning) would strengthen the case for NeurIPS.
- Reproducibility: The implementation is in Shark, which is positive. Please include exact parameter settings, decomposition delay used in baselines, seeds, and links to code/scripts.

Clarity:
The paper is mostly clearly written, with a helpful recap of CMA-ES and explicit algorithms. The CSA approximation is well motivated. However, there are typos/formatting issues in references, an apparent typo in the delay expression (“max { 1, 110d(c1+cµ) }”), and the incorrect eigenvalue statement noted above. Clarify the conditions under which µ = O(log d) is assumed. Summarize the supplementary rotation-error measurements in the main text.

Originality:
Moderately high. Using a triangular Cholesky factor with a fast rank-µ update in standard CMA-ES and modifying CSA with an asymptotic justification appears novel compared to prior Cholesky-based updates limited to specific variants or non-triangular factors. The idea is an engineering improvement with a nontrivial theoretical argument for the CSA approximation.

Significance:
High practical significance for users of full-covariance CMA-ES on moderate-to-high dimensional, non-separable problems. The reported speedups without sacrificing evaluation efficiency should impact practice. The work advances the state of the art in efficient CMA-ES implementations. It also provides a clean path to obtain A^{-1} in O(d^2) when needed.

Pros:
- Clear and motivated algorithmic contribution with optimal O(µ d^2) update and reduced memory.
- Thoughtful justification for the CSA approximation; preserves sampling invariance and shows asymptotic small error in σ update.
- Strong empirical speedups with unchanged evaluation counts on rotated, ill-conditioned benchmarks.
- Open-source implementation.

Cons:
- Incorrect claim about eigenvalues from the Cholesky diagonal.
- Theoretical guarantees are asymptotic and do not cover finite-time error bounds.
- Evaluation excludes failed trials and uses a baseline that may not reflect best practice delay scheduling.
- Limited scope beyond synthetic benchmarks; limited analysis of σ dynamics and rotation error in the main text.

Recommendation:
Borderline accept to accept. If the factual error is corrected, baseline comparisons are tightened (add proper delay schedule and report success/ERT), and the supplement’s rotation-error analysis is summarized, this would be a solid NeurIPS paper with tangible impact for the black-box optimization community.
