Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a practical variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C instead of maintaining C and recomputing its square root. The main novelty is to extend prior Cholesky-update ideas beyond special cases (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES, MO-CMA-ES) to the standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates while preserving performance in terms of function evaluations. The key technical issue is step-size adaptation (CSA), which in the standard algorithm uses C−1/2; replacing it with A−1 neglects an orthogonal factor Et from the polar/RQ decomposition. The authors justify this approximation by arguing (Lemma 1) that, after determinant-normalization, Et stabilizes as C converges in shape, so the cumulative error in the norm of the evolution path vanishes due to exponential forgetting. They present an O(µ d^2) per-iteration update via a rank-one Cholesky update (Algorithm 2), claim optimal storage, and report substantial wall-clock speedups with essentially unchanged evaluation counts on standard (rotated) benchmarks up to d=256, comparing against a reference CMA-ES with delayed decompositions and an alternative non-triangular Cholesky approach (Suttorp et al., 2009).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The Cholesky rank-one update appears correct and efficient; the complexity claims (O(µ d^2)) are plausible given the presented loops. The CSA approximation is justified heuristically via Lemma 1 and continuity arguments; however, there is no formal bound on the induced error or on its impact on step-size adaptation or invariance. The paper is honest about this and provides empirical evidence, including rotated functions and (per the text) additional measurements in the supplement.
- A serious factual error: the paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general; diag(L) contains square roots of the Cholesky pivots, not eigenvalues. Consequently, “eigenvalues for free” and the claim about enforcing lower bounds along principal components based on diag(L) need correction. You can monitor determinant and an orthogonalized coordinate system’s variances, but not the eigenvalues/principal components without an eigendecomposition.
- Experimental methodology: The benchmark suite and rotated variants are appropriate and align with the stated goal (full covariance on non-separable, ill-conditioned problems). Reporting both evaluation counts and wall-clock time is valuable. However:
  - Excluding non-converged Rosenbrock trials biases results; success rates and anytime curves should be reported.
  - The reference baseline recomputes the square root every iteration for the dimensions considered; this penalizes it. The alternative CMA-ES/d partly addresses this, but a clearer discussion of typical decomposition frequencies in standard implementations would help.
  - No experiments under noise, constraints, or real-world tasks are shown; CSA behavior can differ substantially under noise.
- Numerical stability: The paper claims stability benefits (triangular inversion, one factor only). Some discussion of conditioning and accumulated round-off in repeated rank-one updates would strengthen the work (e.g., occasional re-factorization or reorthogonalization policies).

Clarity
- Generally well organized, with helpful algorithms. The CSA approximation and Lemma 1 are explained clearly, including the determinant normalization rationale.
- The manuscript has several typographical issues and reference formatting glitches, which should be cleaned. The big misstatement about eigenvalues must be fixed. Also, be precise about ω/Ω/O notation and about QR/RQ terminology and uniqueness conditions.

Originality
- Prior work (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) explored Cholesky updates and special CMA-ES variants. This paper’s extension to standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates, together with a principled argument for the CSA approximation that avoids polar decomposition, is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution. The complexity-optimality argument for full-covariance CMA-ES is credible.

Significance
- The contribution is practically important: full-covariance CMA-ES remains widely used, and the reported 20× speedup at d=64 (and increasing with d) without increasing function evaluations is impactful. The approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners, especially when function evaluations are cheap relative to linear algebra. While the theory is not fully rigorous for CSA, the empirical evidence suggests the approximation is benign in the tested regimes.

Pros
- Clear, simple algorithmic change with substantial runtime gains and optimal storage.
- Extends prior Cholesky-update ideas to standard rank-µ CMA-ES with a plausible CSA workaround.
- Solid empirical evaluation on rotated, ill-conditioned benchmarks; evaluation counts preserved.

Cons
- Incorrect claim about Cholesky diagonals equaling eigenvalues; related claims need correction.
- CSA justification is heuristic; no bounds on error or invariance properties.
- Experimental gaps: biased handling of non-converged runs; no noisy/real-world tests; baseline fairness could be discussed more carefully.
- Presentation issues (typos, references).

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the eigenvalue claim and rephrase the monitoring/regularization discussion accordingly.
- Report success rates, include non-converged runs in summaries, and provide anytime plots.
- Add experiments with noisy objectives and perhaps a real-world hyperparameter tuning or policy search task.
- Provide ablations quantifying the CSA approximation error (e.g., compare A−1 vs exact C−1/2 occasionally, plot ‖pσ‖ differences) and the measured Et rotations.
- Release code to facilitate adoption and reproducibility.
- Discuss numerical stability and whether periodic re-factorization is needed.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a practically valuable, computationally optimal variant of CMA-ES with strong empirical speedups and a reasonable (if not fully rigorous) theoretical justification for the CSA approximation. With the factual correction and improved experimental reporting, it would be a solid contribution.
