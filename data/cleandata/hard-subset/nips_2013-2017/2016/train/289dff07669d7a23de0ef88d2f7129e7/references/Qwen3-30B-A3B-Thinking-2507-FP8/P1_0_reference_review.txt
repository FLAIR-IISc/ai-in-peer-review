

# Peer Review: "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors provide theoretical justification (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor vanishes as the algorithm converges. This results in an O(µd²) runtime complexity (compared to O(d³) for standard CMA-ES) while maintaining the same number of function evaluations. The paper builds upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), but extends it to the standard CMA-ES with a theoretical foundation for the approximation.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization by addressing a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES. CMA-ES is widely used in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning (e.g., Gomez et al., 2008; Heidrich-Meisner and Igel, 2009), and this work directly enables scaling to higher-dimensional problems where the standard CMA-ES becomes computationally prohibitive.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The key strength is Lemma 1, which rigorously shows that the error introduced by using the Cholesky factor instead of the matrix square root vanishes as the algorithm converges. This addresses a critical concern about the validity of the approximation that previous attempts to use Cholesky factorizations failed to resolve.

2. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The experiments demonstrate significant speedups (20x for d=64) while maintaining identical function evaluation counts compared to the standard CMA-ES. The empirical results align perfectly with the theoretical claims.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The paper provides a complete algorithm (Algorithm 1) and a detailed implementation of the Cholesky rank-1 update (Algorithm 2). The authors have integrated their method into the Shark machine learning library, which enhances reproducibility.

4. **Comprehensive Comparison**: The paper compares against three relevant baselines (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) across multiple benchmark functions with varying dimensionality, providing a thorough evaluation of the approach.

5. **Clear Explanation of Advantages**: The paper effectively articulates the advantages beyond just speed: optimal storage complexity, free eigenvalue computation (for monitoring conditioning), and numerically stable matrix inversion.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Error Analysis**: While Lemma 1 provides a theoretical foundation for the approximation, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the actual error introduced in practice during optimization. It would be valuable to see how the error evolves during the optimization process and whether it has any measurable impact on convergence trajectories.

2. **Limited High-Dimensional Evaluation**: The experiments stop at d=256, but the paper's main motivation is scaling to large problems. A more thorough exploration of performance at higher dimensions (e.g., d=1024 or beyond) would strengthen the case for practical applicability.

3. **Numerical Stability Discussion**: While the paper mentions numerical stability as an advantage, it doesn't provide any analysis or experimental evidence of how the Cholesky update compares to the standard approach in terms of numerical stability, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

4. **Comparison with Other Low-Complexity Approaches**: The paper mentions low-dimensional approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) as alternatives, but doesn't directly compare Cholesky-CMA-ES with these approaches in terms of both runtime and performance.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear, well-supported contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The theoretical justification for the approximation is rigorous and addresses a key limitation in previous work. The experimental results are thorough and directly support the theoretical claims.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria:
- **Quality**: Technically sound with strong theoretical foundation and empirical validation
- **Clarity**: Well-organized, clearly written, with detailed algorithm descriptions
- **Originality**: Extends previous Cholesky-based approaches with theoretical justification for the standard CMA-ES
- **Significance**: Addresses a critical computational bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm, with clear practical implications for scaling to higher dimensions

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS as it advances the state-of-the-art in optimization algorithms with clear theoretical and practical contributions. The approach is relevant to machine learning practitioners working with derivative-free optimization, particularly in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contribution is significant, well-justified, and clearly presented. While there are minor weaknesses in the error analysis and high-dimensional evaluation, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in CMA-ES implementation and provides a practical solution to a well-known computational bottleneck.

The authors have successfully addressed the key limitation of previous Cholesky-based approaches (lack of theoretical justification) while providing a clear implementation with demonstrable performance improvements. This work will be valuable to researchers and practitioners using CMA-ES for high-dimensional optimization problems.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief analysis of the actual error introduced during optimization (not just the theoretical convergence)
2. Include results for d=512 to better demonstrate scalability
3. Provide a more detailed comparison with low-dimensional approximation methods (Loshchilov, 2015) in terms of both runtime and optimization performance
4. Include a brief discussion of potential numerical stability concerns with the Cholesky update

These suggestions would further strengthen an already strong paper, but are not essential for acceptance.
