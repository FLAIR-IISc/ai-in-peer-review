### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES): the `O(d^3)` complexity of decomposing the `d x d` covariance matrix `C` in each iteration. The authors propose a new variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor `A` of the covariance matrix (`C = AA^T`) directly. This reduces the per-iteration complexity to `O(μd^2)`, which is asymptotically optimal for updating a full-rank covariance matrix.

The primary technical challenge is that the standard CMA-ES step-size adaptation (CSA) requires the matrix square root `C^(-1/2)`, which is not equivalent to the inverse Cholesky factor `A^(-1)`. The authors' key contribution is to replace `C^(-1/2)` with `A^(-1)` and provide a theoretical justification for why this approximation is sound. They argue via Lemma 1 that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the rotational component difference between `C^(1/2)` and `A` also converges, meaning the error introduced by their approximation vanishes over time. They support their claims with extensive experiments on benchmark optimization functions, demonstrating that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same convergence in terms of function evaluations as the standard algorithm but with a dramatic reduction in wall-clock time, especially for higher dimensions.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Significant and Practical Problem:** The quadratic/cubic complexity of standard CMA-ES is a major barrier to its application in higher-dimensional problems, which are common in machine learning (e.g., direct policy search with many parameters). This paper tackles this fundamental limitation head-on.

2.  **Principled Approximation:** The core of the paper is not just the procedural change of updating a Cholesky factor, but the theoretical justification for the necessary approximation in the step-size update path `p_σ`. Previous works (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009) have proposed similar factor-update schemes but lacked a rigorous argument for why this modification does not harm the algorithm's dynamics. Lemma 1 provides a clean and insightful argument based on the convergence of the RQ decomposition, lending strong theoretical support to the method. This elevates the work from a useful engineering trick to a solid algorithmic contribution.

3.  **Optimal Complexity:** The resulting `O(μd^2)` time complexity and `O(d^2)` space complexity are asymptotically optimal for any CMA-ES variant that adapts a full covariance matrix. This is a powerful claim and positions the proposed algorithm as a potential new standard implementation.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The authors compare against appropriate baselines: the reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) and a common heuristic of delaying updates (CMA-ES/d). The results clearly show that Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the reference in function evaluations (the key performance metric for derivative-free optimization) while offering a massive speed-up in runtime, scaling as predicted. The inclusion of a competitor Cholesky-based method (Suttorp-CMA-ES) further strengthens the results.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on Convergence Assumption:** The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix `C̄_t` converges. While CMA-ES is known to converge on many problems, this is not guaranteed for all functions. The paper does not discuss the potential behavior of the approximation if `C̄_t` enters a limit cycle or fails to converge. In such a scenario, the rotational error term `E_t^T E_{t-1}` might not approach the identity matrix, potentially leading to a systematic bias in the step-size adaptation. While this may be a minor point in practice, acknowledging this limitation would make the theoretical discussion more complete.

2.  **Minor Technical Inaccuracy:** In the "Properties of the update rule" section, the paper claims that "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix." This is incorrect. The diagonal elements of `A` in the factorization `C=AA^T` are not directly related to the eigenvalues of `C` in a simple way. This statement should be corrected to avoid misleading readers. While the method has other strong benefits (e.g., efficient inversion), this particular claim is false.

3.  **Lack of a Machine Learning Application:** The introduction motivates the work by citing applications in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. However, the experiments are confined to classic numerical optimization benchmarks. While these benchmarks are essential for validating the algorithm's core behavior, demonstrating the speed-up on even a moderately-sized ML problem (e.g., policy optimization for a standard RL environment, or tuning a neural network) would have significantly strengthened the paper's impact for the NIPS audience.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the technical challenge is well-explained, and the proposed solution is presented logically. The notation is consistent. The step-by-step logic from equation (3) to (5), explaining the introduction of the rotational error `E_t`, is particularly effective.
*   **Originality and Significance:** The idea of updating a matrix factor directly is not entirely new in this context. However, the crucial contribution is the formal analysis of the approximation required for the step-size update. This provides the missing piece that makes such an approach theoretically sound for the standard CMA-ES. Given the prominence of CMA-ES, an asymptotically optimal and empirically validated version is a highly significant contribution. This work is very likely to be adopted by practitioners and become the basis for future standard implementations of the algorithm.

### **Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that presents a valuable and well-supported contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. It solves a long-standing practical problem with an elegant and theoretically grounded solution. The experimental results are clear and compelling. Despite the minor weaknesses noted above—which can be easily addressed in a final version—the paper's quality, originality, and significance are very high. It advances the state-of-the-art for a foundational algorithm and will be of immediate use to researchers and practitioners in machine learning and beyond.
