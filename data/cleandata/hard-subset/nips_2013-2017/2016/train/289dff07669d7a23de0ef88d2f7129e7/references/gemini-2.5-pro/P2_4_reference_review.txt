### Summary

This paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES has a runtime complexity dominated by the O(d^3) computation of the covariance matrix's square root, which is required for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA). This cost can be prohibitive for high-dimensional problems (large d). The authors propose a new variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, which reduces the complexity to an asymptotically optimal O(µd^2) for a full covariance matrix update, where µ is typically logarithmic in d.

The core contribution is to maintain and update a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix directly, avoiding the expensive re-computation. The main technical challenge is that the CSA mechanism requires the matrix C^(-1/2), which is not trivially obtained from the Cholesky factor A (where C = AA^T). The authors' key insight is to approximate C^(-1/2) with A^(-1). They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) that this approximation is sound: as the algorithm converges and the shape of the covariance matrix stabilizes, the rotational error introduced by this approximation vanishes due to the exponential weighting in the CSA path.

Empirical results on several benchmark functions demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same convergence in terms of function evaluations as the standard reference implementation, but with a drastically reduced wall-clock time, showing a speed-up of over 20x for d=64.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical reasoning and rigorous empirical evaluation.

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the fast Cholesky update builds upon prior work but makes a crucial new contribution. The central approximation of C^(-1/2) with A^(-1) is the paper's linchpin, and the authors are careful to justify it. The argument in Lemma 1, which states that the rotational difference between successive decompositions diminishes as the covariance matrix shape converges, is elegant and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approximation. While it relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges, which is not strictly guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm, this is a reasonable assumption that is often observed in practice and supported by analysis of simplified models. The authors correctly acknowledge this.

*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental design is sound. The proposed algorithm is compared against appropriate baselines: the standard reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref), a delayed-update variant (CMA-ES/d), and an alternative Cholesky-based approach (Suttorp-CMA-ES). The use of standard, rotated benchmark functions is appropriate for testing the performance and invariance properties of such an algorithm. The results are presented clearly and convincingly support the central claims: equivalent sample efficiency and superior time efficiency. The inclusion of results up to d=256 demonstrates the scalability of the proposed method.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

*   **Organization:** The structure is logical, progressing from the problem statement and background to the proposed solution, theoretical justification, and empirical validation. Each section is focused and contributes clearly to the overall narrative.
*   **Exposition:** The authors do an excellent job of explaining the core problem with CMA-ES and why previous attempts to solve it were insufficient. The explanation of the technical challenge in Section 3—the introduction of an unwanted rotation matrix `E_t` when using a Cholesky factor for the CSA update—is particularly clear and motivates their approximation. The algorithms are presented cleanly. The figures are effective visualizations of the results, using log scales where appropriate to highlight the dramatic performance differences.

### Originality

The work is highly original and makes a significant new contribution to the field of evolutionary computation and derivative-free optimization.

*   **Novelty:** While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix is not entirely new (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this paper is the first to successfully integrate this approach into the standard, modern CMA-ES without compromising its core mechanisms. Previous works either applied to different variants of CMA-ES (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES) or used non-triangular factorizations which lack the uniqueness and stability properties exploited here. The key novelty lies in identifying the rotational error in the CSA update as the primary obstacle and providing a well-justified approximation to overcome it.

*   **Relation to Prior Work:** The paper does an excellent job of situating its contribution within the context of existing literature, clearly articulating the limitations of prior attempts to reduce the complexity of CMA-ES.

### Significance

The significance of this work is very high.

*   **Impact:** CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in black-box optimization with numerous applications in machine learning, from reinforcement learning to hyperparameter tuning. Its cubic complexity has been a long-standing barrier to its application in higher-dimensional settings. This paper provides a practical, efficient, and well-founded solution that effectively lowers this barrier. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is likely to become the new standard implementation for moderate- to high-dimensional problems where a full covariance matrix is still feasible.

*   **Usefulness:** By reducing the per-iteration runtime without increasing the number of function evaluations, this work makes CMA-ES a much more practical tool. Researchers and practitioners who previously might have abandoned CMA-ES for simpler or less effective methods due to computational cost now have a compelling reason to use it for problems of larger scale. The fact that the authors have implemented their algorithm in a public library (Shark) further enhances its potential for immediate impact.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Pro:** Solves a long-standing and important computational bottleneck in a widely used, state-of-the-art optimization algorithm.
*   **Pro:** The proposed solution is elegant, technically sound, and supported by both a novel theoretical argument and strong, convincing empirical evidence.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and accessible.
*   **Pro:** The work has high practical significance and is likely to be widely adopted, advancing the state-of-the-art in derivative-free optimization.
*   **Pro:** The method achieves asymptotically optimal time and storage complexity for a full-rank covariance update.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   **Con:** The theoretical justification relies on the assumption of convergence of the covariance matrix shape, which is not formally proven for the general stochastic algorithm. However, this is a minor point, as the authors acknowledge it and the empirical results strongly validate the approach.

### Overall Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant and practical advance for a fundamental optimization algorithm. It is technically strong, clearly presented, and highly significant. I strongly recommend acceptance.
