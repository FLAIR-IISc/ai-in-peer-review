Summary and relation to prior work
- The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a version of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C instead of recomputing a matrix square root or eigendecomposition. Sampling uses x = m + σAy with y ~ N(0, I), retaining invariance properties of the sampling step.
- The key challenge is step-size adaptation (CSA): the standard CMA-ES uses C−1/2 in the evolution path pσ; replacing C−1/2 by A−1 introduces a rotation Et from the RQ decomposition C1/2 = AEt. The authors argue that tracking this rotation is cubic-time and impractical, so they approximate CSA by omitting Et and justify that the induced error on the norm of pσ vanishes as Ct stabilizes.
- They provide a lemma showing that, for a convergent normalized covariance sequence, the rotations Et stabilize (Et−1T Et → I), so the omission induces a vanishing error due to exponential forgetting in the CSA path.
- They extend efficient rank-one updates to At for the general CMA-ES with rank-1 and rank-µ updates, yielding O(µ d^2) time and optimal O(d^2) storage. A triangular matrix inverse can be computed in O(d^2), so A−1 can be obtained when needed without separate maintenance.
- Experiments on standard noiseless benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers), including randomly rotated versions, show essentially identical function evaluation counts compared to a reference CMA-ES implementation, with order-of-magnitude wall-clock speedups that grow with d. Comparisons to a delayed-update CMA-ES and to a non-triangular Cholesky update (Suttorp et al.) are included.

Strengths
- Clear practical contribution: a drop-in CMA-ES variant that reduces asymptotic per-iteration time to O(µ d^2) and storage to triangular form, with negligible impact on evaluations required.
- Solid algorithmic engineering: correct factorized implementation of rank-1 and rank-µ updates in the Cholesky domain; leveraging triangular solves/inversion; complexity optimality is compelling when full covariance adaptation is required.
- Theoretical intuition: while not a full convergence proof, the lemma and accompanying argument convincingly explain why omitting the rotation in CSA is benign in practice as Ct stabilizes. The focus on the norm of pσ is appropriate.
- Empirical evaluation across multiple dimensions and rotated problems; wall-clock time reported (often missing in this literature); code availability (Shark) enhances impact and reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Incorrect claim about eigenvalues: “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. The diagonal entries of A are not related to the eigenvalues of C except in special cases (e.g., diagonal C). The eigenvalues of A (a triangular matrix) equal its diagonal entries, but these are not the eigenvalues of C. Please correct and reformulate the monitoring suggestion (e.g., use trace, determinant, condition proxy via norms, or periodic EVD if needed).
- Theoretical justification is limited: Lemma 1 assumes convergence of normalized Ct; CMA-ES is stochastic and no convergence of Ct is shown here. The argument is plausible (and matches empirical results), but the formal gap should be acknowledged more explicitly. It would help to bound the error term in ‖pσ‖ as a function of recent rotations (e.g., angle between Et−1 and Et) without requiring full convergence, perhaps using Lipschitz continuity of the Cholesky map and known CMA-ES learning rates c1, cµ.
- Invariance: CSA is already known not to be fully affine-invariant; the proposed approximation further deviates. The paper reports rotation-invariant performance empirically; it would be useful to (i) report a metric of the induced rotations Et (you mention supplementary), (ii) quantify the effect on pσ norms and σ trajectories, and (iii) include non-rotational affine transforms (scalings).
- Experimental fairness: for Rosenbrock, excluding non-convergent runs biases the evaluation. Please report success rates and time-to-target conditional and unconditional statistics. Also clarify the decomposition frequency used in the “reference” CMA-ES (it is typically delayed in practice) and add a comparison to a tuned delay schedule comparable to state-of-the-art implementations to ensure speedups are not overstated.
- Robustness: all benchmarks are noiseless and unconstrained. Given CSA’s sensitivity, results on noisy functions and constraints (or box constraints via resampling) would strengthen the case. Also consider ill-conditioned problems at higher d and compare to LM-CMA/limited-memory variants to delineate regimes where full covariance remains competitive.
- Numerical stability: repeated rank-one Cholesky updates can accumulate round-off. Do you use periodic re-factorization or jitter to maintain SPD and symmetry? Report any safeguards and their frequency/cost.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the Cholesky update machinery is appropriate, the CSA approximation is well motivated, and empirical results support claims. The eigenvalue misstatement and the selective reporting on Rosenbrock should be corrected. Additional robustness experiments would improve confidence.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with useful pseudocode. Some references are malformed and should be fixed. Distinguish clearly between the orthogonal matrix E and the expectation operator to avoid confusion. Precisely state computational complexity including the cost of inverting A per iteration.

Originality
- Extends prior Cholesky updates (Suttorp et al.; Krause & Igel for (1+1)-CMA-ES) to the standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and provides a new CSA approximation with a theoretical rationale. This is a meaningful and nontrivial advance over existing factorized variants.

Significance
- High practical significance: CMA-ES is widely used; reducing per-iteration cost and memory without hurting evaluations will likely see adoption. The approach cleanly targets the regime where full covariance adaptation is still desired (non-separable, ill-conditioned problems).

Questions for the authors
- How often do you recompute A from C (or re-Cholesky) to mitigate numerical drift, if at all?
- What is the empirical distribution of the angle or Frobenius norm of Et−1T Et − I across runs? How many iterations until it stabilizes?
- How sensitive are results to µ and weighting schemes?
- Can you provide results on noisy benchmarks and report success rates on Rosenbrock?

Minor issues and typos
- Fix the eigenvalue claim regarding diag(A).
- Several references are garbled (years, venues); please clean up.
- Clarify the decomposition frequency used in the CMA-ES-Ref baseline.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, practical speedups with minimal change to evaluations.
- Clean algorithmic contribution with open-source implementation.
- Plausible theory and careful empirical study across dimensions and rotations.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees are limited; some claims (eigenvalues) are incorrect.
- Experimental scope omits noisy settings and reports biased statistics for Rosenbrock.
- Baseline configuration may not reflect best practices; needs clarification.
