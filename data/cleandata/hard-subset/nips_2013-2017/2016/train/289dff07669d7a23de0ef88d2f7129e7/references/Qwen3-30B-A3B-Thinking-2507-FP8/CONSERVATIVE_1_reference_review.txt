

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors claim this approach achieves the same optimization performance as the original CMA-ES while reducing runtime complexity from O(d³) to O(µd²) and storage complexity to O(d²). The theoretical justification centers on Lemma 1, which argues that the error from replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ vanishes as the algorithm converges.

The paper makes several reasonable claims that align with prior work on CMA-ES variants. The authors correctly cite the computational bottleneck of matrix square root calculations in CMA-ES and acknowledge previous attempts to address this, such as the delayed update strategies and restricted covariance models. Their approach represents a logical extension of work by Suttorp et al. and Krause and Igel, but with a focus on maintaining the full covariance matrix adaptation.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) provides a reasonable argument for why the approximation might work well in practice. The experimental results demonstrate a significant reduction in wall-clock time (up to 20x faster for d=64) with equivalent function evaluation counts on standard benchmark functions. The authors also correctly identify the benefits of storing the Cholesky factor (eigenvalues for free, stable inversion).

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising results, the paper falls short of NIPS's rigorous standards in several critical areas:

1. **Theoretical justification is insufficient:** Lemma 1 assumes Ct converges to a fixed shape, but CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm that does not necessarily converge to a fixed covariance matrix. The authors provide no evidence that Ct actually converges in practice, nor do they establish how quickly the error vanishes for realistic optimization problems. Without this, the theoretical claim of "no significant change in performance" remains unproven.

2. **Experimental validation is limited:** The experiments only use standard benchmark functions without testing on real-world applications where CMA-ES is typically deployed (e.g., reinforcement learning or hyperparameter tuning). The paper claims "rotation invariance" but provides no results on rotated problems beyond the limited set shown. Crucially, the paper doesn't test on the "Diffpowers" function with "arbitrarily bad conditioning" as mentioned in the introduction, which would be a critical test case for the approximation.

3. **Misleading complexity claims:** The paper claims "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity" but the runtime complexity is O(µd²), which is only optimal if µ = O(1). In standard CMA-ES, µ = O(log d), so the complexity is actually O(d² log d), not O(d²). This is a significant oversight in the theoretical claims.

4. **Lack of statistical validation:** The paper states that "Cholesky-CMA-ES required the same amount of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES" but provides no statistical tests (e.g., t-tests) to confirm this claim. The results in Figure 1 show similar curves, but without statistical evidence, it's impossible to conclude whether the difference is truly insignificant.

5. **Insufficient numerical stability analysis:** The paper presents Algorithm 2 for rankOneUpdate but provides no analysis of potential numerical issues that might arise from the approximation. The Cholesky factorization approach could introduce numerical instability, especially for ill-conditioned problems, which the authors acknowledge as a concern but don't address.

6. **Missing ablation studies:** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the Cholesky approximation from other potential speedups (e.g., the delayed update strategy). It's unclear whether the speedup is primarily due to the Cholesky factorization or other implementation choices.

**Overall assessment:** While the Cholesky-CMA-ES approach is reasonable and the experimental results are promising, the paper fails to provide the rigorous theoretical justification and thorough empirical validation required by NIPS standards. The theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported, and the experimental evaluation is limited to standard benchmarks without addressing critical edge cases. The paper's strongest claim—that the approximation does not deteriorate performance—remains unproven.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper represents a minor technical improvement to an established algorithm but lacks the depth of theoretical justification and comprehensive empirical validation required for NIPS. The authors should provide stronger theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence analysis with error bounds) and conduct more thorough experiments, including testing on real-world problems and critical benchmark functions like Diffpowers. Without these, the claims of "no significant change in performance" cannot be accepted at face value.
