

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes a novel variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization for covariance matrix updates. The core insight is that using the triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full matrix square root can reduce the runtime complexity from O(d^3) to O(µd^2) while maintaining comparable optimization performance. The authors provide theoretical justification (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by this approximation diminishes as the algorithm converges, and they empirically demonstrate significant speedups (up to 20x for d=64) without increasing the number of function evaluations needed to reach a given objective value.

The paper addresses a fundamental computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, which has been widely used in derivative-free optimization for over two decades. Previous approaches to reduce the computational burden (delaying updates, using diagonal approximations, or working with non-triangular Cholesky factors) either sacrifice performance or don't fully address the complexity issue. The authors' key contribution is showing that the Cholesky factorization can be used directly in the standard CMA-ES with minimal performance impact, while achieving asymptotically optimal complexity.

This work stands out for its elegant theoretical insight combined with practical implementation. The authors carefully analyze why the Cholesky approach works (by showing that the error vanishes as the algorithm converges) rather than simply proposing a heuristic. Their empirical validation across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions demonstrates the practical significance of the approach. The paper also highlights additional benefits of the Cholesky approach: optimal storage complexity, free eigenvalue computation, and numerically stable matrix inversion.

The significance of this work is substantial. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization, and its computational limitations have been a barrier to applying it to high-dimensional problems. By providing an approach that scales quadratically rather than cubically with dimension, the authors make CMA-ES applicable to larger problems than previously possible. This could have direct impact in areas like reinforcement learning (where CMA-ES is used for direct policy search) and hyperparameter optimization.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem statement to theoretical justification to empirical validation. The algorithm descriptions are precise, and the experiments are well-designed to demonstrate the claimed advantages.

While the theoretical justification relies on convergence assumptions that might not hold for all optimization problems, the empirical results strongly support the practical viability of the approach. The paper acknowledges this limitation and provides a compelling argument for why the error is negligible in practice. The authors also carefully compare their method to relevant prior work (including Suttorp et al. and Krause and Igel), situating their contribution clearly within the literature.

The main weaknesses are relatively minor in the context of the paper's strong contribution: the empirical validation is limited to standard benchmark functions (though these are the standard tests for CMA-ES), and the paper doesn't explore how this approach would perform on real-world problems beyond the benchmarks. However, given the paper's focus on a core algorithmic improvement, these are acceptable limitations.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in evolutionary computation. It's not just an incremental improvement to an existing algorithm but a bold rethinking of a fundamental component of CMA-ES with clear theoretical justification and empirical validation. The approach is elegant, practical, and addresses a long-standing computational bottleneck in a field where CMA-ES has been the dominant algorithm for decades.

In terms of NIPS criteria:
- Quality: High - Theoretical justification is sound, empirical results are comprehensive for the problem domain
- Clarity: High - Well-structured, clearly written
- Originality: High - The core idea is novel and represents a significant shift in how CMA-ES is implemented
- Significance: High - This could substantially broaden the applicability of CMA-ES to larger optimization problems

Given my preference for novel concepts over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution that advances the field. While it's not a revolutionary new algorithm for machine learning, it's a major improvement to a widely used optimization technique that could have substantial impact across multiple domains where derivative-free optimization is applied.

I recommend acceptance. This paper offers a bold, well-justified approach to a fundamental computational problem in an important optimization algorithm, with clear empirical validation of its practical benefits. The novelty and significance of the contribution outweigh the minor limitations in the theoretical justification and empirical scope.
