The paper proposes a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aiming to reduce runtime and storage complexity by utilizing a Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root for covariance matrix adaptation. The authors argue that this approach, coupled with a specific update rule, preserves the algorithm’s performance while offering computational advantages. They provide a theoretical justification for their approach, alongside empirical results demonstrating faster runtime, particularly in higher dimensions, without sacrificing the number of function evaluations.

The core idea is sound and addresses a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES – the computational cost of covariance matrix manipulation. The motivation is clearly stated, and the problem being tackled is relevant to the NIPS audience. The connection to prior work, particularly Suttorp et al. [2009] and Krause and Igel [2015], is appropriately acknowledged. The theoretical justification, leveraging Lemma 1 regarding the convergence of coordinate system rotations, is a significant strength, attempting to provide assurances against performance degradation. 

However, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from minor stylistic inconsistencies to potentially impactful technical simplifications.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a practical limitation of CMA-ES.
*   Presents a theoretically motivated approach.
*   Demonstrates improved runtime in experiments.
*   Clear exposition of the core concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor in Theoretical Justification:** While Lemma 1 is stated, the argument connecting it to the error introduced by replacing *st* with *ŝt* in equation (5) feels hand-wavy. The claim that the error vanishes relies on *Ct* changing slowly, which isn't guaranteed and isn't rigorously analyzed. The impact of this approximation on the algorithm's performance isn't thoroughly investigated.
*   **Experimental Setup Limitations:** The experimental evaluation is limited in scope. Only a handful of benchmark functions are used, and the comparison is mainly focused on runtime. A more comprehensive analysis of performance on different problem classes (e.g., multimodal, noisy) would strengthen the claims. The exclusion of trials where Rosenbrock did not converge is concerning – it suggests potential instability.
*   **Missing Details in Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 2, the rankOneUpdate procedure, lacks crucial details. It's not immediately clear how the algorithm handles potential numerical instability during the update. The authors mention using LAPACK for efficiency, but don’t describe *which* LAPACK routines are used, hindering reproducibility.
*   **Repetitive and Verbose Writing:** The paper is overly verbose, with several statements repeated across sections. The writing style could be significantly improved for conciseness and clarity. For example, the description of the importance of the Cholesky factor’s diagonal is repeated several times.
*   **Inconsistent Notation & Typos:** Numerous minor inconsistencies in notation exist (e.g., inconsistent use of subscripts and superscripts).  There are several typographical errors throughout the manuscript (e.g., "th Conference" in the introduction). While individually minor, their cumulative effect detracts from the paper’s professionalism.
*   **Insufficient Comparison to Suttorp-CMA-ES:** The comparison to Suttorp et al. [2009] is limited. A more thorough comparison, including a detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the two approaches, would be beneficial.
*    **Unclear Scaling Behavior:** The claim of “asymptotically optimal” complexity should be more rigorously substantiated. While O(µd²) is theoretically appealing, the actual performance in practice (particularly for large *d*) is crucial, and the authors acknowledge limitations related to cache size at d=256.
*   **Missing Discussion of Parameter Sensitivity**: The algorithm's performance may be sensitive to parameters like µ. A sensitivity analysis of these parameters and their impact on convergence and runtime is missing.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The idea is interesting and tackles a real bottleneck in CMA-ES. The theoretical justification, while not perfect, is a good starting point. The empirical results are encouraging.
*   **Con:** The theoretical justification lacks rigor. The experimental evaluation is limited. The paper suffers from numerous minor flaws (writing, notation, typos) that detract from its overall quality. The limited comparison to other related work and the missing discussion of parameter sensitivity are also concerning.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the Cholesky-CMA-ES presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field, the numerous flaws – particularly the lack of rigor in the theoretical justification, the limitations of the experimental evaluation, and the numerous stylistic and technical issues – weigh heavily against acceptance. The paper feels incomplete and insufficiently polished for a NIPS publication.

**Recommendation:** **Reject.**

I recommend rejection. The authors should address the theoretical weaknesses, expand the experimental evaluation, and thoroughly revise the manuscript to improve clarity, consistency, and overall quality. A more robust analysis, coupled with a more polished presentation, would be necessary for reconsideration.
