Summary
The paper proposes a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T and samples via x = m + σ A y with y ~ N(0, I). The key change is to avoid recomputing the symmetric square root C^{1/2} (or an eigendecomposition) and instead update A in O(μ d^2) per iteration via rank-one Cholesky updates. The only algorithmic deviation from standard CMA-ES is in step-size adaptation: the CSA path uses A^{-1}(m_{t+1}-m_t)/σ_t rather than C^{-1/2}(m_{t+1}-m_t)/σ_t. The authors justify this approximation by arguing that the orthogonal factor E_t in the RQ decomposition C^{1/2}_t = A_t E_t changes slowly (Lemma 1: if the normalized covariance C̄_t converges in shape, then E_t stabilizes), so the norm of the CSA path is asymptotically unaffected due to exponential forgetting. Experiments on standard deterministic benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) with random rotations compare the proposed method (Cholesky-CMA-ES) against a reference CMA-ES (with delayed decompositions) and a non-triangular Cholesky update approach. The proposed method matches the number of function evaluations of the reference and yields substantial wall-clock speedups for moderate to high dimensions.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on established CMA-ES theory, rank-one Cholesky updates (e.g., Suttorp et al., Krause & Igel), and LM-/sep-/low-rank CMA-ES variants that reduce complexity by restricting the model. The claimed novelty is an A-based update for the full CMA-ES (with μ > 1), including a justification for using A^{-1} in CSA without tracking the changing orthogonal rotation. This sits between the exact-eigendecomposition CMA-ES and low-rank/diagonal approximations.

Quality
- Positives: The approach is plausible and practically motivated; using triangular factors is a standard idea and rank-one Cholesky updates are well-known and efficient. The asymptotic O(μ d^2) update matches the lower bound implied by full covariance storage and multiplies. The experimental setup uses multiple rotated functions and reports both evaluation counts and wall-clock time, which is appropriate for complexity claims. The CSA approximation is at least qualitatively justified (if the covariance shape stabilizes, the rotation error on the norm diminishes).
- Concerns:
  1) A serious technical error: the paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is incorrect in general. The diagonal of the Cholesky factor contains square roots of the pivots (related to leading principal minors), not eigenvalues. Consequently, the stated benefit “we get the eigenvalues for free” is wrong. This undermines one of the advertised advantages.
  2) The theoretical justification for the CSA approximation is weak. Lemma 1 requires convergence of the normalized covariance C̄_t; CMA-ES does not have a general proof of such convergence under the considered settings (noise-free, multimodal, etc.), and the paper only cites convergence in expectation for simplified algorithms. No non-asymptotic control of the deviation in ‖p_σ‖ is provided, and no bound linking the introduced rotation error to step-size bias or performance is given. Given the importance of CSA for scale adaptation, stronger analysis or at least diagnostics would be expected.
  3) Numerical stability of the repeated rank-one Cholesky updates (Algorithm 2) is not analyzed. Classical cholupdate/choldowndate routines have well-known stability regimes and failure modes; the paper should either reference such routines or provide conditioning/error analyses. It is unclear whether the proposed update handles cases where coefficients or accumulated rounding could cause loss of SPD.
  4) Baselines and coverage: While the authors include a delayed-decomposition reference and a non-triangular Cholesky variant, comparisons to widely used efficient variants (sep-CMA-ES, active CMA-ES, LM-CMA-ES) on appropriate regimes are missing. The proposed method targets full covariance scenarios; still, practitioners often compare time-to-target across these families, and it would be informative to see the tradeoffs.
  5) Evaluation scope: All tests are deterministic, unconstrained, and synthetic. There is no assessment on noisy functions, constraints, or application-motivated tasks (RL policy search, hyperparameter tuning), where step-size robustness is critical. The “rotation invariance” test is briefly mentioned; quantitative metrics of invariance (e.g., success probability or evaluation count distributions pre/post rotation) are not presented in the main text.
  6) Reproducibility and presentation: Some references are malformed; key implementation choices (BLAS/LAPACK versions, threading, machine details) matter for wall-clock comparisons but are not fully specified. Figures are not in the submission, and no code link is provided (the text says the method was added to Shark, but a commit or tag would help).

Clarity
The manuscript is generally readable, with a clear algorithm presentation. However, the incorrect statement about eigenvalues must be corrected. The assumptions underlying Lemma 1 should be stated explicitly and discussed in light of known behavior of CMA-ES. Reference formatting needs fixing.

Originality
Moderate. Maintaining a triangular Cholesky factor and using rank-one updates is not new; the main novelty is extending this to the standard CMA-ES with μ > 1 and arguing that CSA can operate with A^{-1} without tracking the rotation. This is an incremental but practically relevant step.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners who need full-covariance CMA-ES in moderate-to-high dimensions and for whom decomposition costs dominate. However, the missing theoretical guarantees on CSA and limited validation weaken the case for broad adoption. The incorrect eigenvalue claim must be addressed.

Pros
- Practical speedups without loss in evaluation efficiency on standard tests.
- Conceptually simple modification; leverages established linear-algebra routines.
- Asymptotically optimal per-iteration time and storage for full covariance updates.

Cons
- Incorrect claim about “eigenvalues for free” from Cholesky diagonal.
- Weak theoretical guarantees for the CSA approximation; no error bounds or robustness analysis.
- Limited baselines and scenarios (no noise/constraints/application tasks).
- No stability analysis of repeated rank-one updates.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the eigenvalue claim and adjust the discussion of monitoring/conditioning accordingly.
- Provide empirical diagnostics of the CSA path discrepancy relative to the standard method (e.g., distribution of ‖p_σ‖, step-size trajectories) and, if possible, non-asymptotic bounds under mild assumptions.
- Compare against sep-/active-/LM-CMA-ES in regimes where full covariance is still plausible, and include BBOB/COCO-style testbeds with noise.
- Analyze numerical stability of Algorithm 2 (or adopt/benchmark against standard cholupdate routines) and report failure rates/condition numbers.
- Release code and ensure references are complete and properly formatted.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is practically interesting and could be impactful, but the paper contains a factual error about eigenvalues, lacks rigorous support for the CSA approximation, and provides limited validation. With these issues addressed and a broader, stronger evaluation, this work could be suitable for a future submission.

Confidence
High.
