- Summary: Proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES that updates the Cholesky factor to reduce covariance update cost to O(µd^2) while keeping comparable function-evaluation performance and faster runtimes in high dimensions.
- Theoretical justification: Provides a lemma on convergence of coordinate system rotations explaining why Cholesky updates do not degrade performance.
- Computational efficiency: Identifies covariance update as a bottleneck and shows substantial complexity and runtime reductions.
- Empirical validation: Experiments show consistent speedups over CMA-ES variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES), especially at large d.
- Clear writing: Paper is well-organized and explains Cholesky decomposition application accessibly.
- Practical relevance: Approach yields meaningful speedups for real-world high-dimensional tasks like RL and hyperparameter tuning.
- Limited novelty: Core idea builds on prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES work (Suttorp, Krause & Igel); novelty is in extension and analysis, not a radical new concept.
- Dependence on µ: O(µd^2) improvement may diminish for very large µ; constant-factor impact should be discussed.
- Error analysis limited: More detailed analysis needed on the impact of uncorrected coordinate-rotation error on convergence and problem types most affected.
- Cache/memory effects: Runtime sensitivity to cache (noted at d=256) warrants deeper investigation of memory access patterns.
- Quality: 9/10 — technically sound with strong theory and experiments, minor deduction for limited error analysis and µ dependence.
- Clarity: 8/10 — generally clear but some derivations (Cholesky update) need more explanation linking equations to intuition.
- Originality: 7/10 — valuable extension of prior work with strong analysis, but not a radical novelty.
- Significance: 8/10 — likely to be adopted due to notable speedups in high-dimensional optimization.
- Arguments for acceptance: Significant runtime improvements, solid theoretical basis, thorough experiments, addresses a known CMA-ES bottleneck.
- Arguments against acceptance: Builds on prior work limiting novelty and requires further analysis of introduced error and convergence impacts.
- Overall recommendation: Accept — well-motivated, validated improvement with significant practical value; minor additional analyses could strengthen the paper.
