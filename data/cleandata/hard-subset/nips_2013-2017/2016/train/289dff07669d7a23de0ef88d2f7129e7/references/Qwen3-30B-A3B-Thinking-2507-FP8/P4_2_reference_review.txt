

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors build upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who explored Cholesky factorization in evolution strategies, but extend this approach to standard CMA-ES with a theoretical justification for why their approximation doesn't deteriorate performance. The key innovation is providing a convergence argument for the error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2}_t with A^{-1}_t (where A_t is the Cholesky factor of C_t), demonstrating that the error vanishes as the algorithm converges.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with necessary background on CMA-ES and Cholesky decomposition, followed by a detailed description of the proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) and the theoretical justification (Lemma 1). The authors provide empirical evidence through comprehensive experiments on standard benchmark functions across various dimensions, comparing against multiple CMA-ES variants including the standard implementation with delayed matrix decomposition (CMA-ES-Ref), a delayed update version (CMA-ES/d), and the non-triangular Cholesky approach (Suttorp-CMA-ES).

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) provides a solid foundation for why the approximation error becomes negligible during convergence, addressing a key concern in previous attempts to use Cholesky factorization in CMA-ES.
2. The paper clearly demonstrates that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations) as standard CMA-ES while significantly reducing wall-clock time (up to 20x faster for d=64).
3. The algorithmic details are well-presented, including the rankOneUpdate procedure (Algorithm 2), which enables efficient updates of the Cholesky factor.
4. The experimental design is thorough, testing multiple benchmark functions with varying dimensions and rotation invariance properties.
5. The paper identifies and addresses a significant computational bottleneck in CMA-ES (matrix square root computation) that has been recognized in previous literature [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001; Suttorp et al., 2009].

**Weaknesses:**
1. The theoretical justification relies on the convergence of C_t to a certain shape, which is not strictly proven for the randomized CMA-ES algorithm. While the paper provides a reasonable argument for why the error becomes negligible, a more rigorous convergence analysis would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
2. The paper claims "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity" but doesn't provide a formal proof that O(µd²) is indeed optimal for the full covariance matrix adaptation case. This is somewhat implied rather than explicitly proven.
3. The numerical stability of the proposed approach compared to standard CMA-ES is not thoroughly analyzed, which is particularly important given the potential for numerical errors in Cholesky factorization.
4. The empirical evaluation is limited to standard benchmark functions. While these are appropriate for demonstrating the computational advantage, showing results on more complex, real-world optimization problems would strengthen the practical significance.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES with a theoretically justified solution.
- The experimental results demonstrate clear and significant speedups without performance degradation.
- The approach is practical and could be easily integrated into existing CMA-ES implementations.
- The paper makes a clear contribution to the optimization literature with potential impact on machine learning applications where CMA-ES is commonly used.

*Con:*
- The theoretical justification is not as rigorous as it could be, particularly regarding the convergence of C_t.
- The claimed "optimal" complexity is not formally proven.
- The empirical evaluation lacks results on more complex, real-world problems.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and well-executed contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The authors successfully address a significant computational bottleneck in CMA-ES by proposing a Cholesky-based variant with a theoretical justification for its validity. The paper is clearly written, the experiments are well-designed, and the results demonstrate clear practical benefits with minimal performance trade-offs. While the theoretical justification could be strengthened with a more rigorous convergence analysis, the empirical evidence strongly supports the approach's effectiveness.

The work is significant as it provides a practical solution for scaling CMA-ES to higher dimensions while maintaining its optimization performance. This is particularly relevant for machine learning applications like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning where CMA-ES is commonly employed. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field, advancing the state of the art in efficient evolution strategies.

The paper meets NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, and presents a novel approach that advances the state of the art in optimization algorithms. The significance is demonstrated through both theoretical arguments and empirical results showing substantial computational improvements.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. The contribution is valuable, well-executed, and addresses a meaningful problem in the field of optimization.
