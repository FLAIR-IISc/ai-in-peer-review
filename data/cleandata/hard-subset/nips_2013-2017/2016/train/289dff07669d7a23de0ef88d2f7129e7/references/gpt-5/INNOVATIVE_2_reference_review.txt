Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = A A^T via rank-one updates, achieving O(µ d^2) time per iteration and optimal O(d^2) storage while preserving the behavior of full CMA-ES. The key obstacle is that the standard step-size control (CSA) uses C^{-1/2}, not A^{-1}. The authors propose to replace C^{-1/2} by A^{-1}, which introduces a changing orthogonal rotation E_t from the RQ decomposition C^{1/2} = A E. They provide a lemma arguing that E_t stabilizes as the covariance “shape” converges, so the error in CSA vanishes asymptotically (and is small in practice due to exponential forgetting). They give an efficient rank-one update routine for the triangular factor, note ancillary benefits (cheap eigenvalues, cheap inversion, optimal memory), and present experiments on standard and rotated benchmarks up to d=256. Results show the same number of function evaluations as a strong CMA-ES baseline, but orders-of-magnitude lower wall-clock time (e.g., ~20× at d=64). Comparisons include a delayed-eigendecomposition CMA-ES and a non-triangular Cholesky variant (Suttorp et al.).

Relation to prior work: Prior efforts speed up CMA-ES by delaying eigendecompositions or restricting covariance structure (diagonal, low-rank, or limited-memory), risking slower convergence on non-separable problems. Suttorp et al. and Krause & Igel explored updating factorized forms, but either for special cases ((1+1)-CMA-ES, multi-objective) or with non-triangular factors that complicate CSA and stability. This paper extends triangular rank-one updates to the standard CMA-ES with a principled treatment of CSA, positioning the method as a drop-in, full-covariance replacement with optimal asymptotic complexity.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clean and well-motivated approximation for CSA using A^{-1}, together with a convergence argument for the induced rotation. This addresses a longstanding practical blocker to using Cholesky factors in standard CMA-ES without recomputing square roots.
- Asymptotically optimal complexity for full-covariance CMA-ES (O(µ d^2) time, O(d^2) storage) with minimal changes to the algorithmic behavior.
- Practical impact: Large wall-clock speedups with essentially unchanged evaluation counts across a variety of (rotated) benchmarks, including ill-conditioned cases. This directly benefits practitioners who rely on full CMA-ES for non-separable problems.
- Implementation details matter: triangular factor makes inversion and eigenvalue access cheap and numerically stable; only one factor to maintain.
- Empirical study is reasonably comprehensive across dimensions and includes rotation invariance checks.

Weaknesses
- Theory is qualitative: the lemma is asymptotic and assumes covariance “shape” convergence; there are no finite-time error bounds on σ-adaptation or explicit control of the deviation from invariance. Given the centrality of CSA to CMA-ES robustness, a quantitative bound or at least a measured discrepancy between the original and approximate CSA would strengthen the case.
- Invariance: The paper notes the original CSA’s invariance is not proven; this variant further approximates it. Empirically the method remains robust, but the implications for invariance could be discussed more deeply (e.g., failure modes on rapidly changing covariances or under severe noise).
- Experimental reporting: For Rosenbrock, excluding non-convergent runs biases runtime statistics; please report success rates and time-to-target with failures counted (e.g., as timeouts) for fairness. Wall-clock comparisons may also be sensitive to implementation details; a brief CPU profiling and memory footprint comparison would be helpful.
- Baselines: While the choice to focus on full-covariance methods is reasonable, including results for strong reduced-rank/limited-memory CMA-ES (e.g., LM-CMA) would better contextualize when full covariance with optimal O(d^2) updates is preferable.
- Clarity/polish: Some references are malformed; Table 1 and supplementary measurements (rotation magnitude, Et) are essential enough that a distilled version should be in the main text. A brief discussion of numerical stability under long sequences of rank-one updates (e.g., reorthogonalization or re-factorization triggers) would add confidence.

Quality
Technically sound, if modest on formal guarantees. The algorithmic engineering is careful, the derivation of the triangular rank-one updates is clear, and the CSA approximation is well justified heuristically and supported by experiments. The empirical evaluation indicates the core claims (same evals, much faster time) hold across diverse settings. Some experimental and theoretical details could be tightened (success rates, bounds, robustness under noise).

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The algorithm boxes are clear. Please fix reference formatting, include the benchmark table, and bring key supplementary plots (e.g., measured rotation drift and pσ-norm discrepancy) into the main paper.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of updating factorizations is not new; the novelty lies in making triangular Cholesky updates work for standard CMA-ES with a principled CSA approximation and argument about rotation convergence. This is a meaningful and nontrivial extension beyond prior special-case or non-triangular approaches.

Significance
High for the CMA-ES community and practitioners: achieving optimal complexity without sacrificing performance should change default implementations. The speedups are substantial and likely to spur adoption.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Quantify the CSA approximation: measure ∥pσ,new∥ vs ∥pσ,ref∥ and the distribution of σ updates across runs; report Et E_{t-1}^T deviation over time.
- Report success rates and time-to-target with failures included; add robustness under noise and constraints if possible.
- Discuss numerical stability and whether occasional re-factorization is needed.
- Include memory and profiling data to substantiate the “optimal” complexity claims empirically.
- Clarify fairness of baselines (e.g., decomposition delay choices) and consider adding LM-CMA as a contextual baseline.

Overall recommendation: Accept
Rationale: While the theoretical contribution is modest, the paper offers a clean, principled modification that unlocks an optimal-complexity full-covariance CMA-ES with large practical impact and negligible loss in evaluations. This is a meaningful advance for a widely used algorithm and, in my view, worth accepting despite the lack of formal bounds on the CSA approximation.

Confidence: 0.75
