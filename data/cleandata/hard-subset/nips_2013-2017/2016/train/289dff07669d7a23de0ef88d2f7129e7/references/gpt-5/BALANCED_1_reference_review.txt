Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = AAT, rather than C itself or its symmetric square root. Building on rank-one Cholesky update techniques (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), the authors extend these ideas to the standard population CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and provide a modified cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) that replaces the C−1/2 transform in pσ by A−1. They argue that the difference between A−1 and C−1/2 amounts to an orthogonal rotation that vanishes asymptotically if the normalized covariance converges, and hence the impact on step-size control should be small. The resulting per-iteration time complexity becomes O(µ d^2) and storage O(d^2), which the authors claim are optimal for full covariance adaptation. Experiments on classic black-box benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers), including rotated variants, show similar function evaluation counts to a reference CMA-ES, but substantially reduced wall-clock time (e.g., ~20× at d=64). The method is compared to a delayed-eigendecomposition baseline and to a non-triangular Cholesky variant.

Relative to prior work: CMA-ES with delayed decompositions (Hansen & Ostermeier; Hansen 2015) trades accuracy for reduced cubic costs; limited-memory/diagonal variants (Ros & Hansen 2008; Loshchilov 2014/2015; Sun et al. 2013) reduce complexity but sacrifice model expressivity; non-triangular Cholesky updates have been applied in (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES (Suttorp et al. 2009; Krause & Igel 2015). This paper’s main novelty is extending triangular rank-µ updates to standard CMA-ES and justifying the approximate CSA without polar decomposition.

Strengths
- Clear practical contribution: Achieves O(µ d^2) time per iteration with full covariance, avoiding expensive decompositions while keeping evaluation counts essentially unchanged. This is valuable for medium–high dimensions where matrix factorizations dominate.
- Sensible theoretical justification: The lemma and discussion explain why using A−1 in CSA introduces only a rotational error that decays when the normalized covariance shape stabilizes, aligning with intuition about CMA-ES dynamics.
- Empirical evidence: Broad sweep on standard and rotated benchmarks, with 100 trials per setting, shows consistent speedups in wall-clock time and parity in evaluations compared to a strong reference implementation.
- Implementation practicality: Triangular factorization allows easy inversion in O(d^2), and the rank-one update is standard and efficient.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Incorrect claim about eigenvalues: The paper states that the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix. This is false in general; Cholesky diagonals relate to pivots/leading principal minors, not eigenvalues. This needs correction; while not central to the algorithm, it undermines one advertised advantage.
- Invariance and CSA accuracy: The invariance properties are weakened by replacing C−1/2 with A−1 in pσ. The asymptotic argument is plausible but lacks quantitative bounds on the deviation of ‖pσ‖ (and hence σ updates) under non-stationary covariance evolution. Empirical rotation “invariance” is tested, but more targeted diagnostics of CSA bias would help.
- Experimental reporting: For Rosenbrock, excluding non-convergent trials can bias results. Success rates and time-to-target distributions should be reported across all trials. No experiments with noise, constraints, or real ML tasks (RL/hyperparameters) are shown, despite CMA-ES’s use in those settings.
- Numerical stability details: Rank-one updates are typically stable for positive β, but the paper could more clearly discuss stability, conditioning, and accumulation of round-off over many updates at higher d. The footnote claiming optimality could be clarified with a formal lower bound argument (any full dense covariance update must at least read/write O(d^2) data).
- Clarity/minor issues: Some references appear malformed; a few derivations are sketched but could use more detail (e.g., conditions under which the normalized covariance converges; precise assumptions in Lemma 1; effect on the distribution of pσ).

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically sound overall with a solid algorithmic contribution; the CSA approximation is reasonable but not rigorously bounded; the eigenvalue claim is an error that should be corrected. Experimental methodology is mostly solid, with the caveat on excluded trials and missing noisy/constrained settings.
- Clarity: Generally clear with helpful algorithms; some notation and reference formatting issues. The mistaken eigenvalue statement should be fixed; add a brief discussion on numerical stability of Algorithm 2.
- Originality: Extends prior Cholesky-update ideas to standard CMA-ES with rank-µ and provides a new, lightweight CSA justification. This is a meaningful, though incremental, contribution in a widely used method.
- Significance: Practical impact is high for users of CMA-ES on medium/high-dimensional problems where factorization costs dominate. The speedups without loss in evaluation efficiency are compelling.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, significant speedups with maintained solution quality.
- Simple, implementable method added to an open-source library.
- Sound motivation and reasonable theoretical explanation.

Cons for acceptance
- Incorrect statement about eigenvalues; needs correction.
- Limited theoretical guarantees on CSA approximation; lack of quantitative error bounds.
- Incomplete experimental breadth (noisy/constrained/real tasks) and exclusion of failed runs on Rosenbrock.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the Cholesky/eigenvalue claim and revise any downstream claims relying on it.
- Provide quantitative analysis or empirical calibration of the bias/variance in ‖pσ‖ induced by using A−1, possibly including bounds under bounded rotation drift or a sensitivity analysis comparing A−1 vs C−1/2 in controlled settings.
- Report success rates and include all trials; for non-convergent runs, report budgets at which the target is not reached.
- Add experiments with noisy objectives and a constrained or RL/hyperparameter tuning task to validate robustness of the CSA approximation in more realistic settings.
- Discuss numerical stability and conditioning of repeated rank-one updates; include safeguards (e.g., occasional re-factorization checks) and their overhead.
- Clarify the “asymptotically optimal” claim with a brief lower bound argument.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper offers a useful and broadly relevant acceleration of a staple optimization algorithm with convincing empirical gains and a reasonable theoretical rationale. With the correction of the eigenvalue claim and modest strengthening of experimental reporting, it would make a solid NeurIPS contribution.
