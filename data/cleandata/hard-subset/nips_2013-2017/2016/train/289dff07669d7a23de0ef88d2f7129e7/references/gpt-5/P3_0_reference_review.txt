Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C instead of recomputing the matrix square root via eigendecomposition. The rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates are decomposed into rank-1 updates of A in O(µ d^2) time per iteration, yielding optimal asymptotic storage and per-iteration update complexity for full-covariance CMA-ES. The main obstacle is the CSA step-size adaptation, which in the standard algorithm requires C^{-1/2}. The authors replace it by A^{-1}, thereby omitting an iteration-dependent orthogonal rotation. They justify this approximation via a lemma: if the normalized covariance Ct / det(Ct)^{1/d} converges, then the orthogonal factors in the RQ decomposition converge and the rotation error introduced into CSA decays (due to exponential weighting in CSA). Empirically, on rotated benchmarks up to d=256, Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the number of function evaluations of a reference implementation and yields large wall-clock speedups (e.g., ~20x at d=64).

Relation to prior work is appropriate: diagonal/low-rank/restricted models (Ros & Hansen; Loshchilov; Sun et al.), previous updates directly on factorizations (Suttorp et al., Krause & Igel) including (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES, and information-geometric motivation for CMA updates (Akimoto et al.). The contribution here is extending quadratic-time triangular Cholesky updates to standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and addressing the CSA complication.

Strengths
- Well-motivated and practically relevant: eliminating cubic eigendecompositions is impactful for medium-to-large d and cheap objective functions.
- Algorithmically sound: rank-1 updates of a triangular Cholesky factor are well-known and O(d^2); the overall per-iteration update cost O(µ d^2) is optimal for dense full-covariance adaptation.
- Clean integration into standard CMA-ES: sampling via A is standard; the covariance update is mathematically equivalent to the original (modulo numerics); only CSA is approximated.
- Empirical evidence across rotated and ill-conditioned benchmarks indicates unchanged sample efficiency and substantial wall-clock gains.
- Clear exposition of the source of CSA approximation error and why it should diminish as the shape stabilizes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical justification is limited: the lemma assumes convergence of the normalized covariance shape C̄t, which is not guaranteed for the stochastic algorithm in general; no finite-time error bounds or robustness guarantees are provided for non-stationary regimes (e.g., along curved valleys like Rosenbrock). The argument is plausible but qualitative.
- A factual error: “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix” is incorrect in general. The Cholesky diagonal are not eigenvalue square roots unless C is diagonal. Hence “eigenvalues for free” is not a valid advantage. This should be corrected; if eigenvalues are needed, one still requires an eigendecomposition (or iterative methods).
- Baselines and fairness: the “reference” CMA-ES recomputes the matrix square root every iteration for the dimensions considered. In practice, popular implementations recompute every O(d) iterations; the paper includes CMA-ES/d, but a more careful tuning of the delay schedule (and reporting amortized costs) would strengthen claims. A comparison to LM-CMA (Loshchilov) would help contextualize speed/accuracy trade-offs for larger d, even if the objectives are different (full vs limited-memory).
- Numerical stability: rank-1 Cholesky updates are stable for positive-definite updates, but repeated updates plus scaling may accumulate round-off. The paper would benefit from discussing regularization/jitter, reconditioning, and whether downdates ever occur. Algorithm 2’s numerical properties and relation to standard cholupdate/choldowndate routines should be clarified.
- Clarity gaps: the derivation showing that scaling by sqrt(1−c1−cµ) followed by µ+1 rank-1 updates exactly matches Eq. (2) at the C level should be sketched or referenced precisely. For CSA, one can avoid explicitly forming A^{-1} by triangular solves; the implementation details (and complexity) should reflect that. Experimental setup lacks some details (hardware, BLAS settings, cache effects) important when making strong wall-clock claims.

Quality
Technically competent overall; the CSA approximation is reasonable and supported empirically, but lacks rigorous bounds. The incorrect eigenvalue claim is a notable flaw. Experiments are convincing for the regime studied but could include stronger baselines and more thorough runtime accounting.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized; algorithms are explicit. Some statements (eigenvalues) are incorrect; a few derivations and numerical considerations need clarification. References formatting needs cleanup.

Originality
Extends known Cholesky-based update techniques to full CMA-ES with rank-µ and addresses the CSA issue with a new approximation and asymptotic argument. While building on prior work, the package is novel and practically useful.

Significance
High practical significance for users of CMA-ES in ML (policy search, hyperparameter optimization) when full covariance is needed. Likely to be adopted in libraries. Theoretical contributions are modest but helpful.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please correct the claim about Cholesky diagonals and eigenvalues. If you only need principal variances along A’s axes, say so explicitly.
- Can you provide a finite-time bound on the CSA norm error under a Lipschitz assumption on the rotation Et (e.g., ‖Et−1Et^T−I‖ ≤ δt) and quantify its effect on σ updates?
- Detail numerical safeguards: do you add jitter to the diagonal, periodically re-factorize, or monitor SPD violations?
- Report amortized per-iteration costs for all methods, including sampling cost O(λ d^2), and tune the delay schedule for the reference baseline. Include LM-CMA as a complementary baseline to position your method’s regime of advantage.
- Provide code and scripts used for the experiments for reproducibility.

Pros
- Significant wall-clock speedups with unchanged evaluation counts
- Conceptually simple, drop-in replacement for standard CMA-ES
- Optimal per-iteration time and storage for full-covariance adaptation

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees limited; no finite-time analysis
- Incorrect claim about eigenvalues from Cholesky diagonal
- Baseline fairness and numerical stability discussion could be improved

Recommendation
Lean accept, contingent on correcting the eigenvalue claim and clarifying numerical and baseline details. The contribution is practical, broadly useful, and likely to impact the community, even if the theoretical analysis is not fully tight. Confidence: high.
