## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

**Summary:**

This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) dubbed "Cholesky-CMA-ES." The core idea is to update and store the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix instead of the matrix itself, leveraging the fact that sampling from a multivariate normal distribution only requires the factorization, not the inverse or square root directly. The authors argue this reduces both runtime and memory complexity, particularly for high-dimensional problems, while maintaining the algorithm’s performance. They provide a theoretical justification for the approach, focusing on the impact of coordinate rotations during step-size adaptation, and empirically demonstrate the speedup achieved through experiments on benchmark functions.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established foundation of CMA-ES [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001].  It acknowledges previous attempts to speed up CMA-ES by delaying updates [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001], restricting model complexity [Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008], or updating the Cholesky factor directly [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015]. Crucially, the authors differentiate their work by providing a theoretical argument for why their Cholesky-based update *doesn't* deteriorate performance, which was lacking in earlier approaches, especially concerning the global step-size adaptation. They position their work as a refinement of the approach by Krause & Igel [2015] to the full CMA-ES, beyond the (1+1) case.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Justification:**  The paper provides a strong theoretical argument for the validity of the Cholesky-based update. The analysis of coordinate rotations and Lemma 1, while requiring careful consideration, effectively addresses a potential concern regarding the step-size adaptation and demonstrates a diminishing error as the algorithm converges.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The reported speedup, particularly at higher dimensions, is substantial. The theoretical O(µd²) complexity is a significant improvement over the standard CMA-ES’s O(d²) update complexity when full covariance adaptation is necessary.
* **Memory Efficiency:** Storing only the triangular Cholesky factor inherently reduces the memory footprint, which is a practical advantage for large-scale problems.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The explanation of the Cholesky decomposition and its relation to the square root is clear.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the benefits of the Cholesky-CMA-ES in terms of runtime, without sacrificing performance (measured by the number of function evaluations).
* **Practical Implementation:** The implementation within the Shark library is a valuable contribution, making the algorithm readily available to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Numerical Stability:** While the authors mention the advantage of easily computing the inverse of the Cholesky factor, a more detailed discussion of numerical stability concerns with repeated Cholesky updates could be beneficial. Are there scenarios where this approach might be more susceptible to numerical issues than the standard CMA-ES?
* **Benchmarking Scope:**  The benchmark functions are fairly standard. While sufficient to demonstrate the speedup, exploring more challenging, real-world problems (e.g., those arising in reinforcement learning or hyperparameter optimization) could further strengthen the results. The functions in Table 1 do not truly exhibit the ill-conditioning often found in practical problems.
* **Dependence on LAPACK:** The reliance on LAPACK for efficiency, while practical, introduces an external dependency.  Discussing the implications of this dependency (e.g., portability, licensing) would be helpful.
* **Complexity Analysis Caveats:** The claim of "asymptotically optimal time complexity" is qualified with a footnote acknowledging the dependence on the matrix multiplication algorithm.  A more thorough discussion of the impact of different matrix multiplication algorithms could be included.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and theoretically justified improvement to a widely-used optimization algorithm.
* The demonstrated speedup and memory efficiency are significant and offer practical benefits.
* The implementation and thorough empirical evaluation further strengthen the contribution.
* The work is well-aligned with the NIPS focus on machine learning and optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis, while strong, is complex and might be difficult for some readers to fully grasp.
* The limited scope of the benchmarking prevents a comprehensive assessment of the algorithm's performance on truly challenging real-world problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a compelling improvement over the standard CMA-ES in terms of computational efficiency and memory usage, with a solid theoretical foundation. While there are minor weaknesses regarding numerical stability and benchmarking scope, these do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:** **Strong Accept**.  I believe this paper is suitable for presentation at NIPS.
