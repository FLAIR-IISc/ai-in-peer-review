

# Peer Review: "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the computationally expensive matrix square root operation with a Cholesky factorization. The authors claim this approach achieves optimal asymptotic runtime complexity (O(µd²)) while maintaining comparable optimization performance to the standard CMA-ES. The theoretical justification hinges on Lemma 1, which shows that the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ diminishes as the algorithm converges. Empirical results demonstrate significant speedups (up to 20x for d=64) with no increase in function evaluations.

## Strengths

The paper addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, which has been recognized in the literature since Hansen and Ostermeier's original work. The authors correctly situate their work within the context of existing approaches to reducing CMA-ES complexity, particularly Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015). The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) is novel and well-motivated, providing a solid foundation for why the approximation should work well in practice. The empirical evaluation is thorough across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions (d=4 to 256), demonstrating the claimed speedup without compromising function evaluations.

The paper's implementation details are clear and reproducible, with the authors providing specific implementation choices (e.g., using LAPACK for efficiency) and noting important practical considerations like cache effects at large dimensions. The additional benefits of the Cholesky approach—providing eigenvalues for free and enabling efficient matrix inversion—are well articulated.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

While the paper makes a practical contribution, several concerns weaken its theoretical and empirical claims:

1. **Theoretical justification is incomplete**: Lemma 1 assumes Ct converges to a certain shape, but CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm where Ct does not necessarily converge. The paper acknowledges this but fails to provide error bounds for the approximation. Without quantifying the error in the step-size adaptation (equation 5), the claim that "no significant change in function evaluations" is supported only empirically, not theoretically.

2. **Overstated optimality claim**: The paper claims "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity" but doesn't provide a lower bound proof. The complexity O(µd²) is only optimal if µ = O(d), which contradicts the standard CMA-ES where µ = O(log d). The authors note this but don't clarify how their complexity claim holds in practice.

3. **Limited empirical evaluation**: The experiments focus only on standard benchmark functions without real-world applications where CMA-ES is commonly used (e.g., hyperparameter tuning in deep learning). The paper doesn't compare against recent CMA-ES variants like LM-CMA (Loshchilov, 2015) that address the computational bottleneck through different means.

4. **Memory hierarchy effects**: The paper notes that for d=256, the matrix size exceeded L2 cache, affecting performance. This suggests the speedup may diminish at very large dimensions due to memory hierarchy effects, a critical consideration for real-world applications. The paper doesn't explore this limitation further.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper correctly cites Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) as predecessors but doesn't adequately differentiate their approach from the standard CMA-ES. Suttorp et al. proposed a non-triangular Cholesky factorization, while this paper uses a triangular one. The key novelty is extending the Cholesky approach to the standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification, but the paper could better contrast with Loshchilov's LM-CMA (2015), which also addresses computational bottlenecks for large-scale problems.

## Significance

The paper makes a meaningful practical contribution to a widely-used optimization algorithm. The 20x speedup for d=64 is significant for applications where CMA-ES is used (e.g., hyperparameter tuning in deep learning). However, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this translates to real-world performance gains in machine learning applications, which would strengthen its significance claim.

## Recommendation

The paper presents a technically sound and practically valuable extension to CMA-ES with strong empirical results. However, the theoretical justification is incomplete, and the empirical evaluation lacks real-world context. The claims about "optimal" complexity need qualification, and the paper would benefit from addressing the limitations related to memory hierarchy effects at large dimensions.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Solves a well-known computational bottleneck in a widely-used algorithm
- Theoretical justification provides insight into why the approximation works
- Empirical results show significant speedup without performance degradation
- Clear implementation details and reproducibility

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Theoretical justification lacks error bounds for the approximation
- Claims of "optimal" complexity are overstated without lower bound proof
- Limited empirical evaluation (lacks real-world applications)
- Doesn't compare with recent CMA-ES variants like LM-CMA

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid technical contribution that addresses a genuine problem in CMA-ES. While the theoretical justification could be strengthened and the empirical evaluation expanded, the paper makes a valuable practical contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the theoretical limitations and strengthen the comparison with recent CMA-ES variants. The work is relevant to NIPS as it contributes to optimization algorithms widely used in machine learning, particularly in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning.
