

# Peer Review: "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary
This paper proposes a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) called "Cholesky-CMA-ES" that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors demonstrate theoretically and empirically that this modification maintains the same optimization performance as the standard CMA-ES while significantly improving runtime complexity from O(d³) to O(μd²) (where μ = O(log d)) for the covariance matrix update. The paper builds on prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), but extends it to the standard CMA-ES with a theoretical justification for why the approximation does not deteriorate performance. The approach also provides additional benefits including reduced memory requirements, free eigenvalues of the covariance matrix, and numerically stable inversion.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a well-justified theoretical argument (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ in the step-size adaptation vanishes asymptotically as the algorithm converges. The experimental results are thorough and support the theoretical claims, demonstrating that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but with significantly faster runtime (up to 20x faster for d=64). The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that the method scales quadratically and may not be suitable for extremely high-dimensional problems where maintaining a full covariance matrix becomes infeasible.

The paper is complete as a scientific contribution, not merely a position paper, and the authors are careful to evaluate both strengths (speedup, memory reduction) and weaknesses (theoretical complexity is O(μd²) rather than pure O(d²)). However, the theoretical analysis is limited to the step-size adaptation component rather than providing a full convergence analysis of the entire algorithm.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate pseudocode (Algorithms 1 and 2) that makes the proposed method easy to understand. The theoretical justification is presented logically, though some parts of the mathematical explanation could be clearer (particularly regarding the relationship between the RQ-decomposition and the error analysis). The empirical section is well-structured, with appropriate benchmark functions and careful experimental design (including rotation invariance tests). The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results, though some additional implementation details about the Shark library integration would be helpful.

## Originality
The paper makes a valuable contribution by extending previous work on Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants to the standard algorithm with a theoretical justification for why the approximation is valid. While Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) had previously explored Cholesky factorization for specific CMA-ES variants, this paper demonstrates that the approach is valid for the standard CMA-ES with a rigorous theoretical foundation. The paper properly situates its work within the literature and clearly differentiates from previous approaches. The specific combination of using Cholesky factorization for the standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification for the error seems novel and represents a meaningful advancement.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. CMA-ES is one of the most widely used derivative-free optimization algorithms, particularly in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. The computational bottleneck of matrix square root calculation has been a known limitation for high-dimensional applications. The proposed method directly addresses this limitation, providing a practical solution that can be easily integrated into existing implementations. The demonstrated speedup (20x for d=64) makes CMA-ES more applicable to larger optimization problems. The additional benefits (eigenvalues, stable inversion) provide further value beyond just speed. The paper addresses a practical problem in a meaningful way that advances the state of the art in derivative-free optimization.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Solves a real and important computational bottleneck in a widely used algorithm
- Provides solid theoretical justification for the approximation
- Empirical results clearly demonstrate significant speedup without performance degradation
- The approach is simple to implement and provides additional useful benefits (eigenvalues, stable inversion)
- Well-structured and clearly written paper

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical complexity is O(μd²) rather than pure O(d²) (since μ = O(log d)), which slightly overstates the complexity improvement
- Limited to benchmark functions; would benefit from real-world machine learning applications
- Doesn't compare against other recent high-dimensional CMA-ES variants (e.g., Loshchilov's LM-CMA)
- Numerical stability analysis is mentioned but not thoroughly explored

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The technical soundness is excellent, the theoretical justification is convincing, and the empirical results are compelling. The work directly addresses a practical limitation of a widely used algorithm with significant real-world implications.

The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison with recent high-dimensional CMA-ES approaches (e.g., Loshchilov's LM-CMA), and by including experiments on real machine learning tasks to demonstrate practical impact. However, these are minor improvements to an already strong paper.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a significant contribution to an important area of optimization that has direct applications in machine learning. The work represents a meaningful advancement in the state of the art for derivative-free optimization, with clear practical implications.

**Overall Recommendation: Accept**
