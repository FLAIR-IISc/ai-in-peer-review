This paper presents a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, dubbed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aiming to improve runtime and storage complexity by updating and storing the Cholesky factor instead of the covariance matrix directly. The core idea – leveraging the Cholesky decomposition for efficiency – isn’t entirely novel (Suttorp et al. and Krause & Igel have explored related concepts), but the authors claim a theoretical justification for why this approach doesn't deteriorate performance *and* achieves optimal asymptotic complexity, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. They support this with empirical results showing significant speedups, especially in higher dimensions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty (Moderate):** While not a completely groundbreaking idea, the theoretical justification connecting the Cholesky update to maintaining algorithm performance is a significant contribution. The claim of optimal asymptotic complexity is compelling. The extension to the full CMA-ES, moving beyond the (1+1) and MO-CMA-ES contexts of previous work, is a clear step forward.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a fairly detailed mathematical justification, including Lemma 1 regarding the convergence of rotation matrices. The description of the Cholesky update process (Algorithm 2) appears correct and efficient.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate a consistent speedup in runtime as dimensionality increases. The comparison against CMA-ES-Ref and other variants is well-structured, and the results presented are clear. Showing runtime comparisons, rather than just iteration counts, is crucial and appreciated.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The explanation of the motivation and the algorithm itself is easy to follow.
* **Significance:**  A practical speedup for CMA-ES, especially in high dimensions where the matrix square root computation dominates, is genuinely useful. Many real-world applications benefit from efficient derivative-free optimization, and this contribution addresses a key bottleneck.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Depth in Error Analysis:** While Lemma 1 is helpful, the discussion surrounding the approximation introduced by omitting the correction factor in equation (3) feels somewhat hand-wavy. A more rigorous analysis of the error bounds would strengthen the theoretical contribution. The statement that the error "will be small and decrease over time" requires more justification.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments, while well-executed, primarily focus on standard benchmark functions. While these are good for initial validation, exploring the algorithm’s performance on more challenging, real-world problems would be beneficial.
* **Cache Effects:** The authors acknowledge that performance degrades when the matrix size exceeds the L2 cache. This is a crucial limitation that should be discussed more extensively. It suggests that the theoretical asymptotic complexity might not translate directly into practical gains for extremely high-dimensional problems on standard hardware.
* **Comparison to Other State-of-the-Art Methods:** The comparison is primarily focused on variations *within* the CMA-ES family. A comparison to other competitive derivative-free optimization algorithms (e.g., direct search methods, particle swarm optimization) would provide a broader context.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant practical bottleneck in CMA-ES.
* The theoretical justification, while not fully exhaustive, is a novel and important contribution.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear speedup, particularly in higher dimensions.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The error analysis associated with the approximation could be more rigorous.
* The experimental evaluation lacks diversity in terms of problem types.
* The impact of cache effects limits the practical scalability of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a practical speedup for a widely used algorithm, and the theoretical justification is a significant achievement. While some aspects of the error analysis and experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the overall impact of the work is substantial. I believe the novelty and potential significance outweigh the minor weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I am willing to overlook the incomplete error analysis and somewhat limited experimental scope given the strong theoretical justification and compelling empirical results. This work has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of CMA-ES in real-world applications, and that’s a major plus.
