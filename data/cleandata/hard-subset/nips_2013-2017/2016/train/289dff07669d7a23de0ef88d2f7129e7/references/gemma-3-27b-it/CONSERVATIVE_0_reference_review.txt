## Review of CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper presents a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aiming to reduce runtime and memory complexity by updating the Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix instead of the matrix itself. The authors argue that this approach, coupled with a specific update rule, preserves the algorithm’s performance while offering computational advantages, particularly in high dimensions. They provide a theoretical justification for their approach and empirical results comparing it to standard CMA-ES implementations.

The core idea of utilizing the Cholesky decomposition for updates is not entirely novel, as acknowledged by the authors who cite related work [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015]. However, the extension to the full CMA-ES framework and the attempt to provide a theoretical guarantee distinguishing it from previous work are potentially valuable contributions. The identified complexity benefits – achieving O(µd²) update complexity – are indeed significant, especially for larger problem dimensions.

**Strengths:**

* **Potential for Speedup:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES demonstrably offers runtime improvements, especially as dimensionality increases, which is a crucial benefit for practical applications. The experimental results (Figures 1 & 2) clearly show this advantage.
* **Theoretical Motivation:** The authors attempt to ground their method in theoretical considerations, specifically addressing the potential introduction of error due to the removal of the coordinate system correction factor and providing a Lemma to mitigate concerns. While the strength of this theoretical justification is debatable (see Weaknesses), the attempt is appreciated.
* **Clear Presentation of the Algorithm:** The paper provides a clear algorithmic description (Algorithm 1 & 2) and explains the rationale behind the modifications.
* **Well-Established Baseline:** The comparison against a standard CMA-ES implementation with a delayed update strategy provides a valuable benchmark.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Improvement, Not Transformative:** While the speedup is valuable, the work feels more like an optimization *of* CMA-ES rather than a fundamentally new approach to derivative-free optimization. The core CMA-ES algorithm remains largely unchanged.
* **Theoretical Justification is Weak:** The theoretical argument concerning the Lemma 1 and the error introduced by omitting the coordinate system correction factor relies on the assumption of convergence. This assumption is not always valid for CMA-ES, particularly in challenging optimization landscapes. The claim that the error will decrease over time is not definitively proven, and the impact of this error on practical performance requires more rigorous investigation. Simply stating that the error will vanish due to exponential weighting feels insufficient.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup, while reasonable, lacks depth. The use of relatively standard benchmark functions, and the limited number of dimensions explored beyond d=128 (due to L2 cache issues) raise concerns about the generalizability of the results. More extensive testing on a wider range of problems, including high-dimensional real-world applications, is necessary.  The observation that performance degrades at d=256 due to cache limitations should be a warning sign that the method’s scaling isn’t as straightforward as claimed.
* **Lack of Detailed Comparison with Suttorp-CMA-ES:** The comparison with Suttorp et al. [2009] is cursory. A more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the Cholesky-CMA-ES and this earlier work would strengthen the paper. The authors state that the Cholesky-CMA-ES provides the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free, an advantage over Suttorp's approach, but do not explain how this is used to improve performance.
* **Unnecessary Repetition:** The introduction contains redundant statements about the strengths of CMA-ES and the benefits of the proposed method.


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Offers a demonstrable and potentially significant speedup for CMA-ES, especially in high dimensions.
* Provides a theoretical justification, albeit imperfect, for the proposed modification.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The improvement is incremental and lacks fundamental novelty.
* The theoretical justification is not fully convincing.
* The empirical evaluation is limited and may not fully capture the performance characteristics of the algorithm.
* The paper does not thoroughly address limitations and potential drawbacks.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejection**. While the paper presents a technically sound optimization of CMA-ES with a clear benefit in runtime, the lack of groundbreaking novelty, coupled with the relatively weak theoretical justification and limited empirical evaluation, does not meet the high standards for acceptance at NIPS. The paper feels more suited for a more applied conference where incremental improvements are more readily accepted.  The authors need to address the concerns regarding the theoretical justification, expand the empirical validation, and provide a more compelling argument for the significance of their contribution. I suggest a strong revision with a much more thorough evaluation and a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the approach.
