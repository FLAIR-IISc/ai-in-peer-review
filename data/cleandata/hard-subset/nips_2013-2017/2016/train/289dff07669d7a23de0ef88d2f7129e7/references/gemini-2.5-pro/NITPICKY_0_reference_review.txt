**Summary**

This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aimed at reducing the computational and storage complexity of the algorithm. The standard CMA-ES requires an O(d^3) eigenvalue decomposition to update its covariance matrix, which can be a bottleneck for high-dimensional problems (d) or cheap objective functions. The authors propose to maintain and update the Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix directly, which can be done in O(µd^2) time. A key challenge is that the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism in CMA-ES requires the matrix square root inverse (C^(-1/2)), which is not readily available from the Cholesky factor (A, where C=AA^T). The authors' main contribution is to approximate C^(-1/2) with A^(-1) and provide a theoretical argument, based on the convergence of the normalized covariance matrix, that the error introduced by this approximation vanishes as the optimization process converges. Experimental results on several benchmark functions show that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves significant speed-ups in wall-clock time compared to the standard CMA-ES and other variants, without a discernible increase in the required number of function evaluations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and important limitation of CMA-ES, namely its computational complexity, which hinders its application to higher-dimensional problems. The reported speed-ups are substantial and practically relevant.
2.  **Novelty:** While direct updates on a matrix factorization have been proposed before (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), the specific approach to handle the CSA update is novel. The distinction made between a general factorization and a unique triangular one (which constrains rotational degrees of freedom) is insightful and provides a clear advantage over prior work.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental comparison is conducted against appropriate baselines, including the standard reference implementation and a delayed-update variant. The results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed method in terms of runtime.

**Weaknesses**

Despite its promising empirical results, the paper suffers from several flaws ranging from a major technical error to a lack of rigor in its theoretical claims and poor manuscript preparation.

1.  **Major Technical Error:** In Section 3, under "Properties of the update rule," the paper makes the following claim: "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix, that is, we get the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free." This statement is fundamentally incorrect. The diagonal elements of a Cholesky factor L in a decomposition C = LL^T are not, in general, related to the eigenvalues of C. This is only true if C is a diagonal matrix. This error invalidates a significant claimed advantage of the proposed method, namely the ability to monitor the problem conditioning and eigenvalues at no extra cost.

2.  **Weak Theoretical Justification:** The central theoretical argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix sequence C̄_t converges as t→∞. The authors themselves acknowledge this is a strong assumption for a stochastic algorithm like CMA-ES, stating, "we cannot assume convergence of Ct." They then attempt to justify it by referencing work on the convergence of the *expectation* of C_t in *simplified* algorithms. This is not a rigorous justification for the actual algorithm presented. While the intuition that a slowly changing C_t leads to a small error is plausible, the formal theoretical support as presented is weak and relies on an unproven premise.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility Issues:**
    *   In Section 4, the authors state: "For Rosenbrock we excluded the trials in which the algorithm did not converge to the global optimum." This is poor scientific practice. It is crucial to report the failure rates for all compared algorithms. Without this information, the results for this benchmark are potentially biased and uninterpretable, as one algorithm might appear faster simply because it failed more often on difficult initializations.
    *   Footnote 2 is confusing. It states the complexity is "related to the complexity of multiplying two µ× d matrices." The update is described as µ rank-one updates, each of complexity O(d^2), leading to a total of O(µd^2). The connection to µ×d matrix multiplication is not explained and seems extraneous.

4.  **Poor Manuscript Preparation:** The paper exhibits a notable lack of care in its preparation, which undermines its credibility.
    *   **References:** The reference list is corrupted with formatting artifacts (e.g., `Akimoto et al\.,? \Q2012\E, Akimoto et al\.,`), rendering it difficult to read and verify citations. This suggests a failure to properly compile the bibliography before submission.
    *   **Typos:** There are distracting typos, such as "it is it conjectured that it does not" in Section 2.1.
    *   **Inconsistencies:** The complexity of the standard covariance matrix decomposition is O(d^3), but the paper describes it imprecisely as `ω(d^2)`. Citation styles are inconsistent throughout the text (e.g., `[Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001]` vs. `Gomez et al. [2008]`).

**Recommendation**

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a computationally efficient algorithm with promising empirical results, I must recommend **rejection** in its current form. The combination of a major technical error regarding eigenvalues, a weak and non-rigorous theoretical justification for its core approximation, questionable experimental reporting practices, and extremely sloppy manuscript preparation makes it fall short of the high standards expected for NIPS. The core idea is valuable, but the work requires significant revision to correct the technical inaccuracies, strengthen the theoretical analysis, improve the experimental reporting, and undergo a thorough proofreading and formatting pass.
