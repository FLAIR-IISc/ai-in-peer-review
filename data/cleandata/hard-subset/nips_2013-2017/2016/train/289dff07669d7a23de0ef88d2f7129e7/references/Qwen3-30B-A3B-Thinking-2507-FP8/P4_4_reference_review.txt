

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the standard CMA-ES algorithm that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The standard CMA-ES has O(d^3) runtime per iteration due to the need to compute the matrix square root of the covariance matrix, which becomes problematic for high-dimensional optimization. Previous approaches to address this include delaying the matrix square root computation (leading to O(d^2) complexity) or restricting to diagonal covariance matrices (which can significantly degrade performance on non-separable problems). The authors argue that their Cholesky-based approach provides optimal asymptotic complexity for the covariance update while maintaining similar optimization performance. They provide theoretical justification (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor diminishes as the algorithm converges. The paper compares Cholesky-CMA-ES with standard CMA-ES (with delayed matrix square root computation), CMA-ES with fixed delay (CMA-ES/d), and Suttorp-CMA-ES (a non-triangular Cholesky approach) on standard benchmark functions across various dimensions (d = 4 to 256).

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) is well-constructed and addresses a key concern about the validity of replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor. The authors carefully analyze the error introduced and demonstrate that it diminishes as the algorithm converges. The derivation of the Cholesky update rules (Algorithms 1 and 2) is clear and correctly follows from the standard CMA-ES formulation.

2. **Novelty**: The paper makes a clear contribution by extending Cholesky-based updates from special cases (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES) to the standard CMA-ES, providing a theoretically justified approach that maintains performance while reducing complexity. The authors properly distinguish their work from prior attempts (Suttorp et al., Krause and Igel) by highlighting the triangular structure and its advantages.

3. **Significance**: The computational complexity improvement is significant, especially for high-dimensional problems where the standard CMA-ES becomes prohibitively expensive. The empirical results demonstrate a 20x speedup for d=64, with the speedup increasing with dimensionality. The method maintains the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES, which is crucial for practical applications.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized with clear explanations of the standard CMA-ES (Section 2), the proposed approach (Section 3), and experimental results (Section 4). The algorithms are well-described, and the theoretical justification is accessible to readers familiar with optimization.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees**: While Lemma 1 provides a justification for the diminishing error as the algorithm converges, the paper does not provide a formal convergence analysis for the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm itself. It's unclear whether the approximation affects convergence rates or whether the algorithm still converges to the global optimum under the same conditions as the standard CMA-ES.

2. **Experimental Scope**: The experiments are limited to standard benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) and do not include more challenging real-world optimization problems. The paper does not address whether the speedup holds for non-convex or constrained optimization problems, which are common in applications like reinforcement learning.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions using LAPACK for efficiency but doesn't discuss specific implementation details that might affect performance, such as numerical stability considerations or parallelization potential. The claim that "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues" is slightly misleading; they are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix, not necessarily the covariance matrix itself.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides a theoretically justified approach to reduce computational complexity of CMA-ES
- Demonstrates significant empirical speedup (up to 20x) with minimal impact on optimization performance
- Addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES
- Clear presentation of the method and its advantages
- The approach is directly applicable to many current applications of CMA-ES

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond the error analysis in Lemma 1
- Experimental results are confined to standard benchmark functions without real-world validation
- The speedup is asymptotic, and the paper doesn't fully address whether the constant factors make the method practical for moderately sized problems (d < 64)
- The paper could better clarify the relationship between their approach and the "optimal" complexity claim (the O(µd^2) complexity depends on µ = O(log d), but the paper doesn't establish whether this is the optimal complexity for the problem)

## Overall Assessment
The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a theoretically sound and empirically validated method to reduce the computational complexity of CMA-ES. The Cholesky-CMA-ES variant achieves optimal asymptotic complexity for covariance updates while maintaining the same optimization performance as the standard CMA-ES. The theoretical justification is strong, and the experimental results clearly demonstrate the runtime benefits, especially for high-dimensional problems. While the paper could strengthen its theoretical guarantees and expand its experimental validation, the contribution is significant for the field of derivative-free optimization. The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on high-impact algorithmic improvements with clear theoretical and empirical foundations. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a meaningful problem in optimization.
