This paper proposes a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aiming to reduce runtime and memory complexity by directly updating the Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix instead of the matrix square root. The motivation stems from the computational bottleneck imposed by the matrix square root calculation, particularly in high-dimensional spaces. The authors provide a theoretical justification for the approximation and demonstrate empirical improvements in runtime, particularly as dimensionality increases.

The core idea – updating the Cholesky factor directly – isn’t entirely novel, as referenced in the introduction (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). However, the key contribution lies in extending this approach to the *standard* CMA-ES and providing a theoretical argument relating to the convergence behavior despite approximating the step-size adaptation. The authors correctly identify the potential issue with introducing a rotation in the coordinate system due to the Cholesky decomposition and provide a lemma attempting to justify its negligible impact as the algorithm converges.

**Strengths:**

*   **Practical Improvement:** The experimental results clearly demonstrate a significant speed-up in runtime, especially for higher dimensional problems. This is a tangible benefit for CMA-ES users.
*   **Theoretical Motivation:** The attempt to justify the approximation through the analysis of coordinate system rotation is a commendable effort, adding depth beyond a purely empirical contribution. The lemma relating to convergence of the rotation matrix is potentially insightful.
*   **Well-Established Baseline:** The comparison against the standard CMA-ES (with delayed updates) and other variants like Suttorp-CMA-ES provides a solid benchmark.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the modifications to the CMA-ES algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While the application to the *standard* CMA-ES and the theoretical justification are valuable, the core idea of updating the Cholesky factor has precedent. The degree of novelty feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
*   **Theoretical Gap:** The lemma regarding the convergence of the rotation matrix is presented without substantial proof. While intuitively plausible, a more rigorous derivation would significantly strengthen the argument. The connection between this convergence and the impact on optimization performance isn’t fully articulated – it's stated that the error should be small, but a more quantitative assessment would be beneficial.
*   **Empirical Limitations:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. While several benchmark functions are used, the range of problems doesn't fully explore the potential weaknesses of the Cholesky-CMA-ES. Specifically, the impact of the approximation on ill-conditioned problems or those requiring high precision is not thoroughly investigated. The statement that performance is the same in terms of function evaluations is noteworthy; however, if runtime is reduced, it's important to discuss the implication of potentially needing to run the algorithm for fewer iterations to reach a given accuracy.
* **Cache Effects:** The authors acknowledge the impact of exceeding the L2 cache at d=256, but don't delve into the implications for scalability beyond this point. This is a critical limitation given the stated goal of scaling up CMA-ES.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:** The paper presents an incremental improvement to a well-established algorithm. The theoretical justification, while promising, lacks sufficient rigor. The empirical evaluation, while positive, isn't exhaustive enough to fully validate the claims. Given the focus of NIPS on groundbreaking research, the paper doesn’t quite meet the threshold for acceptance in its current form.

**For Acceptance:** The paper addresses a practical bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm. The demonstrated runtime improvement is significant, particularly for high-dimensional problems. The theoretical analysis, even if incomplete, represents a valuable attempt to understand the impact of the approximation. It provides a useful addition for practitioners of CMA-ES.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the work is technically sound and presents a useful improvement, it lacks the substantial novelty and rigorous theoretical backing expected at NIPS. The paper feels more suitable for a more applied conference or journal. The authors should address the gaps in the theoretical justification and expand the empirical evaluation to demonstrate the robustness of the Cholesky-CMA-ES across a wider range of problem types and dimensions. Further, a more in-depth analysis of the cache effects and scalability limitations is needed.
