

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary
This paper proposes a variant of CMA-ES called Cholesky-CMA-ES that replaces the matrix square root operation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors address a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES where the covariance matrix square root computation dominates runtime for large dimensions. While previous work (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) explored Cholesky updates for specialized CMA-ES variants, this paper extends the approach to standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification for why the approximation does not deteriorate performance. The authors provide a convergence argument (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} vanishes asymptotically as the algorithm converges. The paper demonstrates significant runtime improvements with minimal impact on function evaluations on standard benchmark problems.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by providing Lemma 1, which rigorously justifies why the Cholesky approximation maintains performance as the algorithm converges. This addresses a key concern that previous attempts at Cholesky-based CMA-ES might degrade performance.

2. **Practical Impact**: The proposed method achieves asymptotically optimal O(µd²) runtime complexity for covariance updates (vs. O(d³) for standard CMA-ES), while maintaining the same function evaluation count. This is a significant practical improvement for large-scale optimization problems.

3. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against relevant baselines (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions. The results consistently show significant wall-clock time improvements (up to 20x faster for d=64) with no loss in solution quality.

4. **Additional Benefits**: The Cholesky approach provides eigenvalues for free, enables quadratic-time inversion of the covariance matrix, and reduces memory complexity to optimal levels - all valuable practical advantages.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Scope**: While Lemma 1 provides a strong asymptotic argument, the paper doesn't thoroughly analyze the finite-time behavior of the approximation error. For practical optimization problems, the convergence rate matters, and the paper lacks analysis of how quickly the error vanishes in realistic optimization scenarios.

2. **Incomplete Comparison with Alternative Approaches**: The paper compares against CMA-ES variants with delayed covariance updates but doesn't adequately contrast with more recent low-dimensional approximation methods (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015; Sun et al., 2013). It would be valuable to understand how Cholesky-CMA-ES compares to these methods in terms of both speed and solution quality.

3. **Limited Error Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide detailed analysis of the actual error introduced by the approximation in the step-size adaptation. A quantitative measure of this error across different dimensions and problem types would strengthen the theoretical claims.

4. **Scalability Concerns**: While the paper demonstrates speedups up to d=256, it doesn't address how the method would perform on extremely large dimensions (e.g., d > 1000) where even O(d²) operations become expensive. The paper mentions that "if the dimensionality gets so large that maintaining a full covariance matrix becomes computationally infeasible, one has to resort to low-dimensional approximations," but doesn't explain how Cholesky-CMA-ES fits into this landscape.

## Recommendation

The paper presents a solid contribution to derivative-free optimization with a theoretically grounded approach to reducing computational complexity in CMA-ES. The theoretical justification is strong for the asymptotic case, and the empirical results demonstrate clear practical benefits. However, the limited analysis of finite-time behavior and the incomplete comparison with alternative approaches prevent this from being a breakthrough contribution for NeurIPS.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Provides a theoretically justified approach to a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES
- Demonstrates significant practical runtime improvements with minimal loss in solution quality
- Offers additional benefits (eigenvalue extraction, memory efficiency) beyond just speed
- Well-written and thoroughly evaluated against relevant baselines

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- The theoretical justification only covers asymptotic behavior, not finite-time performance
- Lacks comprehensive comparison with recent low-dimensional approximation methods
- Doesn't provide detailed analysis of the approximation error in practical optimization
- Limited discussion of scalability to extremely high dimensions

## Final Recommendation
I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature, but the theoretical analysis could be strengthened to address finite-time behavior, and the experimental comparison could be expanded to include more recent low-dimensional approximation methods. With these improvements, the paper would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS. The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the approximation error and its practical impact on optimization performance across different problem types and dimensions.
