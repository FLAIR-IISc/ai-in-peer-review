## Review of CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm, focusing on a more efficient covariance matrix update using the Cholesky decomposition. The theoretical justification is strong, and the empirical results clearly demonstrate the computational advantages, particularly in higher dimensions. While not a revolutionary departure, it’s a significant refinement to a widely used algorithm. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The CMA-ES is a powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm, well-established in areas like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. A major computational bottleneck is the covariance matrix adaptation step, particularly the square root or Cholesky decomposition. This paper addresses this by proposing a Cholesky-CMA-ES variant that directly updates the Cholesky factor, avoiding the costly recomputation of the matrix square root at each iteration. 

The authors correctly position their work within the existing landscape of solutions. They discuss prior attempts at reducing computational cost through delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) and restricted covariance models (Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008).  They acknowledge the work of Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) on updating Cholesky factors, but highlight that their approach provides a theoretical guarantee of performance preservation within the standard CMA-ES framework, something lacking in previous attempts. The core contribution is extending the work of Krause & Igel (2015) from the (1+1)-CMA-ES to the full CMA-ES, offering a broader applicability.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a strong theoretical justification for the proposed approach, specifically addressing the potential error introduced by avoiding the correction factor in the step-size adaptation. Lemma 1 and the accompanying explanation offer a compelling argument for convergence, even with this approximation.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The empirical results definitively demonstrate the speedup achieved by the Cholesky-CMA-ES, particularly as dimensionality increases. The O(µd²) complexity is a clear improvement, and the experimental validation aligns perfectly with the theoretical predictions. The observed 20x speedup at d=64 is substantial.
* **Optimal Storage:**  The use of the Cholesky factor inherently reduces memory footprint, which is crucial for large-scale optimization problems. This is a significant practical benefit.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanation of the algorithm is clear, building logically from the background on CMA-ES and Cholesky decomposition.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments on a variety of benchmark functions, evaluating both runtime and number of function evaluations.  The inclusion of rotated test functions is also a positive step.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Numerical Stability:** While the authors mention the benefit of efficient inverse computation with the Cholesky factor, they don't delve deeply into the potential numerical stability issues that can arise when repeatedly updating the Cholesky decomposition, particularly with ill-conditioned matrices.  A brief discussion of this aspect and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., re-orthogonalization) would strengthen the paper.
* **Cache Effects:** The authors briefly acknowledge the impact of cache size on performance at d=256. A more in-depth analysis of the memory access patterns and potential optimization strategies to mitigate cache misses would be beneficial.
* **Comparison with other efficient CMA-ES variants:** Although they compare to delayed updates and Suttorp et al.(2009), a brief comparison to more recent, memory-efficient variants like those using low-rank approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) would provide valuable context.  Acknowledging the trade-offs between full covariance adaptation and low-rank approximations would be helpful.
* **Equation (5) explanation:** The justification for equation (5) and its reliance on Lemma 1 is slightly terse. More detailed elaboration on how the error diminishes with convergence would improve readability.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Significant speedup in a widely used optimization algorithm.
*  Strong theoretical justification for the proposed method.
*  Comprehensive experimental validation.
*  Clear and well-written presentation.
*  Addresses a practical bottleneck in CMA-ES.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Minor lack of discussion regarding numerical stability.
*  Limited exploration of cache effects and further performance optimizations.
*  Could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to other recent CMA-ES variants.




**5. Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Page 6, Lemma 1:**  Clarify the assumption that the rotation matrix converges, or acknowledge it as a limitation.
* **Page 7, Equation (5):** Expand the explanation surrounding the error introduced by the approximation and its connection to Lemma 1.
* **Page 8, “Properties of the update rule”**:  Expand on the implications of quadratic complexity in the context of extremely high-dimensional problems.
* **Experiment section:** Provide more detailed information about the hardware used for the experiments.
* **Future Work:**  Mention potential avenues for future research, such as combining the Cholesky-CMA-ES with low-rank approximation techniques for even higher scalability.



**Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable contribution to the field of evolutionary computation. The Cholesky-CMA-ES represents a practical and theoretically sound improvement to a cornerstone algorithm. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.**
