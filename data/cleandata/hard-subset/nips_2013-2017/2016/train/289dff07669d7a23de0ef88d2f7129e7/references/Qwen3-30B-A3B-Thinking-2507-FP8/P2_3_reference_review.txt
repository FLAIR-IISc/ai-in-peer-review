

# Review: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root operation with a Cholesky factorization for covariance matrix updates. The authors demonstrate that their approach maintains the same performance as the standard CMA-ES while reducing computational complexity from O(d³) to O(d²) for the covariance update, with optimal storage complexity. This addresses a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES where the matrix square root computation dominates runtime for large dimensions. The work builds upon previous attempts by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) who explored Cholesky factor updates for specific CMA-ES variants, but this paper provides the first theoretical justification for why the approximation doesn't deteriorate performance in the standard CMA-ES.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical justification for their approach (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ vanishes as the algorithm converges. This theoretical foundation is particularly valuable as previous attempts lacked such justification. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing on standard benchmark functions across multiple dimensions (4 to 256), with 100 trials per function/dimension. The results clearly show that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the reference implementation but achieves significant speedups (up to 20x for d=64), with the speedup increasing with dimensionality. The authors also appropriately discuss the limitations of their approach (e.g., performance degradation when matrix size exceeds L2 cache).

**Clarity (Excellent):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section clearly explains the CMA-ES algorithm and the role of the matrix square root. The proposed method is introduced logically, with the theoretical justification presented as a natural extension of the background. The algorithms (1 and 2) are clearly presented and sufficiently detailed for reproduction. The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented with appropriate figures and tables.

**Originality (Strong):** While the use of Cholesky factorization in CMA-ES has been explored before (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015), this paper makes a significant original contribution by providing a theoretical justification for why the approximation is valid in the standard CMA-ES. Previous work lacked this theoretical foundation, making the approach seem like a heuristic. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by establishing convergence properties of the approximation error. The paper also identifies a subtle but important nuance: the need for the Cholesky factor to be triangular (not just any factorization) to maintain the bijective mapping between the covariance matrix and its factorization.

**Significance (High):** This work addresses a practical bottleneck in CMA-ES, which is widely used in machine learning for hyperparameter tuning and reinforcement learning. The computational complexity reduction is substantial and directly impacts the usability of CMA-ES for higher-dimensional problems. The paper demonstrates that the speedup increases with dimensionality, making it particularly valuable for large-scale optimization problems. For practitioners using CMA-ES, this could mean the difference between feasible and infeasible optimization runs on modern hardware. The authors also note that their approach maintains the ability to compute eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for monitoring problem conditioning, which is valuable for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

**Minor Theoretical Gap:** While the theoretical justification (Lemma 1) is strong, it assumes convergence of the covariance matrix shape (C̄t → C̄). For a randomized algorithm like CMA-ES, the authors might consider discussing how this assumption holds in practice, particularly for non-convex problems where convergence is not guaranteed. The paper mentions "in simplified algorithms the expectation of Ct converges" (Beyer, 2014), but a bit more discussion of how this applies to the specific case of the CMA-ES would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

**Limited Comparison with Other Low-Rank Methods:** The paper compares against CMA-ES variants with delayed updates and a non-triangular Cholesky approach (Suttorp-CMA-ES), but doesn't directly compare with other low-rank CMA-ES variants (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015). While not strictly necessary, a brief comparison would help contextualize the relative advantages of the Cholesky approach versus other dimensionality-reduction strategies.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a clear, well-justified contribution to an important optimization algorithm that is widely used in machine learning. The theoretical foundation, empirical validation, and practical significance are all excellent. The paper addresses a real bottleneck in CMA-ES with a solution that maintains performance while significantly improving computational efficiency. The work is clearly written, technically sound, and provides a valuable tool for practitioners working with derivative-free optimization.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental algorithmic improvement with clear implications for machine learning applications. The approach could be particularly valuable for hyperparameter tuning in deep learning models, where CMA-ES has shown promise but is limited by computational complexity.

The only minor improvement I'd suggest would be to more thoroughly discuss the relationship between the theoretical convergence assumption and the practical behavior of CMA-ES on non-convex problems, but this is not a significant enough weakness to affect the recommendation.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of optimization algorithms with clear practical impact for machine learning practitioners.
