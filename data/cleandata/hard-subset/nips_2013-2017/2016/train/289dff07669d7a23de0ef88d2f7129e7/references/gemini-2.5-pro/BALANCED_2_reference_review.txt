This paper addresses a significant and long-standing computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a cornerstone algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES exhibits a time complexity of at least O(d^3) per iteration due to the need to compute a matrix decomposition (e.g., eigenvalue decomposition) of the d x d covariance matrix. This cost can dominate the runtime for high-dimensional problems, especially when the objective function is fast to evaluate.

The authors propose a new variant, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, which reduces this complexity to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for a full covariance matrix update. The core idea is to maintain and directly update a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than the full matrix itself. This avoids the expensive decomposition step in each iteration. The main technical challenge lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the matrix square root C^(-1/2) for proper normalization. Previous attempts to use Cholesky factors have struggled with this step. The authors' key contribution is to approximate C^(-1/2) with A^(-1), where A is the triangular Cholesky factor. They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) for this approximation, arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this simplification vanishes due to the structure of the CSA update.

The paper validates this new approach through extensive experiments on a suite of standard benchmark functions. The results demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same optimization performance (in terms of required function evaluations) as the standard CMA-ES, while being significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time. The speedup becomes more pronounced as the problem dimension increases, confirming the theoretical complexity analysis.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known, practical, and important limitation of CMA-ES. By significantly reducing the per-iteration time complexity without sacrificing optimization performance, this work has the potential to make CMA-ES a viable tool for a much broader range of higher-dimensional problems in machine learning, such as direct policy search and large-scale hyperparameter optimization.

2.  **Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated. The theoretical argument in Lemma 1, which justifies the approximation in the step-size update, provides a principled foundation for the algorithm's design. This is a considerable improvement over ad-hoc modifications. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against appropriate baselines (including standard CMA-ES with delayed updates and another Cholesky-based variant) across multiple benchmark problems and dimensions. The results are convincing and clearly demonstrate the claimed benefits.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly articulates the problem, reviews prior work, explains the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings, and presents the empirical results in an accessible manner. The inclusion of pseudocode (Algorithms 1 and 2) is helpful for reproducibility.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Incorrect Claim Regarding Eigenvalues:** A significant technical error exists in the "Properties of the update rule" subsection (end of Section 3). The paper claims that "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix". This statement is incorrect for a general symmetric positive definite matrix `C`. The eigenvalues of `C = AA^T` are the squares of the singular values of `A`, which are not, in general, related to the diagonal entries of `A`. This undermines one of the three listed advantages of the proposed method. The authors should correct this statement in the final version. While eigenvalues are useful for monitoring, obtaining them from `A` would require a computation that negates the O(d^2) complexity benefit.

2.  **Support for Theoretical Justification:** The argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges (`C̄_t → C̄`). While the authors rightly note this is not guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm, the argument would be much stronger if supported by empirical evidence from their experiments. The paper mentions in passing that the change in the rotation matrix `E_t` was measured (and is in the supplementary material). Given that the stability of `E_t` is the central pillar of the theoretical justification, a brief summary of this empirical result (e.g., a plot of ||I - E_t^T E_{t-1}||_F over iterations for a representative run) should be included in the main paper. This would provide direct evidence that the core assumption holds in practice.

3.  **Comparison with Suttorp et al. [2009]:** The paper notes a key advantage over the non-triangular Cholesky update of Suttorp et al. is that the triangular factorization is unique (up to sign), which aids convergence. This is an important point that could be elaborated on slightly more. A more detailed technical explanation of why the extra degrees of freedom in a non-triangular factorization can lead to "non-converging sequences of E_t" would strengthen the paper's argument for its specific choice of a triangular factor.

4.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments go up to d=256, which is sufficient to show the trend. However, since the primary motivation is scaling CMA-ES to "large" problems, demonstrating the performance at even higher dimensions (e.g., d=512 or d=1024), where the O(d^3) methods are completely infeasible, would make the impact of the contribution even more compelling.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and practical bottleneck in a widely used and important algorithm.
*   The proposed solution is effective, achieving an optimal O(µd^2) time complexity.
*   The method is supported by both a principled theoretical argument and strong, convincing empirical results.
*   The work is likely to be highly significant and useful for practitioners and researchers using CMA-ES.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   Contains a notable technical error regarding the relationship between Cholesky factors and eigenvalues, which misrepresents one of the method's advantages.
*   The theoretical justification relies on an assumption of convergence that, while plausible, could be better supported with empirical evidence in the main text.

### Recommendation

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a valuable and impactful contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The strengths—solving an important problem with a well-justified and empirically verified method—far outweigh the weaknesses. The technical error concerning the eigenvalues is significant but easily correctable. I recommend **acceptance** for this paper, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the weaknesses listed above, in particular correcting the erroneous claim about eigenvalues, during the revision process.
