

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors provide theoretical justification for why this approximation doesn't deteriorate performance, particularly for the global step-size adaptation. The key contribution is a lemma showing the error introduced by using the Cholesky factor instead of the matrix square root vanishes as the algorithm converges. This builds on previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), but extends it to the standard CMA-ES with a theoretically grounded approach. The paper compares Cholesky-CMA-ES with standard CMA-ES (with delayed matrix square root computation) and other variants on benchmark functions across dimensions from 4 to 256.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for why the Cholesky approximation doesn't harm performance, which is a significant contribution over previous work that lacked this justification. The lemma establishing convergence of the rotation matrix is elegant and well-motivated.

2. **Practical Impact**: The approach achieves asymptotically optimal runtime complexity (O(µd²)) while maintaining the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES. For d=64, the authors report a 20x speedup, with the advantage increasing with dimensionality.

3. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization, precise pseudocode, and thorough experimental design. The background section effectively sets up the problem and related work.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments are comprehensive, covering multiple benchmark functions, dimensionality, rotation invariance, and wall-clock time measurements. The results consistently support the theoretical claims.

5. **Novelty**: While building on prior work, the paper makes a clear distinction by providing the first theoretically justified Cholesky-based CMA-ES for the standard algorithm, not just special cases.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Error Analysis**: While the paper establishes that the error vanishes as the algorithm converges, it lacks quantitative analysis of the error during the optimization process. It would strengthen the paper to show how much the step sizes differ between the two algorithms at various optimization stages.

2. **Comparison with Alternative Approaches**: The paper doesn't sufficiently compare with other low-dimensional approximation methods (e.g., Loshchilov 2015, Poland and Zell 2001) that might be relevant for extremely high-dimensional problems. The discussion of when to use Cholesky-CMA-ES versus these alternatives could be expanded.

3. **Numerical Stability**: The paper mentions numerical stability as an advantage of the Cholesky approach but doesn't provide analysis of potential stability issues, particularly for ill-conditioned problems where the Cholesky factorization might be unstable.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper states that the Cholesky-CMA-ES "reduces the memory footprint," but doesn't quantify the memory savings. For large-scale applications, this could be important.

5. **Limited Problem Scope**: The experiments focus on standard benchmark functions, but it would be valuable to see how the algorithm performs on more complex real-world problems, especially those where CMA-ES is typically applied (e.g., reinforcement learning policy search).

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong technical contribution with clear theoretical justification and compelling experimental results. The paper addresses a genuine bottleneck in CMA-ES (the O(d³) covariance matrix update) with an elegant solution that maintains algorithmic performance while significantly improving computational efficiency. The quality of the work is high, and the significance is clear for practitioners dealing with high-dimensional optimization problems where maintaining the full covariance matrix is feasible.

The paper would be strengthened by more detailed error analysis, comparison with alternative low-dimensional approximation methods, and discussion of numerical stability. However, these are refinements rather than fundamental flaws.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization with clear theoretical justification and practical benefits. The work is well-executed, well-written, and addresses a meaningful problem in the CMA-ES literature. The minor weaknesses identified do not undermine the core contribution, which is both technically sound and practically valuable.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Add a quantitative analysis of the error introduced by the Cholesky approximation during optimization (not just at convergence).
2. Expand the comparison to include other low-dimensional approximation methods (e.g., Loshchilov 2015) to better position Cholesky-CMA-ES in the landscape of covariance matrix approximation techniques.
3. Provide a more detailed discussion of numerical stability implications for ill-conditioned problems.
4. Include a brief analysis of memory usage reduction for different problem dimensions.
5. Consider adding one or two experiments on a more complex, real-world problem (e.g., neural network hyperparameter tuning) to demonstrate practical impact beyond benchmark functions.
