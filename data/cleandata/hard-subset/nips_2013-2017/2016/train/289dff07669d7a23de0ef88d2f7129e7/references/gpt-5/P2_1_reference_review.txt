Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = A A^T and samples via x = m + σ A y. It avoids explicit formation/eigendecomposition of C and replaces the CSA step-size update’s use of C^{-1/2} by A^{-1}. The authors give a simple lemma showing that the orthogonal factor implicit in the polar decomposition converges when the normalized covariance shape converges, arguing the norm of the evolution path used in CSA is only weakly affected asymptotically. They derive rank-one update formulas for A yielding O(µ d^2) time per iteration (with typical µ = O(log d)) and optimal O(d^2) storage. Experiments on standard (rotated) deterministic test functions compare against a reference CMA-ES with delayed eigendecomposition, a version with decomposition every d steps, and a prior nontriangular Cholesky approach, showing essentially identical function-evaluation counts and substantial wall-clock speedups that grow with dimension.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The complexity claims and the derivation of rank-one updates to the triangular Cholesky factor are sound and consistent with prior work (Suttorp et al.; Krause & Igel). The core approximation—using A^{-1} instead of C^{-1/2} in the CSA path—is justified via a convergence-of-rotations argument (Lemma 1) and the exponentially decaying memory of the path. This provides plausible asymptotic correctness but no finite-time guarantees.
- A notable technical error: the paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. The Cholesky diagonal contains the square roots of the pivots, not the eigenvalues; eigenvalues require an eigendecomposition (or at least singular values of A, which are not its diagonal either). The claim that eigenvalues come “for free” should be removed or corrected; one can get determinant easily from the diagonal, but not eigenvalues or principal components.
- Empirics: The experiments are reasonably thorough on classical smooth testbeds, with rotation applied to test invariance. They show the key claim: identical evaluations and large wall-clock gains. However, the study omits noisy or constrained problems and does not report success rates (Rosenbrock failures were excluded). It would strengthen the case to include (i) runs with noise, (ii) success probabilities or run-length distributions, and (iii) ablations quantifying the deviation in ‖pσ‖ induced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} (alluded to in the supplement).
- Numerical stability is not deeply discussed. Repeated rank-one updates can accumulate round-off error; while the update preserves SPD for default CMA-ES parameters, practical remedies (occasional re-factorization, jitter) should be mentioned.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, with clear motivation and algorithms. The discussion around the CSA approximation and its consequences is helpful, and the complexity rationale is explicit.
- Minor issues: typos (e.g., “it is it conjectured”), an apparent formatting error in the update delay formula (“110d(c1+cµ)” likely meant “1/(10 d (c1+cµ))”), and some notational switches between QR/RQ that could confuse readers unfamiliar with these factorizations. The claim about eigenvalues (above) is misleading and should be corrected.
- Reproducibility: defaults follow the CMA-ES tutorial; Shark/LAPACK are used. It would be useful to clearly state code availability and any deviations from standard CMA-ES settings.

Originality
- The idea of updating a Cholesky factor has prior art, but this paper extends it to the standard rank-µ CMA-ES and integrates it with a principled argument for approximating CSA without computing C^{-1/2}. That extension and the associated analysis are novel enough for NeurIPS.
- The position relative to limited-memory/diagonal variants is appropriate: those reduce model capacity, whereas this work targets the full-covariance regime.

Significance
- High practical impact for users of full CMA-ES where function evaluations are cheap-to-moderate and d is large enough that linear algebra dominates. The speedups reported (up to 20× by d=64) are compelling, and the approach is simple to implement and likely to be adopted.
- Theoretical contribution is modest but targeted: it justifies a key approximation well enough for practice without overclaiming convergence proofs for CMA-ES as a whole.

Pros
- Addresses a real bottleneck in a widely used algorithm; yields optimal (quadratic) time and storage for full-covariance CMA-ES.
- Clean algorithmic contribution with explicit rank-one updates and a plausible asymptotic argument for CSA approximation.
- Strong empirical gains in wall-clock time with unchanged evaluation counts on rotated benchmarks.
- Integration into a public library (Shark) increases adoption potential.

Cons
- Incorrect claim about obtaining eigenvalues from the Cholesky diagonal.
- Limited theoretical guarantees (no finite-time bounds; relies on convergence of normalized covariance).
- Experimental scope limited: no noisy/constraint cases; excludes failures on Rosenbrock; success rates not reported.
- Some clarity/typo issues and minor notational inconsistencies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the eigenvalue claim; instead emphasize determinants and efficient triangular solves/inversion. If eigenvalue information is needed (e.g., for enforcing minimum variances along principal components), clarify what is and is not available without EVD.
- Report success rates and include noisy benchmarks; quantify the transient error in ‖pσ‖ and its impact on σ.
- Discuss numerical stability and whether occasional re-factorization is needed in long runs.

Recommendation
- Weak accept. The paper offers a useful, implementable improvement to a staple algorithm, with convincing empirical evidence and reasonable justification of the main approximation. Fixing the eigenvalue claim and adding minor clarifications would make it stronger.

Confidence
- High on the algorithmic and numerical linear algebra aspects; medium on the breadth of empirical validation.
