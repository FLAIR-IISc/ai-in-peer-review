

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to achieve optimal computational complexity. The authors present a theoretically justified approach that maintains the algorithm's performance while significantly reducing runtime complexity from O(d²) to O(µd²) (with µ = O(log d)) and storage complexity from O(d²) to O(d²) (optimal for triangular matrices). The key innovation is their theoretical argument showing that the error introduced by using the Cholesky factor instead of the matrix square root diminishes as the algorithm converges, making this approach viable for the standard CMA-ES.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough background section that properly contextualizes their contribution relative to prior work on CMA-ES variants. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 is particularly strong, establishing that the error from replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ vanishes as the algorithm converges to the optimum. The empirical results demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves identical function evaluation counts to the reference CMA-ES implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) while providing significant speedups in wall-clock time, especially as dimensionality increases (up to 20x faster at d=64). The paper also highlights several practical advantages of their approach: the Cholesky factor provides eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free, and triangular matrices can be inverted in quadratic time.

The novelty of this work is significant. While previous papers (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) explored Cholesky factorizations for specific CMA-ES variants, this is the first to provide a theoretically justified approach for the standard CMA-ES with full covariance adaptation. The paper properly situates its contribution within the literature, acknowledging prior work while clearly differentiating their approach. The authors make a compelling case that their method provides a fundamental improvement in computational complexity without sacrificing performance.

The significance of this work is substantial for practitioners of derivative-free optimization. CMA-ES is widely used in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning, but its computational bottleneck has limited its applicability to high-dimensional problems. By providing a method that maintains the same number of function evaluations while dramatically reducing wall-clock time (especially in high dimensions), this work enables scaling CMA-ES to larger problems where maintaining a full covariance matrix is still feasible. The authors correctly note that their approach is complementary to low-dimensional approximation methods (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015), as it provides a way to scale up CMA-ES to higher dimensions before resorting to approximations that may degrade performance.

While the paper is strong in its core contribution, there are a few minor weaknesses. The theoretical justification assumes convergence of Ct, which isn't guaranteed for randomized algorithms, though the authors provide reasonable arguments for why this would hold in practice. The experiments are thorough but limited to standard benchmark functions; real-world applications would further strengthen the significance. Additionally, the paper doesn't discuss potential numerical stability issues with the Cholesky update, though the authors note they've implemented it in Shark with LAPACK for efficiency.

In terms of the four key criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical justification and comprehensive empirical validation.
- Clarity: Well-written with clear organization and sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- Originality: The approach is novel and well-justified, representing a significant advance over previous work.
- Significance: The work enables scaling CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems without sacrificing performance, which is highly valuable for practitioners.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel contribution NIPS values: it addresses a fundamental computational bottleneck in a widely used algorithm with a theoretically sound and empirically validated approach. While not perfect, the methodological improvements are significant enough that minor theoretical caveats and limited experimental scope shouldn't prevent acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel approach with theoretical justification for a fundamental computational bottleneck in CMA-ES
- Provides asymptotically optimal time and storage complexity for covariance adaptation
- Maintains identical performance (function evaluations) to standard CMA-ES
- Demonstrates significant practical speedups (20x faster at d=64)
- Addresses an important practical limitation of CMA-ES for high-dimensional problems

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Theoretical justification assumes convergence of Ct, which isn't guaranteed for randomized algorithms
- Limited to standard benchmark functions rather than real-world applications
- Doesn't fully explore potential numerical stability issues

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization with a novel approach that is both theoretically sound and practically valuable. The authors have successfully addressed a long-standing computational bottleneck in a widely used algorithm with a clean and elegant solution. Given NIPS's emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, I strongly recommend acceptance.
