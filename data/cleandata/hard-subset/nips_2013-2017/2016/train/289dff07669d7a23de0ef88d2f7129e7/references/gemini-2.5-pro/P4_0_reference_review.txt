This paper presents a new variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a widely used algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES suffers from a computational bottleneck in its covariance matrix update, which requires an eigenvalue decomposition or similar matrix factorization with a complexity of O(d³) in the problem dimension d. This can dominate the runtime when the objective function is cheap to evaluate or when d is large.

The authors propose the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which reduces this complexity to O(μd²), where μ is the parent population size (typically O(log d)). The core idea is to maintain and directly update the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than the full matrix itself. This builds upon previous work that has explored direct updates of matrix factorizations (e.g., Suttorp et al. [2009], Krause and Igel [2015]). The main challenge in applying this to the standard CMA-ES is the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the matrix square root C⁻¹/² for normalization. A Cholesky factorization C = AAᵀ does not directly provide C⁻¹/².

The central contribution of this paper is a principled approximation for the CSA update. The authors recognize that C¹/² can be related to its triangular Cholesky factor A via an RQ-decomposition, C¹/² = AE, where E is an orthogonal matrix. The proposed method approximates C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹, effectively ignoring the rotation Eᵀ. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this approximation in Lemma 1, arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the rotation matrix E also converges. Because the CSA mechanism uses an exponentially weighted average of past steps, the rotational error introduced by the approximation diminishes over time. The paper validates this approach empirically, showing that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant speedup in wall-clock time compared to the standard CMA-ES and other variants, without degrading performance in terms of the number of function evaluations required for convergence.

### **Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and important limitation of a foundational algorithm in black-box optimization. CMA-ES is widely used in machine learning for tasks like hyperparameter tuning and direct policy search in reinforcement learning. By significantly reducing the per-iteration computational cost, this work makes the full-covariance CMA-ES practical for higher-dimensional problems than was previously feasible, thus broadening its applicability.
*   **Quality & Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound. The approximation at the heart of the algorithm is not merely a heuristic but is supported by a clear theoretical argument. While Lemma 1 relies on the assumption of convergence of the covariance matrix shape, which is not strictly guaranteed for the stochastic algorithm, it provides a strong and plausible rationale for why the approximation should work well in practice. The experimental evaluation is rigorous, using a standard set of benchmark functions, including rotated versions to test for invariance properties, and comparing against appropriate baselines, including the standard reference implementation. The results robustly support the paper's claims.
*   **Originality:** While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor directly is not entirely new, the key novelty lies in successfully adapting this technique to the modern, standard CMA-ES algorithm by resolving the issue with the CSA update mechanism. The justification via the convergence of the RQ-decomposition's orthogonal component is a novel and insightful contribution that distinguishes this work from previous attempts.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the context of prior art, explains the proposed algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings, and presents the experimental results in an understandable manner. The inclusion of pseudocode makes the proposed algorithm explicit and aids reproducibility.

### **Weaknesses**

*   **Scope of Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical argument for the approximation relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges. The authors correctly note that this is not formally proven for the full algorithm. The argument would be strengthened by a brief discussion of scenarios where this assumption might be violated (e.g., highly multi-modal problems where the search distribution shape changes frequently) and the potential impact on the algorithm's performance.
*   **Experimental Comparison:** The results for the Suttorp-CMA-ES variant are presented in the figures but are not discussed in detail in the main text. A more explicit analysis comparing the proposed method to this non-triangular Cholesky approach would be beneficial. The paper mentions theoretical advantages of a unique triangular factorization (e.g., numerical stability, free eigenvalues), and relating these to the empirical results would further solidify the contribution.
*   **Dimensionality in Experiments:** While the experiments go up to d=256 and clearly demonstrate the asymptotic advantage, extending the wall-clock time comparisons to even higher dimensions (e.g., d=512) could provide further evidence of the method's practical utility, especially as the authors' goal is to scale up CMA-ES.

### **Summary of Evaluation**

The paper presents a high-quality contribution that is both practically significant and technically sound. It offers a well-justified and effective solution to a long-standing computational issue in one of the most important algorithms for derivative-free optimization. The clarity of the presentation and the strength of the empirical results are commendable. The identified weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a significant and practical speedup for a widely-used optimization algorithm (CMA-ES), expanding its applicability.
2.  The core technical contribution (the approximation for the CSA update) is novel and supported by a sound theoretical argument.
3.  The paper is very clearly written and the experimental validation is thorough and convincing.
4.  The work is of high interest to practitioners and researchers in optimization, reinforcement learning, and automated machine learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The theoretical justification for the key approximation rests on an unproven assumption about the convergence of the covariance matrix.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. This is a solid piece of work that represents a clear advance in the field.
