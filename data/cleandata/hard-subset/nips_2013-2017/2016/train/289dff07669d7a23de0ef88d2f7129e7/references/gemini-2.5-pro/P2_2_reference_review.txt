### **Summary**

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES has a time complexity of at least O(d³) per iteration due to the need for an eigenvalue or square root decomposition of the d x d covariance matrix C. The authors propose a new variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which reduces this complexity to an asymptotically optimal O(µd²) by directly updating the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. The main challenge, which has hindered previous efforts, is that the step-size adaptation rule requires C⁻¹/², which cannot be easily derived from a Cholesky factor. The key contribution of this paper is to approximate C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹, where A is the triangular Cholesky factor, and to provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by this approximation vanishes as the shape of the covariance matrix converges. Empirical results on a suite of benchmark functions demonstrate that the proposed method achieves a significant wall-clock time speedup over standard and delayed-update versions of CMA-ES without any discernible loss in optimization performance (i.e., requiring the same number of function evaluations).

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Arguments for acceptance (Strengths):**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and important limitation of a widely used and powerful optimization algorithm. By drastically reducing the computational overhead of CMA-ES, this work makes the algorithm practical for higher-dimensional problems or scenarios where function evaluations are cheap, thus broadening its applicability in fields like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization.
2.  **Novel and Sound Technical Contribution:** While others (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009) have proposed Cholesky updates, this work is novel in successfully applying it to the modern CMA-ES and, crucially, providing a theoretical justification for the necessary approximation in the step-size adaptation rule. Lemma 1 provides a solid, elegant argument for why the approximation is valid under convergence, which is a significant step beyond a purely heuristic modification.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against appropriate baselines, including the reference implementation and a delayed-update strategy. The experiments cover a range of standard benchmark functions, test for rotation invariance, and clearly demonstrate the claimed speedup across different dimensions. The results robustly support the central claim that performance is maintained while runtime is significantly reduced.
4.  **Asymptotically Optimal Complexity:** The paper achieves O(µd²) time complexity and O(d²) storage for a full covariance matrix adaptation, which is optimal. The additional benefits mentioned, such as obtaining eigenvalues for free and the numerical stability of inverting a triangular matrix, are valuable practical advantages.

**Arguments for rejection (Weaknesses):**

1.  **Reliance on Convergence Assumption:** The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges. The authors themselves note that for a randomized algorithm, this is not guaranteed. While the empirical results suggest the method is robust even without strict convergence, the theoretical guarantee is conditional. The paper could be strengthened by discussing the behavior of the approximation when the matrix shape changes rapidly or oscillates.
2.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on standard, low-cost benchmark functions. While this is appropriate for validating the number of function evaluations, the motivation for the work is often for expensive problems (e.g., policy search) where the algorithm's overhead becomes dominant. Including at least one application-oriented experiment, for instance in hyperparameter tuning for a small neural network, would have made the practical impact even more tangible.

### **Detailed Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is well-defined, and the derivation is sound. The theoretical analysis, while based on an asymptotic assumption, provides strong and novel insight into why the approximation should work. The claims are well-supported by a carefully designed set of experiments. The authors are honest about the approximation being made and its theoretical limitations. This is a complete and polished piece of work.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation and review of related work, making the problem and the gap in the literature easy to understand. The background on CMA-ES is concise, and the explanation of the technical challenge and the proposed solution is logical and easy to follow. The inclusion of pseudocode (Algorithms 1 and 2) is very helpful for reproducibility. The figures are clear and effectively communicate the main results.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The core problem is not new, but the proposed solution is. The key innovation is not just the use of a Cholesky update but the specific way it is integrated into the standard CMA-ES, along with the theoretical argument justifying the approximation of the step-size adaptation path `p_σ`. This successfully addresses a roadblock that has prevented a quadratic-time version of the full CMA-ES from being widely adopted. The work clearly differentiates itself from previous attempts, such as Suttorp et al. (2009), by using a unique triangular factorization and providing a convergence proof for the approximation error.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. CMA-ES is a workhorse algorithm for black-box optimization. This paper provides what is likely to become the new standard, high-performance implementation. Researchers and practitioners who use CMA-ES will be able to solve larger problems faster. The performance improvement is not incremental but a significant change in the practical complexity class (from cubic to quadratic). This advance is likely to be widely cited and used, and the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES will likely be integrated into major scientific computing libraries. It demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art in evolutionary computation.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant, well-justified, and empirically validated improvement to a fundamental optimization algorithm. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
