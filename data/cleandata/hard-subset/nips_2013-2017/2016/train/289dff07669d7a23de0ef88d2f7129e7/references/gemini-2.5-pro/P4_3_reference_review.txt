### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the high computational complexity of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a prominent algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES has a per-iteration runtime that scales cubically with the problem dimension `d` due to the need to compute the square root of the covariance matrix `C`. The authors propose a new variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which reduces this complexity to O(µd^2), where µ is the number of parent points, making the algorithm significantly faster for high-dimensional problems.

The core technical challenge lies in the step-size adaptation mechanism of CMA-ES, which requires normalization by the matrix square root `C^{-1/2}`. Previous attempts to accelerate CMA-ES by directly updating a Cholesky factor `A` (where `C = AA^T`) have been difficult to apply to the standard algorithm because the inverse of a triangular Cholesky factor, `A^{-1}`, is not equivalent to `C^{-1/2}`. The authors' key contribution is to propose using `A^{-1}` as an approximation for `C^{-1/2}` in the step-size update rule. They provide a theoretical justification for this approximation via Lemma 1, arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this approximation diminishes. The paper then presents the complete algorithm, which maintains and updates the triangular Cholesky factor directly, achieving optimal asymptotic time and storage complexity for a full-matrix approach. The effectiveness of Cholesky-CMA-ES is demonstrated through extensive experiments on benchmark optimization problems, showing that it achieves the same performance as the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations while being substantially faster in wall-clock time.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality: Is the paper technically sound?**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithmic modification is well-defined and motivated. The central claim—that Cholesky-CMA-ES significantly reduces runtime without impairing optimization performance—is substantiated by a combination of theoretical reasoning and strong empirical results.

*   **Strengths:** The experimental methodology is sound. The authors compare their method against appropriate and state-of-the-art baselines, including the standard CMA-ES with a delayed update strategy. The experiments cover a range of standard benchmark functions and scale across multiple problem dimensions, providing convincing evidence of the claimed speed-up. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1, while not a formal proof of the overall algorithm's convergence, provides a solid rationale for why the proposed approximation is expected to work well in practice, particularly as the algorithm approaches an optimum. The authors are also careful to acknowledge the limitations of their theoretical argument (i.e., that `C_t` does not formally converge).

*   **Weaknesses:** The argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the convergence of the normalized covariance matrix `C̄_t`. While plausible, this is a strong assumption for a stochastic algorithm. The authors rightly state that only the *expectation* of `C_t` is known to converge in simplified settings. While the empirical results suggest the approximation is effective, the theoretical support remains heuristic rather than a guarantee.

**Clarity: Is the paper clearly written and well-organized?**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with optimization and machine learning.

*   **Strengths:** The introduction effectively frames the problem and surveys existing solutions and their drawbacks. The background section provides the necessary context on CMA-ES and Cholesky decomposition. Section 3, which introduces the core contribution, does an excellent job of first identifying the exact technical barrier (`C^{-1/2}` vs. `A^{-1}`) and then presenting the proposed approximation and its justification. The algorithmic listings and experimental results (figures) are clear and easy to interpret.

*   **Weaknesses:** A minor point for improvement would be to provide a reference for the `rankOneUpdate` procedure in Algorithm 2, as this is a standard numerical linear algebra technique.

**Originality: Are the problems or approaches new?**

The paper makes a novel and important contribution to the literature on evolution strategies.

*   **Strengths:** While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix is not new (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), this work is the first to successfully and justifiably apply this acceleration to the standard, modern CMA-ES algorithm. The key originality lies in identifying the `C^{-1/2}` approximation as the necessary step and providing a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) for its validity. This distinguishes the work from prior attempts that either applied to simplified CMA-ES variants or used non-triangular decompositions, which do not offer the same computational and numerical benefits.

*   **Weaknesses:** The constituent parts—CMA-ES and Cholesky updates—are not new. The novelty is in their effective and well-justified combination to solve a long-standing efficiency problem.

**Significance: Are the results important?**

The significance of this work is very high. CMA-ES is a workhorse algorithm for derivative-free optimization with broad applications in machine learning, including reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning.

*   **Strengths:** The primary contribution is practical: a drop-in replacement for a standard, widely-used algorithm that makes it significantly faster. The demonstrated speed-up (e.g., 20x at d=64) pushes the boundary of problem sizes for which CMA-ES with a full covariance matrix is computationally feasible. By making the algorithm more efficient, this work is likely to be used by many practitioners and researchers, and it could enable new applications of CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems where it was previously too slow. The implementation is also provided in an open-source library, which will facilitate its adoption.

*   **Weaknesses:** The method still scales quadratically with dimensionality, and thus does not address the challenge of "large-scale" optimization where `d` is in the thousands or more. The authors are forthright about this limitation in their conclusion, correctly positioning their work as an improvement for the medium-to-high dimensional regime where a full covariance matrix is still manageable.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and well-known performance bottleneck in CMA-ES, a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization.
*   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES provides a substantial, empirically verified wall-clock time speed-up without degrading optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations).
*   The paper provides a novel and plausible theoretical justification for the key approximation made.
*   The work is clearly presented, technically sound, and well-situated within the existing literature.
*   The practical impact is high, as the algorithm can be readily adopted by users of CMA-ES.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification relies on an assumption of convergence that is not formally guaranteed for the stochastic CMA-ES algorithm. This is a minor point, however, as the empirical evidence is very strong.

### Final Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that presents a valuable, practical, and well-supported contribution. It solves a long-standing efficiency problem for one of the most important algorithms in its class. The proposed method is simple, effective, and well-justified. The clarity of the presentation and the strength of the empirical results make this a compelling submission. I strongly recommend acceptance.
