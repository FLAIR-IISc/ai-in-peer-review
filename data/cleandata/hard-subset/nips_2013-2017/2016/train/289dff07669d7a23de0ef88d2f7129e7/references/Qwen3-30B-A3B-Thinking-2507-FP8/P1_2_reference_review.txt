

# Peer Review: "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to achieve optimal asymptotic runtime and storage complexity. The authors provide theoretical justification showing that the approximation error (replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} where A is the Cholesky factor) vanishes as the algorithm converges. They demonstrate through experiments that their approach maintains the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES while significantly reducing wall-clock time, especially for higher-dimensional problems. This work builds on previous attempts by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) but extends them to the full CMA-ES with a rigorous theoretical foundation.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a clear theoretical justification (Lemma 1) showing that the approximation error diminishes as the algorithm converges, addressing a key concern that previous attempts with Cholesky factorization didn't properly handle. The experimental design is comprehensive, comparing against relevant baselines (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) across multiple benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) and dimensions (d = 4 to 256). The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the reference implementation but significantly less wall-clock time, with speedups reaching 20x for d=64. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the approach still scales quadratically with dimensionality and becomes less effective for very large d where the covariance matrix exceeds memory capacity).

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good use of algorithms to illustrate the implementation details. The theoretical justification is somewhat dense but accessible to experts in evolutionary computation. The experimental section effectively uses figures to demonstrate the runtime advantages. One minor suggestion: the connection between the theoretical justification and the practical performance could be made clearer by explicitly linking the convergence of the rotation matrices (Lemma 1) to the observed runtime improvements in the experiments.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a theoretically grounded extension of Cholesky-based CMA-ES to the standard algorithm. While previous work (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) had explored Cholesky factorization for specific CMA-ES variants, this work extends it to the full CMA-ES with a proper theoretical justification for why the approximation doesn't degrade performance. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work and provide comprehensive references. The paper would have benefited from a more detailed comparison with Loshchilov's (2015) low-dimensional approximation approaches, but this is understandable given their focus on full covariance matrix adaptation.

## Significance
This work addresses a critical bottleneck in CMA-ES: the O(d³) cost of matrix square root computation, which limits the algorithm's applicability to high-dimensional problems. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves optimal O(μd²) runtime complexity without increasing the number of function evaluations, making it highly relevant for applications like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning where CMA-ES is commonly used. The speedups demonstrated (up to 20x for d=64) are substantial and could enable the application of CMA-ES to problems that were previously computationally prohibitive. The paper advances the state of the art in evolutionary computation for derivative-free optimization, with clear potential for practical impact in machine learning.

## Strengths
- Strong theoretical justification for the approximation error
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions
- Clear demonstration of significant runtime improvements without compromising optimization performance
- Optimal storage complexity (O(d²) instead of O(d²) for the Cholesky factor)
- Practical implementation details (Algorithm 1 and 2) that enable reproducibility

## Weaknesses
- Limited discussion of numerical stability during Cholesky updates (though the authors note it's numerically stable)
- Experiments only go up to d=256, which is still relatively small for modern applications
- No discussion of how this approach would scale to the very high-dimensional problems (d > 1000) where CMA-ES is typically not used
- The theoretical justification relies on the assumption that Ct converges, which is not guaranteed for all problems

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of evolutionary computation and derivative-free optimization. The theoretical foundation is solid, the experimental results are convincing, and the approach addresses a genuine bottleneck in CMA-ES that has been recognized for over a decade. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and has clear potential for practical impact in machine learning applications.

I recommend acceptance. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with a novel and effective solution. The minor weaknesses noted are understandable given the specific focus of the work and do not detract from the overall contribution. This work would be valuable to the NIPS community interested in optimization algorithms for machine learning.
