### **Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a highly effective derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard CMA-ES has a complexity of at least O(d³) per iteration due to the need to compute the square root of the d x d covariance matrix C, typically via eigenvalue decomposition. This cost can dominate the optimization process for high-dimensional problems or when the objective function is computationally cheap.

The authors propose a new variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which avoids the explicit computation and decomposition of the covariance matrix. Instead, it directly updates the triangular Cholesky factor A (where C = AAᵀ) of the covariance matrix. While updating the Cholesky factor for the main covariance update rule has been explored before, the critical challenge lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the matrix inverse square root C⁻¹/². A Cholesky factor is not equivalent to a matrix square root.

The core contribution of this work is to justify approximating C⁻¹/² with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor, A⁻¹. The authors provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) showing that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the rotational error introduced by this approximation vanishes. This allows them to replace the O(d³) decomposition with a series of rank-one updates to the Cholesky factor, achieving an overall iteration complexity of O(µd²), which is asymptotically optimal for a full covariance matrix update. The paper validates this approach empirically, showing that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations) as the standard CMA-ES but with a dramatic reduction in wall-clock time.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Soundness:** The theoretical justification for the approximation is the paper's strongest point. The authors correctly identify that simply substituting the Cholesky factor in the CSA update is non-trivial due to the introduction of an arbitrary rotation. Lemma 1 provides a clear and plausible argument for why this error should diminish as the algorithm converges, relying on the continuity of the RQ-decomposition and the uniqueness of the triangular factor. While this relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges, which is reasonable in practice, the authors are honest about this being a justification for an approximation rather than a strict proof of equivalence.
*   **Experimental Support:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The choice of benchmark functions is appropriate, covering different landscape properties (separable, ill-conditioned, non-convex). The comparison against both the standard reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) and a common speedup heuristic (CMA-ES/d) is well-justified. The results strongly support the central claim: the number of function evaluations remains unchanged while the wall-clock time is significantly reduced, with the speedup growing with dimensionality as predicted by the complexity analysis. The inclusion of the Suttorp-CMA-ES variant as another baseline further strengthens the comparison.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   The problem is clearly motivated in the introduction, and the background on CMA-ES is presented concisely.
*   The central technical challenge—the difference between C⁻¹/² and A⁻¹ for the step-size update—is explained with precision. The theoretical justification in Section 3 is easy to follow.
*   The provided pseudocode (Algorithm 1 and 2) gives a clear recipe for implementation, enhancing the paper's utility and reproducibility.
*   The experimental results are presented in clear plots that effectively communicate the main findings.

**Originality:** The work demonstrates solid originality.

*   While the idea of directly updating a factorization of the covariance matrix is not new (as the authors appropriately cite Suttorp et al. [2009] and Krause & Igel [2015]), the key novelty lies in successfully applying this to the *standard* CMA-ES and, crucially, providing a theoretical justification for handling the step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism.
*   Previous works either applied to different variants of CMA-ES (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES) or used non-triangular factors, which, as the authors correctly point out, lack the uniqueness property required for their convergence argument. This paper is the first to provide a principled and effective way to create a fast Cholesky-based version of the full, modern CMA-ES algorithm without compromising its performance.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant.

*   CMA-ES is a workhorse algorithm in both the evolutionary computation community and in machine learning for tasks like hyperparameter tuning and direct policy search in reinforcement learning. Its main practical limitation has always been its poor scaling with dimensionality.
*   This paper provides a solution that effectively removes this computational bottleneck for a wide range of medium-to-high dimensional problems (where d is in the hundreds). The demonstrated speedup (e.g., 20x for d=64) is not a minor incremental improvement but a transformative one, making the algorithm practical for a new class of problems.
*   Given the algorithm's simplicity and the fact that it is an "in-place" replacement for the core update step, it is highly likely to be adopted by practitioners and become the new standard implementation of CMA-ES. The additional benefits, such as optimal storage complexity and free access to eigenvalues, are valuable practical advantages.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** Solves a major, long-standing performance problem in a very widely used and important optimization algorithm.
*   **Pro:** The proposed solution is backed by a sound theoretical argument that explains why the necessary approximation works.
*   **Pro:** The empirical evidence is comprehensive and strongly supports the claims of significant speedup without loss of optimization performance.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and positions its contribution accurately within the existing literature.
*   **Pro:** The work has high practical impact and is likely to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners using CMA-ES.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Con (Minor):** The theoretical justification relies on the assumption of the convergence of the covariance matrix's shape, which is not strictly guaranteed for the stochastic algorithm. However, this is a minor point as the method is presented as a well-justified approximation, and the empirical results are overwhelmingly positive.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant and practical advance for the state of the art in derivative-free optimization. It addresses a critical limitation of the CMA-ES algorithm with a solution that is both theoretically principled and empirically verified. The work is clear, original, and highly significant. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
