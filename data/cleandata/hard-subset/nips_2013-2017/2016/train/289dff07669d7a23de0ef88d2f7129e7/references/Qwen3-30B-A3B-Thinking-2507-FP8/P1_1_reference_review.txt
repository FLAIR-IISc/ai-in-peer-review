

# Peer Review: "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary

This paper proposes a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) called "Cholesky-CMA-ES" that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The standard CMA-ES requires O(d³) time per iteration for matrix square root computation, which becomes a bottleneck for high-dimensional optimization problems. The authors argue that by using a Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root, they can achieve optimal asymptotic time and storage complexity (O(µd²)) without significantly affecting the number of function evaluations needed.

The paper builds on previous work by Suttorp et al. [2009] and Krause and Igel [2015] who proposed using Cholesky factorization for covariance matrix updates, but makes a key theoretical contribution by providing Lemma 1 that justifies why replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ does not deteriorate performance. The authors show that the introduced error becomes negligible as the algorithm converges. The paper includes empirical results demonstrating significant runtime improvements (up to 20x faster for d=64) without increasing the number of function evaluations needed to reach the same objective value.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for the proposed approach. Lemma 1 establishes that the error introduced by the approximation diminishes as the algorithm converges, which is crucial for establishing the validity of the approach. The authors properly address the main theoretical challenge in this line of work (the rotation introduced by the Cholesky factorization).

2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The empirical results are comprehensive, testing the algorithm on multiple benchmark functions with varying dimensions (d=4 to 256) and including rotation invariance tests. The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves similar optimization performance (same number of function evaluations) but with significantly reduced runtime, especially for higher dimensions.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The paper addresses a real computational bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm. The runtime improvements are substantial, with the speedup increasing with dimensionality, making this particularly valuable for large-scale optimization problems in machine learning.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with a clear problem statement, theoretical justification, algorithmic description, and empirical validation. The authors effectively contrast their approach with previous work and explain why their method is theoretically justified.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: While Lemma 1 provides a foundation, the theoretical analysis could be strengthened. The lemma assumes convergence of Ct, but CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm that doesn't necessarily converge in the strict sense. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of error propagation during the early optimization stages when the covariance matrix is changing rapidly.

2. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares with CMA-ES-Ref and Suttorp-CMA-ES, but doesn't compare with more recent CMA-ES variants that have addressed computational complexity (e.g., Loshchilov [2015] proposes an alternative approach with similar goals). The paper also doesn't compare with the most recent CMA-ES implementations that might have incorporated similar optimizations.

3. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions they added their algorithm to the Shark library, but lacks sufficient detail about numerical stability considerations, particularly for ill-conditioned matrices. This is crucial for practitioners who might want to implement the method.

4. **Scalability to Extremely High Dimensions**: While the paper shows good results up to d=256, it doesn't explore how the algorithm performs on extremely high-dimensional problems (d > 1000), where the quadratic scaling might become a bottleneck.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a solid contribution to the optimization literature with clear theoretical justification and strong empirical results. The main weakness is the limited theoretical analysis of the error introduced by the approximation, particularly during early optimization stages. However, the empirical results strongly support the claim that the error is small and diminishes over time.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

*Pros:*
- Addresses a significant computational bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm
- Provides optimal asymptotic complexity (O(µd²)) for covariance updates
- Empirical results consistently show significant runtime improvements without increasing function evaluations
- Clear theoretical justification for why the approximation works
- Simple to implement and integrates well with existing CMA-ES codebases

*Cons:*
- Theoretical analysis of error propagation is limited
- Doesn't compare with the most recent CMA-ES variants addressing computational complexity
- Insufficient detail on numerical stability for implementation
- Doesn't explore performance on extremely high-dimensional problems
- Limited discussion of how this approach relates to other recent low-rank approximation methods

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The contribution is significant, technically sound, and well-supported by both theory and experiments. The paper addresses an important practical issue in a widely used optimization algorithm and provides a clean solution with clear theoretical justification. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a substantial practical benefit for high-dimensional optimization.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the theoretical analysis to provide more detailed bounds on the error introduced by the approximation, especially during early optimization stages.

2. Include a comparison with the most recent CMA-ES variants that have addressed computational complexity (e.g., Loshchilov [2015]).

3. Provide more detailed implementation notes about numerical stability considerations, particularly for ill-conditioned matrices.

4. Extend the experiments to higher dimensions (d > 1000) to better understand the scalability limits of the approach.

5. Clarify the relationship between the proposed method and the Suttorp et al. [2009] approach, explaining why the triangular Cholesky factorization provides advantages over the non-triangular version they proposed.

This paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental computational problem in optimization that is highly relevant to many machine learning applications. The authors have made a clear contribution to the field with both theoretical and practical implications, and their approach provides a practical solution to an important bottleneck in a widely used algorithm.
