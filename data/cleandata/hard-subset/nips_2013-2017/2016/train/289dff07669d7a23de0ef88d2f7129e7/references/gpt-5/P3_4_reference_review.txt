Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = AAT directly, rather than updating C and recomputing its square root/eigendecomposition. The key technical change is to replace the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) term C−1/2(mt+1 − mt)/σt with A−1(mt+1 − mt)/σt. Since, in general, C−1/2 = AE with an orthogonal rotation E that varies over iterations, the authors argue that omitting E introduces a vanishing error under mild convergence assumptions (Lemma 1). They provide a rank-µ, quadratic-time rank-one-update routine for A (extending Krause & Igel’s rank-one case), claim optimal O(µ d^2) time and O(d^2) storage, and present experiments on standard benchmarks showing identical function-evaluation counts to a state-of-the-art CMA-ES implementation but significantly reduced wall-clock times, increasingly so with dimension. They compare against a reference CMA-ES with delayed decomposition, a variant with decomposition every d iterations, and a non-triangular Cholesky-based approach (Suttorp et al.).

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on Suttorp et al. (non-triangular updates) and Krause & Igel (rank-one update in (1+1)-CMA-ES), and positions itself against limited-memory/diagonal approximations (e.g., Ros & Hansen; Loshchilov) that change the model class. The contribution is to bring a theoretically justified triangular Cholesky update to standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and to justify the CSA approximation.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and practical relevance: cubic-time decompositions can dominate runtime; avoiding them without sacrificing eval efficiency is impactful.
- Methodological contribution: extending quadratic-time rank-one Cholesky updates to the full rank-µ CMA-ES in a way that preserves C’s update rule is useful and seems carefully engineered.
- Theoretical insight: Lemma 1 provides a clean explanation for why dropping the orthogonal rotation in CSA should have diminishing impact as C’s shape stabilizes; the discussion about why triangular factors avoid the non-uniqueness issues of non-triangular updates is apt.
- Complexity: Achieving O(µ d^2) per iteration is asymptotically optimal for full covariance adaptation; storage is also optimal for full matrices.
- Empirics: On a broad set of smooth test functions, the method matches the evaluation efficiency of a reference implementation while substantially improving wall-clock time, with the gap widening in higher dimensions. Rotation-invariance was empirically probed.

Weaknesses and concerns
- A serious technical error: “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix” is false. diag(A)^2 are not the eigenvalues of C; they are the Cholesky pivots. You do not get eigenvalues “for free.” This must be corrected; if you rely on eigenvalue information (e.g., for conditioning diagnostics), additional computation is needed.
- Theoretical guarantees are limited: Lemma 1 requires convergence of the normalized covariance sequence C̄t (or at least slow variation). For CMA-ES, such convergence is not proven in general (only in simplified settings), and the paper provides no bound on the error in CSA due to dropping E. An early-phase bound (when C is changing fastest) would be valuable; currently the justification is asymptotic and heuristic.
- Invariance: The CSA modification weakens the affine invariance property. While CMA-ES’s CSA is already not fully affine-invariant, the paper should quantify the degree of invariance loss for the proposed approximation beyond “we tried random rotations” (e.g., track distribution of step-length deviations and angle drift of evolution paths under rotations).
- Numerical stability: Rank-one Cholesky updates can suffer from numerical issues on very ill-conditioned C or after many updates. The paper does not discuss stabilization (jitter on the diagonal, periodic refactorization, thresholding) nor compare to well-known cholupdate/choldowndate algorithms (e.g., LINPACK/LAPACK routines). Error accumulation and reconditioning frequency should be reported.
- Experimental fairness and scope: The reference CMA-ES is said to decompose every iteration for the tested ranges; standard practice often amortizes the eigendecomposition less aggressively in higher d. The CMA-ES/d baseline helps, but a more nuanced sweep of decomposition frequencies would strengthen the case. Also missing are tests under noise, constraints, and extremely ill-conditioned rotated ellipsoids (e.g., κ≥1e12), where CSA behavior can be delicate. Reporting performance for very high d where full C is still barely feasible (e.g., d≈500–1000) would better illustrate scaling and memory claims.
- Presentation issues: Several reference entries have formatting artifacts and missing details; some footnotes bleed into the text; figures/tables are not included here but clarity around Algorithm 2 derivation and its relation to standard methods could be improved.

Quality
- The algorithmic idea is sound and practical. However, the eigenvalue claim is incorrect and should be fixed. The theoretical support is suggestive but not rigorous for general CMA-ES; nonetheless, the empirical evidence is consistent with the claims.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, but the CSA approximation rationale could be made more precise with an explicit error decomposition and a short discussion on when it might fail. Provide references and/or a derivation for Algorithm 2 and discuss numerical stability considerations.

Originality
- Extends known Cholesky-update ideas to full rank-µ CMA-ES with a CSA workaround and a rationale for why it should work. This combination appears novel relative to Suttorp et al. and Krause & Igel.

Significance
- Practically significant for users of full-C CMA-ES in moderate-to-high d where decomposition is a bottleneck. Likely to be adopted in libraries. Conceptually, it advances engineering of CMA-ES rather than foundational theory.

Questions for the authors
1) Please correct the statement about Cholesky diagonals and eigenvalues. Do you rely on eigenvalues anywhere? If you need them, how are they computed?
2) Can you provide empirical or theoretical bounds on the error in ‖pσ‖ when replacing C−1/2 with A−1, especially in the early phase when C changes rapidly?
3) How often do you re-factorize or recondition to mitigate numerical drift? Did you observe loss of positive-definiteness or severe condition-number growth?
4) How sensitive are your results to the frequency of decomposition in the CMA-ES-Ref baseline? Can you plot runtime versus decomposition frequency?
5) Can you include experiments with noise and stronger ill-conditioning, and report metrics of rotation drift (e.g., angle between Et and Et−1) over time?

Pros for acceptance
- Practical speedups with no loss in eval efficiency
- Clean and implementable method with clear complexity benefits
- Useful theoretical intuition (Lemma 1) and solid empirical evidence
- Relevant to a sizable community using CMA-ES

Cons for acceptance
- Incorrect technical claim about eigenvalues
- Limited theoretical guarantees; early-phase behavior not bounded
- Invariance and numerical stability not deeply analyzed
- Experimental baselines could be more comprehensive

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on correcting the eigenvalue claim and strengthening the discussion of numerical stability and invariance; the engineering contribution is solid and useful, and the empirical results are compelling.
