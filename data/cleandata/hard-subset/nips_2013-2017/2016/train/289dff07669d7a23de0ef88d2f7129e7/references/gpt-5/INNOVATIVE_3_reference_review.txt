Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = AAT, avoiding explicit eigendecompositions/square roots. The main technical obstacle is CSA step-size adaptation, which in standard CMA-ES uses C−1/2; replacing this by A−1 introduces an unknown orthogonal matrix evolving over time. The authors argue that the orientation matrices converge (under mild conditions on the normalized covariance), making the error in the CSA norm negligible due to exponential forgetting, and thus justify omitting the orthogonal correction. They derive an O(µ d^2) update via a sequence of rank-one Cholesky updates (including both rank-1 and rank-µ parts) with optimal storage (triangular factor). Empirically, on a range of rotated benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) up to d=256, Cholesky-CMA-ES matches standard CMA-ES in number of evaluations and is dramatically faster in wall-clock time than the reference implementation and than a delayed-update baseline, with speedups increasing with d. They also compare to a non-triangular Cholesky update (Suttorp et al.), showing advantages in speed and stability.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on rank-one Cholesky updates for evolution strategies (Suttorp et al., 2009) and the more recent efficient rank-one updates for special CMA variants (Krause & Igel, 2015). Prior methods either (i) restrict the model (diagonal/low-rank) to get O(d)–O(d log d) updates, at the cost of performance on non-separable problems, or (ii) delay decompositions to amortize cubic costs, leading to outdated matrices. The novelty here is extending triangular Cholesky-factor updates to standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 and providing a principled argument for approximating CSA without the orthogonal factor, thereby retaining full-covariance modeling with optimal O(µ d^2) update and storage.

Quality
- Theory: The core Lemma (convergence of ETt−1Et under convergence of shape-normalized covariance) is sound and the associated continuity argument is reasonable. The argument that CSA-norm error vanishes due to exponential forgetting when orientations change slowly is intuitive and compelling, though not a formal finite-time bound. The claim of asymptotically optimal complexity and storage for full-covariance adaptation is accurate.
- Algorithmic detail: The rank-one triangular update is clearly specified. The integration of rank-1 and rank-µ terms matches the standard CMA-ES update. The discussion of numerical properties (triangular storage, easy inversion, eigenvalues on the diagonal) is useful.
- Missing rigor: The CSA approximation is not accompanied by bounds quantifying the deviation in general non-stationary regimes; the invariance implication is discussed but not proven. However, the empirical parity in function evaluations across rotated functions mitigates this.
- Experiments: The setup is solid (rotationally invariant testing, multiple dimensions, 100 runs). Results convincingly show same evaluations and much lower wall-clock time. It would be good to include noisy and constrained problems and at least one real task (e.g., policy search) where step-size adaptation is critical. Also, report runtime breakdown and memory usage to substantiate “optimal” claims in practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanation of why the orthogonal correction is problematic and the proposed workaround is clear. Algorithms 1 and 2 are useful. Some references are malformed and should be fixed; a few minor typos exist. The discussion of rotation invariance and the measured rotation Et is relegated to supplementary; a brief quantitative summary in the main text would strengthen the case.

Originality
While Cholesky-based updates are known, the extension to standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 and the theoretically motivated CSA approximation are novel and practically important. This is not just an engineering tweak; it opens a clean path to full-covariance CMA-ES with immediate updates in O(µ d^2), removing reliance on delayed decompositions.

Significance
High for the CMA-ES community and practitioners of black-box optimization in ML (policy search, hyperparameter tuning). Matching evaluation counts with large runtime savings (20x at d=64) will likely change default implementations. The method keeps full-covariance performance, unlike low-complexity approximations. Even if full covariance remains O(d^2), this work pushes the practical frontier upward in d.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, implementable algorithm with strong practical impact: full-covariance CMA-ES at O(µ d^2) without delayed decompositions.
- Sensible theoretical justification for the CSA approximation; preserves performance in practice.
- Extensive empirical evidence on rotated benchmarks showing identical evaluation counts and large speedups.
- Optimal storage and additional practical perks (eigenvalues for free, efficient inversion).

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of formal bounds on the CSA approximation; invariance not rigorously characterized.
- Experiments limited to synthetic benchmarks; no noisy/constrained/real-world tasks.
- Comparison to other efficient full-covariance implementations is limited; include e.g., incremental eigendecomposition variants if available.
- Limited discussion of numerical stability safeguards (e.g., damping when eigenvalues approach floor), though alluded to.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a quantitative summary (main text) of the measured Et rotation and CSA-norm error over time.
- Add results on noisy functions and one real RL/hyperparameter tuning task.
- Report memory usage and runtime breakdown by component.
- Clarify whether rotational invariance is practically preserved and under what conditions.
- Discuss/implement damping or minimum variance enforcement; show its impact.
- Fix reference formatting and minor typos.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a meaningful and well-argued advance with high practical significance and sufficient novelty for NIPS, even if some theoretical and experimental aspects could be extended.
