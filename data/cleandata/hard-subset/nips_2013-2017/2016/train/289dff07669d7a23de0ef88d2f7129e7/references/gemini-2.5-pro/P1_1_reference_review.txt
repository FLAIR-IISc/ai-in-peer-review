### Summary

This paper addresses a major computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard CMA-ES has a time complexity of at least O(d²) per iteration due to the need to compute the square root of the d x d covariance matrix C. This computation, particularly for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, can dominate the runtime when the objective function is cheap to evaluate or the dimensionality d is large.

The authors propose a new variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, which avoids this bottleneck by directly updating a Cholesky factor A (where C = AAᵀ) of the covariance matrix. This reduces the complexity of the covariance update to O(µd²), which is asymptotically optimal for a full-matrix method (µ is the parent population size, typically O(log d)). The key challenge, which has hindered previous attempts, is that the CSA update requires the matrix square root C⁻¹/², not just any factor's inverse like A⁻¹. The authors' main contribution is to propose an approximation where C⁻¹/² is replaced by A⁻¹, where A is the unique lower triangular Cholesky factor. They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) arguing that the error introduced by this approximation diminishes as the algorithm converges and the shape of the covariance matrix stabilizes.

Empirical results on a set of standard benchmark functions show that the Cholesky-CMA-ES requires virtually the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES implementation. However, in terms of wall-clock time, it provides a dramatic speedup, which grows with the dimensionality d (e.g., 20x faster at d=64).

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and important problem: the computational cost of CMA-ES. Given the algorithm's widespread use in machine learning (e.g., reinforcement learning, hyperparameter tuning), a method that significantly speeds it up without degrading its optimization performance is a major contribution. This work has the potential to become the new default implementation of CMA-ES for medium-to-large scale problems.

2.  **Novel and Well-Justified Approach:** While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor is not entirely new (as referenced by Suttorp et al. [2009]), this paper is the first to provide a theoretically-motivated and empirically successful way to do so for the standard, full CMA-ES algorithm. The core idea of approximating C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ and justifying it via Lemma 1 is clever and insightful. It provides a principled reason why this approximation should work, moving beyond a purely heuristic modification.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and thoroughly executed. The authors compare their method against appropriate and strong baselines (the reference implementation and a delayed-update variant). The choice of benchmark functions is standard and covers various challenges (e.g., separable, ill-conditioned, non-convex). The results are clear and convincingly demonstrate the two key claims: (1) optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations) is preserved, and (2) runtime performance is substantially improved.

4.  **Asymptotically Optimal Complexity:** The proposed algorithm achieves O(µd²) time and O(d²) space complexity, which is optimal for any method that adapts a full covariance matrix. This provides a strong theoretical advantage over the O(d³) complexity of eigenvalue decomposition or O(d²) for every iteration.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Technical Inaccuracy Regarding Eigenvalues:** A notable weakness is a technically incorrect statement in Section 3 ("Properties of the update rule"). The authors claim: "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix". This is false for a general symmetric positive definite matrix. While true for a diagonal matrix, it does not hold in general. The eigenvalues of C are not directly available from its Cholesky factor A without further, computationally expensive steps (e.g., computing C=AAᵀ and then finding its eigenvalues). This undermines one of the claimed secondary benefits of the method and should be corrected.

2.  **Limits of the Theoretical Justification:** The justification in Lemma 1 relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix C̄t converges. While this is a reasonable assumption on many unimodal benchmark problems where the algorithm identifies the optimal covariance structure, it is less certain on complex, multi-modal landscapes where the search might alternate between different regions, causing the covariance matrix to change more erratically. It would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss this limitation or show empirical evidence (e.g., plotting ||Eᵀt-1Et - I||) that the approximation holds in practice even on more difficult problems like Rosenbrock.

3.  **Clarity of Derivation:** The explanation in Section 3 could be slightly improved for clarity. The transition from the standard `pσ` update to the approximation using `ŝt` could be elaborated on more explicitly by first writing C⁻¹/² as EᵀtA⁻¹t to make the role of the dropped orthogonal matrix `Et` more obvious to the reader from the outset.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is well-written and makes a compelling case for the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The experimental section is a key strength, providing unambiguous evidence of the algorithm's practical benefits.

The primary concern is the technical error about eigenvalues. This should be removed or rephrased. For example, while the eigenvalues are not directly available, the diagonal elements of A are related to the variances along the coordinate axes, and their product is related to the determinant (det(C) = det(A)² = ∏ A²ii), which can still be useful for monitoring. Correcting this would improve the paper's technical quality.

Despite this, the core contribution—the approximation for the step-size update and its empirical validation—is sound and highly valuable. The speedup is not marginal; it is substantial enough to enable the use of CMA-ES on problems of a dimensionality that were previously computationally prohibitive.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical bottleneck in a widely used algorithm.
*   Offers substantial, empirically verified speedups (e.g., >20x).
*   Preserves the excellent optimization performance of the original CMA-ES.
*   Provides a novel and well-motivated theoretical justification for the core approximation.
*   Achieves asymptotically optimal time and space complexity for a full-matrix method.

**Cons:**
*   Contains a significant technical error in claiming that eigenvalues are freely available from the Cholesky factor.
*   The theoretical justification rests on a convergence assumption that may not hold in all scenarios.

### Recommendation

This is a strong paper with a clear and significant contribution. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is likely to become the standard for applying CMA-ES to problems with d > 30-40 where the function evaluation is fast. The weaknesses, particularly the technical error, are correctable and do not undermine the main result of the paper. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Recommendation: Accept**
