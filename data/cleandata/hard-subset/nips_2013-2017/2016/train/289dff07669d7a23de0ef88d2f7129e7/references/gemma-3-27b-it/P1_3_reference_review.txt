## Peer Review: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

**Summary:**

This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) dubbed "Cholesky-CMA-ES". The core idea is to update and store the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix instead of the covariance matrix itself, alongside a theoretical justification for doing so without significant performance degradation. The authors argue that this approach leads to optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity, especially for ill-conditioned, non-separable problems where full covariance adaptation is crucial.  Empirical results demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES while significantly reducing runtime, particularly in higher dimensions.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established foundation of CMA-ES [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001]. It addresses the computational bottleneck of covariance matrix adaptation, a topic explored by several prior works. The authors correctly position their work within the context of methods attempting to reduce this cost: delayed updates [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001], reduced complexity models [Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008], and Cholesky factor updates [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015].  Crucially, this paper distinguishes itself by extending the Cholesky update approach to the *full* CMA-ES, rather than simplified versions, and providing a theoretical argument concerning the impact of not correcting for coordinate rotations during step-size adaptation.  The connection to the work on spectral invariance [Auger, 2015] is also appropriately mentioned.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a compelling theoretical argument regarding the error introduced by omitting the coordinate rotation correction in the step-size adaptation.  Lemma 1, demonstrating the convergence of rotation matrices, is a key contribution. This differentiates the work from prior attempts at Cholesky-based CMA-ES adaptations.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The claim of optimal asymptotic complexity (O(µd<sup>2</sup>)) is significant. The empirical results strongly support the claim of substantial runtime improvements, especially as dimensionality increases.  Figures 1 & 2 clearly illustrate this performance gain.
* **Practical Implementation:**  The authors implemented the algorithm within the Shark library and used LAPACK for efficient computations, demonstrating practical feasibility.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and modifications. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and 2 is helpful.
* **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The use of a variety of benchmark functions, including those designed to expose weaknesses in covariance matrix adaptation (e.g., Diffpowers), is a strength.  The comparison against CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d and Suttorp-CMA-ES provides a thorough evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Lemma 1's Assumptions:** While the Lemma regarding the convergence of rotation matrices is a key result, the paper doesn't fully discuss the conditions under which this convergence holds.  A more in-depth discussion of the assumptions underlying the Lemma would strengthen the theoretical justification. Specifically, it’s not entirely clear how robust the convergence is to deviations from the ideal scenarios discussed.
* **Cache Effects:** The authors acknowledge cache effects impacting performance at d=256, but further investigation into this limitation could be valuable.  Exploring techniques to mitigate these effects or characterizing the range of dimensions where the algorithm performs optimally would enhance the work.
* **Comparison to Alternatives:** While the comparison to Suttorp-CMA-ES is valuable, a more detailed comparison to other dimensionality reduction techniques for CMA-ES (e.g., low-rank adaptations [Loshchilov, 2015]) would provide a more complete picture of the landscape of approaches.
* **Rotation Invariance Experiments:** The paper mentions experiments on rotation invariance (referenced as supplementary material), but the results aren't thoroughly discussed within the main body of the paper. Providing a concise summary of these results would improve clarity.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant improvement in the runtime complexity of CMA-ES, a widely used optimization algorithm.
* The theoretical justification for the approach is well-reasoned and supported by empirical evidence.
* The implementation and experimental evaluation are thorough and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method.
* The work addresses a key bottleneck in CMA-ES, making it more applicable to high-dimensional problems.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical arguments could benefit from a more detailed discussion of underlying assumptions.
* The impact of cache effects and limitations at very high dimensions warrant further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a **strong contribution** to the field of evolutionary computation. The Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a compelling solution to a practical problem in CMA-ES, backed by both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. While some areas could benefit from further elaboration (particularly regarding the assumptions of Lemma 1 and a deeper analysis of cache effects), the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper, with the suggestion that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above through minor revisions. Specifically, expanding the discussion of Lemma 1's assumptions and providing a more detailed summary of the rotation invariance experiments would strengthen the paper considerably.
