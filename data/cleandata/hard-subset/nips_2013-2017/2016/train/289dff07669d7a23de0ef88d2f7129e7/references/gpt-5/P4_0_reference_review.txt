Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T instead of recomputing a matrix square root or eigendecomposition. For sampling, A replaces C1/2 without loss. The main challenge is cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), which in standard CMA-ES uses C−1/2 to normalize steps. The authors replace C−1/2 with A−1, which introduces a per-iteration orthogonal rotation E (from the RQ decomposition of C1/2), and omit the corresponding correction. They justify this approximation by showing that the sequence of rotations becomes asymptotically stationary (ETt−1Et → I) if the normalized covariance converges in shape, implying the norm error in CSA decays due to exponential weighting.

The algorithm achieves O(µ d^2) per-iteration update time (with typical µ = O(log d)), optimal O(d^2) storage, and provides quadratic-time triangular solves and inversion when needed. Prior work updated Cholesky factors for (1+1)-CMA-ES and related variants [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015], and reduced model complexity in other CMA variants [Ros and Hansen, 2008; Loshchilov, 2014, 2015; Sun et al., 2013]. This paper extends rank-one Cholesky updates to the standard (µ, λ)-CMA-ES with rank-µ terms and provides a theoretical argument for the CSA approximation. Experiments on standard benchmark functions (including rotated versions) show similar numbers of function evaluations as a reference CMA-ES and Suttorp-style Cholesky methods, but significantly reduced wall-clock time, with growing speedups as d increases.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The use of Cholesky for sampling is standard. Decomposing the covariance update into rank-one updates applied to the Cholesky factor is sound and builds on known cholupdate techniques. The CSA approximation is the key change. The lemma arguing ETt−1Et → I under convergence of the normalized covariance matrix provides intuition but lacks quantitative error bounds and depends on assumptions (convergence, uniqueness/continuity choices in RQ/QR with sign constraints). The claim that the diagonal of the Cholesky factor provides the square roots of the eigenvalues of C is incorrect (those are pivots, not eigenvalues). Numerical stability of repeated rank-one updates is not analyzed; citing or proving stability guarantees (e.g., conditions on β and reconditioning frequency) would strengthen the work. The experimental results consistently show unchanged evaluation counts and strong runtime gains, but broader test coverage and more detailed reporting would improve confidence.
- Clarity: The paper presents the algorithm and motivation clearly overall, and the pseudocode is helpful. Some notational issues (interchanging QR/RQ, overloading E for both orthogonal matrices and expectation, typos in references, and minor formatting glitches) reduce clarity. The theoretical section should explicitly state uniqueness conventions (e.g., positive diagonal in R/triangular factors) and assumptions behind the continuity argument. Hardware, BLAS/LAPACK usage, and reference implementation details should be documented to assess runtime fairness.
- Originality: The main novelty is adapting the full CMA-ES (with rank-µ updates) to operate directly on a triangular Cholesky factor and proposing a CSA approximation with theoretical justification. This goes beyond previous Cholesky-update variants that targeted different CMA-ES flavors or used non-triangular factors. While the idea of using Cholesky updates is not new, the integration into the standard algorithm and the accompanying analysis represent a meaningful incremental contribution.
- Significance: Reducing per-iteration time from cubic to O(µ d^2) without increasing function evaluations is valuable for high-dimensional black-box optimization when evaluation cost is moderate and full covariance adaptation is needed. The practical speedups are large (e.g., ~20× at d=64) and grow with dimension. This is likely to be useful to practitioners using CMA-ES for policy search and hyperparameter tuning. The approach remains O(d^2), so it does not supplant limited-memory methods for very large d.

Strengths:
- Simple, principled modification of CMA-ES with clear computational benefits (time and memory).
- Retains the core behavior of CMA-ES empirically (same evaluations on multiple benchmarks, including rotated).
- Theoretical rationale for CSA approximation, with a plausible asymptotic argument.
- Implementation details (LAPACK, Shark integration) suggest practical readiness.

Weaknesses:
- Incorrect statement: the diagonal entries of the Cholesky factor are not square roots of the eigenvalues of C; eigenvalues of C are not directly available “for free.”
- CSA approximation lacks quantitative error bounds and may affect invariance properties; theoretical guarantees rely on convergence assumptions and uniqueness/continuity in RQ not fully specified.
- Experimental scope is limited: few function classes, no COCO/BBOB profiling, no noisy/constrained cases, and the fairness of runtime comparisons (optimized EVD vs Cholesky) is not fully established.
- Numerical stability and potential need for periodic reconditioning (e.g., re-Cholesky) are not discussed; Algorithm 2 is not referenced to standard cholupdate literature or analyzed.
- Clarity issues in notation and references (placeholders, typos).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Correct the eigenvalue claim; if eigenvalues are needed, clarify the additional cost (e.g., symmetric eigendecomposition of C or use of Lanczos on A A^T).
- Provide a bound (or empirical analysis) on the CSA norm error versus rotation drift, especially during early iterations when C changes faster, and assess impact on invariance.
- Add experiments on COCO/BBOB, including noisy and ill-conditioned cases, with statistical tests on evaluation counts; report hardware, libraries, and ensure comparable optimizations for baselines.
- Analyze numerical stability of repeated rank-one updates; recommend triangular solves instead of forming A−1 explicitly, and discuss when re-factorization is advisable.
- Clarify RQ/QR conventions (positive diagonals) and continuity assumptions in the lemma; clean up notation and references.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Practical, significant speedups with unchanged evaluation efficiency for full-covariance CMA-ES.
- Conceptually simple method that can be widely adopted.
- Novel integration of Cholesky updates with standard CMA-ES and a reasonable theoretical justification.

Arguments against acceptance:
- One clear technical error (eigenvalues claim) and limited rigor in the theoretical justification of CSA approximation.
- Experimental validation not comprehensive enough to establish robustness across tasks and settings; runtime fairness details could be improved.
- Clarity and presentation issues in notation and references.

Overall, the paper offers a useful and implementable advance for CMA-ES with strong empirical benefits, but it would benefit from correcting technical claims, tightening the theory, and broadening the evaluation.
