This paper addresses the well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), where the per-iteration complexity is dominated by the need to compute the square root of the d x d covariance matrix. The authors propose a variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which avoids the explicit computation and decomposition of the covariance matrix. Instead, it directly maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the matrix. This reduces the time complexity from O(d^3) to an asymptotically optimal O(μd^2) and also reduces storage requirements.

The central challenge in this approach lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism of CMA-ES, which requires the `C^{-1/2}` term to normalize the evolution path. This term cannot be directly replaced by the inverse of an arbitrary Cholesky factor without altering the algorithm's properties. The authors' key contribution is to propose an approximation where `C^{-1/2}` is replaced by `A^{-1}`, where `A` is the unique lower triangular Cholesky factor. They provide a theoretical argument, based on the convergence of the *shape* of the covariance matrix, that the error introduced by this approximation should vanish as the optimization process converges. The paper presents empirical results on a set of standard benchmark functions, showing that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant reduction in wall-clock time compared to the standard implementation, without any discernible degradation in performance as measured by the number of function evaluations.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance and Practical Impact:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem. The quadratic complexity of CMA-ES is a major barrier to its application in higher dimensions, especially when function evaluations are cheap. The demonstrated speedups are substantial (e.g., 20x for d=64) and would represent a significant practical contribution if the method proves robust.
2.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is well-conducted. The proposed method is benchmarked against the appropriate state-of-the-art reference implementation of CMA-ES on a standard suite of test functions. The results are compelling, showing that the approximation does not appear to harm convergence on these problems while providing the expected and significant improvements in runtime.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the technical presentation is clear. The authors do a good job of explaining the problem, the existing work, and the details of their proposed modification.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Methodological Soundness of the Approximation:** The primary weakness of this work lies in the theoretical justification for the core approximation. The CSA mechanism is a principled and crucial component of the standard CMA-ES. The proposed modification replaces the `C^{-1/2}` term, which is essential for achieving (or attempting to achieve) invariance to rotations of the coordinate system, with `A^{-1}`, which is coordinate-system dependent. The justification for this rests on Lemma 1, which relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges. However, as the authors rightly note, for a stochastic algorithm like CMA-ES, `C_t` does not necessarily converge. The argument is then weakened to a heuristic claim that the error will be small if `C_t` changes "slowly." This is a significant departure from the rigorous foundations of the original algorithm and is not supported by a formal analysis of the approximation error. It is unclear under what conditions this heuristic holds or, more importantly, when it might fail.
2.  **Potential for Failure Modes:** The reliance on the "slowly changing" shape of the covariance matrix is concerning. One can conceive of optimization trajectories, particularly in the earlier stages or on more complex landscapes, where the optimal orientation of the search distribution changes rapidly. In such scenarios, the rotation matrices `E_t` could be far from the identity, causing the approximation to be poor and potentially leading to incorrect step-size adaptation. The set of benchmark functions used, while standard, may not be sufficient to expose such failure modes. A more thorough validation would include problems specifically designed to induce rapid rotations of the covariance matrix to test the robustness of the approximation.
3.  **Loss of Invariance Properties:** A key strength of the original CMA-ES is its invariance properties, which contribute to its robustness. The proposed approximation explicitly breaks the rotational invariance of the CSA path update. While the authors correctly note that the standard σ-update is not proven to be fully affine invariant, the `C^{-1/2}` term is the very mechanism by which this property is sought. Replacing it with a term dependent on a specific factorization seems like a step backward from a theoretical standpoint, even if it works well empirically on the tested problems.

In summary, the paper presents a trade-off: it achieves significant and valuable practical speedups at the cost of sacrificing some of the methodological rigor of a well-established and highly trusted algorithm. While the empirical results are impressive, the theoretical justification for the underlying approximation is not fully convincing and relies on assumptions that may not hold in general.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical limitation of a widely used optimization algorithm.
*   The proposed method is simple to implement and offers substantial, empirically verified wall-clock speedups.
*   On standard benchmarks, the method shows no degradation in solution quality (measured in function evaluations), suggesting it is effective in many common scenarios.

**Arguments for Rejection/Revision:**
*   The theoretical justification for the core approximation in the CSA update is weak and relies on heuristic arguments about the convergence of the covariance matrix.
*   The modification compromises the invariance properties that are a key theoretical strength of the original CMA-ES.
*   The experimental validation, while solid, does not explore potential pathological cases where the approximation might fail (e.g., problems requiring rapid covariance matrix rotation).

While the practical benefits are undeniable, my preference is for methods that rest on a solid theoretical foundation, especially when modifying a cornerstone algorithm like CMA-ES. The current justification for the approximation feels more like a post-hoc rationalization of an empirically successful heuristic rather than a principled derivation. The work would be substantially stronger if the authors could provide a more rigorous analysis of the approximation error or, alternatively, more thoroughly characterize the conditions under which their method can be expected to perform well through more targeted experimentation.
