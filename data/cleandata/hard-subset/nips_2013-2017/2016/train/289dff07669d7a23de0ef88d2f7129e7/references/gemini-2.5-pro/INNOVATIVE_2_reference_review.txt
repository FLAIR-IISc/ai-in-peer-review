This paper addresses a well-known and significant bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES): the computational complexity of updating and decomposing the covariance matrix, which scales poorly with the problem dimension `d`. The authors propose a new variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that directly updates the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This reduces the time complexity from O(d^3) for a full decomposition to an asymptotically optimal O(µd^2) per iteration, while also reducing memory requirements.

The central challenge in previous attempts to use Cholesky updates for the standard CMA-ES has been the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the matrix square root C^(-1/2) for normalization, not just any factor whose product is C. The key contribution and most novel aspect of this paper is a clever and well-justified approximation that replaces the required C^(-1/2) term with A^(-1), where A is the lower triangular Cholesky factor. The authors provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the orthogonal transformation relating A to C^(1/2) also converges, meaning the error introduced by this approximation diminishes over time. This is a bold and elegant solution to a long-standing problem that has previously prevented such efficient updates from being applied to the standard, most widely used version of CMA-ES.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly practical problem. While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor is not new in itself (e.g., Suttorp et al. 2009), this work is the first to successfully and rigorously adapt it to the standard CMA-ES algorithm. The proposed approximation for the step-size update is a significant conceptual breakthrough that distinguishes this work from prior, more incremental efforts. The potential impact is substantial, as it could make CMA-ES practical for a much wider range of high-dimensional problems in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization, which are core areas of interest for the NIPS community.

2.  **Bold and Principled Approach:** Instead of simply presenting an engineering "hack," the authors provide a theoretical justification for their approximation. While not a full convergence proof, Lemma 1 offers a convincing rationale for why the approximation does not harm the algorithm's performance. This blend of a bold algorithmic simplification with careful theoretical reasoning is commendable. This is precisely the type of creative, high-risk/high-reward research that pushes the field forward, prioritizing a novel direction over methodological perfectionism.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are clear and compelling. The authors demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations) as the state-of-the-art reference implementation, while being dramatically faster in wall-clock time. The reported speed-up of 20x for d=64 is impressive and directly validates the paper's central claims. The comparison against multiple relevant baselines, including a delayed-update strategy, is thorough.

4.  **Clarity and Quality of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly explains the background, motivates the problem, details the proposed method, and justifies the crucial approximation step. The connection between the matrix square root, the Cholesky factor, and the introduced rotational error is explained with great clarity.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Guarantees:** The theoretical argument hinges on the assumption that the shape of the covariance matrix converges. While this is a reasonable assumption in many optimization scenarios, the paper does not analyze cases where this might not hold. A more formal analysis of the approximation error's dynamics would strengthen the paper, but its absence is not a critical flaw given the paper's focus and strong empirical backing. I view this as a minor point, as the empirical success on a range of difficult benchmark functions largely validates the approach in practice.

2.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on standard numerical optimization benchmarks. While these are appropriate for validating the algorithm's core properties (e.g., rotation invariance, performance on ill-conditioned problems), demonstrating the speed-up on a concrete machine learning task, such as a high-dimensional policy search problem, would have further underscored its relevance to the NIPS audience.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant solution to a long-standing, important problem in a widely used optimization algorithm.
*   The core idea—the justified approximation in the step-size update—is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   The practical impact is huge, enabling the application of CMA-ES to much larger problems.
*   The work achieves asymptotically optimal complexity for its task.
*   Empirical results are unambiguous and strongly support the claims.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical justification is an argument based on convergence, not a formal proof of performance preservation under all conditions.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It is an excellent example of impactful research that combines algorithmic novelty, a clever theoretical insight, and compelling practical results. The paper addresses a significant limitation of a foundational algorithm, and the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is likely to become the new standard implementation. Its contribution is not merely incremental; it is a conceptual leap that solves a problem many have struggled with. The potential benefits for practitioners in the NIPS community are immediate and substantial. This is a high-quality, significant, and original piece of work.
