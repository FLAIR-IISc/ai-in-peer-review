

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors provide theoretical justification for their approximation (replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}) and demonstrate empirical improvements in runtime without compromising function evaluation counts. The work builds on previous efforts to address the O(d^3) complexity bottleneck in CMA-ES, particularly extending the approach of Suttorp et al. (2009) to the standard CMA-ES framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides Lemma 1 establishing that the error introduced by the approximation vanishes as the algorithm converges. This is a significant contribution that addresses a key concern in previous attempts to use Cholesky factorization in CMA-ES.

2. **Practical significance:** The empirical results demonstrate a substantial runtime improvement (up to 20x for d=64) without increasing the number of function evaluations, which is particularly valuable for high-dimensional optimization problems where the standard CMA-ES becomes computationally prohibitive.

3. **Clarity and organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical justification to empirical validation. The algorithm descriptions are precise and the experiments are well-designed.

4. **Novelty:** While previous work has explored Cholesky-based approaches (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this paper provides the first theoretically justified approach for the standard CMA-ES that maintains performance guarantees.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited experimental validation:** The experiments focus primarily on rotationally invariant benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, etc.), but CMA-ES is often applied to non-rotationally invariant problems in practice. The authors should demonstrate performance on more challenging, non-rotationally invariant functions to better establish the practical utility of their approach.

2. **Insufficient numerical stability analysis:** While the paper mentions numerical stability as an advantage, it doesn't provide evidence or analysis of how the Cholesky approach compares to the standard CMA-ES in terms of numerical stability, which is critical for practical implementation.

3. **Optimality claim needs strengthening:** The claim of "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity" requires more justification. While O(μd²) is indeed better than the standard O(d³), the paper doesn't clearly explain why this is optimal or how it compares to other approaches like the low-rank approximations in Loshchilov (2015) in terms of both complexity and performance trade-offs.

4. **Comparative analysis:** The comparison with CMA-ES/d and Suttorp-CMA-ES is limited. The paper should provide more detailed analysis of when each variant might be preferable based on problem characteristics.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results support the main claims. However, the limited scope of the experiments weakens the overall quality assessment. The paper could benefit from additional experiments on more challenging benchmarks.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with CMA-ES.

**Originality:** The theoretical justification for why the approximation doesn't deteriorate performance is novel and represents a meaningful contribution beyond previous attempts at Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants.

**Significance:** The work provides a practical improvement to a widely used algorithm with significant real-world implications, particularly for high-dimensional optimization problems. The speedup increases with dimensionality, making it increasingly valuable as problem size grows.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a valuable practical improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm with strong theoretical justification and empirical validation. While the experimental scope could be expanded to better establish the method's robustness across diverse problem types, the core contribution is significant and well-executed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides a theoretically justified approach to a known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES
- Demonstrates substantial runtime improvements without performance degradation
- Well-written and clearly structured
- Addresses a practical need for practitioners using CMA-ES in high-dimensional settings

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited experimental validation on non-rotationally invariant problems
- Insufficient analysis of numerical stability compared to standard CMA-ES
- Claims about optimality need stronger justification

**Suggestions for improvement:**
1. Expand experiments to include non-rotationally invariant benchmark functions and potentially real-world applications.
2. Add a detailed numerical stability analysis comparing Cholesky-CMA-ES with standard CMA-ES.
3. Strengthen the optimality claim by providing a more thorough comparison with other complexity-reduction approaches (e.g., Loshchilov's low-rank approximations).
4. Include a discussion of when Cholesky-CMA-ES would be preferable to other variants (CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) based on problem characteristics.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the practical implementation of CMA-ES. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
