Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T via a sequence of O(d^2) rank-one updates, thereby avoiding explicit eigenvalue decompositions. The key complication is the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) term pσ which in standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2}. The authors replace C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} (which differs by an orthogonal factor) and argue, via an RQ/polar decomposition argument and a lemma, that the induced rotational error decays as the algorithm converges. They claim O(µ d^2) time per iteration (µ effective parents) and O(d^2) memory, and demonstrate large wall-clock speedups with comparable function evaluation counts on standard (rotated) benchmarks up to d=256.

This work builds on rank-one Cholesky update ideas previously explored for (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), and contrasts with strategies that delay decompositions, restrict structure (diagonal/low-rank: Ros & Hansen, 2008; Loshchilov, 2015), or operate on B, D from the eigen-decomposition. The novelty is an extension to the standard CMA-ES with µ > 1, a theoretically motivated approximation for CSA, and an implementation with favorable asymptotics.

Strengths
- Clear, practical algorithmic contribution: a simple O(µ d^2) update of the triangular Cholesky factor that integrates rank-1 and rank-µ terms, with straightforward sampling and triangular solves.
- Good engineering: reduction in decomposition overhead without sacrificing full-covariance modeling; memory stays O(d^2) with a smaller constant than storing multiple dense matrices.
- Theoretical insight: the RQ/polar decomposition argument and Lemma 1 explain why replacing C^{-1/2} by A^{-1} may be acceptable as the covariance “shape” stabilizes.
- Empirical evidence: across several rotated, ill-conditioned benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers), function-evaluation counts match CMA-ES with frequent decompositions, while wall-clock time improves dramatically (e.g., ~20× at d=64).
- Relevance: CMA-ES remains widely used in ML; speeding up full-covariance CMA-ES is practically significant when objectives are cheap or dimensions moderate.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Incorrect claim about eigenvalues: The paper states that “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. For C = A A^T, diag(A) contains Cholesky pivots, not principal standard deviations; eigenvalues require an orthogonal diagonalization. You do get det(C) from diag(A), but not the spectrum. Claims about “principal components” and enforcing minimum variances along them “for free” should be corrected.
- CSA approximation: The main theoretical support (Lemma 1) assumes convergence of the normalized covariance C̄_t to a fixed shape and yields only an asymptotic statement. There is no quantitative bound on the bias/variance of pσ when using A^{-1} instead of C^{-1/2}, especially during the transient phase (where shape changes are largest) or on non-stationary/noisy problems. Empirically the effect looks small, but the paper would benefit from:
  - Measuring deviation ∥pσ(A^{-1})∥ vs ∥pσ(C^{-1/2})∥ over time, or occasional “ground-truth” normalization to quantify drift.
  - An ablation that refreshes the orthogonal factor E_t occasionally (e.g., via a polar decomposition every τ steps) to show the speed-accuracy trade-off.
- Invariance: Standard CMA-ES’s sampling and covariance update are affine-invariant; CSA is not fully affine-invariant but uses C^{-1/2} to normalize steps. Using A^{-1} further breaks invariance by a rotation. The rotation-invariance experiments are encouraging, but a more explicit quantification (e.g., evaluation counts across random rotations with confidence intervals) would strengthen the claim that practical invariance is preserved.
- Numerical stability: Repeated rank-one updates may accumulate round-off error. Algorithm 2 resembles cholupdate but without a stability discussion. Please clarify:
  - Conditions on β for positive updates (and whether negative updates are supported; e.g., “active” CMA-ES would need downdates).
  - Whether you apply reconditioning or occasional rebuilds from C if loss of SPD is detected.
  - Whether you form A^{-1} explicitly (unnecessary) or use triangular solves for pσ; the latter is generally preferable.
- Baselines and scope: The Suttorp-based baseline adapted to standard CMA-ES is briefly mentioned; more detail is needed to assess fairness. It would be informative to compare to modern low-rank/limited-memory full-covariance variants (e.g., LM-CMA/VD-CMA) in the regime where full covariance is still feasible, to position the proposed method in the accuracy–speed space.
- “Optimal” complexity: While O(µ d^2) per iteration for full-covariance adaptation is asymptotically optimal under dense updates, many CMA-ES implementations already amortize O(d^3) eigendecompositions (e.g., every O(d) iterations) and store only triangular parts. Please nuance “optimal storage” to reflect reduced constants rather than order improvements compared with careful symmetric storage.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a well-motivated algorithm and solid experiments. The CSA theory is qualitative/asymptotic; adding quantitative diagnostics or occasional orthogonal refresh would improve rigor. The eigenvalue claim is a substantive error and should be fixed.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. However:
- Correct the statement about eigenvalues and clarify what diagnostics are available “for free” (determinant, conditioning proxies like log-det, diagonal variances in the current coordinate system).
- Provide more implementation detail for Algorithm 2 (references to standard cholupdate/downdate routines, handling of scaling by √(1−c1−cµ), and guidance on numerical conditioning).
- Several references are malformed; please fix citations.

Originality
Extends prior Cholesky-update ideas to the standard µ > 1 CMA-ES with a CSA approximation and a complexity analysis. The combination and its careful justification are novel and useful.

Significance
High practical impact for users of full-covariance CMA-ES on moderate-to-large d with cheap evaluations. The demonstrated speedups are substantial without loss in sample efficiency on tested problems.

Pros
- Simple, implementable idea with strong practical benefit.
- Maintains full covariance; avoids structural restrictions.
- Good empirical validation on rotated, ill-conditioned benchmarks.

Cons
- Incorrect spectral claim; needs correction.
- CSA approximation justified only asymptotically; lacks quantitative error analysis.
- Limited discussion of numerical stability and negative updates.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct spectral statements; report determinant-/trace-based conditioning from A instead.
- Add diagnostics comparing pσ via A^{-1} vs C^{-1/2}, and/or occasional E refresh ablation.
- Discuss numerical stability (cholupdate/downdate, reconditioning) and avoid forming A^{-1}.
- Clarify Suttorp baseline details; consider adding LM/VD-CMA comparisons.
- Clean up references.

Overall, a strong practical contribution; with the above corrections and clarifications, I would be positive about acceptance.
