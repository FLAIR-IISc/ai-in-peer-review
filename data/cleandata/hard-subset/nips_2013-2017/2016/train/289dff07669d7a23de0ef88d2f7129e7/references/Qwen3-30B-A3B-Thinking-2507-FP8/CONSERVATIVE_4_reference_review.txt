

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm that replaces the matrix square root operation with Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors claim their approach achieves optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity (O(µd²)) while maintaining performance comparable to standard CMA-ES.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with precise algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) and a thorough background on CMA-ES. The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) attempts to address the critical issue of replacing C^{-1/2}_t with A^{-1}_t in the step-size adaptation. The empirical results demonstrate a significant speedup (up to 20x for d=64) with identical function evaluation counts compared to the reference CMA-ES implementation.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is its insufficient empirical validation against established baselines. The authors compare only to a "delayed" CMA-ES variant (CMA-ES-Ref) and a non-triangular Cholesky approach (Suttorp-CMA-ES), but fail to compare against more recent, state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants like LM-CMA (Loshchilov, 2015) or the recent low-rank approximation methods that address similar computational challenges. This is particularly problematic as the authors claim their method is "optimal" without demonstrating how it outperforms or differs from these more recent approaches.

The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) is insufficiently rigorous. The authors claim the error vanishes as Ct converges, but CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm where convergence of Ct is not guaranteed, only expected in simplified models (Beyer, 2014). The paper fails to provide any theoretical bounds on the error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2}_t with A^{-1}_t, nor does it quantify how much this error impacts the optimization process in practice. The claim that "the error caused by replacing st by ŝt is small and to decrease over time" lacks concrete evidence.

The empirical evaluation is limited in scope. The authors test on standard benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, etc.), but fail to demonstrate how the Cholesky-CMA-ES performs on more complex, real-world problems where CMA-ES is commonly applied (e.g., hyperparameter tuning in deep learning or reinforcement learning). The claim that this method "can be regarded as an approximation of the original CMA-ES" without evidence of comparable performance on practical problems is concerning.

The paper also neglects to address an important practical concern: the claimed O(µd²) complexity assumes a naive matrix multiplication implementation. With modern BLAS implementations, the actual speedup might be less significant than claimed, especially for larger dimensions where cache efficiency becomes critical. The paper notes this for d=256 but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the practical speedup across different hardware.

**Significance Assessment:**
While the paper identifies a genuine computational bottleneck in CMA-ES (the O(d³) matrix square root operation), it fails to demonstrate that this specific solution provides meaningful advantages over existing approaches. The authors claim "optimal" complexity, but the speedup they demonstrate (20x for d=64) is marginal compared to the existing literature on efficient CMA-ES variants. The paper does not show that Cholesky-CMA-ES can solve larger problems that standard CMA-ES cannot, which is the main motivation for the claimed significance.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a legitimate computational challenge in CMA-ES, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and practically superior to existing methods. The theoretical justification is insufficient, the empirical validation is limited to simple benchmark functions without comparison to relevant baselines, and the claimed significance does not align with the presented results. The paper would need to substantially strengthen its theoretical foundation, provide more comprehensive empirical validation against recent CMA-ES variants, and demonstrate practical benefits on more challenging optimization problems to be considered for publication.

This work is not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant publication at NIPS, especially given the existence of multiple prior approaches to the same problem. The paper represents an incremental improvement without clear evidence of superiority over established methods.
