This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm, dubbed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aiming to reduce runtime and memory complexity. The core idea is to work directly with the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix instead of the matrix square root, coupled with a theoretical justification for maintaining performance despite omitting a correction factor in the step-size adaptation. The authors provide theoretical analysis and empirical results on benchmark functions, demonstrating a significant speedup, particularly in higher dimensions.

**Quality:** The paper is generally technically sound. The justification for the Cholesky factorization is well-explained, and the theoretical analysis regarding the error introduced by omitting the correction factor (Lemma 1) is reasonable. The experiments are thorough, comparing the proposed method with several CMA-ES variants across a range of benchmark functions and dimensions. However, the discussion surrounding the impact of exceeding the L2 cache size during experiments with d=256 feels a little glossed over. While acknowledged, a more detailed analysis of this performance limitation would strengthen the paper. The convergence analysis relies on the assumption of slowly changing covariance matrices, and a discussion of scenarios where this assumption might not hold would be beneficial. The claim of optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity is reasonable given the approach, but could be explicitly compared to other related work on low-rank or subspace methods for improved clarity.

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly well-written and organized. The motivation and background are clearly presented, and the algorithm description is concise. However, the notation could be improved in some places. For instance, introducing the RQ-decomposition earlier in the paper might make the discussion surrounding Lemma 1 more accessible. The link between equation (6) and the discussion on rotation invariance could be more explicitly highlighted. The inclusion of Algorithm 2 enhances clarity by providing a concrete implementation detail. A minor point: consistently using "Cholesky-CMA-ES" rather than switching between that and "our CMA-ES variant" would improve readability.

**Originality:** The idea of using the Cholesky factorization within CMA-ES is not entirely new, as the authors acknowledge prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015). However, the key novelty lies in the theoretical justification for omitting the correction factor in the step-size adaptation and the extension of the fast Cholesky update to the standard CMA-ES setting. This is a significant contribution.  The paper thoroughly references related work, demonstrating awareness of the existing literature.  The analysis of the error introduced by the approximation and the discussion of its implications are original and insightful.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant. The observed speedup, especially in higher dimensions, could make CMA-ES more practical for a wider range of applications, particularly those involving high-dimensional parameter spaces. The improved memory efficiency is also a valuable advantage. This work advances the state of the art by offering a more efficient implementation of a well-established optimization algorithm. While the improvement builds on existing CMA-ES infrastructure, the runtime reduction has demonstrable practical value. The theoretical insights regarding the step-size adaptation error could inform the development of future CMA-ES variants.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant speedup:** Empirical results demonstrate substantial runtime improvements, particularly with increasing dimensionality.
*   **Sound theoretical foundation:** The authors provide a compelling justification for their approach and analyze the potential error introduced by the approximation.
*   **Practical relevance:** The reduced memory footprint and improved runtime make CMA-ES more accessible for a broader range of applications.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its benefits.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Performance limitations at high dimensions:** The impact of exceeding the L2 cache size in the experiments needs further investigation.
*   **Convergence assumption:** The reliance on slowly changing covariance matrices could be a limitation in certain scenarios.
*   **Limited comparison to other state-of-the-art methods:** A more detailed comparison to more recent or alternative approaches for high-dimensional optimization would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a significant speedup over existing CMA-ES implementations while maintaining comparable performance. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results are compelling.  While there are a few minor areas for improvement, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
