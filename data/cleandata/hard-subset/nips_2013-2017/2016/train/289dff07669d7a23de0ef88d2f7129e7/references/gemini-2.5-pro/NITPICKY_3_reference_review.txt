**Summary**
This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) caused by the need to decompose the d x d covariance matrix C, an operation with cubic complexity. The authors propose a variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that maintains and updates a Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix (where C = AA^T) directly. This reduces the per-iteration complexity to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for a full-rank covariance model. The key challenge lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the matrix square root C^(-1/2) to normalize steps. The authors' central contribution is to approximate C^(-1/2) with A^(-1), effectively ignoring an orthogonal transformation matrix that relates the two. They provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) suggesting that the error introduced by this approximation should vanish as the algorithm converges. The paper presents empirical results on several benchmark functions, demonstrating that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant reduction in wall-clock time compared to standard implementations, without degrading performance in terms of the number of function evaluations required.

**Strengths**
1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known and practically important limitation of CMA-ES, one of the most powerful derivative-free optimization algorithms. Improving its scalability is a valuable contribution.
2.  **Performance:** The empirical results are compelling. The proposed method provides a substantial speed-up in wall-clock time (e.g., a 20x speed-up for d=64), which grows with the dimensionality of the problem, consistent with the theoretical complexity analysis.
3.  **Empirical Soundness:** The experiments appear well-designed, using standard benchmarks, testing for rotation invariance, and comparing against relevant baselines, including a standard reference implementation and an alternative Cholesky-based approach. The finding that the approximation does not noticeably increase the number of required function evaluations is a strong result.

**Weaknesses**
The paper, while presenting a promising approach, is undermined by several significant flaws ranging from technical inaccuracies to a lack of polish in the presentation.

1.  **Technical Incorrectness:** In Section 3 ("Properties of the update rule"), the authors make a factually incorrect claim: "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix". This statement is false for general symmetric positive-definite matrices; it holds only if the matrix is diagonal. For a matrix C = AA^T, the eigenvalues of C are the squares of the singular values of A. This is a fundamental error in linear algebra that invalidates a claimed benefit of the proposed method and casts doubt on the authors' technical rigor.

2.  **Overstated Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical argument in Lemma 1 is presented as a "justification" for the approximation. However, it relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges, which the authors themselves note is not guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm. The argument is more of a plausible heuristic—that the error is small if C changes slowly—than a rigorous proof of performance equivalence. The title's claim of an "Optimal Covariance Update" is also potentially misleading; the update is optimal in terms of computational complexity, but it is an approximation of the original, statistically motivated CMA-ES update.

3.  **Lack of Clarity and Polish:** The manuscript suffers from a notable lack of care in its preparation, which detracts from its overall quality.
    *   **Bibliography:** The reference section is exceptionally poorly formatted. Many entries contain extraneous BibTeX-like artifacts (e.g., `Akimoto et al\.,? \Q2012\E, Akimoto et al\.,`). There are inconsistencies in capitalization of titles, formatting of journal names, and superfluous punctuation (e.g., "...(GECCO),,"). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Typos and Artifacts:** The paper contains several typos and formatting remnants. For example, the footer on the first page appears to be an unedited template string. The sentence at the end of Section 2.1 reads, "...and it is it conjectured that it does not," which is ungrammatical. The notation `x 7→ Bx+ b` is non-standard.
    *   **Reproducibility:** While the authors state that default parameters were used, explicitly listing these parameters in an appendix would enhance the paper's self-containedness and reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses an important, practical problem in a widely used optimization algorithm.
*   The proposed method is simple, effective, and yields significant, well-documented performance improvements in terms of runtime.
*   The empirical evidence strongly suggests the core approximation is viable and does not harm optimization performance on the tested problems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper contains a serious technical error regarding the relationship between a Cholesky factor and the eigenvalues of the original matrix.
*   The theoretical justification for the core approximation is heuristic and its strength is overstated.
*   The manuscript is marred by an unacceptable number of formatting errors, typos, and an exceptionally messy bibliography, indicating a lack of careful preparation.

**Decision**
While the core idea and empirical results are strong, the combination of a significant technical error, an overstated theoretical claim, and the extremely poor state of the manuscript's presentation leads me to recommend **rejection**. The flaws are too numerous and substantial for a paper at NIPS. The work shows promise, but it requires a thorough revision to correct the technical inaccuracies and a meticulous proofreading to bring the presentation up to the expected standard.
