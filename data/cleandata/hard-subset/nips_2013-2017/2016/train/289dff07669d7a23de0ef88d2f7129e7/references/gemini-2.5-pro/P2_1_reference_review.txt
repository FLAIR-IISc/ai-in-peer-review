### **Summary**

This paper presents a new variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, designed to address the algorithm's primary computational bottleneck: the O(d³) complexity of decomposing the covariance matrix at each step. The authors propose directly updating the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, which reduces the per-iteration complexity to O(µd²), where µ is typically logarithmic in the dimension d. The main technical challenge in adapting the standard CMA-ES is the step-size update rule, which requires the matrix square root C⁻¹/². The authors' key contribution is to propose an approximation using the inverse of the Cholesky factor, A⁻¹, instead. They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) arguing that the error introduced by this approximation diminishes as the shape of the covariance matrix converges. Empirical results on a suite of benchmark optimization problems demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires virtually the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES while being significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, especially in higher dimensions.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a significant practical problem:** The quadratic/cubic complexity of the standard CMA-ES is a well-known limitation that hinders its application to higher-dimensional problems where function evaluations are cheap. This work provides a direct and effective solution.
*   **Strong theoretical justification:** Unlike previous attempts to use Cholesky factor updates, this paper provides a novel and elegant theoretical argument (Lemma 1) for why their proposed approximation in the step-size adaptation mechanism is sound. The reasoning that the error vanishes as the rotation component of the decomposition stabilizes is compelling.
*   **Excellent empirical validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. By comparing against the standard reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) and a delayed-update variant (CMA-ES/d), the authors clearly demonstrate the benefits of their approach. The separation of metrics into function evaluations (showing performance parity) and wall-clock time (showing speed-up) is crucial and executed well.
*   **Asymptotically optimal complexity:** The proposed algorithm achieves O(µd²) time and O(d²) space complexity for adapting a full covariance matrix, which is optimal. The paper also highlights other benefits like numerical stability and obtaining matrix eigenvalues "for free."
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem is clearly motivated, the background is concise, the proposed method is explained logically, and the results are presented effectively. Algorithm 1 and 2 provide a clear recipe for implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Reliance on a convergence assumption:** The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix C̄t converges. While the authors acknowledge that this is not guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm, this assumption is central to their claim that the approximation error will vanish. In practice, on complex, non-stationary landscapes, C̄t may not stabilize, potentially leading to a persistent discrepancy in the step-size adaptation. However, the strong empirical results suggest this is not a major issue on the tested problems.
*   **Limited scope of experiments:** The experiments are conducted on standard synthetic benchmark functions. While this is appropriate for validating the core algorithm, demonstrating the speed-up on a real-world machine learning problem (e.g., direct policy search for a moderately high-dimensional controller, or hyperparameter optimization for a deep learning model) would have further strengthened the paper's impact and significance for the NIPS audience.

### **Detailed Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed modification is mathematically sound, building upon established methods for rank-one updates of Cholesky factorizations. The core theoretical contribution, Lemma 1, is sound and provides a solid foundation for the approximation made. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical reasoning and a rigorous set of experiments. The authors are honest about the nature of their approximation and its underlying assumptions, which adds to the work's credibility. This is a complete and polished piece of research.

**Clarity:** The paper is a model of clarity. The authors do an excellent job of distilling the essential components of CMA-ES, pinpointing the exact source of the computational bottleneck, and explaining precisely how their method addresses it. The distinction between the matrix square root (C¹/²) and a Cholesky factor (A), and why this matters for the step-size update (pσ), is articulated perfectly. The figures are clear, well-labeled, and effectively convey the main results—performance equivalence in function evaluations and a dramatic speed-up in runtime. The paper provides enough detail (including pseudocode) for an expert to reproduce the work.

**Originality:** While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix has been explored before (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this paper's contribution is highly original. Previous work either applied to different CMA-ES variants (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES) or used non-triangular decompositions which lack the uniqueness and stability properties exploited here. The primary novelty lies in successfully adapting this technique to the modern, standard CMA-ES by providing a theoretically-justified approximation for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. This is a critical step that previous work had not accomplished. The related work is adequately cited and the paper clearly positions its unique contributions.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. CMA-ES is a workhorse algorithm for derivative-free optimization with numerous applications in machine learning. This paper presents what is arguably the most effective method to date for speeding up the canonical version of the algorithm without compromising its performance on non-separable problems. The practical benefits are substantial: researchers and practitioners can now apply CMA-ES to significantly higher-dimensional problems than before, or solve existing problems much faster, provided the function evaluation is not the sole bottleneck. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a strong candidate to become the new default implementation in standard scientific libraries, making it a contribution that the community is very likely to use and build upon. It demonstrably advances the state of the art in practical black-box optimization.

### **Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that solves a long-standing and important practical problem with an elegant, well-justified, and empirically validated solution. It combines theoretical insight with significant practical impact. The work is of high quality, clearly presented, and makes a novel and significant contribution to the field of numerical optimization, which is of great relevance to the machine learning community. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
