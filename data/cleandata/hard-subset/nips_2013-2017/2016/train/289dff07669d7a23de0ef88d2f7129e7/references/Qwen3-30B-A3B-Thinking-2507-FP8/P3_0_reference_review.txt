

# Peer Review: "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary and Context
This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the computationally expensive matrix square root operation with a Cholesky factorization. The authors argue that this change reduces the runtime complexity from O(d³) to O(µd²) (with µ typically O(log d)), while maintaining comparable optimization performance to the standard CMA-ES. The key insight is that the error introduced in the step-size adaptation by replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ (where Aₜ is the Cholesky factor of Cₜ) becomes negligible as the algorithm converges, as theoretically justified by Lemma 1. This builds upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), but extends it to the full CMA-ES framework with theoretical justification for the approximation.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a novel theoretical justification for why the Cholesky approximation doesn't degrade performance. Lemma 1 establishes that the error in the step-size adaptation vanishes as Ct converges to a certain shape (up to scaling), which is a significant contribution beyond previous work that merely proposed the computational shortcut without justification.

2. **Empirical Validation**: The experimental results are thorough, comparing against multiple CMA-ES variants across a diverse set of benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) and dimensionality (d = 4 to 256). The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves identical optimization performance (number of function evaluations) to standard CMA-ES while significantly reducing wall-clock time (up to 20× faster for d=64).

3. **Practical Impact**: The paper addresses a genuine bottleneck in CMA-ES for large-scale problems, making it more applicable to real-world machine learning problems like hyperparameter tuning and direct policy search. The reduced storage complexity (O(d²) vs O(d²) for the matrix, but with better memory locality) is also a valuable practical consideration.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear notation, well-structured algorithms, and logical flow from theoretical justification to empirical validation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Convergence**: While Lemma 1 provides justification for the error vanishing asymptotically, the paper doesn't establish how this approximation affects the convergence rate of the algorithm in finite time. For a randomized algorithm like CMA-ES, which doesn't converge in the strict sense, it would be valuable to have a more rigorous analysis of how the approximation affects the rate at which the algorithm approaches the optimum.

2. **Lack of Real-World Application**: The paper focuses exclusively on standard benchmark functions. It would strengthen the argument for practical adoption if the authors demonstrated the approach on a real machine learning problem (e.g., hyperparameter tuning for neural networks), which is mentioned in the introduction as a primary application area.

3. **Inadequate Discussion of Hardware Constraints**: The paper notes that for d=256, matrix size exceeded the L2 cache, affecting performance. However, it doesn't thoroughly discuss how this hardware limitation might impact the practical utility of the approach, particularly for larger d where cache effects become more pronounced.

4. **Ambiguous Complexity Claim**: The paper states the complexity is "asymptotically optimal" but doesn't fully justify why O(µd²) is optimal for the problem. The authors claim "apart from the theoretical guarantees, there are several additional advantages" but don't provide a formal proof that this is the minimal possible complexity for the covariance update.

5. **Incomplete Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares against a delayed covariance matrix decomposition variant (CMA-ES-Ref) and Suttorp-CMA-ES, but doesn't compare against other recent CMA-ES variants that address similar complexity concerns, such as Loshchilov's (2015) LM-CMA which uses low-rank approximations.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The theoretical justification for the Cholesky approximation is novel and well-supported, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach. The paper addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in a widely used algorithm with a solution that is both theoretically justified and practically effective.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria well:
- **Quality**: The technical content is sound, the claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments, and the authors are careful in their evaluation.
- **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
- **Originality**: While building on prior work, the theoretical justification and extension to the full CMA-ES framework represent a novel contribution.
- **Significance**: The computational benefits are substantial for large-scale optimization, making CMA-ES applicable to problems where it was previously computationally prohibitive.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature with clear theoretical and empirical justification for a practical algorithmic improvement. The potential impact on real-world applications (particularly in machine learning where CMA-ES is commonly used for hyperparameter tuning and direct policy search) is significant.

## Minor Suggestions for Improvement

1. Clarify the claim that µ = O(log d) in the complexity analysis.
2. Include a brief discussion of how the algorithm might perform on real-world machine learning tasks (even if just as a future work direction).
3. Expand the discussion of hardware constraints and their impact on the practical utility of the approach.
4. Consider adding a comparison against more recent CMA-ES variants that address computational complexity (e.g., Loshchilov's LM-CMA).

This paper represents a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in efficient derivative-free optimization, with clear theoretical justification and practical benefits that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners.
