

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper presents a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to achieve optimal asymptotic runtime and storage complexity. The authors address a significant computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES algorithm, where the matrix square root computation dominates the runtime in high dimensions. While previous work has explored Cholesky factorization for some variants of CMA-ES (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this paper provides the first theoretical justification for why replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} (where C = AA^T) does not deteriorate performance for the standard CMA-ES. The authors prove that the introduced error vanishes as the algorithm converges to the optimum, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

The paper makes a strong case for the practical significance of this work. CMA-ES is widely used in machine learning for direct policy search in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. The proposed method achieves O(μd²) runtime complexity for covariance updates (asymptotically optimal) while maintaining the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES. Empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups (up to 20x for d=64) with increasing dimensionality, making CMA-ES applicable to larger optimization problems.

**Strengths:**
- **Novel Contribution:** The paper introduces a theoretically grounded approach to a significant computational bottleneck in a widely used algorithm. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 is particularly strong and addresses a key concern that previous attempts had not resolved.
- **Practical Impact:** The method provides immediate practical benefits without sacrificing performance, with speedups that increase with problem dimensionality - exactly when the standard CMA-ES becomes computationally prohibitive.
- **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with the algorithm presented in pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and the theoretical justification accessible.
- **Thorough Experiments:** The empirical evaluation compares against multiple CMA-ES variants across a diverse set of benchmark functions with varying dimensionality, providing convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical justification relies on the assumption that Ct converges to a certain shape, which is a simplification for a randomized algorithm (though the authors acknowledge this).
- The paper doesn't address how the method would perform on non-convex or non-smooth functions, though the benchmark functions tested cover a good range of optimization challenges.
- The experimental section could have included more detailed analysis of the error introduced by the approximation, particularly for high-dimensional problems.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a significant, well-justified improvement to a widely used algorithm with clear theoretical and practical benefits.
- The approach is novel in the context of standard CMA-ES, with a clear theoretical foundation that previous attempts lacked.
- The empirical results demonstrate a substantial speedup without compromising optimization performance.
- The work directly addresses a bottleneck that has limited the applicability of CMA-ES to high-dimensional problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The theoretical justification is somewhat limited to the convergence scenario, though the authors provide strong empirical evidence supporting their claims.
- The paper doesn't explore how this method would integrate with other recent CMA-ES variants or extensions.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this paper highly compelling. The authors have identified a fundamental limitation in a widely used algorithm and provided a theoretically grounded solution that offers significant practical benefits. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 is particularly strong and addresses a key concern that previous attempts had not resolved. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the speedup without performance degradation.

While the paper isn't perfect, the minor theoretical limitations and experimental scope are outweighed by the significance of the contribution. The proposed method could enable CMA-ES to be applied to larger optimization problems than previously possible, which has clear practical implications for machine learning applications like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization algorithms, which is highly relevant to NIPS. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and addresses a real bottleneck in a widely used algorithm. The authors have provided both theoretical justification and empirical validation for their approach, making this a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

I recommend acceptance. This paper presents a novel, theoretically grounded, and practically significant improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm, with clear implications for machine learning applications.
