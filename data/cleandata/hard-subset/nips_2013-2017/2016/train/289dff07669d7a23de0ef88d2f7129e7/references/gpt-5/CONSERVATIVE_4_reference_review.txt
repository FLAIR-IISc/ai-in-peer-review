Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = A A^T directly via a sequence of rank-one updates, achieving O(µ d^2) time per iteration and O(d^2) storage. The main technical change relative to standard CMA-ES is an approximation in cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): the usual C^{-1/2} factor is replaced by A^{-1}, which avoids the need to compute a matrix square root/eigendecomposition. A lemma argues that the rotation between the two coordinate systems vanishes asymptotically if the normalized covariance shape converges, suggesting the CSA approximation becomes accurate as the algorithm approaches stationarity. Experiments on classical noiseless benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar/Discus/Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers), dimensions up to 256, and random rotations indicate the same number of function evaluations as a reference CMA-ES but substantially reduced wall-clock time; comparisons with a delayed-decomposition baseline and an earlier non-triangular Cholesky update are included.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on CMA-ES and prior attempts to avoid cubic costs via delayed decompositions (Hansen & Ostermeier) and by updating decompositions directly (Suttorp et al., Krause & Igel). It extends the fast triangular update of Krause & Igel (2015) from µ=1 to standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates, and contrasts with limited-memory/diagonal variants (Ros & Hansen; Loshchilov) that trade modeling capacity for speed.

Strengths
- Practical contribution: an O(µ d^2) update with reduced storage for full-covariance CMA-ES is valuable when function evaluations are cheap or d is moderately large.
- Clear algorithmic description with a concrete rank-one update routine; implementation details (Shark, LAPACK) are provided.
- Empirical results show substantial wall-clock speedups without loss in the number of evaluations on several standard benchmarks, with rotation tests included.
- The asymptotic argument (Lemma 1) clarifies when the CSA approximation may be harmless, aligning with intuition that coordinate rotations stabilize near stationarity.

Weaknesses
- CSA approximation lacks rigorous guarantees. The central theoretical claim relies on convergence of the normalized covariance shape, which is not established for the full algorithm; no quantitative bound is given on the CSA error when covariance changes rapidly (e.g., on ill-conditioned, curved landscapes early in optimization).
- A serious technical mistake: the paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. For SPD C = A A^T, the eigenvalues of C are the squares of the singular values of A, not the squares of its diagonal entries. Consequently, “getting eigenvalues for free” is incorrect; computing them still requires an SVD/eigendecomposition.
- Evaluation scope is narrow: only noiseless deterministic benchmarks and success thresholds. No BBOB/COCO suite results, no noisy or constrained problems, and no sensitivity to rapid covariance changes, where the CSA approximation could be most fragile.
- Baselines and tuning: the reference CMA-ES computes the square root every iteration for the considered d, which may bias wall-clock comparisons. While CMA-ES/d is included, an ablation exploring the time–accuracy trade-off by tuning decomposition frequency to achieve best time-to-target is missing. Active CMA-ES (with negative update weights) and limited-memory variants (LM-CMA-ES) are not reported, though they are relevant practical baselines for runtime.
- Numerical stability is not analyzed. Repeated rank-one updates to A can accumulate round-off error; the paper does not quantify drift, re-factorization frequency, or conditioning safeguards.
- Presentation issues: several references are incomplete or malformed; some claims (affine invariance of the modified CSA) are asserted but not demonstrated.

Quality
Technically promising but undersupported. The fast update for A with rank-µ appears sound and practically useful. However, the key CSA approximation is justified only by an asymptotic argument under assumptions not proven for the algorithm, with no non-asymptotic error control. The incorrect eigenvalue claim is a major flaw that needs correction. Experiments demonstrate speedups but do not probe the method’s limits (noise, constraints, non-stationary phases), nor do they provide a fair Pareto of evals vs time by tuning baselines.

Clarity
Generally readable, with a helpful algorithm box. The CSA discussion and the role of rotations are explained well. However, the erroneous eigenvalue statement, incomplete references, and lack of discussion of numerical stability detract from clarity. It would help to explicitly state what invariances are preserved/broken by the approximation and to summarize known consequences.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of updating decompositions directly is known; the novelty is adapting the triangular update to full CMA-ES with rank-µ and proposing an approximate CSA to avoid polar/EVD computations. This is a useful engineering advance but not a strong theoretical novelty.

Significance
Potentially useful to practitioners of CMA-ES on moderate-to-high dimensions where function evaluations are not dominant. Within the NeurIPS audience, impact is limited by the lack of stronger theory and broader evaluation. Correcting the eigenvalue claim and expanding the empirical scope would increase significance.

Pros
- Practical speedup with full covariance, simple to implement.
- Careful connection to prior decomposition-update work.
- Rotation tests and multiple dimensions, with consistent eval counts.

Cons
- Incorrect claim about eigenvalues from Cholesky factor.
- Weak theoretical guarantees for CSA approximation; no error bounds.
- Narrow evaluation (no BBOB, noise, constraints) and limited baselines/tuning.
- No analysis of numerical stability or re-factorization needs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the eigenvalue claim; if eigenvalues are needed, clarify the additional cost or provide an efficient monitoring proxy (e.g., log-det, diagonal of C, or Ritz approximations).
- Provide non-asymptotic bounds on CSA error or, at least, empirical diagnostics showing error vs. iteration and function class, including difficult, rapidly changing covariances.
- Expand evaluation to BBOB/COCO, include noisy and constrained cases, and report time-to-target under tuned decomposition frequencies for the reference CMA-ES (and active CMA-ES). Include LM-CMA-ES as a runtime baseline where appropriate.
- Report numerical stability: frequency of re-factorization, preservation of SPD, drift in symmetry, and conditioning safeguards.
- Fix references and clarify invariance properties.

Recommendation
Promising direction with clear practical potential, but the current submission has a critical factual error and insufficient theoretical and empirical support for the CSA approximation. I lean toward rejection in its current form; with corrections and a stronger evaluation/theory package, this could be a valuable contribution.
