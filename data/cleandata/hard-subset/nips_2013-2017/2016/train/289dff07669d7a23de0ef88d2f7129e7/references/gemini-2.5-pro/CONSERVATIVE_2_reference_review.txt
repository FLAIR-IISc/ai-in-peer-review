**Summary**

This paper addresses the well-known computational bottleneck of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), which stems from the O(d^3) complexity of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix in each iteration. The authors propose a variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," that maintains and directly updates a Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, reducing the update complexity to O(µd^2). The core technical challenge is that the standard step-size adaptation mechanism requires the matrix square root inverse (C^(-1/2)), which is not trivially available from a Cholesky factor (A). The authors' main contribution is to approximate this term with the inverse of the Cholesky factor (A^(-1)) and provide a theoretical argument, based on the convergence of the covariance matrix shape, to justify why the error introduced by this approximation should be small and diminish over time. Empirical results on several benchmark functions show that the proposed method achieves a significant reduction in wall-clock time compared to the standard CMA-ES without a discernible loss in performance as measured by the number of function evaluations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Practical Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem. The computational cost of the standard CMA-ES is a significant barrier to its application in higher-dimensional spaces. The demonstrated speed-up is substantial and makes the algorithm more accessible for problems where function evaluations are cheap relative to the linear algebra.

2.  **Empirical Soundness:** The experimental evaluation is well-designed. The authors compare their method against appropriate and established baselines, including the standard reference implementation and a delayed-update strategy. The choice of benchmark functions is standard and covers a range of properties (separable, non-separable, ill-conditioned). The results are presented clearly and convincingly demonstrate the computational advantage of the proposed method.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the description of the standard CMA-ES is concise, and the proposed modification is explained logically. The authors are upfront about the approximation being made.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Theoretical Justification is Overstated:** The central weakness of the paper lies in the claim of a "theoretical justification" for the approximation. Lemma 1 and the subsequent argument are based on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix (C̄t) converges. For a stochastic optimization algorithm like CMA-ES, this is a strong assumption that is not generally guaranteed to hold. The argument is more of a plausible heuristic than a rigorous proof. It suggests that *if* the algorithm is converging stably, the error will be small. However, it does not provide any guarantees on the algorithm's behavior or convergence when this assumption is not met, which could occur during the transient phases of optimization. The loss of proven affine invariance, a hallmark of the original CMA-ES, is a significant theoretical concession that is mentioned but perhaps downplayed.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the benchmark functions are standard, they are synthetic and relatively low-dimensional (up to d=256). The introduction motivates the work with applications in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning, yet no experiments are presented in these domains. It would be more compelling to see if the approximation holds up on real-world, noisy, and potentially more complex optimization landscapes where the assumption of a smoothly converging covariance matrix shape might be violated more frequently.

3.  **Novelty is Incremental:** The idea of maintaining a factorization of the covariance matrix to speed up updates is not new, and the authors correctly cite prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015). The main novelty lies in adapting this idea to the standard (µ, λ)-CMA-ES by introducing a specific approximation for the step-size update. While this is a clever and effective engineering solution, it represents an incremental improvement on an established algorithm rather than a fundamentally new approach.

**Critique and Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a valuable engineering contribution that significantly improves the practical usability of CMA-ES for moderately high-dimensional problems. The empirical evidence for the speed-up is strong and convincing.

However, the reviewer is skeptical of the theoretical claims. The justification for the core approximation is heuristic and relies on assumptions about the algorithm's convergence behavior. While the experiments suggest this approximation works well in practice on standard benchmarks, it weakens the rigorous theoretical underpinnings of the original CMA-ES. The authors should be more precise in their language, framing the argument as a well-motivated heuristic rather than a formal "theoretical justification." The loss of affine invariance is a non-trivial trade-off for speed that should be highlighted more prominently.

In summary, the paper presents a sound and well-executed piece of empirical work that provides a useful tool for practitioners. Its primary weakness is the gap between its strong empirical results and its less-than-rigorous theoretical claims. While skeptical of the novelty and theoretical depth, the practical utility is undeniable. For the NIPS community, which values both performance and rigor, this presents a borderline case. The strength of the empirical results and the importance of the problem being solved lean in its favor.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-known and important limitation of a foundational algorithm (CMA-ES).
*   Provides a substantial and empirically validated speed-up in wall-clock time.
*   The method is simple to understand and implement.
*   The paper is clearly written and the experiments are well-conducted.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The core technical contribution relies on an approximation justified by a heuristic argument, not a rigorous proof.
*   The method sacrifices important theoretical properties of the original CMA-ES, notably its affine invariance.
*   The novelty is incremental, building directly on prior work on factorized updates.
*   The experimental validation is limited to synthetic benchmarks.
