This paper addresses a well-known and significant bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES): the computational complexity of updating the covariance matrix. The standard algorithm requires a matrix decomposition to obtain the matrix square root, a step with super-quadratic complexity (typically O(d³)) that dominates runtime in high dimensions. The authors propose a new variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, which maintains and directly updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, reducing the update complexity to an asymptotically optimal O(µd²).

The core contribution is not merely the use of a Cholesky update, which has been explored before (e.g., Suttorp et al. 2009), but the principled way in which the authors handle the crucial step-size adaptation path (p_σ). This update requires normalization by the inverse matrix square root, C^(-1/2). Previous Cholesky-based approaches could not easily accommodate this. The authors' key insight is to approximate C^(-1/2) with the inverse of their triangular Cholesky factor, A^(-1). While this introduces a rotational error, they provide a compelling theoretical argument (Lemma 1) suggesting that this error diminishes as the algorithm converges and the shape of the covariance matrix stabilizes. This is a bold and elegant solution to a difficult problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **High Novelty and Significance:** This is not an incremental improvement. The paper tackles a fundamental limitation of one of the most important algorithms in derivative-free optimization. The proposed solution is clever, combining a known technique (rank-one updates to a Cholesky factor) with a novel, theoretically-justified approximation that makes the whole scheme work for the standard CMA-ES. The resulting algorithm achieves optimal asymptotic complexity for a full covariance matrix update. This work has the potential to become the new standard for implementing CMA-ES, significantly extending its applicability to higher-dimensional problems.

*   **Bold and Principled Approach:** The decision to use an approximation for the step-size update path is a bold one. Crucially, it is not an arbitrary heuristic. The authors provide a clear mathematical justification for why this approximation is sound, linking the vanishing error to the convergence of the covariance matrix's shape. This is exactly the kind of creative and insightful thinking that pushes the field forward.

*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's value. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations) as the reference implementation while being dramatically faster in wall-clock time. The reported 20x speedup for d=64 is highly significant and aligns perfectly with the theoretical complexity analysis.

*   **Technical Elegance:** The choice of a *triangular* Cholesky factor over a general one provides several valuable side-benefits, which the authors clearly articulate: optimal O(d²) storage, efficient quadratic-time inversion, and direct access to the matrix eigenvalues on the diagonal. This demonstrates a thorough and thoughtful design.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Dependence on Convergence Assumption:** The theoretical justification hinges on the assumption that the shape of the covariance matrix converges. While this is a reasonable expectation on many problems where CMA-ES performs well, the authors rightly note that this cannot be guaranteed in the general case. The argument relies on the notion that the matrix changes "slowly," which is not formally defined. However, given the strong empirical results across several standard benchmarks, this appears to be a minor theoretical gap rather than a practical flaw.

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are performed on standard, well-behaved benchmark functions. While essential for validating the core idea, the paper would have been even stronger if it had demonstrated the performance gains on a more complex, high-dimensional machine learning task, such as direct policy search for a moderately complex reinforcement learning agent, which is mentioned as a key application area.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contribution. This work presents a significant, novel, and highly practical advance for a cornerstone algorithm in optimization and machine learning. It is a bold idea, backed by a sound theoretical argument and compelling empirical evidence. The paper is well-written, the contribution is clear, and the potential impact is high. This is precisely the type of paper that NIPS should be proud to publish, as it provides a tool that many researchers and practitioners will likely adopt and benefit from.
