

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization. The authors address a key computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES, where the matrix square root computation dominates runtime with O(d²) complexity per iteration. Previous approaches to reduce this complexity include delaying updates or restricting model complexity, but these often compromise performance. The authors extend prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) to the standard CMA-ES, providing theoretical justification for replacing the matrix square root with a Cholesky factor in the step-size adaptation, which had been a critical gap in previous approaches.

The paper's main contribution is the theoretical analysis (Lemma 1) demonstrating that the error introduced by approximating C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ (where Aₜ is the Cholesky factor) vanishes as the algorithm converges. The authors also provide an efficient O(μd²) update algorithm for the Cholesky factor (Algorithm 1) that achieves optimal asymptotic runtime and storage complexity. Empirical results on standard benchmark functions across dimensions d = 4 to 256 show the Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but significantly less wall-clock time, with speedups reaching 20× for d=64.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical justification for the approximation is rigorous, with Lemma 1 providing a solid foundation for why the error diminishes as the algorithm converges. The experimental design is comprehensive, testing multiple benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) across different dimensions, with rotation invariance tests. The authors acknowledge the algorithm's quadratic scaling with dimensionality, noting that for very large problems, low-dimensional approximations may still be necessary. The implementation details (using Shark library and LAPACK) are sufficient for reproducibility.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The background on CMA-ES (Section 2) is concise yet sufficient for readers familiar with evolutionary algorithms. The key insights about the step-size adaptation approximation are explained with mathematical precision. Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 provide clear implementation guidance. The theoretical arguments are presented logically, and the empirical results are well-organized with appropriate visualizations (Figures 1-3). Some readers unfamiliar with CMA-ES may need to consult the cited references for background, but this is expected given the specialized topic.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a specific gap in previous Cholesky-based approaches to CMA-ES. While Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) proposed Cholesky-based updates for specific CMA-ES variants, the authors here provide a theoretically justified extension to the standard CMA-ES. The key novelty lies in the theoretical analysis showing that the approximation error in step-size adaptation diminishes over time. The paper distinguishes itself clearly from prior work by addressing this specific technical challenge.

**Significance:** The work addresses a practical bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm. CMA-ES is recognized as one of the most competitive derivative-free algorithms for real-valued optimization, with applications in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. The proposed algorithm offers a substantial speedup (20× for d=64) without compromising the number of function evaluations, which is particularly valuable for large-scale problems where computation time is critical. The additional benefits (free eigenvalues, stable inversion) further enhance its practical utility.

**Strengths:**
1. Strong theoretical justification for the approximation (Lemma 1)
2. Comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions
3. Clear demonstration of significant runtime improvements (20× speedup at d=64)
4. Practical implementation details and clear algorithmic description
5. Identification of key advantages beyond speed (eigenvalues, stable inversion)

**Weaknesses:**
1. The theoretical analysis focuses only on the step-size adaptation; the paper doesn't fully analyze how the approximation might affect other aspects of the algorithm's behavior.
2. The empirical results are limited to standard benchmark functions; real-world problem performance could provide additional insight.
3. The paper doesn't explore how the approximation affects the algorithm's performance on problems where the covariance matrix changes rapidly (not just as the algorithm converges).
4. The discussion of limitations could be expanded to include potential failure cases or specific problem characteristics where the approximation might be problematic.

**Pro/Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to a widely used algorithm with practical implications for real-world applications.
- The theoretical justification is rigorous and addresses a critical gap in previous work.
- The empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups without compromising performance.
- The paper provides a complete solution to a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES.

*Con:*
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the step-size adaptation; a more comprehensive theoretical analysis would strengthen the paper.
- The empirical validation could be extended to more complex, real-world problems where CMA-ES is applied.

Overall, this paper makes a technically sound contribution with clear theoretical justification and strong empirical validation. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves optimal asymptotic complexity for covariance updates while maintaining performance, addressing a practical bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm. The work is well-suited for NIPS as it provides a significant improvement to an important algorithm with clear theoretical and practical implications. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization.
