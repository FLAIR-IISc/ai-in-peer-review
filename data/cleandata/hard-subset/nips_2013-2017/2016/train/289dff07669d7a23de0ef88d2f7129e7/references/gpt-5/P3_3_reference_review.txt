Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, and samples via x = m + σ A y with y ~ N(0,I). The key challenge is the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), which in standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2}(m_{t+1}-m_t). The authors propose to replace C^{-1/2} by A^{-1}, arguing that the missing orthogonal factor E from the RQ decomposition C^{1/2} = A E induces only a rotation that varies slowly as the algorithm converges, so the error in the CSA norm decays due to the exponential averaging. They provide a lemma establishing that the sequence of orthogonal matrices E_t converges under mild assumptions on the normalized covariance, which justifies the approximation asymptotically. The resulting algorithm achieves O(µ d^2) time per iteration (µ effective parents), optimal storage (triangular factor), and avoids expensive eigendecompositions. Experiments on standard benchmarks (with random rotations) show essentially unchanged number of function evaluations compared to a reference CMA-ES, but large wall-clock speedups (up to ~20x at d=64).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The Cholesky rank-one update (Algorithm 2) and its use for the rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates are standard and correct; the complexity claims for these updates are reasonable. The asymptotic argument for CSA (Lemma 1) is plausible but weak: it guarantees vanishing error only when the normalized covariance converges and does not provide quantitative bounds for the transient regime when C changes rapidly (which is when step-size control is most critical). A Lipschitz-type perturbation bound for ‖pσ‖ error in terms of ‖C̄_t − C̄_k‖ would materially strengthen the theory.
- A serious error: the manuscript claims that “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix” and thus eigenvalues come “for free.” This is false in general. The diagonal entries of the Cholesky factor are not equal to the square roots of the eigenvalues except in special cases (e.g., diagonal C). This claim should be removed or corrected; if eigenvalues are needed (e.g., for condition-number monitoring), they still require spectral computations or approximations.
- Invariance and CSA: The modified CSA breaks the exact use of C^{-1/2} and may affect rotational/affine invariance properties beyond the already not-fully-proven invariance of CSA. The experiments suggest little impact on eval counts, but the paper would benefit from reporting the rotation-drift analysis (currently in the supplement) in the main text, and from stress-testing scenarios where C changes quickly (e.g., badly conditioned rotated Rosenbrock early phases, ridges).
- Scope of comparison: The baseline appears to be a CMA-ES without active covariance updates (negative weights). Modern “active CMA-ES” variants often use negative weights and can accelerate learning of ill-conditioned covariances. The proposed rank-one Cholesky update currently assumes positive β in Algorithm 2; downdates (β < 0) are more delicate but feasible with cholupdate/downdate routines. Not benchmarking against an active CMA-ES weakens claims about state-of-the-art performance.
- Reproducibility: Pseudocode is provided; however, critical implementation details are missing (e.g., how often A^{-1} is computed; numerical stabilization, re-scaling, safeguarding for SPD preservation). The stated use of LAPACK is good.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Some typesetting issues (e.g., ω(d^2) vs O(d^2), odd constants in the update-delay formula) and minor typos should be fixed. Please clarify:
- Exact amortized complexity of the reference implementation used (eigendecomposition frequency/criterion).
- When and how A^{-1} is computed (triangular inversion per iteration is O(d^2) but nontrivial constants matter).
- Whether negative-weight (active) updates are supported; if not, say so explicitly.

Originality
Updating the triangular Cholesky factor for full CMA-ES (µ > 1) with a CSA approximation is novel relative to Suttorp et al. (non-triangular factor) and Krause & Igel (special cases). The critique of non-triangular factorizations introducing extra degrees of freedom and potential rotational drift is interesting and well argued. The theoretical piece (Lemma 1) is new but modest.

Significance
The work addresses a real practical bottleneck in CMA-ES: the cost of repeated decompositions. Achieving large wall-clock gains without extra function evaluations is impactful for ML applications where evaluations are cheap and d is moderate-to-large (policy search, hyperparameter tuning). However, the lack of support for active CMA-ES and the weak theoretical guarantees limit the immediate generality. Still, this is a useful contribution for the community.

Pros
- Clear, simple idea with practical value; integrates cleanly into CMA-ES.
- Solid engineering: O(µ d^2) updates, optimal storage, A^{-1} in O(d^2).
- Persuasive empirical speedups with parity in function evaluations.
- Insightful discussion of why triangular constraints avoid rotational drift.

Cons
- Incorrect claim about Cholesky diagonals equaling sqrt eigenvalues.
- CSA approximation only asymptotically justified; no quantitative bounds.
- Comparison omits active CMA-ES baselines; no treatment of downdates (β < 0).
- Limited experimental scope: no noisy/constraint settings; success rates omitted (Rosenbrock trials excluded if unsuccessful).

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the eigenvalue claim and revise the discussion of monitoring conditioning (e.g., propose inexpensive surrogates or periodic approximate spectra).
- Provide a bound on the CSA norm error due to using A^{-1}, or at least an empirical diagnostic showing the error magnitude over time across functions and dimensions.
- Extend to active CMA-ES by incorporating numerically stable chol-downdates and compare against active baselines.
- Report success rates, not just medians, and include noise/robustness experiments.
- Move the rotation-drift analysis from the supplement to the main paper.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6/10) if the eigenvalue claim is corrected and limitations are acknowledged; otherwise borderline. The paper offers a practically valuable speedup with minimal downside in function evaluations, and the approach is likely to be adopted, but it needs a more careful and accurate presentation.
