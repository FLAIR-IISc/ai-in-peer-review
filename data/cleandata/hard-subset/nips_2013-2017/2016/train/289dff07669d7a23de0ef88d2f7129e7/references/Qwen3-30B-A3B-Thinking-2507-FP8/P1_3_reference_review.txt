

# Peer Review: "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary

This paper proposes an efficient variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root computation in the standard CMA-ES with a Cholesky factorization. The authors claim their approach achieves optimal asymptotic runtime complexity O(μd²) while maintaining the same performance as the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations. The paper builds on prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who proposed similar Cholesky-based updates for restricted variants of CMA-ES, but extends it to the standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification for why the approximation doesn't deteriorate performance.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach. Lemma 1 establishes that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor vanishes as the algorithm converges, which is a significant contribution. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) across standard benchmark functions and dimensions up to 256. The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but with substantially reduced runtime (up to 20x faster at d=64).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section clearly explains the CMA-ES algorithm and the motivation for the proposed changes. The pseudocode for Algorithms 1 and 2 is clear and well-structured, making the method easy to understand and implement. The experimental setup is well-described, including details about the benchmark functions, dimensions, and evaluation metrics.

**Originality:** While the general idea of using Cholesky decomposition in evolutionary algorithms is not entirely novel, this paper makes a significant contribution by providing a theoretical justification for why the approximation doesn't harm performance in the standard CMA-ES. This is a key advancement over prior work that either didn't provide such justification or applied to restricted variants of CMA-ES.

**Significance:** CMA-ES is widely used in optimization, particularly for reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. Improving its computational efficiency has direct practical value. The paper demonstrates that the Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains the same optimization quality while significantly reducing runtime, which is particularly valuable for high-dimensional problems where the standard CMA-ES becomes computationally prohibitive.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Justification:** While Lemma 1 provides a useful asymptotic argument, the paper doesn't fully address whether the convergence of Ct is guaranteed in the randomized CMA-ES algorithm. The reference to Beyer (2014) about the expectation of Ct converging is helpful but doesn't fully resolve the theoretical concerns about the approximation error in the randomized setting.

**Numerical Stability:** The paper claims that using the Cholesky factor provides "numerically stable computation," but this claim isn't thoroughly supported with empirical evidence or analysis. It would be valuable to see a comparison of numerical stability between the two approaches.

**Limitations of the Approach:** The paper acknowledges that the approach scales quadratically with dimensionality, which becomes problematic for very large d. However, it doesn't discuss how the Cholesky-CMA-ES compares to more recent low-dimensional approximation methods (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) in terms of both performance and computational efficiency for extremely high-dimensional problems.

## Arguments for Acceptance

* Provides a theoretically justified approach to significantly improving the computational efficiency of CMA-ES
* Demonstrates substantial runtime improvements (up to 20x faster) with no loss in optimization quality
* Well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility
* Addresses a practical problem of significant importance to the optimization community

## Arguments Against Acceptance

* The theoretical justification for the approximation isn't fully rigorous for the randomized CMA-ES algorithm
* Numerical stability claims aren't thoroughly supported
* Doesn't fully address how the approach compares to more recent low-dimensional approximation methods for extremely high-dimensional problems

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The runtime improvements are substantial and could have significant practical impact for users of CMA-ES. While there are some theoretical and empirical gaps that could be addressed in future work, these don't undermine the core contribution.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental computational problem in optimization that has direct applications in machine learning (reinforcement learning, hyperparameter tuning). The quality of the work meets NIPS standards, with solid theoretical analysis and comprehensive experiments.

I recommend acceptance, with the understanding that the authors might consider addressing the numerical stability claims more thoroughly in the final version.

## Final Recommendation

**Accept** - This is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in optimization algorithms with practical significance for machine learning applications. The theoretical justification, empirical validation, and clear presentation make it a valuable addition to the literature.
