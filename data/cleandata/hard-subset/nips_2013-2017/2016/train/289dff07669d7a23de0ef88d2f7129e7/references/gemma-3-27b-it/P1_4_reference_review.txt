## Peer Review for "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm, dubbed “Cholesky-CMA-ES”, that replaces the standard matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization update. The core idea is to maintain and update only the Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, leveraging its efficient update properties to reduce computational complexity from O(d<sup>2</sup>) to O(µd<sup>2</sup>) where µ is typically much smaller than d. The authors provide a theoretical justification for why this approximation doesn’t degrade performance, linking it to the convergence behavior of the algorithm and the stability of the coordinate transformations.  Empirical results demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance to the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations, but significantly faster wall-clock time, particularly in higher dimensions.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon existing work aiming to reduce the computational cost of CMA-ES, such as delayed updates [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001] and restricted covariance matrix models [Poland & Zell, 2001]. It directly addresses the limitations of previous attempts to use Cholesky factorization [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015] by providing a theoretical justification for its application to the full CMA-ES framework and extending the approach beyond the (1+1) or multi-objective variants. The work also connects to the broader field of natural gradient optimization and the importance of maintaining covariance information for efficient search.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a solid theoretical argument for the validity of the proposed approximation, based on the convergence properties of the algorithm and the behavior of coordinate rotations. The Lemma 1 is a valuable contribution.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The reduction in computational complexity from O(d<sup>2</sup>) to O(µd<sup>2</sup>) is significant, especially for high-dimensional problems.  The experiments clearly demonstrate this speedup in runtime.
* **Memory Efficiency:** Updating only the Cholesky factor also reduces the memory footprint of the algorithm, which is an important consideration for large-scale problems.
* **Preservation of Performance:** The empirical results convincingly show that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance to the standard CMA-ES in terms of the number of function evaluations required.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings. The explanation of why previous Cholesky approaches were limited is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are comprehensive in terms of benchmark functions and dimensions, the comparison to other fast covariance update schemes (beyond Suttorp et al.) is limited. A more thorough comparison to, for example, LM-CMA [Loshchilov, 2015], would be valuable.
* **Dependency on µ:** The theoretical benefit relies on µ being significantly smaller than d. The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the impact of different µ values on performance and stability. 
* **Practical Considerations for Very High Dimensions:** The authors acknowledge a performance drop at d=256 due to cache limitations. This suggests that further work might be needed to optimize the implementation for extremely high-dimensional problems.
* **Complexity of Update Rule:** Algorithm 2 (rankOneUpdate) appears relatively complex, potentially hindering practical implementation and optimization. A discussion of the implementation challenges and optimizations would be helpful.
* **Impact on Step-Size Adaptation:** The discussion around the impact of replacing `st` with `ŝt` on the step-size adaptation (Equation 5) feels somewhat hand-wavy. A more detailed analysis, potentially with supporting experiments, could strengthen this argument.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. It presents a complete and well-considered piece of work.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and the explanations are generally easy to follow.

**Originality:** The approach of applying Cholesky factorization updates directly to the standard CMA-ES, combined with the theoretical justification provided, is a novel contribution.  The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work in this area.

**Significance:** This work has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of CMA-ES, particularly for high-dimensional optimization problems.  The reduced computational cost could enable the application of CMA-ES to a wider range of problems and larger-scale datasets.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a theoretically sound and practically effective modification to a widely used optimization algorithm.
* Demonstrates a significant speedup in runtime without sacrificing performance.
* Addresses a key bottleneck in CMA-ES: the computational cost of covariance matrix updates.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparison to other fast covariance update methods.
* Dependency on the choice of µ and its impact on performance isn’t thoroughly investigated.
* Potential implementation complexities and performance limitations in extremely high dimensions.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The work is a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (specifically a more comprehensive comparison to other methods and a more detailed discussion of the impact of µ) would further strengthen the paper. I also encourage the authors to discuss potential optimizations for very high-dimensional problems.
