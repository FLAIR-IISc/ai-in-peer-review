

# Peer Review: "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the computationally expensive matrix square root operation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce runtime complexity. The authors address a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES where the O(d³) cost of computing the matrix square root dominates optimization time for high-dimensional problems. While previous work (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) explored Cholesky factorizations for specialized CMA-ES variants, this paper provides a theoretically grounded approach for the standard CMA-ES, with a key contribution being Lemma 1 that justifies why the approximation error vanishes as the algorithm converges.

The paper builds on foundational CMA-ES work by Hansen and Ostermeier (2001) and extends recent efforts to reduce computational complexity (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015; Ros and Hansen, 2008). It distinguishes itself by providing theoretical justification for the approximation, which previous approaches lacked.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper's strongest aspect is Lemma 1, which rigorously demonstrates that the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ (where Aₜ is the Cholesky factor) diminishes as the algorithm converges. This provides a crucial theoretical foundation missing from prior Cholesky-based approaches.

2. **Empirical Validation**: The experimental results (Section 4) are thorough and well-designed. The authors compare against multiple CMA-ES variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) across a range of benchmark functions and dimensions (d = 4 to 256). The key finding—that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations but significantly less wall-clock time—holds consistently across experiments.

3. **Practical Impact**: The proposed method achieves asymptotically optimal time complexity (O(µd²) vs. O(d³) for standard CMA-ES) while maintaining the same storage complexity (O(d²)). The additional benefit of obtaining eigenvalues for free (via diagonal elements of the Cholesky factor) is a valuable practical advantage for monitoring problem conditioning.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with Algorithm 1 providing a clear implementation of the proposed method. The background section effectively contextualizes the problem within the CMA-ES framework.

## Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1. **Theoretical Limitations**: While Lemma 1 provides a compelling argument for convergence, it assumes Ct converges to a certain shape. CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm, and Ct may not converge in practice. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide a more rigorous analysis of the error in non-convergent scenarios or quantify the error bound during the optimization process.

2. **Implementation Details**: The paper states that "we added our algorithm to the open-source machine learning library Shark" but provides insufficient details about the implementation specifics. For reproducibility, the authors should include key implementation choices (e.g., how they handle numerical stability in the Cholesky update, specific parameter settings for Shark).

3. **Comparison with Low-Rank Methods**: The paper briefly mentions that for extremely large dimensions, low-dimensional approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) might be necessary. However, it fails to provide a comparative analysis of Cholesky-CMA-ES versus these methods, which would be valuable for practitioners deciding between approaches.

4. **Cache Effects**: The paper notes that for d = 256, the matrix size exceeded the L2 cache, affecting performance. This suggests that cache efficiency becomes a limiting factor for very large dimensions, which the authors don't address in their complexity analysis. A more nuanced discussion of practical scalability would strengthen the paper.

5. **Complexity Claim Clarification**: The paper claims "asymptotically optimal time complexity" but the complexity O(µd²) is technically correct only if µ = O(1). Since µ typically scales as O(log d), the actual complexity is O(d² log d), which is still better than O(d³) but not quite "asymptotically optimal" in the strictest sense.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient variant of CMA-ES. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 is particularly strong, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the runtime benefits without sacrificing performance.

The main weaknesses are the limited discussion of theoretical limitations in non-convergent cases and insufficient implementation details for reproducibility. However, these are relatively minor compared to the paper's substantial contribution.

**Pro-acceptance arguments**: 
- Provides a theoretically justified method for reducing CMA-ES complexity
- Demonstrates clear empirical benefits in runtime without performance degradation
- Addresses a well-known bottleneck in a widely used algorithm
- Offers practical benefits (eigenvalues for free, better cache behavior)

**Con-acceptance arguments**:
- The theoretical analysis assumes convergence, which is not guaranteed in practice
- Insufficient implementation details for full reproducibility
- Limited discussion of trade-offs with low-rank approximation methods

## Final Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contribution is significant, well-supported by both theory and experiments, and addresses a meaningful practical problem in optimization. The minor weaknesses identified are easily addressable in the camera-ready version. This work would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, particularly for researchers working on optimization algorithms and their applications in machine learning.

The paper advances the state of the art in derivative-free optimization by providing a method that scales better with dimensionality while maintaining the same performance characteristics as the standard CMA-ES. For practitioners dealing with high-dimensional optimization problems, this approach offers a compelling alternative that could significantly reduce computational costs.
