

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary
This paper proposes a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) called Cholesky-CMA-ES that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to achieve better computational complexity. The authors argue that while previous approaches have attempted to use Cholesky factors in CMA-ES, they lack theoretical justification for why this doesn't deteriorate performance. The key contribution is a theoretical argument showing that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor vanishes as the algorithm converges. The proposed method maintains the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but achieves significantly better runtime performance, especially as dimensionality increases.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) is well-constructed and shows that the error introduced by using the Cholesky factor instead of the matrix square root becomes negligible as the algorithm converges. This is a significant contribution as previous approaches lacked such justification. The authors provide a detailed algorithm (Algorithm 1) and a rankOneUpdate procedure (Algorithm 2) for maintaining the Cholesky factor. Their empirical results (Figure 1-3) convincingly show that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but with significantly better runtime performance (up to 20x faster for d=64). The experimental design is thorough, using standard benchmark functions with varying dimensionality and testing rotation invariance.

The paper carefully acknowledges limitations, noting that their method's theoretical justification relies on the assumption that the covariance matrix converges (which is not strictly true for the randomized CMA-ES algorithm), and they properly address the cache effects for very large dimensions (d=256).

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The background section (Section 2) provides a concise yet complete explanation of CMA-ES, making it accessible to readers who may not be familiar with the algorithm. The motivation for the proposed method is clearly articulated in the introduction, and the theoretical justification is presented with sufficient mathematical rigor. The algorithm descriptions are precise and well-documented. The experimental results are presented clearly with appropriate visualizations that effectively communicate the performance gains.

## Originality
The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the CMA-ES literature by providing the first theoretically justified approach to using Cholesky factorization in the standard CMA-ES. While prior work (Suttorp et al., Krause and Igel) has explored Cholesky factorization for CMA-ES variants, they did not provide theoretical justification for why this doesn't deteriorate performance. The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous approaches, particularly in Section 3 where they explain why their approach differs from non-triangular Cholesky factorization methods. The paper is sufficiently different from recent NIPS proceedings, as the most relevant prior work (Suttorp et al., Krause and Igel) appeared in conferences other than NIPS.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. CMA-ES is widely used for derivative-free optimization, particularly in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning, where function evaluations can be expensive. By reducing the computational complexity from O(d^3) to O(d^2) for the covariance update, this method makes CMA-ES more practical for higher-dimensional problems. The empirical results show clear runtime improvements without sacrificing the number of function evaluations, which is critical for practitioners. The paper also highlights that their method provides the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free (through the diagonal elements of the Cholesky factor), which is useful for monitoring optimization progress.

## Strengths
1. Theoretical justification for the approximation is rigorous and well-motivated.
2. The proposed algorithm achieves optimal asymptotic complexity (O(µd^2)) with no loss in performance.
3. Empirical results demonstrate clear runtime improvements with no increase in function evaluations.
4. The paper is well-written with clear organization and thorough experimental validation.
5. The method provides additional useful information (covariance eigenvalues) without extra computational cost.

## Weaknesses
1. The theoretical justification relies on the convergence of the covariance matrix, which is not strictly true for the randomized CMA-ES algorithm. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, a more rigorous argument for the randomized case would strengthen the paper.
2. The paper doesn't address how the method performs on extremely large-scale problems (d > 256) where even O(d^2) might become problematic.
3. The comparison to recent CMA-ES variants like Loshchilov's LM-CMA (which aims to reduce complexity) would have strengthened the significance claim.
4. The claim of "optimal" complexity could be more precisely justified by comparing to other approaches that might achieve similar complexity.

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the CMA-ES literature. The theoretical justification for using Cholesky factorization instead of matrix square root is novel and well-supported, and the empirical results demonstrate clear practical benefits. The paper addresses an important computational bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm, with significant implications for practitioners.

The paper meets all the NIPS review criteria with high quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the paper's core contribution. The authors have provided a well-justified, technically sound improvement to an important algorithm that will be valuable to the optimization and machine learning communities.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. It represents a significant improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm that will likely be adopted by practitioners and researchers in the field. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization.
