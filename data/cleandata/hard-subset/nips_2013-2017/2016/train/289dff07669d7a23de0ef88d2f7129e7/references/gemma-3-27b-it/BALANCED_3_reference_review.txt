This paper presents a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, dubbed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aiming to reduce runtime and memory complexity by utilizing a Cholesky decomposition instead of the traditional matrix square root. The core idea is to update the Cholesky factor directly, avoiding expensive recomputations. The authors provide a theoretical justification for the approach and empirical evidence demonstrating its effectiveness, particularly in higher dimensions.

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in evolutionary computation, specifically CMA-ES [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001], and addresses a known bottleneck: the O(d^2) complexity of covariance matrix adaptation. Prior attempts to address this, such as delayed updates [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001], restricted covariance structures [Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008], and Cholesky factor updates [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015], are well-referenced and contextualized.  The contribution lies in extending the Cholesky update approach to the standard CMA-ES and providing a theoretical basis for its convergence, addressing a limitation of prior work. The connection to information geometry [Akimoto et al., 2012] is also appropriately highlighted.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly identifies the computational bottleneck in CMA-ES and proposes a targeted solution.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a solid theoretical argument, including Lemma 1, supporting the validity of their approach and addressing potential concerns about introducing errors via the approximation. The connection to the convergence of coordinate rotations is insightful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the speedup achieved by the Cholesky-CMA-ES, especially in higher dimensions. The comparison with existing CMA-ES variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) is well-structured and informative.
* **Asymptotic Optimality:** Achieving an optimal runtime and storage complexity is a significant accomplishment.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The authors acknowledge limitations (scaling with d, L2 cache size) and discuss practical implications.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The algorithm is clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Core Technique:**  Updating the Cholesky factor directly isn’t entirely novel [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015].  The primary novelty lies in its application to the *standard* CMA-ES and the accompanying theoretical analysis justifying its use. This nuance could be made more explicit in the introduction.
* **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While the paper provides a theoretical justification, a more detailed empirical analysis of the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor would be beneficial.  Specifically, quantifying the impact of this approximation on the accuracy and robustness of the algorithm. The supplementary material reference is helpful, but integrating some key findings into the main text would strengthen the argument.
* **Benchmarking Scope:** While a diverse set of benchmark functions are used, the experiments could be strengthened by including more real-world applications, particularly those where CMA-ES is commonly employed (e.g., reinforcement learning, hyperparameter tuning).
* **Algorithm 2 details:** Algorithm 2 (rankOneUpdate) lacks some details that would aid implementation and understanding.  Specifically, it’s unclear how 'β' and 'v' relate to the broader CMA-ES update equations. More explanation would be useful.
* **Discussion of Rotation Invariance:** While the paper discusses rotation invariance, the impact of the approximation on this property could be further explored. Does the approximation lead to any loss of rotational invariance in practice?




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant practical limitation of CMA-ES – its computational cost.
* The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a significant speedup, particularly in high dimensions, without sacrificing performance (number of function evaluations).
* The theoretical justification provides confidence in the validity of the approach.
* The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm.
* The contribution is relevant to a wide range of applications that utilize CMA-ES.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The core technique of updating the Cholesky factor isn’t entirely novel.
* A more detailed error analysis and exploration of the impact on rotation invariance would strengthen the paper.
* The benchmark suite could be expanded to include more real-world applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of evolutionary computation. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a significant practical improvement to the standard CMA-ES algorithm, and the theoretical justification and empirical validation are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.  Specifically, I recommend including a more detailed discussion of the error introduced by the approximation, expanding the benchmark suite with real-world applications, and adding more details to Algorithm 2.
