Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T instead of explicitly updating C and recomputing its square root/eigendecomposition. The main challenge is that standard CMA-ES’s step-size adaptation (CSA) uses C^{-1/2}, which is not directly available from A without an O(d^3) polar decomposition. The authors approximate CSA by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}, and argue the resulting error in the pσ norm vanishes as the algorithm stabilizes, leveraging an RQ-decomposition continuity argument (Lemma 1). This yields O(µ d^2) time per iteration (with typical µ = O(log d)) and optimal O(d^2) storage while retaining essentially the same number of function evaluations as the reference CMA-ES. Experiments on standard noiseless benchmarks up to d = 256 show large wall-clock speedups (e.g., ~20x at d = 64) with matching evaluation counts, compared to a state-of-the-art reference with delayed decompositions, a d-step delay variant, and a non-triangular Cholesky-update baseline (Suttorp et al.).

This work builds on and extends: (i) Cholesky/downdate ideas for ES (Suttorp et al., 2009), (ii) the efficient rank-one update for (1+1)-CMA-ES (Krause & Igel, 2015), and contrasts with restricted/low-memory variants (e.g., diagonal CMA-ES; LM-CMA, Loshchilov 2014/2015). The novelty is a principled extension of triangular Cholesky updates to standard multi-parent CMA-ES with an analysis justifying the CSA approximation.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful idea: replace repeated eigendecompositions with efficient triangular Cholesky rank-one updates, and approximate CSA in a way that is theoretically motivated and empirically validated.
- Strong practical significance: order-of-magnitude wall-clock speedups without increasing evaluation counts on relevant benchmarks; optimal asymptotic storage; cheap triangular inversion.
- Theoretical insight: the RQ-based argument (Lemma 1) to explain why the CSA approximation should become accurate as the covariance stabilizes is both elegant and convincing as a rationale.
- Good baselines: includes state-of-the-art CMA-ES with standard delay strategy, a longer-delay variant, and a Cholesky-update baseline in the non-triangular parameterization.
- Open-source implementation in Shark; use of LAPACK.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Critical technical inaccuracy: the paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. They are square roots of the Cholesky pivots (Schur complements), not eigenvalues. What you get “for free” is det(C) via product of diagonal entries, not eigenvalues. Please correct this.
- Theoretical guarantees are qualitative. The CSA approximation is justified by continuity and exponential forgetting, but no quantitative bound is provided on the deviation in pσ or on step-size dynamics, especially during transient phases when C changes rapidly (e.g., early iterations, ill-conditioned landscapes, strong non-stationarities). A bound—even under simplifying assumptions—would strengthen the contribution.
- Experimental scope: only noiseless, unconstrained single-objective functions. No tests with noise, constraints, or multi-modal rugged functions where covariance can change quickly; no large-scale policy search or hyperparameter tuning tasks despite motivating applications. The evaluation would be more compelling with a BBOB-like suite and at least one ML task.
- Baseline fairness/details: the reference CMA-ES’s decomposition delay defaults to 1 for tested settings; while you also include a d-step delay variant, it would help to tune delay schedules for best wall-clock trade-offs. Also, comparisons against LM-CMA (and other limited-memory CMA-ES variants) would contextualize the benefit when d grows further.
- Invariance: CSA is known to break affine invariance; here you further approximate it. You mention measuring rotation effects (Et) in the supplement; including a concise quantitative summary in the main paper (e.g., distribution of rotation angles, impact on σ dynamics) would improve confidence.

Quality
- Generally sound and careful, with a neat algorithmic derivation and plausible theoretical rationale. The incorrect claim about eigenvalues must be fixed. Additional numerical-stability discussion for repeated rank-one updates would be useful (e.g., conditioning, downdates, accumulation of round-off).

Clarity
- The paper is well written overall and the core idea is clearly communicated. Some references are malformed/incomplete; fix citations. Clarify Algorithm 2’s relation to standard cholupdate/downdate and constraints on β. Explicitly state computational costs for each step, including the A^{-1} computation. Consider adding a brief empirical plot that compares the approximate vs exact CSA norm (using polar decomposition on small d) to visualize the approximation error over time.

Originality
- The extension from (1+1)-CMA-ES Cholesky updates to standard CMA-ES with a theoretically motivated CSA approximation is a meaningful and nontrivial step. While the components (Cholesky updates, delayed decompositions) are known, the combination and justification are novel.

Significance
- High practical significance for practitioners who use full CMA-ES on medium-to-large d and care about wall-clock time. The approach should be adopted in libraries. The theoretical insight will likely influence future work on fast, invariant updates.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report the deviation between exact CSA (via polar decomposition) and the proposed approximation on small-to-moderate d across different phases (early vs late) and functions?
- How does the method behave under noise? Any instability in σ or covariance adaptation?
- Can you compare to LM-CMA and discuss crossover points where full covariance becomes infeasible?
- Please correct the eigenvalue claim; perhaps emphasize log-det and conditioning proxies you can compute from A.

Pros
- Substantial speedups with minimal loss, strong practical impact
- Clean algorithmic contribution; optimal asymptotic time/storage for full covariance
- Sensible theoretical justification; easy to adopt

Cons
- Incorrect statement about eigenvalues; needs correction
- No quantitative bounds on CSA approximation error
- Limited experimental scope; missing noisy/ML tasks and LM-CMA comparisons
