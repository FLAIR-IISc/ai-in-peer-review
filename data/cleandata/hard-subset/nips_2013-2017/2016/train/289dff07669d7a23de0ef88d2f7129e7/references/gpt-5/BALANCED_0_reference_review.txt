Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, rather than C itself or its symmetric square root. This enables O(µ d^2) update time and O(d^2) storage, which are argued to be asymptotically optimal for full-covariance adaptation. The main technical challenge lies in step-size adaptation (CSA): standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2} in the evolution path pσ; replacing it by A^{-1} introduces a changing orthogonal rotation, E_t, that is expensive to compute exactly. The authors propose an approximation that omits E_t and provide a justification that the induced error becomes negligible as the covariance “shape” stabilizes, via a simple continuity argument on the RQ decomposition (Lemma 1). They then show empirically that the approximation does not alter the number of function evaluations, while significantly reducing wall-clock time.

This work builds on prior efforts to speed up CMA-ES: (i) delaying covariance factorization [Hansen and Ostermeier; Hansen 2015], (ii) restricting model complexity (diagonal or low-rank/separable variants) [Ros and Hansen; Loshchilov; Sun et al.], and (iii) updating a (non-triangular) factor directly [Suttorp et al.]. It also leverages a rank-one Cholesky update technique previously developed for special CMA-ES cases [Krause and Igel 2015], extending it to the standard rank-µ CMA-ES. Compared to (i), it avoids stale decompositions; compared to (ii), it preserves full covariance expressivity; compared to (iii), it uses a triangular factorization with better storage, faster inversion, and direct access to eigenvalues.

Strengths
- Practical impact: Large and consistent wall-clock speedups (up to 20× at d=64) with essentially unchanged evaluation efficiency on standard benchmarks, including rotated variants.
- Solid engineering: An explicit O(µ d^2) rank-one update for triangular Cholesky factors, avoiding recomputation and offering numerically favorable properties (quadratic-time inversion; eigenvalues via diagonals).
- Sensible complexity claim: For full dense covariance adaptation, the stated time and storage complexities are hard to beat.
- Clear articulation of where the approximation departs from standard CMA-ES (CSA path) and why it should be acceptable; rotation-invariance is empirically probed.
- Reproducibility: Implementation in Shark and comparison to well-established baselines (CMA-ES with/without delays, Suttorp-style factor updates).

Weaknesses
- Theory is light: The justification for omitting the orthogonal factor in pσ relies on convergence of the covariance “shape.” For stochastic CMA-ES on non-convex functions, convergence is not guaranteed; no quantitative bound is given for the CSA error or its effect on σ dynamics.
- Missing robustness tests: No experiments with noise, constraints, or heavy ill-conditioning at higher dimensions; limited discussion of numerical stability under long runs (e.g., whether occasional re-factorization is needed to curb drift).
- Limited baselines: No comparison to modern limited-memory/full-performance variants (e.g., LM-CMA-ES) on wall-clock time vs. evaluations trade-off, nor to restart strategies commonly used in practice.
- Scope of empirical validation: Benchmarks up to d=256 and mostly classical functions; the paper would benefit from showing utility on ML-relevant tasks (RL policy search, hyperparameter tuning) where CMA-ES is frequently applied.
- Some clarity issues: The exposition of Algorithm 2 could use a brief derivation or citation to standard rank-one Cholesky update formulas; the references list has formatting glitches.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The Cholesky rank-one update is standard and appears correctly adapted to the CMA-ES update structure. The CSA approximation is plausible and empirically validated but lacks rigorous error analysis. Numerical stability considerations (e.g., conditioning, periodic re-orthogonalization) are not deeply addressed.
- Empirical support: Results convincingly show identical evaluations and substantial runtime gains on the chosen set. More diverse scenarios (noise, higher dimensions, real tasks) would strengthen claims about general applicability.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, with a good algorithmic summary and motivation. The lemma and its role are clear. However, some readers may want more detail on Algorithm 2’s derivation, and more explicit reporting of the “rotation drift” measurements mentioned for the supplement.

Originality
- Extends previous Cholesky-update ideas to standard rank-µ CMA-ES with a careful treatment of the CSA issue. The triangular-factor focus and associated practical benefits (storage, inversion, eigenvalues) are well-motivated. While incremental, the contribution is nontrivial and addresses a long-standing bottleneck.

Significance
- High practical relevance: Full-covariance CMA-ES remains a strong baseline in black-box optimization; making it much faster without hurting evaluation counts is valuable. Likely to be adopted by practitioners and library maintainers. Theoretical advances are modest.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong practical speedups with preserved optimization performance.
- Clean, implementable algorithm with optimal storage/runtime for full covariance.
- Sensible and well-argued treatment of the CSA approximation, supported empirically.
- Relevance to NeurIPS community through applications in RL and ML hyperparameter tuning.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees on the CSA approximation; no error bounds.
- Empirical evaluation could be broader (noise, real ML tasks; comparison to LM-CMA-ES).
- Numerical stability/drift over very long runs not deeply analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a quantitative analysis or bound on the discrepancy between the exact and approximate pσ update, or at least an empirical ablation where occasional polar decompositions “reset” E_t to measure error accumulation.
- Add experiments on noisy and constrained benchmarks, and at higher d (≥512), including stress tests of conditioning.
- Compare wall-clock and evaluation efficiency to LM-CMA-ES and other scalable variants to contextualize the trade-off between full and limited memory.
- Report the rotation-drift measurements in the main text and correlate them with σ adaptation behavior.
- Discuss numerical stability and whether periodic re-factorization from C is ever needed.
- Include a small real-world application (e.g., RL policy search) to demonstrate practical gains.

Recommendation: Accept (weak accept)
This is a solid, practitioner-relevant contribution that removes a key bottleneck in a widely used algorithm with a simple and well-justified change, offering significant speedups without sacrificing solution quality. While the theory is not deep and the experiments could be broader, the work meets NeurIPS standards for quality and significance in black-box optimization.
