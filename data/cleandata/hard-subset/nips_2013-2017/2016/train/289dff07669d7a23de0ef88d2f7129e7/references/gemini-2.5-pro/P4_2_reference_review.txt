This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a prominent algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity per iteration dominated by the O(d^3) cost of decomposing the d-dimensional covariance matrix `C`. The authors propose a new variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that reduces this complexity to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for an algorithm that adapts a full covariance matrix.

The central idea is to maintain and directly update the lower triangular Cholesky factor `A` of the covariance matrix (`C = AA^T`), rather than updating `C` and re-decomposing it in every step. This approach builds on previous work (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) that explored similar ideas, often for simplified CMA-ES variants. The main challenge in applying this to the standard CMA-ES lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the term `C^(-1/2)`, the inverse matrix square root. This term cannot be directly replaced by the inverse Cholesky factor `A^(-1)` without introducing an error, as `C^(1/2)` and `A` are related by an orthogonal matrix `E` (from the RQ-decomposition `C^(1/2) = AE`). The core contribution of this paper is to propose using `A^(-1)` as an approximation for `C^(-1/2)` and to provide a theoretical justification for why this approximation is sound. The authors prove in Lemma 1 that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the rotation matrices `E_t` also converge, implying that the rotational error introduced by the approximation diminishes over time due to the exponential weighting in the CSA path. The paper validates this new algorithm empirically on several benchmark functions, showing that it requires a similar number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed algorithm is well-defined, and the update rules are clearly specified. The main claim—that the approximation in the step-size update does not significantly harm performance—is supported by both a novel theoretical argument (Lemma 1) and comprehensive empirical evidence. The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method against relevant baselines (the standard reference implementation and a delayed-update variant) across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions. The results robustly demonstrate the claimed speed-up in wall-clock time while maintaining the convergence properties in terms of function evaluations.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It clearly motivates the problem of computational complexity in CMA-ES, reviews prior work, and pinpoints the specific technical challenge (the `C^(-1/2)` term in the CSA update) that has hindered previous attempts at creating a fast Cholesky-based update for the standard algorithm. The theoretical justification is presented logically, and the pseudocode (Algorithm 1 and 2) makes the proposed method unambiguous and aids reproducibility. The figures effectively visualize the experimental results, clearly separating the analysis of function evaluations from wall-clock time.

3.  **Originality & Significance:** While the idea of updating a matrix factorization is not new, the originality of this work lies in providing the first theoretically justified method for applying this speed-up to the complete, state-of-the-art CMA-ES algorithm. Previous works were either restricted to simpler variants (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES) or lacked a formal justification for approximations. The significance of this contribution is high. CMA-ES is a foundational algorithm in black-box optimization with numerous applications in machine learning, including policy search and hyperparameter tuning. By drastically reducing the per-iteration runtime without compromising optimization quality, this work makes CMA-ES practical for higher-dimensional problems or scenarios where the objective function is computationally inexpensive. The Cholesky-CMA-ES is a strong candidate to become the new standard implementation of the algorithm, representing a clear and demonstrable advancement of the state of the art.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Quality:** The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges (`C̄_t → C̄`). The authors correctly note that as CMA-ES is a stochastic algorithm, this convergence is typically analyzed in expectation. The argument that the rotation matrices `E_t` become stable is compelling, but the analysis does not formally bound the error introduced by the approximation or analyze its effect on the algorithm's overall convergence guarantees. Such an analysis would further strengthen the paper, though it may be beyond the scope of a single conference contribution.

2.  **Significance:** The paper's contribution is primarily focused on improving computational efficiency. While the speed-up is significant, the algorithm's complexity remains quadratic in the problem dimension `d`. Therefore, it does not address the challenge of applying CMA-ES to very-high-dimensional problems (e.g., `d` in the thousands), where limited-memory or structured covariance matrix approaches are required. The authors are transparent about this limitation in their conclusion.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and well-executed contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. It solves a long-standing efficiency problem in one of the field's most important algorithms, providing a faster implementation that is backed by both a sound theoretical argument and strong empirical results. The work is clear, technically solid, and highly significant for both researchers and practitioners who use CMA-ES.
