Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C (with C = A A^T) via a sequence of rank-one updates. This replaces the standard practice of explicitly updating C and periodically computing its square root or eigen-decomposition, which can dominate runtime. The key difficulty is the CSA step-size update, which in standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2}. The authors approximate this by using A^{-1} (i.e., solving with the Cholesky factor), thereby omitting a per-iteration orthogonal rotation that would be required to exactly mimic C^{-1/2}. They justify this approximation by showing that the sequence of rotations converges to identity as the normalized covariance stabilizes (Lemma 1), implying the error in the CSA norm vanishes over time due to exponential weighting. They provide an O(µ d^2) rank-one update algorithm for A, argue optimal storage (triangular factors), and show empirical speedups on standard noiseless benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) up to d=256, with essentially unchanged function evaluation counts relative to a strong CMA-ES baseline.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on prior Cholesky-based updates for special CMA-ES variants (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES; Suttorp et al., Krause & Igel) and on constrained/low-rank parameterizations (diagonal, LM-CMA, NES variants). It contrasts with the common “delay eigen-decomposition” trick used in reference CMA-ES implementations. The information-geometric perspective (Akimoto et al.) motivates the covariance updates used. Compared to non-triangular factor updates (Suttorp et al.), the paper emphasizes the uniqueness and smoothness of triangular mapping and its implications for the CSA approximation.

Strengths
- Practical impact: Large wall-clock speedups (up to ~20x at d=64) with essentially unchanged number of function evaluations on standard benchmarks. Straightforward to integrate and released in an open-source library.
- Solid engineering: Clear O(µ d^2) rank-one update for triangular factors; avoids cubic decompositions; excellent memory footprint (d(d+1)/2).
- Theoretical rationale: The CSA approximation is justified by a convergence argument (Lemma 1), with the error vanishing as C stabilizes; this aligns with empirical parity in evaluations.
- Invariance and robustness: Experiments include random rotations to probe rotational invariance in practice, and several ill-conditioned test functions (including Rosenbrock and Diffpowers).
- Relevance: Keeping full covariance without sacrificing evaluations is meaningful for non-separable, ill-conditioned problems where diagonal/limited-memory variants degrade.

Weaknesses
- Technical claim error: The paper states “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix… hence eigenvalues of C for free.” This is incorrect in general. The eigenvalues of a triangular matrix are its diagonal entries, but the eigenvalues of C = A A^T are the squares of the singular values of A, not the squares of A’s diagonal entries. You do get det(C) and log-det cheaply (via diag(A)), and triangular solves, but not eigenvalues “for free.”
- Theory scope: Lemma 1 assumes convergence of the normalized covariance sequence; CMA-ES is stochastic and may not satisfy this in transient or non-stationary phases (e.g., rugged, noisy, or constrained problems). The argument is asymptotic and qualitative; a non-asymptotic bound quantifying the CSA bias due to missing rotations would strengthen the case.
- Empirical scope: Benchmarks are noiseless, unconstrained, and primarily single-funnel. No results on noise, constraints, multi-modality, or RL/hyperparameter tuning tasks (where CMA-ES is often used). The exclusion of non-converged Rosenbrock runs could bias runtime comparisons.
- Baselines and scheduling: The reference implementation’s decomposition frequency equals one for the tested d, which is especially unfavorable. While CMA-ES/d is included, more diverse schedules or optimized implementations (e.g., amortized O(d^2) with caching) would better contextualize speedups.
- Clarity and polish: Some references are malformed; Algorithm 2 could better explain variables and numerical stability; clarify that CSA can be implemented via triangular solves rather than forming A^{-1} explicitly.

Quality
- The covariance update is technically sound and aligns with prior work on rank-one Cholesky updates. The CSA approximation is reasonable and empirically validated, but theoretical guarantees are qualitative and asymptotic. The incorrect claim about eigenvalues should be corrected. Numerical stability of repeated rank-one updates (e.g., downdating issues, conditioning safeguards) should be discussed.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but with notable issues: incorrect eigenvalue statement; typos and reference formatting; missing details on constants, solver choices (triangular solves vs explicit inverse), and the exact decomposition schedule in baselines. Consider including the rotation-mismatch plots (Et) in the main paper.

Originality
- Extends known Cholesky-update ideas to standard CMA-ES with CSA, with a specific approximation and justification. While not a radical conceptual departure, the combination and its practical realization for mainstream CMA-ES is novel and useful.

Significance
- High practical significance for practitioners using full-covariance CMA-ES on medium-to-high d. The method can broadly reduce wall-clock time without sacrificing solution quality. Complementary to limited-memory approaches.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical speedups with preserved evaluation counts.
- Practical contribution likely to be adopted.
- Reasonable theoretical justification for the core approximation.
- Clean complexity and storage improvements.

Cons against acceptance
- Incorrect technical claim about eigenvalues.
- Limited empirical scope (noisy, constrained, or real ML tasks absent).
- Asymptotic theory; lacks quantitative error bounds in CSA approximation.
- Baseline choices could be more favorable/tuned.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the eigenvalue claim; emphasize log-det, determinant, and triangular solves as “for free.”
- Clarify that p_sigma update should use triangular solves rather than forming A^{-1}.
- Provide non-asymptotic analysis or empirical ablation showing CSA bias vs rotation magnitude and its impact on progress rates.
- Add experiments with noise, constraints, and a real ML task (e.g., direct policy search) to demonstrate robustness.
- Improve baseline scheduling and report amortized complexities; include more tuned reference implementations.
- Fix references and polish the exposition; move rotation-mismatch diagnostics into the main text.

Recommendation
Weak Accept. The contribution is practical, impactful, and well-motivated, with convincing empirical evidence on standard benchmarks. However, the paper must correct the eigenvalue claim and would benefit from expanded empirical scope and clearer methodological details.
