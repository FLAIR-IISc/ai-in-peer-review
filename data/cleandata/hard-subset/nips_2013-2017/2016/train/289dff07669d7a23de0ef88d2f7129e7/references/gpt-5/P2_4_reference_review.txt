Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, rather than updating C and recomputing its square root or eigendecomposition. This yields per-iteration time complexity O(µ d^2) and memory O(d^2), with µ the number of selected parents (typically O(log d)), which the authors argue is asymptotically optimal for full-covariance adaptation. The main technical challenge is the step-size adaptation (CSA), which in standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2}. Replacing C^{-1/2} by A^{-1} implicitly introduces a changing rotation; the authors justify ignoring this rotation by a continuity argument (Lemma 1) suggesting the induced error vanishes as C stabilizes. Experiments on standard black-box benchmarks (with random rotations) show essentially identical function evaluation counts but notably reduced wall-clock times (up to ~20x at d=64) compared to a reference CMA-ES and a version with delayed decompositions; the proposed method also outperforms a non-triangular Cholesky approach (Suttorp et al., 2009).

The paper builds on prior work that (i) updates factorizations instead of C (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), (ii) reduces covariance modeling complexity (e.g., diagonal CMA-ES, LM-CMA), and (iii) established CSA and invariance properties (Hansen & Ostermeier; Hansen, 2015; Akimoto et al.). The novelty is extending fast triangular updates to the standard multi-parent CMA-ES while providing a justification for the CSA approximation and demonstrating practical gains without deteriorating evaluation efficiency.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The factorized update of C via a sequence of rank-one updates to A is standard linear algebra and appears correctly adapted to rank-1 and rank-µ CMA-ES updates. The CSA approximation is the riskiest part; the presented lemma (continuity of the RQ decomposition) is mathematically straightforward and supports the intuition that the rotation error diminishes when C changes slowly. However, there is no quantitative bound on the induced CSA error as a function of c1, cµ, or the rate of change of C, nor analysis of early iterations when C changes rapidly.
- Empirical support: The study covers common noiseless benchmarks across d up to 256 with random rotations, reports function evaluations and wall-clock time, and includes 100 trials per setting. The results support the main claims: same evaluations, significantly lower runtime. Two concerns: (1) On Rosenbrock, non-converged trials were excluded, which biases the evaluation; success rates or time-to-target with failures should be reported. (2) The reference baseline computes decompositions every iteration for the chosen d; while this mirrors default settings, stronger baselines (e.g., active CMA-ES, more aggressive delay strategies) could further contextualize gains. Noise robustness is not evaluated.
- Reproducibility: The algorithmic description is clear (pseudocode provided) and the implementation is said to be in Shark with LAPACK. Hardware/runtime environment, BLAS threading, and exact parameter settings are not fully specified in the main text; some of this may be in the supplement.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The explanation of why CSA needs C^{-1/2} and what is lost by using A^{-1} is clear, and Lemma 1 is easy to follow.
- The algorithms are explicit and the rank-one update is given.
- Some references are malformed; please correct citation formatting. Minor editorial issues exist.
- It would help to state more explicitly when the triangular update supports only positive rank updates (no negative weights) and whether active CMA-ES is out of scope.

Originality
- The idea of updating a decomposition is known. The contribution here is to make triangular Cholesky updates practical for standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 and to justify an approximation for CSA so that C need not be recomputed. This is an incremental but nontrivial engineering and algorithmic contribution, with a modest theoretical justification that is, to my knowledge, new in this context.

Significance
- For users of full-covariance CMA-ES, the reported speed-ups without loss in evaluation efficiency are practically important, especially as d grows. The method preserves the desirable invariance properties empirically, and delivers additional benefits (lower memory, quick inversion, eigenvalues “for free”).
- The work does not change the asymptotic dependence on d (still quadratic), so it does not address truly large-scale settings where limited-memory methods dominate; nevertheless, for the wide range where full covariance is still feasible, this is a meaningful advance.

Pros
- Clear, simple modification with strong practical impact; drop-in replacement for full-covariance CMA-ES.
- Solid empirical evidence of substantial runtime gains with unchanged evaluation counts on standard benchmarks and random rotations.
- Reasonable theoretical argument for the CSA approximation; careful discussion of invariance.
- Implementation details (Shark, LAPACK) suggest attention to efficiency; triangular form yields useful byproducts (eigenvalues, A^{-1} cheaply).

Cons
- Theoretical justification for CSA approximation is qualitative; no bounds on error or conditions beyond convergence. Early-iteration behavior is unquantified.
- Excluding non-converged Rosenbrock runs biases evaluation; success rates should be reported.
- Baselines could be stronger: active CMA-ES (negative weights), more modern default settings, and noise settings are missing. It is unclear whether the proposed updates extend to active CMA-ES.
- Some claims (e.g., “optimal memory complexity”) are true only in big-O; existing implementations often do not store both C and C^{1/2}.
- Reference formatting issues.

Questions and suggestions
- Please report success rates (or penalize failures) on Rosenbrock; include time-to-target with a fixed budget.
- Evaluate robustness with noise and on constrained or multi-modal settings.
- Clarify whether and how the triangular updates support active CMA-ES (negative rank-µ weights). If not supported, discuss the impact and potential extensions.
- Provide quantitative diagnostics on the rotation discrepancy and its decay (beyond the supplement), especially during early iterations.
- Detail hardware/BLAS settings and provide code/scripts to reproduce wall-clock results.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a solid, well-motivated, and practically valuable contribution with clear empirical benefits and a plausible theoretical justification. While the theory could be strengthened and the experimental evaluation broadened (success rates, noise, active CMA-ES), the work advances the state of the art for full-covariance CMA-ES. I lean towards acceptance.
