Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, rather than explicitly updating C and recomputing its (eigen-)decomposition. The main technical challenge is that standard CMA-ES uses the symmetric square root C−1/2 in cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA); simply substituting A−1 introduces a changing rotation E^T (from the RQ/polar decomposition) at each iteration. The authors argue, via a continuity lemma, that as the covariance “shape” stabilizes, the rotations converge, and thus the discrepancy between using C−1/2 and A−1 vanishes in norm due to exponential forgetting in CSA. They provide a rank-one Cholesky-update algorithm and show the overall update costs O(µ d^2), which they argue is asymptotically optimal when adapting a full covariance. Empirically, on standard (rotated) benchmarks up to d = 256, Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the original in number of function evaluations and is significantly faster in wall-clock time (e.g., ~20x at d = 64), outperforming both a reference implementation with delayed decompositions and a non-triangular Cholesky update scheme (Suttorp et al., 2009). The work builds on rank-one Cholesky update ideas (Krause & Igel, 2015) and addresses the long-standing bottleneck of repeatedly computing square roots/eigendecompositions in CMA-ES; it also contrasts with diagonal/limited-memory approximations (Ros & Hansen, 2008; Loshchilov, 2014/2015) that may harm performance on non-separable problems.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation of the fast update (Algorithm 2) that turns the rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates into Cholesky updates is plausible and consistent with prior work. The key approximation is in CSA: replacing C−1/2 with A−1. The theoretical justification (Lemma 1 + Eq. 6) is qualitative: if the covariance shape converges, the successive rotations induced by the RQ decomposition converge to identity, and the norm error in pσ diminishes because CSA forgets older steps exponentially. This is a reasonable argument, but it falls short of a quantitative bound on the deviation in σ or a statement about finite-time behavior when C changes quickly (e.g., early in runs or on bent valleys like Rosenbrock).
- Empirical support: The experiments suggest near-identical sample complexity and strong wall-time gains across several benchmarks and random rotations. This is encouraging and addresses a practical concern for CMA-ES. However, some aspects need strengthening:
  - Success rates (especially on Rosenbrock, where non-converged trials were excluded) should be reported to avoid bias.
  - The “same number of function evaluations” claim should be quantified (means, medians, dispersion) across problems and dimensions.
  - Fairness/optimization of baselines should be clarified (e.g., parameters for delay frequency; whether triangular solves were used vs explicit inverses).
- Numerical issues: The paper claims the approach enables “numerically stable computation of the inverse” and provides eigenvalues “for free.” The latter is incorrect: the diagonal entries of a Cholesky factor are not the square roots of the eigenvalues of C in general; they are the square roots of the Cholesky pivots. This should be corrected. Also, in practice one should avoid forming A−1 explicitly and instead use triangular solves to compute A−1v, which are faster and more stable.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The exposition of the CSA approximation is clear, and the algorithms are specified.
- Some notation and reference issues: there are typos in the update frequency expression for the reference implementation; several references have incomplete metadata. Clarify whether the delay parameter is the default recommended setting in Hansen (2015). Provide precise complexity statements (constant factors, whether triangular solves are used).
- Please correct the claim about eigenvalues from the Cholesky diagonal. Consider explicitly citing standard cholupdate/downdate references and discussing numerical stability and any conditioning safeguards (e.g., variance floor, reconditioning).

Originality
- The use of a triangular Cholesky factor in “full” CMA-ES with a principled handling of CSA (even as an approximation) appears novel relative to prior Cholesky updates that were either non-triangular or limited to special CMA-ES variants ((1+1)-CMA-ES, MO-CMA-ES).
- Extending quadratic-time rank-one Cholesky updates to handle the full rank-µ update of standard CMA-ES in O(µ d^2) is a useful engineering/theoretical contribution.
- The theoretical piece (Lemma 1) is simple but apt; it formalizes why the rotation error should decay. Stronger, quantitative guarantees would further elevate originality.

Significance
- From a practical standpoint, the runtime bottleneck in CMA-ES is a critical issue for medium-to-large d on cheap-to-evaluate functions. A demonstrated 10–20x speedup with no loss in evaluations is impactful for the community using CMA-ES for reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning.
- The approach preserves full covariance modeling, avoiding the performance pitfalls of diagonal/limited-memory variants on non-separable, ill-conditioned functions.
- If released, an efficient implementation in a popular library (Shark) could see adoption.

Pros
- Addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES without sacrificing evaluation efficiency.
- Clear algorithmic contribution with optimal storage and asymptotically optimal time for full covariance updates.
- Sensible theoretical argument that the CSA approximation’s error diminishes.
- Solid empirical speedups across standard, rotated benchmarks.

Cons
- Theoretical justification is qualitative; no finite-time error bounds or guarantees under non-stationary C.
- Missing/unclear reporting: success rates (especially on Rosenbrock), dispersion of evaluation counts, sensitivity to rotation in early iterations.
- A factual error about Cholesky diagonals vs eigenvalues; limited discussion of numerical stability and best practices (triangular solves vs inversion).
- Limited scope of experiments: no noisy objectives, constraints, or comparisons against strong low-memory full-CMA variants beyond Suttorp; LM-CMA or sep-CMA could be added to contextualize trade-offs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative comparisons of evaluation counts (means/medians/IQR) and success rates; include statistical tests where appropriate.
- Add ablations showing how the CSA approximation behaves early in optimization (e.g., track ∥pσ∥, σ trajectories, and the measured rotation EtE^T_{t−1} over time; the supplement hints at this—summarize in main paper).
- Consider experiments on noisy functions to test robustness of step-size adaptation under the approximation.
- Replace explicit inversion with triangular solves; discuss numerical conditioning and any regularization steps.
- Correct the eigenvalue claim and clarify the baseline delay settings. If feasible, compare to LM-CMA or sep-CMA to highlight the regime where full covariance with Cholesky is preferred.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and practically meaningful contribution: a well-motivated, implementable modification to CMA-ES that yields substantial runtime savings while preserving performance. With clarification/corrections and slightly stronger empirical reporting (and, ideally, a modest quantitative bound for the CSA approximation), this would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
