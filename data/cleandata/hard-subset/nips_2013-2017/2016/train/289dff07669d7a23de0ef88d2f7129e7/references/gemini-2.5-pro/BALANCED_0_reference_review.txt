### **Summary**

This paper addresses the primary computational bottleneck of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), namely the O(d^3) complexity of decomposing the covariance matrix C in each iteration. The authors propose a new variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES", which maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix (where C = AA^T) directly, reducing the per-iteration complexity to an asymptotically optimal O(µd^2).

The main challenge in using a Cholesky factor instead of the true matrix square root (C^1/2) lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires C^-1/2 to normalize the search steps. Replacing C^-1/2 with A^-1 introduces a time-varying, unknown rotation. The core contribution of this work is to provide a theoretical justification for this approximation. The authors argue, via Lemma 1, that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges during optimization, the error introduced by this rotational difference diminishes. Specifically, the rotation matrix relating the Cholesky factor to the matrix square root stabilizes, causing the inter-iteration rotational change (E_t * E_{t-1}^T) to approach the identity matrix.

The paper demonstrates empirically that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same convergence rate in terms of function evaluations as the standard, state-of-the-art CMA-ES implementation, while being significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, particularly in higher dimensions.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization with wide applications in machine learning, from reinforcement learning to hyperparameter tuning. Its quadratic (or cubic) complexity has been a major barrier to its application in higher-dimensional problems. This paper provides a practical and well-justified solution that significantly lowers this barrier, making CMA-ES accessible to a broader range of problems. The proposed method achieves the best possible asymptotic time and memory complexity for a full-covariance update.

2.  **Strong Originality and Novelty:** While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor directly is not entirely new (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this paper's primary novelty lies in its elegant theoretical treatment of the step-size adaptation approximation. Previous works either did not use the standard CSA rule or could not justify the approximation. The argument presented in Lemma 1, linking the convergence of the covariance matrix shape to the vanishing error of the approximation, is a key insight that provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithm. This is a non-trivial contribution that advances our understanding of how to make CMA-ES more efficient without sacrificing its core principles.

3.  **Excellent Quality and Technical Soundness:** The work is technically strong. The derivation of the algorithm is clear, and the theoretical justification, while based on an assumption of convergence, is well-reasoned. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against appropriate and strong baselines, including the standard implementation with delayed updates (CMA-ES-Ref) and a prior Cholesky-based approach. The use of standard benchmark functions and rotationally invariant tests demonstrates the robustness of the algorithm. The results clearly show that the number of function evaluations remains unchanged while the wall-clock time is dramatically reduced, precisely as claimed.

4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is clearly established, the technical challenge is explained precisely, and the proposed solution is presented in a logical and understandable manner. The inclusion of pseudo-code (Algorithms 1 and 2) makes the implementation details clear. The figures are informative and effectively support the paper's conclusions.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Strength of Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix C̄t converges. The authors correctly note that for a stochastic algorithm, this is not guaranteed, though the expectation may converge in simplified models. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the implications when C̄t does not converge smoothly, for example, during highly non-stationary phases of the search. An empirical validation could be included (perhaps in the supplement) showing that the term ||E_t * E_{t-1}^T - I|| is indeed small and diminishes over the course of typical optimization runs on the benchmark problems. This would provide strong empirical backing for the theoretical argument.

2.  **Minor Technical Inaccuracy:** The paper states in Section 3 that "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix". This is incorrect. This property holds for the Cholesky factorization of a *diagonal* matrix, but not for a general symmetric positive definite matrix. While the eigenvalues of C can be computed from A, it is not this simple. This statement should be corrected for technical accuracy. The claim that the algorithm "provides the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix without additional costs" is therefore misleading.

3.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are well-executed but could be more impactful for the NIPS audience by including a demonstration on a machine learning task. While benchmark functions are essential for validation, showing the significant speed-up on a practical problem like direct policy search for a small RL agent or hyperparameter tuning for a model like an SVM or small neural network would make the contribution's practical relevance immediately apparent and more compelling.

4.  **Comparison with Suttorp-CMA-ES:** The paper includes Suttorp-CMA-ES as a baseline, but the discussion of its results is minimal. The text argues that the proposed triangular factorization is superior due to unique decomposition, optimal storage, and stability. However, the empirical results (Fig. 1) show its performance in terms of function evaluations is identical to the proposed method. A more direct comparison would be beneficial. For example, does the non-uniqueness of the decomposition in Suttorp et al. lead to worse performance on certain problem types not tested here? A clearer empirical demonstration of the claimed advantages over this specific baseline would strengthen the paper.

### **Decision**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant and practical improvement to a classic and widely-used algorithm. The work is novel, technically sound, and clearly presented. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in a final version. The contribution is of high value to both the optimization and machine learning communities. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Solves a well-known, important computational problem for a key algorithm (CMA-ES).
*   Provides the first solid theoretical justification for an efficient Cholesky-based update that approximates the standard CMA-ES step-size rule.
*   Achieves optimal asymptotic time and memory complexity for a full-covariance method.
*   Empirical results are comprehensive and clearly demonstrate a substantial speed-up without performance degradation.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The central theoretical justification rests on a convergence assumption that may not always hold.
*   A minor technical claim about eigenvalues is incorrect.
*   Experiments, while solid, lack a direct application to a machine learning problem.
