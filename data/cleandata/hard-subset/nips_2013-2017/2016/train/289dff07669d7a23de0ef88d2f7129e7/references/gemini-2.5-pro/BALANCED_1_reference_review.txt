### Summary

This paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a powerful black-box optimization algorithm widely used in machine learning. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity of at least O(d^3) per iteration due to the need to compute the square root of the d x d covariance matrix C. The authors propose a new variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that avoids this costly operation by directly maintaining and updating the triangular Cholesky factor A (where C = AA^T). This reduces the per-iteration complexity to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for a full covariance matrix update.

A key challenge in this approach is the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the term C^{-1/2} for normalization. This term is not readily available from the Cholesky factor A. The central contribution of the paper is to propose an approximation using A^{-1} instead of C^{-1/2}. The authors provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) that this approximation is sound, arguing that the rotational error introduced by this substitution vanishes as the algorithm converges and the shape of the covariance matrix stabilizes. Extensive experiments on standard benchmark functions demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations) as the standard CMA-ES, but with a dramatic reduction in wall-clock time, especially for higher dimensions.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and practically important limitation of CMA-ES. By significantly reducing the computational overhead per iteration, this work makes CMA-ES feasible for higher-dimensional problems where the function evaluation is cheap. This is highly relevant to the NeurIPS community, where CMA-ES is used for tasks like direct policy search in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization. The proposed method represents a substantial practical improvement to a cornerstone algorithm.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and elegant. The core idea of replacing the C^{-1/2} term with A^{-1} in the CSA update is well-motivated, and the accompanying theoretical justification in Lemma 1 is clever. While it relies on the assumption of convergence of the normalized covariance matrix, this is a reasonable assumption in the context of a converging optimization algorithm, and the argument that the rotational error vanishes over time is convincing. The empirical results robustly support this theoretical reasoning.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. It provides the necessary background on CMA-ES, clearly articulates the problem, details the proposed solution (Cholesky-CMA-ES), and presents a compelling justification for its key approximation. The inclusion of pseudocode (Algorithms 1 and 2) further enhances the clarity and reproducibility of the work.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare their method against appropriate baselines (standard CMA-ES with different update delays) on a suite of well-established benchmark functions across a range of dimensions. The results clearly demonstrate the primary claims: equivalent performance in function evaluations and a significant speed-up in wall-clock time. The logarithmic scale plots effectively illustrate the growing advantage of Cholesky-CMA-ES as dimensionality increases.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Conditional Theoretical Guarantee:** The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 is insightful but rests on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix `C̄_t` converges. The authors rightly acknowledge that convergence cannot be guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm. While the intuition is strong and empirically supported, it would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss the implications if `C̄_t` does not converge but, for example, oscillates. Does the error in the step-size adaptation remain bounded? This would add a layer of robustness to the theoretical claims.

2.  **Discussion of Suttorp-CMA-ES:** The experiments include a comparison with Suttorp-CMA-ES, which also uses a Cholesky factor update but does not enforce a triangular structure. The plots suggest that its runtime performance is very similar to the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES. However, the results section does not explicitly discuss this comparison. The paper argues that using a unique triangular factorization has advantages (optimal storage, free eigenvalues, stable inversion). It would be beneficial to add a few sentences to the results section discussing the empirical comparison with Suttorp-CMA-ES and reinforcing why the triangular approach is preferable despite similar wall-clock times in these experiments.

3.  **Elaboration on Cache Effects:** The authors note an interesting performance effect for d=256 where the matrix size exceeds the L2 cache. This is a valuable practical observation. A brief elaboration on this could be useful. Does this imply that the practical speed-up might be slightly sub-quadratic for very large `d` on certain architectures, or is this simply a hardware-dependent constant factor effect? Clarifying this would add nuance to the complexity analysis.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that presents a simple, effective, and well-justified solution to a long-standing problem in a widely used optimization algorithm. The contribution is both practical and theoretically sound. The work is of high quality, clearly presented, and its significance to practitioners and researchers using CMA-ES is undeniable. The identified weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with small revisions to the text.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Solves a significant, practical computational bottleneck in a major optimization algorithm (CMA-ES).
*   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is technically sound, with a novel and well-reasoned justification for its key approximation.
*   Achieves asymptotically optimal time and storage complexity for a full-rank update.
*   Empirical results are thorough and convincingly demonstrate a substantial speed-up without loss of optimization performance.
*   The paper is very well-written and clear.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical guarantee for the approximation relies on a convergence assumption that may not hold rigorously in all cases.
*   The core idea of updating a matrix factorization is not entirely new, though the application and justification here are novel and crucial.

In my view, the arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. This paper is a valuable contribution to the field and is well-suited for publication at NeurIPS.

**Decision:** Accept.
