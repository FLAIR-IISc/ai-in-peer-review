

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary
This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce runtime complexity from O(d^3) to O(µd^2) while maintaining theoretical guarantees on performance. The authors argue that their approach is theoretically justified for non-separable ill-conditioned problems and provides empirical evidence of runtime improvements.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, which is well-known to be a significant limitation for high-dimensional optimization. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 is well-motivated and provides insight into why the approximation error diminishes as the algorithm converges. The experimental results demonstrate clear runtime improvements with no loss in function evaluations, which is a meaningful contribution to practitioners working with high-dimensional problems.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Presentation
The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies that undermine professionalism. The citation style is inconsistent throughout (e.g., "Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001" vs "Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001," with unnecessary commas before years). In the introduction, there's a typo: "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" should be "the Conference." The reference list contains numerous formatting errors, including inconsistent use of "et al." and strange characters like "\Q" and "\E" in citations.

### Technical Clarity
The paper's core contribution hinges on Lemma 1, but the proof is poorly presented. The authors state "Let C̄ = ĀE, the RQ-decomposition of C̄" without defining C̄, which appears to be a typo for C̄. The explanation of how the error vanishes is vague - they state "the error depends only on the rotation of coordinate systems" but fail to quantify this error or provide bounds on how quickly it diminishes. The connection between the rotation matrix convergence and the step-size adaptation error remains unclear.

The paper claims "the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a better, asymptotically optimal time complexity of O(µd^2)" but doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis. The time complexity claim appears to assume a naive matrix multiplication implementation, but the paper doesn't justify this assumption or discuss how more efficient multiplication algorithms would affect the complexity.

### Experimental Details
The paper states "For d = 64 the Cholesky-CMA-ES was already 20 times faster than the CMA-ES-Ref" but doesn't provide the actual runtime numbers to verify this claim. The paper also mentions "For d = 256 the matrix size exceeded the L2 cache, which affected the performance of the Cholesky-CMA-ES and Suttorp-CMA-ES" but doesn't explain why this would disproportionately affect the Cholesky-based methods compared to the standard CMA-ES, which also requires matrix operations of similar complexity.

The experimental setup refers to "CMA-ES-Ref" as the reference implementation but doesn't clearly define what "Ref" stands for. The paper states they "added our algorithm to the open-source machine learning library Shark" but doesn't explain why Shark was chosen over other libraries with CMA-ES implementations.

### Theoretical Justification
The paper claims "we can expect this error to be small and to decrease over time as the algorithm converges to the optimum" but provides no theoretical bounds on the error magnitude or convergence rate. The proof of Lemma 1 relies on the convergence of Ct, which is not guaranteed for randomized algorithms like CMA-ES, and the authors don't address this gap.

The paper's claim that "the Cholesky-CMA-ES needs the same amount of objective function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES" is supported only by empirical results without theoretical justification, which is problematic since the step-size adaptation has been modified.

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in CMA-ES and provides empirical evidence of runtime improvements, the numerous technical inconsistencies, lack of theoretical rigor in error analysis, and formatting issues significantly undermine the contribution. The paper's theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported, and the experimental results lack sufficient detail to verify the claimed speedups.

The authors make an important observation about the computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, but their theoretical justification is incomplete, and the paper contains numerous formatting and technical presentation errors that make it difficult to assess the validity of their claims. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues before being considered for publication.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient theoretical justification and multiple technical inconsistencies that prevent a clear assessment of the paper's contribution. The authors should provide a more rigorous error analysis, clarify the theoretical foundation, and address all formatting inconsistencies before resubmission.
