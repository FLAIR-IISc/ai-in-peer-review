The paper proposes a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, which utilizes the Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root for covariance matrix adaptation. The core idea is to leverage existing fast Cholesky update routines to reduce computational complexity, specifically targeting the O(d<sup>2</sup>) bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES. The authors provide a theoretical justification, based on Lemma 1, suggesting minimal performance degradation due to the approximation introduced by avoiding correction factors in the step-size adaptation. Empirical results demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance in terms of function evaluations, but with significantly reduced runtime, particularly in higher dimensions.

**Strengths:**

* **Addressing a Significant Bottleneck:** The paper directly tackles a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, making it more applicable to high-dimensional problems.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The inclusion of Lemma 1 and the accompanying explanation provide a reasonable argument for why the approximation introduced by the Cholesky factorization does not drastically impact performance. While not a rigorous proof of equivalence, it offers valuable insight.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, particularly the scaling with dimensionality (Figure 2), strongly support the claim of reduced runtime without sacrificing performance (in terms of function evaluations). The comparison against CMA-ES-Ref and other variants adds credibility.
* **Clear Presentation of Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 provide a concise and understandable description of the proposed method.
* **Practical Considerations:** Recognizing the limitations related to L2 cache sizes at high dimensionality is a positive sign of careful consideration.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lemma 1's Assumptions:** The convergence assumption within Lemma 1 ("Ct converges to a certain shape") is a strong one and lacks sufficient justification within the paper's context. The authors acknowledge it’s a simplification, but its influence on the theoretical validity is downplayed. It's not clear how robust the approach is when this assumption *doesn’t* hold.
* **Approximation in Step-Size Adaptation:** While the authors acknowledge the error introduced by omitting the correction factor (equation 5), a more thorough analysis of the error bound, even empirically, would strengthen the argument. A discussion of scenarios where this approximation might be problematic would also be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Suttorp-CMA-ES:** The comparison to Suttorp et al. [2009] feels somewhat cursory. A more in-depth analysis of the differences in performance and memory usage would be insightful.  The claim that the Cholesky-CMA-ES avoids numerical instabilities related to separate updates of A<sup>-1</sup> and A is not convincingly demonstrated.
* **Experimental Detail and Statistical Significance:** The results are presented visually, but lack statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests or ANOVA).  While the trends are clear, rigorous statistical analysis is needed to support the claims of significant speedup. The number of trials (100) seems reasonable but should be explicitly stated in the caption of each figure.
* **Minor Formatting Issues:** The citation style is inconsistent.  Akimoto et al. is cited with different formats throughout the paper (e.g., “Akimoto et al\.,? \Q2012\E” and “Akimoto et al\.,? \Q2014\E”).  The notation used throughout is occasionally clumsy (e.g., use of “C̄”).
* **Lack of Discussion on Condition Number:** While the paper mentions ill-conditioned problems, there's no discussion on how the Cholesky-CMA-ES handles them *specifically* or whether the approximation affects its performance in such cases.
* **Overly Strong Conclusion:** The concluding statement proclaiming the Cholesky-CMA-ES as an ideal solution for "scaling up CMA-ES to large optimization problems" is overly assertive, given the remaining limitations and the quadratic scaling with dimensionality.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a critical performance bottleneck in a widely used algorithm.
* Provides a plausible theoretical justification for the proposed approximation.
* Empirically demonstrates significant runtime improvements.
* Algorithm is clearly described and readily implementable.

**Con:**

* Theoretical justification relies on a potentially strong assumption.
* Error analysis of the step-size adaptation approximation is lacking.
* Limited comparison to related work, particularly Suttorp et al.
* Lack of statistical significance testing in the experimental results.
* Minor formatting and notational inconsistencies.
* Overstated conclusion regarding scalability.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the Cholesky-CMA-ES presents a valuable contribution by addressing a key performance limitation of the standard CMA-ES, the paper suffers from several minor but persistent flaws. The theoretical arguments, while insightful, are not fully rigorous. The empirical evaluation, while promising, lacks statistical rigor. These weaknesses, combined with minor formatting issues and an overly strong conclusion, lead me to recommend **rejection**. The idea is strong, and the results are encouraging, but the paper needs substantial refinement before it meets the high standards of NIPS. I suggest the authors address the points raised above, especially the error analysis and statistical validation, and present a more nuanced conclusion.
