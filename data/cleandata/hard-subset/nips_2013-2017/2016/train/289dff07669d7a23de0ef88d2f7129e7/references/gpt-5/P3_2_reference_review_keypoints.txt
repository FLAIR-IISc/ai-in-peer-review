- Proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES that updates lower-triangular Cholesky factor A directly via O(d^2) rank-one updates, avoiding full covariance recomputation.
- Achieves per-iteration time O(µ d^2) and storage O(d^2) while preserving rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates.
- Replaces CSA's C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} (triangular solve) and argues rotational error vanishes as normalized covariance shape converges (Lemma 1 + RQ continuity).
- Empirically matches baseline evaluation counts on rotated benchmarks up to d=256 and yields large wall-clock speedups (e.g., ~20× at d=64).
- Integrates implementation into Shark using LAPACK and outperforms delayed-decomposition and non-triangular Cholesky baselines.
- Builds on prior work (Suttorp et al. 2009; Krause & Igel 2015) but extends efficient Cholesky updates to full rank-µ CMA-ES with a CSA approximation.
- Correctly positions itself versus alternatives (delayed updates, restricted models, LM-CMA, NES) by targeting non-separable ill-conditioned problems needing full covariance.
- Rank-one Cholesky update strategy is standard and sound; Algorithm 2 resembles cholupdate but needs discussion of numerical stability.
- Theoretical CSA argument is reasonable but qualitative; lacks quantitative error bounds and may fail during transients when C changes rapidly.
- Important technical error: claim that Cholesky diagonal entries are square roots of eigenvalues is incorrect and must be fixed (log-det relation is correct).
- Complexity claims are largely correct: avoiding O(d^3) eigendecompositions yields O(µ d^2) per iteration.
- Writing is generally clear with good motivation and algorithmic description, but several references are malformed and notation (ω(d^2)) is unconventional.
- Emphasize that A^{-1} in CSA should be implemented as a triangular solve, not by forming A^{-1} explicitly.
- Request more detail/citation for Algorithm 2 and explicit discussion of its numerical properties.
- Originality: incremental relative to prior work but nontrivial and practically meaningful for standard CMA-ES users.
- Significance: large practical speedups while preserving evaluation efficiency; likely to be adopted and valuable to practitioners.
- Empirical strengths: rotated benchmarks, multiple baselines, wall-clock and evaluation counts, up to d=256, 100 trials.
- Empirical weaknesses: excluding unsuccessful Rosenbrock runs biases results—need success rates and ERT; CSA diagnostics and rotational discrepancy measures are missing.
- Request discussion/experiments on active CMA-ES (negative weights) and feasibility/stability of downdates.
- Ask for reporting of numerical stability over long runs (periodic re-factorization, jitter, SPD breakdowns).
- Suggest adding LM-CMA comparison to contextualize trade-offs at larger d and providing wall-clock breakdowns by component.
- Pros for acceptance: practical speedups, clean algorithm, plausible theory, implementation-ready.
- Cons against acceptance: incorrect eigenvalue claim, qualitative CSA justification without bounds, experimental methodology issues and missing stability/active-CMA discussion.
- Questions for authors: support for active CMA-ES and stable downdates; observed σ drift or loss of invariance and CSA diagnostics; re-factorization/jitter practices and SPD failures; correction of eigenvalue claim and clarification of available spectral info; ablation with occasional exact CSA via polar decomposition.
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept pending corrections (notably eigenvalue claim) and additional experimental diagnostics on CSA and numerical stability.
