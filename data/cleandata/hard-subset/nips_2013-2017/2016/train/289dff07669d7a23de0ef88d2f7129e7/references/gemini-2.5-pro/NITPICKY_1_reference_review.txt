**Summary:**
This paper addresses the well-known computational bottleneck of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), which lies in the O(d^3) complexity of decomposing the covariance matrix at each iteration. The authors propose a variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix directly. This reduces the per-iteration complexity to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for a full-rank update. The primary technical challenge is that the standard step-size adaptation mechanism (CSA) requires the matrix square root C^(-1/2), which is not directly available from the Cholesky factor A. The authors propose an approximation using A^(-1) instead and provide a theoretical argument, based on the convergence of the covariance matrix shape, to justify why this approximation is sound and its error diminishes over time. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves a significant speed-up in wall-clock time compared to standard and other fast variants of CMA-ES, without a discernible loss in performance regarding the number of function evaluations.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a long-standing and practically important issue for a widely used and powerful black-box optimization algorithm. Reducing the computational overhead of CMA-ES makes it applicable to higher-dimensional problems where function evaluations are relatively cheap, a relevant scenario in machine learning (e.g., policy search, hyperparameter tuning).
2.  **Novelty:** While the idea of updating a matrix factorization directly is not entirely new (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this work makes two key contributions. First, it adapts the update for the standard (µ/µ_w, λ)-CMA-ES. Second, and more importantly, it provides a novel theoretical justification (Lemma 1) for approximating the step-size adaptation path, which was a major roadblock for previous attempts.
3.  **Performance:** The empirical evidence is compelling. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES shows a dramatic reduction in runtime on benchmark problems, scaling favorably with dimensionality, while requiring a nearly identical number of function evaluations as the reference implementation. This demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising results, the paper suffers from a number of technical inaccuracies, unclear statements, and a general lack of polish that detract from its quality.

1.  **Technical Errors:** A major technical error exists in the "Properties of the update rule" subsection. The authors claim: "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix". This statement is false. The eigenvalues of the covariance matrix C are not generally available from the diagonal of its Cholesky factor A. This incorrect claim undermines the stated advantages of the method and raises concerns about the authors' technical diligence.

2.  **Theoretical Justification:** The central theoretical argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix C̄_t converges. The authors rightly note that this cannot be guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm and then fall back on weaker arguments that "the expectation of Ct converges" in simplified settings or that the error is small if "Ct changes slowly." This moves the argument from a formal proof to a heuristic justification. The conditions under which the approximation holds or fails are not rigorously explored, which is a critical omission for the paper's core claim.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript has several points of confusion and poor formatting.
    *   The reference list is improperly formatted to an unacceptable degree (e.g., "Akimoto et al\.,? \Q2012\E, Akimoto et al\.,"). This suggests a lack of care in the preparation of the manuscript.
    *   Footnote 1 is cryptic. The connection between the two sentences is unclear, and it fails to adequately explain the concept to a non-expert.
    *   In Section 3, the phrase "currently there are no algorithms known that can update an existing polar decomposition... in less than cubic time" is a strong claim that should be substantiated with a citation.
    *   The description of the CMA-ES-Ref update delay is convoluted. Stating that the formula `max { 1, 1/(10*d(c1+cµ)) }` equals one for the dimensions considered is correct but unnecessarily obscure. It would be clearer to state that for default parameters and the tested dimensions, an update is performed at every iteration.
    *   In Algorithm 1, the update for `p_σ` uses `A_t^{-1}`. Since `A_{t+1}` is computed just before this step, it is not immediately clear if `A_t` is the correct matrix to use (though it is, to be consistent with the standard CMA-ES). This subtlety warrants a brief explanation.

4.  **Experimental Rigor:**
    *   For the Rosenbrock benchmark, trials where the algorithm did not converge were excluded. While common, this practice can hide performance differences if one variant is more susceptible to getting stuck in local optima. The paper should report the rate of non-convergence for each algorithm to ensure a fair comparison.
    *   The paper claims their update is less "prone to numerical instabilities" than maintaining separate updates for A_t and A_t^-1, but provides no evidence or analysis to support this. The stability of the proposed `rankOneUpdate` algorithm, especially with divisions by diagonal elements, is not discussed.

**Conclusion:**
The paper presents a practically significant contribution by speeding up a foundational optimization algorithm. The core idea is sound, and the empirical results are impressive. However, the work is marred by a significant technical error regarding eigenvalues, a theoretical justification that is weaker than claimed, and a notable number of clarity issues and formatting mistakes. These flaws collectively suggest that the paper is not yet ready for publication at NIPS. While the core contribution is valuable, the manuscript requires a thorough revision to correct the technical inaccuracies, strengthen the theoretical discussion, and improve the overall quality of the presentation.

**Recommendation: Reject**
