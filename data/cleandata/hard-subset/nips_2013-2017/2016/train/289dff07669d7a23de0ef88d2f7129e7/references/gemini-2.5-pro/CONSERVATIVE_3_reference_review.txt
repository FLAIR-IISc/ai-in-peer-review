**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the well-known computational bottleneck of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a highly regarded derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard CMA-ES exhibits a time complexity greater than O(d^2) per iteration due to the necessity of computing a matrix square root, typically via an O(d^3) eigenvalue decomposition. The authors propose a variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which avoids this cost by directly maintaining and updating a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This reduces the update complexity to O(µd^2), which is effectively O(d^2). The central challenge in this approach is the step-size adaptation mechanism, which requires the matrix C^(-1/2). The authors propose an approximation, replacing C^(-1/2) with A^(-1), where A is the Cholesky factor. They provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) that this approximation becomes accurate as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, as the rotational difference between the matrix square root and the Cholesky factor diminishes. The paper presents empirical results on standard benchmark functions, comparing the proposed method against the reference CMA-ES implementation and other variants, demonstrating a significant reduction in wall-clock time with no discernible loss in optimization performance (measured in function evaluations).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Practical and Important Problem:** The cubic complexity of the standard CMA-ES is a significant barrier to its application in moderately high-dimensional problems. This work provides a practical solution to make a powerful and established algorithm more efficient.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation on Standard Benchmarks:** The experimental setup is sound. The paper compares against the canonical CMA-ES implementation and other relevant baselines on a suite of well-known benchmark functions. The results compellingly show that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a substantial speed-up in runtime without increasing the number of function evaluations required for convergence. This is a strong piece of evidence for the method's practical utility.
3.  **Methodological Soundness within its Class:** The proposed algorithm is a carefully engineered modification of a classic method. By preserving the core rank-1 and rank-µ update structures and focusing the approximation on the step-size adaptation path, the authors retain much of the original algorithm's well-understood behavior. The use of a triangular factor is also beneficial for storage and numerical stability.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Theoretical Justification is Not Fully Rigorous:** The core novelty of the paper rests on an approximation, and its justification is heuristic. Lemma 1 demonstrates that the rotational error term vanishes *if* the normalized covariance matrix C̄t converges. However, for a stochastic algorithm like CMA-ES, convergence of C̄t is an assumption, not a guarantee, especially on complex, multi-modal, or noisy objective functions. The paper's argument that the error is small if "Ct changes slowly" is an intuitive but not a formal proof of robustness. The performance of the approximation in non-convergent or slowly-converging scenarios is not theoretically bounded.
2.  **Limited Scope of Experimental Validation:** The benchmark functions used, while standard, are primarily problems where CMA-ES is known to perform well and where the covariance matrix can be expected to adapt and converge to a stable shape. The experiments do not explore problem domains that would rigorously test the limits of the core approximation—for example, highly multi-modal functions where the search may transition between basins of attraction (causing abrupt changes in C_t), or problems with significant noise. The paper claims CMA-ES is suitable for such functions, yet the validation for this new variant is not provided for these cases.
3.  **Incremental Originality:** The idea of using Cholesky factor updates to speed up evolution strategies is not new, as the authors rightly acknowledge by citing Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015). The primary contribution is the specific adaptation to the modern (µ,λ)-CMA-ES and the novel justification for the step-size approximation. While this is a valuable engineering contribution, the conceptual leap is modest.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound from an implementation and empirical standpoint. The claims of speed-up are well-supported by the presented evidence. The main reservation regarding quality lies in the gap between the heuristic theoretical argument and the strong claims of equivalence. The work is complete as an empirical study but lacks the theoretical rigor that would make the approximation universally trustworthy.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the problem, motivating their approach, and relating it to prior work. The algorithms are presented cleanly, and the experimental results are easy to interpret.
*   **Originality:** The work is an incremental improvement upon existing ideas. It successfully combines known techniques (rank-one Cholesky updates) with a novel and practical approximation to create a more efficient version of a classic algorithm. This represents solid, evolutionary progress rather than a revolutionary new approach.
*   **Significance:** The practical significance of this work is high. CMA-ES is a cornerstone of derivative-free optimization, and this contribution makes it significantly more usable for problems with dimensionalities in the hundreds. Researchers and practitioners who use CMA-ES for applications like policy search or hyperparameter tuning will likely find this work directly applicable and beneficial. It advances the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable, practical way.

In conclusion, this paper presents a well-executed and valuable piece of engineering that enhances a widely-used, established optimization algorithm. While the theoretical justification for its core approximation is based on strong assumptions and could benefit from a more rigorous analysis, the empirical evidence strongly supports the claim that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a faster, drop-in replacement for the standard CMA-ES on a range of common problems. The practical utility of this contribution is undeniable, though the theoretical novelty is limited.
