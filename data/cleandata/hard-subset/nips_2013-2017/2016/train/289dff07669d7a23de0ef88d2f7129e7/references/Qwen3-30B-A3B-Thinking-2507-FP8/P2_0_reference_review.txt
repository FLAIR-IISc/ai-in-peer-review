

# Review: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) called "Cholesky-CMA-ES" that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors address a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES - the O(d³) cost of computing the matrix square root for covariance matrix updates. Previous approaches to this problem include delaying the square root computation (reducing complexity but potentially increasing function evaluations) or restricting to diagonal covariance matrices (which can degrade performance on non-separable problems). The authors' key contribution is providing a theoretical justification for why their approximation (replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor) does not deteriorate the algorithm's performance, unlike previous attempts. They demonstrate that the error introduced diminishes as the algorithm converges, and they provide empirical evidence that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES but with significantly reduced wall-clock time.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a clear theoretical justification for their approach (Lemma 1 and the subsequent discussion), demonstrating that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor diminishes as the algorithm converges. This theoretical foundation is crucial, as previous attempts to use Cholesky factorization lacked such justification. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing on multiple benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar/Discus/Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) across a range of dimensions (d = 4 to 256). The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES (CMA-ES-Ref) but with significantly reduced runtime - 20x faster for d=64. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the quadratic scaling with dimensionality, which becomes problematic for very large d), demonstrating appropriate scholarly honesty.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section clearly explains the CMA-ES algorithm and the computational bottleneck. The theoretical justification is well-motivated and presented with mathematical rigor. The algorithms (1 and 2) are clearly presented with pseudocode and explanations. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility. The figures effectively illustrate the key findings, particularly showing how the runtime advantage increases with dimensionality. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon, making the paper accessible to a broad machine learning audience.

## Originality

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the CMA-ES literature, which has been well-studied for decades. While previous work (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) has explored using Cholesky factorization for related algorithms, the authors provide the first theoretical justification for why this approach doesn't deteriorate performance in the standard CMA-ES. The key novelty is the rigorous analysis showing that the error in the step-size adaptation diminishes as the algorithm converges. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by addressing the theoretical gap that previous attempts overlooked. The authors also properly reference and position their work within the broader literature on CMA-ES variants.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. CMA-ES is a widely used derivative-free optimization algorithm in machine learning, particularly for hyperparameter tuning and reinforcement learning. The computational bottleneck addressed in this paper is a real limitation that affects practical applications. The demonstrated speedup (20x for d=64) is significant and could enable CMA-ES to scale to larger problems where the covariance matrix update has been prohibitively expensive. The paper provides a practical, drop-in replacement that can be easily integrated into existing CMA-ES implementations. The authors correctly note that for extremely large dimensions (where full covariance matrix maintenance becomes infeasible), one must resort to low-dimensional approximations, but their approach is valuable for problems where maintaining a full covariance matrix is still possible.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is strong overall, I have a few minor concerns:

1. The paper claims "asymptotically optimal time complexity," but the complexity is actually O(μd²) with μ typically O(log d), making it O(d² log d). The authors could clarify this more precisely.

2. The paper doesn't extensively discuss the numerical stability of the Cholesky updates. While they mention it's "numerically stable," a brief discussion of potential numerical issues and how they're mitigated would strengthen the paper.

3. The comparison to other recent CMA-ES variants (e.g., Loshchilov's LM-CMA) would have been useful, though the paper does compare to a delay strategy (CMA-ES/d) and the Suttorp approach.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature with a well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated approach to reducing the computational complexity of CMA-ES. The theoretical justification for why the approximation doesn't deteriorate performance is particularly valuable, as previous attempts lacked this crucial element. The empirical results demonstrate clear practical benefits with no loss in optimization quality.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), makes a clear original contribution (Originality), and addresses a significant practical problem with potential impact (Significance). The minor weaknesses noted don't detract from the overall quality of the work.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. This is a strong, well-executed paper that advances the state of the art in derivative-free optimization, a field of considerable interest to the machine learning community. The authors have provided a practical solution to a real bottleneck in a widely used algorithm, with both theoretical justification and empirical validation.

The Cholesky-CMA-ES variant is likely to be adopted by practitioners and researchers working with CMA-ES, as it offers a straightforward way to improve runtime without sacrificing optimization quality. The paper's contributions are clearly articulated, well-supported, and relevant to the NIPS audience.
