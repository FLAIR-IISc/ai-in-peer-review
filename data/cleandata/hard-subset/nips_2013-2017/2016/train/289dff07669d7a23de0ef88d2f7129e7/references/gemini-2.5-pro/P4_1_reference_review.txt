This paper proposes a new variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), named Cholesky-CMA-ES, which aims to reduce the computational complexity of the algorithm's covariance matrix update. CMA-ES is a state-of-the-art derivative-free optimization algorithm, but its per-iteration time complexity of O(d³) (or O(d²) with delayed updates) for problem dimension d can be a bottleneck, especially when the objective function evaluation is cheap. The authors identify the source of this complexity in the need to update the d×d covariance matrix `C` and subsequently compute its matrix square root, which is required for sampling new candidate solutions and for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism.

The main idea is to maintain and directly update a lower triangular Cholesky factor `A` of the covariance matrix (`C = AA^T`) instead of the matrix `C` itself. The rank-1 and rank-µ updates to `C` in the standard CMA-ES can be translated into a series of O(d²) updates to the Cholesky factor `A`. This approach builds upon prior work that explored direct updates to matrix factorizations (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). However, a key challenge in applying this to the standard CMA-ES lies in the CSA mechanism, which requires the term `C^(-1/2)`. This matrix square root is not readily available from the Cholesky factor `A`.

The central contribution of this work is a principled approximation to overcome this issue. The authors propose replacing `C^(-1/2)` with `A^(-1)`, which is efficiently computable in O(d²) time. They provide a theoretical justification for this approximation in Lemma 1. The lemma states that if the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the sequence of rotation matrices that relate the matrix square root to the Cholesky factor also converges. Consequently, the rotational error introduced by the approximation diminishes over time, especially given the exponential weighting scheme within the CSA path.

The paper presents compelling empirical evidence comparing the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES with several baselines, including the standard implementation with delayed updates (CMA-ES/d) and an approach based on a non-triangular Cholesky update (Suttorp-CMA-ES). On a suite of standard benchmark functions, the results show that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves convergence in a number of function evaluations that is statistically indistinguishable from the original CMA-ES, while being significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, particularly as the dimension `d` increases.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is well-defined, and the central approximation for the step-size adaptation is supported by a sound theoretical argument (Lemma 1). While the argument relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges, which is not formally guaranteed for the stochastic CMA-ES, it provides a strong rationale for why the approximation should work well in practice, especially as the optimization process stabilizes near an optimum. The experimental evaluation is thorough and follows established practices in the field. The claims of achieving optimal O(µd²) time complexity and O(d²) storage complexity for a full-rank covariance update are well-supported and accurate.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clearly stated, and the background on CMA-ES is concise and sufficient for understanding the contribution. The core problem related to the CSA update is explained effectively, leading into the proposed solution and its justification. Algorithm 1 provides a clear overview of the final method. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, and the results, presented in Figures 1, 2, and 3, are easy to interpret and strongly support the paper's conclusions.

**Originality:** The work is novel and makes a distinct contribution to the literature. While the idea of directly updating a Cholesky factor has been explored previously, this paper is the first to successfully and rigorously apply it to the complete, modern CMA-ES algorithm. The key original contribution is the identification and principled resolution of the step-size adaptation (CSA) problem, which was a major roadblock for previous attempts. The theoretical justification based on the convergence of the RQ decomposition's rotation matrix is a novel insight in this context and clearly distinguishes this work from prior art like Suttorp et al. (2009), which used a non-unique factorization without addressing the rotational effects on the CSA path.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. CMA-ES is a foundational algorithm in black-box optimization with numerous applications in machine learning, from reinforcement learning to hyperparameter tuning. Its primary limitation has long been its computational complexity in higher dimensions. This paper presents a practical and effective solution that dramatically reduces runtime without sacrificing the algorithm's excellent performance characteristics. The Cholesky-CMA-ES is a strong candidate to become the new default implementation of CMA-ES for moderately high-dimensional problems where a full covariance matrix is still desirable. Researchers and practitioners are very likely to adopt this improved version, thereby broadening the applicability of CMA-ES.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Solves an important, long-standing problem:** Addresses the key computational bottleneck of the standard CMA-ES algorithm.
*   **Asymptotically optimal complexity:** The proposed method achieves optimal O(µd²) time and O(d²) space complexity for a full covariance matrix update, a significant improvement over the standard O(d³) implementation.
*   **Strong empirical validation:** Experiments clearly demonstrate a substantial speed-up in wall-clock time with no degradation in the number of function evaluations required for convergence.
*   **Principled and novel approach:** The approximation made for the step-size update is well-justified by a novel theoretical argument.
*   **High potential impact:** The proposed algorithm is likely to be widely adopted and could become the new standard for CMA-ES implementations.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
*   The theoretical justification rests on the assumption of the convergence of the covariance matrix's shape, which is a strong condition for a stochastic algorithm. While the authors acknowledge this, a more formal analysis of the error propagation would have further strengthened the claim. However, the empirical results provide compelling evidence that this assumption is not a practical limitation.
