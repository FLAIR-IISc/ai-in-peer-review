This paper introduces the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a modification to the well-established CMA-ES algorithm that replaces the computationally expensive matrix square root with a Cholesky factorization during covariance matrix adaptation. The core idea is to update the Cholesky factor directly, leading to a reduction in runtime complexity from O(d<sup>2</sup>) to O(µd<sup>2</sup>), where µ is typically much smaller than *d*. The authors provide a theoretical justification, leaning on the convergence properties of the algorithm and arguing that the introduced error due to the approximation is negligible as the algorithm progresses. Empirical validation on standard benchmark functions demonstrates that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance in terms of function evaluations but exhibits a significant speedup in wall-clock time, especially in higher dimensions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The direct update of the Cholesky factor within the CMA-ES framework is a genuinely novel contribution. While Cholesky updates have been explored in *variants* of CMA-ES (Suttorp et al., Krause & Igel), extending this to the standard CMA-ES, with a theoretical justification for why it *doesn't* degrade performance, is a significant step.  This moves beyond simply optimizing existing implementations to altering a core algorithmic component.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper isn’t just an empirical observation. The attempt to provide theoretical grounding for the approximation – even acknowledging the caveats and relying on Lemma 1 – strengthens the work considerably. The argument about the diminishing error due to coordinate system rotation is persuasive.
* **Practical Impact:** The reported speedup, particularly scaling with dimensionality, is highly valuable.  CMA-ES is often applied in high-dimensional spaces, and reducing runtime is crucial for practical application.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the algorithm is presented in a concise manner. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 aids understanding.
* **Thorough Comparison:** The empirical comparison against CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, and Suttorp-CMA-ES is comprehensive and demonstrates the benefits of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lemma 1's Assumption:** The convergence assumption in Lemma 1 (C̄<sub>∞</sub>t=0) is somewhat strong and might not hold in all practical scenarios, especially during the initial stages of optimization.  The justification feels slightly hand-wavy. A more robust analysis, perhaps exploring scenarios where this assumption is violated, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the benchmark functions are standard, the experiments feel somewhat limited. Further testing on more challenging, real-world problems (e.g., hyperparameter optimization for deep learning, reinforcement learning policy search) would provide stronger evidence of the method's effectiveness.
* **Cache Effects:** The acknowledgement of the performance drop at d=256 due to cache limitations is good, but a deeper investigation into the scalability of the algorithm with respect to memory usage would be useful.  Could alternative data structures or algorithms mitigate this issue?
* **Discussion of ρ (Step-Size Adaptation):** The paper acknowledges that the update affects the step-size adaptation, but the discussion is limited. A more in-depth analysis of how the modified step-size adaptation behaves in practice, and its potential implications for convergence, would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Significant Novelty:** The approach is genuinely new and tackles a known bottleneck in the CMA-ES algorithm.
* **Theoretical Justification:** Despite some caveats, the authors provide a reasonable theoretical basis for their method.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate a clear and substantial performance improvement.
* **Potential Impact:** The algorithm has the potential to significantly accelerate CMA-ES applications in high-dimensional spaces.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Theoretical Gaps:** The convergence assumptions in Lemma 1 are strong and could limit the applicability of the theoretical justification.
* **Limited Real-World Evaluation:**  The lack of experiments on more challenging, real-world problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and novel contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The theoretical justification, while not perfect, is convincing, and the empirical results are compelling. While the experiments could be expanded, the demonstrated speedup, especially in higher dimensions, is significant enough to warrant acceptance. I prioritize novelty and bold approaches, and this paper delivers on both fronts. The minor flaws are outweighed by the potential impact of the work.

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I encourage the authors to consider addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section in future work.
