**Summary**

This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), which stems from the O(d^3) complexity of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix at each iteration. The authors propose a variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," that directly updates the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This reduces the time complexity to O(µd^2) and the storage complexity to O(d^2), which the authors claim is asymptotically optimal. The main technical challenge lies in the step-size adaptation mechanism (CSA), which requires the matrix square root inverse C^{-1/2}. The proposed method approximates this term with the inverse of the Cholesky factor, A^{-1}. The authors provide a theoretical argument, centered on Lemma 1, to justify why the error introduced by this approximation is expected to be small and diminish as the algorithm converges. Experimental results on several benchmark functions show that the proposed method achieves a significant wall-clock speedup over standard CMA-ES variants without increasing the number of function evaluations required for convergence.

**Strengths**

*   The paper tackles a well-known and practically significant limitation of CMA-ES, a widely used algorithm in derivative-free optimization.
*   The core idea of maintaining and updating a Cholesky factor directly is an elegant approach to reducing computational cost.
*   The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating a substantial reduction in runtime, especially for higher dimensions, while preserving the convergence properties (in terms of function evaluations) of the original algorithm. This makes the method highly relevant for practitioners.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising empirical results, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws, ranging from a major technical inaccuracy to weak theoretical justifications and poor manuscript preparation.

1.  **Major Technical Error:** In the "Properties of the update rule" section, the authors claim: "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix, that is, we get the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free." This statement is fundamentally incorrect for a general non-diagonal matrix. The eigenvalues of C are the squares of the singular values of its Cholesky factor A, not the squares of its diagonal elements. This error invalidates the subsequent claims about monitoring the conditioning of the problem and enforcing bounds on variances "for free." This is a critical misunderstanding of basic linear algebra.

2.  **Insufficient Theoretical Justification:** The central theoretical argument for the validity of the step-size approximation rests on Lemma 1. However, the lemma's proof assumes the convergence of the normalized covariance matrix sequence, `C̄_t`. The paper itself admits, "we cannot assume convergence of Ct" because the algorithm is stochastic. It then weakly bridges this gap by citing that the *expectation* of C_t converges in simplified settings and arguing heuristically that the error is small if C_t "changes slowly." This is not a rigorous justification. A proof based on convergence in expectation does not guarantee the convergence of the stochastic sequence itself, which is what the lemma requires. The theoretical foundation for the paper's core approximation is therefore tenuous at best.

3.  **Careless Manuscript Preparation:** The manuscript exhibits a lack of polish that is unbecoming of a NIPS submission.
    *   The bibliography is replete with formatting errors (e.g., `Akimoto et al\.,? \Q2012\E, Akimoto et al\.,`). This appears to be a BibTeX compilation failure that was not caught during proofreading and makes the references difficult to parse.
    *   There are several typographical and grammatical errors throughout the text (e.g., "and it is it conjectured that it does not" in Section 2.1; "ω(d2)" is used to denote complexity worse than quadratic, which is imprecise when O(d^3) is the standard term for EVD).
    *   Footnote 1 appears to be embedded directly in the main text, disrupting the flow.

4.  **Weak Experimental Baseline:** The primary baseline for runtime comparison, CMA-ES-Ref, is configured to recompute the matrix decomposition at *every* iteration. This is a known inefficient strategy for non-trivial dimensions and serves as a strawman argument that artificially inflates the reported speed-up. While a more reasonable baseline with delayed updates (CMA-ES/d) is included in the plots, the emphasis on the highly inefficient CMA-ES-Ref is potentially misleading.

5.  **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper claims the proposed method has "optimal" runtime and storage complexity. While O(d^2) storage is indeed optimal for a full matrix representation, the O(µd^2) runtime claim is not rigorously proven to be optimal for any general class of covariance matrix adaptation algorithms. Furthermore, the claim that the method is "prone to less numerical instabilities" than separate updates for A_t and A_t^{-1} is stated without any supporting analysis or evidence. Algorithm 2 involves divisions by diagonal elements `A'_{jj}`, which could itself introduce instabilities if these values approach zero, a possibility that is not discussed.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting engineering contribution with promising speedups for a popular optimization algorithm, the combination of a glaring technical error concerning eigenvalues, a hand-wavy theoretical justification for its core approximation, and sloppy manuscript preparation leads me to recommend rejection. The work has potential, but it does not currently meet the technical and presentational standards for publication at NIPS. The authors should correct the fundamental linear algebra mistake, provide a more rigorous analysis of their approximation's error bounds, and thoroughly proofread and properly format their manuscript before resubmission.
