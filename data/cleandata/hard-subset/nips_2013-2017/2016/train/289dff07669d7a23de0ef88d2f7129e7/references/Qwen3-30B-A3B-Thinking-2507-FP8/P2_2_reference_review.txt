

# Review: "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors present a theoretical justification for why this approximation doesn't significantly affect performance, showing that the error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2}_t with A^{-1}_t diminishes as the algorithm converges. The method achieves optimal O(µd²) runtime complexity for covariance updates (compared to O(d²) for standard CMA-ES) while maintaining the same number of function evaluations as the original algorithm. This builds on previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who applied Cholesky factorization to special cases of CMA-ES, but extends it to the standard algorithm with theoretical guarantees.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation with Lemma 1, which demonstrates that the error in the step-size adaptation vanishes as the algorithm converges. This is a significant contribution beyond previous work, which lacked theoretical justification for their approximations. The experimental design is robust, using standard benchmark functions across multiple dimensions (d=4 to 256), with 100 trials per dimension. The results clearly show that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as CMA-ES-Ref but achieves significantly faster runtime (20x faster for d=64), with the speedup increasing with dimensionality. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, noting that while the method improves runtime, it still scales quadratically with dimensionality.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The background section effectively explains the necessary concepts (Cholesky decomposition, CMA-ES update rules), and the proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented with sufficient detail for replication. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the figures (particularly Figure 2 showing the runtime scaling) effectively communicate the key results. The supplementary material addressing rotation invariance is a thoughtful addition that addresses a potential concern.

**Significance:** This work addresses a practical bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm. CMA-ES is a standard tool for derivative-free optimization in machine learning (for reinforcement learning policy search and hyperparameter tuning), and the computational overhead of matrix square root calculations can be prohibitive for high-dimensional problems. The Cholesky-CMA-ES provides a direct, drop-in replacement that significantly speeds up optimization without sacrificing solution quality, making it immediately applicable to practitioners' workflows. The paper's conclusion that it's "particularly useful for scaling up CMA-ES to large optimization problems" is well-supported by the experimental results.

## Weaknesses

**Originality:** While the extension to the standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification is novel, the core idea of using Cholesky factorization for CMA-ES has been explored before (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015). The paper could better highlight how their approach differs from these prior works, particularly in terms of the theoretical justification. The claim of "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity" is somewhat misleading - the complexity is optimal for the full covariance matrix update, but the paper doesn't explicitly compare with approaches that use low-rank approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015), which have better complexity for extremely high dimensions.

**Quality (Technical):** The theoretical analysis relies on the assumption that Ct converges to a certain shape, but CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm without guaranteed convergence. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't provide a rigorous analysis of the convergence properties of the Cholesky-CMA-ES. The paper also doesn't address numerical stability in detail - while it mentions the triangular structure allows for numerically stable inversion, it doesn't provide empirical evidence of numerical stability compared to the standard CMA-ES.

**Significance (Broader Impact):** The paper doesn't discuss how the Cholesky-CMA-ES might integrate with recent advances in CMA-ES (e.g., the LM-CMA approach by Loshchilov, 2015) or how it might perform on non-standard optimization problems (e.g., constrained optimization or multi-objective optimization). While the empirical results are strong, the paper could better articulate how this method fits into the broader landscape of derivative-free optimization algorithms.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong technical contribution that addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides both theoretical justification and empirical validation for the proposed method. The results demonstrate a significant practical improvement in runtime with no sacrifice in solution quality, making this work immediately valuable to practitioners.

The main limitations are the somewhat overstated claim of "optimal" complexity (without explicitly comparing with low-rank approximation methods) and the lack of deeper theoretical analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties. However, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's strong empirical results and clear practical contribution.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a practical and theoretically justified improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm. The runtime improvements are significant (20x faster for d=64) and the method is directly applicable to real-world machine learning problems. While the theoretical justification could be strengthened, the empirical results are compelling and the paper provides clear value to the community.

The paper addresses a practical problem with a solution that is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated, making it an excellent fit for NIPS.
