Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C=AA^T, avoiding explicit computation of C and its eigendecomposition. The covariance update is decomposed into successive rank-one updates of A, yielding O(µ d^2) time per iteration (with µ ≈ O(log d)), and reduced memory O(d^2/2). The key challenge is step-size adaptation: the standard cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) uses C^{-1/2}, but the proposed method replaces it with A^{-1}, omitting the (time-varying) orthogonal factor that relates A to C^{1/2}. The authors justify this approximation via a continuity argument (Lemma 1) suggesting that the rotation error vanishes as C “stabilizes.” Experiments on standard noiseless benchmarks (sphere, cigar/discus/ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, diffpowers) with random rotations up to d=256 show comparable numbers of function evaluations to a reference CMA-ES but substantially reduced wall-clock time; comparisons to a “delayed decomposition” reference and to a non-triangular Cholesky update (Suttorp et al., 2009) are included. The work builds on and extends prior Cholesky update ideas [Suttorp et al., 2009] and rank-one efficiency improvements [Krause & Igel, 2015] to the standard CMA-ES with µ > 1.

Quality
- Positive: The covariance update via a triangular cholupdate-style routine is technically sound and brings the expected computational benefits. The complexity claims for full-covariance adaptation are reasonable. The experimental setup covers multiple test functions, dimensions up to 256, random rotations, and multiple baselines; the practical speedups are convincing.
- Concerns: The central approximation in CSA (replacing C^{-1/2} by A^{-1}) is only weakly justified. Lemma 1 assumes convergence of the normalized covariance sequence and uses continuity of the RQ decomposition to argue that the orthogonal rotations vanish over time. This does not constitute a bound on the CSA error, nor does it address early/mid optimization phases, non-convex landscapes, ill-conditioned cases where C changes rapidly, or noise. No guarantees on step-size stability or invariance are provided. Moreover, the empirical validation avoids noisy and constrained settings where CSA behavior is critical.
- A significant technical error appears in Section 3: “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general; Cholesky diagonal entries are not eigenvalues’ square roots (though the product of their squares equals det(C)). This undermines the claim that eigenvalues are “obtained for free.”
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: The reference CMA-ES computes the matrix square root every iteration (due to the chosen default threshold), effectively penalizing the baseline. Modern implementations amortize eigendecompositions with adaptive frequencies to achieve O(d^2) amortized time. A fair comparison should include tuned update frequencies recommended in practice (e.g., as in Hansen’s code), and report amortized per-iteration complexity and runtime. Comparison to LM-CMA / limited-memory CMA-ES and diagonal/separable CMA-ES variants in the same codebase would strengthen claims. Statistical tests/intervals on function evaluations are missing.
- Numerical stability: Repeated rank-one cholupdates can accumulate numerical errors. The paper does not describe any periodic re-factorization, jitter, or safeguards to maintain SPD-ness and accuracy, nor quantify the deviation between A^{-T}A^{-1} and C^{-1} over runs.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with helpful algorithm boxes. However:
- The CSA approximation and its implications for (non-)invariance should be explained more carefully, with explicit discussion of when and why the error can be large.
- The incorrect statement about Cholesky diagonals/eigenvalues must be corrected.
- Experimental details (hardware, BLAS/LAPACK threading, eigen-update frequency in baselines) should be made precise for reproducibility. Reporting confidence intervals and failure rates would help.

Originality
The idea of updating a Cholesky factor for CMA-ES is not new, but extending a triangular cholupdate to the standard µ-update and integrating it into CMA-ES with an approximate CSA is a meaningful engineering contribution. The novelty is incremental but practical.

Significance
If robust, the approach could materially reduce wall-clock time for full-covariance CMA-ES in moderate-to-high dimensions, which is of interest to practitioners. However, without stronger theoretical guarantees for CSA or broader empirical validation (noisy functions, constraints, real ML tasks like RL policy search or hyperparameter tuning), it is hard to endorse this as a new default. The incorrect eigenvalue claim and potential baseline bias further reduce confidence.

Strengths
- Practical speedup with simple changes; asymptotically optimal storage for full covariance.
- Clean decomposition of rank-µ update into rank-one cholupdates.
- Experiments show substantial runtime gains at fixed evaluation counts on standard testbeds.

Weaknesses
- Weak theoretical support for the CSA approximation; no error bounds or robustness analysis.
- Incorrect claim about Cholesky diagonals and eigenvalues.
- Limited experimental scope (no noise/constraints/real tasks), and potentially unfavorable baseline settings for the reference CMA-ES.
- No discussion of numerical stability safeguards or periodic re-factorization.
- No statistical analysis of evaluation counts; selection bias on Rosenbrock (excluding non-convergent trials).

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the eigenvalue claim and revise the discussion accordingly.
- Provide a quantitative analysis of the CSA error (e.g., bound on deviation in ‖pσ‖, or empirical measurement of Et drift and its effect on σ) and conditions under which it remains small.
- Include noisy benchmarks, constraints, and a real application (e.g., small RL policy search) to test CSA robustness.
- Compare against an optimized CMA-ES with standard amortized eigendecomposition frequency and against LM-CMA; report amortized costs and statistical tests.
- Describe and evaluate numerical stability measures (e.g., periodic rebuild of A from C, jitter).
- Release code and scripts for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical and substantial runtime improvements for full-covariance CMA-ES with minimal changes.
- Solid engineering contribution likely useful to the community.
- Clear presentation of algorithms and complexity.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical grounding for the CSA approximation; invariance and stability are not established.
- Experimental evaluation limited in scope and potentially biased; lacks noisy/realistic settings.
- Technical error regarding “eigenvalues for free.”
- No statistical rigor in comparisons.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and likely useful, but the current version lacks theoretical guarantees for the altered CSA, contains a notable technical mistake, and does not convincingly demonstrate robustness across realistic scenarios. With corrected theory, stronger baselines, and broader experiments, this could become a solid contribution.
