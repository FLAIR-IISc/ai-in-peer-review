**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper addresses the well-known computational bottleneck of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES has a per-iteration complexity of at least O(d^3) due to the eigenvalue decomposition required to compute the covariance matrix square root, C^(1/2). This cost dominates runtime when the objective function is cheap to evaluate or the dimensionality *d* is large.

The authors propose a variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which reduces this complexity to O(µd^2), where µ is the parent population size (typically O(log d)). The core idea is to bypass the explicit formation and decomposition of the covariance matrix C and instead directly maintain and update its lower triangular Cholesky factor, A, where C = AA^T. While previous work (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) has explored direct updates on factorizations, this paper's key contribution is a novel and theoretically-justified approach to handle the critical step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The CSA rule requires the term C^(-1/2), which is not equivalent to the inverse Cholesky factor A^(-1). The authors' main insight is to approximate C^(-1/2) with A^(-1) and provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) for why the resulting error in the adaptation is expected to be small and vanish as the algorithm converges. This is based on the idea that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the rotational component distinguishing C^(1/2) from A also stabilizes, making the approximation sound.

The work is well-positioned relative to other approaches for scaling CMA-ES. It avoids the performance degradation seen in methods that restrict the covariance matrix (e.g., to diagonal) on non-separable problems and the sawtooth performance of methods that delay the matrix update.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Practical Impact:** The paper tackles a long-standing, critical limitation of one of the most successful black-box optimization algorithms. The demonstrated speedup (e.g., 20x at d=64) is substantial and makes the full-covariance CMA-ES practical for a wider range of medium-to-high dimensional problems, particularly in machine learning applications like policy search and hyperparameter tuning, where function evaluations can be relatively fast.

2.  **Novel and Sound Technical Contribution:** The primary novelty is not merely the use of Cholesky updates, but the elegant theoretical justification for approximating the CSA path `p_σ`. Lemma 1 provides a compelling argument for why replacing C^(-1/2) with A^(-1) does not fundamentally break the step-size adaptation mechanism. This is a crucial step forward from previous attempts which either did not apply to the standard (µ,λ)-CMA-ES or lacked this formal reasoning. The use of a triangular factor is also advantageous over non-triangular decompositions as it provides a unique factorization, leading to more stable behavior.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed. The authors compare their method against the standard reference implementation, a delayed-update variant, and a competing Cholesky-based approach on a suite of standard benchmark functions. The results clearly show that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves virtually identical convergence in terms of function evaluations to the reference implementation, while being significantly faster in wall-clock time. This provides strong evidence that the approximation has negligible impact on optimization performance.

4.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly motivates the problem, explains the necessary background on CMA-ES, pinpoints the exact technical challenge, and presents its solution and justification in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. The work is self-contained and provides sufficient detail (e.g., Algorithm 1 and 2) for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Theoretical Justification Relies on an Assumption:** The argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix C̄_t converges. The authors acknowledge that this is not guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm but cite that its *expectation* converges in simplified settings. While the reasoning is plausible and empirically supported, it remains a heuristic justification rather than a rigorous convergence proof for the full algorithm. It would strengthen the paper to more explicitly state this as a limitation and discuss potential scenarios (e.g., highly non-stationary objective functions) where C might not stabilize and the approximation could potentially degrade performance.

2.  **Limited Discussion of Numerical Stability:** While the paper mentions that maintaining separate updates for A and A^(-1) is "prone to numerical instabilities," it does not provide an in-depth discussion or empirical evidence of the numerical stability of its own proposed rank-one update (Algorithm 2), especially in the face of very ill-conditioned matrices. This is a minor point, but given the focus on efficiency, a comment on numerical robustness would be welcome.

**Critique based on NIPS criteria:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, and its claims are strongly supported by both a theoretical argument and comprehensive experiments. It's a complete piece of work that carefully addresses a well-defined problem.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is a model of clarity, making a complex topic accessible. The organization is logical, and the writing is precise.
*   **Originality:** High. While building on the idea of factor updates, the specific way the authors address the CSA mechanism and provide a theoretical basis for their approximation is novel and distinguishes this work clearly from prior art like Suttorp et al. (2009).
*   **Significance:** High. This work is likely to become the new standard for efficient implementations of CMA-ES. Researchers and practitioners using CMA-ES for problems with d in the range of ~50 to ~500 will see immediate and substantial benefits. This directly advances the state of the art in practical derivative-free optimization.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant, practical, and well-justified improvement to a widely used and important algorithm. The contribution is both technically sound and empirically validated. It will be of great interest to the NIPS community, particularly those working in reinforcement learning, automated machine learning, and optimization. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength and impact of the work.
