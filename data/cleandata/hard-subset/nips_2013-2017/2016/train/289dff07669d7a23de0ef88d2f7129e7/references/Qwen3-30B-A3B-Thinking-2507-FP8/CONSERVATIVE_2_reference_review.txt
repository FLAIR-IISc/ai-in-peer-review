

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

## Summary
This paper proposes "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors claim this approach maintains performance while achieving optimal asymptotic runtime complexity (O(μd²)) and storage complexity. The theoretical justification relies on Lemma 1, which argues that the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ vanishes as the algorithm converges. The paper includes empirical comparisons with CMA-ES variants on standard benchmark functions across multiple dimensions.

## Quality
The paper presents a theoretically motivated approach to address a known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES. The authors build on previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), but extend it to the standard CMA-ES. The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) is mathematically sound, though it relies on the assumption that Ct converges to a certain shape, which is not guaranteed for the randomized CMA-ES algorithm. This is a significant limitation, as CMA-ES is a stochastic algorithm without guaranteed convergence properties.

The experimental results show that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves similar function evaluations to CMA-ES-Ref (the reference implementation with delay strategy) but with significantly reduced runtime (up to 20x faster for d=64). However, the paper lacks statistical validation for the claim that "the same amount of function evaluations" is required. The authors state this as a fact based on Figure 1 but provide no statistical tests (e.g., t-tests) to confirm this observation is significant across the 100 trials per dimension.

The comparison with baselines is limited. The authors use CMA-ES-Ref (which already implements a delay strategy to reduce computation) as their reference, rather than the standard CMA-ES implementation. This makes it difficult to assess the true impact of their approach. They also don't compare with other low-rank covariance approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2014, 2015), which might be more appropriate for very high-dimensional problems.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with detailed pseudocode for the proposed algorithm and its components. The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with CMA-ES. However, the experimental section lacks critical details: hardware specifications, exact implementation details of the Shark library modifications, and precise timing metrics to verify the claimed speedup.

## Originality
The core idea of using Cholesky factorization instead of matrix square root is not entirely novel (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015). The novelty lies in extending this approach to the standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification. However, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from previous attempts beyond the specific theoretical justification provided. The authors cite relevant prior work but could better clarify the precise contribution relative to existing literature.

## Significance
The computational bottleneck addressed by this paper is real and important for practical applications of CMA-ES. The potential speedup could be valuable for high-dimensional optimization problems. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this approach compares to other methods for handling high-dimensional optimization (e.g., low-rank approximations). The claim that "the Cholesky-CMA-ES is optimal for scaling up CMA-ES to large optimization problems" is overstated, as it only addresses the covariance update complexity while still requiring O(d²) memory.

## Critical Weaknesses

1. **Lack of rigorous theoretical justification**: The paper's theoretical foundation relies on the convergence of Ct, which is not guaranteed for the randomized CMA-ES. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide empirical evidence that Ct actually converges to the required shape in practice.

2. **Insufficient experimental validation**: The claim of "no significant change in function evaluations" lacks statistical validation. The paper doesn't provide p-values, confidence intervals, or statistical tests to confirm this observation is meaningful.

3. **Incomplete baseline comparison**: The reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) already uses a delay strategy to reduce computation, making it an inappropriate baseline for assessing the value of the proposed method. The paper should have compared against a standard CMA-ES implementation without delay strategies.

4. **Missing numerical stability analysis**: The paper claims "numerically stable computation" but provides no evidence of this. In practice, approximating C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ could introduce significant numerical errors, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

5. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity" but doesn't address the fact that the approach still requires O(d²) memory, which becomes prohibitive for very large d. This limits its applicability to the "large optimization problems" mentioned in the conclusion.

## Strengths

- The paper presents a theoretically motivated approach to a genuine computational bottleneck.
- The algorithm is well-described with detailed pseudocode.
- The empirical results show a clear speedup in runtime for higher dimensions.
- The paper acknowledges the limitations of their approach (e.g., still requiring O(d²) memory).

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses a legitimate computational concern in CMA-ES, it falls short of the rigorous validation required for NIPS. The theoretical justification is incomplete, the experimental validation is insufficient, and the baseline comparisons are misleading. The paper would benefit from:

1. Statistical validation of the function evaluation equivalence claim
2. Comparison with standard CMA-ES (without delay strategies)
3. Numerical stability analysis across different problem types
4. More precise discussion of the limitations (particularly the O(d²) memory requirement)

Without these improvements, the paper presents an interesting but unproven modification to an established algorithm. The claimed advantages are not sufficiently demonstrated against appropriate baselines, and the theoretical justification is incomplete for the randomized algorithm in question.

I recommend rejection. The paper's contribution is incremental and not sufficiently validated for NIPS. The authors should address the methodological gaps before considering publication in a venue requiring high standards of evidence and validation.
