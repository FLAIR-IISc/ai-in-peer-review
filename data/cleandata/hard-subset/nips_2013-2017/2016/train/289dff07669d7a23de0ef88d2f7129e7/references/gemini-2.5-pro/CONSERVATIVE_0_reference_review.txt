### NIPS Review

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), where the O(d^3) complexity of decomposing the covariance matrix C can dominate runtime in high dimensions. The authors propose "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant that avoids explicit computation and decomposition of C by directly maintaining and updating its lower triangular Cholesky factor, A, where C = AA^T. This reduces the update complexity to O(µd^2).

The primary technical challenge is that the standard step-size adaptation mechanism (CSA) in CMA-ES relies on the matrix square root C^(-1/2), which is not equivalent to the Cholesky factor A^(-1). The authors' main contribution is to propose using A^(-1) as an approximation for C^(-1/2) in the CSA update. They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this approximation diminishes. Empirically, the authors compare their method against the standard CMA-ES and other variants on a set of benchmark functions, demonstrating a significant reduction in wall-clock time with no discernible loss in sample efficiency.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The quadratic/cubic complexity of the standard CMA-ES is a long-standing practical limitation. Improving its scalability is a valuable and important contribution, particularly as CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization and is frequently used in machine learning applications like policy search and hyperparameter tuning.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES shows a dramatic speedup in wall-clock time compared to the reference implementation, and this advantage grows with dimensionality as expected. Crucially, this speedup is achieved without a significant increase in the number of function evaluations, suggesting the core approximation does not harm the algorithm's search behavior on the tested benchmarks.

3.  **Methodologically Sound Comparison:** The authors compare their method against a strong and appropriate baseline: the official reference implementation of CMA-ES with its recommended delayed update strategy. This provides a realistic and fair assessment of the practical benefits. The inclusion of other variants like CMA-ES/d and the Suttorp-CMA-ES further contextualizes the contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on an Unproven Approximation:** The central novelty of the paper rests on replacing C^(-1/2) with A^(-1) in the step-size adaptation path. The theoretical justification for this is based on Lemma 1, which assumes the normalized covariance matrix C̄t converges. This is a strong assumption for a stochastic optimization algorithm. While the argument is plausible for unimodal, convex problems where the algorithm is expected to stabilize, it is less convincing for more complex, multi-modal, or non-stationary landscapes where the covariance matrix may continue to change significantly throughout the search. The paper does not sufficiently address the potential impact of the approximation error during the transient phases of the optimization or in scenarios where this convergence assumption does not hold.

2.  **Lack of Deeper Empirical Analysis:** The experiments demonstrate that the method *works*, but they do not provide sufficient insight into *why* the approximation is benign. A more rigorous validation would involve empirically measuring the error introduced by the approximation. For example, the authors could have compared the norm of the step-size path `p_σ` as computed by their method versus the true value computed using the full matrix square root. Without such an analysis, it is difficult to assess whether the success on these particular benchmarks will generalize to other, more challenging problems where step-size adaptation is critical.

3.  **Incremental Originality:** The idea of updating a Cholesky factor to reduce CMA-ES complexity is not entirely new, as the authors themselves acknowledge by citing Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015). The main novelty lies in adapting this idea to the standard (µ,λ)-CMA-ES and providing a new justification for handling the step-size update. While a valuable engineering contribution, the conceptual leap is less significant than a completely new approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a practical and significant improvement to a widely used, canonical optimization algorithm.
*   The empirical results are strong and clearly demonstrate a substantial speedup on standard benchmarks without loss of performance.
*   The method is simple to implement and brings the runtime and storage complexity to the optimal level for a full-covariance matrix approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The theoretical justification for the core approximation is based on an asymptotic convergence argument that may not hold in general, and its practical implications are not fully explored.
*   The experimental validation is not sufficiently thorough. It lacks a direct analysis of the approximation error, and the benchmark suite, while standard, does not test the method in potentially adversarial scenarios (e.g., high multi-modality, noise) where the approximation might fail.
*   The work could be viewed as a clever, but incremental, improvement over existing ideas for accelerating CMA-ES.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a well-executed piece of engineering that results in a practically superior version of the CMA-ES algorithm for problems where d is large but not so large as to prohibit O(d^2) complexity. The speedup is undeniable and addresses a real-world limitation of a foundational algorithm.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by the nature of the core contribution. It is an approximation justified by an asymptotic argument. While the empirical results are positive, I remain skeptical about its robustness. The paper would be substantially stronger if it included experiments that specifically stress-test the approximation—for example, on problems designed to induce rapid changes in the covariance matrix orientation, or a direct measurement of the approximation error. As it stands, the paper presents a useful "trick" that appears to work well in common cases, but it does not fully convince me of its universal soundness. It is a solid contribution, but it feels more like a strong workshop paper or a journal paper on numerical methods than a groundbreaking contribution for NIPS. I am on the borderline, but lean towards rejection due to the lack of a more rigorous validation of the central approximation.
