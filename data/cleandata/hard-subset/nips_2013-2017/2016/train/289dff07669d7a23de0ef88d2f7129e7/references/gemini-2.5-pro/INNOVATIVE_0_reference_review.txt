### Summary
This paper addresses the well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES has a per-iteration complexity dominated by an O(d^3) eigenvalue decomposition of the d-dimensional covariance matrix. The authors propose a new variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that circumvents this expensive step by directly maintaining and updating a triangular Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix. This reduces the time complexity for the update to O(µd^2) and the storage to O(d^2), which are asymptotically optimal.

The core challenge, which has stymied previous efforts, lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the inverse matrix square root C^(-1/2) for normalization. The authors' key contribution is to approximate this term using the inverse of their triangular Cholesky factor, A^(-1). They provide a novel theoretical justification for this approximation, arguing that as the algorithm converges, the rotational component difference between the true square root and the Cholesky factor vanishes. This ensures that the norm of the step-size adaptation path, which is what the update rule uses, remains largely unaffected. The paper validates this approach empirically on several benchmark functions, showing that the Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard implementation while being significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, especially for higher dimensions.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental, long-standing problem in a widely used and important algorithm. While others have attempted to address the complexity of CMA-ES, they have either compromised the algorithm's performance on non-separable problems (e.g., diagonal approximations) or failed to adapt the standard CMA-ES with its crucial CSA mechanism. This work presents an elegant and principled solution that preserves the full power of the covariance matrix adaptation. The potential impact is substantial, as it could make CMA-ES practical for a new range of higher-dimensional problems in fields like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization, and could well become the new standard implementation.

2.  **Bold and Insightful Approach:** The decision to approximate C^(-1/2) with A^(-1) is the critical step, and the paper's justification for it is its most creative contribution. The use of the RQ-decomposition to analyze the relationship between the matrix square root and the Cholesky factor (C^(1/2) = AE) is insightful. The argument in Lemma 1—that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the rotation matrix E also converges, making the approximation asymptotically exact—is a clever piece of reasoning that provides a solid conceptual foundation for the algorithm. This is precisely the kind of bold, theoretically-motivated leap that pushes the field forward.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments convincingly support the paper's main claims. The plots of function evaluations vs. iterations show that the performance of Cholesky-CMA-ES is nearly identical to the reference implementation, validating that the approximation in the CSA does not harm convergence. Simultaneously, the wall-clock time results demonstrate a dramatic and accelerating speed-up as the dimensionality increases, confirming the improved time complexity. The comparison against relevant baselines like CMA-ES/d is also well-executed.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Technical Inaccuracy in a Secondary Claim:** The paper claims that the diagonal elements of the triangular Cholesky factor "are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix." This is incorrect. The eigenvalues of a symmetric matrix C are generally not related to the diagonal entries of its Cholesky factor A in this simple way (unless C is already diagonal). While the eigenvalues can be computed from A, it requires computing the singular values of A, which is not a trivial operation. This error does not undermine the paper's primary contribution but is a notable inaccuracy that should be corrected.

2.  **Theoretical Justification Relies on an Assumption:** The proof of Lemma 1, and therefore the justification for the core approximation, rests on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges. The authors rightly note that for a stochastic algorithm, this is not guaranteed, though the expectation may converge. While the strong empirical results provide compelling evidence that this assumption holds in practice, the theoretical argument is not fully rigorous. For a paper with this level of novelty, this is an acceptable limitation, but it's worth noting that the theoretical backing is heuristic rather than a formal proof of convergence.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   The paper presents a highly novel and elegant solution to a significant and long-standing performance bottleneck in a canonical optimization algorithm.
*   The core idea is supported by a creative theoretical argument that provides a strong conceptual justification for the proposed approximation.
*   The work has high potential for impact, as it can directly replace existing CMA-ES implementations to achieve substantial speed-ups without sacrificing performance.
*   The empirical results are clear, well-presented, and robustly support the paper's central claims.

### Arguments for Rejection
*   The paper contains a clear technical error regarding the relationship between Cholesky factors and eigenvalues, which suggests a minor lack of care.
*   The theoretical foundation is not fully rigorous, as it depends on an unproven convergence assumption.

### Recommendation
This paper is an excellent contribution. It combines a practically important result—a much faster CMA-ES—with a genuinely novel and insightful theoretical argument. The solution is elegant and addresses a problem that has persisted for years. The weaknesses are minor; the technical error regarding eigenvalues is easily correctable and does not affect the main thrust of the work, and the heuristic nature of the theoretical argument is forgivable in light of the idea's novelty and the strong empirical support. This work prioritizes a bold new direction over perfecting every minor detail, and in doing so, it delivers a result of high significance and originality. I strongly recommend acceptance.
