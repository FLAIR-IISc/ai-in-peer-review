### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard CMA-ES has a per-iteration time complexity of at least ω(d^2) (typically O(d^3) due to eigenvalue decomposition) for a d-dimensional problem, which is dominated by the need to compute the square root of the covariance matrix, C^(1/2), for sampling and step-size adaptation. This cost can be prohibitive for problems with large d or computationally inexpensive objective functions.

The authors propose a new variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, which reduces this complexity to O(µd^2), where µ is the number of parent points (typically O(log d)). The key idea is to maintain and directly update a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix (C = AA^T) instead of the full matrix C. While updating Cholesky factors has been proposed before (e.g., by Suttorp et al.), this work makes a novel contribution by successfully adapting it to the standard CMA-ES. The main technical challenge lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the term C^(-1/2). The authors propose approximating C^(-1/2) with A^(-1), which is efficiently computable in O(d^2).

The paper provides a theoretical justification for this approximation. It argues through Lemma 1 that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the rotational error introduced by using A^(-1) instead of C^(-1/2) diminishes. Because the CSA is an exponentially weighted moving average, the impact of past rotational errors decays, making the approximation accurate in practice. The paper presents extensive experiments on standard benchmark functions, comparing Cholesky-CMA-ES with the reference CMA-ES implementation and other variants. The results show that the proposed method achieves the same convergence in terms of function evaluations as the standard algorithm but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, with the speed-up increasing dramatically with problem dimensionality.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a long-standing and practically important problem. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization, used widely in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. Making it significantly faster for high-dimensional problems without sacrificing its excellent performance is a major contribution that will be of immediate use to many researchers and practitioners.
2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The core idea of approximating C^(-1/2) with A^(-1) is simple and elegant, but the justification is non-trivial. The inclusion of Lemma 1 and the associated reasoning provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approximation, elevating the work beyond a mere heuristic or engineering trick. It explains *why* the approximation should work well, linking it to the convergence properties of the algorithm.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental design is sound. The authors compare their method against appropriate and strong baselines, including the standard implementation with its own delay-based speedup mechanism. The key result—that the number of function evaluations remains unchanged while wall-clock time plummets—is clearly demonstrated across a range of benchmark problems and dimensions. This directly validates the central claim of the paper.
4.  **Clarity and Quality of Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly motivates the problem, explains the necessary background of CMA-ES, details the proposed method and its justification, and presents the results in an easy-to-understand manner. The flow from problem to solution is logical and compelling.
5.  **Optimal Complexity:** The resulting algorithm achieves O(µd^2) time and O(d^2) storage complexity, which is asymptotically optimal for any method that adapts a full d x d covariance matrix. The authors also highlight additional benefits, such as obtaining eigenvalues for free and numerically stable inversion.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on an Unproven Convergence Assumption:** The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix `C̄_t` converges. The authors acknowledge that this is not formally proven for the full randomized algorithm, although it holds for simplified versions. While the empirical results strongly suggest the assumption holds in practice, this remains a minor theoretical gap. The performance on problems where the covariance matrix might oscillate or converge very slowly (like Rosenbrock, which was tested) seems good, but it would be interesting to see a discussion on potential failure modes.
2.  **Incremental Nature of the Core Idea:** The idea of updating a factorization of the covariance matrix directly to speed up CMA-ES is not entirely new, and the paper appropriately cites prior work (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). The primary novelty here is the specific way they solve the C^(-1/2) problem for the CSA update in the *standard* CMA-ES and provide a theoretical basis. While this is a crucial and enabling step, the work can be seen as an important and well-executed refinement of an existing research direction rather than a completely new paradigm.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is of high quality and I have only minor suggestions.
*   The authors state that the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues. This is only true if the matrix is diagonal. For a general symmetric positive definite matrix C = AA^T, the diagonal elements of A are not directly related to the eigenvalues of C. However, the eigenvalues of C are the squares of the singular values of A. This minor inaccuracy should be corrected.
*   In the experimental section, the performance drop for d=256 is attributed to exceeding the L2 cache. This is a plausible and interesting practical observation that adds value to the analysis.

### Recommendation: Accept

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. It presents a high-quality, complete piece of work that solves a significant practical problem in a principled way.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Significance:** It significantly speeds up a widely used and important optimization algorithm, making it applicable to higher-dimensional problems.
*   **Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound, supported by both theoretical reasoning and a rigorous empirical study.
*   **Originality:** It provides a novel and well-justified solution to a key challenge (the CSA update) that has hindered previous attempts to create a fast Cholesky-based CMA-ES.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical argument rests on a convergence assumption that is not formally proven for the full algorithm. However, this is a very minor point given the strength of the empirical evidence and the plausibility of the argument.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that clearly advances the state-of-the-art in derivative-free optimization and will be of immediate interest to the NIPS community, particularly those working in reinforcement learning and automated machine learning.
