

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy that replaces the matrix square root operation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors make a compelling case for why this approach can maintain performance while achieving optimal asymptotic runtime and storage complexity. 

The paper builds on the well-known CMA-ES algorithm [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001], which has been widely adopted in derivative-free optimization. The key bottleneck in CMA-ES is the O(d^3) cost of computing the matrix square root at each iteration, which becomes prohibitive for large dimensions. Previous approaches to address this include delaying updates (which can hurt performance) or restricting to diagonal matrices (which sacrifices generality). The authors' insight is to use Cholesky factorization instead of matrix square root, with a theoretical justification that the introduced error diminishes as the algorithm converges.

The paper's main contribution is the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm (Algorithm 1) that updates the Cholesky factor directly, achieving O(µd^2) runtime complexity (with µ typically O(log d)) instead of the standard O(d^3). The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) shows that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with Cholesky factorization becomes negligible as the algorithm converges, which is a significant improvement over previous attempts at this approach [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015]. 

The experimental results are strong and well-designed. The authors compare Cholesky-CMA-ES against state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants on standard benchmark functions across multiple dimensions (d = 4 to 256). The results show that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but with significantly reduced wall-clock time (up to 20x faster for d=64). The speedup increases with dimensionality, which is exactly where the standard CMA-ES struggles most. The paper also demonstrates that the approach maintains rotation invariance, which is crucial for optimization performance.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from previous work, and the related work section is comprehensive. The theoretical justification, while not exhaustive, is sufficient to support their claims about the convergence properties.

The significance of this work is substantial. CMA-ES is widely used in machine learning applications like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning, where computational efficiency is critical. By reducing the runtime complexity while maintaining performance, this approach enables CMA-ES to scale to higher-dimensional problems where it was previously computationally prohibitive. This has clear practical implications for practitioners working with derivative-free optimization.

The main limitation is that the theoretical justification doesn't fully address how the approximation error might affect convergence on non-convex or highly ill-conditioned problems. While Lemma 1 provides a strong argument for the error diminishing as the algorithm converges, a more detailed analysis of the error's impact on convergence rates for challenging optimization landscapes would strengthen the paper. However, the empirical results on standard benchmark functions are convincing, and the paper acknowledges this as a direction for future work.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, practical innovation that NIPS values. It takes a well-established algorithm, identifies a significant computational bottleneck, and proposes a theoretically grounded solution that maintains performance while improving efficiency. The authors have demonstrated both the theoretical soundness and empirical effectiveness of their approach, making a clear contribution to the field.

The paper is of high quality, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the practical application of evolutionary algorithms. The novelty of the approach is clear, and the significance is well-established through both theoretical arguments and empirical results. While there's room for deeper theoretical analysis of the approximation error, the paper's practical contributions and strong empirical validation outweigh this limitation.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It presents a novel, practical solution to a real computational bottleneck in a widely used algorithm, with clear implications for machine learning practitioners. The authors have successfully bridged theory and practice, and their approach has the potential to significantly impact how CMA-ES is applied in high-dimensional optimization problems.
