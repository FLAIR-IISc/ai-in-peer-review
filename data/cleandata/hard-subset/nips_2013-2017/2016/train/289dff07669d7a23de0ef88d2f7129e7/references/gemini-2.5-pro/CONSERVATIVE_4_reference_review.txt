This paper presents Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the well-established Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm. The primary contribution is a method to reduce the computational complexity of the algorithm from O(d³) per iteration, dominated by the eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix, to an asymptotically optimal O(µd²). The key idea is to maintain and directly update a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, avoiding the need for repeated decompositions. This is not a new concept in itself, as the authors acknowledge prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015). However, the main challenge in applying this to the standard CMA-ES lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the term C⁻¹/², the inverse square root of the covariance matrix. The authors' central proposal is to approximate this term with A⁻¹, where A is the triangular Cholesky factor. They provide a theoretical argument, centered on Lemma 1, suggesting that the error introduced by this approximation diminishes as the shape of the covariance matrix converges. The paper validates this approach empirically on several standard benchmark functions, showing a significant reduction in wall-clock time with no discernible degradation in the number of function evaluations required for convergence compared to a reference CMA-ES implementation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper addresses a well-known and significant limitation of the CMA-ES, namely its poor scaling with dimensionality. By providing a method that drastically reduces the wall-clock time for moderately high dimensions (d=64 to 256), the work makes a strong practical contribution. Given the prevalence of CMA-ES in fields like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization, an effective method for speeding it up is highly valuable to the community.

2.  **Methodological Soundness of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is well-conducted. The authors compare their method against appropriate and strong baselines, including the standard reference implementation with a delayed update schedule (CMA-ES-Ref). The choice of standard benchmark functions is appropriate, and the metrics used (function evaluations and wall-clock time) correctly separate the effects of the approximation on optimization performance from the computational speedup. The results presented in Figures 1 and 2 are clear and compelling in demonstrating the practical advantage of the proposed method.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the problem, situate their work within the existing literature, and present their proposed modification and its justification in a logical sequence. Algorithm 1 provides a clear summary of the final method.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Theoretical Justification of the Core Approximation:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the theoretical justification for approximating C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹. Lemma 1 proves that the rotational difference between successive decompositions (Eᵀₜ₋₁Eₜ) approaches the identity matrix *under the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges*. The authors themselves correctly state, "As the CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm, we cannot assume convergence of Ct." This admission significantly undermines the formal claim. The argument then retreats to a more heuristic one: the error is small if Ct changes slowly. This is plausible but lacks rigor. The performance of the algorithm is most critical precisely when the landscape requires rapid adaptation of the covariance matrix, which is when the "changes slowly" assumption is least likely to hold. The paper would be stronger if it either provided a more robust analysis (e.g., bounding the error) or was more circumspect about the theoretical guarantees, framing the contribution primarily as a highly effective and empirically validated heuristic.

2.  **Originality:** While the application is novel and impactful, the core components are not entirely new. The O(d²) Cholesky update has been explored previously. The main novelty lies in the specific approximation for the CSA path and the accompanying theoretical argument. This makes the contribution more of a clever and important engineering improvement upon an established method rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic concept. This is not necessarily a fatal flaw, but it does temper the originality of the work.

3.  **Scope of Empirical Validation:** The benchmark functions used, while standard, are largely well-behaved unimodal functions (Sphere, Ellipsoid, Cigar) or have predictable structure (Rosenbrock). The experiments do not explore scenarios specifically designed to challenge the core approximation. For example, performance on highly multi-modal or noisy functions, where the covariance matrix might be expected to change shape erratically without converging, is not demonstrated. An experiment on a function designed to induce rapid rotation of the principal axes of the covariance matrix would have provided a more strenuous test of the central assumption and increased confidence in the method's robustness.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a significant, practical computational bottleneck in a canonical optimization algorithm.
    *   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES provides a substantial, empirically verified speedup in wall-clock time.
    *   The experiments show no significant loss in optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations) on a range of standard benchmarks.
    *   The paper is clear, well-structured, and the experimental methodology is sound.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   The theoretical justification for the core approximation is weak, relying on a convergence assumption that is not guaranteed to hold for the algorithm in question.
    *   The conceptual novelty is somewhat limited, representing an incremental (though highly effective) improvement on existing work.
    *   The robustness of the approximation has not been tested in adversarial scenarios where the covariance matrix may not change slowly or converge in shape.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a valuable practical improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm. The demonstrated speedup is significant and makes the method applicable to higher-dimensional problems where the standard implementation would be prohibitively slow. However, the theoretical claims are stronger than what is actually proven, as the justification for the key approximation rests on an unproven assumption of convergence. Despite this theoretical weakness, the empirical evidence is strong enough to support the utility of the proposed method. The work is a solid piece of engineering that will likely be of interest and use to practitioners. I recommend acceptance, but I would urge the authors to tone down the language regarding the theoretical guarantees in the final version and more explicitly acknowledge the heuristic nature of the approximation in the absence of a proven convergence result.
