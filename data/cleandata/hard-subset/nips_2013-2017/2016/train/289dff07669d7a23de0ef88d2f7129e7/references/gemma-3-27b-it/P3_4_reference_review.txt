## Review of CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable and technically sound improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm, focusing on reducing computational complexity without sacrificing performance. The Cholesky-CMA-ES variant offers a compelling alternative, particularly for higher-dimensional problems where the matrix square root computation becomes a bottleneck. The theoretical justification is well-presented, and the empirical results clearly demonstrate the benefits in terms of runtime. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper addresses the significant computational cost of covariance matrix adaptation within the CMA-ES framework, specifically the O(d<sup>2</sup>) complexity associated with the matrix square root.  The authors propose utilizing the Cholesky factorization instead, leveraging recent work on updating the Cholesky factor directly (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015).  However, crucially, they recognize and address the theoretical concerns associated with directly applying these prior techniques to the standard CMA-ES, which relies on the matrix square root. Their contribution lies in providing a theoretical justification for this substitution, demonstrating that the approximation introduced by avoiding the correction factor is acceptable, especially with increasing iterations.  They also highlight the benefits regarding memory footprint and potential for faster inversion.

The paper situates itself well within the existing literature on CMA-ES optimization, clearly referencing Hansen & Ostermeier (2001) and subsequent improvements. The discussion of previous efforts to reduce complexity, such as delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) and diagonal matrix restrictions (Ros & Hansen, 2008), is comprehensive and appropriately contextualizes the contribution of this work. They also properly acknowledge related work on alternative optimization strategies, showing awareness of the broader field.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical justification provided for using the Cholesky factorization is convincing and well-articulated. Lemma 1 provides a strong argument for the convergence of the approximation. The analysis of the error introduced by omitting the correction factor is insightful.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm demonstrably reduces runtime complexity to O(µd<sup>2</sup>), offering significant speedups, especially in higher dimensions, as evidenced by the experimental results.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental evaluation across a variety of benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) provides strong evidence for the efficacy of the Cholesky-CMA-ES. The clear presentation of results, including runtime comparisons and scalability analysis, is commendable.
* **Practical Benefits:** The reduction in memory footprint is an additional practical advantage, particularly for large-scale optimization problems.  The ability to efficiently compute the inverse of the Cholesky factor is also a notable benefit.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to CMA-ES-Ref and CMA-ES/d is valuable, a more thorough comparison to other recently developed CMA-ES variants, such as those employing low-rank approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015), would strengthen the paper.  It's unclear how this method fares against algorithms specifically designed for very high dimensions.
* **Discussion of the Error Term:** While the paper provides a theoretical justification for the approximation, a more detailed discussion of the practical impact of the error term introduced by removing the correction factor would be beneficial. What is the typical magnitude of this error in practice, and how does it affect convergence speed?
* **Cache Effects:** The authors acknowledge cache effects influencing performance at d=256.  A more in-depth analysis of the impact of cache size and memory access patterns on the runtime of the Cholesky-CMA-ES would be helpful.
* **Rotation Invariance:** While the authors mention testing rotation invariance, the results presented in the supplementary material are not fully integrated into the main text and their significance isn't explicitly discussed.  More discussion on the practical implications of the observed error in rotation invariance would strengthen the paper.



**4. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Significant reduction in runtime complexity with no detrimental effect on performance (number of function evaluations).
* Strong theoretical justification for the proposed approximation.
* Comprehensive experimental validation demonstrating the benefits of the algorithm.
* Addresses a practical bottleneck in CMA-ES, making it more scalable to higher dimensions.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Comparison to a broader range of state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants could be more comprehensive.
*  Further discussion of the error term and its practical impact would be beneficial.




**5.  Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Expand the Experimental Evaluation:**  Include comparisons to more recent CMA-ES variants, especially those designed for high-dimensional problems.
* **Detailed Error Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the error introduced by the approximation, potentially including a sensitivity analysis.
* **Clarify Cache Effects:**  Discuss the impact of cache size and memory access patterns on performance.
* **Integrate Rotation Invariance Results:**  Fully integrate the results from the supplementary material into the main text and provide a more thorough discussion of their implications.




**Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES variant offers a compelling improvement to the standard algorithm, and the theoretical justification and empirical validation are both convincing.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.**
