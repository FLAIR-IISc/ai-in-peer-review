Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T instead of recomputing a matrix square root/eigendecomposition each iteration. The rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates are decomposed into sequential rank-one modifications to A using a quadratic-time “cholupdate”-style routine, reducing the covariance update complexity to O(µ d^2) and storage to O(d^2). For step-size adaptation (CSA), the authors replace the standard whitened step C^{-1/2}(m_{t+1}-m_t) with A^{-1}(m_{t+1}-m_t), thereby omitting an orthogonal factor E_t that appears in the RQ decomposition of C^{1/2} = A E. They argue that the error introduced by dropping E_t is small and vanishes asymptotically under convergence of the normalized covariance shape. Experiments on standard black-box benchmarking functions compare the new method against a reference CMA-ES (with delayed decomposition), a “delay by d” variant, and an adaptation of Suttorp et al.’s non-triangular update. The authors report essentially identical numbers of function evaluations and substantial wall-clock speedups increasing with d.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical bottleneck in CMA-ES by avoiding repeated eigendecompositions; the O(µ d^2) update and O(d^2) storage are compelling for full-covariance strategies.
- Extends prior work on rank-one Cholesky updates (e.g., Krause & Igel 2015, Suttorp et al. 2009) to the standard µ > 1 CMA-ES.
- The algorithmic idea is simple and potentially impactful for practitioners when full covariance is desired.
- Empirical results suggest similar evaluation efficiency and major runtime savings for moderate d.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and theory
- Major factual error: “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. For C = A A^T, the diagonal of A is not equal to sqrt of the eigenvalues of C; eigenvalues of C are the squares of the singular values of A, which are not given by diag(A). Thus, the claimed “eigenvalues for free” is incorrect and undermines a stated advantage.
- The theoretical justification for dropping the orthogonal factor in CSA is weak. Lemma 1 assumes convergence of the normalized covariance shape and then uses continuity/“uniqueness” of the RQ decomposition to argue E_t^T E_{t-1} → I, implying that the weighted error in the norm of pσ vanishes. Issues:
  - RQ/QR is not unique without fixing sign conventions; continuity needs conditions (e.g., positive diagonal) that are not stated. The lemma’s claim of uniqueness because det C̄ ≠ 0 is wrong.
  - Convergence of C̄_t is a strong assumption; CMA-ES in practice can operate far from such a regime (multimodal functions, noise, non-stationary adaptation). No non-asymptotic bounds are provided on the deviation between norms with and without E_t, and no robustness analysis for slowly/non-converging shapes.
  - The argument only concerns the norm, not the distributional properties of pσ on which CSA formulas and the E{χ} calibration rely.
- “Asymptotically optimal” runtime claim is not rigorously justified. It is plausible that updating a dense full covariance (or its factor) cannot be done sub-quadratically per rank-1 addition, but no formal lower bound is provided; the footnote about matrix multiplication is not sufficient.
- Algorithm 2 (rankOneUpdate) is presented without derivation/justification, numerical stability discussion, or references to standard cholupdate/downdate literature. Conditions on β (here positive) and accumulated rounding errors are not analyzed. This is important for long runs in high d.

Experimental methodology
- The main baseline (CMA-ES-Ref) computes the matrix square root every max{1, 1/(10 d (c1+cµ))} iterations; authors state this equals one for their dimensions, i.e., eigendecomposition every iteration. This seems harsher than common delayed-update strategies; please clarify whether this matches the canonical reference code (Hansen’s implementation) and why stronger delays weren’t used to reduce the amortized cubic cost.
- The adaptation of Suttorp et al.’s non-triangular approach to standard CMA-ES is mentioned but not described in sufficient detail to assess fairness or correctness.
- For Rosenbrock, trials that did not converge to the global optimum were excluded. This biases runtime/function evaluation statistics; success rates should be reported, and unsuccessful runs should be included (e.g., with a budget cap).
- No numerical tables/values are reported for function evaluations or times (only figures are referenced), and the supplementary material with rotation metrics is not available here; difficult to verify claims or reproduce.
- Key settings are under-specified: population size λ and µ selection across d, termination criteria, noise-handling (if any), lower-bound enforcement on variances, handling of numerical drift in A (e.g., periodic reconditioning), and whether triangular solves (preferred) or explicit inversion were used for A^{-1} in CSA.
- Rotation invariance: While you say you rotated all problems, you do not compare performance with and without rotation to quantify any loss due to omitting E_t in CSA. Given the theory is asymptotic and heuristic, a careful invariance test is needed.

Clarity and presentation
- There is confusion between “Cholesky factor of C” and “triangular factor of C^{1/2} via RQ”. Early on, H = C^{1/2} = A E with QR/RQ is used, and later A denotes the Cholesky factor of C. These are different objects; the notation should be disambiguated consistently.
- Several typographical/formatting issues:
  - References are severely malformed (e.g., “Akimoto et al.,? Q2012E”, “Heidrich.Meisner”), inconsistent punctuation, missing venues/years in places.
  - Stray text: “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)”.
  - Footnotes and inline remarks break the flow (“Actually, the complexity is related …”, “Given cc, …”), inconsistent notation (bars/overlines), and occasional grammatical errors (“can not”).
  - Algorithm 1 suggests computing A^{-1}_t explicitly; better to state triangular solve to avoid unnecessary inversion.
- The paper claims “eigenvalues for free,” which as noted is incorrect and suggests a misunderstanding that should be corrected.

Originality and significance
- The idea to update a Cholesky factor directly is not new; the contribution here is to extend it to full CMA-ES with µ > 1 and to approximate CSA by using A^{-1} instead of C^{-1/2}. This is incremental but practically meaningful if done correctly.
- Potential impact is substantial for practitioners, but the theoretical justification is currently not convincing, and the empirical evaluation leaves questions about fairness and robustness.

Questions for the authors
1. Please correct the eigenvalue claim and clarify what spectral information, if any, you can obtain cheaply from A.
2. Can you provide non-asymptotic bounds on the error in CSA due to dropping E_t, or at least empirical evidence comparing CSA statistics (norm of pσ, success rates) with/without rotation over time?
3. What exact delayed decomposition policy did you use for CMA-ES-Ref, and how does it compare to the recommended one in Hansen’s code for your d range?
4. How do you maintain numerical stability of A over long runs (e.g., reconditioning, jitter, enforcing positive diagonals, sign conventions in RQ)?
5. Please provide success rates and include unsuccessful runs in statistics (e.g., with timeouts), especially on Rosenbrock and ill-conditioned functions.

Pros
- Practical speedups with full covariance while preserving evaluation efficiency.
- Clean algorithmic integration and plausible O(µ d^2) updates.
- Addresses a well-known bottleneck.

Cons
- Conceptual mistake about eigenvalues from Cholesky diagonal.
- Weak and partly incorrect theoretical argument (uniqueness/continuity issues; strong convergence assumptions).
- Experimental evaluation lacks important details and fairness checks; biased exclusion of failures.
- Presentation/formatting issues and unclear notation.

Recommendation: Reject
While the practical idea is appealing, the paper currently contains a significant technical error, an insufficiently rigorous theoretical justification for the CSA approximation, and several experimental and presentation deficiencies. I encourage resubmission after correcting the eigenvalue claim, strengthening theory (or providing robust empirical invariance analyses), clarifying baselines, reporting success rates, and fixing notation and references.
