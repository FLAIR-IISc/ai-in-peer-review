### **Summary**

This paper addresses the well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard algorithm's runtime is dominated by the O(d³) cost of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix in each iteration, which is prohibitive for high-dimensional problems or problems with fast-to-evaluate objective functions. The authors propose "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant that maintains and directly updates the Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, reducing the complexity to O(µd²), which is a significant improvement as µ is typically small.

The main technical challenge, and the paper's core contribution, is handling the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. CSA requires the matrix `C⁻¹/²`, which is not directly available from the Cholesky factor `A` (where `C = AAᵀ`). The authors propose approximating `C⁻¹/²` with `A⁻¹`. They provide a novel theoretical justification for this approximation, arguing via Lemma 1 that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this approximation vanishes. Empirical results on a suite of benchmark functions demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a substantial wall-clock time speedup over the standard implementation without degrading optimization performance in terms of function evaluations.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a long-standing and highly practical limitation of one of the most powerful black-box optimization algorithms. CMA-ES is widely used in machine learning for tasks like hyperparameter tuning and direct policy search in reinforcement learning. Making it computationally feasible for higher dimensions significantly broadens its applicability. The demonstrated speedups (e.g., 20x at d=64) are substantial and compelling.

2.  **Novelty and Technical Quality:** While prior work has explored updating matrix factorizations for CMA-ES variants, this paper is the first to successfully integrate it into the standard rank-µ CMA-ES while providing a sound theoretical justification for the necessary approximation. The argument presented in Lemma 1, based on the convergence of the RQ decomposition of the normalized covariance matrix, is elegant and provides strong intuition for why replacing `C⁻¹/²` with `A⁻¹` in the CSA norm calculation is a viable strategy. This insight is a key contribution that has eluded previous attempts.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly motivates the problem, provides the necessary background, methodically develops the proposed algorithm, and presents the supporting theoretical argument and empirical results in a clear and logical manner. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and 2 makes the proposed method easy to understand and implement.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is robust and convincing. The authors compare their method against appropriate baselines, including the reference implementation and a delayed-update strategy. They use a standard set of benchmark functions and importantly, test for rotation invariance, which is a key property of CMA-ES. The results, showing equivalent performance in function evaluations but drastically reduced runtime, strongly support the paper's claims.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Incorrect Claim Regarding Eigenvalues:** The paper makes a factually incorrect claim in Section 3: *"the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix, that is, we get the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free."* This is only true if the covariance matrix `C` is diagonal. For a general symmetric positive definite matrix, the diagonal elements of its Cholesky factor `A` are not directly related to the eigenvalues of `C`. Computing the eigenvalues of `C` still requires an O(d³) operation (e.g., from an SVD of `A`). This error undermines one of the listed advantages of the proposed method. The authors should correct this statement, as it weakens the argument for the superiority of the triangular factorization but does not invalidate the paper's primary contribution of computational speedup.

2.  **Nature of the Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical argument in Lemma 1 is insightful but relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix `C̄t` converges. The authors rightly note that this is not guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm, although the expectation may converge. The justification is therefore heuristic rather than a formal proof that the approximation does not alter the algorithm's convergence properties. While the empirical results provide strong evidence that this is not an issue in practice, the theoretical claims should be phrased with this nuance in mind.

3.  **Comparison with Suttorp-CMA-ES:** The paper introduces a baseline "Suttorp-CMA-ES" based on a non-triangular factorization but provides limited discussion of its performance relative to the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES. The plots show it is slower, but the reasons are not explicitly analyzed. A more direct discussion of why the triangular factorization leads to a faster implementation in practice would strengthen the paper's claims about the benefits of their specific approach.

### **Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The primary contribution—a practical, well-justified, and empirically validated method for dramatically speeding up the standard CMA-ES algorithm—is of high significance to both the optimization and machine learning communities. The work is novel, technically sound, and clearly presented. The weaknesses, particularly the factual error concerning eigenvalues, are correctable and do not detract from the core value of the proposed algorithm and its impressive performance gains. This work represents a major practical advancement for a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization.
