### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the super-quadratic time complexity of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art algorithm for derivative-free black-box optimization. The computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES is the repeated decomposition of the `d x d` covariance matrix `C`, which is typically an O(d^3) operation and dominates the runtime when the objective function is cheap to evaluate or the dimensionality `d` is large.

The authors propose a variant, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," that reduces the complexity of the covariance matrix update to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for a full-rank update. The core idea is to maintain and directly update a triangular Cholesky factor `A_t` (where `C_t = A_t A_t^T`), thereby avoiding the need to form and re-decompose `C_t` in each iteration. This approach builds on previous work (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) that has explored direct updates for factorizations. However, a key challenge in applying this to the standard CMA-ES is the step-size adaptation mechanism (CSA), which requires the term `C_t^{-1/2}` to normalize steps. A Cholesky factor `A_t` is not equivalent to the matrix square root `C_t^{1/2}`. The authors' main contribution is to propose an approximation where `C_t^{-1/2}` is replaced by `A_t^{-1}`. They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) for this approximation, arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the rotational component difference between `A_t` and `C_t^{1/2}` also converges, and the error introduced by the approximation vanishes due to the exponential weighting in the CSA path.

The paper provides empirical validation on several benchmark optimization problems, comparing the Cholesky-CMA-ES to the reference CMA-ES implementation and other fast variants. The results show that the proposed algorithm achieves a significant speed-up in terms of wall-clock time without compromising the convergence rate in terms of function evaluations.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness)**

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The proposed algorithm appears technically sound. The method of updating the Cholesky factor via a series of rank-one updates is a valid and efficient procedure.
    *   The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors compare against appropriate baselines on a suite of standard benchmark functions across a range of dimensions. The results robustly support the central claim that the algorithm reduces runtime without harming optimization performance.
    *   The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 is a valuable contribution. It offers a principled reason for why the approximation in the step-size update should work well, particularly as the algorithm converges. The reasoning, based on the continuity of the RQ-decomposition and the convergence of the rotation matrices, is plausible.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The argument in Lemma 1 rests on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix `C̄_t` converges. While the authors cite work showing convergence of the expectation of `C_t` in simplified settings, this assumption is not guaranteed for the stochastic CMA-ES on arbitrary functions. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the expected performance degradation if `C̄_t` does not converge but oscillates or changes rapidly.
    *   There is a significant factual error in Section 3 under "Properties of the update rule." The authors claim that "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix." This is incorrect; this property holds only for diagonal matrices. Furthermore, the claim that eigenvalues are obtained "for free" is misleading. The entire purpose of the proposed method is to *avoid* the eigenvalue decomposition, which is the standard way to compute the eigenvalues. This method forgoes direct access to the eigenvalues in exchange for speed. This section should be corrected.

**Clarity**

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the background is concise, and the proposed method is explained logically.
    *   The authors clearly identify the key technical challenge (the `C_t^{-1/2}` term) and how their proposed approximation addresses it.
    *   The use of figures to present the experimental results is effective, clearly illustrating the performance trade-offs between the different algorithms in terms of both function evaluations and wall-clock time.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   Aside from the technical inaccuracy mentioned under Quality, the paper is very clear.

**Originality**

*   **Strengths:**
    *   While the idea of updating a factorization of the covariance matrix is not new, this work is the first to successfully apply it to the modern, standard CMA-ES algorithm in a theoretically justified manner.
    *   The key novelty lies in the analysis and handling of the step-size adaptation path (`p_σ`). Previous works either focused on simpler CMA-ES variants where this was not an issue or used non-triangular updates that may lack the uniqueness and stability properties of the proposed approach.
    *   This work successfully resolves a long-standing practical hurdle for the standard CMA-ES, making it a novel and complete contribution.

**Significance**

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization with numerous applications in machine learning (e.g., policy search, hyperparameter tuning). Its high computational complexity is a major practical limitation.
    *   This paper presents a solution that dramatically reduces this complexity, extending the viability of CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems where the function evaluation is not the sole bottleneck.
    *   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES can serve as a near drop-in replacement for the standard algorithm, offering substantial performance benefits. This high practical utility makes it very likely that the method will be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners.
    *   By achieving the optimal O(µd^2) time and O(d^2) storage complexity for a full-covariance update, the work represents a significant advance in the practical implementation of evolution strategies.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  The paper solves a well-known and important practical problem: the high computational cost of the standard CMA-ES algorithm.
2.  The proposed method is technically sound and is supported by strong, convincing empirical evidence across multiple benchmark problems and dimensions.
3.  The work contains a novel theoretical justification for the approximation required to make the fast update scheme compatible with the standard algorithm's step-size control.
4.  The contribution is of high significance to the NIPS community, particularly for practitioners using CMA-ES for hyperparameter optimization or reinforcement learning.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

1.  The paper contains a factual error regarding the relationship between a Cholesky factor and matrix eigenvalues, which misrepresents a property of the algorithm.
2.  The theoretical justification relies on a convergence assumption that may not hold in all practical scenarios.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

This paper presents a valuable and practical contribution to the field of black-box optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES significantly improves the runtime of the standard CMA-ES without sacrificing its excellent optimization performance. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and supported by solid empirical results and a novel theoretical argument. The weaknesses are minor; the factual error regarding eigenvalues is easily correctable and does not invalidate the core algorithm or its performance. The assumption in the theoretical analysis is reasonable, though its limits could be discussed more explicitly.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. The paper's high significance and practical utility make it a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept. (Conditional on correcting the error regarding eigenvalues in Section 3).
