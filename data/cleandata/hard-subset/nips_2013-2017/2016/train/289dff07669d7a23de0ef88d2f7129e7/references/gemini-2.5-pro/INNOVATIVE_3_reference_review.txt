**Summary:**
This paper addresses the primary computational bottleneck of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a prominent algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity dominated by the O(d^3) eigenvalue decomposition required to compute the matrix square root C^(-1/2) for the step-size adaptation mechanism. The authors propose a new variant, the Cholesky-CMA-ES, which circumvents this expensive computation by directly maintaining and updating a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. The key innovation is an approximation for the step-size update path, replacing the true matrix square root with the inverse of the Cholesky factor. The paper provides a theoretical justification for this approximation, arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this substitution (which amounts to a rotation) vanishes. This modification reduces the update complexity to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for a full-rank covariance matrix. Empirical results on a suite of benchmark functions demonstrate that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a dramatic speedup in wall-clock time compared to standard implementations, without a significant change in the number of function evaluations required for convergence.

**Strengths:**
*   **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a long-standing and fundamental problem in a cornerstone algorithm of black-box optimization. The O(d^3) complexity of the covariance matrix update has been a major barrier to applying CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems. This work presents a novel and elegant solution that is not merely an incremental tweak but a fundamental redesign of the update mechanism. The potential impact is substantial, as it makes the full-power CMA-ES algorithm practical for a wider range of problems, particularly in machine learning applications like policy search where the dimensionality can be large but not so large as to necessitate low-rank approximations.
*   **Bold and Well-Justified Approach:** Previous attempts to use Cholesky updates with the standard CMA-ES have been stymied by the specific requirement of the C^(-1/2) term in the step-size adaptation. The authors' decision to use an approximation (A_t^-1) is a bold one, and it is precisely this kind of creative leap that drives the field forward. Crucially, this is not an arbitrary heuristic. The theoretical argument provided in Lemma 1, showing that the introduced rotational error diminishes as the matrix shape converges, provides a strong and intuitive foundation for why this approximation should work well in practice. This is an excellent example of theory-informed algorithm design.
*   **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The experimental results are clear and compelling. The authors compare their method not only against the reference implementation but also against other common speedup strategies (like delayed updates). The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a massive reduction in runtime—becoming 20x faster at d=64—while preserving the sample efficiency (i.e., the number of function evaluations) of the original algorithm. This provides convincing proof of the method's practical utility.
*   **Optimal Complexity:** The paper achieves asymptotically optimal time complexity for the update (O(µd^2)) and optimal storage complexity by using a triangular matrix. This is a strong theoretical result that solidifies the contribution. The additional benefits, such as obtaining eigenvalues for free and the numerical stability of inverting a triangular matrix, are valuable practical advantages.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Reliance on Convergence Assumption:** The theoretical justification for the core approximation hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges to a fixed shape. While this is a reasonable expectation on many problems where CMA-ES performs well, the algorithm itself is stochastic and this convergence is not guaranteed. The paper could be strengthened by a discussion of potential failure modes or problem classes where the matrix shape might oscillate, leading to a persistent error from the approximation. However, given the strong empirical results, this appears to be more of a theoretical incompleteness than a practical flaw.
*   **Synthetic Benchmarks:** The experiments are conducted on standard, but synthetic, optimization benchmarks. While this is necessary for a clean comparison and validation of the algorithm's properties, a demonstration on a real-world machine learning problem (e.g., hyperparameter tuning for a large model or a non-trivial reinforcement learning policy search task) would have made the paper's significance even more apparent.

**Recommendation:**
I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It is a standout contribution that presents a novel, bold, and highly effective solution to a significant, well-known limitation in one of the most important algorithms in derivative-free optimization. The authors have successfully traded a theoretically exact but computationally prohibitive step for a well-justified approximation that is empirically shown to work flawlessly, unlocking dramatic performance gains. This is precisely the kind of work that NIPS should champion: a clever idea that pushes the boundary of what is practical, supported by both theoretical reasoning and strong empirical validation. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core value of the contribution. This work will likely be highly influential and widely adopted by practitioners and researchers who use CMA-ES.
