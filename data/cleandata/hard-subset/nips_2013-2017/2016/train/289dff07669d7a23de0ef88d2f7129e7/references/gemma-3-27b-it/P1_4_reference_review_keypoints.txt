- Proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES: replace covariance matrix square root with Cholesky factor updates to reduce complexity to O(µ d^2).
- Provides theoretical justification (Lemma 1) that the approximation preserves convergence and coordinate stability.
- Empirical results show comparable function-evaluation performance to CMA-ES with significantly faster wall-clock time, especially in higher dimensions.
- Builds on delayed updates and restricted covariance models and extends Cholesky approaches beyond (1+1) or multi-objective variants.
- Connects to natural gradient optimization and the importance of maintaining covariance information.
- Theoretical justification is a key strength of the paper.
- Achieves substantial computational complexity reduction leading to clear runtime speedups.
- Reduces memory footprint by storing and updating only the Cholesky factor.
- Empirically preserves performance in terms of function evaluations.
- Paper is well-written with clear explanations of algorithmic and theoretical details.
- Experiments lack comprehensive comparison to other fast covariance-update methods (e.g., LM-CMA).
- The claimed benefit depends on µ ≪ d; impact of different µ values is not thoroughly analyzed.
- Performance drops at d=256 due to cache limits, indicating practical issues in very high dimensions.
- Algorithm 2 (rankOneUpdate) is relatively complex and may hinder practical implementation and optimization.
- Discussion of the impact on step-size adaptation (replacing st with ŝt) is insufficient and lacks supporting experiments.
- Paper is technically sound with claims supported by theory and experiments.
- The approach is novel in applying Cholesky updates to the full CMA-ES with theoretical backing.
- Work is significant for enabling more efficient CMA-ES use on larger-scale problems.
- Reviewer recommends acceptance with minor revisions to address comparisons, µ impact, and high-dimension optimizations.
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically sound, runtime speedup without performance loss, addresses covariance update bottleneck, clear exposition.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited method comparisons, insufficient study of µ dependency, potential implementation and high-dimension performance issues.
