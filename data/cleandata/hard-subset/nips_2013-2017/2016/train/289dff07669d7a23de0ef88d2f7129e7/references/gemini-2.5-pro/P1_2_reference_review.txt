### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a major practical limitation of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES): its computational complexity in high dimensions. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a per-iteration complexity that is super-quadratic in the problem dimension `d` (typically `O(d^3)` due to eigenvalue decomposition) which becomes a bottleneck when objective function evaluations are cheap.

The authors propose a new variant, the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which reduces this complexity to `O(μd^2)`, asymptotically optimal for an algorithm that maintains a full covariance matrix. The core idea is to maintain and directly update the triangular Cholesky factor `A` of the covariance matrix `C` (where `C = AA^T`), rather than updating `C` and re-decomposing it in every step.

The main technical challenge, and the paper's key contribution, is handling the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) path, which requires a normalization term `C^{-1/2}`. Previous attempts to use Cholesky factors struggled with this, as the inverse of a Cholesky factor, `A^{-1}`, is not equivalent to `C^{-1/2}`. The authors identify that `C^{-1/2}` and `A^{-1}` differ by an orthogonal transformation (a rotation). They then provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, this rotational difference between successive steps vanishes. This justifies approximating `C^{-1/2}` with `A^{-1}` in the CSA update, as the error introduced by this approximation diminishes over time due to the exponential weighting in the path update.

The paper provides strong empirical evidence on several benchmark functions, showing that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations) as the standard CMA-ES, while being significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time. The speedup becomes more pronounced as the dimensionality `d` increases, demonstrating the practical value of the proposed method.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and important bottleneck in one of the most powerful and widely used derivative-free optimization algorithms. By reducing the time complexity without compromising optimization performance, this work makes CMA-ES practical for a larger class of problems, particularly in machine learning (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, policy search) where `d` can be large and function evaluations relatively fast. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES could plausibly become the new standard implementation.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Elegance:** The proposed solution is elegant and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the core technical issue preventing a straightforward application of Cholesky updates (the `C^{-1/2}` term in CSA). The justification for their approximation via Lemma 1 is convincing. While not a formal proof of convergence, it provides a strong theoretical rationale for why the approximation should work well in practice, linking the error to the rate of change of the covariance matrix's orientation. This is a deeper insight than in previous related works.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is strong. The authors compare their method not only to a reference implementation but also to other sensible baselines, like the delayed-update strategy and a prior non-triangular Cholesky approach. The choice of benchmark functions is appropriate, covering different conditioning and separability properties. The results are presented clearly and compellingly demonstrate the claimed speedup while showing no degradation in the number of function evaluations required for convergence.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly motivates the problem, reviews the necessary background on CMA-ES, explains the technical challenge, and presents the proposed solution and its justification in a logical, easy-to-follow manner. The algorithms are clearly stated. This high level of clarity makes the contribution easy to understand and appreciate.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Nature of the Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical argument hinges on the assumption that the shape of the covariance matrix converges, which is not formally guaranteed for the randomized CMA-ES algorithm. The authors are honest about this, stating "we cannot assume convergence of Ct". The justification is therefore more of a well-reasoned heuristic than a rigorous proof. While the strong empirical results largely mitigate this, it remains a slight limitation from a purely theoretical standpoint.

2.  **Incremental Originality:** The general idea of directly updating a factorization of the covariance matrix is not entirely new, and the authors correctly cite prior work (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009). The main novelty lies in correctly adapting this idea to the modern (μ,λ)-CMA-ES by providing a principled and effective solution to the CSA update problem, which had been a major stumbling block. The contribution is therefore a crucial and clever refinement that makes the approach truly viable, rather than a completely new paradigm.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
*   Solves a significant, practical performance bottleneck in a foundational algorithm (CMA-ES).
*   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is significantly faster in wall-clock time with no measurable loss in optimization quality.
*   Provides an elegant and well-reasoned technical solution to a non-trivial problem (the CSA update).
*   The work is supported by strong, clear, and comprehensive empirical results.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and clear.
*   The contribution is likely to have a high impact, potentially changing the standard way CMA-ES is implemented and used.

**Arguments for Rejection (Cons):**
*   The theoretical justification for the core approximation relies on an assumption of convergence that is not formally proven.
*   The core idea of updating a matrix factor directly is not completely novel, though its successful application to standard CMA-ES is.

### Final Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that presents a clear and significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. It addresses a well-known practical limitation of CMA-ES with an elegant, well-justified, and empirically validated solution. The combination of practical utility, technical insight, and clear presentation makes it a high-quality submission. The weaknesses are minor in comparison to the strengths. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a tool that researchers and practitioners are very likely to use.

**I strongly recommend accepting this paper.**
