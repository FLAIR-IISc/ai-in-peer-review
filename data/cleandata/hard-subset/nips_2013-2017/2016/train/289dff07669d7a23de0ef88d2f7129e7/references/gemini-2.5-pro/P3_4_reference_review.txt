### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, designed to address the algorithm's primary computational bottleneck: the O(d³) complexity associated with decomposing the covariance matrix in each iteration. The authors' core idea is to maintain and update the Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix directly, rather than the matrix itself. This reduces the update complexity to O(µd²), which is a significant improvement as µ is typically logarithmic in the problem dimension d.

The main technical challenge lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the matrix `C^(-1/2)` to normalize the evolution path. A Cholesky factor `A` (where `C = AA^T`) is not equivalent to the matrix square root `C^(1/2)`. The authors' key contribution is to propose an approximation where `A^(-1)` is used in place of `C^(-1/2)` and to provide a theoretical justification for this choice. They argue via Lemma 1 that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the orthogonal matrix relating the Cholesky factor to the matrix square root also converges, causing the error introduced by their approximation to vanish over time.

The paper presents empirical results on several standard benchmark functions, comparing the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES against the reference implementation and other fast variants. The results demonstrate that the new method achieves the same convergence in terms of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, with the speed-up becoming more pronounced as the dimension increases.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Practical Impact:** CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization, widely used in machine learning for tasks like hyperparameter tuning and reinforcement learning. The O(d³) complexity has always been a major barrier to its application in higher dimensions. This work provides a well-justified and effective solution that makes CMA-ES practical for a much wider range of problems, directly addressing a long-standing limitation. This is a highly significant contribution.

2.  **Novel and Principled Approach:** While the idea of updating a matrix factorization directly is not entirely new (as the authors correctly cite), the main novelty lies in the careful handling and justification of the approximation required for the CSA mechanism. Previous works either did not apply to the standard CMA-ES or used non-unique factorizations that lack the convergence guarantees presented here. The theoretical argument in Lemma 1, linking the convergence of the covariance matrix shape to the stability of the approximation, is elegant and provides a strong foundation for the method. This elevates the work beyond a simple engineering trick.

3.  **Technical Soundness and Completeness:** The proposed algorithm is well-described. The paper clearly identifies the core technical problem (the `C^(-1/2)` term), explains why a naive substitution is problematic, and provides a reasoned solution. The derivation is clear and the resulting algorithm is practical. Furthermore, the authors correctly identify several secondary benefits of using a triangular Cholesky factor, such as optimal storage complexity, efficient inversion, and cost-free access to the eigenvalues (via the diagonal elements), which are important for monitoring conditioning.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong. The authors compare their method not only to a naive CMA-ES implementation but also to state-of-the-art variants like CMA-ES-Ref (which uses delayed updates) and the Suttorp et al. approach. This provides a convincing demonstration of superiority. The choice of benchmark functions is standard and covers different problem characteristics (separable, non-separable, ill-conditioned). The key result—that function evaluation counts are preserved while wall-clock time is drastically reduced—is clearly shown and supports the paper's claims perfectly.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Strength of the Theoretical Argument:** The justification in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix `C̄_t` converges. While this is a reasonable assumption for many unimodal problems where the algorithm is converging to an optimum, it may be less valid during the transient phase of optimization or on highly multi-modal landscapes where the search distribution might need to change its shape drastically. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of these limitations. For example, is there a risk of the approximation error accumulating and leading to poor step-size adaptation in such non-stationary scenarios? An empirical analysis showing the evolution of `‖E_t E_{t-1}^T - I‖` during a typical run could provide further compelling evidence for the approximation's validity.

2.  **Scope of Experiments:** While the benchmark experiments are well-executed, the paper motivates its contribution by citing applications in machine learning (policy search, hyperparameter tuning). The significance of the work would be even more impactful if demonstrated on at least one such practical, higher-dimensional ML problem. Showing a significant speed-up on, for instance, a direct policy search task for a standard RL environment would make the paper's conclusions undeniable for the NIPS audience.

3.  **Clarity on "Optimality":** The paper claims "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity". This is true under the important condition that a *full* covariance matrix is adapted. This should be stated more explicitly to avoid confusion with methods that assume a restricted (e.g., diagonal) covariance structure and thus have lower complexity (e.g., O(d)). While the conclusion touches upon this, being more precise in the introduction and abstract would be beneficial.

### Overall Evaluation and Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that addresses a critical and practical problem in a widely used optimization algorithm. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a significant advancement over the standard CMA-ES and its existing variants. The contribution is technically sound, well-motivated by a clear theoretical argument, and convincingly validated through thorough experiments. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to strengthening the discussion and broadening the experimental scope rather than any fundamental flaws. This work is of high quality, originality, and significance, and will likely become the new standard implementation of CMA-ES. It will be of great interest to both optimization researchers and machine learning practitioners.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Solves a major, long-standing computational bottleneck in a cornerstone optimization algorithm (CMA-ES).
*   The proposed method is not just a heuristic but is backed by a principled theoretical argument.
*   Empirical results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate a substantial speed-up without sacrificing optimization performance.
*   The work has high practical value and is likely to be widely adopted by the community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical justification relies on an assumption of convergence which may not hold in all scenarios (a minor point).
*   The experiments, while thorough on benchmarks, do not include a direct demonstration on a real-world ML application.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It is a clear, well-executed, and important contribution to the field.
