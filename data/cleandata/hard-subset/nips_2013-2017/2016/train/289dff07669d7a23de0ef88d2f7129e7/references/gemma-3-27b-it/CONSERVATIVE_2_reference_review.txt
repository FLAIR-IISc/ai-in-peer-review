## Review of CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) termed “Cholesky-CMA-ES,” aimed at reducing runtime and memory complexity during covariance matrix updates. The core idea is to operate directly on the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix, avoiding explicit computation of the matrix square root. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this approach, arguing that it doesn't deteriorate performance, and demonstrate empirical speedups on benchmark functions.

The motivation is sound. The O(d<sup>2</sup>) complexity of covariance matrix updates is a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES, particularly for high-dimensional problems. Previous attempts to address this, such as delayed updates or restricting matrix complexity, have their drawbacks. Utilizing the Cholesky decomposition *directly* during updates, as opposed to periodically recomputing it, is a natural and potentially impactful optimization. The argument connecting this to previous work leveraging Cholesky factors, while acknowledging limitations of applying them to the original CMA-ES, is reasonable.

However, despite the intuitive appeal, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant advancement. While the authors claim “no significant change in the number of objective function evaluations” with reduced runtime, the experiments largely focus on runtime *alone*. The number of function evaluations is arguably the more crucial metric for CMA-ES performance, reflecting the algorithm's sample efficiency. Showing equivalent performance on this metric while achieving speedups is vital. The claim that the theoretical justification prevents performance deterioration feels somewhat hand-wavy without a more detailed analysis of the error introduced by approximating the step-size adaptation.  The Lemma 1, while insightful, feels insufficient to fully alleviate concerns about the impact of the approximation on the global step-size adaptation. The authors acknowledge a potential issue with the scaling behavior in extremely high dimensions (d=256) related to L2 cache size, indicating the benefits might be more limited than presented.

The experimental setup is reasonable, utilizing standard benchmark functions and comparing against relevant CMA-ES variants. However, the presentation of results is somewhat superficial. The figures show clear runtime advantages, but lack detailed statistical analysis (e.g., confidence intervals) to assess the significance of these differences. The additional tests mentioned for evaluating rotation introduced by the algorithm are referred to in the text but not shown. It's unclear if the observed speedups translate into practical improvements across a broader range of real-world applications, which is where CMA-ES excels.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a discussion of potential drawbacks beyond the L2 cache limitation. For example, while the Cholesky factor is numerically stable, the accumulation of small errors during repeated rank-one updates could still impact performance in certain scenarios. A more thorough discussion of the algorithm's limitations and potential failure modes would strengthen the paper.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important bottleneck in CMA-ES.
*   Provides a theoretically grounded approach for Cholesky factor-based updates.
*   Demonstrates empirical speedups on standard benchmark functions.
*   Clear writing and well-defined problem statement.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Insufficient focus on the number of function evaluations, the key performance metric for CMA-ES.
*   Theoretical justification for approximation is not fully convincing.
*   Limited statistical analysis of experimental results.
*   Lack of discussion regarding potential drawbacks and limitations beyond the cache size issue.
*   Experiments could be more comprehensive and include a wider range of problem types.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The approach is intuitively appealing and offers a potential performance improvement for CMA-ES, especially in high dimensions.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem and proposed solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper does not convincingly demonstrate a significant advancement in terms of sample efficiency (function evaluations).
*   The theoretical justification feels incomplete and requires further elaboration.
*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and lacks rigorous statistical analysis.




**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the paper presents a technically sound idea with some potential benefits, it lacks the rigorous validation and comprehensive analysis required for acceptance at NIPS. The emphasis on runtime alone is insufficient, and the theoretical justification needs further strengthening. The authors need to demonstrate convincingly that the Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains the sample efficiency of the original algorithm while achieving its speedups, and provide a more thorough discussion of its limitations. I would encourage the authors to address these concerns and resubmit in the future.
