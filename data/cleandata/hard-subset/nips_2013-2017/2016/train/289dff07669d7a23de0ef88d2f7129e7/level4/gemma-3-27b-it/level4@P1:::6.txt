The paper proposes a variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) designed to improve computational efficiency, particularly in updating and storing the covariance matrix. The authors argue that their proposed modification, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, maintains the algorithm’s performance while reducing runtime and memory footprint. This is achieved by utilizing the triangular Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root during the step-size adaptation process.

The CMA-ES [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001] is a powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm widely used in machine learning for tasks like reinforcement learning policy search and hyperparameter tuning [Gomez et al., 2008; Heidrich-Meisner and Igel, 2009a,b; Igel, 2010].  A key computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES is the repeated calculation and decomposition of the covariance matrix, which scales as O(d<sup>2</sup>) where *d* is the dimensionality of the problem.  Previous work has addressed this through delayed updates [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001], restricted model complexity [Poland and Zell, 2001; Ros and Hansen, 2008; Sun et al., 2013; Akimoto et al., 2014; Loshchilov, 2014, 2015], and updating the Cholesky factor directly [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015].  The current paper builds upon this latter line of research.

The authors present a theoretically justified approach for adapting the step-size in the standard CMA-ES using the Cholesky factor, arguing that the resulting algorithm exhibits optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity. They demonstrate empirically that this Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance to the standard CMA-ES in terms of objective function evaluations, while significantly reducing wall-clock time, especially in higher dimensions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a theoretical argument for why replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor should not degrade the algorithm’s performance, which is a crucial contribution.  The lemma regarding the convergence of rotation matrices is particularly valuable.
*   **Practical Improvement:**  The empirical results demonstrate a clear speedup in runtime for the Cholesky-CMA-ES compared to standard CMA-ES implementations, especially as dimensionality increases. This makes the algorithm more practical for large-scale optimization problems.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured, and the algorithm is presented in a clear and concise manner.  Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 are helpful in understanding the proposed method.
*   **Relevance:** Improving the efficiency of CMA-ES is a valuable contribution, as it is a widely used and powerful optimization algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Naming Convention:** The choice of "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is potentially misleading. This name was previously used by Suttorp et al. [2009] for a related, but distinct, approach. The paper should acknowledge this prior work more explicitly and consider an alternative naming convention to avoid confusion.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the theoretical justification is strong, the core algorithmic contribution – iterating through rank-one updates – feels relatively incremental.  The connection to the work of Krause and Igel [2015] could be more thoroughly discussed and contrasted.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:**  A more direct empirical comparison to the Cholesky-CMA-ES of Suttorp et al. [2009] is needed to fully demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. The paper states that the Suttorp et al. method is prone to numerical instabilities, but this claim should be substantiated with further evidence or references.
*   **Misleading Claim of Optimality:** The claim of “optimal” covariance update complexity is too strong. While the method is efficient, optimality is context-dependent.  For example, on problems with specific structures (e.g., separable functions), simpler approaches targeting those structures might be more efficient.  
*   **Figure 3 Interpretation:** The observation of a plateau in Figure 1b, with differing objective function values between CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES, requires further investigation and explanation. The authors should clarify whether this is a consistent observation and its potential cause.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically effective modification to the CMA-ES algorithm.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a significant runtime improvement, particularly for higher-dimensional problems.
*   The work addresses a key bottleneck in CMA-ES and provides a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The algorithmic contribution is relatively incremental.
*   The naming convention is potentially misleading.
*   A more thorough comparison to related work (particularly Suttorp et al. [2009]) is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the algorithmic innovation is not groundbreaking, the theoretical justification and empirical demonstration of improved efficiency are valuable.  However, I recommend addressing the weaknesses regarding naming, comparison to related work, and the claim of optimality before acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Resolve the naming issue** by considering an alternative name for the proposed algorithm.
2.  **Expand the experimental comparison** to include the Cholesky-CMA-ES of Suttorp et al. [2009] and explicitly contrast the proposed approach with that of Krause and Igel [2015].  Update Figures 1, 2, and 3 accordingly, or move the comprehensive results to the Supplementary Material if space is a concern.
3.  **Clarify the "for" loop's contribution** by better explaining its significance or acknowledging its incremental nature.
4.  **Moderate the claim of "optimal" complexity** to reflect the context-dependent nature of optimization efficiency.
