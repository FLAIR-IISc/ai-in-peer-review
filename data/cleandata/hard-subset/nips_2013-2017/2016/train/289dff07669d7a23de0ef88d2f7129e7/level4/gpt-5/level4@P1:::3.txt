Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a Cholesky-based variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C (with C = A A^T), and uses this factor both for sampling and for updating. The rank-1 and rank-µ updates to C are implemented as a sequence of rank-1 updates to A, yielding an O(µ d^2) update with optimal memory usage (storing only the triangular factor). To avoid computing C^{-1/2} in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), the authors replace it with A^{-1} and justify this approximation by showing that, under convergence of the normalized covariance shape, the orthogonal factor in the RQ decomposition stabilizes, so the induced rotation error in the CSA norm vanishes exponentially over time. Empirically, the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the standard CMA-ES in number of function evaluations while substantially reducing wall-clock time, with speedups that increase with dimension. Comparisons include the standard reference implementation with delayed decomposition, a variant with d-step delay, and a non-triangular Cholesky update (Suttorp et al., 2009). The work builds directly on Krause and Igel (2015), who derived a quadratic-time rank-1 update for the triangular factor (in the µ = λ = 1 setting), and on prior covariance-structure simplifications and delay strategies.

Strengths:
- Practical efficiency: The method achieves O(µ d^2) time per iteration for full-covariance adaptation and minimal memory footprint, which is asymptotically optimal for this setting.
- Empirical performance: Across standard benchmarks (including rotated versions), the algorithm matches the reference CMA-ES in evaluations while being substantially faster in wall-clock time; speedups grow with dimension.
- Numerically advantageous: Maintaining a triangular factor provides stable inversion in O(d^2) and “eigenvalues for free” (diagonal entries), which is useful for monitoring conditioning and enforcing variance bounds.
- Clear link to existing CMA-ES machinery: The algorithm is an implementation-level change that preserves the core behavior, aside from the CSA approximation, which is theoretically motivated and empirically validated.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: Relative to Krause and Igel (2015), the main contribution is a straightforward extension from a rank-1 update to handling the standard rank-1 plus rank-µ update by sequencing rank-1 updates on the triangular factor. This generalization, while useful, is incremental.
- Theory relies on convergence assumptions: The justification for replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} in CSA assumes convergence of the normalized covariance shape so that the orthogonal rotation stabilizes. While this aligns with known results on convergence of the expected covariance in simplified settings (e.g., Beyer, 2014), it is not a proof for the full CMA-ES and does not provide non-asymptotic error bounds.
- Minor issues in presentation: There are typographical errors noted below.

Quality:
The algorithmic derivation is sound, given the known triangular rank-1 update results. The approximation in CSA is carefully argued: the lemma shows that under convergence of the covariance shape, the rotation between successive decompositions tends to identity, and due to exponential weighting in CSA, the residual error in the norm becomes negligible. This is a reasonable argument, and references to convergence results (e.g., Beyer, 2014, though not for the exact CMA-ES) increase confidence in the practical validity of the approach. The empirical evaluation, focusing on number of evaluations and runtime across diverse benchmarks and dimensions, supports the claims. The comparison set is appropriate given the focus on full covariance; the observed cache effects at very high dimensions are candidly reported.

Clarity:
Overall the paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} in CSA and the implications for invariance are explained. The algorithms are presented concretely. A few typographical corrections are needed:
- In equation (1), the denominator should be σ_t (not σ).
- The same σ_t issue appears in footnote 2 on page 3.
- Line 94: “which can by be motivated by principles from …” — remove the extra “by”.

Originality:
The core idea of updating the triangular Cholesky factor is not new; prior work has handled rank-1 updates and related decompositions. The novelty here lies in extending to the full CMA-ES with rank-µ updates via a sequence of rank-1 updates and in proposing and justifying the CSA modification that avoids computing C^{-1/2}. This is a useful engineering contribution, but conceptually incremental.

Significance:
For practitioners who need full covariance adaptation on moderately to highly dimensional problems where maintaining C is still feasible, the proposed method materially reduces runtime without increasing evaluations. This can make CMA-ES more competitive in time-constrained settings (e.g., direct policy search or hyperparameter tuning with cheap function evaluations). The contribution is less impactful for very large d where full covariance CMA-ES is already impractical and low-memory variants are preferred.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Demonstrated large runtime savings with essentially unchanged evaluation counts.
- Asymptotically optimal time and memory complexity for full-covariance CMA-ES.
- Simple, numerically stable implementation; provides ancillary benefits (eigenvalues, easy inversion).
- Empirical validation on standard and rotated benchmarks.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Incremental contribution relative to existing triangular rank-1 update methods; extending to rank-µ via sequential rank-1 updates is straightforward.
- Theoretical justification of the CSA approximation hinges on convergence assumptions and does not provide rigorous guarantees for the exact CMA-ES.
- Minor typographical issues.

Overall assessment:
A solid and practical engineering improvement to CMA-ES with convincing empirical support and a reasonable (though assumption-dependent) theoretical rationale for the CSA modification. The novelty is modest, but the utility is clear. The paper would benefit from fixing the noted typos and from clarifying the scope and limitations of the theoretical argument (e.g., referencing Beyer, 2014 to contextualize convergence assumptions).
