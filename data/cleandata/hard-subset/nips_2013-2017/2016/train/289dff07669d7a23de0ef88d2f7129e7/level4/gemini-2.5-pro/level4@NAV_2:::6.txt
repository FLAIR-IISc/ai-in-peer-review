This paper presents a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce the computational and storage costs associated with updating the covariance matrix. The proposed method, which the authors call Cholesky-CMA-ES, maintains and directly updates the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, thereby avoiding the more costly step of decomposing the full matrix in each iteration. This work builds on prior research by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), which also explored updating Cholesky factors. The main modification introduced here involves an approximation in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, where the inverse of the matrix square root is replaced by the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor. The authors provide a theoretical argument that this substitution does not alter the algorithm's behavior in the long run and present empirical results showing that their variant significantly reduces wall-clock time without degrading performance in terms of function evaluations.

### Strengths

*   **Practical Impact:** The proposed modification addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES. For problems where the objective function evaluation is cheap and the dimensionality is moderately high, the cost of updating the covariance matrix can dominate the total runtime. The presented method offers a practical solution that can significantly speed up the optimization process in such scenarios.
*   **Efficiency:** The paper provides a method that achieves an asymptotically optimal O(µd²) time complexity and O(d²) storage complexity for adapting a full covariance matrix. This is a valuable contribution for practitioners who need to use the full-power of CMA-ES but are limited by computational resources.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate that the proposed variant is substantially faster in wall-clock time than the reference implementation, with the speed-up becoming more pronounced as dimensionality increases. Crucially, this is achieved without a discernible increase in the number of function evaluations required to reach the target fitness, validating the authors' central claim.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Positioning and Originality:** The paper's primary weakness is its positioning with respect to existing literature.
    *   **Naming:** The name "Cholesky-CMA-ES" was previously introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a closely related algorithm. The authors do not acknowledge this, which is misleading. They should either choose a different name or explicitly clarify the distinction between their method and the original Cholesky-CMA-ES.
    *   **Incremental Contribution:** The core algorithmic contribution appears to be a generalization of the rank-one update from Krause and Igel (2015) to the rank-µ case by applying it within a loop. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly articulated its precise novelty beyond this extension.
*   **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The empirical evaluation is missing a key baseline. The variant proposed by Suttorp et al. (2009) is a direct competitor but is not included in the experiments. The authors assert that Suttorp's method is "prone to numerical instabilities," but this claim is not substantiated and contradicts the original paper's findings. A direct empirical comparison is essential to properly contextualize the contribution and validate claims of superiority.
*   **Overstated Claims:**
    *   The title's claim of "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" is too broad. This optimality holds only under the assumption that a full d x d covariance matrix must be maintained. For separable or spherical functions, simpler variants (e.g., with a diagonal covariance matrix) have much lower complexity and would be more optimal.
    *   The assertion that this work helps scale CMA-ES to "large-scale optimization" is questionable, as O(d²) complexity is still prohibitive for the dimensions typically associated with that term.
*   **Clarity and Minor Issues:**
    *   The paper should provide a clearer analysis of the empirical results in Figure 1(b) for the Cigar function, where the plateaus for CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES appear to be at different objective function values. This may be an artifact of plotting medians, but it warrants explanation.
    *   There is a minor technical inaccuracy: The statement that "typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step" is not true for modern, efficient implementations. As the authors' own reference implementation demonstrates, this is done periodically to save computation.

In summary, while the paper presents a useful and efficient implementation of CMA-ES for a specific use case, its claims regarding optimality and novelty are overstated, and its experimental evaluation is incomplete. The contribution is more incremental than presented. To be suitable for publication, the authors should resolve the naming issue, provide a more nuanced discussion of the algorithmic contribution relative to prior work, and, most importantly, include a direct empirical comparison with the Suttorp et al. (2009) variant.
