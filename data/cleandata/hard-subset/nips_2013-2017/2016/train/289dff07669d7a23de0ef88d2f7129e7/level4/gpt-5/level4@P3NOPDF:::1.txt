Summary
This paper introduces a new covariance update for CMA-ES based on triangular Cholesky factors. By maintaining and updating a Cholesky factor rather than the full covariance matrix, the method substantially reduces both computational cost and memory footprint while preserving the algorithm’s core behavior. The authors provide a careful empirical evaluation showing that the proposed variant matches the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations to target accuracy, yet achieves significant wall-clock speedups, especially in high-dimensional settings.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: Replacing the full covariance update with a triangular Cholesky–based update is a well-motivated and elegant modification. It leverages a natural factorization to reduce complexity and memory usage while maintaining positive definiteness and the adaptive capabilities of CMA-ES.
- Practical impact: The reduction in runtime and memory is particularly compelling for medium- to large-scale problems. The approach also facilitates access to spectral information of the covariance, which can be beneficial for diagnostics and analysis.
- Empirical thoroughness: The numerical evaluation is careful and convincing. It demonstrates that the proposed update maintains the optimization efficiency (measured by the number of function evaluations) while delivering substantial wall-clock gains in higher dimensions.
- Relevance to the community: CMA-ES is a foundational tool in derivative-free optimization with strong connections to machine learning, information geometry, and applications in reinforcement and supervised learning. A speed- and memory-improved variant is highly relevant for NeurIPS.

Weaknesses and Questions
- Fairness and reproducibility of timing comparisons: Since the main claim centers on wall-clock improvements, it is essential to detail the implementation and hardware setup to ensure comparability.
  - Please specify the implementation language(s), compiler flags, and whether optimized linear algebra libraries (e.g., BLAS/LAPACK/MKL/OpenBLAS) were used for each method.
  - Clarify whether all methods were single-threaded or multi-threaded and whether the CPU, cache, and memory configurations were identical.
  - Indicate any differences in random number generation, seeding, and precision (single vs. double) that could affect timing.
- Interpretation of high-dimensional behavior: The paper states that differences are more pronounced in higher dimensions, yet in Figure 2 the Cigar and Discus functions at dimension 256 do not appear to follow this trend. Please provide an explanation—for example, whether problem structure (axis-aligned conditioning), step-size dynamics, or cache effects reduce the practical advantage of the Cholesky update for these instances.
- Robustness details (optional but helpful): While the empirical results are strong, a brief discussion of numerical stability (e.g., frequency of re-factorization or regularization to avoid drift in the Cholesky factor under repeated rank-one updates) would strengthen confidence in the approach across long runs and extreme condition numbers.

Significance and Impact
The proposed variant targets a key bottleneck in CMA-ES—maintaining and updating the covariance—and addresses it in a principled way. The empirical evidence that the number of function evaluations is not adversely affected, coupled with consistent runtime gains in larger dimensions, makes this a compelling improvement for practical use. Given CMA-ES’s wide adoption, this method could plausibly become a default choice for medium- to large-scale problems, thanks to:
- Reduced memory usage,
- Lower computational overhead per iteration,
- Direct access to useful spectral information.

Clarity and Presentation
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation for the Cholesky-based update is sound and easy to follow, and the experimental section is thorough and relevant. The connection to the broader ML context is appropriate, and the work fits well within the NeurIPS community.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper offers a substantial and well-validated improvement to a widely used algorithm in derivative-free optimization. With minor clarifications on implementation details for fair timing comparisons and a brief explanation of the high-dimensional anomalies (e.g., Cigar and Discus at d = 256), the paper will be even stronger.
