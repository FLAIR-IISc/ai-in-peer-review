This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization by introducing a new, highly efficient variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art stochastic optimization algorithm. CMA-ES operates by adapting the parameters of a multivariate normal sampling distribution, which is equivalent to adapting an underlying metric for the search space. The core innovation of this work is a novel update mechanism for the covariance matrix that leverages its triangular Cholesky factors.

The primary advantages of this new approach are substantial. Compared to the standard CMA-ES update, this method significantly reduces both the computational complexity of the update step and the memory required to store the algorithm's state parameters. These improvements make the algorithm far more scalable and practical for higher-dimensional problems.

The authors provide a careful and thorough numerical evaluation to validate their claims. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that the proposed variant does not deteriorate the performance of the standard CMA-ES in terms of convergence speed, as measured by the number of function evaluations needed to reach a given objective value. At the same time, it achieves a significant reduction in wall-clock time, an advantage that becomes increasingly pronounced as the problem dimensionality grows.

This work is a very strong contribution. I expect this new variant has the potential to become the default implementation of CMA-ES for medium- and large-scale applications. It offers several clear and compelling advantages over the current standard: reduced memory footprint, lower time complexity, and the added benefit of making the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix readily available at no extra computational cost.

The paper is well-written, and the empirical evaluation is rigorous. The topic is also highly relevant to the NIPS community, given that CMA-ES has deep connections to machine learning and information geometry, and is frequently applied in domains such as reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization.

**Minor Comments:**

*   For the CPU time comparisons, please clarify how the comparability between the different implementations was ensured. Specifying details such as the implementation language and compiler would strengthen the experimental claims.
*   In the discussion of Figure 2, the paper notes that "the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences." This observation does not appear to hold for the Cigar and Discus functions at 256 dimensions. It would be beneficial to include a brief explanation for this apparent anomaly.
