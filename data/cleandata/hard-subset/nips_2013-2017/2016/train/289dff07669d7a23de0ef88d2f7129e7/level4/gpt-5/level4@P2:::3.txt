Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a version of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = AA^T instead of C itself. The covariance update combines rank-1 and rank-μ contributions by applying a sequence of rank-1 updates directly to A, yielding O(μ d^2) time and optimal storage. To avoid costly eigen/square-root computations in cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), the method replaces C_t^{-1/2} by A_t^{-1}. The authors justify this approximation via a lemma: if the normalized covariance matrices converge (up to scale), the induced orthogonal rotations between consecutive decompositions converge to the identity, so the error in the CSA path length vanishes due to exponential forgetting. Empirically, on rotated benchmark functions up to d = 256, Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the standard CMA-ES in function evaluations and significantly reduces wall-clock time; it also compares favorably to variants with delayed matrix decompositions and to a non-triangular Cholesky approach.

Relative to prior work, Suttorp et al. (2009) also update a Cholesky factor, but not triangular, which requires storing more parameters and can be less stable. Krause and Igel (2015) derive an efficient rank-one triangular update (with applications to (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES); this paper extends that idea to the standard CMA-ES with full rank-μ updates and provides a theoretical rationale for the CSA modification.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The rank-μ update via a sequence of rank-1 Cholesky updates is correct and practically useful. The CSA approximation is plausible; the lemma is mathematically sound under its assumptions. However, the argument relies on convergence of the normalized covariance sequence, which is not established for the full CMA-ES and no explicit error bounds are given. Numerical stability claims would benefit from more evidence (e.g., conditioning, drift of eigenvalues).
- Empirical evaluation: Benchmarks and rotations are appropriate and show strong wall-clock gains with no degradation in function evaluations. Additional ablations (e.g., measuring the impact of A_t^{-1} vs. C_t^{-1/2} on CSA) would strengthen the case. Comparisons are mostly to CMA-ES baselines; large-scale alternatives such as LM-CMA or sep-CMA-ES could contextualize trade-offs, even if the focus is full covariance adaptation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear algorithmic description (Algorithms 1–2). The motivation for using A_t^{-1} in CSA and the discussion around rotations (E_t) are helpful. There are several typographical and phrasing issues (see Minor comments). Some complexity statements could be sharpened.

Originality
The work is an incremental but meaningful contribution. Extending triangular rank-one updates to full rank-μ in standard CMA-ES and tying this to a theoretically motivated CSA modification is new to my knowledge. The idea of avoiding the polar/eigen decomposition by operating only on a triangular factor is not new, but the integration into standard CMA-ES with an argument about vanishing rotation error is the main novelty.

Significance
Practical significance is high: O(μ d^2) updates with optimal storage and ready access to eigenvalues can substantially reduce wall-clock time in high dimensions while preserving evaluation efficiency, which matters in ML applications where function evaluations are cheap relative to matrix updates. The contribution will likely be adopted in software libraries and used by practitioners who require full covariance adaptation.

Strengths
- Clear, simple algorithm with optimal asymptotic storage and O(μ d^2) update time.
- Sensible CSA approximation with a convergence-based justification.
- Strong empirical speed-ups without added function evaluations across dimensions and rotations.
- Practical advantages: cheap inversion of A, eigenvalues available from the diagonal.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical justification relies on convergence assumptions not proven for full CMA-ES; no quantitative error bounds for using A_t^{-1} in CSA.
- Potential loss of affine invariance through CSA is only informally addressed.
- Limited ablation of the CSA approximation’s effect; rotation drift analysis is relegated to the supplement.
- Baselines do not include large-scale alternatives (e.g., LM-CMA), which would help position the method’s niche.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical ablation isolating the impact of replacing C_t^{-1/2} with A_t^{-1} on CSA (e.g., path length distributions, invariance tests).
- Quantify rotation changes E_t in the main paper and relate them to the CSA error term over time.
- Clarify complexity statements: decomposing C typically costs O(d^3); specify where ω(d^2) is intended and avoid asymptotic ambiguity.
- Report numerical stability diagnostics (e.g., eigenvalue drift, condition numbers) and failure modes on ill-conditioned functions.
- Include or discuss comparisons to LM-CMA/sep-CMA-ES to contextualize when full covariance with O(μ d^2) updates is preferable.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Practical, impactful speed-up with preserved sample efficiency.
- Clean integration into standard CMA-ES; code-ready algorithm.
- Theoretical rationale, albeit under assumptions, for the CSA change.

Cons:
- Incremental relative to prior Cholesky-update work.
- Theoretical guarantees are assumption-based; missing bounds.
- Empirical section could better isolate and analyze the CSA approximation.

Minor comments and typos
- Eq. (1): the denominator should be σ_t (not σ). Same issue in footnote 2 on page 3.
- Line 94: “which can by be motivated...” → “which can be motivated...”.
- Consistency: use “proved,” not “proofed.”
- Complexity wording: “computing the matrix square root or the Cholesky factor ... has time complexity ω(d^2)” is confusing; decomposition is typically O(d^3).
- References contain formatting artifacts (e.g., backslashes, “? \Q2014\E”). Please clean and ensure complete bibliographic details.
- “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...” appears truncated; fix the header artifact.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The contribution is incremental but well executed and practically valuable. Strengthening the empirical analysis of the CSA approximation and clarifying a few theoretical and presentation points would make this paper a solid accept.
