

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root operation with a triangular Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors address a fundamental bottleneck in CMA-ES where updating and storing the covariance matrix dominates runtime and memory requirements. Their key insight is that the Cholesky factor can replace the matrix square root in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism without significantly affecting the algorithm's performance, as theoretically justified in Lemma 1. This leads to an optimal asymptotic runtime complexity of O(µd²) (where µ is typically O(log d)) compared to the standard O(d²) complexity, while maintaining identical objective function evaluation requirements. The work builds on previous attempts using Cholesky factorization (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) but provides a crucial theoretical foundation for why the modification preserves performance in the standard CMA-ES.

The paper makes a significant contribution to derivative-free optimization by solving a practical computational bottleneck. The authors demonstrate through comprehensive empirical evaluation on standard benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable optimization quality to standard CMA-ES while substantially reducing wall-clock time—up to 20x faster at d=64—and the speedup increases with dimensionality. The theoretical analysis is compelling, showing that the introduced error vanishes as the algorithm converges due to the smooth convergence of the Cholesky factor's rotation matrix.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical justification for the proposed modification. The authors carefully evaluate both theoretical properties and empirical performance, addressing the critical question of whether the modification affects optimization quality. The experimental design is robust, covering a range of benchmark functions with different characteristics and dimensions (d=4 to 256), and the results consistently support the claims. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of storage complexity and numerical stability.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background on CMA-ES is concise yet comprehensive, and the proposed method is clearly explained with precise algorithmic descriptions. The theoretical justification is presented logically, and the empirical results are effectively visualized. The manuscript provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

Originality: This work significantly advances previous attempts to use Cholesky factorization in evolution strategies. While Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) explored similar approaches for specific CMA-ES variants, this paper provides the first theoretical justification for applying the method to the standard CMA-ES without compromising performance. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work, particularly in the theoretical guarantees for the standard algorithm.

Significance: The contribution is highly significant for practical applications of CMA-ES in machine learning, where high-dimensional optimization is common. The method enables more efficient use of CMA-ES for problems where updating and storing the full covariance matrix is computationally feasible but becomes prohibitive as dimensionality increases. The paper demonstrates clear practical benefits that will be valuable to researchers and practitioners using CMA-ES for hyperparameter tuning, reinforcement learning, and other optimization tasks.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical foundation for why the modification preserves performance
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions
- Clear demonstration of reduced computational complexity without sacrificing solution quality
- Practical relevance to real-world optimization problems in machine learning
- Detailed analysis of storage complexity and numerical stability

Weaknesses:
- The paper could provide a more detailed explanation of the numerical stability benefits compared to the standard CMA-ES (the authors mention "numerically stable" but don't elaborate on why)
- While the paper discusses low-dimensional approximations (Loshchilov, 2015), it doesn't explore how Cholesky-CMA-ES might integrate with such methods for extremely high-dimensional problems, which is a practical consideration for many applications

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a significant practical problem with a theoretically grounded solution that has clear empirical validation. The contribution is both theoretically sound and practically valuable for a broad range of machine learning applications.

Pro: The paper presents a theoretically justified improvement to an important optimization algorithm with clear empirical benefits. The method has the potential to significantly impact real-world applications of CMA-ES.

Con: The paper could be strengthened by providing a more detailed explanation of the numerical stability properties and exploring potential integration with dimensionality reduction techniques for even higher-dimensional problems.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization with clear practical implications. The theoretical justification and empirical results provide compelling evidence for the value of the proposed approach. I recommend acceptance.
