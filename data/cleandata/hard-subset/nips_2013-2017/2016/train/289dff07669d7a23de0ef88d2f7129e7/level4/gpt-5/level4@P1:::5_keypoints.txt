- Proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES: maintain lower-triangular At with Ct = At Aᵀt to avoid explicit covariance and square-root/eigendecompositions.  
- Replaces CSA’s C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}, removing the need to estimate the changing orthogonal factor E_t.  
- Achieves per-iteration complexity O(μ d^2) and optimal storage, lowering wall-clock time versus delayed-decomposition baselines.  
- Theoretical argument: under convergence of normalized covariance shapes, orthogonal factors E_t stabilize (Eᵀ_{t-1}E_t → I), making the CSA approximation asymptotically harmless.  
- The asymptotic justification is restrictive and not a general proof of equivalence for the randomized algorithm.  
- Implementation and experiments show similar evaluation efficiency to reference CMA-ES and substantially lower wall-clock time on standard benchmarks.  
- Empirical benchmarks cover separable/non-separable, varying conditioning, with and without rotations.  
- Paper relates to prior work on delayed decompositions, structural restrictions, and previous Cholesky-based updates, but targets standard CMA-ES specifically.  
- Main novelty: integrating a numerically stable triangular Cholesky update into full CMA-ES and arguing for the CSA modification.  
- Technical soundness: Cholesky update is well-motivated and delivers the claimed complexity improvements.  
- Empirical support is convincing but would benefit from adding the full, non-delayed CMA-ES as a baseline.  
- Clarity is good; derivation and discussion of the missing orthogonal factor in CSA are well explained.  
- Recommends plotting/diagnosing evolution of E_t or rotation error to show when the approximation holds.  
- Significance: practically important when d is large and function evaluations are cheap (e.g., some ML applications), but limited when evals dominate runtime or d is small.  
- Strengths: significant runtime and memory reduction, clean integration into CMA-ES, stable factor updates, careful comparison to delayed-update baselines.  
- Weaknesses: theoretical results are asymptotic and rely on restrictive assumptions; missing diagnostics for rotation error and a full CMA-ES baseline; limited demonstrated impact in many real-world scenarios.  
- Suggested experiments: track E_t evolution, run full CMA-ES baseline (at least for small d), vary quadratic spectra and measure CSA rotation error, add a real-world task where CMA-ES overhead matters.  
- Arguments for acceptance: practical speedups with preserved evaluation performance, novel and well-motivated method, solid empirical evidence on diverse benchmarks.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of finite-sample theoretical guarantees, limited real-world impact demonstrations, missing diagnostics and full baseline in current paper.  
- Overall conclusion: solid, well-executed contribution that makes CMA-ES more efficient; would be stronger with the recommended additional experiments and diagnostics.
