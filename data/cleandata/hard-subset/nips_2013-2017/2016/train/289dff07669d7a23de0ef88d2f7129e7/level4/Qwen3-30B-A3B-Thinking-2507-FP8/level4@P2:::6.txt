

This paper proposes a variant of CMA-ES called Cholesky-CMA-ES that aims to reduce the computational complexity of covariance matrix updates. The authors claim to achieve optimal time and storage complexity by maintaining triangular Cholesky factors instead of explicitly computing the matrix square root during the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. They demonstrate theoretically that their approach maintains the same behavior as standard CMA-ES in terms of required function evaluations, while reducing both runtime and memory requirements.

The paper builds on prior work in CMA-ES variants that use Cholesky factorizations, particularly Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015). However, the naming of their method as "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is problematic as this term was first introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a similar approach. The authors do not acknowledge this prior work or clarify how their variant differs from the existing Cholesky-CMA-ES framework.

The main technical contribution appears to be a loop that applies the rank-one update to the Cholesky factor for all μ individuals (rather than just for the (1+1)-CMA-ES case considered by Krause and Igel). While this extension to the standard CMA-ES is useful, the algorithmic novelty is limited, as the core update mechanism is already present in prior work.

The paper's claims about "optimal" complexity require clarification. The approach only achieves quadratic time complexity O(μd²), which is optimal for maintaining the full covariance matrix, but this is not "optimal" in the broader sense as the authors suggest. For separable problems, diagonal approximations (e.g., Ros and Hansen, 2008) would require O(d) complexity rather than O(d²), and for spherical problems (e.g., Sphere function), the covariance matrix is unnecessary altogether. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of when the proposed approach is beneficial.

The experimental comparison is incomplete. The authors compare their method only to CMA-ES-Ref (with delayed matrix square root computation) and CMA-ES/d (with delayed updates), but omit a direct comparison to Suttorp-CMA-ES (the original Cholesky-CMA-ES). This omission is significant as the authors claim their approach is superior to Suttorp's method regarding numerical stability, while Suttorp et al. (2009) actually reported stability advantages for their approach. Figure 3's exclusion of Suttorp-CMA-ES is particularly misleading, as it suggests their method outperforms all alternatives without proper comparison.

The paper also contains an important technical inaccuracy regarding the computational complexity. The statement "Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step" is misleading; in standard CMA-ES implementations, the matrix square root is computed periodically (e.g., every max{1, 110d(c₁+cₘ)} iterations), not in every step. This mischaracterization undermines the motivation for the proposed approach.

Regarding significance, the paper's contribution is incremental. While the proposed method does reduce runtime and memory requirements for full covariance matrix maintenance, the complexity remains quadratic in dimensionality, meaning it doesn't address the fundamental scalability limitations of CMA-ES for high-dimensional problems. For very large-scale optimization, low-rank approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) would be necessary, which the authors acknowledge but don't address as part of their contribution.

The paper's strengths include a theoretically sound justification for the proposed modification, a clear description of the algorithmic changes, and empirical evidence showing reduced runtime without increased function evaluations. The implementation in Shark is a practical contribution, and the theoretical analysis of the error introduced by the Cholesky approximation is valuable.

I recommend major revisions before acceptance. Specifically, the authors should:

1. Correct the naming issue by properly acknowledging Suttorp et al. (2009) and clarifying how their variant differs from existing Cholesky-CMA-ES approaches.

2. Expand the experimental comparison to include Suttorp-CMA-ES and Krause's method, with a clear explanation of the differences between these variants.

3. Revise the claims about "optimal" complexity to be more precise, acknowledging that the approach is optimal only when maintaining a full covariance matrix, not in all scenarios.

4. Correct the technical inaccuracy regarding the frequency of matrix square root computation in standard CMA-ES.

5. Clarify the main algorithmic contribution (the loop for μ individuals) and explain why this extension is non-trivial rather than merely incremental.

The paper has potential value in the context of CMA-ES applications where full covariance matrix maintenance is necessary, but the current presentation and claims require significant refinement to properly position the contribution within the literature and accurately represent the novelty and limitations of the approach.
