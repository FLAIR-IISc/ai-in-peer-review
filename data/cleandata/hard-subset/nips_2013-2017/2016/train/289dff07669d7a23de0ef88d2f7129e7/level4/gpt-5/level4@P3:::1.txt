Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C instead of updating C and recomputing its square root. The key algorithmic change is to replace the C−1/2 factor used in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) with A−1. The authors justify this modification by arguing that the orthogonal factor relating C1/2 and A varies smoothly as C stabilizes, so the error introduced in CSA vanishes over time. Building on earlier Cholesky-based updates for special CMA-ES variants (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES; Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), this work extends rank-one factor updates to the standard multi-recombinant CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and shows that the full update can be decomposed into a sequence of quadratic-time rank-one updates to A. The resulting time complexity is O(µ d^2) per iteration (with typical µ = O(log d)) and memory is O(d^2), both optimal for full-covariance adaptation. Empirically, the method matches the standard CMA-ES in number of function evaluations on standard rotated test functions while substantially reducing wall-clock time, particularly in higher dimensions.

Relative to prior work on reducing computational burden—delayed decomposition (Hansen & Ostermeier), diagonal or low-rank/limited-memory models (Ros & Hansen; Loshchilov), or non-triangular factorizations (Suttorp et al.)—this paper provides a theoretically motivated, numerically stable, full-covariance update that preserves performance in evaluations while reducing computation. The information geometry perspective (Akimoto et al.) and known invariance properties of CMA-ES provide useful context for the proposed approximation to CSA.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated algorithmic contribution: a practical O(µ d^2) Cholesky update for the standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 and rank-µ updates.
- Theoretical justification for the CSA approximation based on smooth convergence of the orthogonal factor in the RQ decomposition; good intuition about why the error decays.
- Strong empirical results: same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES, substantial wall-clock gains that grow with dimension; comparisons include a delayed-decomposition baseline and a non-triangular Cholesky variant.
- Implementation details and practical considerations (use of LAPACK; availability in Shark) enhance reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theory: The argument for replacing C−1/2 with A−1 in CSA relies on convergence (or slow change) of the normalized covariance. While plausible, no quantitative bound is provided on the deviation in σ updates or on the impact when C changes rapidly (e.g., in transient phases, multi-modal landscapes, or noisy settings). A brief discussion or empirical stress test under noise would strengthen the claim.
- Invariance: Original CMA-ES with CSA already lacks a formal affine invariance guarantee; the proposed approximation may further affect invariance. The paper measures rotation effects (Et) in the supplement; summarizing these results in the main text would be helpful.
- Empirical protocol: For Rosenbrock, non-convergent trials were excluded. Please report success rates and, if possible, performance metrics conditioned and unconditioned on success to avoid bias.
- Fairness and reproducibility of timing comparisons: Clarify that all implementations use the same language, compiler, BLAS/LAPACK backend, and hardware, and that any algorithm-specific parameters (e.g., decomposition delays) follow recommended defaults.
- Figure 2 interpretation: The statement “the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences” appears less clear for Cigar and Discus at d = 256. The text later mentions L2 cache effects; it would improve clarity to explicitly attribute the reduced speedup at d = 256 to cache behavior and memory bandwidth on large matrices (affecting both Cholesky-CMA-ES and Suttorp-CMA-ES).
- Technical correctness: The paper claims that “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is not generally true; diag(A) are not the square roots of eigenvalues of C=AA^T except for diagonal C. Please correct this statement. While A enables fast linear solves and inversion and can facilitate monitoring (e.g., via cheap bounds on variances along the current sampling axes), exact eigenvalues still require an eigendecomposition or SVD.
- Additional empirical scope: Results focus on noiseless benchmark functions. Given CMA-ES’s use in RL and tuning, it would be valuable to report at least one noisy or real-world task to verify robustness of the CSA approximation.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with a well-engineered rank-one update (Algorithm 2) and a thoughtful CSA approximation. Numerical stability is discussed; adding a brief conditioning analysis or checks (e.g., variance floor enforcement, SPD preservation) would be welcome.
- Experiments are thorough on rotated benchmarks and include multiple baselines. Reporting success rates and confidence intervals would further support the conclusions.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. Please:
  - Fix citation formatting inconsistencies.
  - Correct the statement about Cholesky diagonals and eigenvalues.
  - Move a concise summary of the Et rotation experiment from the supplement to the main text.
  - Clarify implementation comparability across baselines (language, libraries, CPU).

Originality
- The extension of Cholesky updates to standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 combined with a justified CSA approximation is novel relative to prior Cholesky-based variants and delayed/diagonal/limited-memory approaches.

Significance
- High: The method materially reduces computation and memory for full-covariance CMA-ES without increasing function evaluations, making it attractive as a default variant for medium to large dimensions where full covariance is still feasible.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide statistical measures (e.g., median with interquartile ranges) and success rates; avoid excluding failures without reporting.
- Include a short noisy-function experiment to assess CSA robustness.
- Explicitly explain the d = 256 cache effects in the main text.
- Correct the claim about eigenvalues and clarify what monitoring is enabled “for free” with A (e.g., easy solves, inversion, and per-axis variance bounds).
- Detail the computational environment to ensure fair timing comparisons.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong practical impact: substantial speedups with unchanged evaluation efficiency.
- Solid theoretical intuition supporting the CSA approximation.
- Clean integration into standard CMA-ES with optimal asymptotic time and memory for full covariance.
- Good empirical evidence and open-source availability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of quantitative bounds on the CSA approximation; limited analysis under noise.
- Some experimental reporting choices (excluding failures) and missing details on timing environment.
- Minor but important technical misstatement about Cholesky diagonals and eigenvalues.

Overall, this is a valuable, well-executed contribution likely to influence practice. With the suggested clarifications and corrections, it would make a strong NeurIPS paper.
