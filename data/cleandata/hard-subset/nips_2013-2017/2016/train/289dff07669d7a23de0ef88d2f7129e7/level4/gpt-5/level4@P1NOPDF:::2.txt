This paper replaces the eigen-decomposition in CMA-ES with a Cholesky factorization to reduce time and memory complexity. The paper is generally clear, the method appears reasonable, and the experimental results—especially the reported speedups—are promising. I can imagine this approach becoming a new standard variant of CMA-ES.

That said, the originality seems limited. The work appears to be a trivial extension of “A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies.” The methods look essentially identical; if I am mistaken, please correct me and clearly articulate the substantive differences, including any new theory or algorithmic components that are unique to this submission.

Several important elements are missing and should be addressed:

1) Equivalence to formula (2). You should explicitly show whether your algorithm is equivalent to Eq. (2). I understand space constraints and that related results may have appeared previously, but this equivalence is central here and should be demonstrated (at least via a concise proof sketch or a precise mapping of your update to Eq. (2)).

2) Complexity versus CMA-ES-Ref. You claim a computational complexity of O(μ d^2), which matches CMA-ES-Ref. Please explain in detail why your algorithm is faster despite having the same asymptotic complexity. Using Θ-notation would help clarify tight bounds. In particular, break down the per-iteration costs (sampling, mean and covariance updates, path maintenance) and identify which terms have reduced constants or improved memory traffic due to the Cholesky formulation. If memory/storage savings contribute to practical speedups (e.g., better cache behavior), quantify this. If fewer or cheaper matrix operations are required per iteration, make this explicit. A wall-clock analysis across dimensions and μ values would also strengthen the claim.

3) Evolution-path error. You claim that the error in the path is small and decreases over time, which runs counter to Hansen’s claim. Because your analysis is not deterministic, this should be validated empirically. Comparing only final optimization performance is not sufficient. Please include experiments that directly track the path error over iterations across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions, with analyses of variability across runs. If the claim depends on specific hyperparameters (e.g., μ, learning rates), include a sensitivity study.

Minor comments:
- Notation needs careful checking. For example, wk in Algorithm 2 should be αk, and n in Table 1 should be d.
- “F” should be “f” on line 185.
- Ensure consistency of notation throughout (e.g., μ, λ, d; vector/matrix bolding), aligned with CMA-ES conventions.

Overall, this paper presents a practical idea with encouraging empirical speedups, and it could be impactful for practitioners. However, in its current form it lacks clear novelty over prior work and omits essential justifications and validations. Unless the authors (i) demonstrate equivalence to Eq. (2), (ii) provide a rigorous, preferably Θ-based, complexity and runtime analysis that explains the speedups despite identical asymptotic bounds with CMA-ES-Ref, and (iii) add targeted experiments to substantiate the evolution-path error claim, I do not think it is ready for NIPS.
