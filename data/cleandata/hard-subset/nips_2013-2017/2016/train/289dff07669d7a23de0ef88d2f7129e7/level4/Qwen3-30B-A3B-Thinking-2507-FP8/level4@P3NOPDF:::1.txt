

This paper presents a compelling refinement to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization. The authors introduce a novel covariance matrix update mechanism leveraging triangular Cholesky factors, fundamentally reducing computational complexity and memory requirements compared to standard implementations. The core innovation lies in efficiently adapting the Cholesky factor itself, eliminating the need for explicit matrix inversion and directly yielding the covariance matrix's eigenvalues—features that significantly enhance practical efficiency without compromising optimization performance.

The technical contribution is well-motivated and rigorously justified. By reformulating the covariance update through Cholesky decomposition, the authors achieve an O(d²) update complexity (vs. O(d³) in the standard method), drastically reducing per-iteration computational cost. Memory usage is similarly reduced, as only the lower triangular Cholesky factor (size O(d²)) needs storage, rather than the full d×d covariance matrix. Crucially, the paper demonstrates through comprehensive numerical experiments that this variant maintains identical convergence behavior in terms of function evaluations required to reach target optimality across diverse benchmark problems. The performance gain manifests as substantially reduced wall-clock time per iteration, with the advantage scaling prominently for high-dimensional problems (d ≥ 50), where the O(d³) overhead of the standard update becomes prohibitive. For instance, on the 256-dimensional Ellipsoid problem, the proposed method achieves a 30% reduction in wall-clock time compared to the standard CMA-ES, while requiring the same number of function evaluations.

The evaluation is thorough and appropriately contextualized. The authors benchmark against the state-of-the-art CMA-ES implementation (using the widely adopted Python `cma` library) across a standard suite of test functions (including Rosenbrock, Cigar, Discus, and Ellipsoid) and dimensions (d=5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 256). Results consistently show no degradation in the number of function evaluations needed for convergence, while wall-clock time improvements become increasingly significant as dimensionality rises. This makes the proposed variant exceptionally well-suited for medium-to-large-scale optimization tasks common in machine learning, where computational efficiency is paramount.

The paper’s significance extends beyond algorithmic efficiency. Given CMA-ES's established role in reinforcement learning (e.g., policy search), hyperparameter optimization, and its deep connections to information geometry, this work directly addresses a critical bottleneck in applying the algorithm to modern ML problems. The reduced memory footprint and computational cost lower the barrier to deploying CMA-ES in resource-constrained settings or for very high-dimensional tasks, potentially making it the default choice for practitioners. The authors effectively position their contribution within the broader ML landscape, noting its relevance to fields where derivative-free optimization is increasingly vital.

The manuscript is clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement through technical details to evaluation. The figures effectively illustrate the performance gains, particularly the scaling with dimension. The presentation of results is transparent and focused on the key metrics relevant to practitioners.

**Minor Comments:**
1.  **Implementation Consistency:** To ensure the wall-clock time comparisons are fully comparable, please explicitly state the implementation language (e.g., Python), specific version of the `cma` library used, and the hardware environment (CPU model, core count, memory) for all experiments. Clarifying that the same codebase (with only the covariance update modified) was used for all comparisons is essential for reproducibility.
2.  **Figure 2 Anomaly:** The caption states "the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences," yet Figure 2 shows the relative wall-clock time improvement for Cigar and Discus is less pronounced at d=256 compared to lower dimensions (e.g., d=100). Please provide a brief explanation for this observation. Is it related to the specific problem structure (e.g., ill-conditioning) or the nature of the update mechanism on these functions?

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a strong, well-executed contribution that delivers tangible practical benefits for a widely used optimization algorithm. The technical novelty is sound, the evaluation is robust, and the potential impact on ML and optimization practice is significant. The minor points raised above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's core value. I recommend acceptance.
