The CMA-ES algorithm is a state-of-the-art stochastic optimization method that employs multiple learning mechanisms to adapt the parameters of a multivariate normal sampling distribution. This distribution can also be interpreted as an adaptation of an underlying metric. The authors propose a novel variant of the covariance matrix update, utilizing triangular Cholesky factors. This approach offers significant advantages over previous updates, including reduced complexity and lower memory requirements for storing state parameters.

The new method is thoroughly evaluated through numerical experiments. The results demonstrate that the updated algorithm does not compromise the performance of the standard CMA-ES in terms of the number of function evaluations required to reach a target. However, it substantially reduces the wall-clock time, particularly in high-dimensional settings. In my opinion, this paper constitutes a substantial contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed variant of CMA-ES is likely to become the default variant for medium to large-scale applications, given its clear advantages, including reduced memory needs, lower complexity, and the ability to obtain eigenvalues of the covariance matrix at no additional cost.

The paper is well-written, and the approach is carefully evaluated. It is noteworthy to see this contribution at NIPS, as CMA-ES has strong connections to machine learning, information geometry, and is also employed in reinforcement learning and supervised learning.

However, there are a few minor points that require clarification. The authors should explain how they ensured that the different implementations used are comparable in terms of CPU usage. Specifically, they should mention whether the same implementation language was used and provide any relevant details. Additionally, when discussing Figure 2, the authors mention that "the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences." However, this trend does not appear to hold for the Cigar and Discus functions in 256 dimensions. A brief explanation for this discrepancy would be beneficial.
