Summary
The paper proposes a CMA-ES variant claimed to provide “optimal covariance update and storage complexity” while preserving algorithmic behavior. The method is presented under the name “Cholesky-CMA-ES.” The main algorithmic change relative to prior work appears to be applying the rank-one Cholesky update in a loop over all selected individuals rather than once, as in “plus” strategies. Empirically, the paper reports similar performance to a reference CMA-ES and improved computational efficiency.

Strengths
- Clear focus on reducing time and memory for full-covariance CMA-ES while retaining behavior.
- Practical value if a full covariance matrix must be maintained; likely the right direction under that constraint.
- Experimental evaluation shows relevant benchmarks and suggests runtime savings.

Major concerns
1) Naming and prior work: “Cholesky-CMA-ES” was introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a related approach and is well known in the CMA-ES community. The manuscript presents the method as “our CMA-ES variant, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES,” without clearly acknowledging the established name and prior algorithm. This is misleading and obscures the relation to existing methods.

2) Incremental contribution: The core algorithmic difference relative to Krause et al. (2015) appears to be looping rankOneUpdate over all (or all selected) individuals, rather than a single update as in plus strategies. If this reading is correct, the contribution is incremental and essentially amounts to a for-loop change, rather than a new algorithmic principle. The paper should make this distinction explicit and justify the novelty and impact.

3) Comparisons to established Cholesky-CMA-ES variants: The empirical study omits the method of Suttorp et al. (2009), despite directly overlapping claims. Algorithmic differences from Krause et al. (2015) are also not sufficiently dissected. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the true contribution and practical advantages.

4) Claims of optimality: The “optimal covariance update and storage complexity” claim is too broad. While the proposed method may be near-optimal among full-covariance CMA-ES variants, it is not optimal in general: on sphere or separable problems a diagonal (or even isotropic) evolution strategy is more efficient in both time and memory. The manuscript should qualify the claim to the full-covariance setting.

5) Numerical stability: The paper asserts that Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES is prone to numerical instabilities, whereas the original work suggested the opposite. This discrepancy needs to be reconciled with evidence (e.g., failure rates, condition growth, or corrective steps). Omitting Suttorp’s method from Figure 3 while making this claim can mislead readers.

6) Large-scale relevance: The approach remains O(n^2) in time and memory per update. It does not change the large-scale regime where O(n) or O(n log n) methods are necessary. The manuscript should state this explicitly and limit claims accordingly.

Empirical concerns
- In the Cigar function (Figure b), CMA-ES-Ref and the proposed method show plateaus at different objective values. This is unexpected if behavior is preserved and may be an artifact of median plotting or selection of runs. Please clarify aggregation (median vs. mean; over which seeds), show confidence intervals, and/or provide per-run diagnostics to confirm equivalence.
- The reported 2× advantage over Suttorp’s variant (as stated in the manuscript) should be demonstrated empirically under matched implementations and parameter settings. Report both wall-clock and operation counts.

Suggestions for improvement
- Resolve the naming issue. Either (i) explicitly position the method as an extension of the existing “Cholesky-CMA-ES” of Suttorp et al. (2009) with a clear qualifier in the name, or (ii) adopt a distinct name (e.g., “Rank-μ Cholesky-CMA-ES”) that conveys the key difference.
- Add direct comparisons:
  - Empirical: Include Suttorp et al. (2009) and the most relevant Krause et al. (2015) variant(s) in all main figures. If plots become cluttered, keep the best-performing baselines in the main paper and move full comparisons to the supplement.
  - Algorithmic: Provide a side-by-side analysis highlighting exactly what changes from Krause et al. (2015), including complexity per evaluation and per generation, memory, and any stability safeguards.
- Clarify the contribution. If the primary novelty is applying rankOneUpdate in a loop over selected individuals (rank-μ-like update), state this plainly and justify why this matters in practice (e.g., improved conditioning, empirical robustness, or tighter equivalence to standard CMA-ES).
- Qualify the “optimality” claim. Reframe as “near-optimal among full-covariance CMA-ES variants” or similar, and discuss cases where diagonal or limited-memory approaches are preferable.
- Substantiate numerical stability claims about prior work with quantitative evidence (e.g., number of restarts due to Cholesky breakdown, smallest eigenvalue drift, or corrective jitter required), or soften the claim.
- Address the Cigar discrepancy by clarifying plotting methodology and, if necessary, correcting or annotating the figures.
- Report computational costs transparently: per-iteration and per-function-evaluation times, memory footprint, and how these scale with n and λ.

Minor comments
- “Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step.” This is atypical: in standard CMA-ES implementations, the eigendecomposition is performed periodically, not every step. Please correct.
- Ensure consistent terminology for selection and update strategies (e.g., “plus” vs. “comma,” rank-one vs. rank-μ updates) to avoid confusion.
- Provide implementation details sufficient for reproduction (update frequencies, regularization, step-size control, numerical safeguards).

Overall assessment
The paper targets a relevant and practical problem and likely offers a useful engineering improvement for full-covariance CMA-ES. However, the contribution appears incremental, the naming conflicts with established literature, and key empirical and algorithmic comparisons are missing. With a clear acknowledgment of prior work, precise scoping of claims, inclusion of the most relevant baselines (especially Suttorp et al. 2009 and Krause et al. 2015), and a transparent articulation of the actual novelty, the paper could become a solid contribution. As it stands, I recommend revision before acceptance.
