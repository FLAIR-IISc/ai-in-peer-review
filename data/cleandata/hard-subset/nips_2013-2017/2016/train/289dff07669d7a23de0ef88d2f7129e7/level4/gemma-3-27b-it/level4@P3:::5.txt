This manuscript presents the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel covariance matrix adaptation scheme designed to accelerate the well-established CMA-ES algorithm. The core idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix, reducing the computational complexity from O(d<sup>3</sup>) to O(μd<sup>2</sup>), where *d* is the problem dimension and *μ* the number of samples per iteration. This is achieved by approximating the standard CMA-ES update with a scheme operating directly on the Cholesky factor, introducing a difference related to an orthogonal matrix *E<sub>t</sub>*. The authors provide a theoretical argument, based on the convergence of *E<sub>t</sub>* to the identity matrix under certain assumptions, to justify the approximation. The method’s efficacy is demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on standard benchmark functions, comparing its performance against state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants employing delay strategies.

This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. CMA-ES is a powerful and widely used algorithm, particularly valuable for non-convex, black-box optimization problems where gradient information is unavailable or unreliable. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a substantial speedup, particularly for higher-dimensional problems, without sacrificing optimization performance on the tested benchmarks. The theoretical justification, while relying on assumptions about convergence, adds credibility to the proposed approximation. The clear presentation and thorough experimental evaluation further strengthen the contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significant Speedup:** The proposed method demonstrably reduces computational time, a critical advantage for computationally expensive objective functions or high-dimensional problems.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a rationale for the approximation, linking it to the behavior of the orthogonal matrix *E<sub>t</sub>*.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental setup is well-defined, utilizing standard benchmarks and comparisons with relevant state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the key ideas accessible.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Addresses a key bottleneck in CMA-ES—the covariance matrix update—making it more scalable to larger problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumption Dependence:** The theoretical justification relies on assumptions regarding the convergence of covariance matrices, which may not always hold in practice.
*   **Oracle Domination:** The potential benefit of the speedup might be limited if the objective function evaluation dominates the overall runtime, especially in lower dimensions. This is acknowledged by the authors.
*   **Limited Real-World Validation:** The experiments are primarily based on synthetic benchmark functions. A real-world application demonstration would further strengthen the findings.
*   **Convergence of E<sub>t</sub>**: While a theoretical argument for convergence is provided, empirical evidence of the behavior of *E<sub>t</sub>* over optimization trajectories would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm.
*   The proposed method demonstrably improves performance on standard benchmarks.
*   The theoretical justification, while not fully rigorous, provides a plausible explanation for the observed results.
*   The presentation is clear, and the experimental evaluation is thorough.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical impact of the speedup may be limited in scenarios where the objective function evaluation is the dominant cost.
*   The theoretical justification relies on assumptions that may not always hold.
*   The lack of a real-world application demonstration weakens the overall impact.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*   **Empirical Analysis of *E<sub>t</sub>***: Include visualizations or quantitative analysis of the evolution of *E<sub>t</sub>* during optimization runs, perhaps using different norms to characterize its deviation from the identity matrix.
*   **Comparison to Baseline CMA-ES**: Include a direct comparison to the standard CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies for completeness.
*   **Spectral Analysis**:  Consider a simulation study on quadratic functions with varying spectral distributions (as suggested by Stich and Müller, PPSN 2012) to assess the impact of spectral properties on performance and the accuracy of the approximation.
*   **Real-World Application**:  Demonstrate the method's effectiveness on a relevant real-world problem, such as policy gradient optimization in robotics, to showcase its practical benefits.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field. While some limitations exist, the potential benefits of the Cholesky-CMA-ES outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the suggested improvements to further strengthen the empirical validation and analysis.
