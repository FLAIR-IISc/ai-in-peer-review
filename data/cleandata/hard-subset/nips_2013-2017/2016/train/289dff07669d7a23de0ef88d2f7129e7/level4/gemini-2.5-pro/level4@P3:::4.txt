### **Summary**

This paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a highly effective algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity of O(d³) per iteration, dominated by the computation of the matrix square root (or its inverse) of the d x d covariance matrix, which is required for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism.

The authors propose the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant that replaces the expensive matrix square root computation with updates directly on a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. While previous works have explored Cholesky updates for other CMA-ES variants, applying this to the standard algorithm has been challenging due to the C⁻¹/² term in the CSA update. The core contribution of this paper is a novel approach to approximate this term using the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor, A⁻¹. The authors provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the rotational error introduced by this substitution diminishes, and therefore the algorithm's behavior remains largely unchanged.

This modification reduces the time complexity of the covariance matrix update to O(µd²), which is asymptotically optimal for a full-rank matrix. The authors empirically validate their claims by comparing the Cholesky-CMA-ES against the standard implementation (with delayed updates) and another Cholesky-based approach. The results on several benchmark functions show that the proposed algorithm achieves the same convergence in terms of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but with a significant reduction in wall-clock time, especially as the problem dimension *d* increases.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Practical Impact:** The paper tackles a long-standing and practical limitation of one of the most powerful derivative-free optimization algorithms. Reducing the per-iteration complexity from O(d³) to O(µd²) makes CMA-ES applicable to higher-dimensional problems where maintaining a full covariance matrix is still feasible and necessary, for example, in direct policy search for reinforcement learning. The proposed algorithm has the potential to become the new standard implementation.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Theoretical Justification:** The key originality lies in the justification for replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹. Previous attempts have struggled with this step. The argument presented in Lemma 1, which relates the convergence of the covariance matrix shape to the vanishing rotational error, is insightful and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approximation. This is a clever solution to a difficult problem.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The authors compare their method not only to the standard reference implementation but also to alternative speed-up strategies (delayed updates, a non-triangular Cholesky approach). The results clearly demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a substantial speedup in wall-clock time without compromising the number of function evaluations required for convergence, thereby strongly supporting the paper's central claims.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It clearly explains the problem, contextualizes it within prior work, details the proposed solution and its justification, and presents the empirical results in an accessible manner.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Substantiation of Numerical Stability:** The abstract claims the proposed scheme is "numerically stable." While updating a Cholesky factor is often more stable than forming and then decomposing the covariance matrix C (which can lose positive definiteness due to floating-point errors), this point is not elaborated upon or demonstrated in the paper. Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical argument or empirical evidence (e.g., by tracking matrix condition numbers) to support this claim of improved numerical stability?

2.  **Discussion on High-Dimensionality Limits:** The authors rightly position their work as scaling up CMA-ES for problems where a full covariance matrix is still manageable. However, for many modern machine learning applications (e.g., training large neural networks), the dimensionality *d* can be in the thousands or millions, where even an O(d²) complexity is prohibitive. The conclusion briefly mentions resorting to low-dimensional approximations in such cases. A more in-depth discussion on how the proposed framework might potentially be combined with low-rank approximation techniques [e.g., Loshchilov, 2015] would strengthen the paper and provide a clearer roadmap for future work in truly large-scale optimization.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is a strong contribution that provides a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically verified improvement to a foundational optimization algorithm. The paper successfully resolves a key computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, which is of significant interest to both researchers and practitioners in machine learning and optimization. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to expanding on secondary claims and future work rather than any fundamental flaw in the core contribution. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a valuable and practical advancement that is likely to be widely adopted. The paper is of high quality, originality, and significance, making it an excellent fit for NIPS.
