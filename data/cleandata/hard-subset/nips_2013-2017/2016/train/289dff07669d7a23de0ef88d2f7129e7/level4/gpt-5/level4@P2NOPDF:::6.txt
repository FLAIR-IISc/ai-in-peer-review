This paper presents a CMA-ES variant advertised as achieving “Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity,” termed Cholesky-CMA-ES. The stated goal is to preserve the original algorithm’s behavior while reducing computational cost. However, the naming is problematic: “Cholesky-CMA-ES” was introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a closely related method and is well known. The manuscript does not acknowledge this and instead re-introduces the name for its own variant, which risks confusing readers.

Algorithmically, the proposal appears to be a modest, incremental change to Krause et al. (2015). The only substantive change I can identify is invoking rankOneUpdate in a loop over all individuals, rather than once as in plus-strategy CMA-ES. Note that rankOneUpdate already appears in Krause et al.; the distinguishing aspect here is essentially the placement and repetition of the call. If this assessment is inaccurate, the authors should make the differences explicit and justify why they are more than an implementation detail.

Empirically, Figure b) on the Cigar function shows different plateau levels for CMA-ES-Ref and the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES. This may stem from how medians are computed/plotted, but it should be clarified to rule out unintended behavioral deviations.

The claim of “optimal” update and storage complexity is also overstated. On the Sphere function the covariance matrix is unnecessary; on separable problems, a diagonal model suffices. In such settings, the proposed approach is not optimal. Furthermore, relative to the (omitted) Cholesky-CMA-ES by Suttorp, the proposed method is only about twice as cheap. The manuscript claims Suttorp’s method is numerically unstable, whereas the original paper reported the opposite; this contradiction needs reconciliation. The omission of Suttorp’s method from Figure 3 may therefore mislead readers about the contribution’s magnitude.

Regarding large-scale optimization, the complexity remains quadratic, so this work does not fundamentally change the scaling behavior of full-covariance CMA-ES. That said, if one insists on maintaining a full covariance matrix, the proposed modification may be a reasonable way to reduce time and memory overhead.

Overall, the contribution feels too incremental—algorithmically, it largely amounts to introducing a “for” loop around rank-one updates. The paper would be stronger with a clearer positioning relative to prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants and more rigorous empirical comparisons.

Requested revisions:
- Resolve the naming conflict. Either adopt a distinct name or clearly attribute and differentiate from Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause et al. (2015).
- Provide side-by-side empirical comparisons including Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES (and any stabilized variants) and the approach of Krause et al. Update all figures accordingly. If plots become cluttered, display the best-performing methods in the main paper and move full comparisons to the supplement.
- Clarify the exact algorithmic novelty. If the primary change is looping rankOneUpdate over all individuals, state this explicitly and explain why it materially changes efficiency without altering behavior. If there are deeper differences, detail them line-by-line in Algorithm 1 and discuss their impact.
- Investigate and explain the discrepancy on the Cigar function (Figure b)). Confirm whether median aggregation, plotting, or implementation differences cause the different plateaus.

Minor notes:
- “Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step” is incorrect; in standard CMA-ES implementations, EVD is performed periodically, not at every step.
- The paper should more carefully qualify the “optimal” complexity claim: it is conditional on using full covariance rather than problem-adaptive structures (e.g., diagonal or low-rank models).
- If asserting numerical instability of Suttorp’s method, provide empirical evidence and a discussion reconciled with the original paper’s claims.
