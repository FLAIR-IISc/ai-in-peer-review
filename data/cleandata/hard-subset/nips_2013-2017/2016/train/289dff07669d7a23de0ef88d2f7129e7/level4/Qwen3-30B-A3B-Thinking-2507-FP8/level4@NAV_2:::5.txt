

The paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the full covariance matrix update with a numerically stable O(μd²) update using triangular Cholesky factors, where d is the problem dimension and μ is the effective population size (typically O(log d)). This addresses the O(d³) computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES from matrix square root operations. The authors theoretically justify that the introduced approximation—omitting the orthogonal matrix Eₜ in the cumulative step-size adaptation—becomes negligible under the condition that the covariance matrix converges to a fixed shape (Lemma 1), and empirically validate that optimization performance (function evaluations) remains unchanged compared to the standard CMA-ES and delay-based alternatives (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d) across benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Rosenbrock, etc.). The work builds on prior NIPS contributions addressing CMA-ES efficiency, such as Ros & Hansen (PPSN 2008) on diagonal approximations and Suttorp et al. (ML 2009) on Cholesky-based updates for restricted variants, but provides the first theoretically grounded approach for the standard CMA-ES with optimal asymptotic complexity.

**Strengths:**  
- **Quality:** The O(μd²) update is theoretically sound and numerically stable, with Lemma 1 rigorously establishing convergence of Eₜ to the identity under plausible conditions. Empirical results demonstrate no degradation in optimization performance (function evaluations) while achieving significant runtime speedups (20× at d=64), consistent with the complexity analysis.  
- **Clarity:** The algorithmic description (Algorithm 1) and theoretical justification are well-structured. The distinction between Cholesky-CMA-ES and prior Cholesky-based methods (e.g., Suttorp et al.) is clearly articulated, highlighting the storage complexity advantage (optimal O(d²) vs. non-triangular alternatives) and the free eigenvalue computation.  
- **Originality:** The paper presents a novel application of Cholesky factorization to the *standard* CMA-ES with a theoretical foundation for the step-size adaptation modification, distinct from prior work (e.g., Krause & Igel 2015 for (1+1)-CMA-ES). It addresses a fundamental scalability bottleneck in a widely used algorithm.  
- **Significance:** CMA-ES is a cornerstone of derivative-free optimization (e.g., policy search in RL, hyperparameter tuning), and this work enables its application to higher-dimensional problems where covariance updates dominate runtime. The speedup scales with dimensionality, directly addressing a key limitation of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Practical Relevance:** The claimed speedup is most impactful only when objective function evaluations are rapid (e.g., milliseconds) and dimensionality is high (d > 50), which may not align with typical CMA-ES use cases (e.g., d ≈ 10–50 in RL/hyperparameter tuning). The manuscript does not sufficiently quantify how often this scenario occurs in practice.  
- **Empirical Gaps:** The experiments, while thorough for synthetic benchmarks, lack: (1) direct visualization of Eₜ evolution on optimization trajectories (e.g., norm of Eₜ − I over iterations), (2) runtime comparisons for CMA-ES *without* delay strategies (to contextualize the speedup magnitude), and (3) a real-world application (e.g., neural network training) where the speedup demonstrably accelerates the end-to-end process.  
- **Theoretical Scope:** The convergence argument for Eₜ (Lemma 1) assumes covariance matrix convergence, which is not guaranteed for all CMA-ES runs. The paper does not discuss how non-convergent cases might affect performance.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated speedup for a fundamental optimization algorithm with clear complexity benefits; addresses a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES.  
*Con:* Limited practical impact for most current CMA-ES applications; missing experiments to fully validate the theoretical assumptions and real-world utility.

The paper makes a solid technical contribution to optimization, advancing the state of the art in CMA-ES efficiency. While the practical significance may be constrained to specific high-dimensional settings, the theoretical and algorithmic insights are valuable for the field. The authors should address the empirical gaps (Eₜ evolution, no-delay runtime, real-world case) to strengthen the argument for broader applicability.
