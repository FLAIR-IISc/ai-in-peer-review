The paper proposes a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) with improved covariance update and storage complexity. The authors claim that this modification, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES, does not alter the algorithm's behavior but reduces its computational cost. However, the name "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is misleading, as it was first introduced by T. Suttorp et al. in 2009 for a similar algorithm. The authors fail to acknowledge this, instead presenting their variant as a novel contribution.

The proposed method is an incremental modification of the work by Krause et al. (2015). The primary algorithmic contribution is the repeated application of the rank-one update, which is already present in Krause et al.'s work. This modification is achieved by calling the rank-one update in a loop for all individuals, rather than just once as in the "Plus" strategies.

Empirical results show a difference in the observed plateau for the Cigar function in Figure b), with varying objective function values for CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES. This discrepancy may be due to the plotting of median results. To provide a more comprehensive evaluation, the authors should compare their approach to both Suttorp's Cholesky-CMA-ES (empirically) and Krause's Cholesky-CMA-ES (algorithmically).

The claim of "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" is somewhat misleading. This optimality does not hold for the Sphere function, where the covariance matrix is not required, or for separable functions, where only the diagonal elements are necessary. Furthermore, the proposed approach is only twice as efficient as Suttorp's Cholesky CMA-ES, which is omitted from Figure 3. This omission may mislead the reader regarding the actual contribution of the paper. Additionally, the original paper by Suttorp et al. suggests that their approach is not prone to numerical instabilities, contrary to the claims made in this paper.

While the proposed modification may be the most efficient way to reduce computational time and memory when considering the full covariance matrix, it does not significantly impact the algorithm's ability to handle large-scale optimization problems, as the complexity remains quadratic.

Overall, the work is somewhat incremental, with the primary algorithmic contribution being the addition of a single "for" loop. To strengthen the paper, the authors should consider the following modifications: (a) resolve the naming issue, (b) perform experiments with variants of Suttorp et al. and Krause et al. and update all figures accordingly, and (c) provide a clearer explanation of the main algorithmic contribution or justify why the added "for" loop is not the primary contribution.

Minor notes: The statement "Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step" is inaccurate, as eigenvalue decomposition is typically performed periodically in proper implementations.
