This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) termed “Cholesky-CMA-ES,” aiming to reduce computational cost without altering the algorithm’s fundamental behavior. While the empirical results suggest performance comparable to the standard CMA-ES, several issues regarding naming, presentation, and claims of optimality require attention.

A significant concern is the nomenclature. The name “Cholesky-CMA-ES” was previously introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a related, albeit distinct, algorithm. The authors’ failure to acknowledge this prior work and their presentation of their variant as a novel “Cholesky-CMA-ES” is misleading. Furthermore, the work builds incrementally upon Krause et al. (2015), and the core algorithmic contribution appears to be the repeated application of the `rankOneUpdate` function – a component already present in Krause et al.’s work – across all individuals in the population. This is essentially a looped implementation of a technique already known in the literature.

The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating comparable performance, warrants closer scrutiny. Figure b) for the Cigar function reveals differing objective function values during plateaus between the reference CMA-ES and the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES. This discrepancy potentially arises from the method used to plot median results and requires further investigation. 

Crucially, the paper lacks comparison to both Suttorp et al.’s (2009) Cholesky-CMA-ES (empirically) and a detailed algorithmic comparison to Krause et al.’s (2015) related work. The claim of “Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity” is also overstated. While offering benefits in scenarios requiring a full covariance matrix, it's not universally optimal; for example, the Sphere function does not necessitate covariance matrix calculations, and separable functions may be effectively addressed with diagonal covariance matrices alone. The proposed approach only achieves a two-fold reduction in cost compared to Suttorp et al.’s implementation, which the authors claim is numerically unstable – a claim contradicted by the original Suttorp et al. paper. The omission of Suttorp et al.’s method from Figure 3 further exacerbates this issue, potentially exaggerating the contribution of the present work.

While the authors acknowledge the quadratic complexity remains unchanged, and thus the impact on large-scale optimization may be limited, they rightly suggest the modification could be valuable for reducing memory and computation when a full covariance matrix is essential. However, the algorithmic novelty appears minimal—essentially adding a “for” loop to an existing function.

To address these concerns, I recommend the following revisions:

a) **Resolve the naming conflict:** Clearly differentiate the proposed method from the existing Cholesky-CMA-ES by Suttorp et al. (2009) with a new, unambiguous name.
b) **Expand the experimental comparison:** Include a thorough empirical comparison with Suttorp et al.’s (2009) Cholesky-CMA-ES, and a detailed algorithmic comparison with Krause et al. (2015). Update all three figures accordingly. If including all variants renders the figures unreadable, prioritize displaying the best-performing variant in the main text and relocate the complete figures to the Supplementary Material.
c) **Clarify the algorithmic contribution:** Provide a more robust justification for considering the “for” loop in Algorithm 1 a significant algorithmic contribution, or explicitly acknowledge its incremental nature.

Finally, a minor point: the statement “Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step” is inaccurate. In well-implemented CMA-ES algorithms, eigenvalue decomposition is performed periodically, not in every iteration.
