

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a modified covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy that replaces the standard matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to achieve optimal O(μd²) time complexity for covariance updates (vs. O(d³) in standard CMA-ES), while maintaining equivalent optimization performance. The authors address a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES—computational cost of matrix square roots during adaptation—by maintaining a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A_t (where C_t = A_t A_t^T) and adapting it directly via rank-1 updates (Algorithm 1). Crucially, they provide theoretical justification (Lemma 1) for replacing the standard cumulative step-size adaptation term C_t^{-1/2}(m_{t+1}-m_t) with A_t^{-1}(m_{t+1}-m_t), showing that the introduced error vanishes as the algorithm converges (since the orthogonal rotation matrix E_t converges to identity under reasonable assumptions). Empirical validation on standard NIPS benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) demonstrates equivalent function evaluations to the state-of-the-art delayed CMA-ES variant while achieving significant runtime speedups (up to 20× for d=64), with speedup increasing with dimensionality.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical Quality & Originality:** The work directly addresses a fundamental computational bottleneck in a widely adopted algorithm (CMA-ES, Hansen & Ostermeier, NIPS 2001; Hansen, 2015), which has been a standard tool for derivative-free optimization in NIPS applications (e.g., Heidrich-Meisner & Igel, NIPS 2009; Gomez et al., NIPS 2008). Unlike prior attempts (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) that either lacked theoretical grounding or were limited to specific CMA-ES variants (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES), this work provides a rigorous convergence argument for the standard CMA-ES. The approach is a novel combination of Cholesky factorization with CMA-ES adaptation, distinct from diagonal approximations (Ros & Hansen, NIPS 2008) or delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001).  
2. **Clarity & Completeness:** The paper is exceptionally well-structured. Algorithms 1–2 clearly present the method, and the theoretical justification (Lemma 1) is concise and well-motivated. The empirical section properly compares against relevant baselines (delayed CMA-ES and Suttorp-CMA-ES) using standard NIPS benchmarks (e.g., Stich & Müller, PPSN 2012; Omidvar & Li, AI 2011), with results showing no performance degradation.  
3. **Significance:** For high-dimensional problems (d > 64) where covariance updates dominate runtime, this method enables significant speedups without sacrificing solution quality—critical for applications like neural network policy search (e.g., Heidrich-Meisner & Igel, NIPS 2009), where d is often moderate but function evaluations are costly. The optimal O(μd²) complexity and memory footprint (storing only the triangular factor) are theoretically sound advantages over prior methods.

**Weaknesses & Suggestions for Improvement:**  
1. **Practical Relevance Context:** While the paper correctly notes that CMA-ES runtime is negligible for large-scale function evaluations (e.g., RL in robotics), the claim that "this is targeted scenario here" understates the niche applicability. The authors should more clearly frame the contribution as *enabling CMA-ES for larger problems where covariance updates become non-negligible* (e.g., d > 64, or when function evaluations are sub-second, as in hyperparameter tuning). Citing real-world applications where this matters (e.g., Igel, NIPS 2010 for model selection) would strengthen significance.  
2. **Empirical Validation Depth:** The current experiments are strong but could be further bolstered:  
   - *Include Et evolution plots* (as suggested in the draft) for specific trajectories to empirically validate Lemma 1’s convergence. For instance, plot ‖E_t E_{t-1}^T - I‖ over iterations for Rosenbrock (d=64), showing error decay.  
   - *Add spectral variation tests* (as in Stich & Müller, PPSN 2012) to demonstrate robustness across condition numbers, not just fixed benchmarks.  
   - *Compare against the non-delayed CMA-ES* (i.e., computing the square root every iteration) to isolate the speedup from the Cholesky update itself (currently, CMA-ES-Ref uses a delay strategy).  
3. **Limitations Clarification:** The paper states the method "should replace standard CMA-ES if updating/storing the covariance matrix matters," but this is only true for problems where d is large enough to make O(d³) costly (d > 32, per their Fig. 2). For d < 16, the delay strategy (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) is likely superior. This trade-off should be explicitly acknowledged.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel, theoretically grounded, empirically validated speedup for high-dimensional CMA-ES with no loss in solution quality; optimal time/memory complexity; clear presentation; addresses a well-known bottleneck in a core NIPS optimization algorithm.  
*Con:* Niche applicability (d > 32), limited real-world validation, and insufficient depth in empirical analysis of the key theoretical assumption (Et convergence).  

**Recommendation:**  
This is a strong technical contribution that advances a foundational NIPS optimization algorithm. While the empirical validation could be slightly deeper (as suggested), the theoretical justification and clear speedup results are compelling. The work is *highly relevant* to NIPS, as CMA-ES is a standard tool in the field (see Hansen, 2015; Heidrich-Meisner & Igel, 2009). The minor weaknesses are easily addressable via minor revisions. **I recommend acceptance.** The paper’s clarity, novelty, and significance outweigh the limited scope of the empirical validation.
