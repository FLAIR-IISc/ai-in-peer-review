

This work introduces a novel application of triangular Cholesky factorization to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), eliminating the need for explicit square root computations during matrix inverse operations. This modification reduces computational overhead without compromising the algorithm’s performance, as rigorously justified through theoretical analysis. The authors demonstrate that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains identical objective function evaluation counts compared to standard CMA-ES while achieving lower computational runtime, with time savings scaling favorably as problem dimensionality increases. The implementation represents a valuable optimization for practical CMA-ES deployments, particularly in high-dimensional settings.  

The paper’s core contribution—removing square roots via Cholesky factorization—is well-motivated and theoretically supported. However, the claim of "numerically more stable" computation requires deeper theoretical elucidation. Specifically, the authors should clarify how the triangular factorization approach mitigates numerical instability relative to standard CMA-ES, particularly in ill-conditioned scenarios. A concise derivation or reference to stability properties (e.g., error propagation bounds) would strengthen this assertion.  

The empirical results convincingly show reduced wall-clock time without increased function evaluations, aligning with the theoretical runtime reduction. Notably, the time savings become increasingly significant as dimensionality grows, which is a critical advantage for large-scale applications. This scalability is a compelling strength, though the authors could further contextualize the computational savings by comparing absolute runtime reductions across dimensions (e.g., 10D vs. 100D problems).  

A notable opportunity for enhancement lies in addressing high-dimensional practical challenges. The authors suggest that "most practical cases" (e.g., neural network training via direct policy search) involve high-dimensional search spaces, yet the current framework does not explicitly integrate dimensionality-reduction strategies. The low-dimensional approximation technique from Loshchilov et al. (2015), which projects high-dimensional search spaces into manageable subspaces, could synergize with Cholesky-CMA-ES to further accelerate convergence in such scenarios. The authors should evaluate whether combining these approaches would preserve the theoretical guarantees of their method while improving efficiency on real-world problems.  

Minor grammatical improvements would enhance clarity: "the algorithm's runtime complexity is reduced" (not "runtime complexity of the algorithm"), "the method reduces wall-clock time" (not "lessen"), and "the time savings increase with dimensionality" (not "this increases"). The phrase "valuable implementation" is redundant; "practical contribution" or "effective implementation" would be more precise.  

Overall, this work presents a technically sound and impactful refinement to CMA-ES. The theoretical foundation is robust, and the empirical validation is convincing. Addressing the numerical stability justification and exploring integration with dimensionality-reduction techniques would elevate the paper’s relevance for high-stakes applications. The authors should also explicitly discuss potential limitations, such as the overhead of maintaining the triangular factorization during adaptive updates.  

The review of related work is adequate but could better position the contribution against recent CMA-ES variants (e.g., those leveraging low-rank updates). Including a brief comparison of computational costs with alternative matrix decomposition methods (e.g., QR) would further highlight the novelty.  

In summary, this is a strong contribution with clear practical benefits. With minor revisions to clarify theoretical claims and expand on high-dimensional applicability, the paper would make a significant impact at NIPS. The authors are encouraged to address these points in the final version.
