Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix (C = A A^T) instead of explicitly updating C and recomputing its square root. The key change is to execute all rank-one/rank-µ covariance updates directly on A in O(µ d^2) time and O(d^2) memory, and to modify cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}. The authors argue that this approximation preserves the behavior of CMA-ES in terms of objective-function evaluations, because the rotation mismatch introduced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} becomes negligible as the covariance “shape” stabilizes (Lemma 1). Empirically, the method matches the standard CMA-ES in evaluations but is much faster in wall-clock time, with speedups growing with dimension.

This work builds on prior attempts to avoid repeated eigendecompositions or to update factorizations directly, notably Suttorp et al. (non-triangular factor updates) and Krause & Igel (rank-one update for the (1+1)-CMA-ES). It also complements runtime-reduction strategies such as delayed decompositions and structurally restricted models (e.g., diagonal or limited-memory CMA variants). Compared to these, the proposed approach retains a full covariance model while achieving asymptotically optimal storage and O(µ d^2) update time.

Strengths:
- Practical impact: Large wall-clock speedups (up to ~20x at d=64) without increasing the number of function evaluations.
- Clean algorithmic idea: Maintain a triangular Cholesky factor and apply rank-one/rank-µ updates at quadratic cost; invert A in O(d^2) when needed; eigenvalues come “for free” from the diagonal of A.
- Theoretical rationale: Lemma 1 explains why the rotation error in CSA vanishes as the covariance shape stabilizes, supporting the empirical finding that evaluation counts are unaffected.
- Invariance and benchmarking: Experiments include random rotations of test functions, preserving the hallmark rotational invariance of CMA-ES in practice; comparisons include delayed-decomposition CMA-ES and a non-triangular Cholesky update baseline.
- Memory efficiency: Storing only the triangular factor achieves optimal O(d^2) memory.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Numerical stability claim: The paper asserts improved numerical stability but does not precisely articulate why the triangular-factor updates are more stable than alternatives (e.g., repeated eigendecompositions or maintaining both A and A^{-1}). Please provide a theoretical or empirical justification: for instance, conditioning of triangular solves vs. symmetric square roots, stability of the specific rank-one update (Algorithm 2) under finite precision, and whether the update uses numerically robust transformations (e.g., Givens/Householder) or safeguards for downdates.
- CSA approximation error: The argument relies on the convergence (or slow variation) of the covariance shape. Can you bound, in finite time, the effect of EtE_{t−1}^T ≠ I on the step-size update, or at least quantify its empirical magnitude over optimization trajectories (beyond the supplementary rotation-change plot)? An ablation that compares C^{-1/2} vs. A^{-1} on the same runs would strengthen the case.
- Invariance discussion: CSA is known to weaken affine invariance. The proposed substitution introduces an additional rotation mismatch. Please clarify whether any invariance properties are theoretically altered and summarize empirical evidence that rotation invariance of the overall procedure is preserved.
- Scalability and cache effects: Performance degrades once matrices exceed cache (noted around d=256). A brief analysis of memory layout, BLAS usage, and whether blocked updates or batched rank-one updates could mitigate cache misses would be useful.
- Comparison breadth: The paper compares against delayed-decomposition CMA-ES and a non-triangular factor method. Including results (evaluations and time) for limited-memory/low-rank variants (e.g., LM-CMA [Loshchilov, 2014/2015]) would better position the method for very high dimensions, even if those methods trade off evaluations for time.
- Reproducibility: The implementation is integrated into Shark. Please clarify the release status, exact parameter settings (µ, λ, weights), stopping criteria, and hardware/BLAS details to facilitate replication.

Quality:
- The algorithmic contribution is sound, and the derivation from the standard rank-1 and rank-µ updates is clear. The CSA approximation is well motivated, though a finite-time analysis or tighter empirical quantification would improve rigor. Experiments are thorough on classical benchmarks with rotations; adding noisy or constrained cases would broaden applicability.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written. Suggested improvements: sharpen the explanation for numerical stability; explicitly state the cost of Algorithm 2 and when A^{-1} is materialized; summarize the expected size of the CSA rotation error and how it decays; and make the relationship to previous Cholesky-based updates more explicit.

Originality:
- Extending efficient triangular-factor updates from special CMA-ES variants to the standard algorithm with a justified CSA modification is a meaningful and original contribution, distinct from both delayed updates and structural restrictions.

Significance:
- The result is practically significant: many CMA-ES use cases are dominated by covariance updates once evaluations are cheap or dimension is moderate-to-large. Providing a drop-in, full-covariance alternative with the same evaluation efficiency but much lower runtime can change practice.

Suggestions and possible extensions:
- Provide a formal or empirical stability analysis of Algorithm 2, including error growth across many rank-µ updates.
- Quantify the CSA approximation error in practice (e.g., norm differences, induced step-size deviations), and, if feasible, add a lightweight correction term.
- Explore hybrids with low-rank or limited-memory models (e.g., LM-CMA), using Cholesky updates for the low-rank part, to address very high-dimensional settings common in direct policy search for neural networks.
- Investigate blocked/batched updates and memory layouts to improve cache utilization at d ≥ 256.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong practical speedups with unchanged evaluation counts.
- Solid theoretical motivation for the CSA modification.
- Clean, general technique with optimal asymptotic time/memory for full covariance.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical guarantees on finite-time CSA error and numerical stability.
- Comparisons do not include leading limited-memory methods, and scalability beyond a few hundred dimensions is not fully explored.

Overall, this is a useful and well-motivated contribution that can have immediate impact on CMA-ES practice. With clarifications on numerical stability and additional comparisons, it would be even stronger.
