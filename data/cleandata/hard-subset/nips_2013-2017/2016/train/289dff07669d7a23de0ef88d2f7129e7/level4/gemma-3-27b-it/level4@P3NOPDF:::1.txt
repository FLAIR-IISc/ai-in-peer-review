## Review of “…” (Paper ID: NIPS_2016-…)

This paper presents a novel and significant improvement to the covariance matrix update within the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard CMA-ES adapts a multivariate normal distribution by learning the parameters of its covariance matrix, effectively adapting an underlying metric. This work introduces a new update strategy based on triangular Cholesky factors, demonstrably reducing both the computational complexity and memory footprint of the covariance update – a critical bottleneck, particularly in high-dimensional optimization problems. 

The core contribution lies in this more efficient update mechanism. Previous methods for updating the covariance matrix can be computationally expensive and require substantial memory allocation. The proposed Cholesky-based update elegantly circumvents these limitations, offering significant practical advantages. The authors provide a thorough numerical evaluation showcasing that this new variant maintains the performance of the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluation count to achieve a target accuracy, while drastically reducing wall-clock time, especially as dimensionality increases. This is a compelling result.

I believe this work represents a substantial contribution to the field of optimization. The advantages – reduced memory usage, lower computational complexity, and the inherent availability of covariance matrix eigenvalues as a byproduct of the Cholesky decomposition – are compelling. It is highly likely that this variant of CMA-ES will become a preferred implementation, especially for medium-to-large scale problems where memory and computational cost are significant constraints. The paper is well-written and the experimental evaluation is carefully conducted and presented. 

The relevance of this contribution to the NIPS audience is particularly noteworthy. CMA-ES enjoys strong connections to machine learning (e.g., hyperparameter optimization), information geometry, and is frequently utilized in both reinforcement learning and supervised learning contexts. Improvements to this foundational algorithm therefore have broad applicability within the NIPS community.

I have only a few minor suggestions for improvement:

* **Implementation Comparability:** The authors should briefly elaborate on the measures taken to ensure a fair comparison between different implementations used in their evaluation. Specifically, details regarding the programming language(s) used and any compiler/optimization flags applied would be beneficial. Confirming equivalent CPU usage (or controlling for it) across implementations would strengthen the validity of the results.
* **Figure 2 Discussion:** In the discussion of Figure 2, the statement “the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences” does not appear consistently supported by the presented data. For the Cigar and Discus functions at a dimensionality of 256, the differences between the original and proposed update methods seem less pronounced. A brief explanation addressing this observation would enhance the clarity and completeness of the analysis. Perhaps this is a characteristic of these particular test functions, or a statistical fluctuation. Addressing this point would demonstrate a careful consideration of the experimental results.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a valuable and practically relevant advancement to CMA-ES. I recommend acceptance.
