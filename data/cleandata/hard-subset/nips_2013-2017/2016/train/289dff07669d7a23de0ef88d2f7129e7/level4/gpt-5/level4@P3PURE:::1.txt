This paper presents a compelling and timely advance to CMA-ES, the state-of-the-art stochastic (derivative-free) optimizer. CMA-ES adapts the parameters of a multivariate normal sampling distribution—equivalently, an underlying metric—via several learning mechanisms. The authors propose a new covariance update based on triangular Cholesky factors, which substantially reduces both the computational complexity of the update and the memory footprint required to store the algorithm’s state.

The numerical evaluation is careful and convincing. The results show that the proposed update preserves the performance of standard CMA-ES in terms of the number of function evaluations needed to reach a target, while markedly reducing wall-clock time, especially in high-dimensional settings. This is a significant contribution to the optimization community. I expect this variant to become a default choice for medium- to large-scale problems, given its clear advantages: reduced memory usage, lower update complexity, and obtaining the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix essentially for free. The paper is well written, and the empirical study is thorough. It is also fitting for NIPS, given CMA-ES’s deep connections to machine learning and information geometry, and its use in reinforcement learning and supervised learning.

Minor points:
- Please clarify how you ensured computational fairness across implementations. Were all variants implemented in the same language and with comparable low-level optimizations? If so, specify the language and key implementation details.
- In discussing Figure 2, the text states that “the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences.” This trend does not appear to hold for Cigar and Discus in 256 dimensions. An explanation for this behavior would be helpful.
