This paper's main contribution is a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm that utilizes a Cholesky factorization to replace the standard matrix square root operation. This change reduces the computational cost of inverting matrices, leading to a decrease in wall-clock time that becomes more significant as the search space dimensionality increases. The authors provide a theoretical justification demonstrating that this modification does not degrade the algorithm's performance, and the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES requires a comparable number of objective function evaluations to the standard version. Overall, this is a valuable contribution that improves the practical implementation of CMA-ES.

I have two questions for the authors:

1.  Could you provide a more detailed theoretical explanation for the claim that this method achieves a "'numerically more stable' computation"?

2.  While the proposed method effectively lessens the wall-clock time, addressing high-dimensional problems remains a critical concern, especially in practical applications like direct policy search for training neural networks. Have the authors considered combining their framework with low-dimensional approximation techniques, such as those in [Loshchilov, 2015], to further improve scalability?
