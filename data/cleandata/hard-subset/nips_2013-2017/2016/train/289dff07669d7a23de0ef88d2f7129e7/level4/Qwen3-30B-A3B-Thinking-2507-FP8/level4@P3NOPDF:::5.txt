

The manuscript introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that accelerates the covariance update step from O(d³) to O(μd²), where d is the problem dimension and μ (typically O(log d)) is the population size. The key innovation replaces full covariance matrix updates with a lower-triangular Cholesky decomposition, maintaining the Cholesky factor instead of the full matrix and its square root. The authors argue that the method approximates the original CMA-ES under strong asymptotic assumptions: specifically, they show that the orthogonal correction matrix Eₜ (absent in the Cholesky variant) converges to the identity matrix as the covariance sequence approaches a target, rendering the schemes equivalent in the limit. Empirical validation demonstrates comparable convergence behavior and significant speedups on standard test functions, outperforming two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates.

This work represents a solid theoretical contribution to accelerating CMA-ES, a leading algorithm for derivative-free, non-convex black-box optimization. The proposed method achieves substantial computational gains without degrading performance on synthetic benchmarks, which is a meaningful advancement for the field. However, the practical significance of the speedup warrants careful consideration. In most real-world applications of CMA-ES, the runtime of the black-box objective function evaluation dominates the total optimization time. For problems with d ≤ 50 (common in practice), the CMA-ES overhead is negligible unless function evaluations are extremely fast (e.g., <1 ms). The Cholesky-CMA-ES thus targets a niche scenario—high-dimensional problems (d ≫ 50) with very rapid evaluations—that may not reflect typical use cases. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the broader impact hinges on whether this specific regime is sufficiently relevant.

To strengthen the paper’s claims and address practical relevance, I recommend the following empirical enhancements:

1.  **Track Eₜ dynamics**: Include plots showing the evolution of ||Eₜ - I|| (in operator or Frobenius norm) during optimization for representative trajectories. This would provide direct evidence of how closely Eₜ approximates identity and validate the asymptotic assumption. The full CMA-ES could be run alongside the Cholesky variant to compute Eₜ in real-time for comparison.

2.  **Baseline for non-delayed CMA-ES**: Present runtime and performance data for standard CMA-ES (without any delay strategies) on the same benchmarks. This establishes a clear reference point for the speedup claim and clarifies whether the observed gains are due to the Cholesky update or merely the absence of other delays.

3.  **Spectral analysis on quadratics**: Extend the quadratic test suite beyond standard fixed spectra (e.g., following Stich & Müller, PPSN 2012) to include continuous variations in the eigenvalue distribution. Plot solution quality and the error ||Eₜ - I|| against spectral condition numbers. This would rigorously test the method’s robustness to ill-conditioning, a key challenge for CMA-ES.

4.  **Real-world application**: Incorporate a case study from a domain where CMA-ES runtime is genuinely impactful, such as real-time reinforcement learning in robotics (e.g., policy gradient optimization for continuous control tasks). This would demonstrate tangible benefits beyond synthetic benchmarks, addressing the practicality concern directly.

While the theoretical framework is compelling and the speedup is well-documented for the targeted regime, these additions would significantly bolster the paper’s argument for broader relevance. The current empirical validation is sufficient for the technical contribution but leaves the practical motivation somewhat speculative. Addressing even one or two of these suggestions would substantially enhance the paper’s impact.

Overall, the Cholesky-CMA-ES is a technically sound improvement with clear computational advantages. The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature. With the recommended empirical extensions, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As currently presented, it is a *good* paper (solid theory, reasonable experiments) but could be elevated to *excellent* with the proposed additions. I recommend acceptance pending these minor revisions.
