Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C rather than C itself. The key change is in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): the standard update uses C^{-1/2}, whereas the proposed method replaces it with A^{-1}. Because C^{1/2} = A E with E orthogonal (by RQ/QR), the omission of the time-varying rotation E in the CSA constitutes the main approximation. The authors argue that, under mild convergence assumptions on the normalized covariance C̄t, the orthogonal factors converge (E_{t-1}^T E_t → I), so the error in the CSA norm vanishes asymptotically. They extend prior rank-one Cholesky updates (e.g., Suttorp et al. 2009; Krause and Igel 2015) to the standard rank-1 plus rank-μ CMA-ES update, yielding an O(μ d^2) update with optimal storage, while avoiding cubic-time decompositions.

Empirically, on standard black-box benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar/Discus/Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) and under random rotations, Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the number of function evaluations of a reference CMA-ES and outperforms it substantially in wall-clock time as d grows. Comparisons include the reference CMA-ES implementation with a decomposition schedule that, for the tested dimensions, effectively recomputes the square root every iteration (CMA-ES-Ref), a delayed-update baseline (CMA-ES/d), and a non-triangular Cholesky approach adapted from Suttorp et al. The proposed method shows consistent speedups without loss in solution quality, with gains increasing with d.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algebraic decomposition of the rank-1 and rank-μ updates into stable quadratic-time Cholesky updates is sound and well motivated. The CSA approximation is justified by a lemma showing asymptotic alignment of the missing rotations when the normalized covariance stabilizes, and by the empirical observation that evaluation counts are unaffected.
- Empirical support: Experiments cover multiple problem classes, include rotation tests, and report both function evaluations and wall-clock time. Implementation details (Shark + LAPACK) are appropriate.
- Limitations: The asymptotic argument relies on convergence of C̄t and does not cover transient behavior or noisy objectives; the paper would benefit from more empirical analysis of the CSA approximation error and its dynamics. Practical gains are most relevant when function evaluations are cheap and d is moderate to large; this context should be discussed more explicitly.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The exposition of the CSA approximation and the reasoning around E_t is helpful. A few citation formats and minor typos should be corrected. It would help to elevate to the main text some of the supplementary diagnostics (e.g., rotation drift over time).

Originality
The work extends known Cholesky-update ideas to the standard CMA-ES with rank-μ updates and provides a justified CSA approximation that preserves performance while enabling O(μ d^2) time and optimal storage. Relative to delayed decompositions and limited-memory/diagonal variants, this contribution targets the full covariance case with a clean and implementable update. The novelty is incremental but meaningful and practically useful.

Significance
The method can materially reduce runtime and memory for full-covariance CMA-ES in settings where evaluation costs are low-to-moderate and d is sizable—a relevant regime in reinforcement learning (policy search), algorithm configuration, and some hyperparameter tuning tasks. It is less impactful when evaluations dominate runtime or d is very large (where limited-memory methods are preferable). Still, within its target regime, the speedups and memory savings are compelling.

Pros
- O(μ d^2) update with optimal storage and numerically stable rank-1 updates.
- Theoretical justification that the CSA approximation error diminishes as the covariance stabilizes.
- Empirically matches evaluation counts of the standard CMA-ES while providing large wall-clock speedups that grow with d.
- Provides eigenvalues essentially “for free” and allows efficient inversion of A when needed.
- Rotation robustness tested; implementation publicly available.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees for CSA are asymptotic and do not quantify transient errors; robustness under noise is not analyzed.
- Practical impact depends on the cost model; gains are limited when function evaluations dominate.
- Comparisons do not include limited-memory/full-covariance hybrids (e.g., LM-CMA) on very high d.
- Some analyses (e.g., magnitude of rotation drift E_t over time) are relegated to the supplement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Empirically quantify the CSA approximation error:
  - Track norms/angles or a proxy for E_{t-1}^T E_t over time, and correlate with the error in ‖p_σ‖ and with step-size dynamics.
  - Run a paired study where standard CSA (with C^{-1/2}) and the A^{-1}-based CSA are computed in parallel from the same samples to directly compare trajectories.
- Provide a clearer cost-benefit analysis:
  - Report per-iteration CPU time breakdowns and memory footprint; include a break-even analysis versus evaluation cost and dimension.
  - Add fixed-time-budget curves in addition to fixed-accuracy termination to reflect practical use.
- Broaden baselines and scenarios:
  - Include LM-CMA (Loshchilov 2014/2015) and diagonal CMA-ES to delineate regimes where full covariance is still advantageous.
  - Evaluate robustness under noise and constraints; report sensitivity to μ and weighting choices.
- Real-world case study:
  - Add at least one application where evaluations are fast and d is moderate (e.g., direct policy search in simulated robotics or an algorithm-configuration task) to demonstrate end-to-end gains.
- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - Bring key supplementary diagnostics (rotation drift, CSA error) into the main paper.
  - Fix citation formatting issues; define E{χ} explicitly on first use; ensure all algorithmic constants match the tutorial defaults.

Overall assessment
A solid and practical contribution that makes a full-covariance CMA-ES substantially faster and lighter without sacrificing optimization performance. The paper is well executed and, with the additional empirical diagnostics and broader baseline comparisons suggested above, would make a valuable addition to the NIPS program.
