The manuscript presents a new CMA-ES variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that approximates the original covariance adaptation (typically O(d^3)) with an O(μ d^2) scheme, where d is the problem dimension and μ the number of effective samples per iteration (usually O(log d)). The central idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of storing and decomposing the full covariance (or its square root). The resulting deviation from the original CMA-ES arises because the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) in CMA-ES involves the inverse matrix square root and implicitly a (random) orthogonal factor Et each iteration; the proposed method avoids explicitly recovering Et. Under fairly stringent assumptions (notably, convergence of a normalized sequence of covariance matrices to a positive definite limit), the authors argue asymptotically that Et tends to the identity, making the two formulations effectively equivalent. The paper supports correctness and speed improvements with standard runtime and convergence plots on canonical test functions, and compares against two strong CMA-ES baselines that delay full covariance updates. Overall, this is a solid and useful contribution for accelerating CMA-ES, a leading approach for nonconvex black-box optimization when gradient or higher-order information is unavailable or ineffective. The method achieves substantial speedups without observable degradation in optimization performance on synthetic benchmarks.

A general caveat for any CMA-ES acceleration is that, in many practical settings, wall-clock time is dominated by the cost of black-box function evaluations, rendering the algorithmic overhead comparatively minor. Moreover, typical application dimensions are often only a few dozen variables. The targeted regime here—cheap function evaluations (millisecond scale or less) and moderate-to-large d where CMA-ES overhead matters—is important but may be less common. Recognizing that strong theoretical guarantees for CMA-ES are challenging, I suggest several additional empirical analyses that could further strengthen the paper: First, track the evolution of Et along representative optimization trajectories (e.g., via appropriate norms), by running the full CMA-ES and simultaneously computing the approximation for comparison; this would provide direct evidence of Et’s fluctuations over time. Second, for completeness, include results for a CMA-ES variant that recomputes the matrix square root every iteration (i.e., without delay strategies) to isolate the baseline algorithmic cost. Third, on quadratic objectives, perform a controlled study where the eigenvalue spectrum is continuously varied (cf. Stich and Müller, PPSN 2012) and report both solution quality and the Et approximation error as functions of spectral distribution. Fourth, complement the synthetic tests with a real-world experiment where speed genuinely matters and dimensionality remains manageable, for instance in policy search for real-time robotics.
