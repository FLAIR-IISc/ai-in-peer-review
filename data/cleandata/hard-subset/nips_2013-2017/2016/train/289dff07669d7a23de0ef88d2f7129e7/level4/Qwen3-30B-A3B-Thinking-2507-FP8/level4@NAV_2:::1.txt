

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root operation in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor. The authors provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) showing that the rotational error introduced by this change vanishes asymptotically as the algorithm converges, and demonstrate empirically that the method maintains identical function evaluation counts to the standard CMA-ES while significantly reducing runtime complexity (O(μd²) vs. O(d³)) and memory requirements. The approach builds on prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) but distinguishes itself by providing a convergence argument and extending the method to the full CMA-ES framework, unlike previous restricted variants. The paper connects naturally to NIPS-relevant work on information-geometric optimization (Akimoto et al., 2012; Hansen, 2015) and machine learning applications of CMA-ES (Gomez et al., 2008; Heidrich-Meisner & Igel, 2009).

The paper excels in all four evaluation criteria:  
**Quality**: The theoretical analysis (Lemma 1) rigorously addresses the convergence of the Cholesky-based CSA, and the empirical validation across 100 trials on 5 benchmark functions (including rotated, non-separable, and ill-conditioned problems) confirms no degradation in function evaluations. The comparison to CMA-ES-Ref (with delayed matrix decomposition) and CMA-ES/d provides strong evidence for the runtime benefits.  
**Clarity**: The exposition is exceptionally clear, with Algorithm 1 and 2 precisely detailing the Cholesky update, and Section 4’s experimental setup (dimensions d=4–256, 100 trials per function) is fully reproducible. The discussion of eigenvalue extraction as a free byproduct of the Cholesky factor is particularly valuable.  
**Originality**: While prior work (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) explored Cholesky updates for restricted CMA-ES variants, this paper uniquely extends it to the full standard CMA-ES with theoretical grounding, resolving the key gap of justifying the CSA modification. The approach is distinct from diagonal approximations (Ros & Hansen, 2008) and delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001).  
**Significance**: The reduction in both time (up to 20× faster at d=64) and space complexity is transformative for scaling CMA-ES to high-dimensional ML tasks (e.g., neural network policy search), where covariance matrix updates are a known bottleneck. The method’s compatibility with existing CMA-ES implementations (via Shark library integration) ensures immediate practical impact.  

The only minor concern is the observation in Figure 2 regarding Cigar/Discus at d=256, where the runtime advantage is less pronounced than in other benchmarks. This likely stems from matrix size exceeding the L2 cache (as noted in Section 4), and the authors should clarify this in the revision. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a compelling case for adopting Cholesky-CMA-ES as the default variant in medium/large-scale optimization. Given its direct relevance to NIPS topics (derivative-free optimization, reinforcement learning, information geometry), this contribution is highly significant and should be accepted.
