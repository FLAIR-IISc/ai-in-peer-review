

This paper proposes using Cholesky decomposition instead of eigen-decomposition in CMA-ES to reduce time and storage complexity. The writing is clear, and the experimental results—including significant speedup rates—are promising. The method appears reasonable and could potentially become a standard CMA-ES variant. However, the paper falls short of NIPS standards due to critical issues in novelty, theoretical rigor, and completeness.

**Originality and Technical Contribution.** The core method is nearly identical to the rank-one covariance update technique in "A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies" (Hansen et al., 2014). While the authors frame their approach as a "Cholesky-based alternative," the algorithmic steps (e.g., covariance update, path computation) are functionally equivalent to the prior work. The paper fails to demonstrate any non-trivial extension, such as a novel theoretical insight, improved convergence guarantees, or significant performance gains beyond what is already established. This constitutes a trivial adaptation, not a meaningful contribution for a venue like NIPS.

**Essential Theoretical Gaps.** Three critical omissions undermine the paper's validity:  
1. **Equivalence to Formula (2):** The paper claims the Cholesky method implements Equation (2) but provides no proof or derivation. Given the centrality of this claim, a brief algebraic verification (even if referenced to prior work) is essential for reproducibility and credibility.  
2. **Computational Complexity:** The stated complexity O(μd²) matches CMA-ES-Ref. The paper must rigorously justify *why* the proposed method is faster by:  
   - Using Θ(μd²) notation to clarify tight bounds,  
   - Quantifying constant-factor improvements (e.g., through flop counts or cache efficiency),  
   - Explicitly contrasting implementation details (e.g., reduced matrix operations) that yield practical speedups.  
3. **Path Error Claim:** The assertion that "error in the path is small and decreases over time" contradicts Hansen's empirical observations in CMA-ES. Without a deterministic theoretical justification (which the paper lacks), the claim must be validated experimentally. Comparing only final performance metrics is insufficient; the paper should present *trajectories* of path error (e.g., over generations) and compare them to standard CMA-ES.

**Minor Technical Errors.** Several notation inconsistencies weaken the presentation:  
- Algorithm 2: `wk` should be `α_k` (line 2).  
- Table 1: `n` should be `d` (dimensionality).  
- Line 185: `F` should be `f` (objective function).  
These errors suggest insufficient proofreading and distract from the technical content.

**Conclusion.** While the experimental results are compelling, the paper’s failure to establish novelty, address critical theoretical gaps, and correct basic errors renders it unsuitable for NIPS. The method does not advance the state of the art beyond prior work, and the incomplete analysis risks misleading readers about its advantages. The authors should either substantially revise the paper to clarify novel contributions (e.g., by extending the prior method with new theoretical results) or retract it. As written, the work lacks the originality and rigor expected at NIPS. I recommend rejection.
