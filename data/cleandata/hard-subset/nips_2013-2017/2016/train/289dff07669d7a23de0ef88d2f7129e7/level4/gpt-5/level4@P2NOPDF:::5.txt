This manuscript proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a new update scheme for CMA-ES that reduces computational complexity from O(d^3) to O(μ d^2), where d is the search dimension and μ is the per-iteration sample size (typically O(log d)). The key idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix (and its square root). The resulting algorithm avoids estimating a random orthogonal matrix E_t at each iteration. Under fairly restrictive assumptions—specifically, that a sequence of covariance matrices converges to an unknown target—the authors argue that E_t converges to the identity, rendering the proposed scheme asymptotically equivalent to standard CMA-ES.

The paper evaluates the method using standard runtime and convergence plots on common test functions and compares it to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates in different ways. The results indicate substantial speedups without measurable loss in optimization quality on the presented synthetic benchmarks.

Overall, this is a solid and relevant contribution to accelerating CMA-ES, a leading method for nonconvex black-box optimization when derivative information is unavailable or unhelpful. The proposed approach provides a meaningful computational reduction with no observed degradation in solution quality on the reported tests.

That said, a persistent limitation of speed-up proposals for CMA-ES is practical relevance: in many real-world applications, the runtime is dominated by evaluations of the black-box objective, making the optimizer’s overhead negligible. Furthermore, typical CMA-ES use cases often involve problem dimensions in the tens. The regime where optimizer cost matters most—higher dimensions combined with very fast evaluations (milliseconds or less)—is narrower and may be less common in practice, though it is precisely the scenario targeted here.

Given the difficulty of obtaining strong theoretical guarantees for algorithms as intricate as CMA-ES, additional empirical evidence could further strengthen the paper:

- Track the evolution of E_t along representative optimization trajectories. For example, run standard CMA-ES while simultaneously computing the Cholesky-based approximation, and plot norms of E_t − I over time. This would provide direct evidence on the magnitude and dynamics of the rotation component the new scheme omits.

- Report wall-clock timings and performance for standard CMA-ES (without delay strategies) alongside the proposed method and the delayed-update variants. Including raw runtime breakdowns (sampling, updates, and function evaluations) would clarify where the observed gains originate.

- On quadratic functions, conduct a systematic study where the eigenvalue spectrum is varied continuously (e.g., following Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012). Compare solution quality and the error in E_t as a function of spectral conditioning and distribution. This would illuminate when the approximation is tight and when it may deteriorate.

- Add a real-world experiment to complement the simulations. A setting such as policy gradient optimization in real-time robotics, where evaluations are fast and speed matters while dimensionality remains moderate, would be an apt testbed.

In summary, the paper presents a well-motivated, carefully engineered approximation that achieves notable computational savings with competitive performance. Clarifying the conditions under which E_t remains close to identity in practice, expanding the empirical comparisons to include vanilla CMA-ES with detailed runtime breakdowns, exploring performance across controlled quadratic spectra, and demonstrating benefits on a real application would substantially enhance the impact and credibility of the contribution.
