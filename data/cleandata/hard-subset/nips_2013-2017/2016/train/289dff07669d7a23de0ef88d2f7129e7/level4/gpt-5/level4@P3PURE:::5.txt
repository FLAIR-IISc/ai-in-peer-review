This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel update scheme for CMA-ES that reduces the per-iteration complexity from O(d^3) to O(μ d^2), where d is the problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log d)). The principal idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix (and its square root). The resulting difference from the original CMA-ES arises from not estimating the random orthogonal matrix E_t at every step. Under fairly restrictive assumptions—namely, convergence of the sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target covariance—the authors argue that E_t converges to the identity, making the proposed scheme asymptotically equivalent to the original. Empirical results on standard synthetic benchmarks, including runtime and convergence plots, and comparisons against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates by different mechanisms, illustrate both the speed-up and the absence of performance degradation.

Overall, this is a solid and practically motivated contribution to accelerating a celebrated algorithm that is arguably among the best for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the objective’s topology and first- or higher-order information is unavailable or unhelpful. The proposed method substantially reduces computational overhead and maintains solution quality on the reported test cases.

That said, the practical impact of any CMA-ES speed-up is often constrained by a common regime in which the wall-clock time is dominated by the black-box oracle (objective function) rather than by the optimizer itself. In many typical applications of CMA-ES, dimensionality is only a few tens of variables, and the algorithmic overhead is negligible relative to function evaluations. The runtime of CMA-ES becomes a bottleneck primarily when individual evaluations take milliseconds or less and the dimensionality is higher; the method targets precisely this niche, which may be less common in practice.

Because strong theoretical guarantees are notoriously difficult for CMA-ES-like schemes, I recommend additional empirical evidence to further substantiate and contextualize the claims:

- Track the evolution of E_t along selected optimization trajectories, e.g., in different norms. A useful setup would run the full CMA-ES while simultaneously computing the Cholesky-based approximation, enabling a direct comparison that reveals the magnitude and temporal fluctuations of E_t.
- Report actual runtimes and performance of the baseline CMA-ES without any delay strategies, for completeness and to quantify absolute speed-ups attributable specifically to the proposed update.
- On quadratic functions, conduct a controlled study that varies the spectrum continuously (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012). Compare solution quality and the error in E_t as a function of the spectral distribution.
- Include a real-world experiment to complement the simulations. Policy-gradient optimization in real-time robotics is a promising setting where speed matters and parameter dimensionality is moderate.

In summary, the paper addresses an important computational bottleneck in CMA-ES with a clear, well-motivated approach, provides convincing initial evidence, and would be strengthened by the additional experiments above to clarify when and how the benefits manifest in practice.
