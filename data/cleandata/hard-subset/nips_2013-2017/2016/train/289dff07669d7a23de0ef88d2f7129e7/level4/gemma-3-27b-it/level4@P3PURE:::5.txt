The manuscript presents a novel update scheme for the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm, approximating the original covariance matrix adaptation (with complexity O(d<sup>3</sup>)) with a scheme that achieves a complexity of O(μd<sup>2</sup>), where d is the optimization problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix instead of a full covariance matrix (and its square root). A key difference between the Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES lies in the omission of estimating a (random) orthogonal matrix E<sub>t</sub> in each step. Under restrictive assumptions regarding the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target covariance matrix, the authors provide an asymptotic argument demonstrating that the sequence of E<sub>t</sub> will converge to the identity matrix, and thus the two schemes become equivalent. The validity and performance benefits of the Cholesky-CMA-ES are illustrated through standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark objective functions, and comparisons to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives employing delayed covariance matrix updates.

This paper makes a significant contribution to accelerating the well-established CMA-ES algorithm, which is widely regarded as a leading method for non-convex black-box optimization, particularly when limited prior knowledge exists about the objective function’s topology and first or higher-order derivative information is unavailable or unhelpful. The proposed method demonstrably speeds up the algorithm considerably and exhibits no performance degradation on the synthetic test cases. 

However, a common challenge with any speed-up proposal for CMA-ES is that the overall optimization process is often dominated by the runtime of the black-box oracle (the objective function), rendering the CMA-ES runtime itself negligible in comparison. Furthermore, in typical applications of CMA-ES, problem dimensionality rarely exceeds a few tens of variables. The runtime of CMA-ES becomes truly important only when a single function evaluation is very fast (on the order of milliseconds or less) and the dimensionality is higher, which is the target scenario here – although this may be less representative of many practical applications. 

Given the inherent difficulty of obtaining strong theoretical guarantees for complex algorithms like CMA-ES, I recommend a few additional empirical experiments and visualizations to strengthen the presentation of the results. First, it would be insightful to observe the evolution of E<sub>t</sub> along selected optimization trajectories, perhaps by monitoring its behavior in different norms. An experiment comparing the full CMA-ES to the proposed approximation, calculating both simultaneously, could provide further empirical evidence regarding the fluctuations of E<sub>t</sub> over time. Second, including the runtime and performance of standard CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies would offer a valuable point of comparison. Third, while the chosen test functions are common within the evolutionary optimization community, a simulation study on quadratic functions with a continuously varied spectrum of quadratic forms (as suggested in, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012) would be beneficial to analyze solution quality and the error in E<sub>t</sub> as a function of spectral distribution. Finally, a real-world experiment to complement the simulation results would be highly valuable. For example, policy gradients in real-time robotics applications might be a suitable choice, given the importance of speed and relatively constrained parameter spaces.
