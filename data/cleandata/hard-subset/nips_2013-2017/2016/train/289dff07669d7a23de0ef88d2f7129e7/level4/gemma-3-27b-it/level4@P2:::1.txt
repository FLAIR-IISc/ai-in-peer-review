The covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) is a leading derivative-free optimization algorithm, effectively adapting the parameters of a multivariate normal distribution through metric adaptation. This paper introduces a novel variant of CMA-ES employing triangular Cholesky factors for covariance matrix updates. This approach offers significant advantages over existing methods, notably reduced computational complexity and memory requirements. The authors provide a thorough numerical evaluation, demonstrating that their method maintains the performance of the standard CMA-ES—requiring a comparable number of function evaluations—while substantially reducing wall-clock time, especially in higher dimensions. 

This work represents a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES has the potential to become a default variant for medium-to-large-scale problems, offering clear benefits including reduced memory footprint, lower computational cost, and readily available eigenvalues of the covariance matrix. The paper is well-written and the approach is meticulously evaluated. Its relevance to NIPS is particularly strong, given CMA-ES’s connections to machine learning, information geometry, and applications in reinforcement learning and supervised learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is theoretically justified, and the analysis suggests that the approximation introduced by using the Cholesky factor instead of the matrix square root does not significantly degrade performance.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results convincingly demonstrate the speedup achieved by the Cholesky-CMA-ES, particularly as dimensionality increases.
*   **Practical Significance:** The reduction in memory and computational complexity makes this a highly practical improvement to a widely used algorithm.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed method.
*   **Relevance to NIPS:** The CMA-ES algorithm finds applications in areas well-represented at NIPS, such as reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Implementation Details:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implementation, specifically addressing comparability across different implementations (programming language, compiler optimization, etc.).
*   **Dimensionality & Benchmark Function Performance Discrepancy:** The claim that performance improvements increase with dimensionality isn't universally supported by the results (e.g., Cigar and Discus functions at d=256). A brief explanation for these observations would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Scaling Limitations:** While the method offers improvements, the quadratic scaling with dimensionality will eventually become a bottleneck for very high-dimensional problems. Acknowledging this limitation and discussing potential future directions would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   This work provides a significant practical improvement to a state-of-the-art optimization algorithm.
*   The theoretical justification for the proposed method is sound.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a clear advantage in terms of runtime.
*   The work addresses a critical bottleneck (covariance matrix update) in CMA-ES, making it more applicable to larger-scale problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The improvement, while significant, may be incremental for certain problem types (as evidenced by the inconsistencies observed in Figure 2).
*   The quadratic scaling with dimensionality remains a limitation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated improvement to a highly influential optimization algorithm. The Cholesky-CMA-ES has the potential to become a standard variant of the algorithm, and its adoption would be a valuable contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses identified are not substantial enough to detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.
