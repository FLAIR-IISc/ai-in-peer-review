This paper proposes the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that achieves optimal time and space complexity for the covariance matrix update. The standard CMA-ES has a complexity of O(d³) for this update, which can be a bottleneck in high-dimensional optimization problems (where d is the problem dimension) with inexpensive objective function evaluations. The central idea of the proposed method is to maintain and directly update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than the full matrix itself. This reduces the time complexity to O(µd²), where µ is the number of selected samples per iteration (typically logarithmic in d).

This approach requires an approximation within the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which in the standard CMA-ES relies on the inverse of the covariance matrix square root (C⁻¹/²). The authors replace this with the inverse of the Cholesky factor (A⁻¹). The difference between these two terms is an orthogonal matrix, E_t. The authors provide a theoretical argument that under the assumption that the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the sequence of these orthogonal matrices E_t will converge to the identity matrix, making the approximation asymptotically exact. The paper empirically validates the Cholesky-CMA-ES on several standard benchmark functions, comparing its performance in terms of function evaluations and wall-clock time against state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that use a delayed update scheme.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a well-known limitation of CMA-ES, arguably one of the most powerful derivative-free optimization algorithms. The proposed method provides a principled way to reduce the computational overhead of the covariance matrix update to its asymptotic minimum, which is a valuable contribution.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed modification is technically sound. The derivation of the O(µd²) update is a non-trivial extension of prior work. The theoretical justification for the approximation in the CSA mechanism, while based on convergence assumptions, provides a strong rationale for why the method should perform comparably to the original CMA-ES.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-established, the new algorithm is described precisely, and the theoretical argument is easy to follow.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the primary claims of the paper. The Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a substantial wall-clock speed-up over existing methods without any significant degradation in the number of function evaluations required for convergence.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While this is a solid paper, its arguments and impact could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Practical Relevance:** A key consideration for any CMA-ES speed-up is that in many practical applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning of deep models), the runtime is overwhelmingly dominated by the objective function evaluation. The speed-up from this method is only truly significant when function evaluations are fast and the dimensionality is high (e.g., d > 50). It would strengthen the paper to more explicitly define and motivate this target scenario, as it is a relatively niche, though important, use case.
2.  **Direct Empirical Validation of the Core Assumption:** The central argument for the validity of the CSA approximation rests on the convergence of the orthogonal matrix E_t to the identity matrix. However, this is not directly verified in the experiments. To provide stronger empirical evidence, it would be highly instructive to conduct an experiment where the "true" E_t is computed alongside the proposed algorithm, and a metric such as `||E_t E_{t-1}^T - I||_F` is plotted over the course of an optimization run. This would directly visualize whether the rotational error introduced by the approximation indeed diminishes as the algorithm converges.
3.  **More Systematic Benchmarking:** While the chosen test functions are standard in the evolutionary computation community, the analysis could be more rigorous. On quadratic functions, for example, a more systematic study varying the condition number and spectral properties of the Hessian (as in Stich and Müller, 2012) would provide deeper insight into the robustness of the approximation under different conditions.
4.  **Demonstration on a Real-World Problem:** The impact of the paper would be significantly enhanced by including an experiment on a real-world problem that fits the target scenario of fast evaluations and high dimensionality. The introduction mentions direct policy search in reinforcement learning, which could be an excellent application. Demonstrating a tangible wall-clock time advantage on a robotics or control task would make a more compelling case for the method's practical utility.
5.  **Inclusion of a Non-Delayed Baseline:** For completeness, the runtime comparisons would benefit from including the standard CMA-ES *without* any delayed update strategy, even if it is only feasible for lower dimensions. This would provide a clearer upper bound on the achievable speed-up compared to a naive implementation.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method for accelerating a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves optimal asymptotic time and space complexity for the covariance matrix update. The claims are supported by strong empirical results on synthetic benchmarks, showing a significant speed-up with no loss in optimization performance.
*   **Con-acceptance (or for revision):** The practical relevance of the speed-up is limited to a specific regime of problems (cheap functions, high dimensions) that could be better articulated. The core theoretical assumption about the approximation error is not directly verified empirically, and the experimental evaluation could be made more comprehensive with systematic tests and a real-world application.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that improves upon a widely-used algorithm. Addressing the points above, particularly the direct validation of the core assumption and the inclusion of a real-world application, would elevate this work from a good paper to an excellent one.
