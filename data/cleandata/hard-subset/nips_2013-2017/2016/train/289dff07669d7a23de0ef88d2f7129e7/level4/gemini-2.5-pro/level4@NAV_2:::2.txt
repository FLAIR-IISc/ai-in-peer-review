This paper presents Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce the algorithm's time and storage complexity. The standard CMA-ES has a computational bottleneck in updating the covariance matrix, which typically requires an O(d^3) eigendecomposition. The authors propose to instead maintain and update a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, which reduces the update complexity to O(µd^2). The core technical contribution lies in modifying the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism to work with the Cholesky factor. This involves approximating the inverse matrix square root C^{-1/2} with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor A^{-1}. The paper provides a theoretical argument that this approximation is valid under convergence and supports this claim with empirical evidence showing that the new variant achieves significant wall-clock speedups without increasing the number of function evaluations required for optimization.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and critical performance bottleneck in CMA-ES, one of the most powerful derivative-free optimization algorithms. By significantly reducing the computational overhead per iteration, this work makes CMA-ES more practical for higher-dimensional problems or scenarios where function evaluations are cheap. Given the frequent application of CMA-ES in machine learning for tasks like policy search and hyperparameter tuning, a more efficient, scalable, and numerically stable implementation is a highly valuable contribution that is likely to see wide adoption.
*   **Quality:** The proposed algorithm is technically sound, and the experimental results are compelling. The empirical comparison against standard CMA-ES implementations clearly demonstrates a substantial reduction in runtime across various benchmark functions and dimensions, validating the paper's primary claims. The resulting algorithm also achieves optimal O(d^2) storage complexity.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. It effectively motivates the problem, explains the proposed solution, and presents the results in an understandable manner.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

*   **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper's novelty could be better contextualized with respect to Krause and Igel (2015), which introduced a similar efficient rank-one Cholesky update for the (1+1)-CMA-ES. The current work extends this to the more general (µ,λ)-CMA-ES, which involves a rank-µ update. The submission would be strengthened by explicitly detailing the specific technical challenges involved in this generalization and clarifying why it represents a non-trivial step forward.
*   **Justification for the CSA Approximation:** A key part of the proposed method is the approximation made in the CSA path update. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 rests on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges, which is a strong condition for a stochastic algorithm. While the final performance results indirectly support the validity of this approximation, a more direct empirical validation would be more convincing. This leaves a central claim of the paper—that the algorithm's search behavior is unchanged—supported by incomplete evidence.
*   **Clarity of Complexity Discussion:** The complexity analysis, while correct, could be more insightful. The asymptotic improvement from O(d^3) to O(µd^2) is significant. However, providing a more detailed analysis that discusses the leading constant factors involved in the Cholesky update routines versus a full eigendecomposition would offer a clearer explanation for the dramatic practical speedups observed in the experiments.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  To improve self-containment and clarity, it would be beneficial to briefly outline or cite the derivation showing that applying the sequence of rank-one updates in Algorithm 2 to the Cholesky factor is equivalent to applying the full rank-µ update in Equation (2) to the covariance matrix C.
2.  The argument for the validity of the CSA approximation would be substantially strengthened by direct empirical evidence. Could you include an experiment that measures the discrepancy introduced by the approximation? For instance, during an optimization run, you could periodically compute the "true" path pσ using C^{-1/2} and report the cosine distance or angle relative to the path computed by your method. This would provide direct validation that the approximation does not significantly alter the search direction of the CSA mechanism.
3.  Please review the manuscript for minor notational inconsistencies to improve readability. For example:
    *   In Algorithm 2, the variable `b` is updated using `wk`, which appears to be a typo for `αk`.
    *   Table 1 uses `n` for dimensionality, whereas the rest of the paper uses `d`.
    *   In one instance, the objective function is referred to as 'F', but it is 'f' elsewhere.

In conclusion, this paper introduces a valuable and practical improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm with the potential for high impact. The demonstrated performance gains are impressive. However, the contribution would be more solid if the authors could better clarify the novelty over prior work and provide more direct empirical support for the critical approximation at the heart of their method. Addressing these points would make this a very strong submission.
