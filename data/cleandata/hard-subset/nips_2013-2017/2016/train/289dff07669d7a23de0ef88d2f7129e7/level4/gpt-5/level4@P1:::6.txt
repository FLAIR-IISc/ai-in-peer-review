Summary
This paper proposes a CMA-ES variant that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, avoiding explicit eigenvalue decompositions. The key change is to adapt the covariance via a sequence of rank-one updates applied directly to the Cholesky factor, yielding an O(µ d^2) time update with minimal memory. Because the standard CMA-ES’s step-size adaptation (CSA) relies on C−1/2, the authors replace it with A−1, where A is the lower-triangular Cholesky factor (C = A A^T). They justify this approximation by showing that the orthogonal factor in the RQ decomposition converges (or changes slowly), so the omission of the explicit rotation in CSA has diminishing effect on the norm of the cumulative path. Empirically, on standard benchmarks up to d = 256, their method achieves similar numbers of function evaluations to a reference CMA-ES, while substantially reducing wall-clock time and memory.

Relation to prior work
The idea of updating a Cholesky factor instead of a full covariance matrix has a long history in CMA-ES:
- Suttorp et al. (2009) proposed a Cholesky-based update using a non-triangular factorization and discussed numerical stability.
- Krause and Igel (2015) gave an O(d^2) triangular rank-one update compatible with (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES.
The present paper extends this line by applying the triangular rank-one update repeatedly to realize the rank-µ update of standard CMA-ES and by modifying CSA to use A−1 instead of C−1/2, arguing that this does not change evaluation complexity.

Quality
- Theory: The replacement of C−1/2 by A−1 in CSA is motivated via RQ/polar decomposition and a lemma showing that the sequence of rotation matrices E_t stabilizes as C_t’s shape converges (up to scale), implying vanishing error in the norm of the cumulative path due to exponential weighting. This is plausible, but it rests on convergence or slow variation of C_t, which is not guaranteed in general CMA-ES. The authors partially address this by measuring rotations empirically (supplementary). I recommend moving these measurements into the main paper and quantifying how ‖E_t E_{t−1}^T − I‖ behaves across problems and over time.
- Claims about numerical stability of non-triangular updates (Suttorp et al., 2009) contradict the original paper’s discussion. If the authors have evidence of instability in modern implementations or on specific testbeds, please provide quantitative comparisons (e.g., failure rates, condition numbers, divergence indicators).
- Experiments are thorough with respect to standard benchmarks and show clear wall-clock gains. However, I noticed that on the Cigar function (Figure b), the plateau appears at different objective values for CMA-ES-Ref vs. the proposed method. This might be an artifact of plotting medians; please verify and, if needed, clarify.
- Minor: “Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition … in each update step” is inaccurate. Proper implementations compute decompositions periodically; the paper itself later adopts such a delay for the reference implementation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. However:
- Naming is misleading. “Cholesky-CMA-ES” was introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) and is widely known. Please rename or clearly qualify your variant (e.g., “Triangular-Cholesky CMA-ES with CSA via A−1”) and explicitly position it relative to Suttorp et al.’s and Krause and Igel’s methods.
- Please make the algorithmic novelty explicit. As written, Algorithm 1 implements the rank-µ update by applying the known triangular rank-one update in a loop and replaces C−1/2 by A−1 in CSA. If there is more to the contribution, spell it out; otherwise, clarify that this is the central modification and justify its importance.

Originality
The approach is an incremental extension of Krause and Igel (2015) to standard CMA-ES: the main algorithmic change is to apply the rank-one triangular update µ times per iteration and to approximate CSA with A−1. The theoretical argument regarding the CSA approximation is useful, but overall the novelty is modest.

Significance
For scenarios where a full covariance matrix is required (non-separable, ill-conditioned problems), the proposed method appears to provide substantial wall-clock speedups (e.g., ~20x at d = 64) without increasing the number of function evaluations. This is a valuable engineering improvement. However, because the complexity remains O(µ d^2), it does not change the large-scale regime where low-rank or diagonal approximations are necessary. The claim of “Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity” should be qualified as “optimal among methods that maintain the full covariance matrix.” For separable problems or Sphere, diagonal or no covariance adaptation can be more efficient.

Empirical comparisons
The paper includes a “Suttorp-CMA-ES” variant, but the exposition and figures leave the role of this baseline ambiguous (e.g., omission from Figure 3). For a fair assessment:
- Compare empirically against Suttorp et al.’s Cholesky-based variant across all figures or, if space is an issue, include full plots in the supplement and the best-performing variants in the main text.
- Clarify algorithmic differences and expected numerical behavior relative to Suttorp et al. and Krause and Igel.

Pros
- Clear and practical speed/memory improvements when full covariance adaptation is needed.
- Straightforward to implement and integrate into existing CMA-ES codebases.
- Useful theoretical justification for the CSA approximation.
- Provides eigenvalues “for free” and supports efficient inversion of A.

Cons
- Algorithmic novelty is limited; largely a careful composition of known building blocks plus a CSA approximation.
- Naming clashes with established “Cholesky-CMA-ES” terminology.
- Claims about stability and “optimality” need qualification and stronger evidence.
- Theoretical guarantees rely on assumptions (convergence/slow change) that may not always hold; empirical rotation analysis should be moved to the main paper.

Recommendations for revision
a) Resolve the naming issue to avoid confusion with Suttorp et al. (2009).
b) Provide comprehensive empirical comparisons with Suttorp et al.’s and Krause and Igel’s methods; update all main figures accordingly, with full results in the supplement if necessary.
c) Clearly state the core algorithmic contribution (the rank-µ loop with triangular rank-one updates and CSA via A−1) and justify its impact.
d) Substantiate claims about numerical stability with quantitative evidence.
e) Bring rotation-invariance diagnostics (e.g., behavior of E_t E_{t−1}^T) into the main text.
f) Correct the statement about per-iteration eigenvalue decompositions.

Overall assessment
This is a solid engineering improvement with strong practical benefits for full-covariance CMA-ES, but the contribution is incremental. With the requested clarifications, additional baselines, and more careful positioning, the paper would be much stronger. In its current form, I lean toward rejection; with the above revisions, I would be inclined to support acceptance.
