This paper proposes a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art stochastic optimization algorithm, which leverages triangular Cholesky factors to update the covariance matrix. The main idea is to reduce the time and space complexity associated with the traditional CMA-ES algorithm, particularly when dealing with large dimensions.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed method, including its theoretical justification and empirical evaluation. They demonstrate that the new update scheme does not compromise the performance of the standard CMA-ES in terms of the number of function evaluations required to reach a given target. However, it significantly reduces the wall-clock time, especially for large dimensions.

The paper is well-written, and the approach is carefully evaluated. The authors provide a detailed comparison of their method with other CMA-ES variants, including the state-of-the-art implementation with delayed covariance matrix decomposition. The results show that the proposed method achieves a significant speedup, particularly for high-dimensional problems.

One of the key advantages of the proposed method is its ability to reduce the memory requirements and computational complexity associated with the traditional CMA-ES algorithm. Additionally, the method provides the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix without additional costs, which can be useful for monitoring the conditioning of the optimization problem.

The paper is relevant to the NIPS community, as the CMA-ES algorithm has strong connections to machine learning, information geometry, and reinforcement learning. The proposed method has the potential to become a default variant of the CMA-ES algorithm for medium to large-scale optimization problems.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient method for updating the covariance matrix in the CMA-ES algorithm.
* The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation of the proposed method.
* The results show a significant speedup compared to other CMA-ES variants, particularly for high-dimensional problems.

Weaknesses:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the different implementations used in the experiments were made comparable in terms of CPU.
* The authors mention that the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences in wall-clock time between the proposed method and other CMA-ES variants. However, this does not seem to hold for the Cigar and Discus functions in dimension 256. A more detailed explanation for this discrepancy would be helpful.
