This manuscript describes a novel CMA-ES update scheme, the Cholesky-CMA-ES, that approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation (with complexity O(d^3)) by a novel scheme that runs in O(μd^2) where d is the optimization problem dimension and μ the number of samples per iterations (typically O(log(d)). The principle idea of the update is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix rather than a full covariance matrix (and corresponding sqrt of that).

The manuscript relates the new method to previous work on CMA-ES, such as Hansen and Ostermeier (2001), and discusses the limitations of existing approaches to reducing the runtime complexity of CMA-ES, including the use of delayed updates and restricted model complexity. The authors also provide a clear and concise description of the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm and its components, including the update rule for the Cholesky factor and the modification of the cumulative step-size adaptation algorithm.

The theoretical justification for the convergence of the new algorithm is based on the assumption that the sequence of covariance matrices converges to a certain shape, and the authors provide a lemma that establishes the convergence of the rotation matrix Et. However, as noted by the authors, this assumption may not always hold in practice, and the validity of the approximation is ultimately demonstrated through empirical results.

The experimental evaluation of the Cholesky-CMA-ES is thorough and well-executed, with comparisons to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives that delay the full covariance matrix update by different means. The results show that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant speed-up over the other algorithms, while maintaining similar optimization performance.

Strengths of the paper include the clear and concise description of the algorithm and its components, the thorough experimental evaluation, and the demonstration of a significant speed-up over existing algorithms. Weaknesses include the reliance on a restrictive assumption for the theoretical justification, and the limited evaluation of the algorithm on real-world problems.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores high on quality, as the algorithm is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results. The paper also scores high on clarity, as the writing is clear and concise, and the algorithm is well-described. The originality of the paper is also high, as the Cholesky-CMA-ES is a novel approach to reducing the runtime complexity of CMA-ES. The significance of the paper is somewhat lower, as the algorithm is primarily targeted at scenarios where the runtime of the CMA-ES is a significant factor, which may not be the case in many practical applications.

Arguments for acceptance include:

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound algorithm for reducing the runtime complexity of CMA-ES.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed, and demonstrates a significant speed-up over existing algorithms.
* The paper is well-written and clear, making it easy to follow and understand.

Arguments against acceptance include:

* The theoretical justification relies on a restrictive assumption that may not always hold in practice.
* The evaluation of the algorithm is limited to synthetic test cases, and does not include real-world problems.
* The significance of the paper is somewhat lower, as the algorithm is primarily targeted at scenarios where the runtime of the CMA-ES is a significant factor, which may not be the case in many practical applications.

To strengthen the presentation of the results, I would suggest the following additional experiments and plots:

* A simulation study where the spectrum of quadratic forms is varied continuously, and the solution quality and error in Et are compared in dependence of the spectral distribution.
* A real-world experiment that accompanies the present simulation results, such as policy gradients in real-time robotics applications.
* A plot of the evolution of Et on selected optimization trajectories, e.g., in different norms, to provide further empirical evidence about the actual fluctuations of Et over time.
* A comparison of the actual runtimes and performance of CMA-ES without delay strategies, for completeness.
