Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a computationally efficient variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES). The main idea is to maintain and update the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C (with C = A A^T), and to modify the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}. This avoids explicit eigen- or square-root decompositions of C, yielding O(µ d^2) per-iteration time and minimal O(d^2) storage, where µ is the number of selected elites. The authors build on prior work that either delays matrix decompositions, restricts the covariance model (e.g., diagonal or limited-rank approximations), or updates non-triangular factorizations. Compared to Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015), the novelty here is an extension to standard CMA-ES with CSA and rank-µ updates, together with a theoretical argument that the CSA modification preserves performance.

The key theoretical justification is that the discrepancy introduced by using A^{-1} instead of C^{-1/2} in CSA amounts to a sequence of orthogonal rotations E_t that converges as the normalized covariance converges; due to the exponential weighting in CSA, the contribution of nonstationary rotations vanishes asymptotically. Empirically, on rotated and standard benchmarks up to d = 256, Cholesky-CMA-ES requires essentially the same number of function evaluations as a reference CMA-ES while substantially reducing wall-clock time, with speedups increasing in d. The method is implemented in Shark with LAPACK support.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation of rank-one updates on triangular Cholesky factors and the O(µ d^2) update complexity are sound and practically relevant. The lemma relating convergence of the normalized covariance to vanishing rotation error in CSA is clear and plausible, though it rests on convergence assumptions typically satisfied in expectation rather than almost surely. The argument appropriately notes why triangular parameterization avoids non-identifiability present in non-triangular factorizations.
- Numerical stability: Using a triangular Cholesky factor is known to be numerically stable: it maintains positive definiteness by construction, supports stable rank-one updates/downdates, and triangular solves yield A^{-1} implicitly without explicitly inverting C. However, the paper could better substantiate the “numerically more stable” claim by discussing condition numbers, update formulas (e.g., cholupdate-style stability), and failure modes, and by comparing accumulated round-off error versus repeated eigen-decompositions.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are well chosen (Sphere, Cigar/Discus/Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) and include random rotations to test invariance. Results support equal evaluation counts and large runtime reductions, with sensible scaling in d. That said, statistical reporting (variability, confidence intervals) and broader settings (noisy objectives, constraints) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with useful algorithmic pseudocode. A few issues to address:
- Minor typos and formatting in references (incomplete years, inconsistent citation brackets).
- Clarify that “ω(d^2)” is used informally to indicate super-quadratic runtime (e.g., O(d^3) eigen-decomposition).
- The statement that “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix” is not generally correct. The eigenvalues of C = A A^T are the squares of A’s singular values, not the squares of its diagonal entries. Please correct this and, if needed, explain how you monitor conditioning without computing eigenvalues (e.g., via periodic eigendecompositions, Gershgorin bounds, or estimates based on triangular factors).

Originality
While Cholesky updates for CMA-ES variants have been explored, extending a triangular-factor update to full CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and modifying CSA with a convergence-based justification is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution. The asymptotically optimal time/storage characterization within full-covariance CMA-ES is also valuable.

Significance
The paper offers substantial practical impact: for problems where objective evaluations are fast relative to matrix updates (e.g., surrogate-assisted or low-cost simulations), or for moderate-to-large d where full covariance is still desired, the speedups are compelling without sacrificing evaluation efficiency. The method remains O(d^2); for very high-dimensional problems, limited-memory or low-rank approaches (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) will still be necessary. Demonstrating interoperability or hybridization would broaden applicability.

Pros
- Clear algorithmic contribution with a simple drop-in replacement for standard CMA-ES.
- Strong empirical evidence of reduced wall-clock time with unchanged evaluation counts.
- Careful theoretical argument showing CSA modification should not harm performance asymptotically.
- Optimal storage and efficient availability of A^{-1} via triangular solves; practical implementation in Shark.

Cons
- “Numerical stability” is asserted more than analyzed; lacks quantitative error/conditioning analysis.
- Theoretical guarantees hinge on convergence assumptions; early-phase behavior when C changes rapidly is not analyzed or tested in depth.
- Limited experimental breadth: no noisy or constrained benchmarks; no very high-d comparisons or hybrid methods.
- Minor inaccuracies and reference formatting issues; claim about eigenvalues from Cholesky diagonals is incorrect.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Can you provide a bound on the discrepancy in CSA when replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}, e.g., in terms of ||E_t E_{t-1}^T − I|| and the exponential weighting? An empirical plot of this discrepancy over time on challenging functions (e.g., Rosenbrock, rotated Ellipsoid) would be useful.
- Please elaborate on “numerical stability”: include a brief analysis of round-off accumulation in rank-one updates, safeguards to maintain SPD (e.g., jitter), and comparisons to periodic eigen-decompositions.
- Could you report results on noisy benchmarks and constrained settings to assess robustness when C does not settle quickly?
- Consider evaluating or discussing combinations with low-rank/limited-memory approaches (e.g., LM-CMA) to address very high-dimensional problems often encountered in direct policy search for neural networks.
- Clarify the computational cost of forming or applying A^{-1} in practice (triangular solves vs. explicit inversion), and how often it is needed within an iteration.

Recommendation
This is a solid, practically important contribution with a clear path to adoption in CMA-ES toolchains. With minor corrections and expanded discussion of numerical stability and CSA discrepancy, it would be a strong addition to the conference. I lean toward acceptance.
