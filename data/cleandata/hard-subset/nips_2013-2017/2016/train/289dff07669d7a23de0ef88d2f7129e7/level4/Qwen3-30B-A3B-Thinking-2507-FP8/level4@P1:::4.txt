

This paper proposes an efficient variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the standard matrix square root computation with a triangular Cholesky factorization to reduce both time and space complexity. The authors make a compelling case that this modification maintains the algorithm's performance while significantly improving computational efficiency, particularly for high-dimensional problems. This work builds upon previous attempts to optimize CMA-ES, such as the delayed update strategy [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001] and the use of Cholesky factors in specialized variants [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015], but provides a theoretically justified approach that works for the standard CMA-ES.

The paper's main contribution is its theoretical justification for why replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism does not deteriorate the algorithm's performance. The authors demonstrate that the error introduced by this replacement vanishes as the algorithm converges, supported by Lemma 1 which shows that the orthogonal matrix in the RQ-decomposition converges to the identity matrix. This is a significant advancement over previous work that lacked such theoretical grounding. The authors also provide a detailed algorithmic description (Algorithm 1) and implement their approach within the Shark machine learning library, using LAPACK for high efficiency.

The paper excels in quality, providing both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The theoretical argument for why the modification doesn't affect performance is rigorous and well-motivated. The experimental results (Figure 1-3) convincingly demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of objective function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but achieves substantially reduced wall-clock time, with the speedup increasing with dimensionality (up to 20x for d=64). This represents a meaningful practical improvement for applications where computational efficiency matters.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-structured sections that build logically from background to the proposed method and its validation. The algorithm descriptions are precise, and the experimental setup is well-documented. However, the explanation of numerical stability could be strengthened. While the authors mention "numerically more stable" computation (in the draft review), they don't fully explain why this is the case. A more detailed discussion of the numerical advantages of working with triangular Cholesky factors versus the square root would be valuable.

The originality of the work is strong. While previous work has explored Cholesky factorization for CMA-ES variants, this paper provides the first theoretically justified approach for the standard CMA-ES that maintains performance guarantees. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from prior contributions, particularly by addressing the theoretical gap in the CSA mechanism.

The significance of this work is substantial, particularly for real-world applications of CMA-ES. The authors correctly identify that updating and storing the covariance matrix can dominate the runtime in high-dimensional settings, which is especially relevant for applications like direct policy search in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning in supervised learning. The Cholesky-CMA-ES variant directly addresses this bottleneck without compromising solution quality.

Strengths:
- Theoretical justification for the Cholesky factor replacement in the CSA mechanism
- Empirical validation showing identical function evaluation counts but significantly reduced runtime
- Optimal time and space complexity for covariance matrix updates
- Clear algorithmic description with implementation details
- Strong connection to practical applications in machine learning

Weaknesses:
- Limited explanation of why the Cholesky-based approach is numerically more stable
- The discussion of practical applicability to extremely high-dimensional problems could be expanded
- The paper doesn't fully address how this approach integrates with other recent CMA-ES improvements

The authors raise a valid point about combining their approach with low-dimensional approximation methods [Loshchilov, 2015] for extremely high-dimensional problems. While the paper correctly notes that the Cholesky-CMA-ES scales quadratically with dimensionality, which becomes problematic for very large d, the authors don't explore how their method might complement existing dimensionality reduction techniques. A brief discussion of how Cholesky-CMA-ES could be combined with methods like Loshchilov's [Loshchilov, 2015] for even larger problems would strengthen the practical significance of the work.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a clear, technically sound contribution to the optimization literature. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a well-motivated improvement that addresses a genuine bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES algorithm. The theoretical justification is particularly strong, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should expand their discussion of numerical stability and consider adding a brief paragraph on how their approach might integrate with dimensionality reduction techniques for extremely high-dimensional problems. These additions would strengthen the paper's contribution without requiring major rework.
