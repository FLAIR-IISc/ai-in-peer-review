### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) aimed at reducing its time and space complexity. The standard CMA-ES has a per-iteration complexity dominated by an O(d³) eigenvalue decomposition needed to compute the matrix square root of the covariance matrix, C. The authors propose replacing this step with a direct update of the lower-triangular Cholesky factor, A (where C = AAᵀ), which can be performed in O(d²) time.

The primary technical challenge in this substitution is the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the term C⁻¹ᐟ² for normalization. This term cannot be obtained directly from the Cholesky factor A without an expensive O(d³) computation. The core contribution of this paper is to approximate C⁻¹ᐟ² with A⁻¹, the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor. The authors provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) suggesting that the error introduced by this approximation diminishes as the algorithm converges and the shape of the covariance matrix stabilizes.

Empirical results on several benchmark functions demonstrate that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant speedup in wall-clock time compared to the standard implementation, without degrading performance in terms of the number of function evaluations required to reach the optimum.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significant Practical Impact:** The experimental results are compelling. The Cholesky-CMA-ES provides a substantial reduction in runtime, especially for higher-dimensional problems (e.g., a 20x speedup at d=64), with no apparent loss in optimization performance. Given the widespread use of CMA-ES in machine learning for tasks like hyperparameter tuning and policy search, this improved efficiency is a major practical contribution that could lead to its adoption as a new standard implementation.

2.  **Sound and Elegant Approach:** The idea of updating the Cholesky factor directly is a well-motivated approach to reducing complexity. The paper provides a clear and reasoned argument for its central approximation, linking it to the convergence properties of the covariance matrix. The resulting algorithm is not only faster but also more memory-efficient.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. It effectively motivates the problem, explains the technical challenge, and presents its solution and experimental validation in a logical sequence.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)**

1.  **Insufficient Validation of the Core Approximation:** The central claim of the paper rests on the validity of approximating C⁻¹ᐟ² with A⁻¹ in the CSA path update. The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 is conditional on the convergence of the normalized covariance matrix C̄t, which may not hold during a typical optimization run. The authors argue that the error will be small if Ct changes slowly, but this is a heuristic argument. The paper lacks direct empirical evidence to support this crucial claim. The current experiments only compare final performance, which is an indirect measure. A more direct validation is needed to show that the approximation does not introduce significant, potentially harmful, errors in the path update.

2.  **Limited Novelty in a Technical Sense:** The work builds heavily on previous efforts to use Cholesky updates for ES variants. Specifically, Krause and Igel (2015) introduced an efficient rank-one update for the Cholesky factor. While this paper extends the approach to the full rank-µ update of the standard CMA-ES and addresses the critical CSA path update problem, the core update machinery is not entirely novel. The paper could better position its contribution by more clearly distinguishing its conceptual advances from this prior work.

3.  **Lack of Precision in Analysis and Derivations:**
    *   **Complexity:** The paper claims an O(µd²) complexity, which is asymptotically the same as the delayed-update reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref). The dramatic speedup is due to avoiding a costly O(d³) operation and having better constant factors. A more detailed complexity analysis, perhaps using Θ-notation or explicitly comparing the dominant computational costs per iteration, would make the argument more rigorous.
    *   **Equivalence:** The paper states that the update to the Cholesky factor `At` is derived by decomposing Equation (2) into a series of rank-one updates. While this is plausible, the derivation is omitted. For the paper to be self-contained and the claims to be fully verifiable, a sketch of this derivation should be included.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Crucially, provide direct empirical validation for the CSA path approximation.** This could be done by running an experiment where, at each iteration, you compute both the true path vector `pσ,t+1` (using C⁻¹ᐟ²) and the approximated one (using A⁻¹t). Plotting the norm of the difference or the cosine of the angle between these two vectors over time would provide direct and compelling evidence to support your central claim.
*   Clarify the complexity analysis to better explain why your method is practically faster than the reference implementation, despite having a similar asymptotic complexity.
*   Include a brief derivation or a more detailed explanation of how the rank-µ update in Equation (2) is decomposed into the sequence of rank-one updates applied in Algorithm 1.
*   Please correct the minor notational errors mentioned in the draft (e.g., αk in Algorithm 2, n vs. d in Table 1).

**Overall Recommendation**

This paper presents a valuable and practical improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm. The demonstrated speedup is impressive and could have a significant impact. However, the submission's primary weakness is the lack of direct empirical validation for its core theoretical claim regarding the CSA path approximation. The concerns about the precision of the complexity analysis and the novelty relative to prior work are also notable.

In its current form, the paper is a borderline case. The practical contribution is strong, but the scientific rigor could be improved. If the authors can provide direct experimental evidence supporting their central approximation, I would strongly recommend acceptance. Without this, the paper feels more like a strong technical report than a complete scientific contribution suitable for NIPS. I lean towards rejection but would be willing to reconsider if the authors address the validation issue during the rebuttal period.
