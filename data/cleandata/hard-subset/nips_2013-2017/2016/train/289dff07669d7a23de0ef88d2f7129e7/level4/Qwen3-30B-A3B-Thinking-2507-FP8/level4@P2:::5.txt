

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that significantly reduces the computational complexity of the covariance matrix adaptation step. The authors replace the standard matrix square root operation with a lower-triangular Cholesky factorization, reducing the time complexity from O(d³) to O(µd²) where d is the problem dimensionality and µ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log d)). This modification avoids the costly computation of the matrix square root at each iteration while maintaining the algorithm's performance. The key theoretical insight is that the orthogonal matrix Et, which would normally be required for the step-size adaptation, converges to the identity matrix under reasonable assumptions about the convergence of the covariance matrix sequence, making the approximation valid asymptotically.

The paper builds upon prior work on CMA-ES variants, including the diagonal CMA-ES [Ros and Hansen, 2008] and the Cholesky-based approaches for specific CMA-ES variants [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015]. Unlike previous attempts, the authors provide a theoretical justification for why their approximation does not impair performance, which is a significant contribution to the field. The proposed method also offers practical advantages: it achieves optimal storage complexity, provides eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free, and enables numerically stable computation of the inverse Cholesky factor.

The paper's strengths are substantial. It addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm, provides a theoretically grounded solution, and demonstrates significant runtime improvements without compromising optimization performance on standard benchmark functions. The experimental results (Figures 1-3) convincingly show that Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the function evaluation count of the standard CMA-ES while achieving substantially faster wall-clock times, with the speedup increasing with problem dimensionality. The authors also demonstrate the algorithm's implementation within the Shark library, adding to its practical utility.

Regarding the four key criteria: Quality is well-supported through theoretical analysis (Lemma 1) and empirical results. The paper is clearly written with well-organized sections and sufficient detail to reproduce the results. Originality is strong as the authors provide a novel approach with theoretical justification, distinct from prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants. Significance is notable for practitioners working with high-dimensional optimization problems where the covariance matrix adaptation dominates runtime.

However, the paper could benefit from addressing two important considerations. First, while the theoretical speedup is compelling, the practical relevance is somewhat limited by the fact that in most real-world applications, the CMA-ES runtime is negligible compared to the objective function evaluation time. As the authors note, CMA-ES is typically applied to problems with dimensionality not exceeding a few tens of variables, where the proposed speedup would be minimal. The authors should more thoroughly discuss the practical scenarios where this speedup would be meaningful - namely, when function evaluations are very fast (milliseconds or less) and problem dimensionality is high (hundreds of variables), which may be less common than the authors suggest.

Second, the paper would be strengthened by additional empirical evidence. The authors should include: (1) plots showing the evolution of Et over optimization trajectories, which would provide direct empirical evidence of the convergence assumption; (2) runtime comparisons of the standard CMA-ES without any delay strategies (as opposed to the delayed variants used in the experiments), for completeness; (3) a simulation study varying the spectral distribution of quadratic test functions (as suggested by Stich and Müller, PPSN 2012) to better understand the relationship between problem structure and approximation error; and (4) a real-world application example where speed matters, such as policy search in robotics, to demonstrate practical utility beyond synthetic benchmarks.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature by providing a theoretically justified, practically efficient variant of the influential CMA-ES algorithm. While the practical applicability may be somewhat limited to specific high-dimensional, fast-evaluation scenarios, the method is sound and the theoretical justification is compelling. The proposed improvements are both technically significant and well-supported by empirical evidence. The minor suggestions for additional experiments would strengthen the paper's impact and provide a more complete picture of the method's strengths and limitations.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES variant offers a practical and theoretically grounded approach to reducing the computational complexity of the covariance matrix adaptation in CMA-ES, with significant runtime improvements demonstrated on standard benchmarks. While the practical applicability is somewhat constrained to specific scenarios, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and represents a valuable addition to the optimization literature. I recommend acceptance.
