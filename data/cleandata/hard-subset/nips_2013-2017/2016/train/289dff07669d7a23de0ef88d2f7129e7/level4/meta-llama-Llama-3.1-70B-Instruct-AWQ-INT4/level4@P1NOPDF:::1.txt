CMA-ES is the state-of-the-art stochastic optimization algorithm, renowned for its effectiveness in derivative-free optimization. By combining multiple learning mechanisms, it adapts the parameters of a multivariate normal sampling distribution, which can also be viewed as adapting an underlying metric. This paper introduces a novel variant for updating the covariance matrix, leveraging triangular Cholesky factors. This approach offers a significant advantage over previous updates, substantially reducing both the complexity of the update and the memory required to store the algorithm's state parameters. The new method has been meticulously evaluated through numerical experiments.

The results demonstrate that the new update does not compromise the performance of the standard CMA-ES in terms of the number of function evaluations needed to reach a given target. Moreover, it significantly reduces the algorithm's wall-clock time, particularly in high-dimensional settings. In my opinion, this paper makes a highly significant contribution to the field of optimization. I anticipate that this variant of CMA-ES will become the default choice for medium to large-scale optimization problems, given its clear advantages over the current standard method, including reduced memory requirements, lower complexity, and the ability to obtain the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix at no additional cost.

The paper is well-written, and the approach has been carefully evaluated. It is pleasing to see this contribution featured at NIPS, as CMA-ES has strong connections to machine learning, information geometry, and is also employed in reinforcement learning and supervised learning.

Minor suggestions: The authors should provide more details on how they ensured that the different implementations used in the experiments were comparable in terms of CPU performance. Specifically, they should clarify whether the same implementation language was used and, if so, which one.

When discussing Figure 2, the text states that "the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences." However, this does not appear to be the case for the Cigar and Discus functions in 256 dimensions. The authors would benefit from providing an explanation for this discrepancy.
