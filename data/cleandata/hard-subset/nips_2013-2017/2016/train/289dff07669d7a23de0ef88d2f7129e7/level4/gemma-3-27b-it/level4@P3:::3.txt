This paper proposes a modification to the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, which updates the covariance matrix using its Cholesky factorization instead of directly computing and manipulating the covariance matrix itself. The core idea is to leverage the efficient quadratic-time update of the Cholesky factor, reducing both runtime and memory complexity, particularly in high-dimensional spaces. The authors provide a theoretical justification for why this modification should not significantly alter the algorithm's convergence behavior and present empirical results demonstrating improved performance, especially in terms of wall-clock time.

The CMA-ES is a well-established and highly effective derivative-free optimization algorithm (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001; Beyer, 2007; Eiben & Smith, 2015), finding applications in areas like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning (Gomez et al., 2008; Heidrich-Meisner & Igel, 2009a,b). The computational cost of covariance matrix updates, particularly the matrix square root computation, is a known bottleneck. Previous work has addressed this through delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) or restricted model complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008), each with drawbacks. More recent approaches explore updating the Cholesky factor directly (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), but the authors argue that these don’t fully address the original CMA-ES’s reliance on the matrix square root. This paper builds upon this line of work, offering a theoretically grounded and practically efficient alternative.

**Strengths:**

*   **Practical Improvement:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES demonstrably reduces runtime, particularly in higher dimensions, without sacrificing the number of function evaluations. This is a significant practical advantage for problems where function evaluation is expensive.
*   **Theoretical Justification:**  The authors provide a theoretical argument, supported by Lemma 1, explaining why approximating the step-size adaptation using the Cholesky factor should not drastically alter the algorithm’s convergence properties. This is a key contribution, setting this work apart from previous attempts.
*   **Optimal Complexity:** The algorithm achieves optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity for full covariance matrix adaptation, addressing a critical limitation of the standard CMA-ES.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and the technical details are presented with reasonable clarity. The connection to existing work is established effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** While the extension to rank-\mu updates is a logical progression, the contribution feels somewhat incremental, given the prior work by Krause and Igel (2015) on rank-1 updates using the Cholesky factor. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this work significantly expands on their findings.
*   **Convergence Assumption:** The theoretical justification relies on the assumption of covariance convergence, which, while supported by theoretical results (Beyer, 2014), isn’t guaranteed in all cases.  Acknowledging the limitations of this assumption would strengthen the argument.
*   **Minor Technical Errors:** There are minor typos and inconsistencies in the equations (e.g., the denominator in equation (1) and the footnote on page 3).  Careful proofreading is needed. The phrase "which can by be motivated" on line 94 is grammatically incorrect.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments primarily focus on runtime and function evaluation counts. Additional analysis, such as a more detailed comparison of the rotation matrices (Et) mentioned in the conclusion, would provide further insight into the algorithm's behavior and validate the theoretical claims.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a practical bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm.
*   The theoretical justification, even with its assumptions, is a valuable contribution.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a clear performance improvement in runtime.
*   The code contribution (mention of implementation in Shark) is a positive aspect.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty is somewhat limited compared to related work.
*   The reliance on the convergence assumption weakens the theoretical guarantee.
*   Minor technical errors detract from the overall polish of the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm. While the novelty isn't groundbreaking, the combination of theoretical justification and practical performance gains make it a valuable contribution. The minor weaknesses can be readily addressed through careful revision. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding the incremental novelty, convergence assumption, and technical errors. The authors should also consider expanding the experimental analysis to provide further validation of the theoretical claims.
