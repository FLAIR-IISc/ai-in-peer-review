This paper presents a modification to the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) aimed at reducing computational cost and memory usage during covariance matrix updates. The core idea is to utilize the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix and update it incrementally, avoiding explicit computation of the matrix square root in each iteration. The authors argue this approach maintains the performance characteristics of the original CMA-ES while improving efficiency.

The CMA-ES is a well-established, powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001; Beyer, 2007; Eiben & Smith, 2015), widely applied in machine learning for tasks like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning (Gomez et al., 2008; Heidrich-Meisner & Igel, 2009a, 2009b; Igel, 2010). The computational bottleneck in CMA-ES often lies in updating and storing the covariance matrix, particularly in high dimensions. Previous work has addressed this through delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001), restricted model complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008; Sun et al., 2013; Akimoto et al., 2014; Loshchilov, 2014, 2015), or updating the Cholesky factor directly (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). This paper builds upon these efforts, specifically extending the work of Krause and Igel (2015) to the full CMA-ES framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Practical Improvement:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a demonstrable speedup in runtime, particularly as dimensionality increases, without sacrificing the number of function evaluations required. This is a significant practical benefit for applications where covariance matrix updates are computationally expensive.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a theoretical argument, based on Lemma 1, suggesting that the approximation introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor does not fundamentally alter the algorithm’s convergence behavior.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly explains the modification to the CMA-ES algorithm. Algorithm 1 is helpful in illustrating the core change.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, while needing some refinement as noted below, support the claim of improved runtime performance across a range of benchmark functions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Naming Conflict & Lack of Context:** The name “Cholesky-CMA-ES” is already associated with the work of Suttorp et al. (2009). The paper fails to acknowledge this prior work and its context, creating potential confusion.  The authors should clearly differentiate their approach and consider a more distinct naming convention.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** The algorithmic contribution – looping through individuals to apply the rank-one update – feels incremental. The paper needs to more convincingly justify why this seemingly small change represents a significant advance over existing methods, especially those of Krause and Igel (2015) and Suttorp et al. (2009).
*   **Incomplete Comparison:** The paper lacks a direct comparison to the Cholesky-CMA-ES proposed by Suttorp et al. (2009), both algorithmically and empirically.  Furthermore, a more detailed algorithmic comparison with Krause and Igel (2015) is needed.
*   **Misleading Claims & Nuance:** The claim of "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" is overstated. Optimality depends on the problem structure; the approach isn't universally optimal (e.g., Sphere function). The paper also downplays potential numerical instabilities reported in Suttorp et al. (2009).
*   **Figure Interpretation:** The observation of different plateaus in Figure 1(b) for CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES suggests a potential difference in behavior, requiring further investigation and explanation.
*   **Figure Presentation:** The figures, especially those comparing multiple algorithms, may become unreadable with further additions (as suggested for the inclusion of Suttorp et al.'s method). A well-organized Supplementary Material section could alleviate this issue.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a practical limitation of CMA-ES – the computational cost of covariance matrix updates.
*   The proposed modification offers a clear performance improvement in terms of runtime.
*   The theoretical justification, while not a rigorous proof of equivalence, provides a reasonable rationale for the approach.
*   The experimental results, despite some caveats, support the claims made in the paper.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The algorithmic contribution is relatively incremental.
*   The paper fails to adequately address and compare to relevant prior work, particularly Suttorp et al. (2009).
*   The claim of “optimality” is misleading.
*   The lack of clarity regarding the naming conflict and the subtle differences in results (e.g., Figure 1b) raise concerns about the thoroughness of the evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile, albeit incremental, improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a tangible performance benefit, particularly in high dimensions. However, the paper suffers from a lack of thorough comparison to related work and some potentially misleading claims.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **conditional acceptance** pending major revisions. Specifically, the authors *must*:

1.  **Resolve the naming conflict** and provide a clear distinction between their approach and the Cholesky-CMA-ES of Suttorp et al. (2009).
2.  **Perform a comprehensive comparison** with both the Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) approaches, both algorithmically and empirically.
3.  **Moderate the claims of optimality** and acknowledge the limitations of the method in specific scenarios.
4.  **Investigate and explain** the differences observed in Figure 1(b).
5.  **Clearly articulate** the significance of the "for" loop in Algorithm 1 and justify why it constitutes a meaningful contribution.
6.  **Improve the figure presentation** or move additional comparison to a well-structured Supplementary Material section.
