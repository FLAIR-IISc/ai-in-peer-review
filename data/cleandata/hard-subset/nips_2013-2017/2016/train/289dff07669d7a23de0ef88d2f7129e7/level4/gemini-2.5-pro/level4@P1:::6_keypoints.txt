- Proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES that updates the triangular Cholesky factor to reduce covariance update time to O(µd²) and storage to O(d²).
- Uses inverse triangular Cholesky factor A⁻¹ to approximate C⁻¹/² for CSA, with Lemma 1 arguing the approximation error vanishes as covariance shape converges.
- Empirically shows large wall-clock time reductions versus reference CMA-ES with no measurable increase in function evaluations.
- Strength: tackles a practical O(d³) bottleneck in CMA-ES for high-dimensional problems.
- Strength: sensible modification that yields clear runtime improvements and can be state-of-the-art for full-covariance adaptation.
- Strength: provides theoretical justification for the CSA approximation.
- Weakness: novelty appears incremental—primarily extends Krause & Igel (2015)’s rank-one Cholesky update to (µ, λ)-CMA-ES via a looped rankOneUpdate.
- Weakness: naming conflict—“Cholesky-CMA-ES” was used by Suttorp et al. (2009); this should be acknowledged or the algorithm renamed.
- Weakness: missing experimental comparison with Suttorp et al. (2009)’s non-triangular Cholesky method, and the claim that it is numerically unstable lacks supporting evidence.
- Weakness: claims of “optimal” complexity should be qualified (optimal only among full d×d covariance methods; simpler problems allow O(d) methods).
- Weakness: characterization as suitable for “large-scale” optimization is overstated because O(d²) remains prohibitive for very large d (e.g., d > 1000).
- Weakness: need clarity on Figure 1b (Cigar) where CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES curves plateau at different values—could indicate a real convergence difference or plotting artifact.
- Minor: incorrect statement that eigen-decomposition is typically done every update—practical implementations perform it periodically (e.g., every d/10 iterations).
- Conclusion: practically useful and well-justified improvement but incremental relative to prior work; should add the missing baseline, resolve naming, and temper broad claims.
