The manuscript describes a novel CMA-ES update scheme, the Cholesky-CMA-ES, that approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation (with complexity O(d^3)) by a novel scheme that runs in O(μd^2), where d is the optimization problem dimension and μ the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d)). The principle idea of the update is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix rather than a full covariance matrix (and corresponding square root of that).

The authors provide a thorough background on the CMA-ES algorithm, its applications, and the need for reducing its computational complexity. They also discuss previous attempts to address this issue, such as delaying the update of the covariance matrix or restricting its model complexity. However, these approaches have limitations, such as increasing the number of function evaluations or leading to a drastic increase in function evaluations needed to approximate the optimum.

The Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm is then presented, which uses a triangular Cholesky factorization instead of the square root in the standard CMA-ES. The authors provide a theoretical justification for why this modification does not deteriorate the algorithm's performance. They also show that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a better, asymptotically optimal time complexity of O(μd^2) for the covariance update and optimal memory complexity.

The authors conduct experiments to compare the Cholesky-CMA-ES with other CMA-ES variants, including the state-of-the-art CMA-ES with delayed covariance matrix decomposition. The results show that the Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same amount of objective function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time. The speed-up increases with the search space dimensionality, making the Cholesky-CMA-ES a promising approach for scaling up CMA-ES to large optimization problems.

The paper makes a solid contribution to speeding up the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm, which is arguably the best algorithm for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the topology of the objective function and first or higher-order information is difficult to access or not helpful. The proposed method speeds up the algorithm considerably and shows no deterioration of optimization performance on the synthetic test cases.

However, it is worth noting that the overall optimization process is often dominated by the runtime of the black-box oracle (the objective function), and the actual runtime of the CMA-ES is negligible compared to that. Additionally, in typical scenarios where CMA-ES is used, the dimensionality of the problems does not go beyond a few tens of variables. Therefore, the proposed method may be more relevant in scenarios where a single function evaluation is on the order of milliseconds or smaller and the dimensionality is higher.

To strengthen the presentation of the results, I would like to propose a few additional experiments and plots. Firstly, it may be instructive to see the evolution of E_t on selected optimization trajectories, e.g., in different norms. This would give further empirical evidence about the actual fluctuations of E_t over time. Secondly, it may be instructive to see actual runtimes and performance of CMA-ES without delay strategies in this contribution for completeness. Thirdly, although the used test functions are standard in the evolutionary optimization community, I would prefer, on quadratic functions, a simulation study where the spectrum of quadratic forms is varied continuously (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012), and compare the solution quality and error in E_t in dependence of the spectral distribution. Finally, it would be instructive to see a real-world experiment that accompanies the present simulation results. For instance, policy gradients in real-time robotics applications might be a good choice where speed indeed matters and the parameter space is not too large.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient update scheme for the CMA-ES algorithm, which is a significant contribution to the field of optimization.
* The authors provide a thorough theoretical justification for the proposed method and conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate its effectiveness.
* The results show that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant speed-up over the standard CMA-ES without deteriorating its optimization performance.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper may be too specialized for the NIPS audience, as it focuses on a specific algorithm and its optimization.
* The proposed method may not be widely applicable, as it is mainly relevant in scenarios where a single function evaluation is on the order of milliseconds or smaller and the dimensionality is higher.
* The paper could benefit from additional experiments and plots to strengthen the presentation of the results.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a solid contribution to the field of optimization and is worthy of acceptance. However, I would like to see some revisions to address the proposed additional experiments and plots.
