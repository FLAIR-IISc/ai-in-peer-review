CMA-ES is a state-of-the-art randomized (derivative-free) optimization algorithm that adapts the parameters of a multivariate normal sampling distribution, which can also be interpreted as learning an underlying metric. This paper introduces a new covariance matrix update based on triangular Cholesky factors. Compared with prior updates, the proposed approach substantially reduces both computational complexity and the memory required to maintain the algorithm’s state. An additional benefit highlighted by the authors is that the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix become available “for free.”

The empirical evaluation is careful and convincing. The results show that the new update preserves the performance of standard CMA-ES in terms of the number of function evaluations needed to reach a target, while considerably reducing wall-clock time, especially in high-dimensional settings. The experiments support the claim that the method scales more favorably with dimension without sacrificing solution quality.

Overall, this is a significant contribution to optimization. Given its advantages—lower memory footprint, reduced computational cost, and access to eigenvalues without separate decomposition—this variant has strong potential to become a default choice for medium- to large-scale applications. The paper is clearly written, the methodology is sound, and the evaluation is thorough. It is particularly fitting for NIPS, given the close ties between CMA-ES and machine learning, information geometry, and applications in reinforcement and supervised learning.

Minor comments and requests for clarification:
- Implementation comparability: Please explain how you ensured fairness across implementations in terms of CPU and software environment. Were all algorithms implemented in the same language and version, using the same BLAS/LAPACK libraries (if any), compiler and flags, and run on identical hardware under the same OS settings? Clarifying these details would strengthen the wall-clock time comparisons.
- Figure 2 interpretation: The paper states that “the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences.” However, this does not appear to hold for Cigar and Discus at dimension 256. Please comment on why the expected trend is not observed on these functions—e.g., whether it is due to function structure (e.g., near-separability or dominant conditioning), algorithmic parameter interactions, saturation effects in memory bandwidth or cache, or other implementation-level factors.
