The Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) is a state-of-the-art stochastic algorithm for derivative-free optimization. It employs a set of learning mechanisms to adapt the parameters of a multivariate normal sampling distribution, which can be equivalently interpreted as adapting an underlying metric.

This paper proposes a new variant for the covariance matrix update based on triangular Cholesky factors. Compared to previous updates, this approach offers the significant advantages of reducing both the computational complexity of the update and the memory required to store the algorithm's state parameters.

The new method is carefully evaluated through numerical experiments. The results show that the proposed update does not degrade the performance of the standard CMA-ES, measured by the number of function evaluations required to reach a given target value. Concurrently, it significantly reduces the algorithm's wall-clock time, an improvement that is particularly pronounced in high-dimensional settings.

I consider this paper a very significant contribution to the field of optimization. This variant of CMA-ES has the potential to become the default implementation for medium- to large-scale problems due to its clear advantages over the current standard method: reduced memory requirements, lower computational complexity, and the ability to obtain the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix at no additional cost.

The paper is well-written, and the proposed approach is thoroughly evaluated. This work is a welcome contribution to the NIPS community, given that CMA-ES has strong connections to machine learning and information geometry, and has applications in areas such as reinforcement and supervised learning.

**Minor Points:**
*   Could the authors clarify how they ensured that the CPU time comparisons between different implementations are fair? For instance, were the algorithms implemented in the same language?
*   In the discussion of Figure 2, the authors state that "the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences." However, this trend does not appear to hold for the Cigar and Discus functions in dimension 256. An explanation for this observation would be beneficial.
