Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix instead of the full covariance matrix and its square root. This change reduces the per-iteration complexity from the cubic cost of repeated decompositions to O(µ d^2), where d is the dimension and µ is the number of selected samples (typically O(log d)). The key technical change is to modify cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) by replacing the inverse matrix square root C^{-1/2} with the inverse Cholesky factor A^{-1}. This avoids reconstructing the orthogonal factor E_t in the RQ decomposition C^{1/2} = A E, which would be cubic to maintain. The authors justify this approximation by arguing that, under convergence of the (normalized) covariance matrices, the sequence of rotations E_t stabilizes (E_{t-1}^T E_t → I), so the omission introduces a diminishing error in the CSA norm. Empirically, the method matches the standard CMA-ES in the number of function evaluations while substantially reducing wall-clock time, and outperforms alternatives that delay covariance updates or use non-triangular Cholesky updates. The work builds on and extends prior Cholesky-based updates from special CMA-ES variants ((1+1), MO-CMA-ES) and relates to information-geometric interpretations of CMA-ES and to low-complexity CMA variants.

Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic idea: maintain a triangular factor and update it via rank-one operations, yielding optimal asymptotic storage and O(µ d^2) time.
- Thoughtful modification of CSA with a reasoned asymptotic argument explaining why the approximation should not harm performance near convergence.
- Empirical results show identical evaluation counts to the standard CMA-ES and large wall-clock speed-ups that grow with d.
- Practical relevance for regimes where function evaluations are inexpensive and dimensions are moderate-to-large; easy to integrate into existing code bases.
- Good positioning with respect to related work on delayed updates and Cholesky-based strategies.

Weaknesses:
- The theoretical justification hinges on restrictive assumptions (e.g., convergence of a normalized covariance sequence), which may not hold in general stochastic settings; the argument is heuristic rather than a guarantee of equivalence.
- The main benefit is runtime, not evaluation efficiency; in many ML applications the objective evaluation dominates, and d is often only in the tens, potentially limiting practical impact.
- Experimental scope is largely synthetic; no real-world case study where CMA-ES overhead is known to matter (e.g., fast controller tuning or small-model hyperparameter search).
- The comparison set omits a clear baseline explicitly reporting costs when the standard CMA-ES performs decomposition every iteration versus built-in delay strategies, for full transparency.

Quality:
- The method is technically well-motivated and numerically sound; rank-one Cholesky updates are standard and stable. The asymptotic argument for CSA is plausible but not a formal guarantee.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough on standard benchmarks and across dimensions, with convincing runtime and evaluation-count plots.
- Implementation details (use of LAPACK, integration in Shark) support reproducibility.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The distinction between the role of A^{-1} and the omitted E_t in CSA is explained clearly, as is the consequence for complexity.

Originality:
- Extends Cholesky-based updates from special CMA-ES variants to the standard CMA-ES with a principled CSA approximation. While maintaining factorizations is a known idea, the way CSA is adapted and justified here is novel.

Significance:
- High significance for scenarios where CMA-ES overhead matters (cheap evaluations, higher d, tight runtime budgets). Limited immediate impact where evaluations dominate, but provides a better default for full-covariance CMA-ES when feasible.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper:
- Track the evolution of E_t (or the rotation E_{t-1}^T E_t) along optimization trajectories to empirically quantify the approximation error in CSA. A side-by-side run of standard CMA-ES and the proposed variant on the same seeds would be informative.
- Report runtimes for standard CMA-ES both with its default delay strategy and with decomposition forced every iteration, to isolate the cost components.
- Include a systematic study on quadratic functions with continuously varied spectra (e.g., as in Stich and Müller, PPSN 2012), comparing evaluation counts and the CSA error as a function of conditioning and spectral shape.
- Add at least one real-world task where CMA-ES overhead is non-negligible, such as policy search with fast simulators or small-scale hyperparameter tuning, to demonstrate practical gains.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Elegant, simple modification with clear complexity benefits and no observed loss in evaluation efficiency.
- Strong empirical speed-ups that increase with dimension.
- Solid connection to prior work; fills a gap between theory-inspired designs and practical performance.
- Implementation-ready and likely to influence practitioners who rely on full-covariance CMA-ES.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Theoretical equivalence to standard CSA relies on restrictive assumptions; no non-asymptotic bounds.
- Practical relevance may be limited in settings where function evaluations dominate or d is small.
- Primarily synthetic experiments; lack of a real-world showcase.

Overall assessment:
A solid and practically relevant contribution that makes full-covariance CMA-ES significantly faster without harming evaluation efficiency, accompanied by a reasonable (though asymptotic) justification of the CSA modification. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement to add the suggested analyses (E_t dynamics, spectra study) and a real-world case to further strengthen the paper’s impact.
