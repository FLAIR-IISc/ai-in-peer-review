Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that replaces the explicit covariance matrix update and eigen/square-root decompositions with a direct update of a lower-triangular Cholesky factor. The key idea is to maintain At with Ct = At Aᵀt and to adapt both the covariance and the cumulative path in terms of At, thereby reducing the per-iteration complexity from the usual cubic cost to O(μ d^2), where d is the dimensionality and μ is the number of selected samples (typically O(log d)). The main departure from the standard CMA-ES is in the CSA step-size adaptation: the authors replace C^{-1/2} by A^{-1}, which avoids estimating the changing orthogonal factor E_t that relates the Cholesky factor to the matrix square root.

The paper provides a theoretical argument—under restrictive assumptions, namely convergence (up to scale) of the normalized covariance matrices—that the orthogonal factors E_t stabilize (Eᵀ_{t-1} E_t → I). This suggests that omitting the rotational correction in the CSA should have a vanishing effect asymptotically. The authors implement this scheme and compare against two state-of-the-art alternatives that reduce the cost of covariance updates by delaying decompositions, showing similar evaluation efficiency but lower wall-clock time on standard benchmarks.

The work is related to and extends previous efforts to speed up CMA-ES by (i) delayed eigen-decompositions, (ii) structural restrictions (e.g., diagonal or low-rank covariance), and (iii) maintaining a Cholesky factor (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015). Unlike earlier Cholesky-based approaches that are most natural for special CMA variants, this paper targets the standard CMA-ES and provides an argument for why the modified CSA remains effective.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The Cholesky update itself is well motivated and leads to the claimed O(μ d^2) time and optimal storage complexity. The approximation in CSA is justified by an asymptotic argument relying on convergence of the (normalized) covariance shape; this is a restrictive assumption for a randomized algorithm and does not constitute a general proof of equivalence. Nevertheless, the argument is reasonable and the empirical evidence supports the claim that optimization performance (in function evaluations) is preserved on the tested problems.
- Empirical support: The benchmarks are standard and include both separable and non-separable functions with varying conditioning, with and without rotations. The results indicate that Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the evaluation efficiency of the reference CMA-ES while substantially reducing wall-clock time compared to delayed-update baselines. For completeness, it would help to also include the full CMA-ES without delay strategies as an additional baseline.
- Clarity: The paper is generally clear. The derivation, especially the role of the missing orthogonal factor in CSA, is explained well. Including plots or diagnostics that quantify the evolution of E_t (or the induced rotation error) along representative runs would strengthen the readers’ understanding of when the approximation is accurate.

Originality:
The main contribution is an efficient, numerically stable triangular Cholesky update integrated into standard CMA-ES plus a theoretically motivated modification of CSA. While Cholesky-based updates and low-complexity CMA variants exist, applying a triangular factor update to the full CMA-ES with an argument for the CSA approximation is novel.

Significance:
The contribution is practically important in regimes where CMA-ES overhead is non-negligible: higher-dimensional problems where μ = O(log d) and function evaluations are relatively cheap (milliseconds or less). In many real-world black-box optimization tasks, runtime is dominated by function evaluations and d is modest (tens of variables), so the speedups may translate to limited overall gains. That said, the target regime is relevant for certain ML applications (e.g., direct policy search, some hyperparameter tuning), and the method’s optimal memory and time complexity for full covariance adaptation is valuable.

Strengths:
- Significant reduction of per-iteration time and memory with no observed loss in evaluation efficiency on standard benchmarks.
- Clean integration into standard CMA-ES; numerically stable factor updates.
- Clear identification and discussion of the CSA approximation, with an asymptotic justification.
- Careful experimental comparison against strong delayed-update baselines on rotated and ill-conditioned functions.

Weaknesses:
- The theoretical equivalence relies on restrictive assumptions (convergence of the normalized covariance shape); there is no non-asymptotic bound on the CSA error caused by omitting E_t.
- Empirical evaluation lacks (i) a baseline with the full, non-delayed CMA-ES in this paper and (ii) diagnostics of the rotation error over time.
- Practical impact may be limited when function evaluations dominate runtime or when dimensions are small.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Track and report the evolution of E_t (or an appropriate norm of the deviation Eᵀ_{t-1}E_t − I) along optimization trajectories. A useful experiment would run the full CMA-ES and, in parallel, compute the Cholesky-based approximation to directly compare the CSA terms and the induced step-size dynamics.
- Include a baseline with the full CMA-ES without delay, even if only on the smaller d settings, to quantify the exact overhead reduction.
- On quadratic test functions, vary the spectrum continuously as in Stich and Müller (PPSN 2012) and measure both solution quality and the CSA rotation error as a function of spectral distribution.
- Add a real-world experiment where CMA-ES overhead matters (e.g., real-time robotics policy search), to demonstrate the practical relevance beyond synthetic benchmarks.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong practical speedups with preserved optimization performance in function evaluations.
- Novel, well-motivated integration of triangular Cholesky updates into standard CMA-ES.
- Solid empirical evidence on diverse benchmarks, including rotated, ill-conditioned cases.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Theoretical justification is asymptotic and contingent on restrictive assumptions; no finite-sample guarantees.
- Limited demonstration of impact in realistic scenarios where evaluation cost dominates.
- Missing diagnostics for the CSA approximation and a full CMA-ES (non-delayed) baseline in this paper.

Overall, this is a solid and well-executed contribution that makes CMA-ES more computationally efficient without sacrificing performance on standard tests. With the suggested additional experiments, the paper would be even stronger.
