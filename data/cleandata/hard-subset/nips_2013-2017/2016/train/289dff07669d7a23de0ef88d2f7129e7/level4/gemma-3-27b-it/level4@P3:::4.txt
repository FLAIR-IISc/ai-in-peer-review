This paper addresses a key computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES): the updating and storage of the covariance matrix. The authors propose a novel approach utilizing the triangular Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root, aiming to reduce both runtime and memory complexity, particularly in high-dimensional optimization problems. This is achieved by modifying the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The core contribution lies in providing a theoretical justification for why this modification doesn’t negatively impact the algorithm’s convergence properties, alongside empirical validation demonstrating significant speedups, especially with increasing dimensionality.

The CMA-ES is a well-established and powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm, frequently used in machine learning for tasks like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning, as highlighted by the authors’ citations (Gomez et al. [2008], Heidrich-Meisner and Igel [2009a,b], Igel [2010]). Previous attempts to address the computational cost of covariance matrix updates include delaying updates [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001] or restricting model complexity [Poland and Zell, 2001; Ros and Hansen, 2008], each with drawbacks. More recent work utilizing Cholesky factorization [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015] has shown promise, but the authors correctly point out that these methods don’t directly translate to the standard CMA-ES due to its reliance on the matrix square root. This work fills that gap by offering a theoretically grounded and empirically verified alternative.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a crucial theoretical analysis, specifically Lemma 1, explaining why replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor doesn't degrade performance. This addresses a potential concern and strengthens the contribution.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES demonstrably improves runtime, particularly as dimensionality increases, while maintaining the same number of function evaluations. This is a significant practical benefit.
*   **Optimal Complexity:** The authors correctly identify the algorithm’s optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity, highlighting its scalability.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and generally clearly written, effectively explaining the modifications to the standard CMA-ES and the rationale behind them.
*   **Thorough Experiments:** The experimental evaluation on a variety of benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) and across a range of dimensions (4 to 256) provides strong support for the claims.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Numerical Stability:** While claiming numerically superior stability, the paper lacks a detailed explanation of *why* the Cholesky factorization provides this benefit. Expanding on this aspect would be valuable.
*   **Scaling Beyond Quadratic:** The authors acknowledge the quadratic scaling with dimensionality remains a limitation. While this is inherent to full covariance matrix updates, a more detailed discussion of the point at which low-dimensional approximations (e.g., Loshchilov [2015]) become necessary in practice would be useful.
*   **Connection to Existing Cholesky Work:** While acknowledging related work [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015], the connection could be made even more explicit, particularly regarding the advantages of this approach over those alternatives. A more detailed comparison with Krause and Igel [2015] would be particularly helpful.
*   **Experiment Detail:** The experimental section could benefit from more detail regarding the experimental setup. For instance, how were the rotation matrices generated? Providing the exact parameters used in the experiments would increase reproducibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a well-known bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm.
*   The theoretical justification is strong and provides confidence in the method's validity.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a clear and significant performance improvement.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The improvement is primarily in runtime rather than a fundamental algorithmic advance (e.g., fewer function evaluations).
*   The discussion of numerical stability is somewhat lacking in detail.
*   The quadratic scaling remains a limitation for very high-dimensional problems.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES provides a practical and efficient improvement to the standard CMA-ES, particularly for high-dimensional problems. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper, particularly the theoretical justification and empirical results, outweigh them. I believe this work is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
