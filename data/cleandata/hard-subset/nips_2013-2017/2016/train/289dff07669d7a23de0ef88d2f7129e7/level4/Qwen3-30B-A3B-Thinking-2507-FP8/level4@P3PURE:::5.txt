

The manuscript introduces a novel CMA-ES update scheme, Cholesky-CMA-ES, which approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation (with complexity O(d³)) by maintaining and updating a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix instead of the full covariance matrix. This approach reduces the per-iteration complexity to O(μd²), where d is the problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log d)). The key difference from standard CMA-ES arises from the omission of estimating a random orthogonal matrix Εₜ at each step. Under restrictive assumptions—specifically, convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target—the authors provide an asymptotic argument that Εₜ converges to the identity matrix, implying equivalence between the schemes in the limit. The proposed method's validity and speedup are demonstrated via standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark objective functions, with comparisons to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates via different mechanisms.  

This paper presents a solid contribution to accelerating the CMA-ES algorithm, which remains the leading method for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the objective's topology and gradient information is unavailable or unhelpful. The Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves significant speedups without degrading optimization performance on synthetic test cases. However, a critical limitation of any CMA-ES speedup proposal is that, in practice, the optimization process is typically dominated by the black-box oracle (objective function evaluation runtime), rendering the CMA-ES runtime negligible. This is especially true for problems with dimensionality up to a few tens of variables, which are common in CMA-ES applications. The targeted scenario—where CMA-ES runtime matters (e.g., very fast function evaluations and higher dimensions)—may be less realistic for typical use cases.  

While theoretical guarantees for CMA-ES are notoriously difficult to establish, the following empirical additions would strengthen the paper:  
1. **Evolution of Εₜ**: Plot Εₜ's evolution (e.g., in operator or Frobenius norm) along optimization trajectories using full CMA-ES as a baseline, with the Cholesky approximation computed simultaneously. This would empirically validate Εₜ's fluctuations and convergence.  
2. **Baseline runtime comparison**: Include runtime data for standard CMA-ES (without delay strategies) to contextualize the speedup.  
3. **Spectral sensitivity analysis**: For quadratic functions, conduct a simulation study varying the spectrum of the quadratic form (as in Stich & Müller, PPSN 2012), reporting solution quality and Εₜ error against spectral distribution.  
4. **Real-world validation**: Incorporate a real-world application where speed is critical, such as policy gradient methods in real-time robotics control with a moderate-sized parameter space.  

These additions would address the practical relevance of the speedup and provide deeper insights into the method's behavior.
