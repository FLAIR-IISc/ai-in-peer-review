### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a widely used derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard CMA-ES algorithm's time and space complexity are dominated by the need to update and store the d x d covariance matrix, C, and compute its matrix square root in each iteration, an O(d^3) operation. The authors propose a new variant, the Cholesky-CMA-ES, which circumvents this expensive step. The core idea is to maintain and directly update the triangular Cholesky factor, A, of the covariance matrix (where C = AA^T) in quadratic time (O(µd^2)).

The main technical novelty lies in modifying the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The standard CSA relies on the inverse of the matrix square root (C^{-1/2}) for normalization. The authors propose replacing this with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (A^{-1}). They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this approximation becomes negligible. Empirical results on several benchmark functions demonstrate that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES requires virtually the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES, but achieves a significant reduction in wall-clock time, particularly as the problem dimensionality increases.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Significant Practical Problem:** The quadratic/cubic complexity of the standard CMA-ES covariance update is a well-known limitation, especially for problems where function evaluations are relatively cheap. This paper provides a well-motivated and effective solution to this long-standing issue.

2.  **Novel and Well-Justified Approach:** While updating a Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix has been explored before (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), those methods were not directly applicable to the standard CMA-ES due to the CSA's reliance on the true matrix square root. This paper's key contribution is the principled approximation within the CSA, supported by a theoretical argument that the introduced error diminishes as the algorithm converges. This is an elegant solution to a tricky problem.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method against the standard reference implementation and other variants across a range of benchmark functions and dimensionalities. The results are clear and compelling: the Cholesky-CMA-ES consistently matches the optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations) of the standard algorithm while being substantially faster in terms of wall-clock time. The inclusion of plots showing performance on individual trials (Figure 3) effectively illustrates the practical impact.

4.  **Optimal Complexity:** The proposed algorithm achieves O(µd^2) time complexity and O(d^2) space complexity, which is asymptotically optimal for any method that maintains a full covariance matrix. This represents a fundamental improvement over the standard O(d^3) implementation.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Elaboration on Numerical Stability:** The abstract states that the proposed update scheme is "numerically stable," and the body of the paper mentions that maintaining separate updates for A and A⁻¹ can be "prone to numerical instabilities." While these claims are plausible, the paper would be strengthened by a brief theoretical or qualitative explanation of why direct updates on the triangular Cholesky factor (as implemented in Algorithm 2) offer superior numerical stability compared to other methods, such as forming and then decomposing the covariance matrix.

2.  **Discussion on the Limits of Applicability:** The authors correctly note in the conclusion that for extremely high-dimensional problems where storing a full d x d matrix is infeasible, one must resort to limited-memory or low-rank approximations (e.g., LM-CMA). A minor but useful addition would be a short discussion on whether the Cholesky update framework presented here could potentially be combined with or inform such low-rank approximation methods in future work. For instance, could this efficient rank-one update mechanism be adapted for updating low-rank factors of the covariance matrix? This would help position the work more clearly in the broader landscape of large-scale optimization.

**Evaluation**

*   **Quality: High.** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical justification for the CSA approximation is solid, and the claims are well-supported by thorough experimental results. The work is complete and carefully presented.
*   **Clarity: High.** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It effectively explains the problem with the standard CMA-ES, details the proposed solution, and presents the results in an understandable manner. The algorithms are clearly stated.
*   **Originality: High.** The paper presents a novel and significant modification to a canonical algorithm. The key insight of approximating C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ in the CSA step and justifying it theoretically distinguishes this work from previous attempts at using Cholesky factorizations with CMA-ES.
*   **Significance: High.** The CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization with numerous applications in machine learning (e.g., policy search, hyperparameter tuning). This work makes the algorithm significantly more practical for medium-to-high dimensional problems (d in the hundreds), broadening its applicability. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a strong candidate to become the new standard implementation, as it offers a substantial performance gain with no discernible downside in optimization quality.

**Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that presents a valuable and well-executed contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
