This manuscript introduces the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel update scheme for the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) designed to improve computational efficiency. The standard CMA-ES, renowned for its performance in derivative-free optimization, suffers from a cubic time complexity (O(d^3)) related to covariance matrix updates. This work proposes approximating the covariance matrix adaptation with an algorithm that achieves a complexity of O(μd^2), where d is the dimensionality of the optimization problem and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea involves maintaining and updating a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix. A key theoretical contribution lies in demonstrating, under certain convergence assumptions, that the difference between the Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES diminishes as the algorithm progresses, due to the convergence of an orthogonal matrix *E<sub>t</sub>*.  The authors validate their approach through experiments on benchmark functions, comparing its performance against state-of-the-art CMA-ES implementations employing delayed covariance updates.

This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of black-box optimization. CMA-ES is widely regarded as a highly effective algorithm for non-convex problems where gradient information is unavailable or unreliable. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a substantial speed-up, potentially broadening the applicability of CMA-ES to larger-scale problems.  Experimental results demonstrate that the new method maintains comparable optimization performance to existing CMA-ES variants while offering a marked reduction in computational time, particularly as dimensionality increases.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the computational bottleneck of standard CMA-ES (covariance matrix updates) and motivates the need for a more efficient approach.
*   **Novelty:** The Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a genuinely novel approach to approximating covariance matrix adaptation, moving beyond simply delaying updates.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a theoretical argument, albeit with assumptions, justifying why the approximation should not significantly degrade performance.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the speed-up offered by the Cholesky-CMA-ES on standard benchmark functions.
*   **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is logically structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Relevance:**  While the theoretical speed-up is compelling, the practical impact may be limited. In many applications, the evaluation of the objective function (the "black-box oracle") dominates the overall runtime, making the CMA-ES update time a relatively minor factor.
*   **Dimensionality Dependence:** The advantage of the Cholesky-CMA-ES becomes most apparent at higher dimensionalities, which may be less common in practical applications.
*   **Convergence Assumptions:** The theoretical justification relies on assumptions about the convergence of the sequence of covariance matrices. Relaxing these assumptions could strengthen the theoretical foundation.
*   **Limited Empirical Analysis:** While the benchmark results are encouraging, additional experiments could further bolster the claims.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to improving the efficiency of a widely used and highly effective optimization algorithm.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a significant speed-up without sacrificing performance on standard benchmarks.
*   The work addresses a well-recognized computational bottleneck in CMA-ES.
*   The manuscript is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The practical impact of the speed-up may be limited by the dominance of objective function evaluation time in many applications.
*   The theoretical justification relies on potentially strong convergence assumptions.
*   Additional empirical validation could strengthen the claims.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Analysis of *E<sub>t</sub>*:**  Include visualizations or quantitative analysis of the evolution of the orthogonal matrix *E<sub>t</sub>* over optimization trajectories.  Comparing *E<sub>t</sub>* obtained with both the full CMA-ES and the Cholesky-CMA-ES would provide empirical evidence supporting the theoretical claim.
*   **Runtime Comparison with Baseline:** Include a runtime comparison to the standard CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies for completeness.
*   **Spectral Sensitivity Analysis:** Conduct a simulation study on quadratic functions with varying spectral distributions (inspired by Stich & Müller, 2012) to assess the impact of spectral properties on performance and the error in *E<sub>t</sub>*.
*   **Real-World Application:**  Demonstrate the effectiveness of the Cholesky-CMA-ES on a relevant real-world application where computational speed is critical, such as policy gradient methods in robotics.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The Cholesky-CMA-ES represents a valuable optimization for a widely used algorithm. While the practical impact may vary depending on the specific application, the theoretical and empirical evidence presented are compelling.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the suggestions for improvement.
