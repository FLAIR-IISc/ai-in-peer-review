

This paper proposes an efficient variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the standard matrix square root computation with a triangular Cholesky factorization to reduce time and space complexity. The authors demonstrate that this modification maintains the same optimization performance in terms of objective function evaluations while significantly reducing computational overhead. The work builds upon prior efforts to improve CMA-ES efficiency, particularly referencing the Cholesky-based approaches by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), but makes a significant contribution by providing a theoretically justified adaptation that preserves the algorithm's behavior while achieving optimal complexity.

The paper's main strengths are substantial. Regarding quality, the authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for their approach, including Lemma 1 which establishes that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor diminishes as the algorithm converges. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple CMA-ES variants across various benchmark functions and dimensions (4 to 256). The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves identical optimization performance to standard CMA-ES (measured in function evaluations) while demonstrating significant speedups in wall-clock time, with the speedup increasing substantially with dimensionality (up to 20x faster at d=64). The authors also provide valuable insights about the numerical stability benefits, noting that the Cholesky factorization provides eigenvalues for free and enables efficient inversion.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured explanations of the CMA-ES algorithm and the proposed modifications. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and the rankOneUpdate procedure (Algorithm 2) are particularly well-designed and would enable replication. The discussion of the relationship between the Cholesky factor and the matrix square root, as well as the theoretical justification for the approximation, is exceptionally clear.

In terms of originality, this work makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a theoretically sound approach that addresses a persistent computational bottleneck in CMA-ES. While previous work has explored Cholesky-based approaches (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015), the authors' theoretical justification for why the standard CMA-ES behavior is preserved is novel and significant. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by addressing the critical issue of the covariance matrix square root in the cumulative step-size adaptation mechanism.

The significance of this work is substantial for practitioners working with high-dimensional optimization problems. CMA-ES is widely used in machine learning applications like hyperparameter tuning and reinforcement learning policy search, where computational efficiency is crucial. The authors' demonstration that their approach maintains identical optimization performance while significantly reducing runtime makes this particularly valuable for real-world applications.

A minor weakness is that the paper does not fully address the practical limitations of the approach. While the authors mention that for extremely high-dimensional problems one would need to resort to low-dimensional approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015), they do not explore how the Cholesky-CMA-ES could be combined with such methods to handle even larger problems. The paper states that "the Cholesky-CMA-ES scales quadratically with the problem dimensionality," but does not clarify what constitutes "too large" for this approach. For instance, the authors note that at d=256, the matrix size exceeded L2 cache, which affected performance. This raises the question of how the authors would handle problems where d is on the order of 1000 or more, where even quadratic complexity might become prohibitive.

The paper also could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the numerical stability aspects. While the authors claim "numerically more stable computation" (as noted in the draft review), they don't provide specific theoretical or empirical evidence to support this claim beyond the Cholesky factorization's inherent properties. A brief discussion of how the Cholesky approach avoids potential numerical issues associated with the matrix square root computation would strengthen this point.

The authors' claim that their method "can and should replace the standard CMA-ES if updating and storing the covariance matrix matters" is well-supported by their empirical results. However, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between the Cholesky-CMA-ES and other approaches for different problem characteristics. For instance, how does the performance compare to the diagonal CMA-ES (Ros and Hansen, 2008) on problems where the diagonal approximation is appropriate?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature. The theoretical foundation is sound, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the practical implications are clear. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES should be considered for adoption in any application where computational efficiency is a concern and the full covariance matrix adaptation is necessary.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a well-justified solution that advances the state of the art in derivative-free optimization. The authors have clearly demonstrated that their approach provides substantial computational benefits without compromising optimization performance. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed with additional discussion in the final version, but they do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.
