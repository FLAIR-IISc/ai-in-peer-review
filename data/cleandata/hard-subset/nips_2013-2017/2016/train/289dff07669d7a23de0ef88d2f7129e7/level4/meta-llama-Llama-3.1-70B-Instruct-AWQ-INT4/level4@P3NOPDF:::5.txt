The manuscript introduces a novel update scheme for the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES. This scheme approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation, which has a complexity of O(d^3), by a novel method that operates in O(μd^2), where d is the dimension of the optimization problem and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea behind this update is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix rather than a full covariance matrix and its corresponding square root.

The primary difference between the Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES lies in the absence of estimating a random orthogonal matrix Et at each step. Under certain restrictive assumptions regarding the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target covariance matrix, the authors provide an asymptotic argument that the sequence of Et will converge to an identity matrix, thereby rendering the two schemes equivalent.

The manuscript validates the Cholesky-CMA-ES and demonstrates its general speed-up through the use of standard runtime and convergence plots on selected objective functions. Furthermore, it compares the proposed method to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives that delay the full covariance matrix update by different means. This paper makes a solid contribution to accelerating the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm, which is arguably the best algorithm for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the topology of the objective function and first or higher-order information is difficult to access or not helpful.

The proposed method significantly speeds up the algorithm without compromising optimization performance on the synthetic test cases. However, a common weakness of any speed-up proposal for CMA-ES is that, in practice, the overall optimization process is often dominated by the runtime of the black-box oracle (the objective function). As a result, the actual runtime of the CMA-ES is frequently negligible compared to that of the oracle. Moreover, in typical scenarios where CMA-ES is employed, the dimensionality of the problems rarely exceeds a few tens of variables. The runtime of CMA-ES only becomes a significant factor when a single function evaluation is on the order of milliseconds or smaller, and the dimensionality is higher. This is the targeted scenario in this manuscript, which may be less realistic in practice.

Given that theoretical guarantees are notoriously challenging to obtain for complex schemes like CMA-ES, I propose a few additional empirical experiments and plots to strengthen the presentation of the results. Firstly, it would be instructive to visualize the evolution of Et on selected optimization trajectories, for instance, in different norms. An experiment where the full CMA-ES is used, and the approximation is calculated simultaneously and compared, would provide further empirical evidence about the actual fluctuations of Et over time.

Secondly, including actual runtimes and performance of CMA-ES without delay strategies would be beneficial for completeness. Thirdly, although the used test functions are standard in the evolutionary optimization community, I would prefer a simulation study on quadratic functions where the spectrum of quadratic forms is varied continuously (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012). This would enable a comparison of the solution quality and error in Et in dependence of the spectral distribution.

Lastly, it would be beneficial to include a real-world experiment that accompanies the present simulation results. For instance, policy gradients in real-time robotics applications might be a suitable choice, where speed is crucial, and the parameter space is not too large.
