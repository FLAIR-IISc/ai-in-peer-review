## Review of CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper presents a modification to the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) leveraging Cholesky decomposition instead of eigenvalue decomposition to reduce computational time and memory usage, particularly in high-dimensional optimization problems. The core idea – updating the Cholesky factor directly – is sound and addresses a known bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES implementation. The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and proposed approach. The experimental results, demonstrating significant speedups, are encouraging and suggest a practical benefit to adopting this modified algorithm.

The CMA-ES is a widely used and highly effective derivative-free optimization algorithm, frequently applied in machine learning for tasks like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning (citing Hansen & Ostermeier 2001, Beyer 2007, Eiben & Smith 2015, Gomez et al. 2008, and others). The computational cost associated with covariance matrix updates, scaling with O(d<sup>2</sup>) or worse, is a significant limitation, especially as problem dimensionality *d* increases. Previous efforts to address this issue, such as delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier 2001) or restricting model complexity (Poland & Zell 2001, Ros & Hansen 2008), introduce trade-offs between computational efficiency and convergence speed. More recent work utilizing Cholesky factorization (Suttorp et al. 2009, Krause & Igel 2015) has shown promise, but this paper argues that existing methods don’t fully leverage the potential of Cholesky decomposition within the standard CMA-ES framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Practical Improvement:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a tangible improvement in runtime, especially for larger dimensionalities, without sacrificing the number of function evaluations required for convergence – a crucial benefit.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a theoretical argument, rooted in the convergence behavior of the algorithm and the properties of Cholesky decomposition, to support the claim that the approximation introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor does not significantly deteriorate performance.  Lemma 1 is a particularly important contribution.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the core concepts and modifications to the CMA-ES algorithm.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate consistent speedups over standard CMA-ES implementations, validating the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** While the paper presents a valuable optimization, the core technique of updating the Cholesky factor directly builds heavily on the work of Krause & Igel (2015) and Suttorp et al. (2009). The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how it extends these prior works, rather than presenting it as a largely novel approach. The reviewer’s concern regarding the similarity to the “More Efficient Rank-one…” work is valid and should be directly addressed.
*   **Complexity Analysis Clarity:** The claim of O(µd<sup>2</sup>) complexity is correct, but doesn't fully explain the observed speedup. A more detailed discussion of *constant factors* and a comparison using Θ-notation to highlight the practical improvement over the standard CMA-ES would be helpful.  Why is the constant factor reduced by this approach?
*   **Error Analysis:** While the paper argues the introduced error is small and decreases over time, empirical validation beyond final performance metrics is lacking. Investigating the behavior of the rotation matrix *E<sub>t</sub>* over iterations, as mentioned in the discussion of Lemma 1, would strengthen the theoretical argument and provide further confidence in the approach. Experiments specifically designed to quantify the error introduced by the approximation would be valuable.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** The review correctly points out minor notational inconsistencies (wk, 'F' vs 'f', 'n' vs 'd') that should be addressed in a revised version.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The proposed method provides a significant practical speedup for CMA-ES, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
*   The theoretical justification, while not fully conclusive, provides a reasonable basis for the observed performance.
*   The paper is well-written and presents a clear and concise explanation of the algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The originality of the work is limited, as it largely builds upon existing techniques for updating Cholesky factorizations.
*   The complexity analysis lacks sufficient detail to fully explain the observed speedup.
*   The error analysis is incomplete, and further empirical validation is needed to support the claim that the approximation does not significantly impact performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable, albeit incremental, improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm. The performance gains are significant and the theoretical arguments are reasonable. However, the limited originality and the lack of a more thorough error analysis prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance in its current form. I believe the paper could be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses outlined above.

**Recommendation:**  **Reject with encouragement to revise.** The authors should focus on more clearly articulating the contributions relative to prior work, providing a more detailed complexity analysis, and including additional experiments to validate the error analysis. A revised version addressing these points would be a valuable contribution to the field.
