The manuscript presents a novel update scheme for CMA-ES, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, which approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation process—typically with a complexity of O(d^3)—with a more efficient scheme running in O(\mu d^2), where *d* is the optimization problem dimension and \mu is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix instead of a full covariance matrix and its square root. A key difference from the original CMA-ES lies in the omission of estimating a random orthogonal matrix *E<sub>t</sub>* at each step. Under specific assumptions concerning the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices towards an unknown target, the authors provide an asymptotic argument demonstrating that this sequence of *E<sub>t</sub>* matrices converges to the identity matrix, thus establishing equivalence between the two schemes. The validity and performance gains of Cholesky-CMA-ES are demonstrated through standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark objective functions, and by comparison to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants employing different delay strategies for the full covariance matrix update. This work constitutes a valuable contribution to accelerating the well-regarded CMA-ES algorithm, widely considered one of the most effective methods for non-convex, black-box optimization problems where limited information is available about the objective function’s topology and gradient-based methods are impractical. The proposed method achieves substantial speed-ups without compromising optimization performance on the tested synthetic cases. However, a common challenge for CMA-ES speed-up proposals is that the overall optimization time is frequently dominated by the black-box oracle (objective function) evaluation, rendering the CMA-ES runtime comparatively negligible. Furthermore, problem dimensionalities in typical CMA-ES applications rarely exceed a few tens of variables. Consequently, the runtime of CMA-ES becomes significant only when function evaluations are very fast (on the order of milliseconds or smaller) and the dimensionality is high – a scenario targeted by this work, though potentially less common in practice. Given the inherent difficulty in obtaining strong theoretical guarantees for complex algorithms like CMA-ES, I recommend a few additional empirical investigations to strengthen the results. First, visualizing the evolution of *E<sub>t</sub>* along selected optimization trajectories—perhaps through different norms—would be insightful. An experiment running both the full CMA-ES and the approximation simultaneously, allowing for direct comparison, could provide further empirical evidence of *E<sub>t</sub>’s* temporal fluctuations. Second, including runtime and performance results for standard CMA-ES without delay strategies would offer a more complete comparison. Third, for the quadratic test functions, a simulation study varying the spectrum of quadratic forms continuously (as in Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012) would allow for a more nuanced comparison of solution quality and error in *E<sub>t</sub>* as a function of the spectral distribution. Finally, complementing the simulation results with a real-world experiment—such as policy gradients in real-time robotics—where speed is critical and the parameter space is manageable, would be highly beneficial.
