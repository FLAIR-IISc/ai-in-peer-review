This paper addresses the computational complexity of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm. The main computational bottleneck in CMA-ES is the update of the covariance matrix, which typically involves an O(d^3) eigenvalue decomposition. To mitigate this, the authors propose a new variant, which they call "Cholesky-CMA-ES," that directly updates the Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix in O(µd^2) time. The key modification involves an approximation in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, where the inverse of the matrix square root is replaced by the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor. The authors provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) suggesting that the error introduced by this approximation diminishes as the algorithm converges. Empirical results on several benchmark functions demonstrate that the proposed method achieves a significant reduction in wall-clock time compared to the standard CMA-ES without increasing the number of function evaluations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known and practically important limitation of CMA-ES. Reducing the per-iteration complexity from O(d^3) to O(d^2) without degrading optimization performance is a valuable contribution that makes CMA-ES more applicable to higher-dimensional problems where function evaluations are relatively cheap.

2.  **Quality:** The proposed modification is technically sound and the theoretical argument for why the approximation in the CSA step is reasonable is compelling. The argument that the rotation matrices (Et) converge as the covariance matrix shape stabilizes provides a good intuition for the method's effectiveness.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments clearly demonstrate the primary benefit of the algorithm: a significant speedup in wall-clock time that increases with the problem dimension. The results in Figure 2 are particularly convincing. The fact that this speedup is achieved with a similar number of function evaluations (Figure 1) supports the claim that the algorithm's convergence behavior is preserved.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed to strengthen its contribution.

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper's contribution appears to be an incremental extension of previous work, particularly Krause and Igel (2015). The `rankOneUpdate` algorithm, which is central to the proposed method, was presented in their work for (1+1)-strategies. The main algorithmic novelty in this paper seems to be the application of this update in a loop to handle the rank-µ update of the standard CMA-ES. The paper would be much stronger if the authors could better articulate the novelty of their contribution beyond this extension.

2.  **Naming and Acknowledgment of Prior Art:** The name "Cholesky-CMA-ES" was previously introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a similar algorithm that also maintains a Cholesky factor. Using the same name without acknowledging or clearly distinguishing from this well-known prior work is confusing and potentially misleading. The authors should address this conflict, for instance by renaming their algorithm or by explicitly stating how their "Cholesky-CMA-ES" differs from and improves upon Suttorp's version.

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The experiments omit a crucial baseline: the Cholesky-based method of Suttorp et al. (2009). The authors dismiss this prior work by stating that their own approach has advantages "compared to approaches using a non-triangular Cholesky factorization (e.g., Suttorp et al. [2009])". They also claim the Suttorp et al. method is "prone to numerical instabilities," but this assertion is not supported by evidence in this paper and seems to contradict the claims in the original work. A direct empirical comparison of runtime and convergence performance against the Suttorp et al. algorithm is essential for a fair assessment of the proposed method's contribution to the state of the art.

4.  **Overstated Claims:**
    *   The title's claim of "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" is too strong. The complexity is optimal only under the assumption that a full-rank covariance matrix is necessary. For problems with simpler structure (e.g., separable or spherical functions), O(d) methods that update only the diagonal are more efficient. The claim should be qualified to reflect this.
    *   The paper suggests the method is a step towards large-scale optimization, but the O(d^2) complexity still limits its applicability to very large `d`. This framing should be toned down.

5.  **Unexplained Empirical Results:** In Figure 1b (Cigar function), the median performance of CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES appear to converge to different objective function values. This contradicts the central claim that the modification does not change the algorithm's behavior. This discrepancy should be investigated and explained.

**Summary of Recommendations:**

*   **Clarify Contribution:** Explicitly differentiate the algorithmic novelty from Krause and Igel (2015).
*   **Resolve Naming Issue:** Acknowledge the work of Suttorp et al. (2009) and either rename the algorithm or clearly justify the reuse of the name.
*   **Strengthen Experiments:** Include an empirical comparison with the Suttorp et al. (2009) Cholesky-CMA-ES variant in all relevant figures.
*   **Refine Claims:** Qualify the "optimality" claim and moderate the language around "large-scale" optimization.
*   **Correct Minor Inaccuracies:** The statement that EVD is "typically" performed in each iteration is incorrect for optimized implementations; it is usually done periodically. This should be corrected.

Overall, the paper presents a useful and efficient implementation of CMA-ES for problems where maintaining a full covariance matrix is necessary. The proposed modification is likely the most efficient method available for this specific scenario. However, the concerns about incremental novelty, the incomplete experimental comparison, and the misleading naming and claims currently limit the paper's overall impact. Addressing these issues would significantly improve the quality of the manuscript.
