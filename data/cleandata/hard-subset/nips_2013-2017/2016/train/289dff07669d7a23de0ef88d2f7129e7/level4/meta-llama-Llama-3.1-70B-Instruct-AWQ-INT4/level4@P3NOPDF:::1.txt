The Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) is a state-of-the-art stochastic optimization algorithm that combines multiple learning mechanisms to adapt the parameters of a multivariate normal sampling distribution. This distribution can be equivalently viewed as an adaptation of an underlying metric. The authors of this paper propose a novel variant of the CMA-ES algorithm, specifically designed to update the covariance matrix using triangular Cholesky factors. This new approach offers significant advantages over previous updates, including reduced complexity and memory requirements.

The proposed method is thoroughly evaluated through numerical experiments, demonstrating that the new update does not compromise the performance of the standard CMA-ES in terms of the number of function evaluations required to reach a given target. Moreover, the new update substantially reduces the wall-clock time of the algorithm, particularly in high-dimensional spaces. This contribution is highly significant in the domain of optimization, and it is likely that this variant of CMA-ES will become the default method for medium to large-scale optimization problems. The advantages of this approach are clear: reduced memory requirements, decreased complexity, and the ability to obtain eigenvalues of the covariance matrix at no additional cost.

The paper is well-written, and the authors have carefully evaluated their approach. The connection between CMA-ES and machine learning, information geometry, and reinforcement learning makes this contribution a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could provide additional details on how they ensured that the different implementations used in the experiments were comparable in terms of CPU performance. Specifically, it would be helpful to know whether the same implementation language was used for all experiments and whether any optimizations were employed.

One minor point of clarification is needed regarding Figure 2. The authors state that "the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences" between the new and standard CMA-ES algorithms. However, this trend does not appear to hold for the Cigar and Discus functions in 256 dimensions. A brief explanation for this discrepancy would be helpful to provide a clearer understanding of the results.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of optimization, and its connection to machine learning and related areas makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.
