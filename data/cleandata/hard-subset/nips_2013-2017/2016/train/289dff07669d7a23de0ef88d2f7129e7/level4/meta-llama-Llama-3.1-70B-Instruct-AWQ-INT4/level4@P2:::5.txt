The manuscript "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" presents a novel update scheme for the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a widely used algorithm for real-valued derivative-free optimization. The proposed method, called Cholesky-CMA-ES, approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation with a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix update, reducing the time complexity from O(d^3) to O(μd^2), where d is the optimization problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration.

The authors provide a theoretical justification for the convergence of the new algorithm, showing that the sequence of orthogonal matrices Et, which are not estimated in the Cholesky-CMA-ES, converges to an identity matrix under certain assumptions. The validity and speed-up of the Cholesky-CMA-ES are demonstrated through experiments on standard benchmark functions, comparing it to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives that delay the full covariance matrix update.

The paper makes a solid contribution to speeding up the CMA-ES algorithm, which is particularly useful for large-scale optimization problems where the dimensionality is high, and the runtime of the black-box oracle is relatively fast. The proposed method shows no deterioration of optimization performance on the synthetic test cases.

However, it is worth noting that the overall optimization process is often dominated by the runtime of the black-box oracle, and the actual runtime of the CMA-ES is negligible in many scenarios. Therefore, the benefits of the proposed method may be more pronounced in specific scenarios where the dimensionality is high, and the function evaluations are relatively fast.

To further strengthen the presentation of the results, I would like to propose a few additional empirical experiments and plots. Firstly, it would be instructive to see the evolution of Et on selected optimization trajectories, e.g., in different norms, by comparing the full CMA-ES with the Cholesky-CMA-ES approximation. Secondly, it would be useful to include actual runtimes and performance of CMA-ES without delay strategies for completeness. Thirdly, a simulation study on quadratic functions with varying spectral distributions could provide further insights into the solution quality and error in Et. Lastly, a real-world experiment, such as policy gradients in real-time robotics applications, could accompany the present simulation results and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method.

In terms of the paper's quality, the manuscript is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical justification. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are presented in a clear and intuitive manner. However, some minor improvements could be made to the writing style, such as providing more detailed explanations of the related work and the implications of the results.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning, and I recommend its acceptance for publication.
