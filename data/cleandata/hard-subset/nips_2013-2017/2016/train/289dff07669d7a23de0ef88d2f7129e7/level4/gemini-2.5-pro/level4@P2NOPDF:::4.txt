This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) by leveraging a triangular Cholesky factorization. The primary contribution is replacing the standard algorithm's computationally expensive eigendecomposition—used to find the principal axes of the covariance matrix—with a more efficient update based on the Cholesky factor. This change is designed to reduce the per-iteration computational cost, which is a known bottleneck for CMA-ES in high dimensions. The authors provide a theoretical justification to demonstrate that this reformulation preserves the performance characteristics of the original algorithm. The empirical results confirm that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES reduces runtime complexity without a significant change in the number of objective function evaluations needed for convergence. Overall, this work is a valuable contribution that improves the practical applicability of CMA-ES.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses a significant and well-known limitation of CMA-ES: the O(N^3) complexity of the eigendecomposition, where N is the dimensionality of the search space.
*   The proposed solution is elegant and theoretically sound. The authors clearly show how to adapt the update rules of CMA-ES to operate directly on the Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix.
*   The claim that the method reduces wall-clock time, with the benefit increasing with dimensionality, is well-supported by the experimental results. The fact that this is achieved while maintaining a similar convergence profile in terms of function evaluations is a strong result.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  The authors state that the Cholesky-based computation is "numerically more stable." While Cholesky factorization is generally known for its excellent numerical properties, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed theoretical explanation or empirical analysis of this benefit within the specific context of the CMA-ES update equations. Could the authors elaborate on why this formulation provides superior stability compared to the standard approach?

2.  My understanding is that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires approximately the same number of objective function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES, but significantly lessens the wall-clock time, with this advantage growing as the search space dimensionality increases. Could the authors confirm if this is the correct interpretation of the performance trade-off?

3.  While the proposed method improves scalability, its O(N^2) complexity may still be a bottleneck for very high-dimensional problems, such as training large neural networks in direct policy search. Have the authors considered combining their Cholesky-based framework with low-dimensional approximations, such as the limited-memory or low-rank covariance matrix updates proposed by Loshchilov (2015) and others? A discussion on the potential synergies or challenges of integrating these approaches would be a valuable addition, as it would address the practical concerns of applying CMA-ES to extremely large-scale problems.
