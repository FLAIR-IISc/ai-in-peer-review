This manuscript introduces a new update mechanism for CMA-ES, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES. The proposed method aims to approximate the standard covariance matrix adaptation, reducing its computational complexity from O(d³) to O(μd²), where d represents the problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core concept involves maintaining and updating a lower-triangular Cholesky factor directly, instead of the full covariance matrix and its corresponding matrix square root. This approach deviates from the original CMA-ES by forgoing the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix, Et, in each iteration.

The authors provide a theoretical justification for this approximation under specific, and rather restrictive, assumptions—namely, the convergence of the covariance matrix sequence to a target matrix. They argue that, asymptotically, the sequence of Et matrices converges to the identity matrix, thereby rendering the two schemes equivalent in the limit.

To demonstrate the validity and computational speed-up of Cholesky-CMA-ES, the paper presents standard runtime and convergence plots. These experiments are conducted on a selection of benchmark objective functions, and the results are benchmarked against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that also employ delayed covariance matrix updates.

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the ongoing effort of accelerating the CMA-ES algorithm. CMA-ES is widely regarded as a top-performing method for non-convex black-box optimization, particularly when the objective function's topology is unknown and gradient information is unavailable or unhelpful. The proposed method achieves a considerable speed-up without any apparent degradation in optimization performance on the presented synthetic benchmarks. However, despite the general importance of this acceleration, a notable weakness—common to many CMA-ES speed-up proposals—is its practical applicability. In real-world scenarios, the total optimization time is typically dominated by the objective function evaluations, rendering the internal runtime of the CMA-ES algorithm itself negligible in comparison. Furthermore, CMA-ES is most commonly applied to problems with relatively low dimensionality. The scenario where this speed-up becomes significant—problems with high dimensionality and extremely fast function evaluations (on the order of milliseconds or less)—is the one targeted by this work, but its practical prevalence is questionable.

Given the notorious difficulty of establishing theoretical guarantees for a complex algorithm like CMA-ES, I suggest several additional empirical experiments that could significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.

First, visualizing the evolution of the omitted `Et` matrix during optimization runs would be highly instructive. An experiment could be designed where the full CMA-ES is run, while the proposed approximation is computed in parallel. Tracking a norm of the difference would provide direct empirical evidence of `Et`'s fluctuations and its deviation from the identity matrix over time.

Second, for the sake of completeness, the paper should include baseline results showing the performance and actual runtimes of the standard CMA-ES without any update-delaying strategies.

Third, while the benchmark functions are standard, the analysis could be deepened. Specifically, for quadratic objective functions, a simulation study that systematically varies the eigenvalue spectrum of the quadratic form (e.g., following the methodology of Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012) would be beneficial. This would allow for an analysis of how solution quality and the approximation error in `Et` depend on the spectral properties of the problem.

Fourth, the inclusion of a real-world application would greatly enhance the impact of the paper by complementing the current simulation-based results. An area such as policy gradient optimization for real-time robotics, where computational speed is critical even in moderately-sized parameter spaces, could serve as a compelling case study.
