The manuscript introduces a novel update scheme for the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), referred to as the Cholesky-CMA-ES. This scheme approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation, which has a complexity of O(d^3), by a method that operates in O(μd^2), where d represents the dimension of the optimization problem and μ denotes the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea behind the update is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix instead of a full covariance matrix and its corresponding square root. The primary difference between the Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES lies in the omission of estimating a random orthogonal matrix Et at each step. Under certain restrictive assumptions regarding the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target covariance matrix, the authors provide an asymptotic argument that the sequence of Et will converge to an identity matrix, thereby rendering the two schemes equivalent. The manuscript validates the Cholesky-CMA-ES and demonstrates its general speed-up through the use of standard runtime and convergence plots on selected objective functions, as well as comparisons with two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives that delay full covariance matrix updates by different means. This paper makes a significant contribution to accelerating the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm, which is arguably the best algorithm for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the objective function's topology and first or higher-order information is difficult to access or not helpful. The proposed method substantially speeds up the algorithm without compromising optimization performance on the synthetic test cases. However, a common weakness of any CMA-ES speed-up proposal is that, in practice, the overall optimization process is often dominated by the runtime of the black-box oracle (the objective function), and the actual runtime of the CMA-ES is frequently negligible in comparison. Furthermore, in typical scenarios where CMA-ES is employed, the dimensionality of the problems rarely exceeds a few tens of variables. The runtime of CMA-ES only becomes a significant factor when a single function evaluation is on the order of milliseconds or smaller, and the dimensionality is higher, which is the targeted scenario in this manuscript (albeit potentially less realistic in practice). As theoretical guarantees for an involved scheme like CMA-ES are notoriously difficult to obtain, I suggest a few additional empirical experiments and plots to strengthen the presentation of the results. Firstly, it would be instructive to visualize the evolution of Et on selected optimization trajectories, e.g., in different norms. I propose an experiment where the full CMA-ES is used, and the approximation is calculated simultaneously and compared, providing further empirical evidence about the actual fluctuations of Et over time. Secondly, it would be beneficial to include actual runtimes and performance of CMA-ES without delay strategies in this contribution for completeness. Thirdly, although the test functions used are standard in the evolutionary optimization community, I would prefer a simulation study on quadratic functions where the spectrum of quadratic forms is varied continuously (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012), and compare the solution quality and error in Et in dependence of the spectral distribution. Lastly, it would be valuable to include a real-world experiment that accompanies the present simulation results, such as policy gradients in real-time robotics applications, where speed is indeed crucial, and the parameter space is not excessively large.
