This manuscript presents a novel update scheme for the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), termed Cholesky-CMA-ES. The core innovation lies in approximating the full covariance matrix update—typically O(d<sup>3</sup>) in complexity, where *d* is the problem dimension—with a scheme that scales as O(μd<sup>2</sup>), with μ being the population size (often O(log(d))). This is achieved by maintaining and updating a lower-triangular Cholesky decomposition of the covariance matrix, rather than the full matrix and its square root.

The key difference between Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES arises from omitting the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix, *E<sub>t</sub>*, at each iteration. To justify this approximation, the authors provide an asymptotic argument, based on restrictive assumptions regarding the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices, demonstrating that *E<sub>t</sub>* converges to the identity matrix under these conditions, thereby establishing equivalence between the two schemes. 

The paper validates the proposed method and demonstrates its speedup through standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark objective functions. Performance is compared against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that employ delay strategies for the full covariance matrix update. The results convincingly demonstrate a substantial acceleration of the CMA-ES algorithm without compromising optimization performance on the tested synthetic problems. 

This work represents a significant contribution to the field of non-convex black-box optimization. CMA-ES is widely regarded as one of the most effective algorithms in scenarios where little is known about the objective function’s topology and gradient information is unavailable or ineffective.  The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES substantially reduces computational cost in these contexts. 

However, a common caveat for any speedup of CMA-ES is that the overall optimization time is often dominated by evaluations of the black-box objective function. In many practical applications, the time spent within the CMA-ES algorithm itself is negligible.  The Cholesky-CMA-ES is most impactful when function evaluations are extremely fast (on the order of milliseconds or less) and the problem dimensionality is relatively high—a scenario which, while important, may not always reflect typical use cases, where problem dimensions rarely exceed a few tens of variables.

Given the inherent difficulty of establishing rigorous theoretical guarantees for complex algorithms like CMA-ES, I recommend a few additional empirical investigations to further strengthen the manuscript. First, visualizing the evolution of *E<sub>t</sub>* along optimization trajectories—perhaps by examining its norm over time—would provide valuable insight into the approximation’s behavior.  A simultaneous run of both full CMA-ES and Cholesky-CMA-ES, calculating the approximation *E<sub>t</sub>* during the full CMA-ES run, could offer empirical evidence of the fluctuations in *E<sub>t</sub>*. Second, including performance results for standard CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies would serve as a useful baseline for comparison. 

Third, when evaluating performance on quadratic functions, a simulation study systematically varying the spectrum of the quadratic form—as suggested by Stich and Mueller (PPSN 2012)—would be beneficial.  This would allow for a more nuanced analysis of solution quality and the error in *E<sub>t</sub>* as a function of the spectral distribution. Finally, complementing the synthetic results with a real-world experiment would significantly enhance the paper's impact.  Applications such as policy gradients in real-time robotics, where speed is critical and the parameter space is moderately sized, could provide a compelling demonstration of the method’s practical utility.
