

The manuscript introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel update scheme for covariance matrix adaptation that approximates the standard CMA-ES (with O(d³) complexity) using a lower-triangular Cholesky factorization. This reduces per-iteration complexity to O(μd²), where d is the problem dimension and μ (typically O(log d)) is the population size. The approach maintains and updates the Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix and its square root. The key distinction from standard CMA-ES arises from omitting the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix E_t at each step. Under restrictive assumptions—specifically, convergence of the covariance sequence to an unknown target—the authors provide an asymptotic argument showing E_t converges to the identity matrix, rendering the schemes equivalent in the limit. The method’s validity and speedup are demonstrated via standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark functions, comparing favorably against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates.

This work makes a solid contribution to accelerating CMA-ES, arguably the premier algorithm for non-convex black-box optimization when derivative information is unavailable. The proposed method significantly reduces computational cost without degrading performance on synthetic test cases. However, a critical limitation applies to all CMA-ES speedup proposals: in practice, the optimization runtime is typically dominated by the black-box objective function evaluations (the oracle), making the CMA-ES runtime negligible unless function evaluations are extremely fast (e.g., milliseconds or less). Furthermore, CMA-ES is most commonly applied to problems with dimensions below a few tens—where the proposed speedup is less relevant. The targeted scenario (high dimensionality with rapid evaluations) is plausible but represents a niche application, potentially limiting broader impact.

Given the inherent difficulty in establishing theoretical guarantees for CMA-ES, I recommend three additional empirical analyses to strengthen the presentation:  
1. **Tracking E_t evolution**: Show E_t’s behavior over optimization trajectories (e.g., in spectral or Frobenius norms) by simultaneously computing the full CMA-ES and the Cholesky approximation. This would empirically validate the convergence of E_t to identity.  
2. **Baseline runtime comparison**: Include actual runtime data for standard CMA-ES (without delay strategies) to contextualize the speedup claim relative to the baseline.  
3. **Spectral sensitivity study**: Extend the quadratic function experiments to systematically vary the spectrum of the quadratic form (as in Stich & Müller, PPSN 2012), reporting solution quality and E_t error as a function of spectral condition number.  

A real-world case study would further bolster the argument. For instance, applying the method to real-time robotics policy optimization—where parameter spaces are moderately sized but speed is critical—would demonstrate tangible utility beyond synthetic benchmarks. While the current experiments are adequate for technical validation, these additions would more convincingly address the practical relevance of the speedup.
