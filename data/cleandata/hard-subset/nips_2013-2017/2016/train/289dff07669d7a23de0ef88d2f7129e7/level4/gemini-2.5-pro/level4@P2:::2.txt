This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) to reduce its computational complexity. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity dominated by the O(d³) cost of eigenvalue decomposition of the d x d covariance matrix. The authors propose replacing this step with a direct, quadratic-time update of the matrix's triangular Cholesky factor. A key challenge is that the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism in CMA-ES relies on the inverse matrix square root (C⁻¹/²), which is not directly available from a Cholesky factorization (C = AAᵀ). The core contribution of this paper is to approximate the CSA path by using the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (A⁻¹) instead. The authors provide a theoretical argument, based on the convergence of the covariance matrix's shape, that the error introduced by this approximation should be small and diminish over time. Empirically, the proposed "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is shown to achieve significant wall-clock speedups over standard implementations on several benchmark functions, without increasing the number of function evaluations required for convergence.

### Strengths

1.  **Practical Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and important bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm. The experimental results demonstrate a dramatic reduction in runtime (e.g., a 20x speedup for d=64), which could significantly extend the applicability of CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems, particularly in machine learning domains like policy search where function evaluations can be relatively cheap.
2.  **Efficiency:** The proposed method achieves asymptotically optimal O(μd²) time complexity for a full covariance matrix update and optimal O(d²) storage complexity. This is a substantial improvement over the standard O(d³) approach.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, clearly motivating the problem and explaining the proposed solution and its theoretical underpinnings.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

The paper is promising, but several major points need to be addressed to strengthen the contribution and justify its acceptance.

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** My primary concern is the perceived novelty of the contribution. The work appears to be a direct extension of Krause and Igel (2015), which introduced an efficient rank-one update for the Cholesky factor in the context of (1+1)-CMA-ES. This paper generalizes that idea to the rank-μ update used in the standard CMA-ES. While this is a valuable and necessary extension, the conceptual leap seems incremental. The authors should more clearly articulate the novel technical challenges involved in extending the update from rank-one to rank-μ and how their solution overcomes them. Is the decomposition of the rank-μ update into a series of rank-one updates a standard technique, or does it require a novel approach?
2.  **Insufficient Validation of the Core Approximation:** The central claim justifying the method's correctness is that replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ in the CSA path (Equation 3) is a valid approximation. The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) rests on the assumption that the covariance matrix's shape converges. However, this is an asymptotic argument, and the behavior during the transient phase of optimization is critical. The paper lacks any direct empirical validation of this core approximation. Relying solely on final performance metrics (i.e., number of function evaluations) is indirect and insufficient. To make a convincing case, the authors should provide experiments that directly measure the impact of this approximation. For example:
    *   How does the norm of the approximated evolution path `p_σ` compare to the true path computed using the standard method over the course of optimization?
    *   The argument relies on the sequence of rotation matrices `E_t` converging such that `Eᵀ_{t-1} E_t → I`. Can the authors plot a metric of this convergence (e.g., `||Eᵀ_{t-1} E_t - I||_F`) during a typical run to show that this condition holds in practice?
3.  **Lack of Detail in Complexity Analysis:** The paper states the complexity is O(μd²), but the provided reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) also has a similar amortized complexity because it only performs the expensive decomposition step periodically. To substantiate the claims of superior performance, the paper would benefit from a more detailed computational analysis. A discussion using Θ-notation or an explicit operation count comparing a single iteration of Cholesky-CMA-ES versus an amortized iteration of CMA-ES-Ref would more clearly explain the source of the significant practical speedup.
4.  **Completeness of a Self-Contained Paper:** For the paper to be self-contained and reproducible, it would be beneficial to briefly show how the standard covariance update in Equation (2) is decomposed into the sequence of rank-one updates applied in Algorithm 1. While referencing prior work is appropriate, this decomposition is central to the proposed algorithm, and its inclusion would greatly improve clarity.

### Minor Comments

*   In Algorithm 2, the variable `γ` is defined but then used in the subsequent line as `γ α_k`. Based on similar algorithms, it seems `γ` might be intended to replace the denominator. Please clarify this update step.
*   There are a few notational inconsistencies. For example, the problem dimension is `d` throughout the text but referred to as `n` in the header of Table 1. Please ensure consistency.

### Summary and Recommendation

This paper presents a practically important and computationally efficient implementation of the CMA-ES algorithm. The demonstrated speedups are impressive and could make this the new default for many users.

However, the current manuscript has significant weaknesses regarding its perceived incremental novelty, the lack of direct empirical evidence for its core theoretical claim, and a need for greater detail in the complexity analysis. The validation of the CSA path approximation is a critical scientific point that must be addressed with dedicated experiments, not just final performance comparisons.

Due to these concerns, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. However, I believe the paper addresses an important problem and the work has significant potential. If the authors can thoroughly address the weaknesses outlined above—especially by providing direct empirical validation of their central approximation and clarifying the novelty over prior work—it could become a strong paper for a future venue.
