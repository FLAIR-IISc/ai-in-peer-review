This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) to reduce its computational complexity. The standard CMA-ES algorithm's runtime is often dominated by the O(d³) cost of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix in each iteration. The authors introduce the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which avoids this cost by directly maintaining and updating a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This update has a time complexity of O(µd²), which is asymptotically optimal for an algorithm that adapts a full covariance matrix. A key challenge is that the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism in the standard CMA-ES requires the inverse of the matrix square root (C⁻¹/²), not the inverse of a Cholesky factor (A⁻¹). The authors' main theoretical contribution is to justify replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹, arguing that while this introduces a rotational error, this error diminishes as the algorithm converges and the shape of the covariance matrix stabilizes. Empirical results on several benchmark functions demonstrate that the proposed method achieves a significant wall-clock speedup compared to a reference CMA-ES implementation without increasing the number of function evaluations required for optimization.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and important bottleneck in CMA-ES: the cubic complexity of the covariance matrix update. Reducing this to quadratic complexity significantly extends the applicability of CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems where function evaluations are relatively cheap, a scenario common in areas like direct policy search.

2.  **Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The use of rank-one updates for the Cholesky factor is a valid and efficient technique. The paper provides a clear theoretical argument (Lemma 1) to justify the approximation made in the CSA path. This justification, based on the convergence of the rotation matrix in the RQ-decomposition, is a valuable contribution that provides confidence that the modification does not fundamentally alter the algorithm's behavior.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. Algorithm 1 clearly outlines the proposed method, and the connection to the standard CMA-ES is well-explained. The experimental results are presented clearly and support the central claim of a significant reduction in wall-clock time.

4.  **Practical Value:** The proposed algorithm offers a substantial practical benefit. The empirical results show a speedup of over 20x for d=64, which is a dramatic improvement. If the performance in terms of function evaluations is indeed identical to the standard algorithm, this variant would be a strong candidate to replace existing implementations for problems where computational cost is a factor.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper's positioning with respect to existing literature is a major concern.
    *   **Naming:** The name "Cholesky-CMA-ES" was previously introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a similar algorithm that also updates a Cholesky factor. The authors do not acknowledge this, instead presenting the name as their own. This is a significant oversight that can create confusion in the field.
    *   **Incrementality:** The core algorithmic contribution appears to be an incremental extension of the work by Krause and Igel (2015), which showed how to perform a rank-one update on a Cholesky factor for a (1+1)-CMA-ES. The proposed method extends this to the rank-µ update by applying the rank-one update in a loop. The paper should more clearly articulate its novelty in this context.

2.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The empirical evaluation is missing comparisons to the most relevant baselines, which weakens the paper's conclusions.
    *   The algorithm by Suttorp et al. (2009) is a direct competitor and should have been included in the empirical comparison. The authors dismiss it by claiming it is "prone to numerical instabilities," but this contradicts the claims in the original Suttorp et al. paper and is not substantiated with any evidence here.
    *   The authors should also discuss the algorithmic differences with Krause and Igel (2015) in more detail.

3.  **Potentially Misleading Claims:**
    *   The title's claim of "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" is an overstatement. While the complexity may be optimal for methods maintaining a *full* covariance matrix, it is not optimal in an absolute sense. For separable problems, diagonal CMA-ES variants have linear complexity, and for spherical functions, no covariance matrix is needed at all.
    *   Positioning the work as a solution for "large-scale optimization" is also questionable, as the O(µd²) complexity still limits its applicability to truly high-dimensional problems (e.g., d > 1000).

4.  **Clarity of Results:** In Figure 1(b) (Cigar function), the plateau reached by the Cholesky-CMA-ES appears to be at a different (worse) objective function value than the reference CMA-ES. This contradicts the central claim that the modification does not change the algorithm's behavior in terms of function evaluations. This discrepancy needs to be explained.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following revisions:

1.  **Address the Naming Issue:** Acknowledge the prior work of Suttorp et al. (2009) and choose a different name for the proposed algorithm to avoid confusion, or clearly position it as a specific variant of "Cholesky-CMA-ES".

2.  **Clarify Novelty:** Explicitly detail the contribution beyond Krause and Igel (2015). If the main contribution is the extension to the rank-µ case and the theoretical justification via Lemma 1, this should be stated clearly.

3.  **Strengthen Empirical Evaluation:** Include the Suttorp et al. (2009) algorithm in all experimental comparisons. This is crucial for fairly assessing the proposed method's performance and contribution. Furthermore, provide evidence to support the claim about the numerical instability of Suttorp's method.

4.  **Explain Discrepancies:** Clarify the difference in performance observed on the Cigar function in Figure 1(b).

5.  **Refine Claims:** Moderate the claims about "optimality" and "large-scale" optimization in the title, abstract, and conclusion to be more precise (e.g., "optimal for full covariance matrix updates").

### Summary

The paper presents a practical and well-justified method for accelerating CMA-ES, which is a valuable contribution. However, its significance is currently obscured by a failure to properly position the work with respect to key prior art, an incomplete empirical evaluation, and some overstated claims. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and clarify its contribution to the field.
