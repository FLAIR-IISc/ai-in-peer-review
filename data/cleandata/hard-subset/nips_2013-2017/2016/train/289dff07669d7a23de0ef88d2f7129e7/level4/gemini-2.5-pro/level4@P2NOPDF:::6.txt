This paper introduces a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that aims to achieve "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity." The central claim is that this modification reduces the computational and memory costs of the algorithm without altering its search behavior. While the goal of improving the efficiency of CMA-ES is valuable, the paper suffers from several major issues regarding novelty, positioning with respect to prior art, and clarity of its contributions.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Naming and Acknowledgment of Prior Work:** A significant concern is the naming of the proposed algorithm as "Cholesky-CMA-ES." This name was first introduced by T. Suttorp et al. (2009) for a similar, well-known algorithm that also uses a Cholesky decomposition to improve efficiency. The manuscript fails to acknowledge this prior work and its established name, instead presenting the name as novel ("Our CMA-ES variant, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES"). This is a serious issue of academic attribution that could mislead readers unfamiliar with the literature. The paper must properly situate its work in the context of both Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause et al. (2015).

2.  **Incremental Contribution:** The algorithmic contribution appears to be an incremental modification of the work by Krause et al. (2015). From my understanding, the core novelty is the application of the `rankOneUpdate` operation within a loop over all individuals in the population, as shown in Algorithm 1. The paper should provide a clearer justification for why this change constitutes a significant contribution beyond the existing framework of Krause et al. As presented, it appears to be a minor algorithmic adjustment.

3.  **Overstated and Misleading Claims:**
    *   The claim of "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" is too strong. The notion of "optimal" is highly context-dependent. For instance, on separable or linear fitness landscapes (e.g., the Sphere function), methods that update only a diagonal covariance matrix or do not use one at all would be far more efficient. The proposed method's optimality is confined to the specific context of algorithms that maintain a full covariance matrix.
    *   The paper claims the Suttorp et al. (2009) algorithm is prone to numerical instabilities, which appears to contradict the claims made in the original 2009 paper. This assertion needs to be substantiated with evidence or a more detailed explanation.
    *   By omitting the Suttorp et al. (2009) variant from the empirical comparisons in Figure 3, the paper may mislead the reader about its relative performance. The proposed approach is roughly twice as fast as Suttorp's, which is a valuable but not transformative improvement. This baseline is essential for a fair evaluation of the contribution.

4.  **Inconsistent Empirical Evidence:** The premise that the proposed variant does not change the behavior of CMA-ES is challenged by the empirical results. In Figure b), the performance on the Cigar function shows a plateau at different objective function values for the reference CMA-ES and the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES. This suggests a behavioral difference that needs to be investigated and explained. It may be an artifact of the plotting methodology (e.g., using medians), but it undermines the core claim and requires clarification.

5.  **Limited Impact on Large-Scale Optimization:** The paper's relevance to large-scale optimization is questionable. The primary bottleneck for CMA-ES in high dimensions is its quadratic complexity in both time and memory, and this work does not change that fundamental scaling. While the constant-factor improvements are beneficial, this method does not represent a breakthrough for applying CMA-ES to truly large-scale problems. I do acknowledge, however, that the proposed modification might be one of the most efficient implementations available when a full covariance matrix is deemed necessary.

**Suggestions for Revision:**

To strengthen the paper, I strongly recommend the following modifications:

a)  **Resolve the Naming Issue:** The authors must acknowledge the prior Cholesky-CMA-ES by Suttorp et al. (2009) and choose a new, unambiguous name for their variant to avoid confusion. The relationship and differences to both Suttorp et al. and Krause et al. must be clearly articulated.

b)  **Expand Empirical Comparisons:** The experiments should be re-run to include direct comparisons with the variants from both Suttorp et al. and Krause et al. All three figures should be updated to include these crucial baselines. If including all variants makes the figures unreadable, the main text should present the most relevant comparisons, with the complete set of results provided in the supplementary material.

c)  **Clarify the Algorithmic Contribution:** The authors need to better explain why their main algorithmic change—placing the `rankOneUpdate` in a "for" loop—is a non-trivial and significant contribution. If my interpretation is incorrect, the paper must provide a clearer explanation of its core technical novelty.

**Minor Note:**

The statement, "Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step," is inaccurate for modern, efficient implementations. This computationally expensive operation is usually performed only periodically (e.g., every `n/10` generations), not in every single step.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, the paper in its current form is too incremental and fails to properly contextualize its contribution within existing literature. The naming issue is a critical flaw, and the empirical evaluation is incomplete. While the proposed efficiency improvement is useful, the claims of optimality and unchanged behavior are not fully substantiated. I recommend rejecting the paper at this time but would be willing to reconsider a substantially revised version that addresses the major concerns outlined above.
