The manuscript introduces a novel CMA-ES update scheme, Cholesky-CMA-ES, which approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation (O(d^3)) with a scheme running in O(μ d^2), where d is the problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log d)). The principal idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix (and its square root). The key difference from standard CMA-ES is that the proposed method avoids estimating a (random) orthogonal matrix E_t at each step. Under restrictive assumptions—namely, that the sequence of covariance matrices converges to an unknown target covariance—the authors give an asymptotic argument that E_t converges to the identity, making the two schemes asymptotically equivalent.

The paper evaluates the method using standard runtime and convergence plots on selected objective functions and compares it against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that defer full covariance updates by different mechanisms. The contribution is solid: it accelerates the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm—arguably the leading approach for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the objective’s topology and derivative information is unavailable or unhelpful—without observed loss in optimization performance on the synthetic benchmarks.

That said, a general caveat applies to any CMA-ES speed-up: in practice, the overall optimization time is often dominated by the black-box oracle (objective function) evaluations, rendering the CMA-ES overhead comparatively negligible. Moreover, typical CMA-ES applications involve problem dimensions of only a few tens of variables. Only when individual function evaluations cost on the order of milliseconds or less and the dimensionality is higher does the algorithmic overhead become a significant factor—this is the target scenario here, which may be less common in practice.

Because strong theoretical guarantees are notoriously difficult for an algorithm as intricate as CMA-ES, I suggest the following additional empirical studies to further strengthen the results:

- Track the evolution of E_t along selected optimization trajectories, e.g., using different matrix norms. An experiment where standard CMA-ES is run and the Cholesky-based approximation is computed in parallel would allow a direct comparison and provide empirical evidence about the magnitude and temporal fluctuations of E_t.

- For completeness, report actual runtimes and performance of standard CMA-ES without delay strategies alongside the proposed approach and the two delayed-update baselines.

- On quadratic functions, include a simulation study that continuously varies the spectrum of the quadratic form (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012). Compare solution quality and the error in E_t as a function of the spectral distribution.

- Add a real-world experiment to complement the simulations. Policy gradient methods in real-time robotics, where speed matters and the parameter space is moderate, would be a fitting application domain.

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated and practically relevant speed-up for CMA-ES, with convincing initial evidence. The additional experiments above would clarify when and why the approximation is tight (via E_t analysis), establish broader baseline comparisons, probe sensitivity to conditioning on quadratics, and demonstrate impact in a realistic setting where computational overhead truly matters.
