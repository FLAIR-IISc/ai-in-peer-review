This paper presents Cholesky-CMA-ES, a computationally efficient variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES). The main contribution is a reformulation of the algorithm's update steps to operate directly on the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This clever modification circumvents the need for the matrix square root operation, which is a primary computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES, thereby reducing the per-iteration complexity of updating the covariance matrix.

The strengths of this work are clear and significant:

1.  **Reduced Computational Complexity:** The proposed method successfully reduces the runtime complexity of the covariance matrix update from O(N^3) to O(N^2), where N is the dimensionality of the search space. The authors provide experimental evidence showing that this translates to a tangible reduction in wall-clock time, with the benefit becoming more pronounced as the dimensionality increases.

2.  **Performance Equivalence:** A key achievement of this work is that the computational speed-up is attained without negatively impacting the algorithm's search performance. The paper includes a sound theoretical justification and empirical results demonstrating that the Cholesky-CMA-ES requires a comparable number of objective function evaluations to converge as the standard CMA-ES.

3.  **Practical Value:** This paper offers a valuable and practical improvement to a widely used and powerful optimization algorithm. By addressing a known computational limitation, the authors make CMA-ES more accessible for higher-dimensional problems where per-iteration cost is a limiting factor.

While the contribution is valuable, the paper could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Elaboration on Numerical Stability:** The authors claim that the Cholesky-based computation is "numerically more stable." While this is generally true for Cholesky factorization compared to other matrix operations, a more explicit theoretical explanation within the context of CMA-ES would be beneficial. Could the authors elaborate on why the standard approach might suffer from numerical issues and how operating directly on the Cholesky factor mitigates these specific risks? A more detailed discussion would substantiate this claim and add depth to the analysis.

2.  **Scalability in High Dimensions:** The Cholesky-CMA-ES effectively reduces the wall-clock time per iteration. However, for many modern applications, such as training large neural networks in direct policy search, the dimensionality (N) can be extremely large. In such scenarios, even an O(N^2) complexity for computation and memory can be prohibitive, and the number of required function evaluations (which often scales with N) remains the primary challenge. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss the potential for combining their efficient update framework with methods specifically designed for high-dimensional settings. For example, could the Cholesky-based updates be integrated with low-dimensional approximation techniques, such as the one proposed by Loshchilov (2015)? Addressing this would position the work more broadly and highlight its relevance to practitioners facing very large-scale problems.

In summary, this is a well-executed paper that presents a solid and useful improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm. By replacing the costly matrix square root with an efficient Cholesky factor update, the authors have developed a faster variant without sacrificing performance. Addressing the questions regarding numerical stability and scalability in very high dimensions would further enhance the impact and completeness of this work.
