This manuscript introduces a novel update scheme for CMA-ES, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES. The proposed method aims to reduce the computational complexity of the covariance matrix adaptation from O(d³) to O(μd²), where d is the problem dimension and μ is the population size (typically O(log d)). The core idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix directly, instead of the full matrix and its decomposition. This approach differs from the standard CMA-ES by omitting the estimation of an orthogonal matrix E_t in each iteration. The authors provide an asymptotic argument, under restrictive assumptions about the convergence of the covariance matrix sequence, that E_t converges to the identity matrix, thereby establishing the equivalence of the two schemes in the limit. The paper demonstrates the speed-up and performance of Cholesky-CMA-ES through runtime and convergence plots on standard benchmark functions, comparing it against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that employ update delay strategies.

This paper presents a solid contribution towards accelerating the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm. CMA-ES is a leading method for non-convex black-box optimization, particularly when the objective function's topology is unknown and gradient information is unavailable or unreliable. The proposed method achieves a considerable speed-up without any apparent degradation in optimization performance on the presented synthetic benchmarks.

Despite the method's clear potential, there is a general concern for any proposal to speed up CMA-ES: in many practical applications, the overall runtime is dominated by the evaluation of the objective function (the black-box oracle). In such cases, the computational cost of the CMA-ES update step itself is often negligible. Furthermore, CMA-ES is typically applied to problems with dimensions in the tens. The runtime of the CMA-ES update only becomes a significant bottleneck—and thus the proposed speed-up becomes impactful—in scenarios with very fast function evaluations (e.g., on the order of milliseconds or less) and higher dimensionality. The practical relevance of this targeted scenario could be better motivated.

Given the difficulty of providing theoretical guarantees for a complex algorithm like CMA-ES, the paper's empirical evaluation could be strengthened with the following additions:

1.  **An analysis of the evolution of the orthogonal matrix E_t.** It would be instructive to track the deviation of E_t from the identity matrix (e.g., using a suitable matrix norm) over the course of optimization runs. This could be achieved by running the full CMA-ES while simultaneously calculating the Cholesky-CMA-ES approximation, providing direct empirical evidence for the paper's core assumption about the behavior of E_t.

2.  **A direct comparison with the standard CMA-ES.** For completeness, the empirical comparison should include the original CMA-ES without any update delay strategies as a baseline for both runtime and optimization performance.

3.  **A more systematic study on quadratic functions.** While the test functions used are standard, a more detailed analysis on quadratic objective functions would be valuable. Specifically, a study investigating how performance and the error in the E_t approximation vary as a function of the conditioning (i.e., the spectral distribution of the Hessian), perhaps by continuously varying the spectrum of the quadratic form (e.g., as in Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012).

4.  **A real-world application.** To complement the results on synthetic benchmarks, an experiment on a real-world problem would strengthen the paper. An application like policy optimization in real-time robotics, where evaluation times can be short and computational speed is critical, could be a compelling test case.
