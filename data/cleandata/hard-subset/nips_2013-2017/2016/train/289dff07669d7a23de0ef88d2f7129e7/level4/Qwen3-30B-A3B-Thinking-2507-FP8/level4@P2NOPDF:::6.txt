

The paper introduces a variant of CMA-ES termed "Cholesky-CMA-ES," claiming improved computational efficiency without altering algorithmic behavior. However, this name is misleading: Suttorp et al. (2009) first proposed a Cholesky-based CMA-ES variant, and the term is well-established in the literature. The authors fail to acknowledge this prior work, instead presenting their approach as novel under the same name. Algorithmically, the contribution is a minor modification to Krause et al. (2015): replacing a single rank-one update with a loop over all individuals (present in Krause’s work but applied differently in Plus strategies). The core novelty is limited to this loop, which is not sufficiently justified as a significant advancement.

Empirically, Figure 2(b) shows differing plateau values for CMA-ES-Ref and the proposed method on the Cigar function. This discrepancy likely stems from how median results are plotted, but the paper does not address this. Crucially, the authors omit comparisons with Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES (which they incorrectly claim is numerically unstable, contradicting Suttorp’s original stability analysis) and Krause’s variant. Figure 3’s exclusion of Suttorp’s method misrepresents the paper’s actual contribution. The claim of "optimal covariance update and storage complexity" is inaccurate: for separable functions (e.g., Sphere), the full covariance matrix is unnecessary, and diagonal approximations suffice. The proposed method only achieves a ~2× speedup over Suttorp’s approach (not "optimal"), while retaining quadratic complexity—meaning it does not advance large-scale optimization capabilities. The only valid claim is that it offers marginal efficiency gains when the full covariance matrix is required.

The paper’s algorithmic contribution is overly incremental. The key modification (the loop in Algorithm 1) is trivial and not novel, yet the authors present it as a substantial improvement. The "optimal" complexity claim is unsupported for non-degenerate cases, and the omission of critical baselines undermines the empirical evaluation. The authors should clarify whether the loop modification is indeed non-trivial or explain why prior work (Krause, Suttorp) did not adopt it.

**Required revisions:**  
(a) **Resolve naming conflict:** Explicitly acknowledge Suttorp et al. (2009) and clarify how the proposed variant differs from theirs (e.g., "We introduce a *modified* Cholesky-CMA-ES, distinct from Suttorp’s approach").  
(b) **Expand empirical comparison:** Reproduce Figures 2–4 with results for Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES, Krause’s variant, and the proposed method. If space constraints render the figures cluttered, present the most informative comparisons in the main text and include full results in Supplementary Material.  
(c) **Clarify algorithmic novelty:** In Algorithm 1 and the text, explicitly state that the primary change is the loop over individuals (not the rank-one update itself), and justify why this loop is necessary or advantageous over existing implementations. Avoid overstating novelty.

**Minor corrections:**  
- The statement "an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step" is inaccurate. Eigenvalue decomposition is typically done *periodically* (not in every step) in standard CMA-ES implementations to reduce computational cost. This should be corrected.

**Overall assessment:**  
The work is technically sound but lacks sufficient novelty. The naming confusion, omitted comparisons, and unsupported complexity claims weaken the contribution. While the efficiency gain for full-covariance scenarios is valid, it is not a breakthrough. The revisions above are essential to position the work accurately within the literature. Without them, the paper risks misleading readers about its significance. As presented, the incremental nature of the contribution does not meet NIPS’s standard for novelty. The suggested changes would strengthen the paper’s rigor and clarity, but the current version falls short of the expected contribution level.
