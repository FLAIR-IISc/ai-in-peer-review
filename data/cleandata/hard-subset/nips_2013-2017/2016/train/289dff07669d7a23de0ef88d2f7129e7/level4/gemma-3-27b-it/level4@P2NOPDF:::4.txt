This paper presents a valuable improvement to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm by incorporating a triangular Cholesky factorization. The primary contribution lies in eliminating the square root operation present in the standard CMA-ES inverse calculation, thereby reducing computational cost, particularly as dimensionality increases. The authors provide a theoretical justification demonstrating that this factorization does not negatively impact the algorithm’s performance, and experimental results confirm that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains comparable performance to standard CMA-ES in terms of the number of objective function evaluations, while exhibiting a reduction in runtime. 

The reduction in runtime complexity is a significant benefit, and the paper clearly articulates this advantage. However, the justification for the claimed numerical stability achieved through this factorization could be strengthened with further theoretical elaboration. While the paper states the computation is “numerically more stable,” a more detailed explanation of *why* this is the case would be beneficial.

Notably, the runtime advantage of Cholesky-CMA-ES grows with the dimensionality of the search space, which is particularly relevant to many modern applications. Given this, the authors should consider discussing potential synergies with low-dimensional approximation techniques, such as those proposed by Loschilov [2015]. Many practical problems, including direct policy search and neural network training, are characterized by high dimensionality, and addressing this challenge is critical for broader applicability. Exploring a combination of the Cholesky factorization with low-dimensional approximations could lead to further performance gains and increased scalability.

Overall, this work represents a worthwhile contribution to the CMA-ES literature. The technique is sound, well-justified, and demonstrates a clear practical advantage. Addressing the points raised above – a deeper theoretical explanation of numerical stability and a discussion of potential combinations with dimensionality reduction techniques – would further strengthen the paper.
