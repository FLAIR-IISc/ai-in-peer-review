This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) by replacing the standard eigendecomposition with a Cholesky decomposition. The stated goal is to reduce the computational and storage complexity of the algorithm.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The proposed approach is methodologically sound, and the empirical results are promising, demonstrating significant speedups over the standard CMA-ES implementation. Given these performance gains, the proposed method has the potential to become a widely adopted variant of CMA-ES.

However, despite its practical merits, the paper has several major weaknesses that prevent a recommendation for acceptance.

The primary weakness of this work is its limited novelty. The proposed method appears to be a direct extension of the algorithm presented in "A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies." The core update mechanisms seem functionally identical, which raises questions about the paper's distinct contribution over this prior work. I would encourage the authors to clarify the key differences and novel theoretical or algorithmic insights that distinguish their method from this predecessor.

Furthermore, several critical discussions and justifications are missing from the paper:

1.  **Equivalence to Standard CMA-ES:** The paper does not formally demonstrate that the proposed update rule is mathematically equivalent to the standard CMA-ES covariance matrix update (formula 2). While this may have been established in prior work, its inclusion is essential for the self-containment and theoretical soundness of this paper. A brief proof or a clear reference to one should be included.

2.  **Complexity Analysis:** The authors state that the computational complexity is O(μd²), which is asymptotically the same as the reference CMA-ES implementation. This O-notation hides the constant factors that are responsible for the observed speedup. To properly justify the performance claims, a more detailed complexity analysis is needed. For instance, using Θ-notation or providing a step-by-step operation count would clarify exactly where the computational savings originate and why the proposed algorithm is faster in practice.

3.  **Evolution Path Error:** The authors claim that the error in the evolution path is small and diminishes over time. This assertion appears to contradict previous analyses (e.g., by Hansen), which suggest that such errors can accumulate. The paper's theoretical justification for this claim is not definitive. Therefore, empirical validation is crucial. Simply comparing the final optimization performance is insufficient. The authors should present experiments that specifically track the evolution of this error over generations to substantiate their claim.

**Minor Comments:**

*   There are several notational inconsistencies that should be corrected. For example, in Algorithm 2, `wk` should be `αk`, and in Table 1, `n` should be `d`.
*   There is a typo in line 185, where 'F' should be 'f'.

In summary, while the paper presents a computationally efficient and promising variant of CMA-ES, its contribution is undermined by a significant lack of perceived novelty and the absence of critical theoretical and empirical justifications. The issues regarding the algorithm's equivalence, a detailed complexity analysis, and the unsubstantiated claim about path error are major concerns. For these reasons, I cannot recommend acceptance of the paper in its current form.
