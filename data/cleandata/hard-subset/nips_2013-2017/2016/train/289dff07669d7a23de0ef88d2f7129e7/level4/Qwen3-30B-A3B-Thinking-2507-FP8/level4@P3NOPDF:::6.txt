

The paper introduces a modification to CMA-ES termed "Cholesky-CMA-ES," claiming reduced computational cost without altering algorithmic behavior. However, this naming is problematic: the term was first established by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a related method, and the authors fail to acknowledge this prior work. Instead, they present their variant as novel under this name, creating confusion. The algorithmic contribution is marginal: it adapts Krause et al. (2015) by replacing a single rankOneUpdate call with a loop over all individuals (Algorithm 1). This is not a new insight but a straightforward implementation detail—effectively, the sole change is adding a loop, which the authors do not adequately justify or contextualize.  

Empirically, the paper’s claims are undermined by incomplete comparisons. Figure 2b shows inconsistent plateaus on the Cigar function between CMA-ES-Ref and the proposed method, likely due to differences in median calculation (e.g., sampling variance or aggregation method). Crucially, the authors omit comparison to Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES—a critical omission, as Suttorp’s method already achieves similar storage complexity. The paper’s assertion that their approach is "optimal" is misleading: on separable functions (e.g., Sphere), the covariance matrix is unnecessary, rendering the proposed update irrelevant. Even for non-separable problems, the claimed 2× speedup over Suttorp’s method is questionable; Suttorp explicitly addresses numerical stability, contradicting the paper’s implication that their variant avoids this issue. Figure 3 further misrepresents the contribution by excluding Suttorp’s variant, creating a false impression of novelty.  

The paper’s core claim—that this modification resolves "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"—is overstated. While the approach reduces memory usage by a constant factor (from O(n²) to O(n²/2)), the complexity remains quadratic, offering no advantage for large-scale optimization (where linear methods are standard). The "optimal" label is inappropriate, as simpler alternatives exist for separable cases (e.g., diagonal covariance updates). The authors’ focus on storage efficiency neglects that CMA-ES’s primary bottleneck is computational cost, not memory.  

The paper’s contribution is thus highly incremental. The "for" loop in Algorithm 1 is a minor implementation tweak, not a substantive advancement. The authors do not clarify why this loop is necessary or why prior work (Krause et al.) did not adopt it, nor do they explain how this differs from Suttorp’s approach. This lack of distinction weakens the paper’s novelty.  

**Recommendations for revision:**  
1. **Resolve naming conflict:** Explicitly acknowledge Suttorp et al. (2009) and clarify how the proposed method differs from their Cholesky-CMA-ES. Avoid using the same name without qualification.  
2. **Expand empirical comparison:** Re-run experiments to include Suttorp’s variant and Krause’s method (not just CMA-ES-Ref). Update Figures 2 and 3 to show all three variants; if space is constrained, present key comparisons in the main text and full results in supplementary material.  
3. **Clarify algorithmic contribution:** In Algorithm 1 and the text, explicitly state that the only change is the loop over individuals. Justify why this loop is necessary (e.g., "to maintain consistency with rank-μ updates") and address why Krause et al. did not implement it. If the loop is redundant, admit this.  
4. **Correct misleading claims:**  
   - Remove "optimal" from the title/abstract; replace with "reduced-storage" or similar.  
   - Clarify that storage complexity is reduced by a constant factor (not "optimal").  
   - Note that numerical stability concerns are unfounded for Suttorp’s method (per their original work).  
5. **Minor corrections:**  
   - The statement "an eigenvalue decomposition is performed in each update step" is inaccurate; it is typically done periodically (e.g., every 10–20 iterations) in standard implementations.  

**Overall assessment:** The paper presents a minor technical adjustment with insufficient novelty. The naming conflict, omitted comparisons, and overstated claims significantly detract from its contribution. While the approach may offer modest practical benefits for specific use cases (e.g., when full covariance is required), the paper fails to demonstrate this convincingly or distinguish itself from prior work. With the suggested revisions—particularly resolving the naming issue and adding comparative experiments—the paper could be strengthened. As written, it lacks sufficient novelty to warrant publication in NIPS.  

**Summary of key concerns:**  
- **Novelty:** Algorithmic contribution is trivial (a single loop); prior work (Suttorp, Krause) is not properly contextualized.  
- **Empirical rigor:** Missing comparisons to Suttorp’s method; inconsistent results in Figures 2b/3.  
- **Technical accuracy:** "Optimal" complexity claim is incorrect; numerical stability misrepresentation.  
- **Clarity:** Ambiguous algorithmic justification; misleading title.  

The authors must address these issues to position their work meaningfully within the CMA-ES literature. Without revisions, the paper’s incremental nature and unresolved conflicts with prior art render it unsuitable for publication.
