This paper presents a well-motivated and carefully evaluated variant of CMA-ES that replaces the explicit covariance update and decomposition with updates on a triangular Cholesky factor. CMA-ES is a leading derivative-free optimization method that adapts a multivariate normal search distribution; its standard implementation is dominated by storing and updating the full covariance matrix and computing its square root. The authors propose to maintain only a triangular Cholesky factor and to modify the cumulative step-size adaptation by using the inverse of this factor instead of the inverse square root of the covariance. This yields a numerically stable, quadratic-time update with minimal memory footprint. Empirically, the method matches the standard CMA-ES in the number of function evaluations required to hit a target value, while significantly reducing wall-clock time, especially in higher dimensions.

Strengths and significance:
- The contribution addresses a core bottleneck of CMA-ES—time and space complexity of full covariance adaptation—without sacrificing optimization performance. Given CMA-ES’s widespread use in machine learning (e.g., reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning), this is a highly practical and impactful improvement.
- The approach is theoretically motivated. By analyzing how the triangular factor relates to the square root via RQ/orthogonal decompositions, the paper argues that the modification to CSA introduces only a small, vanishing error under mild conditions. This is a clear advance over previous non-triangular Cholesky updates that lack a one-to-one mapping and can induce instability.
- The algorithm achieves O(µ d^2) time per iteration (with typical µ = O(log d)) and optimal storage, while also providing eigenvalues “for free” from the diagonal of the triangular factor. Efficient inversion of the triangular factor in quadratic time is another practical benefit.
- The experimental evaluation is thorough and relevant. Comparisons include a reference CMA-ES with delayed decompositions, a delayed-update baseline (CMA-ES/d), and a non-triangular Cholesky update (Suttorp-CMA-ES). Tests on standard rotated benchmarks show equal evaluation efficiency but substantial runtime gains that grow with dimension.

Quality and clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The algorithmic description is precise, and the reasoning behind the CSA approximation and its error behavior is explained convincingly.
- The empirical results are presented in a way that facilitates fair comparison, with appropriate baselines and dimensional scaling.

Originality:
- Building on prior work that updates Cholesky factors (e.g., Suttorp et al., Krause and Igel), this paper’s key novelty is to make triangular-factor updates compatible with standard CMA-ES, including a principled modification of CSA. The analysis of the induced rotation and its vanishing effect is original and important.

Potential weaknesses and questions:
- The approximation in CSA is not accompanied by formal bounds on evaluation overhead relative to the standard method; the support is empirical and asymptotic. The paper argues persuasively, but a brief discussion of any known corner cases would be useful.
- In the experiments, Rosenbrock trials that did not converge to the global optimum were excluded. Please clarify how often this occurs and whether exclusion affects relative comparisons.

Pros:
- Significant runtime reduction with full covariance adaptation.
- Optimal storage and fast access to eigenvalues.
- Numerically stable, quadratic-time inversion of the factor.
- No increase in function evaluations across benchmarks.

Cons:
- CSA approximation lacks non-asymptotic guarantees; discussion could be expanded.
- Some experimental details (hardware and implementation comparability) should be clarified.

Minor suggestions:
- Please explain how you ensured comparability across implementations: same programming language, compiler and flags, BLAS/LAPACK version, and identical hardware? A short subsection on the experimental setup (including CPU model, cache sizes, threading, and whether all methods share the same linear algebra backend) would strengthen the runtime claims.
- In discussing Figure 2, the text states “the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences.” This seems less evident for Cigar and Discus at dimension 256. An explicit explanation would help. If this is due to cache effects (e.g., matrices exceeding L2 cache), please state this clearly in the caption or main text and, if possible, quantify the impact.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong and practically important paper. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES retains the optimization behavior of standard CMA-ES while achieving substantial reductions in runtime and memory, and it does so with a clear theoretical rationale. I expect this variant to become a default choice for medium- to large-scale settings where full covariance adaptation remains feasible. I recommend acceptance.
