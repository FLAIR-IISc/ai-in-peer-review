CMA-ES is a leading stochastic, derivative-free optimization algorithm. It adapts the parameters of a multivariate normal sampling distribution—effectively learning an underlying metric—through a combination of sophisticated learning mechanisms. This paper presents a novel covariance matrix update strategy based on triangular Cholesky factors. A key advantage of this approach is a substantial reduction in computational complexity and memory requirements compared to traditional CMA-ES update schemes. 

The proposed method is rigorously evaluated through numerical experimentation. The results demonstrate that this new update maintains the performance of standard CMA-ES, achieving comparable function evaluation counts to reach a target objective value, while simultaneously and significantly reducing wall-clock time, particularly in high-dimensional problem spaces.

I believe this work represents a significant contribution to the field of optimization. This new variant of CMA-ES is likely to become a default choice for medium- to large-scale problems due to its clear advantages: lower memory footprint, reduced computational complexity, and the readily available eigenvalues of the covariance matrix as a byproduct of the Cholesky factorization. The paper is well-written and the evaluation is thorough. It is appropriate to showcase this work at NIPS, given CMA-ES’s strong connections to machine learning, information geometry, and its applications in areas such as reinforcement learning and supervised learning.

I have a few minor suggestions for improvement. First, it would be beneficial to clarify how the comparability of different implementations was ensured, specifically regarding the underlying hardware and software (implementation language, compiler versions, etc.). Ensuring equivalent CPU environments strengthens the validity of the performance comparisons. Second, the statement accompanying Figure 2, “the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences,” appears inconsistent for the Cigar and Discus functions at dimension 256. Providing an explanation for this observation would be valuable.
