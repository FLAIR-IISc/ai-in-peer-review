This paper presents a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce the computational complexity of the covariance matrix update. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a complexity of O(d³) per iteration due to the eigendecomposition required for the update, which can be a bottleneck for high-dimensional problems (large `d`). The authors propose a method, which they name Cholesky-CMA-ES, that directly updates the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This reduces the time complexity of the update to O(µd²) and the storage complexity to O(d²). A key challenge is that the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism in the standard CMA-ES requires the inverse of the matrix square root (C⁻¹/²), which is not directly available from the Cholesky factor. The authors address this by using the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (A⁻¹) as an approximation. They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) arguing that as the covariance matrix shape converges, the error introduced by this approximation vanishes. Empirical results on several benchmark functions demonstrate that the proposed method achieves a significant reduction in wall-clock time compared to a reference CMA-ES implementation, without a discernible loss in performance regarding the number of function evaluations.

**Strengths**

*   The paper addresses a well-known and important practical limitation of the CMA-ES algorithm: its high computational cost in high dimensions.
*   The proposed modification is sensible and leads to a clear and significant improvement in runtime, as demonstrated by the experiments. For problems where adapting a full covariance matrix is necessary, this method is likely a state-of-the-art approach in terms of computational efficiency.
*   The theoretical argument in Section 3, which justifies the approximation of C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹, is a valuable contribution. It provides a sound rationale for why the modification should not degrade the algorithm's search behavior.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Novelty and Relation to Prior Work**: The work appears to be an incremental extension of previous research, particularly Krause and Igel (2015), which introduced a similar rank-one update for the Cholesky factor in the context of (1+1)-CMA-ES. The main algorithmic contribution of this paper seems to be extending this update to the more general (µ, λ)-CMA-ES by applying the `rankOneUpdate` function within a `for` loop (Algorithm 1). The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could more clearly articulate their specific contributions beyond this extension.

2.  **Algorithm Naming and Potential for Confusion**: The name "Cholesky-CMA-ES" was previously introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a related algorithm that also updates a Cholesky factor (albeit a non-triangular one). Using the same name for a different variant is likely to cause confusion in the literature. The authors should acknowledge this and consider renaming their algorithm to better distinguish it from the Suttorp et al. version.

3.  **Missing Experimental Baselines**: The empirical evaluation is incomplete because it omits a direct comparison with the Suttorp et al. (2009) method. The Suttorp et al. approach also achieves a quadratic-time update and is a highly relevant baseline. The authors claim that this prior method is "prone to numerical instabilities," but this contradicts the claims in the original paper. This assertion should be supported by evidence or a more detailed discussion. Without this comparison, it is difficult to accurately assess the practical advantage of the proposed triangular factorization over the non-triangular one.

4.  **Scope of Claims**:
    *   The claim of "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" should be qualified. While the complexity is optimal *for an algorithm that maintains a full d x d covariance matrix*, it is not optimal in a broader sense. On simpler problems (e.g., separable or spherical functions), specialized CMA-ES variants that adapt only a diagonal matrix or a single step-size have much lower complexity (e.g., O(d)).
    *   The paper suggests the method is suitable for "large-scale optimization." However, the O(d²) complexity is still prohibitive for truly large-scale problems (e.g., d > 1000), where methods with linear or sub-quadratic complexity are generally required. The significance of the work for large-scale optimization should be stated more cautiously.

5.  **Clarity on Experimental Results**: In Figure 1b (Cigar function), the performance curves for CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES appear to plateau at different objective function values. The authors should clarify whether this indicates a meaningful difference in convergence or if it is merely an artifact of plotting median results.

**Minor Points**

*   The introduction states that "typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step." In most efficient implementations of CMA-ES, this expensive operation is performed periodically (e.g., every d/10 iterations), not in every single step. The description should be corrected for accuracy.

**Conclusion**

The paper presents a practically useful and well-engineered improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm that significantly reduces its computational overhead. The theoretical justification for the proposed approximation is sound. However, the contribution appears incremental when viewed in the context of prior work by Krause & Igel (2015) and Suttorp et al. (2009). The evaluation is weakened by the omission of a key baseline, and some of the claims regarding optimality and large-scale applicability are overstated. To be a stronger contribution, the paper should address the naming conflict, include a comparison with the Suttorp et al. method, and more clearly position its novelty with respect to existing literature.
