The manuscript introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel approach to updating the covariance matrix in the CMA-ES algorithm. This method achieves a computational complexity of O(\mu d^2), where d is the problem dimension and \mu is the sample size, representing a significant improvement over the original CMA-ES’s O(d^3) complexity. The core idea involves maintaining and updating a Cholesky decomposition of the covariance matrix, bypassing the need for a full covariance matrix and its square root.  A key difference from standard CMA-ES lies in the omission of estimating a random orthogonal matrix, Et, at each iteration. The authors provide an asymptotic argument, based on assumptions regarding the convergence of covariance matrix sequences, demonstrating that Et converges to the identity matrix, thereby justifying the approximation. The efficacy and speedup of Cholesky-CMA-ES are demonstrated through standard runtime and convergence analyses on benchmark functions, alongside comparisons to two current state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants employing covariance update delays. 

This work represents a valuable contribution to accelerating the widely-regarded CMA-ES algorithm, which excels in non-convex, black-box optimization scenarios where objective function topology is unknown and derivative information is unavailable. The proposed method demonstrably speeds up the algorithm without compromising optimization performance on the tested synthetic problems. However, a common challenge for CMA-ES speed-up techniques is that the overall optimization time is frequently dominated by the cost of evaluating the black-box objective function itself, rendering the CMA-ES runtime negligible in many practical applications. Furthermore, CMA-ES is typically applied to problems with dimensionality on the order of tens of variables. The benefits of this work are most pronounced when function evaluations are extremely fast (milliseconds or less) and the dimensionality is high – a scenario which, while important, may be less common in practice. 

Given the theoretical complexities inherent in analyzing CMA-ES, several additional empirical investigations would strengthen the manuscript. First, visualizing the evolution of Et along optimization trajectories (e.g., using different norms) – by running both the full CMA-ES and the Cholesky approximation in parallel – would provide further insights into the behavior of Et over time. Second, including runtime and performance data for standard CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies would provide a more complete comparison. Third, on quadratic functions, a simulation study varying the spectrum of quadratic forms (following, for example, Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012) and examining solution quality and error in Et as a function of spectral distribution would be beneficial. Finally, including a real-world application, such as policy gradients in real-time robotics where computational speed is critical and the parameter space is relatively constrained, would further validate the method’s practical impact.
