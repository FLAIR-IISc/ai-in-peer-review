The manuscript describes a novel CMA-ES update scheme, the Cholesky-CMA-ES, that approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation (with complexity O(d<sup>3</sup>)) with a scheme running in O(µd<sup>2</sup>), where d is the optimization problem dimension and µ the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix rather than a full covariance matrix (and its square root). The difference between the Cholesky and original CMA-ES arises from not explicitly estimating an orthogonal matrix E<sub>t</sub> in each step. Under relatively strong assumptions – specifically, assumptions about the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to a target covariance matrix – the authors provide an asymptotic argument that the sequence of E<sub>t</sub> will converge to the identity matrix, implying equivalence between the two schemes. The validity and speed-up of the Cholesky-CMA-ES are demonstrated using standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark functions, and comparisons to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives employing delayed covariance matrix updates.

This paper makes a solid contribution to accelerating the well-regarded CMA-ES algorithm, which is arguably the best option for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the objective function’s topology and derivative information is unavailable or unhelpful. The proposed method significantly speeds up the algorithm without apparent deterioration in optimization performance on the synthetic test cases. However, a common limitation of speed-up proposals for CMA-ES is that, in practice, the overall optimization process is often dominated by the runtime of the black-box oracle (the objective function). Frequently, the actual runtime of CMA-ES is negligible compared to this. Furthermore, in typical CMA-ES applications, problem dimensionality rarely exceeds a few tens of variables. The speed-up is most impactful when a single function evaluation takes milliseconds or less and the dimensionality is high – a scenario targeted here, though perhaps less common in practice.

Given the theoretical challenges in providing guarantees for a complex scheme like CMA-ES, I suggest a few additional empirical experiments and plots to strengthen the presentation. First, visualizing the evolution of E<sub>t</sub> along selected optimization trajectories – perhaps in different norms – would be insightful. This could be achieved by simultaneously running the full CMA-ES and its approximation, and comparing the resulting E<sub>t</sub> values. This would offer further empirical evidence regarding the magnitude of E<sub>t</sub>’s fluctuations over time. Second, including runtime and performance results for the standard CMA-ES *without* delay strategies would provide a more complete comparison. Third, while the used test functions are standard in the evolutionary optimization community, a simulation study on quadratic functions varying continuously in their spectral distribution (as in Stich and Müller, PPSN 2012) could be valuable. This would allow for comparison of solution quality and error in E<sub>t</sub> as a function of the spectral distribution. Finally, a real-world experiment alongside the simulation results would be beneficial. For instance, policy gradients in real-time robotics applications, where speed is critical and parameter spaces are not excessively large, might be a suitable choice.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant speedup:** The proposed method demonstrably accelerates the CMA-ES algorithm, especially in higher dimensions.
*   **Theoretical justification:** The authors provide a theoretical argument (albeit under assumptions) for why the approximation should not negatively impact performance.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The experiments show that the Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains comparable performance to the standard CMA-ES on benchmark functions.
*   **Practical relevance:** While the runtime of the black-box function often dominates, speeding up CMA-ES is still valuable, particularly for large-scale problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Strong assumptions:** The theoretical justification relies on assumptions about the convergence of covariance matrices.
*   **Limited real-world validation:** The experiments are primarily conducted on synthetic benchmark functions.
*   **Oracle runtime dominance:** The speedup may be less impactful in scenarios where the objective function evaluation is computationally expensive.
