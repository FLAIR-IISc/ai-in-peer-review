This manuscript introduces the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel update scheme that approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation. The proposed method reduces the complexity from O(d³) for the original CMA-ES to O(μd²), where d is the problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The principal idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix directly, rather than the full covariance matrix and its square root. The difference between Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original algorithm arises from omitting the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix, E_t, at each iteration. Under restrictive assumptions—namely, the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to a target matrix—the authors provide an asymptotic argument that the sequence of E_t matrices converges to the identity matrix, at which point the two schemes become equivalent. The paper demonstrates the validity and speed-up of Cholesky-CMA-ES using standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark functions, comparing it against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that use different strategies to delay the full covariance matrix update.

This paper makes a solid contribution to speeding up the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm. CMA-ES is arguably the leading algorithm for non-convex black-box optimization, particularly when the objective function's topology is unknown and gradient information is unavailable or unhelpful. The proposed method accelerates the algorithm considerably while showing no deterioration in optimization performance on the synthetic test cases presented.

Despite the general importance of this work, a potential weakness of any proposal to speed up CMA-ES is that, in practice, the overall optimization time is typically dominated by the evaluation of the black-box objective function. Consequently, the runtime of the CMA-ES algorithm itself is often negligible in comparison. Furthermore, in typical applications, the dimensionality of problems solved with CMA-ES rarely exceeds a few tens of variables. The runtime of CMA-ES only becomes a significant factor in scenarios with very fast function evaluations (on the order of milliseconds or less) and higher dimensionality. This appears to be the target scenario for this work, though its practical relevance could be debated.

As theoretical guarantees are notoriously difficult to obtain for a complex scheme such as CMA-ES, I would like to propose several empirical experiments that could strengthen the presentation of the results:

1.  It would be instructive to visualize the evolution of the orthogonal matrix E_t over selected optimization trajectories, perhaps by plotting one of its norms. I envision an experiment where the full CMA-ES is run, while the Cholesky approximation is calculated in parallel. This would provide direct empirical evidence of the fluctuations of E_t over time and better characterize the nature of the approximation.

2.  For completeness, it would be valuable to include baseline results showing the actual runtimes and performance of the standard CMA-ES without any delay strategies.

3.  Although the test functions used are standard in the evolutionary optimization community, I suggest a more systematic simulation study on quadratic functions. Specifically, the analysis could be strengthened by continuously varying the spectrum of the quadratic form (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012) and evaluating the solution quality and the error in E_t as a function of the spectral distribution.

4.  Finally, a real-world experiment would be a compelling addition to complement the current simulation results. For instance, policy gradient methods in real-time robotics applications might be a suitable test case, as optimization speed is often critical and the parameter space is of moderate size.
