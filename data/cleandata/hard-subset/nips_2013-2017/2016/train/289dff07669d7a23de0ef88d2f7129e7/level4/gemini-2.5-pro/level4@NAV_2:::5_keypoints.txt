- Introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES that maintains a lower-triangular Cholesky factor to avoid O(d^3) matrix square root computations, yielding O(µ d^2) updates.  
- Key approximation: replace the inverse matrix square root in CSA with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor.  
- Theoretical claim: if normalized covariance matrices converge, the orthogonal component Et converges, making the approximation asymptotically exact.  
- Empirical finding: substantial wall-clock speedups over state-of-the-art CMA-ES with delayed updates, without worsening function-evaluation efficiency.  
- Strength (quality): technically sound derivation and clear Cholesky update; runtime claims supported by experiments.  
- Weakness (quality): theoretical justification depends on a strong convergence assumption that may not hold in practice; practical implications underexplored.  
- Clarity: well-written and organized with clear motivation, algorithm description, and relation to prior Cholesky-CMA-ES work.  
- Originality: novel, theoretically motivated way to handle CSA with Cholesky updates, addressing a known obstacle.  
- Significance: can enable CMA-ES for higher dimensions or very fast-evaluation problems, though such scenarios may be uncommon.  
- Argument for acceptance: addresses a major computational bottleneck, is technically sound, achieves asymptotically optimal runtime, and shows empirical speedups.  
- Argument against acceptance: limited immediate practical impact for typical expensive-evaluation tasks; reliance on asymptotic/convergence assumptions; experiments could be broader.  
- Suggested improvement: empirically validate Et convergence by tracking Et or Et–E_{t-1} using full CMA-ES vs. the approximation.  
- Suggested improvement: add baseline comparisons to standard CMA-ES without delayed updates for a clearer runtime upper bound.  
- Suggested improvement: evaluate on more challenging benchmarks (e.g., quadratics with varying Hessian spectra) to test robustness to conditioning.  
- Suggested improvement: include a real-world fast-to-evaluate application (e.g., robotics policy search) to demonstrate practical relevance.
