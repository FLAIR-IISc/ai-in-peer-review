

This paper proposes replacing the eigen-decomposition with a Cholesky decomposition to reduce the time and storage complexity of the CMA-ES algorithm. The authors present Cholesky-CMA-ES, which maintains triangular Cholesky factors instead of the covariance matrix itself, achieving optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity for full covariance matrix adaptation. This builds on previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who explored Cholesky-based updates for specific CMA-ES variants, but extends it to the standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification for maintaining algorithmic performance.

Strengths: The paper is clearly written with well-organized exposition. The theoretical justification for the convergence of the modified algorithm is sound, and the empirical results demonstrate significant speedups (up to 20x for d=64) without increasing the number of function evaluations needed to reach the same objective value. The authors provide a thorough comparison against CMA-ES-Ref (with delayed decomposition), CMA-ES/d (delayed update for d steps), and Suttorp-CMA-ES. The paper also highlights important practical advantages, such as numerically stable inversion of the Cholesky factor in quadratic time and free eigenvalue computation.

Weaknesses: The originality of the contribution is limited. The core method appears to be a straightforward extension of Krause and Igel (2015), who already proposed an efficient rank-one update for the Cholesky factor. The paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from this prior work, which is particularly concerning given the NIPS emphasis on significant novelty. The theoretical claim that the error in the path decreases over time (contrary to Hansen's perspective) is not sufficiently validated with experiments—comparing only final performance is insufficient; the authors should present how the error evolves during optimization. Additionally, the complexity claim of O(μd²) is identical to CMA-ES-Ref, and the authors do not provide a detailed Θ analysis explaining why their implementation is faster in practice.

The paper also contains several important omissions: it does not explicitly show the equivalence of their algorithm to the standard CMA-ES update formula (2), despite this being essential for understanding the method's validity. The notation contains errors that need correction (e.g., wk in Algorithm 2 should be αk, n in Table 1 should be d), and there's a typographical error where 'F' should be 'f' in line 185.

Pro acceptance: The method is technically sound, the results are promising for practical applications, and the approach could become a new standard for CMA-ES in large-scale optimization problems where covariance matrix updates matter.

Con acceptance: The originality is insufficiently demonstrated, the theoretical claims require stronger empirical validation, and the paper does not adequately differentiate itself from prior work, particularly Krause and Igel (2015).

Given the conference's emphasis on significant novelty, the current version does not meet NIPS standards. The authors should significantly strengthen the originality argument by clearly differentiating their contribution from prior work, provide a detailed Θ analysis of the speedup, and conduct experiments specifically validating the error convergence claim. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
