This paper’s main contribution is to apply a triangular Cholesky factorization to CMA-ES, thereby avoiding explicit matrix square roots in the standard algorithm and reducing the cost associated with matrix inversions. The authors provide a theoretical justification indicating that the modification does not degrade optimization performance. Empirically, the method lowers runtime complexity without a significant change in the number of objective function evaluations. Overall, this is a valuable and practical implementation of Cholesky factorization within CMA-ES.

I have several requests for clarification and suggestions:

- Numerical stability: The paper claims the proposed computation is “numerically more stable.” Please provide a clearer theoretical explanation of this claim. In particular, under what assumptions (e.g., conditioning of the covariance matrix or typical update magnitudes) and in what precise sense (e.g., bounds on round-off error accumulation, reduced sensitivity to perturbations) does the Cholesky-based scheme achieve greater stability than the standard approach?

- Objective evaluations vs. wall-clock time: It appears that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires roughly the same number of objective function evaluations as standard CMA-ES, while significantly reducing wall-clock time, with the advantage growing in higher dimensions. Please confirm this interpretation and, if possible, quantify how the wall-clock time savings scale with dimensionality and population size.

- High-dimensional settings: In many practical applications—such as training neural networks in direct policy search—high dimensionality is a critical concern. Could the authors consider combining their framework with a low-dimensional (e.g., limited-memory) approximation as in Loshchilov (2015)? Such a hybrid could potentially retain the runtime benefits of the Cholesky-based updates while further improving scalability in very high-dimensional search spaces.

In summary, the contribution is well-motivated and the implementation appears effective. A more rigorous treatment of the numerical stability claim and a discussion of integration with low-dimensional approximations would strengthen the paper and broaden its applicability to large-scale problems.
