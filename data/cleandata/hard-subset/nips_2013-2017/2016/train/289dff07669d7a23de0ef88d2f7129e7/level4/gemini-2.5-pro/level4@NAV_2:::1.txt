This paper presents a significant algorithmic improvement to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art algorithm for derivative-free optimization widely used in machine learning for tasks like direct policy search and hyperparameter tuning. The core contribution addresses the primary computational bottleneck of CMA-ES: the complexity of updating and storing the covariance matrix, which scales poorly with the problem dimension, d. The authors propose the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant that maintains and directly updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This approach achieves an asymptotically optimal time complexity of O(µd^2) and optimal storage complexity. A key insight is the modification of the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, replacing the computationally expensive inverse matrix square root with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor. The paper provides a solid theoretical argument that this approximation does not degrade performance, as the rotational error it introduces diminishes while the algorithm converges. The work builds upon prior research aimed at reducing CMA-ES complexity but offers a more robust solution that preserves the full covariance matrix, which is essential for non-separable and ill-conditioned problems.

The paper has numerous strengths and makes a valuable contribution.
*   **Significance and Impact:** The proposed method is a highly practical advancement. By significantly reducing the wall-clock time and memory footprint without compromising the number of function evaluations, the Cholesky-CMA-ES is poised to become the new standard implementation for medium- to large-scale problems. This makes the power of CMA-ES accessible to a broader range of applications within machine learning and beyond. The added benefit of obtaining the covariance matrix eigenvalues "for free" is also a notable advantage for monitoring the optimization process.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and rigorously presented. The theoretical justification for the CSA approximation is convincing. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing the proposed algorithm against relevant baselines across a suite of standard benchmark functions. The results compellingly demonstrate a substantial speed-up that increases with dimensionality, confirming the theoretical complexity analysis.
*   **Clarity and Originality:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. It adeptly motivates the problem and presents its solution in a precise manner. While the idea of using Cholesky updates has been explored before, this work's novelty lies in its successful and theoretically justified integration into the standard CMA-ES framework, particularly in its handling of the CSA mechanism.

The paper is excellent, but two minor points could be addressed to further strengthen the manuscript:
*   To ensure the fairness of the wall-clock time comparisons, it would be helpful to explicitly state that the different implementations (e.g., CMA-ES-Ref, Suttorp-CMA-ES) are comparable in terms of their underlying codebases (e.g., same programming language, use of optimized libraries like LAPACK throughout). This would solidify the conclusion that the observed speed-ups stem from the algorithmic innovation rather than implementation differences.
*   There is a slight incongruity between the experimental results and their description. The text claims that for runtime, "the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences." However, in Figure 2, the performance gap for the Cigar and Discus functions appears to lessen at d=256. A brief explanation for this observation, perhaps referencing the hardware cache effects the authors mention, would add nuance to the analysis.

In summary, this is a very strong paper presenting a significant and practical contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a clear improvement over the current standard, and its adoption could have a considerable impact on practitioners. The work is of high quality, original, and clearly communicated.
