This paper presents the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce the computational complexity associated with updating and storing the full covariance matrix. The standard CMA-ES has a complexity of at least O(d³) per iteration due to the eigenvalue decomposition required for the matrix square root. The authors propose maintaining and updating a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix directly. This reduces the time complexity for the update to O(µd²) and the storage complexity to O(d²), which is asymptotically optimal.

A key challenge in this approach is that the standard Cumulative Step-size Adaptation (CSA) mechanism in CMA-ES relies on the inverse of the matrix square root (C⁻¹/²), which cannot be replaced one-to-one by the inverse of a Cholesky factor (A⁻¹) without introducing rotational artifacts. The core contribution of this paper is to propose such a replacement and provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) for why this approximation is sound. The authors argue that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the sequence of rotation matrices relating the square root and the Cholesky factor also converges, making the error introduced by the approximation negligible over time. The paper validates this approach empirically on several benchmark optimization problems, demonstrating a significant reduction in wall-clock time compared to standard and other efficient CMA-ES variants, without a corresponding loss in optimization performance (i.e., the number of function evaluations remains the same).

This work builds upon a line of research aimed at improving the scalability of CMA-ES. Previous work has explored delaying matrix updates, restricting the covariance matrix structure (e.g., to diagonal), or updating non-triangular Cholesky factors. The proposed method is most closely related to Krause and Igel (2015), who showed how to perform a rank-1 update on a triangular Cholesky factor. This paper extends that work to handle the full rank-µ update of the standard CMA-ES by decomposing it into a sequence of rank-1 updates.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Practical Impact:** The paper addresses a well-known and significant bottleneck in CMA-ES, a widely used and powerful optimization algorithm in machine learning and other fields. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES provides a practical, "drop-in" replacement for the standard algorithm that yields substantial speedups, especially for high-dimensional problems where the objective function is relatively cheap to evaluate. This significantly extends the applicability of full-covariance CMA-ES.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed algorithm is technically sound. The use of sequential rank-1 updates for the Cholesky factor is elegant, and the method is numerically stable. The additional benefits, such as obtaining the matrix eigenvalues "for free" from the diagonal of the Cholesky factor and the ability to efficiently compute the inverse, are valuable practical advantages.

3.  **Theoretical Justification:** The analysis provided for approximating the CSA update is a key contribution. While relying on the assumption of convergence for the covariance matrix shape, Lemma 1 provides a clear and intuitive rationale for why replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ does not degrade the algorithm's behavior. This justification is crucial for trusting the approximation.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. The comparison against a reference implementation, a delayed-update variant, and the non-triangular Cholesky approach by Suttorp et al. (2009) clearly shows that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the best wall-clock performance without sacrificing convergence speed in terms of function evaluations.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality:** The primary weakness is the incremental nature of the contribution. The core idea of updating a triangular Cholesky factor via a rank-1 update was previously established by Krause and Igel (2015). The extension to the rank-µ update by applying a sequence of rank-1 updates is a straightforward and logical generalization. The main novelty therefore lies in the analysis of the CSA mechanism and the empirical demonstration of its effectiveness in the full CMA-ES context. The authors could strengthen the paper by more explicitly positioning their work relative to Krause and Igel (2015) and highlighting why this generalization is non-trivial.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

1.  **Strengthening Lemma 1:** The assumption in Lemma 1 that the normalized covariance matrix converges is central to the theoretical argument. The authors correctly state that this cannot be guaranteed for a randomized algorithm. However, to bolster confidence in this assumption, they could cite existing theoretical work that supports it. For example, Beyer (ECJ, 2014) provides convergence analysis for simplified evolution strategies, showing that the expectation of the covariance matrix converges. Mentioning such related theoretical results would strengthen the justification.

2.  **Minor Corrections:** The manuscript is clearly written but contains a few minor typos that should be corrected:
    *   In Equation (1) and the corresponding footnote 1 (incorrectly labeled as 2 on page 3), the step-size in the denominator should be σₜ, not σ.
    *   On page 3, line 94, in the phrase "which can by be motivated by principles from...", the word "by" should be deleted.

In summary, this is a well-executed paper that presents a significant practical improvement to a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES is faster, more memory-efficient, and numerically stable. While the conceptual leap from prior work is arguably incremental, the careful analysis of the CSA approximation and the substantial, empirically-verified performance gains make this a valuable and useful contribution to the field. The work is of high quality and significance and is likely to be adopted by practitioners using CMA-ES.
