Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T instead of working directly with C and its matrix square root. The key change is to replace the C^{-1/2} term in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) by A^{-1}, together with a theoretical argument (via an RQ/polar-decomposition view) that the resulting error in the step-size path decays as the algorithm converges. This yields a quadratic-time, minimal-storage update for the full covariance case, with claimed empirical parity in function evaluations relative to the standard CMA-ES and substantial wall-clock speedups.

The work builds on prior Cholesky-based updates for CMA-ES variants, notably Suttorp et al. (2009), who update a non-triangular factor, and Krause & Igel (2015), who derive efficient rank-one Cholesky updates for special cases (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES, µ = 1). This paper generalizes Cholesky-based updates to the standard rank-µ CMA-ES, integrates them with CSA via A^{-1}, and argues that the change preserves performance in evaluations while improving time and memory complexity. Empirical comparisons against a reference CMA-ES implementation (with delayed eigen-decompositions), a delayed-update baseline, and an adapted Suttorp variant are provided on standard benchmarks with and without random rotations.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: covariance updates and decompositions dominate cost in CMA-ES at moderate-to-large d; a numerically stable, quadratic-time update with optimal storage is valuable.
- Technical contribution: extension of triangular Cholesky rank-1 updates to rank-µ updates for standard CMA-ES, plus a plausible argument (Lemma 1) that the error induced by using A^{-1} in CSA diminishes as the covariance stabilizes in shape.
- Practical benefits: eigenvalues of C are available “for free” from diag(A), and A^{-1} can be computed in O(d^2); storage is optimal (triangular factor only).
- Empirical results: on a broad set of noiseless benchmarks, the proposed method matches the number of function evaluations of the reference CMA-ES while achieving large wall-clock speedups that grow with d.

Weaknesses and suggestions for improvement
- Novelty relative to Krause & Igel (2015): The paper would benefit from a crisper delineation of what is genuinely new. My understanding is that (i) you extend triangular rank-1 Cholesky updates to the full rank-µ CMA-ES covariance update (Eq. 2), (ii) you modify CSA by using A^{-1} instead of C^{-1/2}, and (iii) you provide a convergence-based justification for why this does not harm performance. Please make these distinctions explicit, and contrast to Suttorp et al. (non-triangular factor, extra degrees of freedom, potential instability).
- Equivalence to the covariance update: The paper states that Eq. (2) can be decomposed into rank-1 updates and implemented via Algorithm 2, but does not supply a self-contained derivation. Even a short appendix that proves the exact equivalence (up to floating-point error) between applying Algorithm 2 to A and performing the rank-1 and rank-µ updates in Eq. (2) would be essential for completeness.
- Complexity discussion: You claim O(µ d^2) time, which matches the asymptotic cost of forming rank-µ updates, but CMA-ES-Ref’s per-iteration cost depends on the eigendecomposition schedule. In your experimental setting, the reference recomputes the square root every iteration (effectively Θ(d^3)), hence the large observed speedup. Please:
  - Use Θ-notation where appropriate and provide a per-iteration cost breakdown for all compared algorithms (sampling, ranking, covariance update, decomposition).
  - Clarify the amortized complexity of CMA-ES-Ref under delayed updates, and reconcile with your O(µ d^2) claim by discussing constant factors, memory locality, and when each approach becomes preferable.
  - Report profiling numbers to substantiate that the covariance update dominates runtime in your experiments.
- CSA modification and invariance: Replacing C^{-1/2} by A^{-1} breaks exact affine invariance and, per Hansen (2015), may affect CSA. You argue the error in the path norm vanishes as E_t stabilizes (Lemma 1). This is plausible, but the argument is not deterministic and relies on slowly changing C. Please strengthen the empirical validation beyond final performance:
  - Offline, compute both p_σ using C^{-1/2} and using A^{-1} (you can form C^{-1/2} for analysis only) and report the norm ratio and angle between paths over time, across functions and d.
  - Quantify the rotation drift ∥E_t^T E_{t-1} − I∥ and correlate it with the p_σ discrepancy; bring key plots from the supplement into the main paper.
  - Compare performance on rotated vs unrotated functions during the transient phase, not only at convergence.
  - Explore cases with slowly converging C (e.g., Rosenbrock) and high conditioning; include noisy objectives if possible.
- Numerical stability: You claim numerical stability; please add bounds or empirical evidence under high condition numbers, and discuss safeguards (e.g., variance lower bounds, reconditioning) and failure modes.
- Experimental scope: Consider larger d and report cache effects (you note L2 effects at d=256). Provide wall-clock vs evaluation-count trade-offs for the delayed-update baseline over a range of delay schedules. Include ablations for µ and population size λ to confirm O(µ d^2) scaling.
- Reproducibility: Since you integrated into Shark, please provide a link or commit hash, and list parameter settings and seeds.

Quality
The algorithmic idea is sound, and the Cholesky updates are a principled way to achieve quadratic-time adaptation. The theoretical argument for CSA modification is suggestive but not conclusive; targeted diagnostics as suggested above would significantly strengthen the paper. The experiments are thorough on standard noiseless benchmarks and convincingly demonstrate speedups.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. However, please correct notational and typographical issues:
- In Algorithm 2, wk should be αk; in Table 1, n should be d; “F” should be “f” (line ~185).
- Clean up reference formatting and ensure consistent use of symbols across text and algorithms.
- State all assumptions explicitly in Lemma 1 and explain the limitations (e.g., randomized setting, noise).

Originality
The contribution appears incremental but practically meaningful: a careful extension of Cholesky-based updates to standard CMA-ES with a CSA modification and justification. Clarifying the delta to Krause & Igel (2015) and Suttorp et al. (2009) would address concerns about novelty.

Significance
If validated, the proposed variant is a strong candidate for becoming a practical default for full-covariance CMA-ES in moderate-to-large dimensions, due to the substantial speedups without loss in evaluations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, impactful practical improvement with optimal time and storage for full covariance updates.
- Maintains performance in evaluations while greatly reducing runtime.
- Backed by reasonable theoretical intuition and broad experiments.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES work unless differences are made explicit.
- The CSA modification lacks direct empirical validation of the claimed small error beyond final outcomes.
- Missing derivation of equivalence to the standard covariance update.

Overall, I lean toward rejection in the current form, but I believe the paper could be strong with the additions above: a clear statement of novelty, an explicit equivalence proof, a precise complexity and profiling analysis, and targeted diagnostics validating the CSA modification.
