

Title: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the standard matrix square root operations with triangular Cholesky factorizations for covariance matrix adaptation. CMA-ES is widely recognized as one of the most effective derivative-free optimization algorithms for real-valued problems, with significant applications in machine learning (particularly in reinforcement learning policy search and hyperparameter tuning) and other domains. The authors address a fundamental computational bottleneck in the standard algorithm: the O(d²) time complexity per iteration due to the matrix square root computation required for covariance adaptation.

The key contribution is a theoretically grounded approach that maintains the same asymptotic behavior in terms of function evaluations while significantly reducing computational complexity and memory requirements. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical justification showing that replacing the matrix square root with a triangular Cholesky factor in the cumulative step-size adaptation mechanism (equation 3) does not substantially affect the algorithm's performance. This is a critical advancement over previous attempts to use Cholesky factors in CMA-ES, which either lacked theoretical justification or were limited to specific variants of the algorithm.

The paper demonstrates that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves optimal asymptotic time complexity of O(µd²) for covariance updates (with µ typically O(log d)), while maintaining the same storage complexity as the standard CMA-ES. This represents a significant improvement over the standard implementation, which requires O(d²) time per iteration. The empirical evaluation shows that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES (with delayed matrix square root computation), but with substantially reduced wall-clock time, particularly for higher dimensions. For example, at d=64, the proposed method is approximately 20 times faster than the reference implementation.

Beyond computational efficiency, the paper identifies several valuable secondary benefits. First, the Cholesky factorization provides the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free through its diagonal elements, which is useful for monitoring problem conditioning. Second, the Cholesky factor can be inverted in quadratic time, avoiding numerical instabilities that might arise from maintaining separate updates of the factor and its inverse. Third, the storage complexity is optimal as only the triangular Cholesky factor needs to be stored.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, comparing against relevant baselines including the standard CMA-ES with delayed matrix square root computation (CMA-ES-Ref), a delayed update variant (CMA-ES/d), and an adapted version of the non-triangular Cholesky approach (Suttorp-CMA-ES). The authors test on a diverse set of benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) across multiple dimensions (d ∈ {4, 8, 16, ..., 256}), with 100 trials per function/dimension combination. The results consistently demonstrate the computational advantages of the proposed method, with the performance gains becoming increasingly pronounced as dimensionality increases.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a careful balance between theoretical justification and empirical validation. The connection to machine learning applications is appropriately emphasized, highlighting the relevance of this optimization technique to the NIPS community. The authors have clearly considered the practical implications of their work, noting that Cholesky-CMA-ES provides a viable path to scaling CMA-ES for larger optimization problems where maintaining a full covariance matrix is still computationally feasible.

Minor concerns:
1. The paper should clarify how the implementations were made comparable in terms of computational resources (e.g., programming language, compiler optimizations, hardware specifications).
2. The statement that "the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences" (in Figure 2) does not hold for Cigar and Discus at dimension 256. It would be helpful to provide an explanation for this anomaly.

This is a strong contribution that addresses a practical bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm. The theoretical justification is sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the practical implications are clearly articulated. The paper demonstrates significant computational advantages without compromising optimization performance, making it a valuable addition to the field of derivative-free optimization. Given the strong connections to machine learning applications and the importance of efficient optimization algorithms in modern machine learning, I believe this paper would be a significant contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
