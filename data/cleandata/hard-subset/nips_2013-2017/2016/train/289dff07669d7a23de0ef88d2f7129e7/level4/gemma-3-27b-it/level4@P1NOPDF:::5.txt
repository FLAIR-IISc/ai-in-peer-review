The manuscript presents a novel update scheme for CMA-ES, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, which approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation—typically with a complexity of O(d<sup>3</sup>)—with a scheme that scales as O(μd<sup>2</sup>), where *d* is the optimization problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix instead of a full covariance matrix (and its square root). The difference between Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES stems from the omission of estimating a (random) orthogonal matrix *E<sub>t</sub>* in each step. Under fairly restrictive assumptions—specifically, assumptions regarding the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target covariance matrix—the authors provide an asymptotic argument demonstrating that the sequence of *E<sub>t</sub>* will converge to the identity matrix, and thus the two schemes become equivalent. The validity and performance speed-up of Cholesky-CMA-ES are illustrated using standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark objective functions, and by comparing its performance to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives that employ different delay strategies for full covariance matrix updates.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to accelerating the well-regarded CMA-ES algorithm, which is widely considered among the best methods for non-convex, black-box optimization problems, particularly when little is known about the objective function’s topology and first- or higher-order information is unavailable or unhelpful. The proposed method significantly speeds up the algorithm without sacrificing optimization performance on the synthetic test cases. While this is generally important, a common limitation of any speed-up proposal for CMA-ES is that, in practice, the overall optimization process is often dominated by the runtime of the black-box oracle (the objective function), rendering the actual runtime of CMA-ES negligible in comparison. Furthermore, in typical applications of CMA-ES, the problem dimensionality rarely exceeds a few tens of variables. The runtime of CMA-ES becomes a significant factor only when a single function evaluation is on the order of milliseconds or faster *and* the dimensionality is sufficiently high—a scenario that is specifically targeted by this work, though perhaps less common in real-world applications.

Given the inherent difficulty of obtaining theoretical guarantees for a complex scheme like CMA-ES, I suggest a few additional empirical experiments and corresponding visualizations to strengthen the presentation of the results. First, it would be insightful to observe the evolution of *E<sub>t</sub>* along selected optimization trajectories, for example, by monitoring its norms. I envision an experiment where the full CMA-ES and the approximation are calculated simultaneously, allowing for a direct comparison and providing further empirical evidence regarding the actual fluctuations of *E<sub>t</sub>* over time. Second, for completeness, including actual runtimes and performance results for CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies would be beneficial. Third, while the chosen test functions are standard in the evolutionary optimization community, I recommend a simulation study on quadratic functions where the spectrum of quadratic forms is varied continuously (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012). This would allow for a comparison of solution quality and the error in *E<sub>t</sub>* as a function of the spectral distribution. Finally, it would be instructive to include a real-world experiment to complement the current simulation results. For example, policy gradients in real-time robotics applications might be a suitable choice, as speed is critical in such contexts and the parameter space is typically not excessively large.
