

The paper introduces a variant of CMA-ES termed "Cholesky-CMA-ES," claiming it achieves "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" without altering the algorithm's behavior. This naming is problematic, as "Cholesky-CMA-ES" was first proposed by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a related approach and is well-established in the literature. The authors incorrectly present their variant as novel under this name without acknowledging prior work. The method itself is an incremental modification of Krause et al. (2015), with the sole algorithmic change being the replacement of a single rankOneUpdate call (used in Plus strategies) with a loop over all individuals. This loop is the core of Algorithm 1, yet the paper fails to clearly articulate its significance or address why this is non-trivial.

Empirical results in Figure 3b show differing plateaus on the Cigar function between CMA-ES-Ref and the proposed method. This discrepancy likely stems from how median results are plotted, but the paper does not investigate this. Crucially, the authors omit comparisons to both established variants: Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES (for empirical validation) and Krause et al.’s method (for algorithmic context). Figure 3’s omission of Suttorp’s variant misrepresents the contribution, as the claimed "optimal" complexity is misleading. For separable functions like Sphere, the full covariance matrix is unnecessary—diagonal updates suffice—and the proposed method offers no advantage over simpler approaches. While the method is approximately twice as efficient as Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES (which the paper incorrectly claims is numerically unstable; Suttorp’s work actually addresses this), the paper fails to acknowledge this prior work or justify the omission.

The complexity argument is overstated: the method retains quadratic time complexity for large-scale optimization, as the covariance matrix remains full-rank. The authors’ assertion of "optimal" complexity thus conflates efficiency within the full-covariance framework with broader optimality. While the modification may indeed be the most practical improvement for scenarios requiring the full covariance matrix, this incremental nature weakens the contribution. The algorithmic novelty is minimal—it amounts to a single loop over individuals—and the paper does not sufficiently defend why this loop is necessary or why prior implementations (e.g., Krause et al.) did not adopt it.

To address these issues, the authors should:  
(a) Correct the naming to avoid confusion with Suttorp et al. (2009), and explicitly reference their work;  
(b) Re-evaluate all experiments against Suttorp’s and Krause’s variants, updating Figures 1–3 to include these comparisons (or relocate full results to supplementary material if figures become cluttered);  
(c) Clarify in Algorithm 1 and the main text that the core contribution is the loop over individuals, and justify why this is non-trivial (e.g., by explaining why Krause et al.’s implementation did not include it).  

Minor note: The claim that "an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step" is inaccurate. Standard implementations perform this periodically, not in every iteration. This should be corrected.  

Overall, the paper’s contribution is incremental. While the proposed modification may offer modest efficiency gains within the full-covariance paradigm, the lack of proper contextualization, misleading terminology, and omission of key prior work significantly undermine its novelty and impact. The suggested revisions are essential to clarify the technical contribution and position the work accurately within the literature.
