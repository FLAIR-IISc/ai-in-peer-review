This paper considers the Cholesky covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy, extending the covariance update to utilize both rank-1 and rank-µ updates for the triangular Cholesky factor. The authors demonstrate that this modification, and specifically the replacement of the matrix square root with the triangular Cholesky factor in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, does not significantly alter the algorithm’s behavior under convergence assumptions. Empirical results are presented to support this claim and demonstrate performance gains.

The paper builds upon existing work in efficient CMA-ES updates, particularly that of Krause and Igel [2015], which explored rank-1 updates with Cholesky factorization. This work presents a straightforward generalization to the rank-µ update, framed as a sequence of rank-1 updates. While the extension is logical, the novelty relative to prior work is incremental. The theoretical justification provided, relying on Lemma 1 concerning the convergence of the Cholesky factor, is valuable, however, the convergence of the covariance matrix *Ct* in CMA-ES has been previously established (e.g., Beyer, ECJ, 2014), though not necessarily with the exact formulae employed here. Strengthening the connection to existing convergence proofs could bolster the theoretical foundation.

The strengths of the paper include the clear presentation of the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm, the theoretical justification for its approximation, and the empirical demonstration of its speedup, particularly as dimensionality increases. The algorithm's reduced runtime and memory complexity are significant advantages, especially for large-scale optimization problems. 

However, there are several weaknesses. First, minor typographical errors exist (e.g., in equation (1), the σ in the denominator should be σt; and on page 3, footnote 2 contains a similar error). Second, a minor grammatical error appears in line 94 (“which can by be motivated…” – remove "by"). Third, the analysis largely focuses on asymptotic complexity, and a more detailed discussion of the constant factors and practical implications of the approximation (introduced by replacing *st* with *ŝt*) would be beneficial. It would be helpful to analyze how quickly the rotation matrix *Et* converges in practice, and the potential impact on performance during early iterations. Finally, while the experiments demonstrate a speedup, a more comprehensive comparison against other recent CMA-ES variants, beyond those presented, would strengthen the evaluation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a well-defined and theoretically justified approximation to the standard CMA-ES.
*   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves improved computational efficiency (runtime and memory) without sacrificing the number of function evaluations.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a clear performance advantage, particularly in higher dimensions.
*   The work addresses a practical bottleneck in CMA-ES – the cost of covariance matrix updates.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the contribution is incremental, building directly upon existing work (Krause and Igel, 2015).
*   The theoretical analysis relies on convergence assumptions that are not fully justified within the specific context of the modified CSA.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, including comparisons to a broader range of recent CMA-ES variants.
*   Minor typographical and grammatical errors are present.

**Overall:** This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. While the novelty is incremental, the practical benefits of the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES, combined with the theoretical justification, make it a worthwhile addition to the literature. Addressing the minor weaknesses identified would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions.
