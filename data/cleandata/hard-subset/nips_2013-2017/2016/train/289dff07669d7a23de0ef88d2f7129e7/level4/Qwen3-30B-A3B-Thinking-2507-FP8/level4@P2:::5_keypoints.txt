- Introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES: replaces matrix square root with lower-triangular Cholesky factorization to reduce covariance adaptation cost.  
- Reduces time complexity from O(d^3) to O(µ d^2) (µ typically O(log d)), avoiding costly per-iteration matrix square roots.  
- Theoretical claim: the orthogonal matrix Et converges to the identity under reasonable covariance convergence assumptions, making the approximation asymptotically valid.  
- Builds on prior CMA-ES and Cholesky-based work but provides a novel theoretical justification absent in earlier variants.  
- Practical benefits: optimal storage complexity, free access to covariance eigenvalues, and numerically stable inverse Cholesky computation.  
- Empirical strengths: matches standard CMA-ES in function evaluations while achieving substantially faster wall-clock times, with speedups growing with dimension (Figures 1–3).  
- Implementation provided within the Shark library, demonstrating practical utility.  
- Quality: solid theoretical analysis (Lemma 1) and reproducible empirical results; paper is clearly written and well organized.  
- Originality: strong — presents a distinct, theoretically grounded approximation compared to prior Cholesky approaches.  
- Significance: important for practitioners working on high-dimensional optimization where covariance adaptation dominates runtime.  
- Practical limitation: in many real-world problems objective evaluations dominate runtime and dimensions are modest, so the speedup may be of limited practical relevance.  
- Recommendation to authors: more thoroughly discuss realistic scenarios where the speedup matters (very-fast evaluations and high-dimensional problems).  
- Suggested additional experiments: plot Et evolution over trajectories; runtime comparisons against standard CMA-ES without delay strategies; spectral-distribution simulations on quadratics; and a real-world fast-evaluation high-dim application (e.g., robotics policy search).  
- Overall judgment: technically sound, valuable contribution with compelling theory and experiments; recommended for acceptance.
