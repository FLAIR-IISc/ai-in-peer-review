Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that updates and maintains a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix and its eigendecomposition. This reduces the per-iteration computational complexity from O(d^3) in the eigendecomposition step to O(mu d^2), where d is the dimension and mu the number of selected samples (typically O(log d)). The key difference from standard CMA-ES is that the method avoids estimating the orthogonal factor Et at each step. Under restrictive assumptions—specifically, that the covariance sequence converges to a fixed target covariance—the authors argue that Et converges to the identity, rendering the two schemes asymptotically equivalent. Empirical results on standard benchmarks compare favorably to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that defer or approximate full covariance updates, showing notable speed-ups without loss in optimization performance.

Strengths
- Clear and practical algorithmic idea: maintaining a Cholesky factor directly avoids repeated eigendecompositions.
- Significant computational savings with no observed loss of solution quality on tested functions.
- A plausible asymptotic equivalence argument that illuminates when and why the approximation should match CMA-ES.
- Comparisons to reasonable baselines that also aim to reduce covariance update costs.

Limitations and concerns
- Practical impact can be limited in common black-box settings where function evaluations dominate runtime; CMA-ES overhead is often negligible unless evaluations are very cheap and d is moderately large.
- The theoretical argument relies on strong convergence assumptions, leaving behavior in the transient, non-asymptotic regime less understood.
- The empirical suite is somewhat narrow and lacks controlled studies that probe sensitivity to spectrum, dimension, and noise.
- The paper does not report results for plain CMA-ES (without delay strategies) in this work’s runtime/performance framework, making it harder to quantify absolute gains.
- The numerical stability of repeated Cholesky updates/downdates over long runs is not fully discussed.

Requests and suggestions for improvement
1) Track Et during optimization.
- In runs of full CMA-ES, compute the corresponding Cholesky-based approximation in parallel and report measures of deviation, e.g., norms of Et − I, principal angles, or spectral radii, over time.
- Show trajectories for several functions and dimensions to quantify transient fluctuations and convergence of Et to identity.

2) Include the unmodified CMA-ES baseline.
- Report wall-clock times and iterations to target across dimensions, with and without noise, for the vanilla algorithm.
- Provide a runtime breakdown per iteration (sampling, recombination, covariance update, eigendecomposition) and memory usage, to clearly demonstrate where the proposed method saves time and by how much.

3) Controlled quadratic testbed.
- Use a family of quadratic functions with systematically varied spectra (condition numbers, eigenvalue decay profiles, clustered spectra), akin to Stich and Müller (PPSN 2012).
- Compare convergence rate, final solution quality, and Et deviation as functions of the spectral distribution and dimension.

4) Real-world or near-real-time application.
- Add an application where evaluation cost is low and speed matters, such as policy search in robotics with real-time constraints, online controller tuning, or fast simulation-based tasks. This would bolster the claim of practical relevance for cases where optimizer overhead is non-negligible.

5) Numerical stability and maintenance of the factor.
- Detail the rank-one and rank-mu Cholesky update formulas used, including any safeguards (e.g., jitter, reorthogonalization, or periodic refactorization).
- Report how often (if ever) a full refactorization is needed, its cost, and the impact on accuracy and speed.

6) Sensitivity analyses.
- Explore sensitivity to mu and standard CMA-ES hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates c1, cmu, and step-size control). Provide ablations to show robustness and identify regimes where the approximation is most beneficial.

7) Clarify scope of the asymptotic argument.
- Discuss when Et can be far from identity in finite-sample regimes (e.g., multimodal landscapes, anisotropic noise), and whether one can bound its deviation empirically or heuristically.
- If possible, include simple non-asymptotic diagnostics that practitioners can monitor to detect when the approximation might degrade performance.

Presentation suggestions
- State complexities and constants precisely, including the frequency of any expensive operations (e.g., if baseline methods perform eigendecomposition every k iterations).
- Position the contribution against related simplifications of CMA-ES (e.g., limited-memory or diagonal variants), clarifying novelty and trade-offs.
- Include concise pseudocode and commit to releasing reference code to facilitate adoption and reproducibility.

Overall assessment
This paper presents a compelling, well-motivated simplification of CMA-ES that replaces expensive eigendecompositions with efficient Cholesky updates, achieving substantial speed-ups without observed performance degradation on standard benchmarks. The contribution is most impactful in regimes with moderate-to-high dimension and inexpensive evaluations, where optimizer overhead matters. While the asymptotic equivalence argument is informative, the paper would be significantly strengthened by broader empirical characterization—especially tracking Et, including the plain CMA-ES baseline, controlled spectral studies, and at least one real-world application—along with a discussion of numerical stability. With these additions, I would be strongly in favor of acceptance.
