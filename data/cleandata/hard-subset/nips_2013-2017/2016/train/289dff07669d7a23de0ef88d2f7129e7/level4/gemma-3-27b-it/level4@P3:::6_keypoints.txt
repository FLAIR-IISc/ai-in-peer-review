- Paper proposes updating the CMA-ES covariance via incremental Cholesky factor updates to reduce runtime and memory without recomputing the matrix square root each iteration.  
- Claims the approach preserves original CMA-ES performance characteristics while improving efficiency.

Strengths:
- Practical improvement: demonstrable runtime speedup, especially in high dimensions, without increasing function evaluations.  
- Theoretical justification: Lemma 1 argues the Cholesky approximation does not fundamentally change convergence behavior.  
- Clear presentation: well-structured exposition and Algorithm 1 effectively illustrate the modification.  
- Empirical validation: experiments generally support runtime improvements across benchmark functions.

Weaknesses:
- Naming conflict & context: fails to acknowledge prior “Cholesky-CMA-ES” by Suttorp et al. (2009), causing potential confusion.  
- Incremental contribution: looping through individuals for rank-one updates seems a small/ incremental change requiring stronger justification.  
- Incomplete comparison: lacks direct algorithmic and empirical comparison to Suttorp et al. (2009) and more detailed comparison to Krause & Igel (2015).  
- Misleading claims & nuance: “optimal covariance update and storage complexity” is overstated and ignores problem-dependent limits and reported numerical instabilities.  
- Figure interpretation: observed plateaus in Figure 1(b) suggest behavioral differences that need investigation.  
- Figure presentation: adding more comparisons may clutter plots; better organization or supplementary material is needed.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a practical CMA-ES limitation (covariance update cost).  
- Offers clear runtime performance improvements.  
- Provides a reasonable theoretical rationale for the approach.  
- Experimental results support the main claims despite caveats.

Arguments for rejection:
- Contribution is relatively incremental.  
- Fails to adequately compare and position against relevant prior work (especially Suttorp et al.).  
- Claims of optimality are misleading.  
- Naming conflict and unexplained empirical nuances raise evaluation thoroughness concerns.

Overall assessment:
- A worthwhile but incremental improvement with tangible benefits; however, lacking thorough comparisons and some overstated claims.

Recommendation (conditional acceptance pending major revisions):
- Resolve the naming conflict and clearly differentiate from Suttorp et al. (2009).  
- Perform comprehensive algorithmic and empirical comparisons with Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015).  
- Moderate optimality claims and acknowledge method limitations.  
- Investigate and explain differences seen in Figure 1(b).  
- Justify the significance of the for-loop in Algorithm 1 as a meaningful contribution.  
- Improve figure clarity or move extended comparisons to well-structured supplementary material.
