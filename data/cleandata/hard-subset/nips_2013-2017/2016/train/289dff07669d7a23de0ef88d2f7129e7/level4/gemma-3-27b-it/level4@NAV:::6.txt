The paper introduces a modification to CMA-ES, termed “Cholesky-CMA-ES,” aiming to reduce computational cost without altering the algorithm’s fundamental behavior. However, the chosen nomenclature is problematic, as “Cholesky-CMA-ES” was previously used by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a related method, a connection the authors do not acknowledge. Instead, the work presents its variant as a novel contribution. The proposed method builds incrementally upon Krause et al. (2015), specifically differing in the repeated application of a rank-one update—a procedure already present in Krause et al.—to each individual within the population, rather than once per generation as in Plus strategies.

Empirical results, specifically in Figure b) on the Cigar function, reveal discrepancies in the observed plateau objective function values between the reference CMA-ES and the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES, potentially stemming from the presentation of median results. A crucial comparison is missing: the paper lacks a direct empirical evaluation against the Cholesky-CMA-ES of Suttorp et al. (2009) and a detailed algorithmic comparison to the approach by Krause et al. (2015). 

The claim of “Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity” is overstated. While beneficial in scenarios requiring the full covariance matrix, it does not represent an optimal solution for all problems – particularly those, like the Sphere function or separable functions, where a diagonal representation is sufficient. Furthermore, the proposed method provides only a two-fold improvement in cost compared to the Cholesky CMA-ES by Suttorp et al., a method the authors claim is numerically unstable, contrary to the original publication. The omission of Suttorp et al.’s method from Figure 3 further obscures the true contribution of this work. 

Although the proposed approach does not fundamentally alter CMA-ES’s quadratic complexity for large-scale optimization, it may represent a practical improvement for scenarios demanding the full covariance matrix. Overall, the algorithmic contribution – essentially the addition of a "for" loop – appears incremental. 

I recommend the following revisions: a) Address the naming conflict; b) Include experimental comparisons to both Suttorp et al.’s and Krause et al.’s methods, and revise all three figures accordingly. If this leads to overcrowding, prioritize the best-performing variant in the main text and relocate the full figures to the Supplementary Material; c) Clearly articulate the significance of the "for" loop in Algorithm 1, or justify why it should not be considered the primary algorithmic contribution.  Finally, a minor point: the statement “Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step” is inaccurate, as eigenvalue decomposition is typically performed periodically in proper implementations.
