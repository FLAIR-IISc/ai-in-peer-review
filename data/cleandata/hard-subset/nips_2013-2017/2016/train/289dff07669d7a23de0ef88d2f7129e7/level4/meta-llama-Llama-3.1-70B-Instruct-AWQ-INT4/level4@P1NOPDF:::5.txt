The manuscript presents a novel update scheme for the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), dubbed Cholesky-CMA-ES, which approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation with a reduced computational complexity of O(μd^2) compared to the original O(d^3), where d is the dimension of the optimization problem and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea behind this update is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix instead of a full covariance matrix (and its square root). The primary difference between Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES lies in the absence of estimating a random orthogonal matrix Et at each step. Under certain restrictive assumptions regarding the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target covariance matrix, the authors provide an asymptotic argument that the sequence of Et converges to an identity matrix, rendering the two schemes equivalent.

The validity and efficiency of Cholesky-CMA-ES are demonstrated through standard runtime and convergence plots on selected objective functions, as well as comparisons with two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives that delay full covariance matrix updates using different methods. This paper makes a significant contribution to accelerating the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm, which is arguably the best algorithm for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the topology of the objective function and first or higher-order information is difficult to access or unhelpful. The proposed method substantially speeds up the algorithm without compromising optimization performance on synthetic test cases.

However, a common weakness of any CMA-ES speed-up proposal is that, in practice, the overall optimization process is often dominated by the runtime of the black-box oracle (the objective function), and the actual runtime of CMA-ES is frequently negligible in comparison. Moreover, in typical scenarios where CMA-ES is employed, the dimensionality of the problems rarely exceeds a few tens of variables. Only in cases where a single function evaluation is on the order of milliseconds or smaller and the dimensionality is higher do the runtime of CMA-ES and its speed-ups become crucial, which is the targeted scenario here (albeit potentially less realistic in practice).

Given that theoretical guarantees for an involved scheme like CMA-ES are notoriously challenging to obtain, I suggest a few additional empirical experiments and plots to strengthen the presentation of the results. Firstly, it would be instructive to visualize the evolution of Et on selected optimization trajectories, e.g., in different norms. I envision an experiment where the full CMA-ES is used, and the approximation is calculated simultaneously and compared, providing further empirical evidence about the actual fluctuations of Et over time. Secondly, it would be beneficial to include actual runtimes and performance of CMA-ES without delay strategies in this contribution for completeness. Thirdly, although the used test functions are standard in the evolutionary optimization community, I would prefer a simulation study on quadratic functions where the spectrum of quadratic forms is varied continuously (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012), and compare the solution quality and error in E_t in dependence of the spectral distribution. Lastly, it would be instructive to include a real-world experiment that accompanies the present simulation results, such as policy gradients in real-time robotics applications, where speed is indeed crucial and the parameter space is not excessively large.
