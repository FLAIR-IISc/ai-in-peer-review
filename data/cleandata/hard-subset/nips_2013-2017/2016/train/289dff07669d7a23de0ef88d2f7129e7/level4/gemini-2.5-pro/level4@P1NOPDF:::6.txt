This paper proposes a variant of CMA-ES with a claimed "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity." The authors assert that their modification reduces the computational cost of the algorithm without altering its search behavior. While the goal is laudable, the paper suffers from several significant issues regarding its claims, its relation to prior work, and the incrementality of its contribution.

**1. Naming and Relation to Prior Work**

The proposed method is named "Cholesky-CMA-ES." This choice of name is misleading, as "Cholesky-CMA-ES" was first introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a similar, well-known algorithm. The authors fail to acknowledge this prior work or justify their use of an established name, instead introducing their method as "Our CMA-ES variant, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES." This is a serious oversight that must be corrected.

Furthermore, the paper omits a proper comparison with closely related work. The proposed method appears to be an incremental modification of the algorithm presented by Krause et al. (2015). A detailed algorithmic comparison to the Krause et al. variant is necessary to precisely delineate the novel contributions of this paper.

Crucially, an empirical comparison to the original Cholesky-CMA-ES by Suttorp et al. is missing. The authors claim that Suttorp's method is prone to numerical instabilities, but this contradicts the claims made in the original Suttorp et al. paper. This discrepancy requires justification. The proposed approach is only about twice as fast as the Suttorp et al. variant. The omission of this important baseline from the experimental results in Figure 3 significantly overstates the performance gains and could mislead readers about the magnitude of the paper's contribution.

**2. Novelty and Algorithmic Contribution**

The primary algorithmic contribution appears to be the application of the `rankOneUpdate` function (also present in the work of Krause et al.) in a loop over all individuals, rather than once per generation. From this perspective, the novelty of the work seems very limited, essentially amounting to the addition of a "for" loop to an existing procedure. The authors should either clarify why this interpretation is incorrect and detail other substantive contributions, or acknowledge the highly incremental nature of their work.

**3. Overstated Claims and Empirical Discrepancies**

The central claim of "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" is overstated. This claim of optimality only holds under the strong assumption that a full covariance matrix is required. On simpler problem classes, such as the Sphere function, a covariance matrix is unnecessary. Similarly, for separable functions, maintaining only a diagonal covariance matrix is sufficient and far more efficient. The claim should be qualified to reflect these limitations.

There also appears to be a discrepancy in the empirical results. The authors claim their modification does not change the algorithm's behavior, yet in Figure b), the performance on the Cigar function shows a clear difference. The plateaus for the baseline "CMA-ES-Ref" and the proposed "Cholesky-CMA-ES" occur at different objective function values. The authors should investigate and explain this difference, which may be an artifact of their plotting methodology (e.g., using medians) or may indicate a more fundamental change in the algorithm's dynamics.

**4. Impact on Large-Scale Optimization**

While the proposed modification offers a welcome reduction in time and memory, it does not fundamentally alter the landscape for large-scale optimization with CMA-ES. The complexity remains quadratic in the problem dimension, which is the primary bottleneck for applying CMA-ES to high-dimensional problems. Nevertheless, I concede that for applications where a full covariance matrix is deemed necessary, this modification may represent the most efficient implementation available.

**Summary and Required Revisions**

In its current form, the paper appears to be a highly incremental work with overstated claims and an inadequate treatment of prior art. To strengthen the paper and make it suitable for publication, I recommend the following essential revisions:

1.  **Resolve the Naming Conflict:** The authors must address the use of the name "Cholesky-CMA-ES." They should either choose a new name to avoid confusion or provide a compelling justification for using the existing one, along with a thorough discussion of the Suttorp et al. (2009) algorithm.

2.  **Expand Comparisons:** The experimental evaluation must be expanded to include both the Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause et al. (2015) variants. All figures should be updated to include these crucial baselines. If the figures become too cluttered, the primary comparison can be shown in the main text, with complete results provided in the supplementary material.

3.  **Clarify the Contribution:** The authors need to better articulate their core algorithmic contribution. They should explicitly address whether it is primarily the "for" loop in Algorithm 1 or explain why this view is an oversimplification.

**Minor Comment:**
The statement, "Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step," is inaccurate. In most standard and efficient implementations of CMA-ES, this computationally expensive operation is performed only periodically, not in every single generation.
