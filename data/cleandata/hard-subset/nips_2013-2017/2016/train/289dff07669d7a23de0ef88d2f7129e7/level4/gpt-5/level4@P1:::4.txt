Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates only the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = AAT, instead of computing and storing C and its matrix square root. The key change is to modify the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): the original CSA uses C−1/2, while the proposed variant replaces this with A−1. Because A and C1/2 differ by a rotation (via an RQ/polar decomposition), the authors argue that the error introduced by dropping this rotation in CSA is small and vanishes as the algorithm converges. Building on prior work that used Cholesky updates in special CMA-ES variants (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES) and the rank-one Cholesky update of Krause and Igel (2015) for µ = 1, this paper extends the approach to standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and provides a theoretical justification that the modification should not deteriorate performance in terms of function evaluations. The method reduces the per-iteration complexity of covariance adaptation to O(µd^2) with minimal memory. Experiments compare against a reference CMA-ES with delayed decompositions, a variant with d-step delays, and an adapted non-triangular Cholesky update (Suttorp et al., 2009), showing essentially identical numbers of function evaluations but substantially lower wall-clock time, especially as d grows. The work is positioned relative to diagonal/low-rank variants (e.g., Ros and Hansen, 2008; Loshchilov, 2014/2015) and delayed decompositions, and claims advantages when a full covariance is needed on non-separable, ill-conditioned problems.

Strengths:
- Clear and well-motivated algorithmic contribution: replace C and C−1/2 with a triangular Cholesky factor and its inverse, updating A via efficient rank-one operations.
- Theoretical support: Lemma 1 shows that, under convergence of the normalized covariance shape, the rotation between C1/2 and A stabilizes, making the CSA approximation harmless in the limit; the error is further down-weighted by the CSA’s exponential averaging.
- Asymptotically optimal storage (triangular factor only) and quadratic-time updates; efficient A−1 computation and “free” access to the eigenvalues of C (via the diagonal of A).
- Empirical results across standard benchmarks (including rotated versions) show identical function-evaluation counts to the reference CMA-ES and large runtime gains (e.g., ~20× at d = 64), consistent with the complexity analysis.
- Good discussion of practical properties (e.g., cache effects at d = 256) and of when full covariance is warranted versus low-dimensional approximations.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Numerical stability: The paper states the approach is “numerically stable” and highlights the stability of triangular updates and inversion, but a more explicit argument or bound would strengthen the claim. For instance, how do rounding errors accumulate in Algorithm 2 and in repeated updates to A and A−1? How does this compare empirically to eigen/sqrt-based updates in terms of condition numbers and error growth?
- Theoretical scope of the CSA approximation: Lemma 1 requires convergence of the normalized covariance shape C̄t. CMA-ES is stochastic and C may change slowly but not strictly converge, especially on multimodal or noisy problems. Can the authors (i) characterize the error in ‖pσ‖ when C undergoes drift, and (ii) bound the impact on step-size adaptation when EtETt−1 deviates from I? An ablation that isolates the CSA approximation (e.g., comparing norms computed via A−1 vs via an explicit C−1/2 on small d) would be informative.
- Invariance: The paper notes that CSA is not proven to preserve affine invariance in the original CMA-ES, and here C−1/2 is approximated by A−1. Please clarify empirically how much the CSA approximation affects rotation (spectral) invariance, beyond the supplementary Et rotation measurements. For example, report any measurable differences in success probability or eval counts under random rotations at higher d.
- Experimental scope: The benchmarks are standard and informative, but they are noiseless and unconstrained. Given the stated applicability to reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning, robustness under noise and constraints would be valuable. Also, since the method reduces wall-clock time primarily through O(µd^2) updates, an expanded comparison at higher d with low-rank/limited-memory CMA-ES (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) would clarify the practical crossover points (full covariance vs low-rank).
- Reporting details: Please provide full hardware/compiler/BLAS details and reproducibility information (seeds, stopping criteria) in the main text. The L2 cache discussion is useful; a brief sensitivity study (e.g., blocking or packed storage variants) could help practitioners.

Quality:
- Claims are generally well-supported by analysis and experiments. The CSA approximation is motivated and partially justified; additional empirical quantification of the approximation error would improve confidence.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written overall. It would help to explicitly state where the proposed method departs from standard CMA-ES (only CSA) and to summarize the consequences for invariance. A brief intuitive explanation of Algorithm 2’s update and its numerical properties would aid readability.

Originality:
- The extension of triangular Cholesky updates to full CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and the CSA modification, together with the convergence-based justification, constitute a meaningful and original contribution relative to Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015).

Significance:
- The approach yields substantial practical speed-ups without increasing function evaluations, which is important for medium-to-large d problems where full covariance adaptation is still feasible and beneficial.

Suggestions and questions for the authors:
- Provide a more formal or empirical justification for the “numerically stable” claim (e.g., backward-error analysis, conditioning of A and A−1 over long runs).
- Quantify the CSA approximation error: compare ‖pσ‖ computed with A−1 versus with an explicitly computed C−1/2 on small-to-moderate d, and relate any differences to step-size dynamics and final performance.
- Discuss whether hybrid schemes are possible, e.g., combining the proposed triangular-factor updates with low-rank or limited-memory approximations (Loshchilov, 2015) to handle very high-dimensional settings encountered in direct policy search for neural networks.
- Clarify the dependence of runtime on µ and typical µ(d) settings; include sensitivity to µ and to the delay strategies in the baselines.
- Consider adding experiments with noise and/or constraints to demonstrate robustness in realistic ML scenarios.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong practical impact with clear speed-ups at no cost in function evaluations.
- Conceptually simple, elegant modification with solid theoretical motivation and optimal storage/runtime for full covariance CMA-ES.
- Careful empirical study on standard benchmarks, including rotated cases.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Theoretical guarantees for the CSA approximation are asymptotic and rely on conditions that may not hold broadly; the empirical quantification of approximation error is limited.
- Experiments focus on noiseless, unconstrained problems; applicability to noisy RL-like settings is asserted but not demonstrated.
