The covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) is a powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm widely used in machine learning. This work addresses a key computational bottleneck in CMA-ES: the updating and storage of the covariance matrix, which dominates time and space complexity. The authors propose a numerically stable, quadratic-time covariance matrix update scheme based on maintaining the triangular Cholesky factor, minimizing memory requirements. This necessitates a modification to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, replacing the inverse of the square root of the covariance matrix with the inverse of the Cholesky factor. Critically, the authors argue, and empirically demonstrate, that this modification doesn’t alter the algorithm’s convergence behavior in terms of required objective function evaluations. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES, therefore, offers a significant improvement in runtime and memory usage when covariance matrix adaptation is crucial, such as in non-separable, ill-conditioned problems.

The paper begins with a clear introduction to the CMA-ES and its applications, effectively motivating the need for more efficient covariance matrix handling. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, referencing previous attempts to address this issue, such as delaying updates [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001] or restricting model complexity [Poland and Zell, 2001; Ros and Hansen, 2008]. They also correctly acknowledge related work using Cholesky factors [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015] and highlight the limitations of these approaches when applied to the standard CMA-ES. 

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical justification for the proposed modification and its empirical validation. The authors provide a compelling argument, based on the convergence of coordinate system rotations (Lemma 1), for why replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor does not negatively impact performance. The experimental results, presented in Figures 1-3, clearly demonstrate the speedup achieved by the Cholesky-CMA-ES, particularly as dimensionality increases. The comparison with existing CMA-ES variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) is thorough and provides strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach. The experiments demonstrate that the number of function evaluations remains consistent, while wall-clock time is significantly reduced.

However, a few areas could benefit from further clarification. While the authors state the computation is "numerically more stable," a more detailed explanation of *why* this is the case would strengthen the argument. The relationship between the Cholesky decomposition and numerical stability is well-known, but explicitly stating the benefits within the context of CMA-ES would be beneficial. Additionally, the discussion on the limitations of the approach for extremely high-dimensional problems is somewhat brief. While the authors mention the potential need for low-dimensional approximations [Loshchilov, 2015], further exploration of how the Cholesky-CMA-ES could be integrated with such techniques – or a more in-depth discussion of scenarios where it remains competitive despite high dimensionality – would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is well-motivated, theoretically justified, and empirically validated.
*   **Significant Improvement:** The Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a substantial speedup in runtime without compromising performance (number of function evaluations).
*   **Practical Relevance:** The improved efficiency makes CMA-ES more applicable to large-scale optimization problems, particularly in machine learning.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its benefits.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Discussion of High-Dimensionality:** The discussion of scaling to extremely high dimensions could be more thorough.
*   **Numerical Stability Explanation:** A more detailed explanation of the numerical stability benefits would be helpful.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES is a practical and efficient improvement to a widely used algorithm, and the authors have provided a compelling case for its adoption. I recommend acceptance.
