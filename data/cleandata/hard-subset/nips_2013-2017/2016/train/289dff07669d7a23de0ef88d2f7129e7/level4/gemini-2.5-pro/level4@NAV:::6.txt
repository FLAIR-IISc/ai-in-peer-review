This paper presents a variant of CMA-ES that purports to achieve "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity." The central thesis is that this modification reduces computational cost without altering the algorithm's fundamental behavior.

A significant issue arises with the chosen name, "Cholesky-CMA-ES." This name is already established in the literature, having been introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a related algorithm. The manuscript fails to acknowledge this prior work, instead presenting the name as novel ("Our CMA-ES variant, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES").

Furthermore, the proposed method appears to be an incremental modification of the work by Krause et al. (2015). The primary algorithmic distinction seems to be the application of the `rankOneUpdate` function within a loop over all individuals, whereas it is called only once in plus-strategies like the one presented by Krause et al.

The empirical results raise questions. For instance, in Figure b), a discrepancy is visible on the Cigar function, where the performance plateau occurs at different objective values for the proposed method versus the reference CMA-ES. This may be an artifact of how median results are plotted, but it requires clarification. A more substantial weakness is the lack of comparative analysis. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an empirical comparison against the Suttorp et al. algorithm and a direct algorithmic comparison to the Krause et al. variant.

The claim of "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" is overstated. This optimality does not hold in general cases, such as on the Sphere function where a covariance matrix is unnecessary, or on separable functions where a diagonal matrix suffices. The reported performance gain is also put into perspective when considering that the proposed method is only about twice as efficient as the Suttorp et al. version, which is notably absent from the comparisons. The paper's justification for this omission—that the Suttorp et al. method is prone to numerical instability—directly contradicts the claims made in the original 2009 paper. Consequently, omitting this key baseline from Figure 3 risks misrepresenting the actual contribution and performance advantage of the proposed work.

Regarding large-scale optimization, the impact of this work is limited. Since the computational complexity remains quadratic, the algorithm does not fundamentally become more scalable. Nevertheless, it is acknowledged that for scenarios where a full covariance matrix is maintained, this modification may represent the most efficient implementation currently available. Overall, the contribution feels overly incremental, seemingly boiling down to the addition of a single "for" loop in the algorithm.

To improve the manuscript, the following revisions are strongly recommended:
1.  Address the naming conflict with the established "Cholesky-CMA-ES" by Suttorp et al.
2.  Conduct a thorough experimental comparison including the variants from both Suttorp et al. and Krause et al., updating all three figures accordingly. If the figures become cluttered, the main text can feature the most relevant comparison, with comprehensive results moved to a supplementary section.
3.  Clarify the core algorithmic contribution. The authors should either explicitly state that the innovation lies in the "for" loop within Algorithm 1 or provide a more detailed explanation to counter this interpretation.

Minor Note:
The paper states that an eigenvalue decomposition is "typically" performed at each step. In most standard implementations, this computationally expensive operation is performed only periodically, not on every iteration.
