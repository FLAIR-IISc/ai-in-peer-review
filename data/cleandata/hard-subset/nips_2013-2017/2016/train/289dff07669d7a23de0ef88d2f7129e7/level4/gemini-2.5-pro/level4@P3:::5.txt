This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, an efficient variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES). The primary contribution is a novel update scheme that reduces the per-iteration time complexity for updating the covariance matrix from O(d^3) to an asymptotically optimal O(µd^2), where d is the problem dimension and µ is the population size. The core idea is to maintain and directly update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than re-computing the matrix decomposition in each step. A key challenge is that the standard Cumulative Step-Size Adaption (CSA) mechanism relies on the inverse matrix square root, which is not directly available from the Cholesky factor. The authors address this by approximating this term with the inverse of the Cholesky factor. They provide a theoretical argument that under the assumption of convergence of the covariance matrix shape, the rotational error introduced by this approximation vanishes over time. The paper's empirical evaluation on several standard benchmark functions demonstrates that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant wall-clock speed-up compared to state-of-the-art CMA-ES implementations, without any discernible loss in optimization performance (i.e., requiring the same number of function evaluations).

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, which is arguably the state-of-the-art algorithm for derivative-free, black-box optimization. By reducing the complexity of the covariance update, this work makes the algorithm more scalable to higher-dimensional problems, especially when function evaluations are computationally inexpensive.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The proposed method is a clever and well-justified approximation. While previous work has explored Cholesky updates for CMA-ES variants, this paper successfully adapts the idea to the standard CMA-ES, including a novel handling of the CSA mechanism. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1, while based on convergence assumptions, provides a sound rationale for why the approximation should work well in practice.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides the necessary background to understand the contribution. The proposed algorithm is presented precisely.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the primary claim of the paper: the Cholesky-CMA-ES is substantially faster in terms of wall-clock time without degrading sample efficiency on the tested benchmarks. The comparison with delayed-update strategies is appropriate and highlights the superiority of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The main weaknesses relate to the practical scope and the depth of the empirical analysis. The following suggestions could help strengthen the paper's contribution.

1.  **Practical Applicability:** A key consideration, which could be discussed more explicitly, is that the proposed speed-up is most impactful in scenarios where the objective function evaluation is computationally cheap relative to the matrix operations. In many real-world applications of CMA-ES, the function evaluation is the primary bottleneck, which can make the speed-up from an O(d^3) to O(µd^2) update negligible in practice, particularly for moderate dimensions (e.g., d < 100). The paper would be stronger if it more clearly delineated the target problem classes where this contribution is most relevant.
2.  **Analysis of the Approximation Error:** The theoretical justification hinges on the convergence of the rotation matrix `Et`. This core assumption could be empirically validated. It would be highly instructive to run an experiment where the standard CMA-ES is used, while simultaneously tracking the proposed Cholesky approximation. Plotting the evolution of the rotational error (e.g., using a matrix norm to measure the deviation of `Et` from the identity matrix over time) on a few optimization trajectories would provide direct empirical evidence to support the theoretical argument.
3.  **Expanded Experimental Analysis:** While the chosen benchmarks are standard, the analysis could be more comprehensive.
    *   It would be beneficial to include a comparison against a baseline CMA-ES with no update delay (i.e., a full O(d^3) update every iteration) for completeness, as this is the algorithm being approximated.
    *   On quadratic functions, a more systematic study where the eigenvalue spectrum of the Hessian is varied (e.g., following the methodology in Stich and Mueller, 2012) would provide deeper insight into how the approximation behaves under different levels of ill-conditioning and separability.
4.  **Demonstration on a Real-World Problem:** The current results are entirely on synthetic benchmarks. The paper's motivation mentions applications like direct policy search in reinforcement learning. Including even a small-scale, real-world experiment (e.g., hyperparameter tuning or a simple policy search task) would significantly bolster the claims of practical utility and demonstrate that the observed benefits translate beyond synthetic functions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a clear, significant, and well-executed contribution to improving a foundational algorithm in derivative-free optimization.
*   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is likely to become the standard implementation for problems where the internal computations of CMA-ES are a bottleneck.
*   The theoretical justification is sound, and the empirical results are strong and convincing.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical impact is limited to a specific subset of problems (high-dimensional with cheap function evaluations), and this scope is not fully discussed.
*   The empirical validation, while positive, could be more thorough by directly analyzing the approximation error and by including a real-world application.

In summary, this is a solid and valuable paper that makes a strong technical contribution. The proposed method is elegant, effective, and well-supported by both theory and experiments. While the paper could be strengthened by a broader discussion of its practical scope and a more in-depth empirical analysis, its current form presents a clear advance for the CMA-ES algorithm.
