Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = AAT, rather than C itself or its symmetric square root. The key algorithmic idea is to realize the standard CMA-ES rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates as a sequence of rank-1 updates applied directly to the Cholesky factor, yielding O(µ d^2) time per iteration and optimal O(d^2) storage when adapting the full covariance. A second contribution is a modified cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): it replaces the inverse square root C−1/2 in the evolution path update with A−1, which is cheap to compute and update. Because the Cholesky factor differs from the symmetric square root by an orthogonal rotation, the authors argue (via a continuity argument and Lemma 1 under a convergence-of-shape assumption) that the error introduced in CSA is small and vanishes as the algorithm approaches a stationary covariance shape. Empirically, the authors find that the modified CSA preserves the number of function evaluations to reach target accuracy, while substantially reducing wall-clock time relative to a reference CMA-ES and to a non-triangular Cholesky update (Suttorp et al., 2009). The work builds on and extends prior efforts to decrease CMA-ES computational cost via delayed decompositions, structured/diagonal models (e.g., Ros and Hansen, 2008; Loshchilov, 2014/2015), and factor-based updates (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015).

Strengths
- Clear, practical algorithm that integrates a triangular Cholesky update with the standard rank-µ CMA-ES, including an efficient rank-one update routine.
- Achieves asymptotically optimal per-iteration time complexity O(µ d^2) for full-covariance CMA-ES and optimal storage, with an efficient way to compute A−1 when needed.
- Provides a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) suggesting the CSA approximation error diminishes as the covariance shape stabilizes.
- Solid empirical results across standard black-box benchmarks and random rotations showing similar evaluation counts but large wall-clock speedups that increase with dimension.

Weaknesses
- Novelty appears incremental relative to Krause and Igel (2015), who already used rank-one updates to a triangular Cholesky factor; the main additions are the rank-µ extension (as a sequence of rank-1 updates) and the CSA modification with a convergence-based justification.
- Theoretical support for the CSA modification relies on a convergence-of-shape assumption and continuity arguments. There are no quantitative finite-time error bounds, nor a proof that affine invariance is preserved.
- Experimental scope is limited: only noiseless benchmarks, modest dimensionality (up to 256), and exclusion of non-convergent Rosenbrock trials (which may bias results). No ablations isolating the effect of the CSA modification or systematic testing under rapidly changing covariances.
- A technical claim appears incorrect: the diagonal entries of a Cholesky factor are not, in general, the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.

Quality
The algorithmic component (maintaining A via rank-1 updates to realize rank-1 and rank-µ terms) is technically sound and well-motivated. The CSA modification is plausible and supported by a reasonable argument (Lemma 1), but the analysis is qualitative and contingent on convergence assumptions that do not hold in general CMA-ES. Empirical evidence that evaluation counts are preserved is encouraging, though stronger validation (including statistics and broader testbeds) would increase confidence. Implementation aspects (use of Shark and LAPACK) suggest attention to numerical stability and performance.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a useful background section. There are several minor typos and notational issues (listed below). It would help to explicitly state the conditions under which Lemma 1 holds, clarify the scope of “asymptotically optimal” complexity, and correct the statement about Cholesky diagonals and eigenvalues. Providing more experimental details (e.g., budgets, stopping criteria treatment of non-convergence) would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. The work extends the triangular Cholesky update idea to the full rank-µ CMA-ES and proposes a modified CSA leveraging A−1 instead of C−1/2 with a convergence-based justification. Given prior work by Krause and Igel (2015) and Suttorp et al. (2009), much of the contribution is a careful and useful engineering integration rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic concept.

Significance
Practical impact is potentially high for users who need full-covariance CMA-ES in medium-to-high dimensions, as the method yields substantial speedups without degrading evaluation efficiency. The approach is relevant to ML applications of black-box optimization (e.g., policy search, hyperparameter tuning). Conceptual impact is more modest due to the incremental nature and limited theory.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Correctness: The claim that “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix” is incorrect. Cholesky diagonals relate to leading principal minors, not eigenvalues. Please revise and clarify what quantities you intend to monitor (e.g., variances along coordinate axes or approximate principal variances via eigendecomposition if needed).
- Theory: Clarify the assumptions for Lemma 1 (e.g., convergence of normalized covariance shape, uniqueness/stability of the RQ decomposition). If possible, provide a bound on the CSA error term as a function of the rate of change of Et to complement the asymptotic statement.
- Experiments:
  - Include ablations comparing original CSA vs. the proposed A−1-based CSA on the same factor update to isolate its effect.
  - Report statistics (median, IQR, success rates) and do not exclude failures from summaries; instead, report success probabilities.
  - Add noisy and constrained benchmarks, and at least one real-world task, to demonstrate robustness and practical relevance.
  - Consider larger d where full covariance is still feasible (e.g., 512, 1024) to further validate scalability.
  - Provide memory measurements and per-iteration timing breakdowns.
- Related work: Discuss LM-CMA and other limited-memory/diagonal approaches in more depth to position when full covariance is preferable despite quadratic cost.
- Notation/typos:
  - Equation (1): The denominator should be σt (similarly in footnote 2 on page 3).
  - Line 94: “which can by be motivated” → remove the extra “by.”
  - Ensure consistent use of σt across equations (some places omit the subscript).
  - Verify the use of QR/RQ terminology and define it once.

Pros
- Practical, implementable speedup for full-covariance CMA-ES with optimal storage and strong empirical gains.
- Reasonable justification that the CSA modification has negligible long-run impact on step-size control.
- Careful consideration of numerical aspects (triangular updates, efficient inversion).

Cons
- Incremental contribution relative to prior Cholesky update work; the rank-µ extension is straightforward via sequential rank-1 updates.
- Theoretical support for CSA modification is asymptotic and assumption-dependent; no finite-time guarantees.
- Limited experimental breadth and some reporting choices that may bias results.
- Contains at least one technical inaccuracy (Cholesky diagonal vs. eigenvalues).

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The work is well-executed and practically valuable, but the novelty over prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES updates appears limited, and the theoretical support for the CSA modification is not fully convincing. I would be inclined to accept if the authors (i) correct the technical error, (ii) strengthen the empirical evaluation (including ablations and broader benchmarks), and (iii) clarify theoretical assumptions and bounds.
