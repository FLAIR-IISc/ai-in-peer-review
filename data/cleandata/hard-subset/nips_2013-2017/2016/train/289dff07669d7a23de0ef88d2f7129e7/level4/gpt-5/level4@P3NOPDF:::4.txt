Summary
The paper proposes a Cholesky-based variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular factor of the covariance matrix instead of relying on explicit matrix square roots or eigen-decompositions. This design removes the need to compute matrix inverses and substantially reduces computational overhead while preserving the search behavior and evaluation efficiency of standard CMA-ES. The authors provide a theoretical justification indicating that the algorithm’s performance is not degraded.

Strengths
- Clear computational benefit: By updating a Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, the method avoids costly eigen-decompositions and repeated inversions. This meaningfully reduces wall-clock time, with the benefit increasing in higher dimensions.
- Theoretical grounding: The paper includes a justification that the search distribution and convergence properties are preserved to a satisfactory extent, suggesting no significant increase in the number of objective function evaluations relative to standard CMA-ES.
- Practical relevance: The approach is a valuable implementation improvement for CMA-ES, with potential to broaden its applicability in time-sensitive settings.

Weaknesses and Questions
- Numerical stability claim: The paper asserts that the Cholesky-based computation is “numerically more stable,” but the explanation is brief. Please provide a more detailed and formal argument. For instance:
  - What specific sources of numerical error in standard CMA-ES (e.g., accumulated asymmetry in C, eigen-decomposition sensitivity for ill-conditioned matrices, loss of positive definiteness) are mitigated by the triangular factor updates?
  - Under what conditions does the Cholesky update maintain positive definiteness without frequent re-factorization or ad-hoc repairs? Are there worst-case bounds on error accumulation or drift from symmetry/PD?
  - How does the conditioning of C evolve relative to standard CMA-ES, and does the Cholesky update introduce or reduce bias in the covariance adaptation?
- Complexity characterization: While the empirical speedups are compelling, the theoretical complexity could be stated more crisply. A side-by-side comparison of per-generation amortized costs (e.g., eigen-decomposition O(n^3) every k generations versus O(n^2) triangular updates per generation) would help readers anticipate when the method yields the largest gains.
- High-dimensional regimes: The manuscript focuses on runtime improvements at fixed evaluation counts, but high-dimensional search often requires structural approximations. It would be valuable to discuss or experiment with combining this approach with low-rank or limited-memory variants (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) to address dimensions in the thousands, as commonly encountered in direct policy search or neural network training.
- Empirical scope: If feasible, please include more results on:
  - Ill-conditioned and non-separable benchmarks to test the stability claims under challenging covariance structures.
  - Larger dimensions (e.g., n ≥ 1000) to quantify scaling and confirm that evaluation counts remain comparable to standard CMA-ES while maintaining clear wall-clock advantages.
  - Sensitivity analyses (step-size control interactions, learning rates for covariance updates) to ensure that algorithmic hyperparameters do not require substantial retuning.
- Implementation details: Clarifications that would improve reproducibility and interpretation:
  - Any safeguards used (e.g., jitter on the diagonal, periodic re-factorization, symmetry enforcement).
  - Memory footprint of the Cholesky representation vs. standard CMA-ES, and whether the method facilitates parallel sampling/evaluation.
  - Numerical precision requirements (single vs. double) and their impact on stability and speed.

Significance
This is a solid algorithmic engineering contribution: it retains the strengths of CMA-ES but significantly lowers per-iteration overhead by avoiding expensive matrix operations. Given the central role of CMA-ES in black-box optimization and the practical importance of wall-clock time, these improvements are meaningful. The work is likely to be adopted by practitioners, especially when objective evaluations are moderately expensive and computational overhead is non-negligible.

Clarity and Presentation
The paper is generally clear, but the following would further improve readability:
- Provide an explicit derivation of the triangular update rules and any approximations used, including a concise statement of invariants that guarantee positive definiteness.
- Present a consolidated complexity table comparing standard CMA-ES and the Cholesky-based variant, along with a brief discussion of amortization over generations.
- Expand the related work section to situate the method among low-rank, diagonal, and limited-memory CMA-ES variants, and discuss complementarity with low-dimensional approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015).

Suggestions
- Strengthen the theoretical section on numerical stability with formal statements or lemmas, referencing standard results on Cholesky updates/downdates and stability of triangular factorizations for SPD matrices.
- Evaluate or at least discuss integration with low-rank or limited-memory approximations to address scalability in very high dimensions.
- Provide more extensive benchmarks highlighting ill-conditioned settings and very high-dimensional problems, together with ablations on any stability safeguards.
- Release reference code and scripts to facilitate adoption and reproducibility.

Overall Assessment
A well-motivated and practically impactful contribution. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves notable computational savings without apparent loss in effectiveness measured by function evaluations. With a stronger treatment of numerical stability, clearer complexity analysis, and discussion of high-dimensional extensions, this paper would be a compelling addition to the literature and of high value to practitioners.
