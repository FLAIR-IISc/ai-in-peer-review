This paper proposes replacing the explicit covariance square root in standard CMA-ES with a triangular Cholesky factorization, thereby avoiding matrix square roots and inverses and reducing computational cost. The authors provide a theoretical justification suggesting that the modification preserves the algorithm’s search behavior and performance. Empirically, the method lowers runtime complexity and wall-clock time without increasing the number of objective function evaluations. Overall, this is a valuable and practical implementation of Cholesky factorization for CMA-ES.

I would appreciate a clearer theoretical explanation for the claim of improved numerical stability. In particular, please clarify why maintaining and updating a Cholesky factor is numerically more stable than computing explicit inverses or square roots. For example, do you provide bounds on the accumulation of round-off error, conditioning advantages of triangular solves over matrix inverses, or guarantees that positive definiteness is preserved under finite-precision updates? It would also help to discuss any required re-factorizations, jitter/regularization strategies, and how error propagation compares to the eigendecomposition-based CMA-ES.

The empirical results suggest that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires roughly the same number of objective evaluations as standard CMA-ES while substantially reducing wall-clock time, with benefits that grow with problem dimension. Given that many practical applications (e.g., training neural networks in direct policy search) are high-dimensional, I encourage the authors to consider integrating low-dimensional or low-rank approximations, such as the approach of Loshchilov (2015), within the proposed framework. A discussion of compatibility, potential hybrid designs (e.g., maintaining a Cholesky factor for a principal subspace with a diagonal remainder), and expected complexity and accuracy trade-offs would strengthen the paper.

In summary, the contribution is technically sound and practically relevant. Clarifying the theoretical basis for numerical stability and exploring combinations with low-dimensional approximations would further improve both the rigor and the applicability of the approach.
