

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces matrix square root computations with triangular Cholesky factorization to reduce time and space complexity. The authors demonstrate that this approach maintains the same optimization performance as standard CMA-ES while achieving optimal asymptotic complexity (O(μd²) for covariance updates and O(d²) for storage). This builds on prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who proposed Cholesky-based approaches for specialized CMA-ES variants, but extends the method to handle full rank-μ updates in the standard CMA-ES framework.

The paper's main contribution is a theoretically justified extension of Cholesky-based updates to handle the full rank-μ update mechanism in standard CMA-ES. The authors provide Lemma 1 to show that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor vanishes as the algorithm converges, which addresses a key concern about the approximation's impact on algorithm behavior. They empirically demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of objective function evaluations as standard CMA-ES but achieves significant runtime improvements (up to 20x faster for d=64), with the speedup increasing with dimensionality.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a technically sound contribution to an important optimization algorithm. The theoretical analysis (Lemma 1) is rigorous and directly addresses the core concern about approximation quality.
2. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple CMA-ES variants across diverse benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) and dimensions (d=4 to 256).
3. The authors provide clear implementation details and make their code available, enhancing reproducibility.
4. The paper correctly identifies the practical importance of reducing computational complexity for high-dimensional optimization, which is relevant to machine learning applications like hyperparameter tuning and reinforcement learning.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty is somewhat incremental. While the extension from rank-1 to rank-μ updates is technically sound, it represents a natural generalization of Krause and Igel (2015) rather than a major conceptual advance. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what makes this generalization non-trivial.
2. The theoretical justification for covariance convergence (which underpins Lemma 1) could be strengthened. Beyer (2014) has already established convergence for CMA-ES in a simplified setting, which would provide stronger theoretical grounding for the authors' argument.
3. The paper contains several minor technical errors that require correction: Equation (1) should have σ_t in the denominator (not σ), the same correction applies to footnote 2 on page 3, and line 94 contains a grammatical error ("can by be" should be "can be").
4. The discussion of practical limitations could be more comprehensive. While the paper notes cache effects for d=256, it doesn't adequately compare the approach to other methods for very high-dimensional optimization (e.g., Loshchilov 2015).

Quality: The paper is technically sound with appropriate theoretical analysis and thorough experimental evaluation. The empirical results support the main claims, though the theoretical justification would be stronger with better connection to existing convergence results for CMA-ES.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and clear, though some technical details in the theoretical sections could be presented more accessibly. The algorithm descriptions are precise and implementation details are well-specified.

Originality: The core idea of using Cholesky factors for covariance updates is not new, as established by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015). The main contribution is the extension to rank-μ updates, which represents a natural but incremental step. The paper would better establish novelty by more clearly differentiating this contribution from prior work.

Significance: The paper addresses a practical computational bottleneck in CMA-ES implementation, which is relevant to machine learning practitioners. The demonstrated speedup (up to 20x for d=64) is significant for applications where function evaluations are expensive. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the incremental nature of the contribution and the fact that efficient implementations of standard CMA-ES already exist for many practical applications.

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution that addresses a practical concern in CMA-ES implementation. The paper demonstrates a useful improvement with clear empirical results. However, the novelty is somewhat limited compared to the work of Krause and Igel (2015), and the theoretical justification could be strengthened with better connection to existing literature on CMA-ES convergence.

Recommendation: Weak Accept

Pro: 
- Addresses a meaningful practical computational bottleneck
- Provides rigorous theoretical justification for the approximation
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing significant speedup
- Clear implementation details and reproducibility

Con:
- Incremental contribution compared to prior work on Cholesky-based CMA-ES
- Theoretical justification could better connect to existing convergence results
- Minor technical errors in the paper that need correction
- Limited discussion of broader implications for high-dimensional optimization
