

This paper presents an important improvement to the CMA-ES (Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy), one of the most successful derivative-free optimization algorithms for real-valued problems. The authors propose a variant called Cholesky-CMA-ES that replaces the standard covariance matrix square root computation with a triangular Cholesky factorization, significantly reducing both time and space complexity while maintaining the algorithm's performance characteristics. This work builds on prior efforts to improve CMA-ES efficiency, including the delayed update strategy [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001] and Cholesky-based updates for specialized variants [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015], but provides a theoretically grounded approach applicable to the standard CMA-ES.

The key innovation lies in their careful theoretical analysis showing that replacing C^{-1/2}_t with A^{-1}_t (where A_t is the Cholesky factor) introduces negligible error in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The authors prove that the error vanishes as the algorithm converges, supported by Lemma 1 demonstrating that the orthogonal component of the Cholesky decomposition converges to identity. This theoretical justification distinguishes their work from previous attempts that lacked such rigorous analysis.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the optimization literature by achieving asymptotically optimal O(µd^2) time complexity for covariance updates (compared to O(d^3) in standard CMA-ES) while maintaining optimal O(d^2) storage complexity. The approach also provides eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free, which is valuable for monitoring problem conditioning. The empirical results confirm that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES but achieves significant speedups (up to 20x for d=64), with the performance gap widening as dimensionality increases.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The theoretical justification is thorough, and the experimental design is appropriate, using standard benchmark functions across multiple dimensions while testing rotation invariance. The results are compelling, with Figures 1-3 effectively demonstrating the time complexity advantage without compromising solution quality.

This work is highly relevant to NIPS, as CMA-ES has found significant applications in machine learning, particularly for direct policy search in reinforcement learning [Gomez et al., 2008; Heidrich-Meisner and Igel, 2009] and hyperparameter tuning [Igel, 2010]. The authors' approach directly addresses a computational bottleneck that limits CMA-ES's applicability to large-scale problems, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper's strengths include:
1. Theoretical justification for the Cholesky approximation
2. Asymptotically optimal time and space complexity
3. Practical benefits (free eigenvalue computation, numerical stability)
4. Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple benchmark functions
5. Clear relevance to machine learning applications

The primary weakness is a minor clarification needed regarding implementation details. To ensure fair comparison between algorithms, the authors should specify whether all implementations (Cholesky-CMA-ES, CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, and Suttorp-CMA-ES) were implemented in the same language and environment, and whether they used identical numerical libraries (e.g., LAPACK as mentioned in the paper). This is particularly important given the significant performance differences observed in Figure 2.

Regarding Figure 2, the statement "the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences" appears inconsistent with the Cigar and Discus results at dimension 256, where the speedup does not continue to increase as expected. The authors should provide an explanation for this deviation, as it may indicate a potential limitation of their approach or implementation details that affect performance at extreme dimensions.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of optimization with clear practical benefits. The authors have demonstrated that their Cholesky-CMA-ES variant can replace the standard CMA-ES in medium-to-large scale optimization problems without sacrificing solution quality, while offering substantial computational advantages. Given the widespread use of CMA-ES in machine learning applications, this work has the potential to become the default implementation for many practitioners.

The paper meets all four NIPS quality criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical justification and empirical results are comprehensive and well-supported.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with detailed algorithms and explanations.
- Originality: The approach provides a novel solution to a known computational bottleneck with theoretical justification.
- Significance: The work addresses a practical limitation in an important optimization algorithm with clear benefits for machine learning applications.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper to NIPS. It represents a significant, well-executed improvement to a widely used optimization method with clear potential for impact in machine learning and related fields.
