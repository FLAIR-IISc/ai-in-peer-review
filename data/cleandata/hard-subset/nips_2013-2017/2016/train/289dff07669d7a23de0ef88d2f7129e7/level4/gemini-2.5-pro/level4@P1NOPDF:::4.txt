This paper's main contribution is the application of triangular Cholesky factorization to the CMA-ES algorithm. This modification eliminates the need for the matrix square root operation used in the standard CMA-ES, thereby reducing the computational cost of key matrix operations, such as sampling and covariance matrix updates. The authors present a theoretical justification demonstrating that this change does not degrade the algorithm's performance.

The proposed method successfully reduces the algorithm's runtime complexity without significantly altering the required number of objective function evaluations. The paper shows that while Cholesky-CMA-ES requires a similar number of objective function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES, it reduces the wall-clock time, an advantage that increases with the search space dimensionality. This paper represents a valuable and practical contribution to improving the efficiency of CMA-ES.

I have a few questions and suggestions for the authors:

1.  The paper states that the proposed computation is "numerically more stable." Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical explanation to substantiate this claim?

2.  While the efficiency gains are promising, many practical applications, such as training neural networks in direct policy search, involve very high-dimensional problems. This high-dimensionality remains a significant concern. Have the authors considered combining their framework with methods designed for such settings, for instance, the low-dimensional approximations proposed by Loshchilov (2015)? Integrating such an approach would address a critical scalability challenge and could significantly broaden the impact of this work.
