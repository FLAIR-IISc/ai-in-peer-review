### Summary

This manuscript introduces the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel update scheme designed to accelerate the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES). The key innovation is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix directly, rather than the full matrix. This approach reduces the computational complexity from O(d³) to O(μd²) per iteration, where d is the problem dimension and μ is the population size (typically O(log d)). The difference between the proposed method and the original CMA-ES arises from omitting the estimation of an orthogonal matrix at each step. The authors provide an asymptotic argument that, under restrictive assumptions about the convergence of the covariance matrices, this omitted orthogonal matrix converges to the identity, making the two schemes theoretically equivalent in the limit. The paper validates the speed-up and performance of Cholesky-CMA-ES through runtime and convergence experiments on standard benchmark functions, comparing it against two other state-of-the-art accelerated CMA-ES variants.

### Strengths

This paper presents a solid and well-motivated contribution to the field. CMA-ES is arguably the state-of-the-art algorithm for non-convex, black-box optimization problems where gradient information is unavailable or unreliable. Therefore, improvements to its computational efficiency are of significant interest to the community. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a considerable speed-up on the presented synthetic test cases without any apparent degradation in optimization performance, which is a commendable result. The technical idea is elegant and clearly explained.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its practical motivation and the scope of its empirical evaluation. While I believe the work is valuable, the following points should be addressed to strengthen the contribution.

**1. Practical Relevance:**
A key challenge for any CMA-ES speed-up proposal is that in many real-world applications, the overall optimization time is overwhelmingly dominated by the evaluation of the objective function (the "black-box oracle"). The internal computations of CMA-ES only become a bottleneck when function evaluations are extremely fast (e.g., sub-millisecond) *and* the problem dimension is high. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough justification of this target scenario or by demonstrating its relevance in a compelling real-world problem.

**2. Need for Broader Empirical Validation:**
While acknowledging the difficulty of providing strong theoretical guarantees for a complex scheme like CMA-ES, the current empirical evidence could be made more compelling. I propose the following experiments to strengthen the paper:

*   **Directly Validate the Core Assumption:** To provide stronger empirical support for the central theoretical assumption (i.e., that the orthogonal matrix E_t converges to the identity), I suggest an experiment where the standard CMA-ES is run while the Cholesky approximation is computed in parallel. Plotting the evolution of a suitable norm of E_t (e.g., the Frobenius norm of E_t - I) over the course of optimization on several test problems would provide direct evidence of its behavior and fluctuations. This would give the reader a much better sense of how valid the approximation is in practice.

*   **Include a Standard Baseline:** The current experiments compare Cholesky-CMA-ES only against other accelerated variants. For completeness and clarity, a direct comparison against the standard, non-accelerated CMA-ES should be included. This would provide a clearer baseline for both runtime and optimization performance.

*   **Systematic Analysis on Quadratic Functions:** The evaluation on standard benchmark functions is useful, but a more controlled study would be insightful. I recommend conducting experiments on quadratic objective functions where the conditioning of the problem (i.e., the eigenvalue spectrum of the Hessian) is systematically varied. This would allow for a more nuanced analysis of how the algorithm's performance and the accuracy of the Cholesky approximation depend on the problem's structure. The work of Stich and Mueller (PPSN 2012) provides a good example of such an experimental design.

*   **Demonstrate Real-World Applicability:** To address the concerns about practical relevance, the inclusion of a real-world experiment would be highly beneficial. A problem where fast function evaluations and moderate-to-high dimensionality make the CMA-ES runtime a genuine bottleneck would be ideal. For instance, applications in real-time policy optimization for robotics, where policies are often parameterized and evaluations can be quick, could serve as a compelling case study.

In conclusion, this paper introduces a promising method for accelerating CMA-ES. The core idea is sound and the initial results are positive. However, its practical impact and the robustness of its underlying assumptions could be substantiated more thoroughly with the suggested experiments. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and make a more convincing case for the adoption of Cholesky-CMA-ES.
