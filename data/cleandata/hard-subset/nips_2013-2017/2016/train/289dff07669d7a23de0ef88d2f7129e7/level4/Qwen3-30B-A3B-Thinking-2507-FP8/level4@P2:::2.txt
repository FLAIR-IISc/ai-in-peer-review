

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces matrix square root computations with triangular Cholesky factor updates to reduce time and space complexity. The authors argue this approach maintains the algorithm's performance while achieving optimal O(μd²) runtime and storage complexity, verified through empirical comparisons on standard benchmark functions (e.g., Sphere, Rosenbrock). The work builds on prior efforts like Krause & Igel (2015), which introduced Cholesky updates for specialized CMA-ES variants, but claims theoretical justification for extending this to the standard CMA-ES via modifications to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism.

**Strengths:**  
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with a thorough background section contextualizing the problem within NIPS literature (e.g., Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001; Krause & Igel, 2015). Experimental results demonstrate significant practical benefits: Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable function evaluations to the reference CMA-ES-Ref (with delayed decomposition) but reduces wall-clock time by up to 20× at d=64 (Fig. 2), with speedup increasing with dimensionality. The storage complexity reduction (optimal O(d²) vs. O(d²) for CMA-ES-Ref) and free eigenvalue extraction from Cholesky factors are compelling advantages for large-scale applications like neural network training.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Originality:** The core method is not novel but a direct extension of Krause & Igel (2015), which already proposed Cholesky updates for CMA-ES variants. The paper fails to clarify how the *theoretical justification for CSA adaptation* (Sec. 3) advances beyond this prior work. Without demonstrating a *new* insight—e.g., why the error from replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ is provably negligible *for standard CMA-ES*—the contribution risks being perceived as incremental.  

2. **Quality & Theoretical Rigor:** The claim that "error decreases over time" (Sec. 3) lacks deterministic proof and relies on empirical validation. Hansen (2015) explicitly notes CSA’s affine invariance is *not* preserved under such modifications, yet the paper does not address this tension. Crucially:  
   - The equivalence to Eq. (2) is asserted but not proven (e.g., via algebraic derivation or reference to Krause & Igel’s framework).  
   - Complexity claims use O(μd²), but the reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) also operates in O(μd²) *with delays*. The paper must clarify *why* Cholesky-CMA-ES is faster *in practice* (e.g., by comparing constant factors, cache efficiency, or Θ(μd²) vs. Θ(μd² + d³) for explicit square roots). The discussion of cache effects at d=256 (Fig. 3) is a partial step but insufficient.  

3. **Significance:** While the speedup is promising, the paper does not sufficiently justify why this *replaces* standard CMA-ES in practice. The claim that it "should replace the standard CMA-ES if updating/storing the covariance matrix matters" overlooks trade-offs (e.g., negligible error *only* in converged regimes). For NIPS, significance requires demonstrating *broader impact*—e.g., enabling new applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning in large models) where prior CMA-ES was infeasible.  

**Specific Revisions Needed:**  
- **Originality:** Explicitly contrast Cholesky-CMA-ES’s CSA modification with Krause & Igel (2015). Clarify the *novel theoretical insight* (e.g., Lemma 1’s convergence argument for Et) that enables its use in *standard* CMA-ES.  
- **Quality:** Prove equivalence to Eq. (2) or provide a concise derivation. Replace O(μd²) with Θ(μd²) and rigorously compare constant factors against CMA-ES-Ref (e.g., "Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves Θ(μd²) with a 15× smaller constant due to cache-friendly updates").  
- **Validation:** Add experiments tracking the error metric (||Eₜ⁻¹Eₜ − I||) over iterations (not just final performance) to validate the "error decreases over time" claim.  
- **Minor:** Correct notation (e.g., "wₖ" → "αₖ" in Algorithm 2; "n" → "d" in Table 1; "F" → "f" in line 185).  

**Recommendation:**  
This paper addresses a valid practical bottleneck in CMA-ES but currently falls short of NIPS’s standards for originality and theoretical depth. The empirical results are strong, but the contribution lacks a *new* theoretical foundation or clear distinction from prior work. With revisions to clarify the novel insight (Sec. 3), rigorously justify complexity claims, and validate error dynamics, this could be a valuable contribution. As-is, it does not meet NIPS’s bar for significance and originality. **Reject** pending substantial revisions addressing the above points.
