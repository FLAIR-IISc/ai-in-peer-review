- Proposes replacing explicit covariance square root/inverse in CMA-ES with a triangular Cholesky factorization to avoid matrix square roots/inverses and reduce computational cost.
- Provides theoretical justification that the Cholesky modification preserves the algorithm’s search behavior and performance.
- Empirical results show reduced runtime complexity and wall-clock time without increasing the number of objective function evaluations.
- Overall assessment: a valuable and practical implementation of Cholesky factorization for CMA-ES.
- Requests clearer theoretical explanation for claimed numerical stability, specifically why maintaining/updating a Cholesky factor is more stable than computing explicit inverses or square roots.
- Asks for specific analyses: bounds on round-off error accumulation, conditioning advantages of triangular solves versus matrix inverses, and guarantees that positive definiteness is preserved under finite-precision updates.
- Requests discussion of implementation details affecting stability: required re-factorizations, jitter/regularization strategies, and how error propagation compares to eigendecomposition-based CMA-ES.
- Notes that benefits grow with problem dimension and encourages exploring integration with low-dimensional/low-rank approximations (e.g., Loshchilov 2015) for high-dimensional applications.
- Suggests discussing compatibility and potential hybrid designs (e.g., Cholesky on a principal subspace with a diagonal remainder) and expected complexity/accuracy trade-offs.
- Concludes the contribution is technically sound and practically relevant, but strengthened theoretical stability analysis and exploration of low-rank hybrids would improve rigor and applicability.
