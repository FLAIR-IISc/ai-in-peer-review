This manuscript presents a compelling advancement to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm, introducing the Cholesky-CMA-ES. The core innovation lies in approximating the covariance matrix update – typically O(d<sup>3</sup>) complexity, where *d* is the problem dimension – with a scheme operating at O(μd<sup>2</sup>), where μ is the population size (often O(log(d))). This is achieved by maintaining and updating a lower-triangular Cholesky decomposition instead of the full covariance matrix and its square root. The difference between Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES stems from omitting the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix, *E<sub>t</sub>*, in each iteration.

The authors provide an asymptotic argument, under specific assumptions regarding the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices, demonstrating that *E<sub>t</sub>* converges to the identity matrix, implying equivalence between the two schemes in the limit.  The practical validity and speed-up afforded by Cholesky-CMA-ES are convincingly illustrated through standard runtime and convergence plots across benchmark objective functions, and comparative performance against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES implementations that employ covariance update delay strategies.

This work represents a significant contribution to accelerating the CMA-ES algorithm, widely regarded as a leading method for non-convex, black-box optimization problems where limited prior knowledge of the objective function's topology exists and gradient information is unavailable or unhelpful. The proposed method achieves substantial speed improvements without compromising optimization performance on the tested synthetic benchmarks. 

However, a common challenge with CMA-ES speed-up proposals lies in the practical dominance of the black-box oracle’s runtime.  In many applications, the time spent evaluating the objective function vastly outweighs the computational cost of the CMA-ES update itself. Furthermore, the dimensionality of problems commonly tackled with CMA-ES often remains relatively low (tens of variables). Consequently, the benefits of a faster CMA-ES update are most pronounced when function evaluations are extremely fast (on the order of milliseconds or less) and dimensionality is high – a scenario which, while targeted here, may be less frequent in practical applications. 

Despite the strong empirical results, strengthening the theoretical and empirical grounding would be beneficial. As rigorous theoretical guarantees are difficult to obtain for complex algorithms like CMA-ES, additional empirical analysis is warranted. I propose the following experiments:

1. **Evolution of *E<sub>t</sub>***: Conduct an experiment tracking the evolution of *E<sub>t</sub>* along selected optimization trajectories. Simultaneously run the full CMA-ES and the Cholesky-CMA-ES approximation and compare the *E<sub>t</sub>* matrices in various norms. This would provide direct empirical evidence of the fluctuations of *E<sub>t</sub>* over time and support the asymptotic convergence argument.

2. **Baseline CMA-ES Performance:** For completeness, include runtime and performance results for the standard CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies on the presented benchmark functions. This provides a clear baseline against which to assess the improvements of both the delayed update strategies and the Cholesky-CMA-ES.

3. **Spectral Analysis on Quadratic Functions:**  Investigate performance on quadratic functions with systematically varied spectra of quadratic forms (e.g., following the approach in Stich & Mueller, PPSN 2012).  Compare the solution quality and the error in *E<sub>t</sub>* as a function of the spectral distribution. This would provide insights into the algorithm's behavior under well-defined conditions and potentially reveal sensitivities to ill-conditioning.

4. **Real-World Application:** Complement the simulation results with a real-world experiment where computational speed is critical. Policy gradient methods in real-time robotics applications could be a suitable choice, offering a parameter space that isn’t excessively large, but where faster optimization can significantly impact performance.



Overall, this is a well-written and significant paper. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a promising path towards scaling CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems and applications where fast optimization is essential. Addressing the suggested experimental extensions would further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution.
