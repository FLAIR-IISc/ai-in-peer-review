This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce its computational and storage complexity. The standard CMA-ES algorithm's performance bottleneck is often the `O(d^3)` eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix `C`, which is required in every iteration. The authors propose replacing this step with a more efficient `O(d^2)` update of the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. The main technical challenge lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the matrix square root `C^{-1/2}`. The authors' key contribution is to replace this term with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor, `A^{-1}`, and provide a theoretical justification arguing that as the algorithm converges, the error introduced by this approximation becomes negligible. Empirical results on several benchmark functions demonstrate that the proposed "Cholesky-CMA-ES" achieves a significant speedup in wall-clock time compared to the standard implementation, without increasing the number of function evaluations required to find a solution.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and critical limitation of CMA-ES, one of the most powerful derivative-free optimization algorithms with numerous applications in machine learning. Improving its scalability is an important and impactful research problem.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling, showing a substantial reduction in runtime, especially for higher-dimensional problems. The reported speedup (e.g., 20x for d=64) is impressive and clearly demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method.
*   **Potential Impact:** If the method's theoretical underpinnings are sound, it has the potential to become the new standard implementation for CMA-ES, making the algorithm accessible for higher-dimensional problems where it was previously computationally infeasible.

**Weaknesses and Major Concerns**

Despite the promising results, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed.

1.  **Limited Originality:** My main concern is that the work appears to be an incremental extension of previous research, specifically Krause and Igel (2015), "A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies." The method presented seems to directly apply the rank-one Cholesky update from that paper to the rank-1 and rank-µ updates of the standard CMA-ES. The paper needs to more clearly articulate its novel contributions beyond this extension. If my understanding is incorrect, I would welcome clarification from the authors.

2.  **Missing Derivation and Incomplete Analysis:** The paper is missing some crucial details that are essential for evaluating its claims.
    *   **Equivalence of Updates:** The authors should explicitly show how the series of rank-one updates applied to the Cholesky factor `A` in Algorithm 1 is equivalent to the covariance matrix update rule in Equation (2). While this may have been proven in prior work and seems plausible, its inclusion is vital for the paper to be self-contained and for the reader to verify the method's correctness.
    *   **Complexity Analysis:** The paper claims an `O(µd^2)` complexity, which is technically the same big-O class as the amortized complexity of the reference CMA-ES implementation that performs a full decomposition every `d` steps. To properly justify the dramatic speedup seen in experiments, the analysis should be more detailed. A discussion using `Θ` notation, or a clearer comparison of the per-iteration costs versus the amortized costs, would make the theoretical advantage much more explicit.

3.  **Insufficient Validation of the Core Approximation:** The central theoretical argument of the paper—that replacing `C^{-1/2}` with `A^{-1}` in the CSA path update does not harm performance—is not sufficiently supported.
    *   The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 relies on the convergence of the (shape of the) covariance matrix `C_t`, a condition that is not guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm. The argument that the error is small if `C_t` changes slowly is an informal one.
    *   This approximation is a critical modification to the standard algorithm, and claims about its validity require strong evidence. However, the experiments only provide an indirect validation by comparing the final number of function evaluations. To convincingly support the paper's main claim, direct empirical validation is necessary. For example, the authors could conduct an experiment that measures the norm of the CSA path vector `p_σ` under both the original and the proposed formulation, and show that the difference is indeed small and diminishes over the course of an optimization run. This is especially important as, to my knowledge, this claim may run contrary to established understanding regarding the CSA mechanism's invariance properties.

**Minor Comments**

*   Please check the notation carefully. For instance, in Algorithm 2, `wk` appears to be a typo for `αk`. In Table 1, the dimension is denoted `n` but should be `d` for consistency.
*   Line 185: There is a typo, 'F' should be 'f'.

**Conclusion**

While the paper tackles an important problem and presents promising empirical results, the concerns regarding its originality, the lack of essential derivations and a more rigorous complexity analysis, and the insufficient validation of its central theoretical claim are too significant to overlook. In its current form, I do not believe the paper meets the high standards for publication at NIPS. Addressing the major concerns outlined above would be necessary to make this a solid contribution.
