The paper proposes a variant of CMA-ES with a focus on reduced covariance update and storage complexity, termed “Cholesky-CMA-ES.” The authors argue that this modification maintains the algorithm’s behavior while reducing computational cost. However, the naming choice is misleading; “Cholesky-CMA-ES” was previously introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a similar algorithm and is well-established in the literature. This prior work is not acknowledged, and the authors instead present their variant as “Our CMA-ES variant, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES.” The proposed method builds upon the work of Krause et al. (2015), with the primary algorithmic contribution being the repeated application of the `rankOneUpdate` function (also present in Krause et al.’s work) to all individuals, rather than the single application used in Plus strategies. 

Empirical results on the Cigar function (Figure b) show a discrepancy in the observed plateau objective function values between CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES, potentially due to the method used for plotting median results. The paper should include a comparison to both the Cholesky-CMA-ES of Suttorp et al. (empirically) and a detailed algorithmic comparison to the Cholesky-CMA-ES of Krause et al. The claim of “Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity” is somewhat misleading; it does not represent an optimal solution for all problems, such as the Sphere function where the covariance matrix is unnecessary, or separable functions where only a diagonal approximation is sufficient. Furthermore, the proposed approach is only a constant factor (two times) cheaper than the Cholesky CMA-ES of Suttorp et al. While the paper claims the latter is prone to numerical instabilities, the original publication suggested the opposite. The omission of Suttorp et al.’s method from Figure 3 is particularly problematic, as it obscures the true contribution of the present work.

Although the proposed approach likely does not fundamentally alter CMA-ES’s capabilities regarding large-scale optimization—given its continued quadratic complexity—I acknowledge that it may represent the most effective approach to saving time and memory when utilizing the full covariance matrix. Overall, the work appears incremental; algorithmically, the core contribution is essentially a single “for” loop. I recommend the following revisions: a) Resolve the naming conflict; b) Include experiments comparing the proposed method to the variants of Suttorp et al. and Krause et al., and update all three figures accordingly. If the resulting figures become overly complex, present only the best-performing variant in the main text and include the full figures in the Supplementary Material; c) Clearly articulate that the main algorithmic contribution is the “for” loop in Algorithm 1, or provide justification for why this is not the case.

Minor comment: The statement “Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step” is inaccurate. In practice, and in well-implemented CMA-ES algorithms, eigenvalue decomposition is performed periodically, not in every iteration.
