The manuscript proposes a new CMA-ES update scheme, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation (O(d^3)) with a scheme running in O(mu d^2), where d is the problem dimension and mu is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log d)). The principal idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix (and its corresponding square root). The key difference from standard CMA-ES is that Cholesky-CMA-ES does not estimate a (random) orthogonal matrix E_t at each step. Under fairly restrictive assumptions—specifically, that a sequence of covariance matrices converges to an unknown target covariance matrix—the authors provide an asymptotic argument that E_t converges to the identity, implying the two schemes become equivalent.

The paper demonstrates the validity and speed-up of Cholesky-CMA-ES using standard runtime and convergence plots on selected test functions and by comparing against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that defer full covariance updates by different mechanisms. Overall, this is a solid contribution to accelerating the celebrated CMA-ES, which is arguably among the best options for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the objective’s structure and derivative information is inaccessible or unhelpful. The proposed method substantially reduces computational cost without degrading optimization performance on the synthetic benchmarks.

That said, a general caveat for any CMA-ES speed-up is that, in practice, total runtime is often dominated by the black-box oracle (the objective function), making CMA-ES’s own overhead negligible. Moreover, CMA-ES is frequently applied to problems with only a few tens of variables. The runtime of CMA-ES itself becomes critical mainly when single function evaluations take milliseconds or less and the dimensionality is higher—the scenario targeted here, which may be less common in practice.

Because strong theoretical guarantees are notoriously difficult for algorithms as intricate as CMA-ES, I suggest the following additional empirical studies to strengthen the results:
- Track the evolution of E_t along selected optimization runs, e.g., in different norms. An experiment where the full CMA-ES is executed while the approximation is computed in parallel would allow a direct comparison and reveal the temporal fluctuations of E_t.
- For completeness, report actual runtimes and performance of vanilla CMA-ES (without delay strategies) within this contribution.
- On quadratic functions, conduct a simulation study where the spectrum of the quadratic form is varied continuously (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012), and compare both solution quality and the error in E_t as a function of the spectral distribution.
- Include a real-world experiment to complement the simulations—for instance, policy-gradient methods in real-time robotics, where speed indeed matters and the parameter space is not too large.
