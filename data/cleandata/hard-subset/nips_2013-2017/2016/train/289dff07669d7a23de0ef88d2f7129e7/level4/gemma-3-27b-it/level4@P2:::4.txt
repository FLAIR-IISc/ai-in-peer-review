This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) leveraging the triangular Cholesky factorization to avoid costly square root computations during covariance matrix updates. The core idea is to replace the matrix square root used in the standard CMA-ES with its Cholesky factor, leading to a reduction in runtime complexity, particularly in high-dimensional problems. The authors provide a theoretical justification for why this approximation maintains the algorithm’s performance and demonstrate empirical results showing significant speedups in wall-clock time without increasing the number of objective function evaluations. This work builds upon prior efforts to accelerate CMA-ES, such as those employing delayed updates [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001] or restricted covariance models [Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008], and offers a compelling alternative with strong theoretical grounding. It's related to recent work utilizing Cholesky factors [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015], but extends these approaches to the full standard CMA-ES.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a clear computational advantage, achieving optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity for full covariance matrix adaptation. The theoretical analysis addressing the potential impact on performance is a significant contribution, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits, particularly as dimensionality increases. The code contribution within the Shark library is also valuable, facilitating reproducibility and adoption. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, effectively explaining the technical details of the proposed method.

However, some areas could benefit from further clarification. While the authors state the computation is "numerically more stable," a more detailed explanation of *why* this is the case would be beneficial. The reasoning behind the approximation introduced by replacing the matrix square root with its Cholesky factor in the step-size adaptation could be expanded upon.  Although the paper acknowledges the quadratic scaling with dimensionality, a discussion of strategies for handling extremely high-dimensional problems, such as combining the approach with low-dimensional approximations [Loshchilov, 2015], would enhance its practical relevance, especially for applications like neural network training. The comparison with other CMA-ES variants, particularly Suttorp-CMA-ES, is well-presented, but further analysis of scenarios where the proposed method might *not* outperform alternatives would strengthen the evaluation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant speedup:** The Cholesky-CMA-ES demonstrably reduces runtime, especially in higher dimensions, without sacrificing performance.
*   **Strong theoretical justification:** The paper provides a solid theoretical basis for the proposed approximation.
*   **Practical implementation:** The availability of the code within the Shark library promotes reproducibility and wider adoption.
*   **Addresses a key bottleneck:** The work directly tackles a major computational cost in CMA-ES – covariance matrix updates.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Quadratic scaling:** The algorithm’s quadratic complexity still limits its applicability to extremely high-dimensional problems.
*   **Limited discussion of limitations:** A more comprehensive discussion of scenarios where the method might be less effective would be valuable.
*   **Minor clarity issues:** Further elaboration on the numerical stability and approximation details would improve understanding.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a practical and theoretically sound improvement to the standard CMA-ES algorithm. While there are minor areas for improvement, the benefits clearly outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
