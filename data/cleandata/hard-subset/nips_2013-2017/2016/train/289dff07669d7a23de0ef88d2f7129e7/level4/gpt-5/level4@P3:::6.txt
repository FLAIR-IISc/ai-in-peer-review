Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a CMA-ES variant that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C rather than C itself, and modifies cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) by replacing C−1/2 with A−1. The authors argue that because the triangular factor changes smoothly and the induced sequence of rotation matrices converges (under mild assumptions), the modification does not alter the number of objective-function evaluations required. They provide a quadratic-time rank-one update procedure for A, yielding an O(µ d^2) per-iteration update (with µ the effective parental set size), optimal O(d^2) storage, and inexpensive access to eigenvalues and A−1. Empirically, on standard benchmarks (including rotated versions) up to d = 256, the method reportedly requires roughly the same number of evaluations as a reference CMA-ES while achieving substantial wall-clock speedups.

The work builds directly on prior efforts to reduce the cost of covariance adaptation: periodic decomposition [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001; Hansen, 2015], structural restrictions (diagonal/separable) [Ros and Hansen, 2008; Loshchilov, 2014, 2015], and factor-based updates. In particular, Suttorp et al. (2009) proposed a “Cholesky-CMA-ES” that maintains a non-triangular factor, and Krause and Igel (2015) provided a fast rank-one update for triangular Cholesky factors in special CMA-ES variants (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES). The present paper extends triangular-factor rank-one updates to the standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 and adapts CSA to avoid computing C−1/2.

Strengths
- Practical contribution: For full-covariance CMA-ES, the proposed approach reduces runtime and memory without degrading evaluation efficiency, which is valuable in practice when function evaluations are inexpensive relative to matrix updates.
- Sound engineering: Triangular factors give optimal storage; diagonal elements provide eigenvalues for free; A−1 can be computed in O(d^2), and the update routine is standard and numerically stable.
- Theoretical support: The argument that the CSA substitution induces only a vanishing error as the covariance shape stabilizes is plausible and well-motivated (though not a full convergence proof).
- Empirical results: Broad benchmark suite, rotation-invariance checks, and scaling studies showing large wall-clock gains (e.g., >20× at moderate d).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Naming and attribution: The term “Cholesky-CMA-ES” was introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a related algorithm. The paper should clearly acknowledge this and distinguish triangular vs non-triangular factor variants in the title and text to avoid confusion.
- Incremental novelty: Algorithmically, the main change relative to Krause and Igel (2015) is to apply the rank-one update repeatedly to realize the rank-µ term and to approximate CSA by using A−1. This is a useful extension but conceptually modest.
- Scope of “optimal” claims: The title and abstract risk overgeneralization. The O(µ d^2) time and O(d^2) storage are optimal only when adapting the full covariance; for separable problems a diagonal update is O(d) and more appropriate. The paper later notes this, but the messaging should be tightened.
- Baselines and fairness: The paper mentions an adaptation of Suttorp-CMA-ES, but some figures appear to omit it (e.g., Figure 3). To assess the true gain, please include consistent comparisons against both Suttorp et al. (non-triangular factor) and a carefully tuned delayed-decomposition CMA-ES across all plots. Algorithmic differences relative to Krause and Igel (2015) should be made explicit.
- Numerical stability claims: The paper claims non-triangular factor updates are “prone to numerical instabilities,” whereas Suttorp et al. reported the opposite. This should be resolved with empirical evidence (e.g., conditioning, breakdown rates, and accuracy of reconstructed C) and citations.
- CSA approximation analysis: The lemma assumes convergence of the normalized covariance shape; however, CMA-ES is randomized and may not strictly satisfy this. It would help to quantify the finite-time error induced by replacing C−1/2 with A−1, e.g., by reporting the angle between consecutive rotation matrices, or an ablation that uses exact CSA intermittently to bound any drift.
- Experimental details: On Cigar, the plateau height differs between methods. Please clarify how medians are computed and include dispersion (IQR or error bars). Also discuss success rates on Rosenbrock (trials excluded) and whether the CSA modification affects step-size dynamics or invariance in practice.
- Clarity and references: The statement that “typically, an eigenvalue decomposition … in each update step” is misleading; most implementations decompose periodically. Several references have formatting issues and should be corrected.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The algorithmic core (rank-one updates for A, CSA approximation) is technically sound, and the complexity claims are correct for full covariance. Theoretical justification is heuristic but reasonable. Experiments are solid overall, though baselines need to be consistently included and analyzed with dispersion.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but the distinction from prior “Cholesky-CMA-ES” work, the scope of “optimal,” and reference formatting require improvement. Algorithm 2 could benefit from a short derivation or pointer to standard Cholesky rank-one update literature.
- Originality: Limited. The work extends known triangular-factor updates to the standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 and modifies CSA, accompanied by a useful but modest theoretical argument.
- Significance: Moderate. The method is a practical speed/memory improvement for full-covariance CMA-ES but does not change the asymptotic barrier for large-scale settings.

Recommendations and requested revisions
- Resolve the naming/attribution issue; consider a distinct name (e.g., Triangular-Cholesky CMA-ES) and clearly position relative to Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015).
- Include comprehensive empirical comparisons with Suttorp-CMA-ES (non-triangular) and a strong delayed-decomposition CMA-ES across all figures; if plots become cluttered, show best performers in the main text and full comparisons in the supplement.
- Clarify the algorithmic novelty beyond looping rank-one updates; if the CSA modification is the main conceptual contribution, state this plainly and analyze it (finite-time error metrics, ablation).
- Calibrate “optimal” claims to the full-covariance setting and discuss when diagonal/limited-memory variants are preferable.
- Provide evidence regarding numerical stability, or rephrase claims to match prior reports.
- Fix minor clarity issues and references; correct the statement about decomposition frequency; add dispersion and success-rate stats; discuss the Cigar plateau discrepancy.

Pros
- Simple, practical improvement with substantial runtime and memory benefits for full-covariance CMA-ES.
- Plausible theoretical justification for CSA modification.
- Broad empirical evaluation and strong observed speedups.

Cons
- Incremental novelty; potential naming confusion with prior “Cholesky-CMA-ES.”
- Incomplete/uneven baseline comparisons and limited analysis of CSA approximation error.
- Overly broad “optimal” messaging outside the full-covariance regime.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject in current form due to incremental novelty and missing/ambiguous baselines and naming. With the requested clarifications and comparisons, this could become a solid practical contribution.
