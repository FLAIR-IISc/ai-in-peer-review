The manuscript introduces a novel update scheme for the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), referred to as the Cholesky-CMA-ES. This scheme approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation, which has a complexity of O(d^3), using a novel approach with a complexity of O(μd^2), where d is the dimension of the optimization problem and μ is the number of samples per iteration, typically O(log(d)). The core idea behind this update is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix instead of a full covariance matrix. The primary difference between the Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES lies in the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix Et in every step.

Under certain restrictive assumptions regarding the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target covariance matrix, the authors provide an asymptotic argument that the sequence of Et will converge to an identity matrix, thereby rendering the two schemes equivalent. The validity and efficiency of the Cholesky-CMA-ES are demonstrated through standard runtime and convergence plots on selected objective functions, as well as comparisons to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives that delay the full covariance matrix update by different means.

This paper makes a significant contribution to accelerating the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm, which is arguably the best algorithm for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the topology of the objective function and first or higher-order information is difficult to access or not helpful. The proposed method substantially speeds up the algorithm without compromising optimization performance on the synthetic test cases. However, a common weakness of any speed-up proposal for CMA-ES is that, in practice, the overall optimization process is often dominated by the runtime of the black-box oracle (the objective function), and the actual runtime of the CMA-ES is frequently negligible in comparison.

Furthermore, in typical scenarios where CMA-ES is employed, the dimensionality of the problems rarely exceeds a few tens of variables. Only in cases where a single function evaluation is on the order of milliseconds or smaller and the dimensionality is higher does the runtime of CMA-ES play a crucial role, which is the targeted scenario in this paper. While theoretical guarantees are notoriously challenging to obtain for complex schemes like CMA-ES, I propose several additional empirical experiments and plots to strengthen the presentation of the results.

Firstly, it would be instructive to visualize the evolution of Et on selected optimization trajectories, e.g., in different norms. An experiment where the full CMA-ES is used and the approximation is calculated simultaneously and compared would provide further empirical evidence about the actual fluctuations of Et over time. Secondly, including actual runtimes and performance of CMA-ES without delay strategies would enhance the completeness of the contribution. Thirdly, although the used test functions are standard in the evolutionary optimization community, a simulation study on quadratic functions with a continuously varied spectrum of quadratic forms (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012) would be beneficial. This would enable a comparison of the solution quality and error in E_t in dependence of the spectral distribution.

Lastly, a real-world experiment accompanying the present simulation results would be valuable. For instance, policy gradients in real-time robotics applications could be a suitable choice, where speed is crucial and the parameter space is not excessively large.
