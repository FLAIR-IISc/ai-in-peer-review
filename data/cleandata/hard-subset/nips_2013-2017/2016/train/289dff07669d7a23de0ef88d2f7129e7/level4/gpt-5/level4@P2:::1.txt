Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix instead of the full matrix or its square root. The key change is to update the factor in O(µ d^2) time via a sequence of rank-one updates, yielding optimal asymptotic storage and runtime for full-covariance adaptation. To make this compatible with CMA-ES’s cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), the authors replace the inverse matrix square root with the inverse triangular factor. They justify this approximation by showing that the induced rotational discrepancy vanishes as the covariance shape stabilizes, so the effect on CSA decays over time. Empirically, across standard black-box benchmarks (with and without rotations) and dimensions up to 256, Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the standard CMA-ES in function evaluations while substantially reducing wall-clock time, with speedups that increase with dimension. Comparisons include a reference CMA-ES with delayed decomposition, a version with d-step delay, and an adapted non-triangular Cholesky update.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on updating factorizations rather than the covariance directly (e.g., Suttorp et al.) and on more recent rank-one Cholesky updates for special CMA-ES variants (Krause & Igel). The novelty is an extension to the standard CMA-ES with a theoretically motivated modification of CSA, along with a practical, numerically stable algorithm that preserves performance in evaluations.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The factor-update derivation and the rank-one update algorithm are clear and appropriate. The CSA modification is well-motivated: the analysis shows that the rotational error disappears as the covariance shape converges and is further mitigated by the exponential forgetting in CSA. That said, the argument relies on stability of the covariance shape; formal finite-time bounds or robustness analysis under nonstationary regimes would strengthen the claims.
- Empirical validation: The experiments are well chosen and consistent with the stated goals (same evaluations, reduced runtime). The rotation-invariance tests and multiple dimensions are appropriate. It would help to detail experimental parity to substantiate the runtime advantages (see minor comments).
- A potential accuracy issue: the manuscript claims that the diagonal elements of the triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the covariance. In general, diag(L) corresponds to Cholesky pivots; eigenvalues of C = L L^T are not given by diag(L)^2 except in special cases. Please revise the statement (one still gains numerically stable access to L and L^{-1}, which is useful for conditioning control).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the theoretical justification is concise. A few clarifications would improve readability and reproducibility:
- Specify implementation details used for timing: same language/compiler, BLAS/LAPACK versions, threading, CPU/NUMA settings, memory allocation strategies, and whether all variants share the same codebase except for the update routines.
- Explain the observation in Figure 2 where speedup appears less pronounced on Cigar/Discus at d = 256 (e.g., cache effects, memory bandwidth limits, µ scaling, or algorithmic specifics of those functions).
- State how µ scales with d in all experiments and whether µ affects observed speedups.
- Report per-iteration breakdown (sampling, ranking, covariance/factor updates) to attribute runtime differences.
- Clarify rotation application (one fixed random rotation per run) and provide seeds/configs for reproducibility.
- Consider releasing code or scripts.

Originality
The extension of factor-based updates to the standard CMA-ES with a principled CSA modification and a convergence-based justification is novel and practical. This is a meaningful advance over prior Cholesky-update methods that were limited to specific CMA-ES variants or required non-triangular factors with drawbacks.

Significance
The contribution is important for medium-to-large dimensional full-covariance CMA-ES. Achieving O(µ d^2) updates with reduced memory and good numerical properties can make CMA-ES more practical in ML tasks (e.g., policy search, hyperparameter tuning) where function evaluations are not extremely expensive but dimensions are sizable. The paper also carefully delineates the limits: for very high dimensions, full-covariance updates remain quadratic and limited-memory approaches may be preferable.

Pros
- Clear, principled algorithmic contribution with strong practical impact.
- Asymptotically optimal storage and improved runtime complexity for full covariance.
- Theoretical justification for the CSA modification and why its effect is small.
- Extensive experiments showing identical evaluation counts and significant runtime gains.
- Numerically stable access to L and L^{-1}; practical for conditioning control.

Cons
- Theoretical analysis is asymptotic and assumes stabilizing covariance shape; finite-time guarantees are not provided.
- Runtime comparisons would benefit from more detailed parity controls and breakdowns.
- The claim about “getting eigenvalues for free” via the Cholesky factor is inaccurate and should be corrected.
- Limited discussion of behavior when d is very large and cache/memory bandwidth dominates.

Minor suggestions
- Document implementation comparability (language, libraries, BLAS, threading, hardware) to ensure fair timing.
- Add an explanation for diminished speedup on Cigar/Discus at d = 256 (likely cache/L2 effects or function-structure-induced µ/iteration differences).
- Include an ablation isolating the CSA modification’s impact (e.g., measuring the rotation error term over time).
- Consider additional baselines such as limited-memory CMA-ES (LM-CMA) to contextualize when full-covariance Cholesky-CMA-ES remains advantageous.
- Provide more details on numerical stability safeguards (e.g., lower bounds on diagonal entries, jitter additions).

Overall evaluation
A strong, well-executed contribution. The method is practically valuable, theoretically motivated, and carefully evaluated. With minor clarifications and a correction regarding eigenvalues, I recommend acceptance.
