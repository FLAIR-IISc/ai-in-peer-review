This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a prominent algorithm in derivative-free optimization. The primary contribution is a new variant, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES, that significantly reduces the time and memory complexity associated with updating and storing the covariance matrix.

**Summary of the Paper**

The CMA-ES algorithm's performance is often limited by the O(d^3) complexity of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix in each iteration, which is necessary for sampling new candidate solutions and for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. This paper proposes to circumvent this by directly maintaining and updating a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. While updating Cholesky factors in O(d^2) time has been explored before, a key challenge has been the CSA update, which requires the inverse of the matrix square root (C^{-1/2}), not just any factor.

The authors' core innovation is to approximate C^{-1/2} with the inverse of the lower triangular Cholesky factor (A^{-1}) within the CSA update. They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) arguing that this approximation is sound. Specifically, they show that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the sequence of rotation matrices that relate the matrix square root to the Cholesky factor also converges, causing the error introduced by the approximation to diminish over time. This insight allows them to achieve an asymptotically optimal O(μd^2) update complexity and O(d^2) storage complexity.

The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is empirically evaluated against the standard implementation of CMA-ES (with delayed updates) and another Cholesky-based variant on a suite of standard benchmark functions. The results demonstrate that the new method achieves a significant reduction in wall-clock time, especially in higher dimensions, without compromising the optimization performance in terms of the number of function evaluations required to find a solution.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and critical limitation of one of the most powerful derivative-free optimization algorithms. The proposed solution is not merely an incremental improvement but a fundamental enhancement that makes CMA-ES more practical for medium-to-large scale problems (where d is in the hundreds). Given the widespread use of CMA-ES in machine learning for tasks like direct policy search and hyperparameter tuning, this contribution is highly relevant and likely to have a substantial impact. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES has a strong potential to become the new default implementation.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The authors clearly identify the specific challenge of adapting the CSA mechanism and provide a well-reasoned theoretical argument for why their approximation is justified. The argument based on the convergence of the RQ-decomposition is elegant and provides confidence that the method is principled, not just an ad-hoc heuristic.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the background on CMA-ES is concise and sufficient, and the proposed method is explained with precision. The distinction between their approach and previous attempts to use Cholesky factorizations is well-articulated.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive. The authors compare their method against appropriate baselines on a diverse set of benchmark functions designed to test different aspects of the optimization process. The inclusion of both function evaluation counts and wall-clock time effectively demonstrates that the computational speedup comes with no loss in sample efficiency.

5.  **Practical Advantages:** Beyond the primary complexity improvements, the paper correctly highlights other practical benefits, such as obtaining the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix (useful for monitoring conditioning) at no extra cost and the numerical stability of updating a single triangular factor.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The weaknesses of this paper are minor and mainly relate to providing additional details for clarity and reproducibility.

1.  **Comparability of Implementations:** The wall-clock time comparisons are central to the paper's claims. To strengthen these results, the authors should clarify the specifics of the implementations. For instance, were all compared algorithms implemented in the same language (e.g., C++ using the Shark library)? Were they compiled with identical optimization settings and linked against the same high-performance linear algebra libraries (e.g., LAPACK, BLAS)? This information is crucial for ensuring a fair comparison.

2.  **Discussion of High-Dimensionality Results:** In Section 4, the authors note that for d=256, the matrix size exceeded the L2 cache, affecting performance. This is a good insight. However, in Figure 2, the plots for the Cigar and Discus functions show that the speedup of Cholesky-CMA-ES relative to the Suttorp-CMA-ES variant appears less pronounced at d=256 than at d=128. It would be beneficial for the authors to comment on this specific observation. Could this be due to different memory access patterns between the triangular and non-triangular update algorithms, leading to different cache-miss behaviors? A brief discussion would add depth to the analysis.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Solves a major computational bottleneck in a state-of-the-art optimization algorithm.
    *   The proposed method is efficient, practical, and achieves asymptotically optimal complexity.
    *   Supported by both a sound theoretical justification and strong, comprehensive empirical evidence.
    *   The paper is clear, well-written, and the contribution is of high significance to the NIPS community.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The weaknesses are very minor and pertain to clarifying experimental details, which can be easily addressed in a final version. There are no significant flaws in the methodology or conclusions.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a valuable and impactful contribution to the field of optimization. It is technically strong, clearly presented, and thoroughly validated.
