This paper proposes a modification to the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) leveraging the triangular Cholesky factorization to avoid the computationally expensive calculation of the matrix square root. The core idea is to replace the inverse of the square root of the covariance matrix with the inverse of the Cholesky factor, accompanied by a modification to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The authors provide a theoretical justification for why this substitution maintains the algorithm’s performance and demonstrate through experiments that it reduces runtime complexity, particularly in higher dimensions, without significantly altering the number of objective function evaluations. This work builds upon previous attempts to use Cholesky factors within CMA-ES [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015], addressing a limitation of those approaches—the inability to directly replace the matrix square root required by the standard CMA-ES. The paper appropriately contextualizes the CMA-ES within the broader field of derivative-free optimization [Beyer, 2007; Eiben and Smith, 2015] and its applications in machine learning, such as reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning [Gomez et al., 2008; Heidrich-Meisner and Igel, 2009a,b; Igel, 2010].

The strengths of this work are numerous. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a significant improvement in computational efficiency, especially as dimensionality increases, as clearly demonstrated by the experimental results. The theoretical justification for the modification to the CSA mechanism is a valuable contribution, providing confidence in the algorithm’s behavior. The authors also correctly identify the asymptotic optimality of the proposed approach. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details, with connections made to relevant prior work. The experimental setup is comprehensive, comparing the new method against several variants of the CMA-ES on a range of benchmark functions.

However, some weaknesses could be addressed. While the authors mention numerical stability, a more detailed explanation of *how* the Cholesky factorization leads to improved stability compared to eigenvalue decomposition would be beneficial. The discussion of the error introduced by replacing  `st` with `ŝt` could be expanded, potentially with an empirical analysis of the error’s magnitude in practice.  The conclusion briefly mentions the potential need for low-dimensional approximations for extremely high-dimensional problems [Loshchilov, 2015], but a more thorough discussion of how this approach might integrate with the Cholesky-CMA-ES would strengthen the work. For example, could the Cholesky factorization be applied to a reduced subspace?  The experiments are robust but limited to the benchmark functions provided. Considering more complex real-world applications, particularly within machine learning, would further validate the approach’s practical utility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant reduction in runtime complexity, particularly for high-dimensional problems.
*   Strong theoretical justification for the proposed modification.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method and experimental results.
*   Addresses a key bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES (covariance matrix update).
*   Empirical results demonstrate comparable performance in terms of function evaluations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The improvement is primarily in runtime, not in the number of function evaluations, which may not be critical for all applications.
*   The discussion of numerical stability could be more detailed.
*   The potential integration with low-dimensional approximation techniques is not fully explored.
*   Limited evaluation on real-world applications outside of standard benchmark functions.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES is a practical and theoretically sound improvement to a widely used algorithm. While some minor improvements could be made, the benefits outweigh the drawbacks, and I recommend acceptance.
