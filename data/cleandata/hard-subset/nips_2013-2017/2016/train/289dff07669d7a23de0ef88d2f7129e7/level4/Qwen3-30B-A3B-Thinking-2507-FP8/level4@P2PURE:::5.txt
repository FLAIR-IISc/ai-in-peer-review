

The manuscript introduces a novel CMA-ES update scheme, Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the O(d³) covariance matrix adaptation with an O(μd²) method (where d is the problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration, typically O(log d)). The approach maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix and its square root. The key distinction from standard CMA-ES arises from omitting the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix E_t at each step. Under restrictive assumptions—specifically, convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target—the authors provide an asymptotic argument that {E_t} converges to the identity matrix, rendering the schemes equivalent in the limit. The validity and speedup are demonstrated via standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark functions, comparing against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates. This work makes a solid contribution to accelerating CMA-ES, a leading algorithm for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the objective's structure and derivative information is unavailable. The proposed method significantly reduces runtime without degrading performance on synthetic test cases. However, a general limitation of CMA-ES speed-up proposals is that the optimization process is often dominated by black-box oracle runtime (objective evaluations), making CMA-ES runtime negligible in practice. This scenario is relevant only when function evaluations are sub-millisecond and dimensionality is high—conditions that may be less common in typical applications where problem dimensions rarely exceed a few tens. Given the difficulty of establishing theoretical guarantees for CMA-ES, I recommend three additional empirical analyses to strengthen the results: (1) Track the evolution of E_t during optimization trajectories (e.g., in different norms) by simultaneously computing the approximation alongside full CMA-ES; (2) Include runtime and performance metrics for standard CMA-ES without delay strategies for completeness; (3) Extend the quadratic function experiments to a spectral variation study (as in Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012), varying the spectrum of quadratic forms and reporting solution quality and E_t error dependence on spectral distribution. Finally, a real-world application—such as reinforcement learning policies in real-time robotics with moderate-sized parameter spaces where speed is critical—would further validate the method's practical impact.
