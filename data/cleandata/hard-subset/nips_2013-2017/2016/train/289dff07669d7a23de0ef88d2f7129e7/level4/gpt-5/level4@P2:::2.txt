Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix instead of recomputing the covariance and its eigendecomposition. The core idea is to perform quadratic-time rank-one updates directly on the Cholesky factor for both the rank-1 and rank-µ covariance terms, and to modify cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) by replacing C^{-1/2} with the inverse of the triangular factor A^{-1}. A theoretical argument (via an RQ/polar decomposition perspective) suggests that the rotation error introduced by using A^{-1} in place of C^{-1/2} is small and vanishes as the covariance shape stabilizes. Empirically, the method matches the standard CMA-ES in function evaluations and yields large wall-clock speedups, especially in higher dimensions, while reducing memory usage and providing eigenvalue information “for free.”

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on two lines of work: (i) updating decompositions rather than the full covariance, notably Suttorp et al. (2009), and (ii) efficient rank-one Cholesky updates, as in Krause & Igel (2015), which treated special cases (e.g., µ = λ = 1 or specific CMA variants). The claimed novelty is extending quadratic-time Cholesky updates to the full, standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and providing a justification for the CSA modification that avoids computing C^{-1/2}, plus empirical evidence that this does not degrade performance.

Strengths
- Practical impact: large, consistent speedups in wall-clock time without increasing function evaluations, with benefits growing in dimension.
- Sound engineering: quadratic-time, numerically stable updates to a triangular factor; optimal storage; easy access to eigenvalues; efficient inversion of the triangular factor.
- Empirical breadth: multiple standard benchmarks with rotations; scaling to higher dimensions than typical CMA-ES studies.
- Clarity: the manuscript is generally well organized and readable.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Novelty relative to Krause & Igel (2015): As presented, the contribution appears to be a natural extension of earlier rank-one Cholesky updates to the full CMA-ES with rank-µ and modified CSA. Please make the distinct technical contributions explicit: what, beyond integrating known rank-one updates, is new (e.g., a provably stable rank-µ update schedule, the CSA approximation/justification, new invariance results)? A side-by-side comparison with Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) would help position the originality.
- Equivalence to the covariance update (eq. 2): The paper states that the Cholesky factor update corresponds to applying the standard rank-1 and rank-µ updates to C. A concise derivation or theorem (with proof sketch or a reference plus any required adaptations) showing that Algorithm 2 yields Ct+1 identical to (2) when applied in the given sequence is essential, since correctness hinges on this equivalence.
- Complexity discussion: The manuscript claims O(µ d^2) per iteration. In contrast, CMA-ES-Ref often pays O(d^3) per eigendecomposition unless updates are delayed. Please clarify:
  - The precise per-iteration cost of each variant (using Θ notation and operation counts).
  - How the delay strategy used in CMA-ES-Ref in your experiments impacts the effective complexity.
  - Why your approach is faster even when CMA-ES-Ref delays eigendecompositions (constant factors, no “batching” spikes, cache behavior, etc.).
  - Empirically validate scaling by reporting slopes on log-log plots and flop-normalized runtimes.
- CSA approximation and error analysis: The theoretical argument shows the rotation error tends to vanish as the covariance shape stabilizes, but it is non-deterministic and may conflict with observations that CSA is sensitive to mis-normalization (cf. Auger, 2015). Please add targeted experiments:
  - Compare the trajectory of ‖pσ‖ and σ across iterations between the reference CMA-ES and Cholesky-CMA-ES.
  - Measure ‖Et Eᵀ_{t-1} − I‖ (or equivalent) over time to demonstrate decaying rotation drift.
  - Show the distribution of pσ norms against E{χ} to confirm the CSA behaves as intended.
  - Provide ablations isolating the impact of using A^{-1} versus C^{-1/2}.
- Invariance: Since CSA invariance is subtle, report rotation invariance metrics (beyond final performance) in the main text, not only supplementary, and discuss any deviations.
- Claims of optimality: The statement of asymptotically optimal O(µ d^2) update should be supported with a clear lower-bound argument (e.g., processing µ rank-one directions times d-dimensional structures implies Ω(µ d^2) operations under naive matrix multiplication).
- Experimental details: Please clarify implementation fairness (BLAS/LAPACK usage, cache effects), the chosen µ as a function of d (and its impact on cost), and provide memory usage comparisons. Consider reporting results for d > 256 with careful memory-bound discussion, and include CMA-ES-Ref at larger d using more aggressive delay if that is common practice.

Minor comments
- Notation/typos: In Algorithm 2, wk should be αk. In Table 1, use d instead of n. Line 185: “F” should be “f”. Please proofread for consistency (e.g., vector/matrix bolding, indices, and normalization constants).
- Citations: Ensure complete and correctly formatted references (several entries look incomplete).
- Terminology: Be precise when contrasting “Cholesky factor” versus “matrix square root,” and when discussing RQ vs polar decompositions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong practical contribution: substantial speedups without loss in function evaluations; likely to impact users of CMA-ES in ML and beyond.
- Clean, numerically stable method with reduced memory and convenient byproducts (eigenvalues).
- Good empirical results across standard benchmarks and dimensions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty as currently positioned; appears as an incremental extension of prior Cholesky-update work without sufficiently highlighted unique contributions.
- Missing core validation: lack of a clear equivalence proof to the standard covariance update and insufficient empirical analysis of the CSA approximation and invariance.
- Complexity discussion needs sharpening to avoid confusion with CMA-ES-Ref’s costs under delayed eigendecomposition.

Overall evaluation
This is a promising and practically valuable contribution, but in its current form it does not yet meet the NIPS bar. With a clearer statement of novelty, a formal derivation of equivalence to (2), a more rigorous complexity analysis, and experiments that directly validate the CSA approximation and invariance properties over time, I would be inclined to support acceptance. At present, I recommend rejection with encouragement to resubmit after these issues are addressed.
