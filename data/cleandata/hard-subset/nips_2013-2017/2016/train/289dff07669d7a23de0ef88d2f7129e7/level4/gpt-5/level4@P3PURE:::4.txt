Summary
This paper proposes to maintain and update a triangular Cholesky factorization within CMA-ES, thereby eliminating the explicit matrix square root used in the standard algorithm and reducing the cost associated with matrix inverses. The authors provide a theoretical justification suggesting that the modification does not degrade the algorithm’s performance. Empirically, the method reduces runtime without a significant change in the number of objective function evaluations. Overall, this is a valuable and practical implementation of Cholesky factorization for CMA-ES.

Strengths
- Clear computational benefit: by avoiding explicit matrix square roots (and associated inverses), the method reduces per-iteration runtime, with gains that appear to grow with the problem dimension.
- Theoretical support: the paper provides a justification indicating that the proposed updates do not deteriorate performance relative to standard CMA-ES.
- Empirical behavior: the number of objective function evaluations is essentially unchanged, while wall-clock time decreases, which is an attractive trade-off in practice.

Questions and points for clarification
- Numerical stability claim: Please provide a more detailed theoretical explanation of why the proposed computations are “numerically more stable.” What properties of the Cholesky-based updates (e.g., factor maintenance, avoidance of certain decompositions, conditioning effects) ensure improved numerical behavior, and under what assumptions? Formal bounds or a comparison of error propagation versus the standard CMA-ES pipeline would strengthen this claim.
- Evaluation count vs. wall-clock time: It appears that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires roughly the same number of objective evaluations as standard CMA-ES but achieves lower wall-clock time, with benefits increasing in higher dimensions. It would be helpful to make this trade-off explicit in the text, including a concise breakdown of where the computational savings arise in the update steps.

Suggestions
- Consider integrating low-dimensional approximations as in [Loshchilov, 2015] within the proposed framework. In many practical applications—such as training neural networks in direct policy search—high dimensionality is a critical concern. Discussing compatibility with such approximations, and potentially demonstrating a combined approach, could substantially broaden the method’s applicability to large-scale settings.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-motivated contribution that pragmatically improves CMA-ES runtime through Cholesky factorization while preserving evaluation efficiency. Addressing the theoretical basis for the claimed numerical stability and exploring integration with low-dimensional approximations would further strengthen the paper and its impact on high-dimensional applications.
