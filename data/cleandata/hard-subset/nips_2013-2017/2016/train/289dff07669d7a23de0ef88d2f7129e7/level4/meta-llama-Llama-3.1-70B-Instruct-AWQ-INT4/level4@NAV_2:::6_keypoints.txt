- Proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES reimplements CMA-ES covariance update with lower time/storage but the name was used earlier by Suttorp et al. (2009) and should be acknowledged to avoid confusion.
- The main algorithmic novelty is incremental: repeatedly applying rankOneUpdate in a loop across individuals, building on Krause et al. (2015).
- Empirical differences exist (e.g., plateau in Fig.1(b) with different objective values for CMA-ES-Ref vs Cholesky-CMA-ES), suggesting behavior is not identical.
- The paper should include empirical comparisons to Suttorp et al.’s Cholesky-CMA-ES and algorithmic comparison to Krause et al.
- Claiming “optimal” covariance update/storage is misleading — not optimal for all cases (e.g., Sphere function where full covariance isn’t needed, or separable problems needing only diagonal).
- The method is only about twice as efficient as Suttorp et al.’s omitted algorithm, contrary to statements about that method’s numerical instability (original paper did not claim instability).
- Omitting Suttorp et al.’s method from Figure 3 may mislead readers about the paper’s actual contribution.
- For full-covariance scenarios the proposed approach can be a good time/memory saver, but it does not change CMA-ES complexity for large-scale problems.
- Paper is technically sound with solid theoretical and empirical support, but the contribution is incremental.
- Authors should either resolve the naming conflict, run experiments including Suttorp and Krause variants, and update figures, or better justify the significance of the modification.
- Minor correction: eigenvalue decomposition is typically done periodically in proper implementations, not at every update as stated.
- Clarity is good overall, but statements about “optimal” complexities should be rephrased to avoid confusion.
- Significance is moderate: addresses an important efficiency issue but needs broader evaluation in large-scale contexts.
- Arguments for acceptance: useful efficiency improvement, thorough analysis and experiments, tackles an important optimization problem.
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, missing comparisons and naming acknowledgment, and a misleading optimality claim.
