

This paper proposes a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the standard matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce time and storage complexity. The authors develop the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm, which maintains triangular Cholesky factors instead of the full covariance matrix, thereby achieving O(µd²) time complexity for covariance updates (compared to O(d³) for standard CMA-ES). The authors provide theoretical justification showing that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor diminishes as the algorithm converges, and they empirically demonstrate significant speedups (up to 20x for d=64) without increasing the number of function evaluations required to reach a given solution quality.

The paper builds upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who explored Cholesky-based updates for restricted CMA-ES variants. The authors' contribution lies in extending this approach to the standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification for the convergence properties, which previous works lacked. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making a strong case for the practical utility of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
The paper's main strength is its practical significance. For large-scale optimization problems where covariance matrix updates become computationally prohibitive, the Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a clear advantage without sacrificing solution quality. The experimental results are convincing, showing consistent speedups across multiple benchmark functions and increasing with dimensionality. The authors also provide valuable implementation details, including how the method integrates with existing CMA-ES libraries. The theoretical argument for the diminishing error as the algorithm converges is a notable contribution that addresses a key concern with previous approaches.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness is limited originality. While the authors provide a theoretically justified extension of previous work, the core method closely follows Krause and Igel (2015), who already demonstrated a quadratic-time Cholesky update for CMA-ES variants. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from this prior work. Specifically, the authors should clarify how their theoretical justification differs from or improves upon previous approaches, particularly regarding the claim that their method "does not deteriorate the algorithm's performance."

The paper also lacks sufficient detail regarding the complexity analysis. While the authors claim O(µd²) complexity, they do not distinguish between the asymptotic behavior and the practical constants that matter for real implementations. A more precise Θ(µd²) analysis would be valuable, as the paper's experimental results suggest that the constant factors in the O-notation are significant for practical performance.

Most critically, the paper fails to provide experimental validation for its key theoretical claim that the error in the path (pσ,t+1) diminishes over time. The authors state this should be true "as the algorithm converges to the optimum," but they only compare final performance metrics, not the trajectory of the error. Without direct measurement of how the error evolves during optimization, it's difficult to assess the validity of this claim. The authors should include plots showing how the rotation matrix Et changes over iterations, as suggested in their supplementary material.

The paper also contains minor but important notational inconsistencies that undermine clarity: Algorithm 2 uses "wk" instead of "αk," and Table 1 refers to "n" instead of "d" for dimensionality. These should be corrected.

**Overall Assessment:**
The Cholesky-CMA-ES represents a practical improvement to an important optimization algorithm with clear benefits for large-scale problems. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature, particularly for practitioners working with high-dimensional CMA-ES applications. However, the limited originality and insufficient theoretical and experimental validation of the core claim about diminishing error prevent it from meeting the threshold for acceptance at NIPS.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Strong practical contribution, clear experimental results showing significant speedups without performance degradation, well-written and well-organized.
*Con:* Limited originality (closely follows prior work), insufficient theoretical justification for the key error claim, missing experimental validation of the error evolution, minor notational issues.

The paper should be rejected in its current form due to the insufficient theoretical justification and lack of experimental validation for the core theoretical claim. However, with additional work to better distinguish the contribution from prior work, provide a more precise complexity analysis, and include experiments validating the error claim, this could be a strong contribution to the field. The authors should revise to explicitly clarify how their theoretical justification differs from previous work, provide Θ-notation complexity analysis, and include experimental evidence of the error diminishing over time.
