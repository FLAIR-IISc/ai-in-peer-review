This manuscript introduces the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel update scheme designed to accelerate the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES). The proposed method approximates the original covariance matrix update, which has a computational complexity of O(d³), with a more efficient scheme that runs in O(μd²), where d is the problem dimension and μ is the population size (typically O(log(d))). The principal idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix directly, rather than adapting the full matrix.

The key difference between the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES and the standard algorithm lies in forgoing the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix, E_t, at each iteration. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this approximation under specific, restrictive assumptions regarding the convergence of the covariance matrix sequence. They argue that asymptotically, the sequence of E_t matrices will converge to the identity matrix, at which point the two schemes become equivalent. The paper demonstrates the practical speed-up and performance of Cholesky-CMA-ES through runtime and convergence plots on standard benchmark functions, comparing it against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that also aim to reduce computational overhead.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

This paper makes a solid contribution toward improving the efficiency of the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm. CMA-ES is arguably one of the most powerful algorithms for non-convex, black-box optimization, particularly when the objective function's topology is unknown and gradient information is unavailable or unreliable. The proposed method achieves a significant speed-up without any apparent deterioration in optimization performance on the synthetic test cases presented.

However, a general weakness of any proposal to accelerate the internal computations of CMA-ES is the context of its typical application. In practice, the overall runtime of an optimization process is often dominated by the evaluation of the black-box objective function. The internal runtime of the CMA-ES algorithm itself frequently becomes negligible in comparison. Furthermore, CMA-ES is often applied to problems where the dimensionality does not exceed a few tens of variables. The scenario targeted by this work—where the algorithm's runtime is a significant bottleneck—arises primarily when function evaluations are extremely fast (e.g., on the order of milliseconds or less) and the problem dimension is high. While important, this scenario may be less common in practice.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

Given that strong theoretical guarantees for a complex scheme like CMA-ES are notoriously difficult to obtain, the paper's empirical evaluation could be strengthened with a few additional experiments to provide deeper insight and bolster the authors' claims.

1.  **Directly Analyze the E_t Approximation:** It would be highly instructive to visualize the evolution of the orthogonal matrix E_t during typical optimization runs. An experiment could be designed where the full CMA-ES is executed, while the Cholesky approximation is calculated in parallel. Plotting a norm of the difference between the true E_t and the identity matrix over time would provide direct empirical evidence for the paper's core assumption and reveal how much E_t actually fluctuates on different problems.

2.  **Include a Standard CMA-ES Baseline:** For completeness, the empirical comparison should include the standard CMA-ES without any update-delaying strategies. This would provide a clearer picture of the performance trade-offs relative to the original, unmodified algorithm.

3.  **Systematic Study on Quadratic Functions:** While the benchmark functions used are standard, a more controlled study on quadratic objective functions would be valuable. By systematically varying the condition number (i.e., the spectrum of the eigenvalues of the Hessian matrix), the authors could rigorously test the algorithm's performance and the accuracy of the E_t approximation as a function of problem conditioning. This would provide a more fine-grained understanding of the method's limitations (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012 for a similar approach).

4.  **Demonstration on a Real-World Problem:** The current results are based entirely on synthetic benchmarks. The paper would be significantly more compelling if it included an application to a real-world problem where computational speed is a critical factor. For instance, policy optimization in reinforcement learning, particularly for real-time robotics applications, could be a suitable domain where function evaluations can be relatively fast and the parameter space is moderately large. Such an experiment would convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.
