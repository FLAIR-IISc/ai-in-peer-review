- Proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES that updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor to approximate covariance adaptation, reducing complexity from O(d^3) to O(μ d^2).

- Central idea: maintain and update Cholesky factor instead of full covariance or its square root to avoid costly decompositions.

- Main deviation from CMA-ES arises from avoiding explicit recovery of the random orthogonal factor Et used in cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA).

- Under stringent assumptions (convergence of normalized covariance sequence to a positive definite limit), the authors argue asymptotically that Et → I, making the approximation equivalent to CMA-ES.

- Empirical results on canonical test functions show substantial speedups without observable degradation in optimization performance.

- Comparisons are made against two strong CMA-ES baselines that use delayed full covariance updates.

- General caveat: in many practical problems, black-box function evaluations dominate wall-clock time and typical dimensions are only a few dozen, so algorithmic overhead reductions may be less impactful.

- Suggestion 1: track the evolution of Et along representative trajectories (e.g., via norms) by running full CMA-ES and comparing approximations.

- Suggestion 2: include a baseline that recomputes the matrix square root every iteration (no delay) to isolate baseline algorithmic cost.

- Suggestion 3: on quadratic objectives, vary the eigenvalue spectrum (cf. Stich & Müller 2012) and report solution quality and Et approximation error versus spectral distribution.

- Suggestion 4: add a real-world experiment where speed matters and dimensionality is moderate (e.g., real-time robotics policy search).
