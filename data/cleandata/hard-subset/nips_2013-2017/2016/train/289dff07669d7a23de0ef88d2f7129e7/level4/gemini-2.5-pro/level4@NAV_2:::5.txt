This paper presents the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce the computational complexity of the algorithm's update steps. The central contribution is a method to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix directly, which avoids the O(d³) cost of repeatedly computing the matrix square root. This modification achieves an asymptotically optimal time complexity of O(µd²) for the update. The key approximation is made within the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, where the inverse of the matrix square root is replaced by the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor. The authors provide a theoretical argument that under the assumption that the sequence of normalized covariance matrices converges, an orthogonal matrix component (Et) in the decomposition also converges, making the approximation asymptotically exact. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated empirically on several standard benchmark functions, where it is shown to significantly reduce wall-clock time compared to state-of-the-art CMA-ES implementations that use delayed updates, without degrading performance in terms of required function evaluations.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

*   **Quality:** The proposed algorithm is technically sound, and the derivation of the Cholesky update is clear. The claims of improved runtime are well-supported by the empirical results on the selected benchmarks. The theoretical justification for the approximation in the CSA update, however, rests on the relatively strong assumption of the convergence of the sequence of covariance matrices. While plausible, this may not always hold, and the paper would be stronger if the practical implications of this assumption were explored more deeply.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation for the work, the description of the algorithm, and the underlying approximation are all explained clearly. The connection to prior work on Cholesky updates for CMA-ES variants is also well-established.

*   **Originality:** The work presents a novel and important modification to the standard CMA-ES. While accelerating CMA-ES and using Cholesky updates are not new ideas, this paper provides a specific, theoretically-motivated approach to handle the CSA update, which has been a key obstacle to applying efficient Cholesky updates to the standard CMA-ES algorithm. The proposed method appears to be a new and valuable contribution to the field.

*   **Significance:** The CMA-ES is a foundational algorithm for derivative-free optimization with numerous applications in machine learning. Therefore, a method that substantially reduces its computational complexity is of high potential significance. This work could enable the application of CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems or to scenarios where function evaluations are very cheap, making the algorithm's own complexity a bottleneck. However, a key weakness is that in many practical applications of CMA-ES, the runtime is dominated by the evaluation of the objective function itself. The scenarios where this speedup is most impactful—high-dimensional problems with millisecond-scale or faster function evaluations—may be less common in practice, which could limit the immediate applicability of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in one of the most important and widely used algorithms for black-box optimization.
*   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is technically sound and achieves an asymptotically optimal O(µd²) runtime for the covariance update.
*   Empirical results clearly demonstrate a significant wall-clock time speedup over standard implementations, with no observable degradation in convergence speed (in terms of function evaluations).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical impact may be limited, as in many real-world scenarios the cost of the objective function evaluation, not the CMA-ES update, is the primary performance bottleneck.
*   The theoretical justification for the approximation relies on asymptotic arguments and convergence assumptions that may not hold during the entire optimization process.
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with a demonstration on a real-world problem and a more rigorous analysis on synthetic benchmarks.

**Detailed Suggestions for Improvement:**
To further strengthen the paper, the authors might consider the following suggestions:

1.  **Empirical Validation of the Approximation:** The core of the theoretical argument is that the rotation matrix `Et` converges. It would be highly instructive to provide empirical evidence for this. An experiment could be run using the full CMA-ES while simultaneously calculating the proposed approximation. A plot showing the evolution of a norm of `Et` or the difference `Et - E_{t-1}` over an optimization trajectory would provide direct insight into how quickly the approximation becomes valid.

2.  **Baseline Completeness:** The comparisons are made against CMA-ES variants with delayed updates. For completeness, it would be valuable to include results for the standard CMA-ES *without* any delay strategy to establish a clearer upper bound on the runtime and provide a more direct comparison.

3.  **More Challenging Benchmarks:** The performance of CMA-ES is known to depend heavily on the conditioning of the objective function. A more systematic study on quadratic functions where the spectrum of the Hessian is varied continuously (e.g., as in Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012) could provide deeper insights into the robustness and behavior of the proposed approximation under different problem geometries.

4.  **Real-World Application:** The manuscript's impact would be significantly enhanced by including an experiment on a real-world problem where the proposed speedup is practically relevant. For instance, a direct policy search task in robotics or another domain with a fast-to-evaluate objective function would serve as a compelling use case.
