This paper introduces the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel update scheme that approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES). The proposed method reduces the computational complexity of the update from O(d³) to O(µd²), where d is the problem dimension and µ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea is to maintain and directly update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than computing the full matrix and its square root. The approximation, and the main difference from the original CMA-ES, arises from foregoing the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix, E_t, at each step. The authors provide a theoretical argument that under the assumption that the sequence of covariance matrices converges, the sequence of E_t matrices will converge to the identity, making the proposed scheme asymptotically equivalent to the original. The authors demonstrate the validity of their approach and the resulting speed-up through experiments on standard benchmark functions. They compare Cholesky-CMA-ES against state-of-the-art variants that employ different delay strategies for the covariance matrix update, presenting results in standard runtime and convergence plots.

**Strengths:**
This paper makes a solid contribution by addressing the computational scalability of the CMA-ES, which is arguably one of the most effective algorithms for non-convex black-box optimization, particularly when gradient information is unavailable or unreliable. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES method speeds up the algorithm considerably while, according to the empirical results, showing no deterioration in optimization performance on the synthetic test cases. The theoretical justification, although based on convergence assumptions, provides a sound rationale for the proposed approximation.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
While the proposed speed-up is significant, a general criticism that applies to this work is that in many practical applications of CMA-ES, the overall optimization time is dominated by the evaluation of the objective function (the black-box oracle). The internal runtime of the CMA-ES algorithm itself often becomes negligible in comparison. Furthermore, typical use cases for CMA-ES involve problem dimensions that do not exceed a few tens of variables. The scenario where this work provides the most benefit—high dimensionality combined with cheap function evaluations (on the order of milliseconds or less)—is important but may represent a smaller subset of real-world problems.

As theoretical guarantees are notoriously difficult to obtain for a complex algorithm such as CMA-ES, the paper could be strengthened by including a few additional empirical investigations:

1.  **Visualizing the Approximation Error:** It would be highly instructive to visualize the evolution of the orthogonal matrix E_t over the course of several optimization runs. An experiment where the standard CMA-ES is run, while the corresponding E_t is computed and its distance from the identity matrix is tracked (e.g., using a suitable matrix norm), could provide valuable empirical evidence on how quickly it converges and the magnitude of its fluctuations. This would directly test the core assumption of the paper.

2.  **Including a Full-Complexity Baseline:** For completeness, the performance comparison should include the original CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies. This would serve as a true baseline for the O(d³) complexity and would more clearly highlight the trade-offs made by the delay-based methods versus the Cholesky approximation.

3.  **Systematic Analysis on Quadratic Functions:** While the chosen benchmark functions are standard, a more systematic study on quadratic functions would be beneficial. Following work like Stich and Mueller (2012), the authors could continuously vary the eigenvalue spectrum of the quadratic's Hessian and analyze both solution quality and the approximation error in E_t as a function of the spectral distribution. This would provide deeper insight into how the problem's conditioning affects the proposed approximation.

4.  **Demonstration on a Real-World Problem:** The paper would be strengthened by including a real-world application that complements the results on synthetic benchmarks. The authors mention direct policy search in their introduction, and an application in this domain (e.g., policy optimization for a reinforcement learning agent) would be a compelling case study where optimization speed can be critical and the parameter space is large enough for the proposed method to demonstrate its practical value.
