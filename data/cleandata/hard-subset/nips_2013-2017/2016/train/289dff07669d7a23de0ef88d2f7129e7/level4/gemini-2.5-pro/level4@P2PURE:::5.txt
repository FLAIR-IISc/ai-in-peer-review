This manuscript introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel update scheme that reduces the complexity of the standard covariance matrix adaptation. It approximates the original O(d³) update with a method that runs in O(μd²), where d is the problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log d)). The principal idea is to directly maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than the full matrix itself. The key difference from the standard CMA-ES is that this method avoids the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix, E_t, in each step. The authors provide an asymptotic argument that, under restrictive assumptions about the convergence of the covariance matrices, the sequence of E_t matrices will converge to the identity matrix. In this limit, the two schemes become equivalent.

The paper demonstrates the validity and speed-up of Cholesky-CMA-ES through runtime and convergence plots on standard benchmark functions. The proposed method is compared against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that also aim to reduce complexity, albeit through different delay mechanisms.

This paper presents a solid contribution by proposing a method to accelerate the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm. CMA-ES is arguably one of the best algorithms for non-convex black-box optimization, particularly when the function's topology is unknown and gradient information is unavailable or unhelpful. On the synthetic benchmarks presented, the proposed method achieves a considerable speed-up without any apparent degradation in optimization performance.

However, a key weakness, which applies to many proposals for accelerating CMA-ES, concerns its practical relevance. In many real-world applications, the total optimization time is dominated by the evaluation of the objective function (the black-box oracle), rendering the runtime of the CMA-ES update itself negligible. This is especially true for the typical use-cases of CMA-ES, which often involve problem dimensions of only a few tens of variables. The speed-up offered by this method becomes significant only in scenarios with very fast function evaluations (e.g., on the order of milliseconds) and higher dimensions. The paper targets this specific scenario, but its practical prevalence is debatable.

Given that theoretical guarantees for a complex algorithm like CMA-ES are notoriously difficult to obtain, the empirical case for this method could be strengthened with the following experiments and analyses:

1.  It would be instructive to visualize the evolution of the orthogonal matrix E_t over the course of representative optimization runs (e.g., by plotting one of its matrix norms). An experiment running the full CMA-ES while simultaneously calculating the Cholesky approximation would provide direct empirical evidence of how E_t fluctuates and deviates from the identity matrix in practice.

2.  For completeness, the experiments should include a direct comparison of runtime and performance against the standard CMA-ES implementation without any complexity-reduction strategies. This would establish a clearer baseline.

3.  While the benchmark functions are standard, a more systematic study on quadratic functions would be beneficial. Instead of using fixed test cases, the authors could vary the spectral properties (e.g., condition number) of the quadratic form continuously (as in Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012). This would allow for a more thorough analysis of how solution quality and the error in the E_t approximation depend on the problem's structure.

4.  The paper's claims would be significantly reinforced by a real-world experiment that complements the simulation results. An application like policy gradient optimization for real-time robotics, where evaluation speed is critical and the parameter space can be moderately large, would be a compelling test case.
