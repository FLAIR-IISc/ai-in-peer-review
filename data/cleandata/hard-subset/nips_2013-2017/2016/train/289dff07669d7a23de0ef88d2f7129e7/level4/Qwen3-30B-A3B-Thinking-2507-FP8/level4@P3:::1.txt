

This paper proposes a significant improvement to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a highly influential derivative-free optimization algorithm widely used in machine learning applications. The authors introduce "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant that replaces the computationally expensive matrix square root operation in the standard CMA-ES with a numerically stable Cholesky factorization approach. This modification reduces the time complexity of covariance matrix updates from O(d³) to O(μd²) (where μ is the selection pressure parameter, typically O(log d)), while maintaining the same number of objective function evaluations required to reach a given solution quality. The authors provide theoretical justification for why this approximation does not deteriorate the algorithm's performance, demonstrating that the introduced error diminishes as the algorithm converges. Empirical results confirm that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves substantial runtime improvements, particularly for high-dimensional problems, without sacrificing solution quality.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The CMA-ES has been featured in several NIPS proceedings (e.g., Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001; Igel, 2010; Heidrich-Meisner & Igel, 2009a,b), and the authors properly cite key contributions including the foundational CMA-ES work (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001), related approaches using Cholesky factors (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), and previous efforts to reduce computational complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008). The authors clearly distinguish their contribution from prior work, particularly by providing a theoretical justification for the Cholesky factor approach that was missing in previous attempts.

The paper excels in quality. The theoretical argument (Lemma 1) is sound and demonstrates that the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ diminishes as the algorithm converges. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using standard benchmark functions across multiple dimensions (4 to 256) with 100 trials per function. The results clearly show that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same solution quality as the standard CMA-ES (measured in function evaluations) while significantly reducing runtime, especially for larger dimensions. The implementation details are well-explained, including how the Cholesky factor updates are performed (Algorithm 2) and the practical considerations for memory efficiency.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors present the problem, motivation, and solution in a logical sequence, with clear mathematical notation and well-structured algorithms. The theoretical justification is accessible to readers familiar with optimization and matrix algebra. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, though the draft review rightly points out that more information about implementation consistency would strengthen the comparison between methods.

The originality of this work is strong. While previous work has explored Cholesky factor approaches (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), this paper is the first to provide a theoretically justified modification that doesn't require the algorithm to be run on a different problem structure (e.g., for the (1+1)-CMA-ES rather than standard CMA-ES). The key insight—replacing the matrix square root with a Cholesky factor while maintaining the same convergence behavior—represents a novel contribution to the CMA-ES literature.

The significance of this work is substantial. For machine learning practitioners using CMA-ES for hyperparameter tuning or direct policy search in reinforcement learning, the proposed variant offers immediate practical benefits: reduced memory requirements, faster runtime (particularly for high-dimensional problems), and additional free information (eigenvalues of the covariance matrix). The authors correctly note that while low-dimensional approximations exist for extremely high-dimensional problems (Loshchilov, 2015), they risk significant performance degradation. Cholesky-CMA-ES provides an optimal middle ground for problems where full covariance matrix adaptation is still feasible but computationally challenging.

Strengths:
- Theoretical justification for why the Cholesky approach doesn't degrade performance
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple dimensions and benchmark functions
- Clear demonstration of reduced computational complexity and memory requirements
- Practical implementation details that would facilitate adoption
- Significant practical benefits for machine learning applications

Weaknesses:
- The draft review correctly notes that Figure 2 appears inconsistent for Cigar and Discus at d=256; the authors should explain why the performance gains diminish at this dimension for these specific functions (likely due to cache effects as mentioned in the paper)
- More detail about the implementation consistency would strengthen the comparison (e.g., confirming all methods use the same language and numerical libraries, and that the reference implementation uses the same delay strategy as described)
- The paper doesn't fully explore the impact of the Cholesky approximation on the algorithm's invariance properties, though the authors correctly note that the CSA update isn't proven to preserve affine invariance

Overall, this paper represents a substantial contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization with clear practical implications for machine learning. The authors have made a compelling case for why Cholesky-CMA-ES should replace the standard CMA-ES when computational efficiency is a concern. Given the widespread use of CMA-ES in machine learning applications, this work has the potential to significantly impact practitioners' optimization workflows.

Pro-acceptance arguments: Theoretical soundness, clear practical benefits, comprehensive evaluation, relevance to machine learning applications, and strong potential for adoption.

Con-acceptance arguments: Minor inconsistencies in Figure 2 (which should be clarified) and a need for more implementation details to ensure fair comparison.

This paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's of high quality, clearly written, original, and highly significant for machine learning practitioners. I recommend acceptance.
