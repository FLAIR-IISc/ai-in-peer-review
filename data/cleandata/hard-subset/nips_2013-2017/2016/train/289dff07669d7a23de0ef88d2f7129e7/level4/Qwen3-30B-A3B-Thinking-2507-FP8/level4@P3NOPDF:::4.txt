

This paper presents a refined implementation of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) leveraging triangular Cholesky factorization to eliminate square-root operations during matrix inversion. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that this modification reduces computational overhead without degrading algorithmic performance, as validated through theoretical analysis and empirical results. Specifically, the authors show that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves identical objective function evaluation counts compared to standard CMA-ES while significantly reducing wall-clock runtime—particularly for high-dimensional problems where the computational savings scale with dimensionality. The theoretical justification provided for the maintained performance is a notable strength, though further clarification on numerical stability would strengthen the work.

The elimination of square-root operations directly targets a key computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, where the standard Cholesky decomposition requires costly square-root calculations during covariance updates. By operating on the Cholesky factor itself (a lower-triangular matrix), the method avoids these operations, reducing the per-iteration complexity from O(d³) to O(d³) with a smaller constant factor, where d is the search space dimension. Crucially, the authors establish that this reformulation preserves the convergence properties and solution quality of the original algorithm, as evidenced by experiments on standard benchmark functions. The runtime improvements are empirically significant, with wall-clock time reductions becoming increasingly pronounced as d grows—addressing a critical practical concern for applications like neural network training, where high-dimensional search spaces are the norm.

The paper’s main contribution is well-motivated and technically sound. However, two points warrant deeper exploration. First, the authors claim the method is "numerically more stable" but provide no theoretical or empirical evidence to substantiate this. While avoiding square roots may reduce floating-point error in specific contexts, numerical stability in covariance updates depends on multiple factors (e.g., condition number, scaling). The authors should explicitly clarify *why* the proposed approach enhances stability—e.g., by avoiding ill-conditioned square-root operations or reducing error propagation—and provide supporting experiments (e.g., condition number comparisons or error analysis across problem instances).

Second, the authors note that the method’s runtime gains are most relevant for high-dimensional problems, yet they do not address how the approach might integrate with existing dimensionality-reduction techniques. The low-dimensional approximation framework of Loshchilov (2015), which exploits problem structure to reduce effective dimensionality, could synergize with Cholesky-CMA-ES to further accelerate optimization in practical settings like direct policy search for neural networks. The authors should consider whether their method could be combined with such techniques, as this would directly address the scalability challenges in real-world applications where high dimensionality is unavoidable. A brief discussion of this integration potential (e.g., as a preprocessing step or within the update rules) would significantly enhance the paper’s practical impact.

In summary, this work offers a valuable computational refinement to CMA-ES with clear empirical benefits for high-dimensional optimization. The theoretical foundation is strong, but deeper justification for numerical stability and exploration of synergies with dimensionality-reduction methods would elevate the contribution. The paper is well-written and technically rigorous, making it suitable for publication with minor revisions addressing these points. The proposed method represents a practical step toward more efficient evolutionary optimization in large-scale machine learning contexts.
