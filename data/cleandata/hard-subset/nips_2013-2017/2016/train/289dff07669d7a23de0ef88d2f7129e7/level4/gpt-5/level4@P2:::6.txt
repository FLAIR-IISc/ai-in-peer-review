Summary
This paper proposes a CMA-ES variant that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, and modifies the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) to use the inverse Cholesky factor instead of the inverse matrix square root. The aim is to retain the behavior of standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations while reducing per-iteration time and memory. A key theoretical argument is that the rotation induced by using A−1t (the inverse Cholesky factor) instead of C−1/2t vanishes as the covariance stabilizes, making the CSA approximation asymptotically accurate. Empirically, the method matches the number of evaluations of a reference CMA-ES while substantially reducing wall-clock time, especially in higher dimensions. The authors claim optimal O(µd^2) update and storage complexity among full-covariance adaptations.

Relation to prior work
- Suttorp et al. (2009) introduced a Cholesky-factor update for CMA-ES (often referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES) using a non-triangular factor and demonstrated numerical stability in practice.
- Krause and Igel (2015) derived an efficient quadratic-time rank-one update for a triangular Cholesky factor (Ct = AtATt) for µ = 1, which has been used in (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES.
- Other acceleration strategies include delayed eigen decompositions, structural restrictions (e.g., diagonal or limited-memory updates), and low-rank/limited-memory CMA-ES.

The present paper extends the triangular rank-one update to the standard rank-µ CMA-ES by applying the rank-one routine for each of the µ weighted steps and adapting CSA to avoid C−1/2. Compared to Suttorp et al., the emphasis here is on triangular factors (optimal storage) and an explicit CSA approximation argument.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: the covariance update and decomposition dominate runtime and memory.
- Practical contribution: a drop-in replacement that preserves evaluation counts and yields large wall-clock speedups as d grows, with asymptotically optimal O(µd^2) time and O(d^2) storage for full-covariance adaptation.
- Sensible theory: the CSA approximation is justified by a rotation convergence argument, and the use of a triangular factor gives free access to eigenvalues and a cheap inverse.
- Experiments: broad benchmark coverage, rotation-invariance testing, and consistent speedups observed.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Naming/credit: “Cholesky-CMA-ES” is widely associated with Suttorp et al. (2009). Please either (i) acknowledge and clearly distinguish your method (e.g., “Triangular-Cholesky-CMA-ES”) or (ii) adopt a different name to avoid confusion. The current phrasing suggests novelty of the name and method that is not accurate.
- Novelty: Algorithmically, the main change relative to Krause and Igel (2015) is to apply rankOneUpdate in a loop for a rank-µ update and adjust CSA (A−1t in place of C−1/2t). This is a valuable engineering extension, but incremental. Please make the scope of algorithmic novelty explicit and explain why this extension is nontrivial (e.g., stability, numerical details, CSA behavior).
- Baselines: A head-to-head empirical comparison with Suttorp et al. (2009) should be prominent. The text mentions a “Suttorp-CMA-ES” variant in your experiments; however, its absence from key figures (e.g., Fig. 3) risks overstating the advance. Please include Suttorp’s method in the main plots or at least provide full comparisons in the supplement with a concise summary in the main text. Also clarify the numerical stability claim—Suttorp et al. report stable behavior, whereas the manuscript suggests the opposite without evidence.
- Claims of “optimality”: The “optimal covariance update and storage complexity” claim is scoped to full-covariance CMA-ES. For separable problems (e.g., Sphere, diagonal structure), diagonal or limited-memory variants are strictly better. Please qualify the claim and discuss when your approach is preferable versus diagonal/LM-CMA-ES.
- CSA approximation: The theoretical justification relies on the covariance “shape” stabilizing. Please quantify the approximation error empirically (e.g., measure ∥EtETt−1 − I∥ over time, ablate the CSA approximation’s effect on step-size dynamics) and discuss cases where Ct evolves slowly (e.g., Rosenbrock) and the impact on performance.
- Plot interpretation: On Cigar, the observed plateau differs between CMA-ES-Ref and your method. If this is due to median aggregation or stopping criteria, please clarify and, if needed, show additional statistics (quartiles or mean ± std) to avoid misinterpretation.
- Minor clarity: “Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition … in each update step” is not standard in high-quality implementations; it is performed periodically. Please revise.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The method is technically sound, with a reasonable theoretical argument and strong empirical evidence for speedups. Missing or de-emphasized baselines and an unqualified optimality claim lower confidence in some conclusions. Including Suttorp and providing stability/CSA ablations would strengthen the evidence.
- Clarity: Generally well organized. Improve credit/naming, scope the novelty precisely, and clarify the CSA approximation and plotting choices. Correct the statement about per-iteration eigen decompositions.
- Originality: The contribution is incremental—extending a known triangular rank-one update to rank-µ CMA-ES and coupling it with an approximated CSA. The practical value is high, but the conceptual novelty is modest.
- Significance: For problems where full covariance is warranted and feasible, the method offers meaningful speed and memory benefits without harming evaluation efficiency. It does not change the quadratic scaling, so impact on truly large-scale settings is limited compared to diagonal/limited-memory approaches.

Pros
- Substantially faster wall-clock time with similar evaluation counts
- Optimal storage for full-covariance adaptation; easy access to eigenvalues and A−1
- Simple, implementable update with good numerical properties

Cons
- Naming/attribution conflict; insufficient differentiation from prior Cholesky-CMA-ES
- Incremental novelty
- Key baseline (Suttorp et al.) not consistently shown; stability claim needs evidence
- “Optimality” overclaimed outside the full-covariance regime

Requested revisions
1) Resolve the naming/credit issue explicitly.  
2) Include strong empirical comparisons with Suttorp et al. (2009) and discuss Krause and Igel (2015) algorithmically; update all figures accordingly (show best in main text, full plots in the supplement if needed).  
3) Clarify the algorithmic novelty beyond the “for” loop and provide ablations for the CSA approximation (including rotation metrics and step-size dynamics).  
4) Qualify the “optimality” claim and discuss when diagonal/LM variants are preferable.  
5) Fix minor clarity issues (e.g., periodic eigen decompositions).

Overall recommendation
Promising and practically useful, but currently reads as an incremental extension with credit and evaluation gaps. With the requested comparisons, clearer positioning relative to prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES, and CSA ablations, the paper would make a solid contribution for practitioners who need full-covariance CMA-ES at reduced runtime and memory.
