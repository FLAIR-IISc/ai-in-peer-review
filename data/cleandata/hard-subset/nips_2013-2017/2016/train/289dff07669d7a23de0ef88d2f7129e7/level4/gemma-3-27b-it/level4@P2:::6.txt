This paper presents a modification to the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) aimed at reducing computational cost and memory usage during covariance matrix updates. The authors propose the “Cholesky-CMA-ES,” which leverages a triangular Cholesky factorization instead of directly computing and storing the covariance matrix. They argue this approach maintains the algorithm’s performance while improving efficiency, particularly for high-dimensional problems. The core idea involves adapting the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism by replacing the matrix square root with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor.

The CMA-ES is a well-established and powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm, frequently used in machine learning for tasks like reinforcement learning policy search and hyperparameter tuning [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001; Beyer, 2007; Eiben & Smith, 2015]. A key bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES is the computational complexity of updating and storing the covariance matrix, typically requiring O(d<sup>2</sup>) time and space. Prior work has addressed this through techniques like delayed updates [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001], restricted model complexity [Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008], and updating the Cholesky factor directly [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015]. The current work builds upon this existing research, particularly the approach by Krause and Igel [2015], by extending it to the full CMA-ES framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Potential for Improved Efficiency:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a promising avenue for reducing the computational burden of the CMA-ES, especially in high-dimensional spaces, by avoiding explicit covariance matrix computation.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a theoretical argument for why their modification should not negatively impact the algorithm’s performance, focusing on the convergence behavior and coordinate system rotations. Lemma 1, which discusses the convergence of rotation matrices, supports this claim.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper generally presents the method and its theoretical basis in a clear and understandable manner. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the proposed approach.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance (in terms of function evaluations) to the standard CMA-ES while significantly reducing runtime, particularly as dimensionality increases.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Naming Convention:** The use of the name “Cholesky-CMA-ES” is potentially misleading. This name has previously been used by Suttorp et al. [2009] for a related but distinct algorithm. The authors should address this ambiguity and clearly differentiate their approach.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** The algorithmic innovation appears relatively incremental. The core change seems to be applying an existing rank-one update (already present in Krause & Igel [2015]) iteratively across all individuals, which could be perceived as a straightforward extension rather than a substantial novel contribution. A more detailed discussion of why this simple modification hasn't been previously explored or why it's a significant improvement would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The empirical evaluation would be strengthened by a direct comparison to the Cholesky-CMA-ES variant proposed by Suttorp et al. [2009], as well as a detailed algorithmic comparison to Krause & Igel [2015]. The authors allude to the difference between their work and Suttorp et al. but a direct empirical evaluation is needed.
*   **Misleading Claim of Optimality:** The claim of “optimal covariance update and storage complexity” is potentially overstated. The optimality is conditional on the need for a *full* covariance matrix. For problems with specific structures (e.g., separable functions) or low-dimensional effective dimensionality, other approaches may be more efficient.
*   **Inconsistent Results/Presentation:** The observation of a different plateau in Figure 1(b) for CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES warrants further investigation and explanation. The presentation of median results could be masking important variations, and a more thorough analysis of individual trial performance might be informative.
*   **Minor Point:** The statement that eigenvalue decomposition is performed “in each update step” is inaccurate. Standard implementations typically employ delayed updates to reduce computational cost.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a practical and potentially impactful modification to the CMA-ES that addresses a known performance bottleneck.
*   The theoretical analysis provides some justification for the approach’s effectiveness.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a significant speedup in runtime without sacrificing performance.
*   The work is clearly written and addresses a relevant problem in the optimization community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The algorithmic contribution is relatively incremental.
*   The naming convention could cause confusion with existing work.
*   The comparison to related algorithms (Suttorp et al., Krause & Igel) is incomplete.
*   The claim of “optimality” is potentially misleading.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a worthwhile, though incremental, improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a practical way to reduce runtime and memory usage, particularly in high-dimensional optimization problems. However, the authors should address the issues related to naming, algorithmic novelty, and comparison to existing work to strengthen the paper's contribution. I lean towards **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, resolving the naming issue, performing a more comprehensive comparison to related work (both empirically and algorithmically), and clarifying the claim of optimality would significantly improve the paper.
