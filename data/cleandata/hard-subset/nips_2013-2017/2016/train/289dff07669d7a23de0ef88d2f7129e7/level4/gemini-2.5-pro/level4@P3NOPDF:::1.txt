This paper introduces a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The core contribution is a new update mechanism for the covariance matrix based on its triangular Cholesky factors. This approach significantly reduces the time complexity of the update from O(N^3) to O(N^2) and lowers the memory requirements for storing the algorithm's state parameters. The authors conduct a thorough numerical evaluation, demonstrating that these efficiency gains are achieved without deteriorating the convergence performance of the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Algorithmic Contribution:** This work represents a highly significant and practical contribution to the field of optimization. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm, and improving its computational and memory efficiency, especially for higher-dimensional problems, is of great importance.

2.  **Clear Practical Benefits:** The proposed variant offers several compelling advantages over the standard implementation, positioning it to become the new default for medium to large-scale applications. These advantages include:
    *   A substantial reduction in the computational complexity of the covariance matrix update.
    *   Lower memory overhead.
    *   The direct availability of the covariance matrix's eigenvalues as a by-product of the update, which can be valuable for analysis and termination criteria.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's claims are supported by a rigorous and convincing empirical study. The experiments clearly show that the new update scheme maintains the excellent convergence properties of the original CMA-ES while dramatically reducing wall-clock time, with the benefits becoming more pronounced as dimensionality increases.

4.  **High-Quality Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The methodology is carefully explained, and the results are presented effectively.

5.  **Relevance to NIPS:** This is an excellent contribution for the NIPS community. CMA-ES has strong connections to machine learning, including applications in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning for supervised learning, as well as theoretical links to information geometry. An advancement of this caliber is of broad interest to the conference audience.

**Minor Points for Improvement:**

1.  **Comparability of Implementations:** For the sake of reproducibility and to further strengthen the claims regarding wall-clock time, it would be beneficial to provide more details on the experimental setup. Could the authors clarify whether the different CMA-ES variants were implemented in the same programming language and using comparable libraries? This detail would confirm that the observed speedups are attributable solely to the proposed algorithmic improvements rather than implementation differences.

2.  **Clarification on Figure 2:** In the discussion of Figure 2, the authors state, "the higher the dimensionality, the more pronounced the differences." While this trend holds for most functions, it appears less evident for the Cigar and Discus functions when moving from dimension 64 to 256. In these cases, the performance gap seems to narrow slightly at the highest dimension. A brief comment or explanation for this observation would be appreciated, as it might offer additional insight into the algorithm's behavior on specific problem landscapes.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that presents a significant and well-executed improvement to a foundational optimization algorithm. The proposed method is likely to have a lasting impact and become widely adopted. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
