- Introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES that approximates covariance adaptation, reducing complexity from O(d^3) to O(μ d^2).  
- Core idea: maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix and its square root.  
- Key distinction: the method omits estimating a random orthogonal matrix Et at each step.  
- Under a restrictive assumption (covariance sequence converges), authors argue asymptotically Et → I, making the schemes equivalent.  
- Empirical validation: runtime and convergence plots on benchmarks comparing to two delayed-update CMA-ES variants.  
- Contribution: provides a solid acceleration of CMA-ES with significant runtime reduction without degrading performance on synthetic tests.  
- Major limitation: overall optimization time is typically dominated by expensive black-box function evaluations, so CMA-ES runtime improvements are often negligible.  
- Practical relevance limited: speedups matter mainly when evaluations are very fast and dimensionality high, whereas CMA-ES is commonly used in low-dimensional (≤ tens) problems.  
- Recommended experiment 1: visualize evolution of Et (e.g., various norms) by running full CMA-ES alongside the approximation.  
- Recommended experiment 2: include comparisons to standard (non-delayed) CMA-ES for completeness.  
- Recommended experiment 3: perform a spectral-variation study on quadratic functions to compare solution quality and Et error across spectra.  
- Recommended experiment 4: provide a real-world validation where speed is critical (e.g., policy-gradient optimization in real-time robotics).
