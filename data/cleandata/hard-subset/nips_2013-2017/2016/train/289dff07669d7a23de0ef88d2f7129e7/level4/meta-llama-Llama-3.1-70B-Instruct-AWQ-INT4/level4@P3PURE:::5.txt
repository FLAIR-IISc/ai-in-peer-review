The manuscript introduces a novel update scheme for the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES. This scheme approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation, which has a complexity of O(d^3), by a novel approach that runs in O(μd^2), where d is the dimension of the optimization problem and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea behind the update is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix rather than a full covariance matrix and its corresponding square root.

The primary difference between the Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES lies in the absence of estimating a random orthogonal matrix Et at each step. Under certain restrictive assumptions regarding the convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target covariance matrix, the authors provide an asymptotic argument that the sequence of Et will converge to an identity matrix, thereby rendering the two schemes equivalent.

The validity and efficiency of the Cholesky-CMA-ES are demonstrated through standard runtime and convergence plots on selected objective functions, as well as comparisons with two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives that delay the full covariance matrix update using different methods. This paper makes a significant contribution to accelerating the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm, which is arguably the best algorithm for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the topology of the objective function and first or higher-order information is difficult to access or not helpful.

The proposed method substantially accelerates the algorithm without compromising optimization performance on the synthetic test cases. However, a common limitation of any speed-up proposal for CMA-ES is that, in practice, the overall optimization process is often dominated by the runtime of the black-box oracle (the objective function), and the actual runtime of the CMA-ES is frequently negligible in comparison. Moreover, in typical scenarios where CMA-ES is employed, the dimensionality of the problems rarely exceeds a few tens of variables. The runtime of CMA-ES only becomes a critical factor when a single function evaluation is on the order of milliseconds or smaller, and the dimensionality is higher, which is the targeted scenario in this work (although this may be less realistic in practice).

Given that theoretical guarantees are notoriously challenging to obtain for complex schemes like CMA-ES, I suggest conducting a few additional empirical experiments and plots to further strengthen the presentation of the results. Firstly, it would be instructive to visualize the evolution of Et on selected optimization trajectories, e.g., in different norms. An experiment where the full CMA-ES is used, and the approximation is calculated simultaneously and compared, would provide further empirical evidence about the actual fluctuations of Et over time.

Secondly, including actual runtimes and performance of CMA-ES without delay strategies in this contribution would be beneficial for completeness. Thirdly, although the used test functions are standard in the evolutionary optimization community, I would prefer a simulation study on quadratic functions where the spectrum of quadratic forms is varied continuously (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012), and compare the solution quality and error in Et in dependence of the spectral distribution.

Lastly, it would be instructive to include a real-world experiment that accompanies the present simulation results. For instance, policy gradients in real-time robotics applications might be a suitable choice, where speed is crucial, and the parameter space is not too large.
