This paper proposes a Cholesky covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) utilizing both rank-1 and rank-µ updates for the triangular Cholesky factor. The core contribution lies in theoretically justifying why this modification to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism does not significantly alter the algorithm’s performance, assuming convergence of the covariance matrix. Empirical results are presented to validate this claim.

The paper builds upon existing work in adapting the CMA-ES to reduce computational complexity. Previous efforts, such as Krause and Igel (2015), explored updating the triangular Cholesky factor using a rank-1 update. This work extends that approach to incorporate a rank-µ update, effectively generalizing the procedure through a sequence of rank-1 updates. While the extension appears straightforward, the authors provide a theoretical justification for its validity, which is valuable. It's worth noting that the convergence of the covariance matrix *Ct* in CMA-ES has been previously analyzed (e.g., Beyer, 2014), providing additional context for Lemma 1, which relies on this convergence assumption.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a theoretical argument for why approximating the standard CMA-ES with the Cholesky factorization should not significantly impact performance. This is a crucial contribution, as simply applying the Cholesky factorization without justification could lead to instability or decreased convergence rates.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers improved computational efficiency, particularly in terms of runtime complexity (O(µd²)), and reduced memory footprint, which is crucial for high-dimensional optimization problems.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly outlines the proposed method. The algorithm is presented in a concise and understandable manner (Algorithm 1 & 2).
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Experiments demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance to the standard CMA-ES in terms of the number of function evaluations, while offering significant speedups in wall-clock time, especially as dimensionality increases.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the theoretical justification is valuable, the extension from rank-1 (Krause & Igel, 2015) to rank-µ updates feels somewhat incremental. The authors acknowledge this in their conclusion, but the limited novelty might impact the paper's significance.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments demonstrate speedups, a more extensive comparison with other state-of-the-art optimization algorithms beyond the CMA-ES variants would strengthen the paper’s claims of superiority.  Evaluating performance on a wider range of benchmark functions, including those with different characteristics (e.g., constraints, multi-modality), would also be beneficial.
*   **Minor Errors:** There are a few minor typographical errors.  Specifically, in equation (1), the σ in the denominator should be σt. The same error appears in footnote 2 on page 3. Also, in line 94, "which can by be motivated…" should read “which can be motivated…”
*   **Dependence on Convergence Assumption**: The theoretical justification relies heavily on the assumption of covariance matrix convergence. While reasonable, acknowledging the potential impact of deviations from this assumption would enhance the robustness of the analysis.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretical justification for a practical optimization that improves the efficiency of CMA-ES, a widely-used algorithm.
*   Demonstrates significant speedups in runtime without sacrificing optimization performance.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental, building directly on prior work (Krause & Igel, 2015).
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   Minor typographical errors are present.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm, offering increased computational efficiency without sacrificing performance. The theoretical justification is a key strength, and the experimental results support the claims of speedup. While the contribution may be considered incremental, it is a solid and well-executed piece of work that should be of interest to researchers and practitioners working in derivative-free optimization and its applications in machine learning. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the identified typos and potential to expand the scope of the experiments.
