The manuscript introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel update scheme for covariance matrix adaptation that offers a computational advantage over the standard CMA-ES algorithm. The core idea is to approximate the covariance matrix update – typically O(d^3) complexity, where *d* is the problem dimension – by maintaining and updating a Cholesky decomposition, reducing the complexity to O(μd^2), with μ being the population size (often O(log(d))). This avoids explicitly estimating a random orthogonal matrix *E<sub>t</sub>* at each step, a key difference from the original CMA-ES. The authors provide an asymptotic argument, based on assumptions regarding the convergence of covariance matrix sequences, to demonstrate that *E<sub>t</sub>* converges to the identity matrix, implying equivalence between the two schemes under these conditions.

The paper validates the Cholesky-CMA-ES through runtime and convergence analyses on benchmark functions, comparing its performance against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants employing delayed covariance updates. This work represents a valuable contribution to accelerating CMA-ES, a highly effective algorithm for non-convex, black-box optimization problems where gradient information is unavailable or unhelpful. The proposed method demonstrably speeds up the algorithm without compromising optimization performance on the tested synthetic problems. 

However, a common limitation of CMA-ES speed-up strategies is that the overall optimization time is frequently dominated by evaluations of the objective function itself, particularly when problem dimensionality is modest (tens of variables). The benefits of this method are most pronounced when function evaluations are very fast (milliseconds or less) and dimensionality is high – a scenario that may not always reflect practical applications. 

While obtaining rigorous theoretical guarantees for complex algorithms like CMA-ES is challenging, several empirical investigations could further strengthen the manuscript. First, visualizing the evolution of *E<sub>t</sub>* along optimization trajectories (e.g., using different norms) – comparing it to the full CMA-ES calculation in parallel – would provide additional insight into its behavior. Second, including results for standard CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies would offer a complete performance baseline. Third, a simulation study on quadratic functions with continuously varied spectral distributions (following, for example, Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012) could assess solution quality and *E<sub>t</sub>* error in relation to spectral characteristics. Finally, demonstrating the method’s efficacy on a real-world application, such as policy gradients in real-time robotics, would showcase its practical benefits in a speed-critical context.
