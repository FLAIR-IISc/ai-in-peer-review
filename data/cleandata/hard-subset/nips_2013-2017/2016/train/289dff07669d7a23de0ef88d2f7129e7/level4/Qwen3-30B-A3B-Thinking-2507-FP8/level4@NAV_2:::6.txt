

The paper introduces a variant of CMA-ES termed "Cholesky-CMA-ES" that replaces the matrix square root in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor to reduce time and space complexity. The authors argue this modification maintains the algorithm's performance while achieving optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity for full covariance updates, as verified empirically on benchmark functions. This work extends prior efforts (e.g., Krause and Igel, 2015; Suttorp et al., 2009) by adapting the Cholesky update to the standard CMA-ES framework. However, the naming "Cholesky-CMA-ES" conflicts with Suttorp et al. (2009), who first proposed this name for a related algorithm, and the authors do not acknowledge this prior work. The algorithmic contribution is modest: it generalizes Krause and Igel's rank-1 update to a loop over all individuals (µ updates) rather than a single update, which is a minor extension.  

**Strengths:**  
- The theoretical justification for the CSA modification (Lemma 1) is sound, showing that the introduced error diminishes as the algorithm converges.  
- The empirical results demonstrate clear runtime improvements (up to 20× faster for d=64) without increasing function evaluations, validating the practical benefit for non-separable problems.  
- The storage complexity is indeed optimal (O(d²) vs. O(d³) for explicit decomposition), and the approach provides eigenvalues of the covariance matrix at no extra cost.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Originality:** The naming conflict with Suttorp et al. (2009) is problematic and misrepresents the novelty. The algorithmic novelty is limited to the loop over µ individuals, which is not sufficiently emphasized or contrasted with prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants.  
- **Significance:** The claim of "optimal covariance update and storage complexity" is overstated. The method does not reduce complexity for separable functions (where diagonal updates suffice) or spherical problems (where covariance adaptation is unnecessary), and the runtime remains quadratic in d. The paper should clarify that this is optimal *only* for full covariance updates in non-separable settings.  
- **Quality:** The empirical comparison omits Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES (which the authors claim is numerically unstable), potentially misleading readers about the contribution. The plateau in Figure 1(b) for the Cigar function appears inconsistent with median results, suggesting possible analysis issues.  
- **Clarity:** The distinction between the authors’ variant and Suttorp’s work is unclear, and the discussion of numerical stability lacks evidence. The statement "typically an eigenvalue decomposition is performed in each update step" is inaccurate—state-of-the-art implementations (e.g., Hansen, 2015) use delayed updates.  

**Recommendations:**  
1. **Rename** the method (e.g., "Cholesky-based CMA-ES" or "CMA-ES with Cholesky-Adapted CSA") to avoid confusion with Suttorp et al. (2009).  
2. **Add empirical comparisons** against Suttorp’s method (for runtime/stability) and Krause’s method (for algorithmic clarity), updating all figures accordingly. If figures become cluttered, include full comparisons in supplementary material.  
3. **Nuance the "optimal" claim** to specify applicability only to non-separable problems requiring full covariance adaptation.  
4. **Clarify the algorithmic contribution** by explicitly stating that the key extension is the loop over µ individuals in the CSA step (not a new update rule).  

Overall, the work offers a practical improvement for full-covariance CMA-ES but understates its incremental nature and misrepresents prior work. With the proposed revisions, it would make a solid contribution to optimization tooling.
