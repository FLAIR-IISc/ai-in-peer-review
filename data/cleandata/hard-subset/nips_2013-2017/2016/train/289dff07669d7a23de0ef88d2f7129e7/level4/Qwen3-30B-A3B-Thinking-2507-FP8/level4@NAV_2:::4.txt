

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor. The authors theoretically justify that this modification preserves the algorithm's performance in terms of objective function evaluations while reducing time and space complexity to O(µd²) and O(d²), respectively. The work builds upon prior NIPS contributions such as Ros and Hansen (2008), who introduced diagonal approximations, and Krause and Igel (2015), who explored Cholesky updates for specialized CMA-ES variants. Unlike these, the proposed method provides a theoretically grounded solution for full covariance adaptation without compromising performance, addressing a critical bottleneck in high-dimensional optimization.

**Strengths:**  
- *Quality:* The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) rigorously demonstrates that the error from replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ vanishes as the algorithm converges, supported by comprehensive empirical validation across 12 benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Rosenbrock, etc.) with dimensions up to 256. The runtime experiments confirm a 20× speedup at d=64 versus the standard CMA-ES-Ref, with identical function evaluations.  
- *Originality:* This is the first work to provide a theoretically sound Cholesky-based CSA for standard CMA-ES, distinct from prior attempts (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) that either lacked convergence guarantees or applied only to restricted cases. The method’s optimal asymptotic complexity (O(µd²)) and storage requirements (O(d²)) are novel for full covariance adaptation.  
- *Significance:* The approach directly addresses a key scalability barrier in CMA-ES for high-dimensional problems (e.g., neural network training in policy search), where covariance updates dominate runtime. The empirical results show the speedup increases with dimensionality, making it highly relevant for NIPS applications in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization.  
- *Clarity:* The paper is well-organized, with clear algorithmic descriptions (Algorithms 1–2) and thorough experimental setup. The connection between theoretical guarantees and empirical results is compelling.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- The theoretical explanation for *numerical stability* (mentioned in the abstract) is insufficiently detailed. While the paper states the Cholesky approach avoids unstable matrix square root computations, it does not elaborate on *why* the inverse Cholesky factorization inherently reduces numerical errors compared to standard CMA-ES (e.g., via condition number analysis or comparison to alternative decompositions). This gap weakens the claim of "numerically stable" computation.  
- The discussion of combining with low-dimensional approximations (Loshchilov, 2015) is superficial. The authors note that for extremely high dimensions, low-rank methods may be necessary, but they do not explore how Cholesky-CMA-ES could *integrate* with such approaches (e.g., as a building block for hybrid schemes) or quantify the crossover point where low-rank methods become preferable. This limits the practical guidance for real-world applications like large-scale neural network training.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Theoretically rigorous, empirically validated speedup with identical optimization quality, optimal complexity for full covariance adaptation, and clear relevance to NIPS applications.  
*Con:* Insufficient depth on numerical stability justification and limited exploration of integration with existing low-dimensional approximation techniques.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a strong, well-supported contribution to scalable derivative-free optimization, directly addressing a key NIPS-relevant challenge. The minor weaknesses are easily addressable in revision and do not undermine the core technical merits. The work advances the state of the art in both theoretical understanding and practical efficiency for CMA-ES.
