

This paper proposes a Cholesky-CMA-ES variant that replaces the matrix square root in the standard CMA-ES with a triangular Cholesky factor for covariance matrix adaptation. The authors demonstrate that this modification maintains the algorithm's performance while reducing time complexity from O(d³) to O(µd²) and storage complexity to optimal levels. The key contribution extends prior work by Krause and Igel (2015), which considered only rank-1 updates for the Cholesky factor, to include rank-µ updates through a sequence of rank-1 operations. The authors provide theoretical justification (Lemma 1) showing that the modified cumulative step-size adaptation does not significantly alter algorithm behavior under convergence assumptions of the covariance matrix. Empirical results on standard benchmark functions demonstrate comparable performance with substantially reduced runtime, particularly for higher-dimensional problems.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important practical challenge in CMA-ES implementation. The connection to previous work is clear: while Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) proposed Cholesky-based updates for specific CMA-ES variants, this work extends the approach to the standard CMA-ES with both rank-1 and rank-µ updates. This is significant because the standard CMA-ES relies on the matrix square root, which cannot be directly replaced by a Cholesky factor without theoretical justification. The authors' Lemma 1 provides this justification by showing that the introduced error vanishes as the algorithm converges.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical argument is sound and well-structured, and the experimental design is comprehensive, testing multiple benchmark functions across various dimensions. The authors correctly identify the practical importance of their contribution: for large-scale optimization problems where covariance matrix updates dominate runtime, the proposed method offers significant speedups without compromising solution quality. The empirical results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of objective function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but achieves substantial reductions in wall-clock time, with the speedup increasing with problem dimensionality (up to 20x for d=64).

However, the paper's contribution to the field is somewhat incremental. Krause and Igel (2015) already demonstrated the feasibility of Cholesky-based updates for a specific CMA-ES variant (the (1+1)-CMA-ES), and this work extends their approach to the full standard CMA-ES. While this extension is technically sound, it does not introduce fundamentally new theoretical insights or address a previously unsolved problem. The connection to Beyer's convergence analysis (2014) for CMA-ES could strengthen the theoretical justification for Lemma 1, as Beyer's work provides a more rigorous foundation for the convergence assumptions.

The paper is generally well-written, though it contains some minor grammatical errors that should be corrected. For instance, in equation (1), the denominator should be σ_t, not σ. Additionally, in line 94, "which can by be motivated" should be "which can be motivated." These are easily fixable but should be addressed before publication.

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound with appropriate theoretical justification and thorough experimental validation. The algorithm implementation is clear, and the empirical results support the claims. The only weakness is the limited theoretical foundation for the convergence assumption in Lemma 1.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm description is precise, and the experimental setup is thoroughly documented. Minor grammatical issues are the only clarity concerns.

Originality: The extension of Cholesky-based updates to the standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates is novel, but it builds directly on prior work by Krause and Igel (2015). The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from this prior work, which weakens the perceived novelty.

Significance: The practical significance is strong for high-dimensional optimization problems where covariance matrix updates are computationally expensive. The demonstrated speedups (up to 20x for d=64) represent a meaningful improvement for real-world applications. However, the significance is limited to cases where maintaining the full covariance matrix remains feasible, as the paper itself acknowledges.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a practical, efficient implementation of CMA-ES with optimal time and storage complexity
- Demonstrates theoretically justified extension of prior Cholesky-based approaches
- Empirical results confirm comparable performance with substantial runtime improvements
- Addresses an important practical challenge in CMA-ES implementation

Arguments against acceptance:
- Contribution is incremental rather than fundamentally novel
- Limited theoretical justification for convergence assumptions
- Insufficient differentiation from prior work by Krause and Igel (2015)
- Does not introduce new theoretical insights into CMA-ES behavior

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution that addresses a practical implementation challenge in CMA-ES. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the paper makes a valuable practical contribution to the field, particularly for large-scale optimization problems. With minor revisions to strengthen the theoretical justification and better contextualize the contribution relative to prior work, this paper would be a strong candidate for publication. The reviewers should consider the practical significance of the approach, as it offers a straightforward implementation that can be adopted by practitioners without sacrificing solution quality.
