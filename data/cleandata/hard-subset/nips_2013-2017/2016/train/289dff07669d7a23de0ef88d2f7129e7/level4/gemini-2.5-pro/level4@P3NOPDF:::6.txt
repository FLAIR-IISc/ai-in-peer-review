### Summary

The paper proposes a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to have lower computational and storage complexity for the covariance matrix update. The authors claim that their modification, which directly updates the Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, reduces the complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) without altering the fundamental behavior of the algorithm.

### Strengths

The primary strength of this work lies in its practical utility. For applications where a full covariance matrix is necessary, the proposed method of directly updating the Cholesky factor is an effective engineering solution to avoid the costly O(n³) eigenvalue decomposition typically associated with CMA-ES. The reduction in computational overhead is a valuable contribution, and the approach may represent one of the most efficient ways to manage a full covariance matrix update in CMA-ES.

### Weaknesses and Major Concerns

Despite its practical merit, the paper has several significant issues in its current form that undermine its contribution.

1.  **Misleading Naming and Omission of Prior Work:** The name chosen for the algorithm, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," is highly problematic. This name was first introduced and is well-known for a similar algorithm developed by Suttorp et al. (2009). The authors fail to acknowledge this seminal work and instead introduce their variant as "Our CMA-ES variant, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES." This is a major scholarly oversight that misrepresents the novelty of their work and could confuse readers.

2.  **Incremental Contribution:** The core algorithmic novelty appears to be a minor modification of the work by Krause et al. (2015). Specifically, the main difference seems to be the application of the `rankOneUpdate` function within a `for` loop for each individual in the population, as opposed to a single aggregated update. The paper needs to better justify why this change constitutes a significant conceptual advance worthy of a publication, rather than an incremental implementation detail.

3.  **Inadequate and Potentially Misleading Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical analysis is insufficient and omits crucial comparisons.
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The paper must include an empirical comparison to the original Cholesky-CMA-ES by Suttorp et al. The authors claim Suttorp's method is prone to numerical instabilities, which contradicts the claims in the original paper and requires empirical evidence. Omitting this key baseline from Figure 3 gives a skewed perspective of the proposed method's relative performance and novelty.
    *   **Unexplained Discrepancies:** The authors claim their modification does not change the algorithm's behavior. However, Figure b) shows a clear difference in the objective function value at the plateau on the Cigar function between the reference CMA-ES and the proposed method. This contradiction needs to be addressed. It could be an artifact of how the median results are plotted, or it could indicate a genuine, unacknowledged change in the search dynamics.

4.  **Overstated Claims:**
    *   The claim of "Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" is too strong. This optimality holds only under the assumption that a full O(n²) covariance matrix is required. On simpler problem classes, such as separable or sphere functions, more efficient approaches that do not require a full covariance matrix (e.g., using a diagonal matrix) would be superior.
    *   The paper's positioning as a contribution to large-scale optimization is questionable. An O(n²) complexity is still prohibitive for truly large-scale problems, and this modification does not fundamentally change the scalability limitations of CMA-ES.
    *   The performance gain over the omitted Suttorp et al. algorithm appears to be a factor of two, which is a significant but not transformative improvement. The presentation should be calibrated to reflect this more accurately.

### Suggestions for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I strongly recommend the following revisions:

1.  **Resolve the Naming and Citation Issue:** Acknowledge the work of Suttorp et al. (2009) and select a new, unambiguous name for the proposed variant to avoid confusion and properly credit prior art.

2.  **Expand and Clarify the Experimental Study:**
    *   Perform a thorough empirical comparison that includes the Suttorp et al. variant and the Krause et al. variant. This is essential for properly contextualizing the paper's contribution.
    *   If including all variants makes the figures unreadable, present the most critical comparisons in the main text and provide the complete set of figures in the supplementary material.
    *   Investigate and explain the performance discrepancy observed on the Cigar function in Figure b).

3.  **Clarify the Algorithmic Contribution:** Provide a more detailed explanation of the paper's core algorithmic novelty. If the primary contribution is the loop structure in Algorithm 1, the authors must convincingly argue why this is a non-trivial and significant improvement over existing methods.

### Minor Comments

The statement, "Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step," is inaccurate for modern implementations. To amortize the O(n³) cost, this decomposition is typically performed periodically (e.g., every n/10 generations), not in every single step.
