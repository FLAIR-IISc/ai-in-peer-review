

The paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the standard matrix square root computation with a lower-triangular Cholesky factorization. This modification reduces the per-iteration computational complexity from O(d³) to O(μd²), where d is the problem dimension and μ (typically O(log d)) is the number of successful samples per iteration. The core innovation lies in maintaining and updating the Cholesky factor directly, avoiding explicit covariance matrix storage and square root computations. Crucially, the authors argue that the approximation—replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ (where Cₜ = AₜAᵀₜ)—does not degrade optimization performance. They provide theoretical justification via Lemma 1, showing that the orthogonal correction matrix Eₜ converges to identity as the covariance matrix stabilizes near the optimum, making the error in the step-size adaptation negligible under typical convergence conditions. Empirical validation on standard benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Rosenbrock, etc.) demonstrates equivalent function evaluation counts to the standard CMA-ES (with delayed decomposition, CMA-ES-Ref) but significantly faster wall-clock times, with speedups of up to 20× for d=64. The method also offers practical advantages: optimal storage complexity, free eigenvalue extraction for conditioning monitoring, and numerically stable inversion.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence argument for Eₜ (Lemma 1) is well-motivated and addresses a key gap in prior work (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), which lacked justification for performance equivalence.  
2. **Practical Impact:** The O(μd²) complexity is asymptotically optimal for full-covariance adaptation, directly addressing a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES scalability. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate runtime improvements without compromising solution quality.  
3. **Clear Presentation:** The algorithm pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and complexity analysis are well-structured, making the contribution accessible to the optimization community. The comparison to CMA-ES-Ref (delayed decomposition) and Suttorp-CMA-ES provides strong baselines.  

**Weaknesses and Considerations:**  
While the theoretical and empirical contributions are sound, two considerations warrant attention:  
1. **Practical Relevance:** The authors rightly note that CMA-ES runtime is often negligible compared to objective function evaluations in real-world applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, policy search). The speedup is most impactful when function evaluations are cheap (e.g., milliseconds) and d is large (e.g., d > 64). However, the paper could better contextualize this niche—most CMA-ES applications (e.g., reinforcement learning) operate on d ≪ 64. A brief discussion of *which* scenarios would benefit most (e.g., large-scale neural network training where function evaluations are fast) would strengthen the argument.  
2. **Empirical Depth:** The supplementary experiments on Eₜ evolution (referenced in Section 4) are promising but not fully integrated into the main text. To bolster confidence in the asymptotic argument, the authors should:  
   - Include plots of ∥Eₜ − I∥ over iterations for key trajectories (e.g., Rosenbrock, d=128), showing convergence to zero.  
   - Extend the quadratic test suite (as suggested by Stich & Müller, 2012) to systematically vary the spectral condition number and report both solution quality and Eₜ error.  
   - Compare against a *non-delayed* standard CMA-ES (without the O(d³) delay strategy) to isolate the Cholesky update’s effect from the delay heuristic.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a technically solid contribution that elegantly resolves a persistent scalability bottleneck in CMA-ES. The theoretical justification for performance equivalence is compelling, and the empirical results robustly support the claimed speedup. While the practical impact is context-dependent, the method is clearly superior for high-dimensional problems where covariance updates dominate runtime—a scenario increasingly relevant in modern ML (e.g., large-scale policy search). The paper advances the state-of-the-art in derivative-free optimization and provides a valuable tool for the community.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept**. The work meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance. The minor suggestions above are constructive refinements, not critical flaws. The method is both novel (addressing a gap in prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES work) and impactful for a core optimization algorithm widely used in ML. The authors have clearly demonstrated its value for scaling CMA-ES to larger problems where full covariance adaptation remains feasible.
