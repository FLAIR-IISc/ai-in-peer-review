This paper addresses the primary computational bottleneck of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a state-of-the-art algorithm for derivative-free black-box optimization. The dominant cost in CMA-ES, particularly for high-dimensional problems where function evaluations are cheap, is the `O(d^3)` complexity of decomposing the `d x d` covariance matrix in each iteration. The authors propose a new variant, the "Cholesky-CMA-ES", which reduces this complexity to an asymptotically optimal `O(µd^2)` by directly maintaining and updating a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix.

The core technical contribution lies in adapting the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The standard CSA relies on the inverse of the matrix square root (`C^{-1/2}`) for normalization, which is computationally expensive and cannot be easily substituted with a Cholesky factor. The authors propose replacing `C^{-1/2}` with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (`A^{-1}`). They provide a compelling theoretical justification (Lemma 1) that this approximation is sound. The reasoning is that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the sequence of orthogonal matrices relating the matrix square root to the unique triangular Cholesky factor also converges, meaning the rotational error introduced by the substitution diminishes over time. This insight allows the algorithm to retain the performance characteristics of the original CMA-ES while gaining significant computational advantages.

The paper is a very strong contribution to the field of optimization and is highly relevant to the NIPS community, where CMA-ES is frequently used for applications such as direct policy search in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper presents a major practical improvement to a cornerstone optimization algorithm. By drastically reducing both the time and memory complexity of the covariance matrix update without sacrificing optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations), the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is poised to become the new standard implementation for medium- to large-scale problems. This work makes CMA-ES accessible and efficient for a broader range of problems.

2.  **Technical Quality and Originality:** The proposed method is elegant and technically sound. While previous work has explored updating Cholesky factors, this paper is the first to provide a well-justified method for incorporating this idea into the standard CMA-ES, particularly by tackling the challenge within the CSA mechanism. The theoretical argument for why the approximation works is a novel and important contribution that provides confidence in the approach.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The authors conduct a careful and comprehensive experimental comparison against the standard CMA-ES and other variants on a suite of benchmark functions. The results convincingly demonstrate the primary claims: the Cholesky-CMA-ES requires a similar number of function evaluations to converge but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time. The performance gains scale impressively with the problem dimension, as predicted by the complexity analysis.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established, the new method is described precisely, and the theoretical justification is presented logically. The connection to previous work is clearly articulated, highlighting the novelty of the current contribution.

5.  **Additional Practical Benefits:** Beyond the primary complexity improvements, the Cholesky-CMA-ES offers other advantages. It is numerically stable, and because the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are related to the eigenvalues, it provides information about the conditioning of the covariance matrix essentially for free.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is of very high quality, and the points below are minor suggestions for further strengthening the manuscript.

1.  In the discussion of Figure 2, the authors note that the speedup becomes more pronounced with increasing dimensionality. An interesting observation is the slight tapering of this trend for the Cigar and Discus functions at `d=256`. The text correctly explains this phenomenon as an effect of the matrix size exceeding the L2 cache. This is an important practical insight. To make it more prominent, I suggest adding a brief note about this hardware-related effect directly in the figure caption.

2.  The paper states that the algorithm was implemented in the Shark library using LAPACK for efficiency. This is excellent for reproducibility. To further solidify the empirical claims about wall-clock time, it would be beneficial to explicitly confirm that all compared variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, etc.) were implemented and benchmarked within the same language and computational framework, thus minimizing confounding variables arising from different implementation qualities.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a significant and practical advance for a widely used optimization algorithm. The method is novel, theoretically justified, and empirically validated. It is well-written and will be of great interest and utility to both researchers and practitioners in machine learning and optimization. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
