This paper presents Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that achieves optimal quadratic time and space complexity for the covariance matrix update. The standard CMA-ES is a powerful derivative-free optimization method, but its computational cost is dominated by the O(d^3) complexity of computing the matrix square root of the d x d covariance matrix in each iteration. Previous work has attempted to reduce this cost by delaying updates or restricting the covariance matrix structure, often at the cost of optimization performance. More recent approaches, such as those by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015), have proposed updating a Cholesky factor directly, but adapting this to the standard CMA-ES is non-trivial due to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism's reliance on the inverse matrix square root, C^{-1/2}. The key contribution of this work is to replace this term with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor, A^{-1}, and to provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by this approximation is expected to be small and decrease as the algorithm converges. The authors empirically validate that this modification does not degrade performance in terms of function evaluations while significantly reducing wall-clock time.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and significant bottleneck in one of the most widely used derivative-free optimization algorithms. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES makes the algorithm significantly more practical for higher-dimensional problems where function evaluations are relatively cheap, such as in direct policy search. The empirical results, showing a speed-up of over 20x for d=64, demonstrate a clear and impactful advance.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The proposed modification to the CSA update is supported by a solid theoretical argument (Lemma 1) regarding the convergence of the rotation matrices, which provides confidence that the approximation is well-founded. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using standard benchmark functions and comparing against relevant baselines, including a state-of-the-art reference implementation. The claims of reduced runtime are well-supported by the experiments.
*   **Originality:** While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor is not entirely new, the novelty lies in successfully integrating this into the standard CMA-ES by providing a justified approximation for the CSA mechanism. This specific modification, which enables the use of a triangular Cholesky factor while preserving performance, is a novel and important contribution that differs from previous attempts.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It effectively motivates the problem, reviews prior work, explains the technical challenge posed by the CSA update, and presents its solution and justification in a logical sequence. The inclusion of algorithms and detailed experimental results makes the work easy to understand and assess.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The paper claims in the abstract and Section 3 that the proposed update is "numerically stable." While this is often true for Cholesky updates compared to forming and decomposing a covariance matrix, this point is not further substantiated in the paper. A brief theoretical explanation or reference explaining why this rank-one update to the Cholesky factor (Algorithm 2) is numerically more stable would strengthen this claim.
*   The conclusion correctly positions the work as an improvement for problems where a full covariance matrix is still feasible, and acknowledges that for extremely high dimensions, low-dimensional approximations are necessary. The paper could be further improved by briefly discussing the potential for combining the proposed efficient update mechanism with such low-rank methods (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015). This would provide valuable context for practitioners and highlight important avenues for future research in scaling CMA-ES.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that provides a practical and well-justified solution to a long-standing efficiency issue in CMA-ES. The proposed algorithm should replace the standard implementation in many use cases.
