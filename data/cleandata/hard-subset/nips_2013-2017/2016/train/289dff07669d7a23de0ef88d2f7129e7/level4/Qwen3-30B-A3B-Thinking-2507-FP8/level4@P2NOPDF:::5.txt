

Review:  
This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel update scheme for the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that reduces computational complexity from O(d³) to O(μd²), where d is the problem dimension and μ (typically constant, not O(log d)) is the number of samples per iteration. The method replaces full covariance matrix updates with a lower-triangular Cholesky decomposition, eliminating the need to estimate a random orthogonal matrix Eₜ at each step. Under restrictive convergence assumptions—specifically, that the sequence of covariance matrices approaches an unknown target—the authors argue asymptotically that Eₜ converges to the identity matrix, rendering the schemes equivalent.  

The contribution is evaluated via standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark functions, comparing Cholesky-CMA-ES against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates. The method demonstrates significant speedups without performance degradation on synthetic tests, offering a valuable acceleration for the CMA-ES algorithm. As the de facto standard for non-convex black-box optimization when derivative information is unavailable, this work addresses a meaningful bottleneck.  

However, the practical relevance of the speedup requires careful consideration. In most CMA-ES applications, the dominant cost is the black-box function evaluation, not the CMA-ES update itself. For problems with d ≤ 50 (common in practice), CMA-ES runtime is negligible compared to oracle calls. The proposed speedup is only impactful when function evaluations are extremely fast (e.g., milliseconds) *and* dimensionality is high—scenarios that are uncommon in typical use cases. Thus, while the theoretical and empirical results are sound, the paper should more clearly contextualize the target domain where this acceleration matters.  

Theoretical guarantees for CMA-ES are notoriously challenging, so empirical validation is essential. To strengthen the presentation, I recommend three key additions:  
1. **Tracking Eₜ evolution**: Visualize Eₜ’s trajectory (e.g., in Frobenius or spectral norms) during optimization by running both full CMA-ES and Cholesky-CMA-ES in parallel on representative trajectories. This would empirically confirm the asymptotic convergence claim and quantify fluctuations in Eₜ.  
2. **Baseline runtime comparison**: Include runtime data for standard CMA-ES (without delay strategies) to clarify the magnitude of the speedup in realistic settings.  
3. **Spectral sensitivity analysis**: Extend the quadratic test suite (as in Stich & Mueller, PPSN 2012) to vary the spectrum of quadratic forms continuously. Plot solution quality and Eₜ error against spectral condition numbers to demonstrate robustness to ill-conditioning—a common challenge in black-box optimization.  

A final, compelling addition would be a **real-world case study**. For instance, applying the method to real-time robotics policy gradients (e.g., reinforcement learning for agile locomotion) where low-latency parameter updates are critical and problem dimensions remain moderate (e.g., d < 50). This would directly address the practical relevance gap and validate the approach beyond synthetic benchmarks.  

While the core contribution is solid and the empirical results are convincing, the current presentation understates the narrow practical niche where speedups are meaningful. The suggested experiments would not only bolster the paper’s claims but also align the work more closely with the needs of practitioners. The Cholesky-CMA-ES method is a technically sound advancement, and with these refinements, it would make a stronger case for adoption in high-performance black-box optimization.
