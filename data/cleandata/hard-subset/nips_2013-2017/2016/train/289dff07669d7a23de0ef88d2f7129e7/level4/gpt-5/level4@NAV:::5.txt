The manuscript presents a new CMA-ES update variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation (cost O(d^3)) with a novel scheme running in O(mu d^2), where d is the problem dimension and mu is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The main idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix (and its square root). The discrepancy between Cholesky-CMA-ES and the original CMA-ES arises from not estimating a (random) orthogonal matrix E_t at each step. Under fairly restrictive assumptions (i.e., assuming convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target covariance), the authors give an asymptotic argument that E_t converges to the identity, making the two schemes asymptotically equivalent. The validity and overall speed-up of Cholesky-CMA-ES are demonstrated using standard runtime and convergence plots on selected objective functions and by comparisons with two state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives that defer full covariance updates by different mechanisms. This paper constitutes a solid contribution to accelerating the celebrated CMA-ES algorithm, which is arguably the leading method for non-convex black-box optimization when little is known about the objective’s topology and first- or higher-order information is hard to obtain or unhelpful. The proposed approach substantially reduces computational cost and shows no loss in optimization performance on the synthetic test cases. That said, a general limitation of any CMA-ES acceleration is that, in practice, total optimization time is often dominated by black-box oracle (objective) evaluations, and the CMA-ES runtime is frequently negligible by comparison. Moreover, in typical CMA-ES applications, dimensionality rarely exceeds a few tens of variables. Only when single evaluation times are on the order of milliseconds or less and the dimension is higher does the CMA-ES runtime become a significant factor; this is the targeted scenario here (which may be less common in practice). As theoretical guarantees are notoriously difficult for a complex scheme like CMA-ES, I suggest several additional empirical studies and plots to strengthen the results. First, it would be informative to track the evolution of E_t along selected optimization trajectories, e.g., in different norms; I envision running the full CMA-ES while computing the approximation in parallel for direct comparison, thereby providing empirical evidence on the temporal fluctuations of E_t. Second, for completeness, it would be helpful to include actual runtimes and performance of CMA-ES without any delay strategies in this paper. Third, although the test functions used are standard in the evolutionary optimization community, I would prefer, for quadratic objectives, a simulation study that continuously varies the spectrum of the quadratic forms (see, e.g., Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012), and compares solution quality and the error in E_t as a function of the spectral distribution. Fourth, it would be valuable to complement the simulations with a real-world experiment—e.g., policy gradients in real-time robotics—where speed truly matters and the parameter space is of moderate size.
