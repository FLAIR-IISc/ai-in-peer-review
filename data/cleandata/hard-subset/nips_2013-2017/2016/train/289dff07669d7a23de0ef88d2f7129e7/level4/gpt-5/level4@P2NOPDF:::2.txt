This paper replaces the eigen-decomposition in CMA-ES with Cholesky-based updates to reduce time and memory costs. The paper is generally clear, the method appears sound, and the experimental speedups are promising. The approach could plausibly become a standard CMA-ES variant. However, the contribution seems incremental: it appears to be a straightforward extension of “A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies,” with essentially the same method. If this interpretation is incorrect, please clarify the novel elements.

Major concerns:
1) Equivalence to Eq. (2): Even if space is limited and prior work contains a proof, this paper should provide at least a self-contained derivation or proof sketch demonstrating equivalence to formula (2). This is central to validating the method.
2) Complexity and speed: You claim O(μ d^2) complexity, which matches CMA-ES-Ref. Please explain in detail—preferably using Θ-notation and an operation-level breakdown—why your implementation is faster. Identify the constant-factor savings (e.g., avoided eigen-decompositions, fewer matrix–vector products, cache-friendly triangular solves), and quantify them analytically or via microbenchmarks.
3) Path error claim: You state that the path error is small and decreases over time, which conflicts with Hansen’s prior claim. Because your theoretical analysis is not deterministic, you should empirically validate this with dedicated measurements of path error over iterations across functions and dimensions. Comparing only final optimization performance is insufficient to support this claim.

Minor comments:
1) Notation: Please check carefully. For example, wk in Algorithm 2 should be alpha_k; n in Table 1 should be d.
2) Typo: “F” should be “f” in line 185.

Recommendation: While the idea is practically appealing and the results are encouraging, the paper in its current form lacks sufficient novelty and omits essential analyses. With a clear articulation of what is new relative to prior rank-one Cholesky-based updates, a proof (or proof sketch) of equivalence to Eq. (2), a precise complexity comparison that explains the observed speedups, and experiments that directly evaluate the path error over time, the work would be much stronger. As it stands, I do not think it meets the bar for NIPS.
