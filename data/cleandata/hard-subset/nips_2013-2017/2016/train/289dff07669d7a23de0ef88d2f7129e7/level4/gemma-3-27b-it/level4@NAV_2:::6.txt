The paper proposes a modification to the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) aimed at reducing computational cost and memory usage during covariance matrix updates. The authors present the Cholesky-CMA-ES, which utilizes a triangular Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root, coupled with a modification to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The core idea is to update the Cholesky factor directly, achieving quadratic time complexity. The authors argue this modification maintains the algorithm’s performance while improving efficiency.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in CMA-ES optimization, notably Hansen and Ostermeier [2001], which introduced the algorithm, and subsequent efforts to address the computational bottleneck of covariance matrix updates, such as those by Poland and Zell [2001], Ros and Hansen [2008], Suttorp et al. [2009], and Krause and Igel [2015]. The proposed method extends the Cholesky factor update approach from Krause and Igel [2015] to the standard CMA-ES framework.

The strengths of the paper lie in its rigorous theoretical justification for the proposed modification and its empirical validation. The authors provide a clear argument, supported by Lemma 1, for why replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor in the CSA mechanism does not fundamentally alter the algorithm's convergence behavior. The experimental results demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance to the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations, while offering significant speed improvements, particularly in higher dimensions. The analysis of runtime complexity and memory footprint is also well presented.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The naming of the algorithm as "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is potentially confusing, as this name was previously used by Suttorp et al. [2009] to describe a different, albeit related, approach. This omission could lead to ambiguity and requires clarification. The algorithmic contribution – applying rankOneUpdate in a loop – feels incremental and requires more detailed justification of its significance. While the authors acknowledge the existing work by Krause and Igel [2015], a more direct algorithmic comparison would strengthen the paper. The empirical comparison, particularly Figure 2, shows relatively small differences and a better visual comparison to Suttorp et al. [2009] and Krause and Igel [2015] would be valuable. The claim of "optimal" complexity is somewhat overstated, as it doesn't consider scenarios where simpler covariance structures (e.g., diagonal matrices) suffice. Additionally, the observed discrepancy in the plateau region of the Cigar function in Figure 1(b) warrants further investigation and discussion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated modification to the widely used CMA-ES algorithm.
*   Offers a practical improvement in runtime and memory efficiency, particularly for high-dimensional optimization problems.
*   The analysis and presentation are generally clear and well-structured.
*   Addresses a relevant challenge in CMA-ES optimization – the computational cost of covariance matrix updates.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The algorithmic contribution is relatively incremental.
*   The naming convention is potentially misleading.
*   The empirical comparisons could be more comprehensive, including results against existing Cholesky-CMA-ES variants.
*   The claim of “optimal” complexity may be an overstatement.



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. While the algorithmic novelty is limited, the theoretical justification and empirical validation are convincing. Addressing the naming issue and strengthening the comparisons to related work would further enhance the paper's impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
