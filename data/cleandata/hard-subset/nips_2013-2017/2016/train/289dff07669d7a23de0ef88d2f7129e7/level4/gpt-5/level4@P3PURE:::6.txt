This paper proposes a CMA-ES variant claimed to provide “Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity,” arguing that the modification preserves algorithmic behavior while reducing computational cost. The method is introduced as “Cholesky-CMA-ES,” which is problematic: this name was first used by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a closely related algorithm and is already well known. The paper does not acknowledge this naming conflict and instead states, “Our CMA-ES variant, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES.”

From a technical standpoint, the proposal is an incremental modification of Krause et al. (2015). The only substantive algorithmic change is to call rankOneUpdate (already present in Krause et al.) in a loop for all individuals, rather than once as in Plus strategies. This should be clearly explained, since it appears to be the main algorithmic difference.

Empirically, the results raise questions. On the Cigar function in Figure b), the observed plateau differs between CMA-ES-Ref and the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES. This may stem from how medians are plotted, but it should be addressed explicitly. More importantly, the experimental evaluation should include a comparison to the Cholesky-CMA-ES of Suttorp et al. (empirically) and the algorithmic variant of Krause et al. (algorithmically). The omission of the Suttorp variant from Figure 3 is especially concerning and risks overstating the contribution.

The claim of “Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity” is also misleading. On the Sphere function, no covariance matrix is needed; on separable functions, a diagonal covariance suffices. In these cases, the claimed optimality does not hold. Even when maintaining a full covariance, the proposed approach is only about twice as efficient as the Cholesky-CMA-ES of Suttorp, which is omitted from the comparisons. While the paper suggests that Suttorp’s approach suffers from numerical instabilities, the original work indicates the opposite; this discrepancy needs careful reconciliation with evidence.

Regarding scalability, the proposed approach does not change the large-scale optimization picture: the complexity remains quadratic. That said, if one insists on using the full covariance matrix, the modification may indeed be among the most practical ways to reduce runtime and memory within that design choice.

Overall, the contribution appears too incremental—algorithmically it amounts to introducing a “for” loop to apply rankOneUpdate across all individuals. To make the paper suitable for publication, I recommend the following:

- Resolve the naming issue to avoid confusion with Suttorp et al. (2009).
- Include experiments with the variants of Suttorp et al. and Krause et al., and update all three figures accordingly. If the figures become cluttered, show only the best-performing variant in the main text and provide full figures in the Supplementary Material.
- Clearly articulate that the primary algorithmic modification is the loop over rankOneUpdate in Algorithm 1, or provide a convincing explanation of additional contributions beyond this change.

Minor note: The statement “Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step” is inaccurate. In proper implementations, the eigendecomposition is performed periodically, not at every update.
