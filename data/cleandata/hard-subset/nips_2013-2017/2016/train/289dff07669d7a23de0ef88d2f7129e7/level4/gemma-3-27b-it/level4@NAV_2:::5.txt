The manuscript describes a novel CMA-ES update scheme, the Cholesky-CMA-ES, that approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation (with complexity O(d^3)) with a scheme running in O(µd^2), where d is the optimization problem dimension and µ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log(d))). The core idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky matrix instead of a full covariance matrix. The difference compared to the original CMA-ES stems from not explicitly estimating an orthogonal matrix Et in each step. Under specific assumptions regarding the convergence of covariance matrices, the authors argue that the sequence of Et will converge to the identity matrix, rendering the two schemes asymptotically equivalent. The validity and speed-up of the Cholesky-CMA-ES are demonstrated through runtime and convergence plots on benchmark functions, comparing it to state-of-the-art CMA-ES alternatives with delayed covariance matrix updates. This paper provides a valuable contribution to accelerating the well-established CMA-ES algorithm, a leading method for non-convex, black-box optimization, especially when limited information is available about the objective function’s topology and derivatives are inaccessible or unhelpful. The proposed method significantly speeds up the algorithm without compromising optimization performance on synthetic test cases. A potential limitation, common to optimization speed-up proposals, is that the overall optimization process often remains dominated by the runtime of the objective function evaluation itself, especially when dealing with lower-dimensional problems. The proposed method is most advantageous when function evaluations are fast and dimensionality is high—a scenario potentially less frequent in practice. Given the difficulty of obtaining rigorous theoretical guarantees for complex schemes like CMA-ES, I suggest further empirical evaluation to strengthen the presentation. First, visualizing the evolution of Et along optimization trajectories (e.g., using norms) in comparison to the full CMA-ES could provide more empirical support for its convergence towards the identity matrix. This could involve running both algorithms concurrently. Second, including performance results for the full CMA-ES without delay strategies would offer a more complete comparison. Third, on quadratic functions, a simulation varying the spectrum of quadratic forms (inspired by Stich and Müller, PPSN 2012) would demonstrate how solution quality and the error in Et correlate with spectral distributions. Finally, a real-world application, such as policy gradients in robotics, where speed is critical and dimensionality is manageable, would provide compelling evidence of the method’s practical benefits.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is based on a solid mathematical foundation and addresses a key bottleneck in CMA-ES – the covariance matrix update.
*   **Originality:** The approach of directly updating the Cholesky factor, coupled with the theoretical justification for its validity, is a novel contribution.
*   **Significance:** The potential speed-up, particularly for high-dimensional problems, is significant and could broaden the applicability of CMA-ES.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, method, and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practical Relevance:** The benefits might be less pronounced in scenarios where the objective function evaluation is the dominant cost.
*   **Empirical Coverage:** While the experimental results are promising, extending them to real-world applications and a wider range of synthetic benchmarks would further strengthen the claims.
*   **Theoretical Assumptions:** The convergence argument relies on assumptions that may not always hold in practice.
