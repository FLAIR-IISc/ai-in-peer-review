The paper presents a modification to CMA-ES, termed “Cholesky-CMA-ES,” aiming to reduce computational cost without altering the algorithm’s behavior. This naming is problematic, as “Cholesky-CMA-ES” was previously used by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a related algorithm – a connection the authors fail to acknowledge. Instead, they present their variant as “Our CMA-ES variant, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES.” The method builds incrementally upon the work of Krause et al. (2015), with the primary algorithmic contribution being the iterative application of the rankOneUpdate operation (also present in Krause et al.’s work) to all individuals, rather than the single application used in Plus strategies. 

Empirical results on the Cigar function (Figure b) reveal a discrepancy in the observed plateau objective function values between the reference CMA-ES and the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES, potentially due to the plotting of median results.  The paper lacks a comparison to both the Cholesky-CMA-ES implementation by Suttorp et al. (empirically) and a detailed algorithmic comparison to the approach of Krause et al. The claim of “Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity” is potentially misleading; it does not represent an optimal solution for all problems, particularly those like the Sphere function where the covariance matrix is unnecessary, or separable functions where a diagonal approximation suffices.  The proposed method offers only a two-fold reduction in cost compared to the Cholesky CMA-ES variant by Suttorp et al., which the authors claim is prone to numerical instability – a claim contradicted by the original Suttorp et al. paper. The omission of Suttorp et al.’s method from Figure 3 further obscures the true contribution of this work. 

While the quadratic complexity remains, potentially limiting benefits for large-scale optimization, I acknowledge that this modification may represent the best practical improvement for scenarios requiring a full covariance matrix. Overall, the work appears too incremental—algorithmically amounting to the addition of a single “for” loop. I recommend the following revisions: a) Resolve the naming conflict; b) Include experimental comparisons to the variants of Suttorp et al. and Krause et al., and update all three figures accordingly. If this compromises readability, present the full figures in the Supplementary Material; c) Clearly articulate the significance of the “for” loop in Algorithm 1, or explain why its inclusion should not be considered a substantial algorithmic contribution.  Finally, the statement “Typically, an eigenvalue decomposition of the covariance matrix is performed in each update step” is inaccurate, as proper implementations typically perform this decomposition periodically.
