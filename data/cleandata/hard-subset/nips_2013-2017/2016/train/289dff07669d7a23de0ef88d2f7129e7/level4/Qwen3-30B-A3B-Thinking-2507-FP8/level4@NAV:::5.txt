

The manuscript introduces a novel CMA-ES update scheme, Cholesky-CMA-ES, which approximates the original covariance matrix adaptation (complexity O(d³)) via a method operating in O(μd²), where d is the problem dimension and μ is the number of samples per iteration (typically O(log d)). The core idea maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix (and its square root). The distinction from standard CMA-ES arises from omitting the estimation of a (random) orthogonal matrix Et at each step. Under highly restrictive assumptions (specifically, convergence of a sequence of covariance matrices to an unknown target), the authors present an asymptotic argument demonstrating that Et converges to the identity matrix, thereby rendering the schemes equivalent. The method’s validity and speedup are validated through standard runtime and convergence plots on benchmark objective functions, comparing it against two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates. This work constitutes a solid contribution toward accelerating the CMA-ES algorithm, widely regarded as the premier method for non-convex black-box optimization when minimal problem knowledge exists and derivative information is unavailable or unhelpful. The proposed approach significantly reduces runtime without compromising performance on synthetic test cases. However, a critical limitation of any CMA-ES speed-up is that, in practice, the overall optimization time is dominated by the black-box oracle (objective function evaluation), making CMA-ES runtime negligible unless function evaluations are very fast (e.g., milliseconds) and dimensionality is high—conditions that may be uncommon. Given CMA-ES is typically applied to problems with dimensions ≤ tens, the targeted scenario has limited practical relevance. While theoretical guarantees for CMA-ES are exceptionally challenging, I recommend three additional empirical analyses to strengthen the results: (1) Visualize the evolution of Et (e.g., in various norms) across optimization trajectories by simultaneously computing the full CMA-ES and its approximation; (2) Include runtime and performance data for standard CMA-ES without delay strategies for completeness; (3) Conduct a spectral variation study on quadratic functions (varying the spectrum continuously, as in Stich and Mueller, PPSN 2012), comparing solution quality and Et error against spectral distribution; (4) Provide a real-world validation, such as policy gradient optimization in real-time robotics, where speed is critical and parameter spaces are modest.
