

The paper proposes a variant of CMA-ES called "Cholesky-CMA-ES" that replaces the matrix square root with a triangular Cholesky factor in the covariance matrix adaptation process. The authors claim this modification achieves optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity (O(μd²)) while preserving the algorithm's performance in terms of objective function evaluations. The paper builds on prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), though it misleadingly introduces its approach as a new variant without proper attribution to the existing "Cholesky-CMA-ES" name used in the literature.

Quality: The technical contribution is modest, consisting of a minor algorithmic adjustment to Krause and Igel's approach—applying the rank-one update in a loop for all individuals rather than once as in "plus" strategies. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 is sound, showing that the error from replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor vanishes as the algorithm converges. However, the claim of "optimal" complexity is misleading: the method is only optimal for non-separable ill-conditioned problems, not for separable functions where diagonal adaptation suffices (e.g., Sphere). The empirical results (Figure 1b) show a discrepancy in the plateau objective values between CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES, which the authors fail to explain—this may stem from how median results are plotted. The paper also incorrectly claims Suttorp et al.'s method is "prone to numerical instabilities," contradicting their original paper.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from background to method to experiments. However, the naming confusion undermines clarity, and the paper's claims about optimality require more nuance. The implementation details in Algorithms 1-2 are precise and useful for replication.

Originality: The algorithmic contribution is incremental. The core idea (using Cholesky factors for covariance updates) was already explored by Suttorp et al. (2009), and Krause and Igel (2015) had already extended it to the standard CMA-ES. The paper fails to properly distinguish its contribution from these prior works, which weakens its novelty claim. The authors do not explain why the "for" loop in Algorithm 1 is a meaningful innovation rather than a straightforward extension.

Significance: The practical benefit of reducing covariance update complexity from O(d³) to O(d²) is modest, as the method still scales quadratically with dimensionality. For large-scale optimization where full covariance matrices become infeasible, low-dimensional approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) remain necessary. The paper does not convincingly argue why this minor improvement is significant compared to existing approaches. The omission of Suttorp's method in Figure 3 misleads readers about the actual contribution, as the proposed approach is only marginally faster than Suttorp's method (not "two times cheaper" as claimed).

Strengths: The theoretical justification for the algorithm's behavior is well-developed, and the empirical results demonstrate a clear runtime advantage (up to 20× faster for d=64). The implementation in Shark and careful experimental design across multiple benchmark functions are commendable.

Weaknesses: The most serious flaw is the failure to properly compare with Suttorp's Cholesky-CMA-ES, which the authors claim to be "prone to numerical instabilities" without justification. This omission, combined with the misleading naming, significantly undermines the paper's credibility. The paper also overstates its contribution by claiming "optimal" complexity without qualifying that this applies only to specific problem types.

The paper would be strengthened by: (1) correcting the naming issue and properly citing prior work; (2) including a direct comparison with Suttorp's method in all figures; (3) clarifying the conditions under which the method is beneficial (non-separable ill-conditioned problems) versus when it's unnecessary (separable problems); and (4) addressing the discrepancy in Figure 1b.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound but incremental improvement to CMA-ES. While the implementation is practical and the runtime benefits are demonstrated, the lack of proper comparison with existing Cholesky-based approaches and the overstated claims about optimality weaken its contribution. The work is valuable for practitioners who need to optimize CMA-ES for problems where full covariance updates are feasible but expensive, but it does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS. I recommend rejection due to the lack of sufficient novelty and the misleading presentation of the contribution.
