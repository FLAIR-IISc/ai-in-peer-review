

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor, resulting in a method they call "Cholesky-CMA-ES." The authors claim this modification reduces time and space complexity while maintaining the algorithm's performance. The work builds on previous approaches by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who also explored Cholesky factor updates for CMA-ES variants, though the authors do not adequately acknowledge these prior contributions.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the optimization literature, addressing a practical bottleneck in CMA-ES implementation. The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 is reasonable, showing that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor diminishes as the algorithm converges. The experimental results demonstrate a significant speedup in wall-clock time (up to 20x for d=64) while maintaining equivalent function evaluation counts compared to the reference implementation. The authors also correctly note that the Cholesky-CMA-ES provides the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free, which is useful for monitoring problem conditioning.

The paper's main strengths include:
1. A clear and well-structured presentation of the algorithmic modification
2. A theoretically sound argument for why the modification preserves performance
3. Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions
4. Practical implementation details that facilitate reproducibility

However, several significant weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution:

First, the naming of the proposed method as "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is problematic and misleading. Suttorp et al. (2009) first introduced the term "Cholesky-CMA-ES" for a similar approach, and the authors' failure to acknowledge this is a serious oversight. The paper states "our CMA-ES variant, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES," which implies novelty where none exists. This is particularly concerning given that the authors' method is a direct extension of Krause and Igel (2015), who also used the Cholesky factor for covariance updates in a different CMA-ES variant.

Second, the algorithmic contribution is minimal. The core modification is simply replacing a single rank-one update with a loop over all μ individuals (as seen in Algorithm 1). The authors claim this is "a numerically stable quadratic-time covariance matrix update scheme," but this is essentially the same approach as Krause and Igel (2015), extended to the standard CMA-ES. The paper fails to properly compare with Suttorp's method, which the authors claim is "prone to numerical instabilities" but Suttorp et al. (2009) explicitly state their approach is numerically stable.

Third, the claim about "optimal" complexity is misleading. The authors state their method achieves "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity," but this is only true for non-separable ill-conditioned problems where the full covariance matrix is necessary. For separable functions (e.g., Sphere), the optimal approach would be to use only the diagonal elements, which the authors acknowledge but do not address. The paper would be stronger if it explicitly discussed when their method provides advantages over simpler alternatives.

Fourth, the experimental section has critical omissions. Figure 1 shows results for Cholesky-CMA-ES versus CMA-ES-Ref, but omits Suttorp-CMA-ES, which the authors claim is "prone to numerical instabilities." This omission is problematic because it creates a misleading impression of the contribution. The plateau observed in Figure 1b for the Cigar function appears to differ between CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES, but the authors do not explain this discrepancy, which may relate to their median-based plotting approach.

The paper's significance is limited by its incremental nature. While the proposed modification does offer practical benefits for applications where full covariance matrix updates are required, it does not address the fundamental scalability limitations of CMA-ES (which remains O(d²) complexity). The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion but do not sufficiently emphasize that this approach does not solve the large-scale optimization problem (where d is very large), which is better addressed by methods like Loshchilov (2015) that use low-dimensional approximations.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and the algorithmic description is accessible. The experimental setup is thorough and appropriate for the claims made. However, the failure to properly contextualize the work relative to prior literature significantly reduces the paper's impact.

In summary, while the paper presents a technically sound modification to CMA-ES with practical benefits, its contribution is substantially weakened by:
1. Inadequate acknowledgment of prior work (Suttorp et al. 2009)
2. Minimal algorithmic novelty (essentially extending Krause and Igel 2015)
3. Misleading claims about "optimal" complexity
4. Omission of critical experimental comparisons

I recommend rejection, as the work does not sufficiently advance the state of the art for a venue like NIPS. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to properly contextualize their contribution, address the naming issue, provide fair comparisons with existing methods, and clarify the scope of their "optimal" complexity claims before it would be suitable for publication. The current version fails to demonstrate sufficient originality given the prior literature.
