- Proposes "Cholesky-CMA-ES" replacing covariance matrix square root with a triangular Cholesky factor to reduce storage/runtime to O(μd²).  
- Claims optimal asymptotic complexity and preserved evaluation performance, but presents this as a new variant despite prior use of the name in literature.  
- Technical contribution is modest: a minor adjustment to Krause & Igel’s approach (looping rank-one updates for all individuals).  
- Lemma 1 soundly shows the error from using the Cholesky factor vanishes as the algorithm converges.  
- The "optimal" complexity claim is misleading — optimality holds for non-separable ill-conditioned problems, not separable ones where diagonal adaptation suffices.  
- Empirical discrepancy: Figure 1b shows differing plateau objective values between CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES that the authors do not explain (possibly due to median plotting).  
- Incorrectly claims Suttorp et al.'s method is "prone to numerical instabilities," contradicting the original paper.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, but naming confusion and overstated claims reduce clarity.  
- Algorithms 1–2 are precisely described and useful for replication.  
- Originality is limited: core idea and Cholesky-based updates were explored by Suttorp (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015); the paper fails to distinguish its novelty.  
- Practical significance is modest: complexity reduced to quadratic but still scales poorly for large-scale problems where low-rank approximations are needed.  
- Omission of Suttorp's method from Figure 3 misleads readers; claimed speedup over Suttorp is overstated.  
- Strengths: solid theoretical justification, demonstrated runtime advantage (up to ~20× for d=64), implementation in Shark, and careful experiments.  
- Weaknesses: failure to properly compare with prior Cholesky methods, misleading naming and optimality claims, and unexplained empirical discrepancies.  
- Recommended fixes: correct naming/citations, include direct comparisons with Suttorp in all figures, clarify when the method is beneficial (non-separable ill-conditioned vs separable), and address Figure 1b discrepancy.  
- Overall: technically sound but incremental; insufficient novelty and misleading presentation lead to a recommendation to reject for NIPS.
